Abstract
Thisstudyfocusesonoptimizingacooperativedeliverysystemthatcombinesalarge-capacity
but slow carrier and two fast yet capacity-limited unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs). The
primary objective is to minimize the carrier‚Äôs total travel time while ensuring all delivery
points in predetermined order are serviced by the UAVs. We formulate this problem with an
emphasis on the Ô¨āight patterns of the two UAVs. We propose a novel optimization approach
that breaks down the complex problem into manageable subproblems by considering pairs
of UAV Ô¨āights. This decomposition enables more efÔ¨Ācient computation and better scalability
than a prior approach. Numerical experiments demonstrate that in a test instance with 20
delivery points, our method reduces computation time by over 90% and decreases the carrier‚Äôs
travel time by approximately 40% compared to an existing approach. These results suggest
signiÔ¨Ācant potential for improving the efÔ¨Āciency of cooperative delivery systems, which can
be applied to logistics and various vehicle coordination problems.

2 Literature Review
2.1 Cooperative delivery system
As can be seen in a recent survey paper by Macrina et al. (2020), the number of papers related
to drone-aided routing has increased rapidly. Synchronization in vehicle routing problems
was also surveyed in Drexl (2012).
Due to range limitations on the battery capacity, many researchers tend to consider drones
within a cooperative system with heterogenous vehicles. Thomas et al. (2024) studied a single
truck multi-drone routing and scheduling problem for last-mile parcel delivery. Manyam et al.

(2016) discussed a cooperative routing problem with a ground vehicle and a drone. The drone
must visit a series of targets while keeping a communication distance range from the ground
vehicle. Since the rendezvous points are selected from predetermined candidate locations
(not as continuous variables), they solved the path planning problem with a mixed-integer
linear programming (MILP) formulation and proposed a heuristic algorithm. Their algorithm
is capable of Ô¨Ānding optimal solutions for up to 40 target points. The rendezvous points are
also focused in Golden et al. (2023) with arc-based and path-based formulations. Luo et al.
(2017) also proposed two heuristics to solve a routing problem for a ground vehicle and its
carried drone. The Ô¨Ārst heuristic algorithm constructs an entire tour for all targets during the
rescue mission without considering any limitations and it searches sub-tours based on the
operation capacity of the drone. Their second algorithm starts by building a tour for candidate
locations for the ground vehicle, and then assigns the targets to the edges traversed by the
ground vehicle. Garone et al. (2008) discussed optimal and heuristic solutions for the fast-
rescue path planning problem. In particular, they focused on a scenario in which a ground
carrier and a carried drone can meet together. The drone can frequently change its battery
while the ground vehicle is moving on the road. Murray and Raj (2020) established an MILP
formulation with multiple drones and developed a three-phase heuristic method that can be
applied to problems with 100 customers. Cavani et al. (2021) also focused on MILP with
multiple drones, and they formulated the problem as a compact MILP formulation with valid
inequalities. Their numerical method is an exact decomposition based on a branch-and-cut
method. Raj and Murray (2020) discussed a variant of truck-drone delivery problem that
handles the drone speed as decision variables, whereas the take-off and landing locations are
chosen from predeÔ¨Āned target points. Also, Garone et al. (2012) proposed a mixed-integer
nonlinear programming (MINLP) model to decide launch and landing points such that a set
of target points can be visited in a minimum time. However, the MINLP formulation can
only tackle small instances in their numerical experiments (up to seven points).
2.2 Second-order cone programming
SOCP is an extension of linear programming and it is characterized by second-order cone
constraints of the form ‚ą•Ax + b‚ą•‚Č§cT x + d with appropriate matrix A, vectors b, c and
scalar d, where ‚ą•¬∑ ‚ą•denotes the Euclidean norm. SOCP problems appear in many applica-
tions, for example, portfolio optimization, robust least-squares problems and antenna array
weight design. We refer readers to the survey by Lobo et al. (1998). The Euclidean Jordan
algebra provides a theoretical framework of interior-point methods for solving SOCP, linear
programming, and semideÔ¨Ānite programming in a uniÔ¨Āed way (Faybusovich 1997). Commer-
cial solvers like Mosek and CPLEX, as well as open-source solvers such as SeDuMi, SDPT3,
and ECOS, are widely used. The range of SOCP applications has recently been extended to
tree breeding problems (Safarina et al. 2020, 2019; Yamashita et al. 2018), and numerical
methods for solving speciÔ¨Āc SOCP problems efÔ¨Āciently have been developed (Pirhaji et al.,
2018; Sheen and Yamashita, 2022).
2.3 Second-order cone programming for cooperative delivery system
Following the work of Garone et al. (2012), Klauňáco et al. (2014) investigated the path planning
problem and showed a more efÔ¨Ācient model. They demonstrated how to convert the mixed-
integer nonlinear formulation into a mixed-integer second-order cone problem (MISOCP).

In addition, Klauňáco et al. (2014) provided a numerical approach with multiple target visits

Gambella et al. (2018) also presented an MISOCP formulation for the ship-drone coop-

of drones, but also the visiting sequence of targets. For better efÔ¨Āciency, they developed a

drone, there is growing attention to the application of multiple drones and ships. As an exten-

simulate the trajectories of two drones‚Äô crossing paths.
In contrast to Chen et al. (2020) that considered the path planning of two drones, Fahradyan
et al. (2019) extended the cooperative system to a case with two ships and one drone. They
proposed an MISOCP formulation and a heuristic algorithm to decide the route for the three
vehicles. The main idea of their heuristic is a relaxation of integer constraints. The result
in their paper supported the advantage of their heuristic; when the number of target points
increases to 16, the computational time of their heuristic grows linearly, whereas that of the
MISOCP method increases exponentially.
2.4 Limitations of existing approaches and motivation for a novel methodology
This paper focuses on a cooperative delivery system involving one ship and two drones.
Most existing works have considered truck-drone systems with MILP formulations, where
candidate locations for drone take-off and landing are predetermined on a discrete set. Such
assumptions are not applicable for ship-drone systems, where take-off and landing locations
must be optimized as continuous variables in the Euclidean space. To allow location opti-
mization, Garone et al. (2008) proposed a MINLP formulation. However, when the problem
can be modeled as an SOCP, general nonlinear solvers are often inefÔ¨Ācient compared to meth-
ods tailored to SOCP. Several studies, such as Klauňáco et al. (2014), Gambella et al. (2018),
and Erdoňėgan and YńĪldńĪrńĪm (2021), formulated the problem as MISOCP. Nonetheless, these
models are restricted to single-drone settings and cannot capture the complex coordination of
multiple drones. Chen et al. (2020) extended the formulation to handle two drones, but relied
on PSO. While PSO is a useful heuristic approach, it cannot guarantee numerical accuracy
obtained by interior-point methods for SOCP.
These limitations underscore the need for a new SOCP-based method that can handle
multiple drones and continuous location variables, achieving accurate and scalable solutions.
The full MISOCP model introduced in Section 3 will focus on the two-drone case and
continuous locations, and it can be naturally extended to more than two drones. To ensure
efÔ¨Āciency, we particularly consider the relationship between two drones by constructing a
pairwise MISOCP problem. This is similar to Chen et al. (2020), but our approach is based
on SOCP more explicitly, which enables accurate computation of the take-off and landing
locations.

Though the simpliÔ¨Āed model reduced the number of binary variables, the MISOCP models
involve second-order cone constraints, so the computation time for solving even the simpliÔ¨Āed
model (6) is still demanding.
To efÔ¨Āciently obtain high-quality solutions, we propose two iterative second-order cone
programming (SOCP) methods called ISOCP1 and ISOCP2. The key idea behind ISOCP1
is to Ô¨Ārst get rough initial estimates of the take-off and landing locations using a fast single-

utilizing a pairwise SOCP as a subproblem to determine the scenario for each pair enables

5 Numerical Experiments
To evaluate the two SOCP-based proposed methods, ISOCP1 and ISOCP2, we conducted
numerical experiments using two types of test instances; randomly generated instances in
Section 5.1 and test instances from CVRPLIB repository (Uchoa et al., 2017) in Section 5.2.
The test environment was a Linux server with Xeon Gold 6226R CPU (2.90GHz, 16 cores)
and 192 GB memory space. We used the Julia language to implement ISOCP1 and ISOCP2.
We employed CPLEX 20.1 with 16 threads to solve SOCP and MISOCP problems, and we
stopped CPLEX when the computational time exceeded a limit of 600 seconds (this limit is
about 60 times as long as the longest computation time of ISOCP2). To obtain the visiting
sequence q1, . . . , qn, we applied the Lin-Kernighan heuristic (Helsgaun, 2000), which is
considered one of the best heuristic methods for solving the traveling salesman problem. The
actual value of M (big-M) in the mathematical models was calculated in a similar way to
Klauňáco et al. (2014), and this is essentially equivalent to computing M based on a perspective
reformulation (G√ľnl√ľk and Linderoth, 2010).

two drones. We generated q1, . . . , qn randomly in the two-dimensional Euclidean region
bounded by [0, 50 (km)]√ó[0, 50 (km)] on the x-axis and y-axis. We varied the instance size
n from 10 to 400. The ship and drone velocities were taken from Kurita (2021); vs = 18

R = 21/60 hour (21 minutes). For the PSO implementation, the same parameters as Chen

5.3 Practical Implications and Limitations
The proposed ISOCP2 method was faster and more accurate than the PSO method. These
results will be useful for real-time applications in logistics and emergency response. For
instance, rapid delivery to multiple target points plays a crucial role in disaster-affected
areas. Numerical methods to efÔ¨Āciently coordinate two drones with appropriate take-off and
landing locations can reduce response time.
While ISOCP2 provides both scalability and high solution accuracy, it assumes a Ô¨Āxed
order of target points and relies on pairwise coordination. In more complex scenarios involv-
ing dynamic target sequences or more drones, this assumption may limit its applicability.

6 Conclusion
In this research, we have developed an innovative approach to the cooperative delivery sys-
tem of a single carrier ship with two drones. We formulated this problem as a mixed-integer
second-order cone programming (MISOCP) model and demonstrated its NP-hardness, even
with a Ô¨Āxed target sequence. To develop a more streamlined model with fewer binary vari-
ables, we focused on the Ô¨āight combinations of the two drones, considering the AABB,
ABAB, and ABBA scenarios. By integrating these scenarios into a single model, we derived
a pairwise MISOCP model. Excluding the ABBA scenario further simpliÔ¨Āed the model. To
solve these models efÔ¨Āciently, we proposed two heuristic methods that utilize the pairwise
scenarios. Our Ô¨Āndings revealed that the second method, ISOCP2, is particularly effective,
yielding high-quality solutions swiftly. ISOCP2 incorporates the Ô¨Ānal adjustment phase to
improve the solution accuracy without signiÔ¨Ācantly increasing computational cost. Exten-
sive numerical experiments highlighted the efÔ¨Āciency of the proposed techniques, with the
binary-free ISOCP2 method achieving a 16-fold speedup over a particle swarm optimization
approach.
Looking forward, our research opens three promising avenues. Firstly, expanding the
model to accommodate more drones, which inherently introduces more scenarios. However,
similartoourcurrentapproach,somescenariosmaybeunnecessary,especiallywhenfocusing
on completion time. Secondly, we envision adapting the model to more general settings, such
as varying drone speeds or battery capacities, as explored in studies such as Dorling et al.
(2016). Thirdly, we extend our approach by relaxing the assumption that the visiting order
is Ô¨Āxed by a TSP solution, based on the pairwise coordination developed in this paper. This
extension increases complexity, but enables more Ô¨āexible and efÔ¨Ācient routing, especially
when the visiting order affects the optimal route. These extensions could further enhance
the applicability and robustness of our models in complex, real-world cooperative delivery
systems.
