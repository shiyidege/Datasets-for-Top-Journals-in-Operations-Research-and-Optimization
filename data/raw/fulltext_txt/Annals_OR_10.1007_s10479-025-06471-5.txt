Abstract
We study scheduling problems on parallel dedicated machines and assume that a speciﬁc
job can only be processed on one speciﬁc machine. We concentrate on solving scheduling
problems involving convex resource allocation and address three of the most fundamental
measures in scheduling theory, i.e., makespan, total load, and total weighted completion time.
Firstly, we focus on position-independent workloads, and then we study the setting of general
position-dependent workloads, i.e., the workloads are not restricted to be either monotone
functions of the job positions or any speciﬁc functions. In all problems, we assume a common
continuous and non-renewable (limited) resource and adapt known results from scheduling
theory to solve the considered problems.
Keywords Scheduling · Parallel dedicated machines · Convex resource allocation ·
Makespan · Total weighted completion time · Total load
1 Introduction
We study scheduling problems where a set of jobs are to be processed on m parallel dedicated
machines.Thejobsarepartitionedinadvanceintom disjointsubsetssuchthataspeciﬁcsubset
must be processed on one and only one of the dedicated machines, implying that each job
has to be processed on precisely one machine. Moreover, each job is deﬁned by its workload.
We assume that the system shares a common limited, non-renewable continuous recourse
that can be consumed to decrease the processing times of the jobs.
In this paper, we address the inﬂuential subject of controllable processing times. In the
ﬁrst phase of deterministic scheduling theory, researchers assumed that the job processing
times are constants that are known in advance. More advanced research challenged this model
and introduced variable job processing times. The ﬁrst concept to be introduced was that of
position-dependent processing times, and later, the concept of controllable processing times.
Studies involving position-dependent processing times were motivated by the researchers’

ing times due to the learning effect or increased processing times as the result of aging
or deterioration effects, see, e.g., the monograph of Strusevich and Rustogi (2017), which

processing time of a job is a function of its start time in a given schedule. Later studies
extended this method and introduced position-dependent processing times, where the pro-
cessing times are not restricted to either monotone functions of the job’s starting time and/or
the job’s positions in the sequence or speciﬁc functions. At ﬁrst, the input was considered a
vector of basic processing times and a learning/deterioration function, and later evolved to a
matrix of n × n job-position processing times, see, e.g., Mor and Mosheiov (2018).
Studies with controllable processing times assume that the processing times are not nec-
essarily given constants. However, in contrast to the job-position approach, the processing
times of the jobs are now a function of the resources allocated to the processing opera-
tions of the jobs. Thus, the processing times may change when supplementary resources are
allocated. This approach reﬂects various real-life problems where the improvement in the
deployment of inputs in the production system is achieved by allocating extended resources
to job processing. Oron (2016) claims that “In reality, there exist many applications where
job processing times are controlled by the allocation of non-renewable resources such as
sub-contracting, additional budget, fuel, electricity, fertilizer, etc.”
The early studies considered that the actual job processing time is either a linear or
non-linear function of the quantity of allocated resources to the processing operations of
the jobs. One of the most essential non-linear functions is where the actual job processing
time is a convex decreasing function of the amount of resource allocated to the processing
of the job. At ﬁrst, the input was assumed to be a vector consisting of n workloads, as is
evident in the comprehensive survey of Shabtay and Steiner (2007). The authors claim that the
convex function reﬂects the fundamental law of diminishing marginal returns, conﬁrming that
productivity increases at a decreasing rate with the consumed amount of resource. According
to Shabtay and Steiner (2007), convex resource allocation can be found in “many actual
government and industrial operations and … very large scale integration (VLSI) circuit
design, where the product of the silicon area (resource) and the square of time spent equals
a constant value (the workload) for an individual job”. Shabtay and Steiner (2007) provide
various theoretical and practical problems involving wide-ranging objective functions on
different machine settings, varying from single to multi-machine settings. Recent studies
addressing convex resource allocation include, among others: Wang and Wang (2012), Geng
et al., (2019), Sun et al., (2019), Liang et al., (2020), Liu and Jiang (2020), Lu et al., (2021),
Mor et al., (2021), Mosheiov et al., (2021), Wang et al. (2021), Shabtay (2022), Zhao et al.
(2022), Wang et al. (2023), and Wang and Wang (2023).
As in position-dependent processing times, the vector of n workloads eventually evolved
to n × n job-position workloads matrix. Oron (2016) was the ﬁrst to introduce the model of
n×n job-position workloads. The author assumed a convex resource allocation and position-
dependent workloads, such that a continuous non-renewable resource can be consumed to
decrease the processing times of the jobs. Where continuous means that any existing resource
can be assigned to any given job. Oron (2016) proved that, instead of solving a highly
computationally complex constrained Non-Linear Program (NLP), the optimal sequence
can be achieved by solving a Linear Assignment Problem (LAP), implying a polynomial

any speciﬁc functions, on condition that the cost function can be formulated as the scalar

As mentioned above, we focus on the setting of m parallel dedicated machines. Actual life
manifestations of this machine setting are, e.g., the scenario where each machine is contracted
to a speciﬁc agent (client) or that each machine can process only a speciﬁc product. Papers
addressing parallel dedicated machines that were published recently include, for instance,
Cheng et al., (2019), Lee and Jang (2019), Harbaoui and Khalfallah (2020), Cheng et al.,
(2021), Kim and Lee (2021), Módos et al., (2021), Demirtas (2022), Hoffmann et al., (2022),
Hajji et al., (2023), Lee et al., (2023), Mor and Mosheiov (2024), and the overview of
manufacturing systems by Dlamini et al. (2023).
In this study, we combine the setting of m parallel dedicated machines and convex resource
allocation with a continuous non-renewable resource. The addressed system-level objective
functions are (i) minimizing the makespan, (ii) minimizing the total load, and (iii) mini-
mizing the total weighted completion time. Firstly, we assume a vector of n workloads, and
secondly, extend the ﬁndings to the general case where the input consists of n×n job-position
workloads.
The paper is organized as follows: Sect. 2 contains the notation and the formulation of
the problems. In Sect. 3, we address the setting of position-independent workloads. In Sect.
3.1–3.2, we introduce closed-form solutions to minimize two different scheduling measures,
the makespan and the total load, respectively. In Sect. 3.3, we provide an exponential dynamic
programming algorithm for minimizing the total weighted completion time of the system and
a closed-form solution for the special case of minimizing the total completion time of the
system. In Sect. 4, we address the setting of position-dependent workloads. Similarly, in Sect.
4.1–4.2, we provide closed-form solutions for corresponding ways to minimize the makespan
and the total load. In Sect. 4.3, we present an exponential dynamic programming algorithm
for minimizing the total weighted completion time and a closed-form solution for the total
completion time of the system. Conclusions and ideas for future research are provided in the
last section.

5 Conclusion
For a complete exposition, we note that Shabtay and Steiner (2007) postulated that there are
four different types of scheduling problems with controllable processing times:
Type 1: Minimizing the total integrated cost of the scheduling measure and consumed
resource.
Type 2: Minimizing the scheduling measure subject to an upper bound on the available
resource.
Type 3: Minimizing the consumed resource subject to an upper bound on the scheduling
measure.
Type 4: Identifying the set of Pareto-optimal schedules for the scheduling measure and
the consumed resource.
Thus, challenging and exciting future research can address (i) Types 1, 3, and 4 with
convex resource allocation; (ii) Types 1–4 assuming linear controllable processing times;
(iii) More complex machine settings such as ﬂexible machines or reconﬁgurable machines,
see the survey of Dlamini et al. (2023).
