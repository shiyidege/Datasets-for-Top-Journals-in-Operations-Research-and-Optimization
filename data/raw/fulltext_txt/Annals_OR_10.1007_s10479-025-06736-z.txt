Abstract
This study investigates the application of Multiple Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) 
methods for portfolio selection in the Vietnamese stock market using daily stock price data 
from the VN100 index spanning January 2015 to November 2023. Creating 150 criteria 
based on stock returns, volatility, and correlation, we employ five popular MCDA methods 
and four weighting methods to compare the performance of up to 500 portfolios. While 
MCDA methods may not effectively differentiate between stocks with higher and lower 
future returns, they consistently excel in selecting portfolios with superior risk-adjusted 
returns and lower drawdown compared to both the benchmark and the traditional Mean-
Variance (MV) method. The PROMETHEE II (Preference Ranking Organization Method 
for Enrichment of Evaluations) method stands out as a robust performer, and the CRITIC 
(Criteria Importance Through Intercriteria Correlation) weighting method emerges as a 
reliable choice for constructing portfolios with favorable risk-adjusted returns. Moreover, 
the MCDA methods demonstrate potential computational efficiency over the tested MV 
implementation, enhancing their practicality. These findings highlight the practical utility 
of MCDA, particularly PROMETHEE II with CRITIC weighting, for navigating the com­
plexities of portfolio optimization in dynamic emerging markets like Vietnam, offering a 
compelling alternative to traditional approaches.

PROMETHEE II · Decision support systems · Vietnamese stock markets

1  Introduction
Optimizing portfolio selection in emerging markets is challenging due to dynamic envi­
ronments and characteristics that often diverge from assumptions of traditional portfolio 
theories. Vietnam, with its rapid economic growth (IMF, 2024a; World Bank, 2019) and a 
vibrant, complex stock market (Cuong & Hai, 2024; World Bank, 2019), represents a com­
pelling case for exploring advanced portfolio optimization methods beyond conventional 
models.
The Vietnamese stock market is marked by high volatility, limited liquidity, investor 
heterogeneity, dominant retail investor behavior, complex governance, and imperfect infor­
mation disclosure (IMF, 2024a; World Bank, 2019; FiinGroup, 2024; Phan et al., 2018; 
Vo, 2017; OECD, 2022; U.S. Department of State, 2023). These features undermine core 
assumptions of standard approaches like the Mean-Variance (MV) model (Markowitz, 
1952), suggesting that MV’s application is especially limited in Vietnam, where risk and 
return dynamics are complex and multi-dimensional (Michaud, 1989).
To address these market complexities, Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) meth­
ods have gained traction in financial decision-making (Aouni et al., 2018; Spronk et al., 
2005; Zopounidis & Doumpos, 2002). MCDA enables the integration of diverse, often con­
flicting criteria–quantitative and qualitative–offering a more flexible alternative to mean-
variance optimization (Aouni et al., 2018; Doumpos & Zopounidis, 2022; Salo et al., 2024). 
Although MCDA methods have been applied in some Vietnamese finance studies (Nguyen 
et al., 2022), there remains a lack of comprehensive research directly comparing multiple 
MCDA techniques for portfolio selection, especially when accounting for Vietnam’s dis­
tinctive market factors and benchmarking against MV and the market index.
This study addresses this gap by systematically applying and comparing five prominent 
MCDA methods (PROMETHEE II (Albadvi et al., 2007), TOPSIS, VIKOR, WSM, and 
WPM (Zhou & Ang, 2008; Wang et al., 2024)) and four criterion weighting approaches 
(CRITIC, CILOS, Entropy, and Equal Weights (Ozcalici, 2022)) for portfolio selection 
among VN100 constituents from January 2015 to November 2023. Their performance is 
benchmarked against portfolios based on the VN100 index and the MV method. Vietnam’s 
market capitalization–about 68% of GDP as of May 2024 (Vietnam News, 2024)–under­
lines the practical value of developing nuanced, market-specific portfolio strategies. By pro­
viding empirical evidence of MCDA’s effectiveness in this environment, the study informs 
investment decisions for both domestic and international investors, as well as policymakers. 
Our methodology employs an extensive set of 150 criteria based on stock returns, volatility, 
and correlation, aligning with approaches in Alali and Tolga (2019).
This study offers important contributions to the Operations Research (OR) community 
by systematically applying and benchmark-testing multiple MCDA techniques and weight­
ing methods in a real-world, complex emerging market context. Our analysis highlights 
practical implementation challenges, methodological differences, and performance out­
comes across MCDA approaches, providing insights for the application of multi-criteria 
decision-making in portfolio optimization. While our findings have implications for finance 
practice, our primary focus is on advancing OR methodologies and their utility in navigating 
non-traditional, high-uncertainty environments such as Vietnam’s stock market. In pursuit 
of these contributions, our study addresses the following research questions:

2  Literature review
2.1  Traditional portfolio optimization approaches
The Mean-Variance (MV) optimization model, introduced by Markowitz (1952), has been 
the cornerstone of modern portfolio theory. The MV method focuses on constructing a port­
folio that offers the highest expected return for a given level of risk, or equivalently, the low­
est risk for a given level of expected return. This method quantifies risk through the variance 
or standard deviation of portfolio returns and assumes that investors are risk-averse, seeking 
to maximize returns while minimizing risk.
The MV framework has been widely adopted and has significantly influenced investment 
strategies and financial models. However, the MV method has limitations, particularly in 
its reliance on the estimation of expected returns and covariances, which can be unstable 
and sensitive to input errors Michaud (1989). Moreover, the MV method considers only the 
mean and variance of returns, potentially overlooking other important factors influencing 
investment decisions.
2.2  Multi-criteria decision analysis in portfolio selection
To address the limitations of the MV method, researchers have explored alternative 
approaches that can incorporate multiple criteria beyond risk and return. Multi-Criteria 
Decision Analysis (MCDA) methods have emerged as powerful tools in this context, allow­
ing decision-makers to evaluate alternatives based on a set of conflicting criteria (Xadonis 
et al., 2010, 2013, 2023). The application of multicriteria decision aid (MCDA) methods

to financial problems gained significant traction as the complexity of financial decision-
making increased. A seminal review by Zopounidis and Doumpos (2002), a categorized 
bibliographic study by Xidonas et al. (2010a, 2010b) and, more recently, Kandakoglu et 
al. (2023) have comprehensively documented the contributions of MCDA in finance and 
portfolio selection, highlighting its ability to handle multiple conflicting criteria inherent 
in areas such as portfolio selection, credit risk assessment, and corporate finance, thereby 
providing a structured approach beyond traditional single-objective optimization. Build­
ing upon earlier work, the field has continued to evolve, with subsequent comprehensive 
reviews further solidifying the role of MCDA in finance. Notably, Spronk et al. (2005), in 
their chapter within the updated Multiple Criteria Decision Analysis: State of the Art Sur­
veys handbook, provide a broad overview of the methodologies and applications prevalent 
in more recent years, underscoring the sustained relevance of MCDA for tackling contem­
porary financial complexities.
The chronological evolution of MCDA applications in financial decision-making begins 
with Dominiak (1997) pioneering work on the Warsaw Stock Exchange, where MCDA 
methods were utilized to enhance investment decision processes. Tamiz et al. (1997) con­
ducted a comparative analysis within the British FTSE 100 index, demonstrating the appli­
cability of MCDA techniques in developed markets. Similarly, Zopounidis et al. (1998) 
applied the ADELAIS multi-objective linear programming system on the Athens Stock 
Exchange, highlighting the adaptability of MCDA techniques to different market contexts.
Bouri et al. (2002) combined ELECTRE (Elimination and Choice Expressing Real­
ity), AHP (Analytic Hierarchy Process), and PROMETHEE on the Tunisian stock market, 
emphasizing the synergy of different MCDA techniques in enhancing portfolio selection. 
Christian and Nadine (2002) introduced the ELECTRE-TRI and MINORA (Multicriteria 
Interactive Ordinal Regression Analysis) techniques on the Paris Stock Exchange, show­
casing advanced methods for synthesizing criteria associated with common risk. Bana e 
Costa and Soares, (2004) implemented MACBETH (Measuring Attractiveness by a Cat­
egorical Based Evaluation Technique), focusing on normalizing weights and comparing 
performance with benchmark models.
Specific MCDA methods have been applied to the stock selection problem. For example, 
Albadvi et al. (2007) utilized the PROMETHEE method, an outranking approach, to rank 
stocks within the Tehran Stock Exchange by first evaluating industries and then individual 
companies based on multiple criteria. Fazli and Jafari (2012) introduced a hybrid MCDA 
model on Iran’s stock exchange, showcasing the evolving complexity and robustness of 
MCDA applications. Ünvan (2019) utilized TOPSIS (Technique for Order Preference 
by Similarity to Ideal Solution) analysis on BIST30 stocks in Turkey, further illustrating 
MCDA’s adaptability to different market environments. Nguyen and Gordon-Brown (2012) 
applied Simple Additive Weighting (SAW) and TOPSIS for portfolio allocation using trap­
ezoidal fuzzy numbers, highlighting the incorporation of uncertainty in decision-making 
models.
Beyond theoretical proposals, research has also focused on empirically comparing the 
effectiveness of different MCDM techniques in practical portfolio selection. For instance, 
Patari et al. (2018) compared median-scaling, TOPSIS, AHP, and additive DEA for US 
equity selection using value and momentum factors. Their findings suggest that these meth­
ods can successfully identify stocks with superior future performance, although they also 
highlighted that the choice of MCDM method can lead to significantly different portfolio

characteristics and factor exposures, indicating that method selection itself is a crucial con­
sideration. More recent studies have continued to expand the application of MCDA meth­
ods, Alali and Tolga (2019) presented the TODIM method (an acronym in Portuguese for 
Interactive Multi-criteria Decision Making), applying it to portfolio allocation using empiri­
cal US equity data and demonstrating its impact on portfolio performance. Ozcalici (2022)

risk-adjusted performance in empirical tests.
A significant challenge lies in bridging the gap between sophisticated theoretical portfolio 
optimization models and practical asset management. Addressing this, Xidonas et al. (2016, 
2021) proposed an integrated multiobjective optimization framework designed explicitly 
for practical application. Their approach incorporates crucial real-world constraints often 
ignored in simpler models, such as cardinality limits, minimum transaction sizes (buy-in 
thresholds), and transaction costs, thereby enhancing the operational relevance of the result­
ing portfolio strategies.
2.3  The Vietnamese stock market context and research gap
While the preceding sections reviewed the evolution from traditional MV (Markowitz, 
1952) to the application of MCDA methods in finance globally (Zopounidis & Doumpos, 
2002; Spronk et al., 2005; Aouni et al., 2018), a deeper understanding of the specific context 
of the Vietnamese stock market is crucial to motivate the present study. As highlighted in 
the Introduction, Vietnam presents a unique environment for portfolio optimization due to 
several interconnected factors that challenge conventional assumptions.
Vietnam’s economy has undergone a remarkable transformation, evolving into one of 
Southeast Asia’s most dynamic emerging markets with high average GDP growth rates 
(IMF, 2024a). However, this growth has been accompanied by increased economic and 
market volatility, particularly in recent years, influenced by global shocks (like interest rate 
hikes) and domestic adjustments (such as financial sector disruptions, real estate and cor­
porate bond market turbulence, and specific bank distress events) (IMF, 2024a, b; World 
Bank, 2019). The stock market, established in the early 2000s, has grown substantially but 
retains characteristics typical of frontier/emerging markets, including higher volatility and 
lower liquidity compared to more mature counterparts (Cuong & Hai, 2024). This volatility 
is not merely noise but often linked to policy interventions by the State Bank of Vietnam 
and specific economic events (IMF, 2024a, b). Lower liquidity, reflected in moderate turn­
over ratios reported by World Bank (2019) and constrained by a relatively low free-float of 
shares (estimated around 45.5% (FiinGroup, 2024)), implies potentially higher transaction 
costs and execution risk. Importantly, Cuong and Hai (2024) show that liquidity dimensions 
like bid-ask spreads and market depth significantly impact stock returns, especially during 
crises like the COVID-19 outbreak, suggesting liquidity risk is a distinct factor beyond 
price variance. Some analyses also suggest foreign investor trading might negatively affect 
market liquidity (Tri, 2017).

The market’s classification as a Frontier Market by MSCI (U.S. Department of State, 
2023; MSCI, 2020) points towards potential market inefficiencies. This is further suggested 
by the dominance of retail investors (approx. 61% ownership as of mid-2024 (FiinGroup, 
2024)), who exhibit documented behavioral biases like overconfidence and herding tenden­
cies (Phan et al., 2018). In contrast, institutional investors (both domestic and foreign) dis­
play clear preferences for larger, older firms with better corporate governance (e.g., smaller 
board sizes, separation of CEO and Chairman roles), VN30 membership, lower leverage, 
and high book-to-market ratios (Nguyen & Tran, 2022). This investor heterogeneity chal­
lenges models based on homogenous, rational agents.
Furthermore, the regulatory environment is dynamic, with ongoing reforms related to 
foreign ownership limits (FOLs) (U.S. Department of State, 2023; Global Referral Group, 
2025), market infrastructure (U.S. Department of State, 2023), and transparency initiatives 
aimed at achieving an Emerging Market status upgrade by providers like FTSE Russell 
(Vietcap, 2024). However, inconsistent regulatory interpretation and enforcement can pose 
challenges for investors (U.S. Department of State, 2019). State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs) 
account for roughly a third of GDP, as estimated by OECD (2022), and face governance 
challenges such as lack of transparency, potentially limited board autonomy, and prefer­
ential access to resources (OECD, 2022; U.S. Department of State, 2019). These factors 
add another layer of complexity and potential information asymmetry (World Bank, 2019; 
Vo, 2017; OECD, 2022). The SOE equitization (privatization) process, while reducing the 
number of SOEs significantly since the 1990 s, continues to face hurdles (Dang et al., 2021; 
OECD, 2022).
These specific characteristics–non-standard volatility, significant liquidity effects, poten­
tial inefficiencies linked to frontier status and investor behavior, regulatory uncertainty, and 
SOE governance concerns–collectively render traditional portfolio optimization methods 
like MV (Markowitz, 1952), which rely on simplifying assumptions (normal distributions, 
risk=variance, rational homogenous investors, efficient information), potentially inadequate 
for the Vietnamese market, as argued by Michaud (1989). They underscore the need for 
approaches that can handle multiple, diverse criteria, including qualitative factors and dif­
ferent risk dimensions.
MCDA methods offer a promising alternative framework due to their inherent ability 
to integrate various quantitative and qualitative criteria (Aouni et al., 2018; Doumpos & 
Zopounidis, 2022). In Vietnam, Nguyen et al. (2022) evaluated bank performance during 
COVID-19 using MCDA. However, these studies typically focus on specific sectors or rank­
ing problems rather than comprehensive portfolio optimization. Ozcalici (2022) explored 
various weighting techniques, but no study has systematically compared the effectiveness of 
different MCDA methodologies (such as PROMETHEE II (Albadvi et al., 2007), TOPSIS, 
VIKOR, WSM, WPM) and weighting techniques specifically for constructing and manag­
ing equity portfolios within the unique Vietnamese context, nor rigorously benchmarked 
performance against traditional MV optimization and market indices.
Therefore, a significant gap persists in the literature regarding the comparative effective­
ness of various MCDA strategies for practical portfolio optimization tailored to the specific 
complexities and dynamics of the Vietnamese stock market. This study directly addresses 
this gap by providing a systematic evaluation and comparison, offering insights into the 
applicability and potential benefits of using MCDA techniques in this important and evolv­
ing market. The inclusion of methods like PROMETHEE II, alongside others, and consid­

3  Methodology
3.1  Dataset
For our experiments, comprehensive stock price data was employed, utilizing monthly price 
information based on returns derived from close prices. The dataset encompasses the stocks 
listed in the VN100 index, a benchmark index comprising 100 stocks representing the Viet­
namese financial market. The dataset spans an extensive timeframe, commencing in January 
2015 and extending through November 2023. The selection of the VN100 index as our data 
source ensures the inclusion of a diverse range of stocks, contributing to the robustness and 
representativeness of our analysis. The data, amounting to a substantial 177,299 observa­
tions, was curated from reputable financial sources, with index composition details sourced 
from www.investing.com in the format of the OHLC (Open, High, Low, Close) data which 
is structured to provide a comprehensive representation of monthly stock price movements. 
This approach to data selection and extraction is pivotal to ensuring the reliability and accu­
racy of our findings, laying the foundation for the subsequent analyses and conclusions 
drawn from the study.
3.2  Data processing
In this segment, we delve into the process of converting preprocessed data into the requi­
site criteria for decision-making. The criteria employed in this study were derived from a 
set utilized in the research conducted by Alali and Tolga (2019) and Ozcalici (2022). Alali 
and Tolga (2019) incorporated nine criteria in their investigation, specifically short-term 
return, mid-term return, long-term return, short-term standard deviation, mid-term standard 
deviation, long-term standard deviation, short-term correlation, mid-term correlation, and 
long-term correlation.
In a parallel approach, Ozcalici (2022) adopted similar criteria but modified the temporal 
scope into smaller intervals. Instead of three sub-periods (short-term, mid-term, and long-
term), they segmented the look-back periods of 250 days into 50 sub-periods of 5 days, 
termed as “sectional.” Subsequently, they calculated the average return, standard deviation, 
and correlation for each of these sub-periods, along with cumulative return, standard devia­
tion, and correlation for periods ranging from 246 days to 1 day before the portfolio selec­
tion point. Ultimately, Ozcalici (2022) arrived at 297 distinct criteria.
For our study, we partially aligned with their methodology, specifically considering their 
cumulative criteria, which encompass the rolling mean, standard deviation, and correlation 
from the last 250 days up to the last 5 days. We opted to exclude their "sectional" criteria, 
deeming it improbable for typical investors to base stock evaluations on the performance 
over a mere 5 days of the preceding year.

3.4  Weighting methods
In this section, we delve into four prominent methods associated with objective criteria 
weighting, which currently stand out in the existing literature. It is important to note that, 
in the future, this set of methods can be expanded to incorporate other relevant approaches. 
1. CRITIC (Criteria Importance Through Intercriteria Correlation) Assigns weights 
based on the contrast intensity and conflict among criteria, capturing both the variability 
and the inter-criterion correlation (Diakoulaki et al., 1995).
2. CILOS (Criterion Impact Loss) Evaluates the importance of each criterion by measur­
ing the impact of its removal on the overall evaluation (Zavadskas & Podvezko, 2016).
3. Entropy Method Calculates weights based on the entropy value of each criterion, reflect­
ing the amount of information provided by the criterion (Lotfi & Fallahnejad, 2010).
4. Equal Weights Assigns equal importance to all criteria, serving as a baseline for 
comparison.
To implement these weighting methods, we utilized the pyDecision library (Pereira et al., 
2024), which offers a comprehensive suite of weighting techniques, including CRITIC, 
CILOS, Entropy, and Equal Weights. This library facilitated the efficient computation of 
criterion weights and enabled us to analyze the impact of different weighting schemes on 
portfolio selection and performance.

3.6  Trading experiments
After gaining insight into the computation of decision criteria and the estimation of weights 
for each criterion, along with understanding how MCDA methods utilize these criteria to 
rank alternatives, this section delves into the setup of trading experiments. The evaluation 
of the potential of these methods for portfolio selection is discussed. Our dataset comprises 
price data spanning from January 2015 to November 2023, covering approximately 1,773 
trading days. The criteria, derived from the returns of the preceding 250 trading days, result 
in a decision vector of 150 criteria for each of around 100 stocks, considering a total of 1523 
trading days from April 2016 to November 2023 after the criteria calculation process.
The trading experiments are designed to incorporate each of the five MCDA methods 
(Weighted Sum, Weighted Product, VIKOR, TOPSIS, and PROMETHEE II) on a daily 
basis, paired with each of the four weighting methods (Equal-weight, Entropy, CILOS, and 
CRITIC). These methods utilize a decision vector consisting of 150 criteria to rank the 100 
stocks in the VN100 index, resulting in a total of 20 combinations (5 × 4 = 20). The top-
1 3
333

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 353:321–351
ranking m stocks are identified as “Top” portfolios, while the bottom-ranking m stocks are 
labeled as “Bottom” portfolios. This dual portfolio approach allows for a comparison of the 
methods in effectively distinguishing between high-ranking and low-ranking stocks. While 
“Bottom” portfolios could be valuable for implementing shorting or Long-Short strategies, 
these were excluded due to the complexities associated with shorting in the Vietnamese 
stock market. Shorting individual stocks is often either unavailable or subject to various 
restrictions in emerging markets like Vietnam. Obtaining shares for shorting can be chal­
lenging and may involve high borrowing fees, rendering the shorting of individual stocks 
impractical.
Each of the chosen portfolios is subsequently retained for the next k days, with each stock 
allocated an equal weight. The determination of the optimal holding period is approached 
by selecting k from the set {5, 10, ..., 25}–indicating that the portfolios will be held for up 
to the next 25 trading days, approximately a month. Shorter holding periods, such as 1 or 2 
days, are omitted due to the Vietnamese stock market’s T+2 settlement cycle, wherein settle­
ment occurs two business days after the order is executed.
Given the daily selection of portfolios over the 1,523 trading days, an overlapping period 
arises where a new portfolio is established each day while the portfolio from the previous 
day is still being held. To address this, the weight of each stock in the portfolio is divided 
by k, meaning that if k = 5, 1/5 of the capital is used to purchase today’s portfolio, 1/5 for 
tomorrow’s, and so on until the fifth day.
Similarly, m is chosen from {5, 10, ..., 25} to cover up to around 50% of the index for 
both “Top” and “Bottom” portfolios. Consequently, there are a total of 5 × 4 × 5 × 5 = 500 
combinations for the “Top” and “Bottom” portfolios combined.
So how can we assess the effectiveness of the MCDA methods in tackling the portfo­
lio selection problem? In addition to comparing the performance of the “Top” and “Bot­
tom” portfolios, as previously discussed, this study introduces a benchmark portfolio for the 
VN100 index. This benchmark portfolio mirrors one that encompasses every stock in the 
index with equal weight and is retained for varying periods, specifically for k ∈{5, 10, .., 25} 
days. When coupled with evaluating straightforward methods like Weighted Sum or Equal 
Weight, we ultimately have three distinct approaches to gauge the efficacy of MCDA meth­
ods in portfolio selection.
Also to further enhance the robustness of our comparative analysis, we incorporate the 
traditional Mean-Variance (MV) optimization model proposed by Markowitz (1952) as an 
additional benchmark in our study. The MV framework serves as a foundational approach 
in portfolio optimization, balancing expected returns against portfolio variance to construct 
an efficient frontier of optimal portfolios. By including the MV approach, we aim to evalu­
ate how our MCDA methods perform relative to a well-established optimization technique.
In applying the MV approach to our dataset, we utilize the same look-back period of 
the last 250 trading days, consistent with the criteria creation for our MCDA methods. We 
generate efficient portfolios by solving the quadratic optimization problem that minimizes 
portfolio variance for a given level of expected return, subject to the constraints of full 
investment and no short selling. This process is repeated for different investing periods to 
assess performance over time.
The inclusion of the MV approach allows for a direct comparison between traditional 
optimization methods and MCDA techniques in the context of the Vietnamese stock market. 
This comparison is particularly relevant given the unique market characteristics, such as 
1 3
334

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 353:321–351
higher volatility and lower liquidity, which may impact the effectiveness of different port­
folio optimization strategies.
Finally, to conduct a thorough evaluation and comparison of the diverse methods and 
benchmarks, it is essential to establish a well-defined set of performance metrics. Within the 
scope of this paper, four distinct metrics are computed for each portfolio, namely annualized 
return, annualized standard deviation, annualized Sharpe Ratio, and Maximum Drawdown. 
Each of these metrics provides unique insights into the performance characteristics of the 
portfolios under consideration.
The annualized return serves as a fundamental measure, offering a comprehensive view 
of the portfolio’s overall profitability over a yearly timeframe. Complementing this, the 
annualized standard deviation measures the extent of variability or risk associated with 
the portfolio’s returns. A lower standard deviation suggests a more stable and less volatile 
investment.
The Sharpe Ratio assumes a central role in this evaluation, serving as the primary metric 
for assessing strategy performance. This ratio quantifies the excess return of a strategy per 
unit of risk undertaken. A higher Sharpe ratio indicates not only a higher return but also a 
more efficient use of risk, signifying superior risk-adjusted performance.
In addition to these metrics, the Maximum Drawdown is considered. This metric pro­
vides insights into the largest loss experienced by the portfolio over a specific period, offer­
ing a perspective on the downside risk and potential for significant capital erosion.
By employing these comprehensive metrics, the analysis aims to provide a multifaceted 
assessment, allowing for a nuanced understanding of the performance dynamics and risk 
profiles associated with each portfolio. The emphasis on the Sharpe ratio underscores its 
significance as a key indicator for evaluating the efficiency of investment strategies in man­
aging risk and generating returns.
4  Result and discussion
This section presents the empirical results of our portfolio selection experiments. We begin 
by summarizing the key findings upfront: MCDA methods, particularly PROMETHEE II 
combined with the CRITIC weighting scheme, consistently generate portfolios with supe­
rior risk-adjusted performance (higher Sharpe Ratios, lower Maximum Drawdowns) com­
pared to both the VN100 benchmark and the traditional Mean-Variance (MV) optimization 
approach within the Vietnamese market context. While MCDA does not perfectly separate 
stocks based on future absolute returns alone, its strength lies in navigating the market’s vol­
atility and complexity, aligning with our motivation to find suitable strategies for Vietnam’s 
unique dynamics. Furthermore, the tested MCDA implementations demonstrate notable 
computational efficiency advantages over our benchmark MV implementation, enhancing 
their practical applicability. The following subsections provide detailed analyses supporting 
these main conclusions.
Given the substantial number of 500 portfolios in total, conducting a detailed analysis 
of each one individually would be impractical. Consequently, this study will present an 
in-depth examination of selected portfolios, offering insights into their performance. The 
subsequent sections will shift the focus towards a comparative analysis, delving into distinc­
tions between each MCDA method or weighting method. This comparative approach will

4.4  Computational efficiency analysis
Beyond performance metrics, the practical feasibility of portfolio optimization methods 
often hinges on their computational efficiency. Computational efficiency is a critical factor 
in portfolio optimization, especially when dealing with large datasets and the need for rapid 
decision-making in financial markets (Jobst et al., 2001). Methods that are computation­
ally intensive may not be practical for real-time applications or high-frequency trading. 
In this section, we compare the computational efficiency of the selected MCDA methods 
and weighting schemes with the traditional MV optimization method based on the specific 
implementations used in our study.
To evaluate computational efficiency, we recorded the execution times of each method 
during the portfolio selection process on our hardware in Python. The execution times for 
the specific implementations used in this study, along with theoretical time complexities 
where applicable, are presented in Table 6.
The theoretical time complexities generally align with the observed execution times for 
our implementations. MCDA methods like PROMETHEE II, involving pairwise compari­
sons, have higher computational demands (O(n2m)) compared to simpler aggregation or 
distance-based methods (O(nm)).

The standard MV method, when solved using generic quadratic programming solvers, 
can exhibit cubic time complexity (O(n3)), primarily due to matrix operations like inver­
sion needed for dense covariance matrices (Markowitz, 1952; Luenberger, 1998). In our 
tests, the specific MV implementation yielded an execution time of 0.0595 s.
However, it is crucial to acknowledge significant advancements in specialized algorithms 
designed specifically for computing the entire mean-variance efficient frontier, particularly 
for problems with standard box constraints. Markowitz’s own Critical Line Algorithm 
(CLA) provides a parametric approach (Markowitz, 1956). Niedermayer and Niedermayer 
(2010), using a highly optimized Fortran 90 implementation of an enhanced CLA, reported 
computing the full efficient frontier for N=2000 assets with dense matrices in just 0.78 s 
(Niedermayer & Niedermayer, 2010). Similarly, Hirschberger et al. (2010) developed a 
Parametric Quadratic Programming (PQP) procedure capable of computing the entire fron­
tier for dense matrix problems with N=2000 assets in approximately 37 s and N=3000 assets 
in about 100 s (Hirschberger et al., 2010). These specialized algorithms demonstrate that 
computing the exact efficient frontier for large-scale, box-constrained MV problems can be 
achieved with remarkable speed, significantly faster than suggested by the complexity of 
generic QP solvers or less optimized implementations.
Therefore, while our tested MCDA implementations (e.g., PROMETHEE II with 
CRITIC taking approx. 0.017s) were faster than our tested MV implementation (0.0595s), 
this comparison reflects the specific tools used in this study. State-of-the-art specialized 
algorithms like enhanced CLA can compute the full MV efficient frontier much faster than 
either (Niedermayer & Niedermayer, 2010). The relative efficiency can also depend on 
whether the goal is to compute a single point or the entire frontier, and the complexity 
increases substantially if non-standard constraints (e.g., cardinality constraints, turning the 
problem NP-hard (Cesarone et al., 2013; Liagkouras & Metaxiotis, 2018; Anagnostopoulos 
& Mamanis, 2011)) are introduced, which might alter the comparative advantage depending 
on how different methods handle such constraints. Nevertheless, the observed efficiency of 
the MCDA methods used here supports their potential for practical application, particularly 
in scenarios requiring frequent rebalancing or where highly specialized MV solvers are not 
readily available or applicable due to specific problem structures.

5  Conclusion and future works
5.1  Conclusion
In this study, we conducted an in-depth analysis of the potential application of Multiple 
Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) methods for portfolio selection in the Vietnamese stock 
markets. Utilizing stock price data from the VN100 index spanning from January 2015 to 
November 2023, we created 150 criteria based on stock returns, volatility, and correlation 
over a window of up to 250 previous days. Employing five popular MCDA methods (PRO­
METHEE II, TOPSIS, VIKOR, Weighted Sum, and Weighted Product) and four widely-
used weighting methods (CRITIC, CILOS, Equal-weight, and Entropy), we executed a 
comprehensive trading experiment to compare the performance of up to 500 portfolios con­
sisting of the top-ranking and bottom-ranking stocks.
Our findings indicate that while the selected MCDA methods may not effectively differ­
entiate between stocks with higher and lower future returns, they exhibit efficacy in select­
ing portfolios with superior risk-adjusted returns and lower drawdown compared to both the 
benchmark and the MV method. Notably, the PROMETHEE II method consistently outper­
formed other MCDA methods across various settings, showcasing its robustness in portfolio 
selection. When compared to the MV method, portfolios constructed using MCDA methods 
demonstrated higher annualized returns and Sharpe Ratios, indicating better performance 
in terms of risk-adjusted returns. Moreover, our computational efficiency analysis revealed 
that the tested MCDA implementations, particularly when combined with efficient weight­
ing schemes like CRITIC, offer notable advantages over our tested MV implementation in 
terms of execution time. While acknowledging that highly specialized MV algorithms can 
be significantly faster, the observed efficiency of the MCDA methods used here supports 
their practicality, especially in scenarios requiring frequent rebalancing or where specialized 
MV solvers are not readily available or applicable.
In terms of weighting methods, the CRITIC method emerged as a reliable choice, often 
outperforming other alternatives and the MV method. Despite not dominating the other 
weighting methods as the PROMETHEE II method dominates the other MCDA methods, 
the CRITIC method demonstrated consistent effectiveness in constructing portfolios with 
favorable risk-adjusted returns. The inclusion of the MV method in our analysis further 
underscores the effectiveness of the weighting methods; while the MV method provided 
some improvement over the benchmark, it fell short when compared to the portfolios 
derived from the weighting methods, particularly in terms of Sharpe Ratios and maximum 
drawdowns
In summary, our study underscores the potential of MCDA methods, particularly PRO­
METHEE II, in enhancing portfolio selection in the Vietnamese stock markets. The CRITIC 
weighting method has been identified as a robust companion in achieving superior risk-
adjusted performance. By demonstrating both superior risk-adjusted performance and 
notable computational efficiency compared to our benchmark MV implementation, MCDA 
methods, particularly PROMETHEE II paired with CRITIC weighting, present a compel­
ling and practical alternative for portfolio construction in the specific context of the Viet­
namese stock market. This work adds to the understanding of financial decision-making 
and provides a foundation for further research on the intersection of MCDA methods and 
portfolio optimization in dynamic market environments.

5.2  Future works
While our study sheds light on the application of MCDA methods for portfolio optimization 
in the Vietnamese stock market, several areas present opportunities for further research:
 
●
Incorporation of Additional Criteria Future studies could include non-financial criteria 
such as environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors, sustainability measures, 
and climate risk indicators. Including these variables may enhance the investment deci­
sion-making process, especially as more data becomes available in the Vietnamese mar­
ket. Building on this, our forthcoming research focuses on creating a comprehensive set 
of ESG criteria specific to several markets including Vietnam (Goutte et al., 2023; Diep 
et al., 2024). These studies aim to establish robust frameworks for incorporating ESG 
considerations into portfolio optimization, which could significantly benefit investors 
seeking sustainable investment opportunities in both emerging and developed markets.
 
●
Comparative Analysis Across Markets Extending the analysis to other emerging and 
developed markets would help assess the generalizability of our findings. Comparing 
the effectiveness of MCDA methods across different market conditions could provide a 
broader understanding of their applicability.
 
●
Dynamic Market Conditions and Adaptive Models Investigating the performance of 
MCDA methods during periods of market stress or volatility, such as financial crises 
or economic shocks, could offer insights into their robustness. Additionally, developing 
adaptive models that adjust to changing market dynamics may further enhance portfolio 
performance.
 
●
Integration with Machine Learning Techniques Combining MCDA methods with ma­
chine learning algorithms could improve predictive accuracy and decision-making ef­
ficiency. This integration may enable the development of more sophisticated models 
capable of handling larger datasets and complex market patterns (Xadonis et al., 2025).
