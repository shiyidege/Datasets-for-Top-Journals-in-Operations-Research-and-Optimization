Abstract
We investigate the existence and structure of Markov-perfect equilibria of discrete-time 
dynamic games in which players are risk averse and have time preferences consistent with 
discounting. We establish the existence of a Markov-perfect equilibrium when each player 
strives to maximize the expected exponential utility of the present value of the time stream 
of rewards. Also, we give sufficient conditions for a Markov-perfect equilibrium to be 
myopic, namely to be a sequence of Nash equilibria of static games. The myopia results 
are applied to a dynamic oligopoly model in which firms choose prices and production 
quantities, encounter stochastic demand and hold inventories.
Keywords  Dynamic game · Risk-sensitive · Discounting · Markov-perfect · 
Equilibrium · Myopic · Oligopoly
1  Introduction
Most equilibria results for dynamic oligopoly implicitly presume that decision makers are 
risk-neutral; cf. Shubik (1959), Friedman (1977) and Fudenberg and Tirole (1986). More 
generally, the literature on N-person nonzero-sum sequential games focuses on risk-neu­
tral criteria although the decision makers in many motivating phenomena are likely to be 
sensitive to risk (Başar & Olsder, 1982). Starting with Shapley (1953),1 sequential games 
with risk-neutral players have been shown to possess equilibria under increasingly general

This paper investigates sequential games with players who are sensitive both to risk 
and timing. Abundant data confirm that people are sensitive to risk and timing in diverse

criteria are used in Shubik and Thompson (1959) and Sanghvi (1978) to study stochastic 
processes induced by competing decision makers. This paper investigates the existence and 
structure of equilibria of sequential games in which players are sensitive to both risk and 
timing.
For portals to the literature on risk-sensitive Markov decision processes (MDPs), see 
White (1988), Bäuerle and Rieder (2014), Bäuerle and Jaśkiewicz (2024) and Alexander 
and Sobel (2024). However, the literature on risk-sensitive sequential games is sparse. This 
paper leans heavily on Shinde (1991) which Monahan and Sobel (1997) use to analyze a 
dynamic marketing model. With respect to risk-sensitive criteria in game models, there are 
equilibrium existence theorems in Basu and Ghosh (2018) and this paper, but neither exis­
tence theorem implies the other. Basu and Ghosh (2018) has deterministic rewards2 and a 
denumerable state space while Theorem 1 in Sect. 2 has random rewards and a finite state 
space. Both existence theorems are based on models with Markovian dynamics.

The remainder of the paper presents and applies sufficient conditions for a risk-sensitive 
sequential game to have a myopic Markov equilibrium. A myopic equilibrium, which is a 
sequence of Nash equilibria of static games, facilitates qualitative analysis and computa­
tion. Myopic solutions have been identified for many risk-neutral MDPs and some dynamic 
oligopoly models (Arrow et al., 1958; Nerlove & Arrow, 1962; Veinott, 1965; Kirman & 
Sobel, 1974; Sobel, 1990b). Section 3 presents general sufficient conditions for a risk-sensi­
tive sequential game to have a myopic Markov equilibrium. Section 4 shows that the condi­
tions are satisfied when players’ rewards are additively separable with respect to state and 
action, and the dynamics depend only on players’ actions. Section 5 shows that the condition 
is satisfied if players’ rewards and the expected dynamics are affine functions of the state 
with constant coefficients. The dynamic oligopoly model in Sect. 6, in which firms choose 
price and production quantities, encounter stochastic demand, and hold inventories, satisfies 
the conditions in Sects. 4 and 5.

7  Summary
This paper analyzes the existence and structure of equilibria in non-cooperative dynamic games 
with players who are sensitive to both risk and timing. A player’s dual sensitivity is represented 
by the expected value of the inter-period exponential utility of the present value of the sequence 
of payoffs as follows: E[−exp(−λj
∑

denote player j’s risk parameter, discount factor and random payoff in period t. The existence 
of risk-sensitive Markov-perfect equilibria is proven in Sect. 2 where the first obstacle is that 
equilibria do not generally exist among stationary strategies. The second obstacle is that the usual 
risk-neutral existence proofs use mappings that are contractions, but here the mappings are only 
non-expansive. Nevertheless, the paper establishes the existence of equilibria by (i) considering 
the set of Markov strategies which includes stationary strategies as a proper subset, (ii) using an 
appropriately general fixed point theorem and (iii) restricting the state space to be finite and play­
ers’ rewards to be discrete random variables with finite support.

An equilibrium of a dynamic game is myopic if it consists of a sequence of Nash equilibria 
of static (single-period) games. Sections 3, 4, 5, 6 concern myopic equilibria of risk-sensitive 
dynamic games. They establish a general sufficient condition for a risk-sensitive sequential game 
to have a myopic Markov-perfect equilibrium (Sect. 3). Subsequent sections show that the con­
dition is satisfied when players’ rewards are additively separable with respect to state and action 
and the dynamics depend only on players’ actions (Sect. 4). The general sufficient condition is 
satisfied too if players’ expected rewards and the expected dynamics are affine functions of the 
state with constant coefficients (Sect. 5). The application of the myopia results to a dynamic 
oligopoly model in which firms choose prices and production quantities, encounter stochastic 
demand and hold inventories (Sect. 6) extends previous results for the same model with a risk-
neutral criterion.
Appendix: Unintended adverse consequence of scalar rewards in an 
MDP
Much of the literature on risk-sensitive MDPs and sequential games cited in Sect. 1 makes the 
implicit assumption that conditional rewards are real-valued functions of the current state and 
action (all players’ actions in the case of sequential games) instead of functions that take values 
as random variables. In Chung and Sobel (1987), an example on pages 50 and 61 shows the 
adverse consequence of the implicit assumption. The following example is simpler and sup­
presses notational dependence on player j (because J = {1} in an MDP).
In an MDP, let s1 and a1 denote the initial state and action, let Xt be the random reward 
in period t, and for an arbitrary strategy let B = ∑
t βt−1Xt in which 0 ≤β < 1. The goal 
of much of the literature on risk-sensitive MDPs and sequential games is, for each λ > 0 
and i ∈S, to identify a strategy π∗ such that Eπ∗|s1=i(−e−λB) ≥Eπ|s1=i(−e−λB) for all 
π in a suitably large class. Call such a π∗ risk-sensitive optimal; were it to exist, it might 
depend on λ and i (and the class of alternative π).
Let r(i, k) = E(X1|s1 = i, a1 = k) and ¯B = ∑
t βt−1r(st, at). A close reading of the 
literature reveals that most papers employ ¯B instead of B, and seek a decision rule ¯π such 
that E¯π|s1=i(−e−λ ¯
B) ≥Eπ|s1=i(−e−λ ¯
B) for a suitably large class of π. The following 
counterexample for a risk-sensitive MDP shows that a maximal ¯π can fail to be risk-sensi­
tive optimal.
Let 
S = {1, 2}, A1 = {1, 2}, A2 = {1}, P{sn+1 = 2|Xn, an} ≡1,
P{Xn = 0|sn = 1, an = 1} = 1 
and 
P{Xn = 1|sn = 1, an = 2} =
P{Xn = −1|sn = 1, an = 2} = 0.5. So r(1, 1) = r(1, 2) = 0. Let πI and πII denote the 
respective strategies of taking actions 1 and 2 in state 1, with BI and BII denoting the 
associated present values of the rewards from initial state 1, and ¯BI and ¯BII denoting 
the corresponding present values of the expected rewards. Both I and II are maximal with 
respect to E¯π|s1=1(−e−λ ¯
B) because ¯BI = ¯BII = 0, for all λ > 0. However, for all λ > 0,
