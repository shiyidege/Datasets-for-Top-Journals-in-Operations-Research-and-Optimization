Abstract
In the context of healthcare services, a variety of reforms have been performed in some
countries to coordinate supply and demand. For example, China has implemented a hier-
archic healthcare system for this end. As a core challenge pertaining to such reforms, a
healthcare referral mechanism needs to be implemented and calibrated efﬁciently. We con-
sider a hierarchic healthcare system where each patient permitted at a high-level hospital
decides whether to be transferred to a low-level hospital, based on their expected utility max-
imization. In particular, this paper examines two typical mechanisms and their coordination
efﬁciency of referral service: the subsidy scheme and the penalty scheme. Methodologically,
by modeling a three-stage Stackelberg game within a queueing framework, we investigate
the healthcare referral rate determined by both the high-level hospital and its patients, as well
as the reimbursement rate, subsidy rate, and penalty rate determined by the funder alongside
the proﬁt- and welfare-maximizing objectives of the high-level hospital. By deriving and
comparing the equilibrium outcomes (e.g., referral rate, reimbursement rate, funder’s wel-
fare, patient/social welfare, operating proﬁt, and waiting time) associated with each scheme
and the two different objectives of the high-level hospital, we reveal that: (i) when the bud-
get is relatively sufﬁcient (i.e., above a certain threshold), the high-level hospital always
transfers out more patients under the welfare-maximizing criterion than that under the proﬁt-
maximizing criterion; however, the optimal referral rates under the welfare-maximization
and proﬁt-maximizing objectives are respectively above and below that under the social
welfare maximization criterion. (ii) The penalty scheme generally dominates the subsidy
scheme in terms of higher social/patient welfare, higher funder’s welfare, shorter waiting
times, and lower patient referral rates. (iii) When the budget is sufﬁcient and the difference
between the prices charged by the high-level and low-level hospitals is relatively small, the
funder is recommended to encourage a welfare-maximizing hospital to refer out its patients.
In contrast, when the budget is relatively limited, a penalty scheme for a proﬁt-maximizing
high-level hospital should be adopted. Finally, extensive numerical experiments have been
performed to glean more insights, such as the outperformance of the penalty scheme over
the subsidy scheme under different scenarios of available budget levels. These insights can
readily facilitate policymakers and regulators in developing a coordinating scheme.

1 Introduction
Hierarchic healthcare systems have recently received widespread attention. For example, two-
tier healthcare service systems, consisting of primary care physicians or general practitioners
and specialists, are common in the United States and the United Kingdom. The gatekeepers
(i.e., primary care physicians in the United States or general practitioners in the United King-
dom) control patients’ access to specialist services (Adida & Bravo, 2019). In Hong Kong,
Australia, and Canada, the healthcare service systems consist of public and private hospitals
(Teymourifar et al., 2021). In China, the healthcare services in such hierarchic systems are
delivered by different levels of hospitals, including high-level hospitals (e.g., comprehensive
hospitals) and low-level hospitals (e.g., community-based health service centers, primary
hospitals); cf. Li et al., (2021a, b).
Many high-level or public hospitals often become overwhelmed with crowded patients,
most of which are beyond their service capacity. Consequently, their patients are more likely
to suffer from long waiting times. In contrast, many low-level hospitals or private hospitals are
mostly under-utilized, operating below their capacities (Li et al., 2017; Song & Wen, 2015).
Not only has such an imbalance between demand and service capacity caused tremendous
operational inefﬁciency and waste of resources, but it has also recently posed a serious social
issue. For high-level or public healthcare hospitals, one possible solution to address long
waiting times is to increase the service capacity. However, expanding service capacity is
overwhelmingly expensive, and some governments, such as Hong Kong, Australia, Turkey,
and Canada, have enforcedly banned expanding their capacity status quo (Qian et al., 2017;
Teymourifar et al., 2021).
Considering the limitation of high-level or public hospitals’ capacities, some countries’

a referral service policy for non-emergency medical needs (Wang et al., 2021; The State
Council of PRC 2017). Their aim is thus to supply appropriate service capacity with sufﬁcient
patient demand for healthcare services at all different levels of hospitals (Hua et al., 2016).
For instance, in Hong Kong and Australia, patients are encouraged to shift from public
hospitals to private hospitals (Qian & Zhuang, 2017). In China, the patient referral policy
from the high-level hospitals to the low-level hospitals has been prevailingly leveraged in the
hierarchic healthcare system (Rajagopalan & Tong, 2022). According to a recent report by
the state council of the PRC, by the end of 2023, almost all cities in China should have been
implementing a healthcare referral policy.1
However, a series of issues have been emerging while coordinating patients and hospitals
under the referral policy. Those issues mostly stem from (1) the lack of incentives for high-
level hospitals to refer patients to their counterparts, and (2) the absence of motivation for
patients to transfer to low-level hospitals. Referring patients to their counterparts means proﬁt
loss for high-level hospitals; the high-level hospitals cannot collect medical payments or any
forms of compensation if patients are transferred to their low-level peers (World Bank Group
et al., 2016). This is particularly impactful to those high-level hospitals, which are proﬁt-
driven.Thereisanothertypeofhigh-levelhospital,whichispublicandmainlywelfare-driven.
Those public, welfare-driven hospitals are more prevailing in some healthcare systems such
as Western Europe (e.g., UK, France, Germany, etc.) and Asia (e.g., China, Singapore, etc.)
(Qian et al., 2017), and they focus on maximizing the welfare, which is the sum of their
operating proﬁts and patient utility. The proﬁt loss caused by patient referrals, therefore,
might be offset by the utility gain; however, the utility gain can only be realized after patients

utility gain, the proﬁt loss is immediate and clear. Therefore, regardless of the hospital type,
motivations are critical to encouraging high-level hospitals to transfer patients to their low-
level counterparts (Andritsos & Tang, 2014; Brekke et al., 2016), and some countries have
started to develop and design incentive schemes to ensure a successful implementation of
the healthcare referral program (Araz, et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021a, 2021b). In our study, we
developed two types of models: welfare-driven and proﬁt-driven.
From patients’ perspectives, they are unwilling to be transferred to low-level hospitals,
whose service level is perceived as lower than their counterparts. Therefore, government
interventions are required to differentiate reimbursement mechanisms between the two hos-
pitals and to motivate patients to transfer. Therefore, patients visiting low-level hospitals must
be reimbursed more (i.e., with a higher reimbursement rate) than those visiting high-level
ones, while considering the budget constraints (General ofﬁce of the state council of PRC,
2019).
This prevailing practice motivates us to explore two payment schemes aiming to stimulate
patient referrals by high-level hospitals: the subsidy scheme and the penalty scheme. With
the former scheme, the healthcare funder/government subsidizes the referring hospital (i.e.,
the high-level hospital) to transfer patients, which has been implemented in some countries.
For example, the Health and Family Planning Commission of Jiangxi Province in China
announced in 2016 that a high-level hospital is awarded 600 RMB per patient transferred to
a low-level hospital. In addition to the per-patient award, the Chinese government proposed
to subsidize high-level hospitals for their patient referrals to improve the performance of the
healthcare referral systems (General Ofﬁce of the State Council of PRC, 2019). In Turkey
and Canada, the governments subsidize private hospitals so that they can set a lower price
to attract patients transferred from public hospitals (Teymourifar et al., 2021). The penalty
scheme has also been adopted in practice to improve service quality; the Health Commission
of China set the patient waiting time as a metric to evaluate the performance of a high-level
hospital in 2023.2 “We and some other high-level hospitals will be ﬁnancially penalized by
the government if the patients’ waiting time exceeds a threshold”, which was revealed by
several overcrowded high-level hospitals we interviewed in China. The purpose of the penalty
scheme is to improve healthcare quality by creating a mechanism that forces proﬁt-driven,
high-level hospitals to transfer patients downstream and cut down waiting time. In addition,
such fees collected from high-level hospitals can be utilized to reimburse those transferred
patients to reinforce the desired outcome.
Nevertheless, the budget is given and limited, and different schemes (i.e., subsidy and
penalty) inevitably lead to different reimbursement rates at each hospital. Therefore, it is
important to characterize the impacts of these two payment schemes on patient referrals and
to assess their performance, including the funder’s welfare, patient/social welfare, operating
proﬁt, and total waiting times. Furthermore, we need to ascertain how to achieve the coordina-
tion and cooperation goals among patients, hospitals, and the funder, in terms of healthcare
performance. In this paper, we explore the performance measures under the subsidy and
penalty schemes considering mild illness patients (e.g., common and chronic patients) in a
hierarchic healthcare referral system that consists of a healthcare funder, a high-level hos-
pital, a low-level hospital, and heterogeneous patients. To compare the role that the subsidy
and penalty schemes can play in promoting a proﬁt-maximizing hospital’s healthcare referral
program, we also include a basic setting in which a high-level hospital aims to maximize
its welfare (i.e., the sum of its operating proﬁt and patient utility). In what follows, we shall

make a few remarks regarding notation and denote the three different settings under this

In particular, we aim to investigate the following questions: (1) how does the healthcare
funder determine the reimbursement rate in terms of the patient’s healthcare referral under
the three settings? How do both the funder’s budget and the wait cost impact the healthcare
referral under each setting? (2) How does a payment scheme (i.e., the subsidy and penalty
schemes associated with a proﬁt-maximizing hospital) affect the equilibrium referral rate and
key operational performance (e.g., funder’s welfare, proﬁt, patient/social welfare, and wait-
ing times)? Subject to the public healthcare budgets, what type of incentive schemes (either S
or P) should the government choose to coordinate the proﬁt-maximizing hospital’s referral
services? (3) How does a high-level hospital’s objective function (i.e., welfare maximiza-
tion vs. proﬁt maximization) affect the referral service and the corresponding operational
performance?
To address the above problems, methodologically, we develop a three-stage Stackelberg
game model under a queueing framework that captures the interactions among the hetero-
geneous patients, the high-level hospital, and the healthcare funder. The healthcare funder,
who acts as the leader, maximizes the total welfare for all three settings. The funder makes
decisions on (1) the equilibrium reimbursement rate for setting (W), where Hospital 1 is
welfare-driven, (2) both equilibrium reimbursement and subsidy rates for setting (S), where
the proﬁt-driven Hospital 1 is subsidized for patient referrals, and (3) both optimal reim-
bursement and penalty rates for setting (P), where the proﬁt-driven Hospital 1 is penalized
for the excessive waiting time. Given the reimbursement, and subsidy or penalty rates set
by the policy-maker, the high-level hospital behaves as the second player of the game, who
determines the referral rate for mild illness patients to maximize either its own welfare for
setting (W) or its own expected proﬁt for both settings (S) and (P). Note that our study con-
siders the proportion of mild illness patients to the total number of patients at the high-level
hospital as an exogenous variable. This proportion can, in the long run, be obtained from
real-world data. Finally, once the reimbursement rate is given, each patient originally admit-
ted at the high-level hospital, who is heterogeneous in service sensitivity, decides whether to
be transferred to the low-level hospital, by maximizing their expected utility.
To address the aforementioned research questions, we leverage backward induction to ana-
lyze the game-theoretic queueing model and then obtain the optimal equilibrium outcomes
for each setting. To answer the ﬁrst question, we ﬁnd that when the wait cost is sufﬁciently
high and the proportion of mild illness patients at the high-level hospital is lower than a
certain threshold, the rate of reimbursing a high-level hospital should be strictly set under
(or equal to) an upper-bound threshold, such that all the mild illness patients are referred
downstream. Otherwise, there exists unique equilibrium reimbursement (for both hospitals)
and referral rates (for Hospital 1). Furthermore, the optimal referral rate maximizing the
funder’s welfare can be achieved by the reimbursement policy if and only if the budget is
sufﬁcient and above a certain threshold. On the contrary, when the budget is tight and below
the threshold, the equilibrium referral rate is always lower than the optimal referral rate
maximizing the funder’s welfare. Importantly, regarding the second question, it is shown
that, compared with the subsidy scheme, the penalty scheme is more effective (or at least as
effective) in diverting patients downstream (in terms of the funder’s welfare, patient/social
welfare, and total waiting time). Regarding the third question, we show that there exist condi-
tions in which the public budget must exceed a certain threshold. It implies that, compared to
a proﬁt-maximizing high-level hospital, a welfare-maximizing high-level hospital can refer

2 Literature review
Our work studies the impacts of reimbursement, subsidy, and penalty schemes on the coor-
dination efﬁciency of patient referral and the operational performance of the hierarchic
healthcare system. In what follows, we review the research streams of healthcare service
and operations management (see some comprehensive reviews by Dai & Tayur, 2020; Has-
sin, 2016; Keskinocak & Savva, 2020) regarding healthcare referral services and payment
schemes (including reimbursement, subsidy, and penalty schemes).
Over the past decade, there has been an increasing body of literature studying the issue of
the healthcare referral process, beginning with the pioneering work of Shumsky and Pinker
(2003); cf. Hasija et al. (2008), Lee et al. (2012), Adida and Bravo (2019), and Agha et al.
(2022). In these studies, the upstream referral process of patients is the most popular, i.e., the
primary care physicians (PCPs) serve as the ﬁrst-level gatekeepers. The PCPs either treat
patients by themselves or refer them to a second-level specialist. These studies primarily
employ a queueing-theoretic approach to study the issues in the following two aspects:
the referral rate (threshold) and the incentive mechanism. In the empirical stream, Nam
et al. (2010) investigate the impact of competition on the management of referral alliances
consisting of specialists and multiple generalists. In the health economics literature, Allard
et al. (2011) consider that the altruism towards patients is described by the patients’ health
utility, and is incorporated into the total utility function of the general practitioner and the
specialist. They study the referral process of the altruistic general practitioner and ﬁnd that
only the most altruistic general practitioner with low ability systematically refers patients
to a specialist. More recently, Adida and Bravo (2019) focus on patient healthcare referral
between a service requester (who is a primary caregiver responsible for managing the health
of a population of patients) and a service provider, wherein a patient who requires advanced
care outside of the requester’s expertise is referred to a provider. In contrast to such an
upstream referral, in some countries, such as China, the healthcare referral ﬂow of patients
is greatly advocated to be from the high-level hospitals (like specialists) to the low-level
123

Annals of Operations Research
hospitals (PCPs) to balance the patient ﬂow between the two levels of the hospital. For the
objective function of minimizing the service providers’ operating costs, Liu et al. (2015) and
Li et al. (2017) leverage queueing models to study the referral situation, and then develop
contracts based on the optimal referral strategy.
Our study belongs to this latter situation, and some salient features are provided as fol-
lows. First, we consider that every patient freely chooses whether to refer from a high-level
hospital to a low-level hospital at their discretion based on their own utility. The various
behaviors behind patients’ referral choices have been documented in some empirical and
healthcare management literature (cf. Hassin & Haviv, 2003 and Hassin, 2016 for comprehen-
sive reviews; Wang et al., 2021). Second, we analyze the impact of the funder/government’s
healthcare reimbursement policy (see Zhou et al., 2017) on patients’ referral choices. How-
ever, Zhou et al. (2017) do not address other issues associated with the coordination and
wait-time reduction of a healthcare referral system. Finally, our optimization performance
measures are more generic and comprehensive than those in the extant literature, considering
that we include not only the operating proﬁt (equivalent to operating cost), but also the patient
utility, waiting times, and social welfare.
In terms of payment schemes, healthcare funders typically focus on the innovation of
the reimbursement policy, and accordingly, researchers develop various models to analyze
how to improve operational performance. For example, Adida et al. (2017) and Guo et al.
(2019) compare the performance measures (e.g., revisit rate, waiting times, and utility) under
the fee-for-service (FFS) and bundled payment (BP) schemes. They reveal that, for a larger
size of the patient population, the BP scheme outperforms the FFS scheme only in terms
of utility and revisit rate. In addition, Andritsos and Tang (2018) develop a co-production
model for both the hospital and the patient in order to explore and compare the efﬁciency of
different reimbursement schemes, including FFS, BP, and P4P (pay-for-performance). It is
shown that the BP scheme can be more effective in terms of lowering patient costs and the
funder’s payments to the hospital. By examining different scales of healthcare systems, Liu
et al. (2015) show that P4P via cost-sharing contracts, and FFS via cost-sharing contracts,
can improve coordination of the healthcare system under certain situations. In the health
economics literature, Allard et al. (2011) analyze and compare the incentive properties of
different payment mechanisms (P4P, capitation, and fundholding) regarding patient referral
efﬁciency. Their study proves that capitation dominates P4P and fundholding in terms of
inducing the most referrals to expensive specialty care. Ata et al. (2012) examine the Medi-
care hospice reimbursement policy and the hospice’s incentives for patient management in
the United States Medicare scheme. Li et al., (2021a, b) evaluate the operational efﬁciency
of the fee-for-capacity (FFC) and performance payment (PP) in terms of facilitating capacity
reallocation. They show that the selection of the payment scheme will not alter the health-
care system coordination such that the equilibrium operational outcomes are identical under
either scheme. By contrast, this paper considers healthcare referrals and their coordination
mechanisms, which is different from Li et al., (2021a, b) from two perspectives. First, our
focus is to examine the operations of the hierarchic healthcare system from the perspective of
service demand rather than capacity supply, i.e., we study the welfare- and proﬁt-maximizing
hospitals’ patient referrals. Li et al., (2021a, b) consider the high-quality medical resources
sinking from high-level hospitals to low-level hospitals. Second, the subsidy and penalty
schemes considered in our paper are used to motivate high-level hospitals to refer patients.
However, in the study of Li et al., (2021a, b), the FFC and PP schemes are considered to
compensate high-level hospitals for resource sinking.

study, on the other hand, shows that the penalty scheme performs better (at least the same in
some cases) than the subsidy scheme in terms of social/patient welfare, the funder’s welfare,
and waiting time.

To effectively implement the healthcare referral program, the funder, who acts as the
regulator of the healthcare system, must not only induce Hospital 1 (i.e., the high-level
hospital) to transfer its patients but also incentivize patients to willingly go to Hospital 2 (i.e.,

reimbursement rate for patients seeking treatment from each hospital. In addition, the funder
must determine the subsidy or penalty rate to Hospital 1 to promote the patient referral.
Speciﬁcally, to maximize the total welfare of the hierarchic healthcare system, the funder
must set (1) the two hospitals’ reimbursement rates under setting (W), where Hospital 1 is
welfare-driven, (2) the two hospitals’ reimbursement rates and the subsidy rate of Hospital
1 under setting (S), where the proﬁt-driven Hospital 1 is subsidized to refer patients, and (3)
the two hospitals’ reimbursement rates and the penalty rate of Hospital 1 under setting (P),
where the proﬁt-driven Hospital 1 is penalized when the waiting time is too long.

Theorem 1 suggests that, for a high wait cost, the funder’s equilibrium welfare for setting
(P) and (W) are always higher than that for setting (S). Recall that setting (P) has been
concluded more cost efﬁcient for it can generate additional income from penalty (cf. Propo-
sition 7). Setting (S), on the contrary, demands a budget for subsidizing hospitals without the
income from penalty. Such a difference in net budget is reﬂected in the funder’s welfare; set-
ting (P) always generates more funder’s welfare than the setting (S). However, whether the
scheme comparison in terms of patient transfer follows the funder’s welfare or not depends
on the wait costs.
When the wait cost is sufﬁciently high and above the threshold ds. , transferring all patients
from Hospital 1 (regardless of the objective types, proﬁt- or welfare-maximizing) to Hospital
2 always improves the funder’s welfare and the social welfare. However, the size of the budget
determines the performance in terms of the referral rate. As shown in Case (i) of Theorem 1,
123

Annals of Operations Research
there exist two thresholds Rs, 1 (denoted as an upper-bounded threshold) and Rw, 2 (denoted
as a lower-bounded threshold). The three schemes can be equally effective in coordinating
patient referrals only when the funder budget is abundant, greater than the upper-bounded
threshold. If the funder budget is limited, below the lower-bounded threshold, however, both
settings (S) and (P) fail to achieve the optimal referral rate of the system, while the penalty
scheme delivers a better performance than its counterpart (i.e., in the system referral rate).
When the budget is medium (i.e., between the upper- and lower-bound), both settings (P)
and (W) yield the same equilibrium in the patient referral rate, while setting (S) delivers
the least. When considering the between-hospital difference in patient perceived values,
and it is larger than the between-hospital price difference (i.e., (α −1)v > p1 −p2), we
observe that an increasing referral rate is always effective in reducing the waiting time and
eventually improving social welfare. In summary, for a medium or high budget, the penalty
scheme outperforms its counterpart in motivating patient referrals, reducing waiting time,
and improving social welfare. When the budget is medium–low, the penalty scheme is more
effective particularly to a proﬁt-driven than a welfare-driven Hospital 1 for it can improve
its operating proﬁt. When the budget is relatively low and below the threshold R00, the three
settings show identical patient referral rate, total waiting time, and social welfare under both
the full coverage and partial coverage.
When the wait cost is relatively low, not above the threshold ds, the comparison of equi-
librium results among the three settings also depends on the budget size; see Case (ii) of
Theorem 1. In contrast to Case (i) of Theorem 1, we observe that a welfare-maximizing
Hospital 1 transfers more patients than a proﬁt-maximizing Hospital 1 when the wait cost is
relatively low and the budget is sufﬁciently high (i.e., above a threshold Rw, 5). Referring
patients can always boost social welfare by decreasing waiting time and increasing patient
utility, which is perceived as valuable to Hospital 1 with the welfare-maximization objective.
Patient referrals, however, inevitably lose patients, and the source of revenue, is thus not
beneﬁcial to a hospital aiming to maximize proﬁt. Consequently, a high budget enables the
equilibrium referral rate for setting (W) to be closer to that for setting (P), which is the opti-
mal system equilibrium, resulting in a lower waiting time and higher social welfare (at a cost
of the funder’s welfare). Similarly, when the budget is limited (i.e., below Rw, 5), the penalty
scheme motivates more patient referrals from the proﬁt-driven Hospital 1; therefore, setting
(P) is more effective than setting (W) in coordinating the patient referral and improving the
healthcare operations performance.
In summary, we can conclude from Theorem 1 that for a high wait cost, when the budget
is adequate, the penalty scheme and subsidy scheme yield the same performance, except for
the funder’s welfare. However, when the budget is tight, the penalty scheme outperforms
the subsidy scheme in terms of social welfare, funder’s welfare, patient referral, and total
waiting time. In particular, when both the wait cost and budget are respectively low, below
certain thresholds, the penalty scheme is effective in improving referral efﬁciency. For this
case, its (even the subsidy scheme’s) operation performances are higher (lower) than those
of the system with the welfare-maximizing Hospital 1.

5 Concluding remarks and future studies
This paper investigates the payment schemes in the perspective of coordinating a hierarchic
healthcare referral system among multiple healthcare parties: a healthcare funder, two differ-
ent levels of hospitals, and heterogeneous patients. We consider three settings of healthcare
referral from a high-level hospital to a low-level hospital. (1) Under the ﬁrst setting (i.e.,
setting (W)), the healthcare funder determines the rates of reimbursing patients at two dif-
ferent levels of hospitals while considering how the high-level hospital can maximize its
welfare. (2) Under the second setting (i.e., setting (S)), the healthcare funder determines the
patients’ reimbursement rates, as well as the rate at which to subsidize a high-level hospital
that seeks to maximize its operating proﬁt. (3) Under the third setting (i.e., setting (P)), the
healthcare funder determines the patients’ reimbursement rates and the rate of penalizing a
proﬁt-maximizing high-level hospital for excessive waiting times.
For each setting, we devised a three-stage sequential game model within a queuing
framework, based on which we compared their operational performance. By studying the
123

Annals of Operations Research
equilibrium referral rate under each setting, we delivered multiple managerial insights. First,
the implementation efﬁciency of the healthcare referral program depends on the size of bud-
get. In particular, there exist certain thresholds of budget level. When the budget is relatively
abundant and above the corresponding threshold for each setting, the optimal referral rate
that maximizes the funder’s welfare can be achieved via a healthcare reimbursement policy.
More importantly, by comparing the equilibrium results associated with the subsidy and
penalty schemes, it was shown that a penalty scheme is more efﬁcient than, or at least as
efﬁcient as, a subsidy scheme to control patient transfer and to lower total waiting times.
More speciﬁcally, under the conditions in which the wait cost is moderately high, if the
public budget is sufﬁciently low and below a certain threshold, the equilibrium referral rate
under the penalty scheme is closer to the benchmark case (associated with the overall system)
than that under the subsidy scheme. Therefore, in contrast to the subsidy scheme, the penalty
scheme effectively improves the overall performance in terms of the funder’s welfare, patient
utility, and social welfare at the sacriﬁce of some operating proﬁts. By comparing the refer-
ral performance of a welfare-maximizing hospital with a proﬁt-maximizing hospital, both
analytically and numerically, the main ﬁndings of our study are summarized as follows. The
welfare-maximizing high-level hospital may not always outperform its proﬁt-maximizing
counterpart in terms of all performance metrics (i.e., the waiting times, the funder’s wel-
fare, operating proﬁt, patient utility, and social welfare). When the budget is sufﬁcient and
above a threshold, a welfare-maximizing hospital outperforms a proﬁt-maximizing hospital
in terms of waiting times and patient utility; however, a proﬁt-maximizing high-level hospi-
tal outperforms in terms of the funder’s welfare and operating proﬁt. In particular, when the
between-hospital price gap is small, a welfare-maximizing high-level hospital always gener-
ates more social welfare. When the budget is insufﬁcient and below a threshold, whether or
not a welfare-maximizing high-level hospital dominates a proﬁt-maximizing high-level hos-
pital depends on which scheme, subsidy or penalty, is implemented. If the proﬁt-maximizing
high-level hospital is subsidized (resp., penalized) by the funder (resp., for excessive waiting
times), the welfare-maximizing (resp., proﬁt-maximizing) high-level hospital is more effec-
tive in terms of improving the funder’s welfare, patient utility, and social welfare, along with
reducing waiting times, but is less effective in reducing proﬁt loss.
In summary, our managerial insights are provided as follows. First, for a sufﬁciently
high budget, the penalty and subsidy schemes yield the same efﬁciency for stimulating
a proﬁt-maximizing high-level hospital to refer its patients; for a relatively low budget,
the penalty scheme always dominates the subsidy scheme. Second, for a sufﬁciently high
budget, a welfare-maximizing hospital will refer more patients to lower waiting times and
improve patient utility, but this results in a lower operating proﬁt as well as social welfare,
compared with that of a proﬁt-maximizing high-level hospital; for a relatively low budget,
a welfare-maximizing high-level hospital penalized/subsidized by the funder is more/less
effective in terms of improving all the operational performances (except the operating proﬁt)
compared to its proﬁt-maximizing counterpart. This implies that when the budget is short,
the subsidy/penalty scheme leads to a decrease/increase in operational performance and an
increase/decrease in waiting times.
These useful insights can be readily leveraged by the government or healthcare funders
to create a coordinating scheme that can be more effectively implemented in the hierarchic
service systems. However, we acknowledge that other issues not addressed in this study would
be worthy of further discussion. First, we only considered a high-level hospital and a low-
level hospital in our study. However, practically, there are a variety of hospital levels within
the healthcare ecosystem. The two types of hospitals in our analysis are rather selective
and representative of other types of hospitals, such as comprehensive care providers and
123

Annals of Operations Research
primary care providers. Hence, our managerial insights can be readily extended to a multi-
level hospital setting. Second, we assumed an exogenous treatment price at both hospitals, but
this can be extended to consider the decisions on treatment prices at two different hospitals.
Third, in our analysis of healthcare market coverage, we assumed that the public budget, as
well as the two hospitals’ reimbursement rates, have a corresponding lower bound such that
potential patients are fully covered. By following our analytical approach, it would also be of
interest to investigate a partial healthcare coverage scenario in which some patients choose
to exit the hierarchic healthcare system and join other systems. Last but not least, a more
critical issue is how to incorporate the capacity level of the low-level hospital when receiving
referrals from the high-level hospital (Song et al., 2020).
