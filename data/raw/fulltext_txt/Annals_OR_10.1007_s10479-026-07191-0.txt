Abstract
Agriculture in developing countries is characterized by a predominant reliance on small-
holder farming and by the multitude of challenges these producers face. These challenges
stem from socio-economic structural inequalities speciﬁc to these countries, as well as from
environmental drivers such as climate change. Smallholder agriculture is particularly sen-
sitive to issues including food security, climate adaptation, biodiversity, sustainable use of
environmental resources, and market access. Operations Research (OR) methods have been
widely used as decision-support tools in agriculture for decades. This paper reviews forty-four
years of publications addressing the application of OR methods to smallholder agriculture in
developing countries. The reviewed literature is analysed along multiple dimensions, includ-
ing modelling scales, applied OR techniques, decision objectives and constraints, and the
challenges addressed. Beyond identifying descriptive trends–such as the growing use of
micro-scale modelling, the diversiﬁcation of methodological approaches, and the promi-
nence of policy-oriented applications–the paper synthesizes the ﬁndings into an integrative
conceptual framework. This framework articulates how OR has been embedded in small-
holder decision-making by linking the determinants shaping research scope and objectives,
the corresponding methodological responses, and the mediation and application of research
outputs across decision levels. The ﬁnal section discusses future perspectives for extend-
ing the impact of OR-based decision support, including the potential role of information
and communication technologies in improving accessibility for small-scale farmers and the
challenges associated with agricultural digitalization in developing countries.

1 Introduction
A signiﬁcant proportion of the population in developing countries depends on agriculture
for their livelihood. According to World Bank data, agricultural employment in low-income
countries accounted for 60% of the workforce in 2021. Moreover, 76.3% of the extremely
poor and 60.7% of the moderately poor rural workers in developing countries are engaged
in agriculture Castañeda et al. (2018). Despite their crucial role in food production, small-
scale farmers often live in remote, environmentally fragile areas and belong to marginalized
and disenfranchised communities (IFAD and UNEP 2013). These farmers face numerous
challenges, including limited resources and education, which create a vicious cycle of soil
degradation, erosion, and water scarcity. Additionally, they struggle with economic insecu-
rity and risk due to socio-economic instability and unpredictable weather conditions. These
constraints, further exacerbated by climate change, threaten food security, economic growth,
and the well-being of smallholder farmers and their families.
The application of Operations Research (OR) to smallholder agriculture in developing
countries is highly relevant due to its broad impact and its potential to improve the well-being
of a large and vulnerable population. Several literature reviews have examined OR methods
in agriculture. For instance, Ahumada and Villalobos (2009) reviewed planning models in
agrifood supply chains, while Plà et al. (2014) explored OR opportunities in agriculture.
However, these reviews have primarily adopted the perspective of developed countries or
intensiveagriculturalproduction,regardlessoflocation.Asaresult,theyoverlookagricultural
challenges speciﬁc to smallholder farming systems and subsistence agriculture, which are
often shaped by structural inequalities and precarious livelihoods.
More broadly, the way OR is applied to agricultural decision-making differs markedly
across countries at different stages of development. In developed countries, OR applications
in agriculture have largely evolved within contexts characterized by capital-intensive pro-
duction systems, standardized data infrastructures, and strong institutional support, often
focusing on efﬁciency gains, logistics optimization, and technology-driven precision man-
agement (Ahumada & Villalobos, 2009; Plà et al., 2014). In contrast, in developing and
least-developed countries, OR models are more frequently designed to address subsistence
constraints, exposure to climatic and market risks, scenario analysis, and policy support,
within contexts that are highly constrained in terms of data availability, institutional capacity,
and implementation channels. This heterogeneity underscores the need for context-sensitive
modelling approaches and motivates the focus of the present review on smallholder agricul-
ture in developing countries.
In the context of smallholder agriculture in developing countries, many decision-making
problems revolve around food security, access to markets, climate adaptation, irrigation man-
agement, and the sustainable use of natural resources. Addressing these challenges requires
analytical models that explicitly account for the speciﬁc constraints, objectives, and risk
environments faced by smallholders, and that inform decision-making at multiple, interre-
lated levels, ranging from farm management to policy design. While a substantial and diverse
body of OR-based research has emerged in this area, the review literature remains fragmented.
Existing reviews have addressed OR in developing countries from broader cross-sector per-
spectives, have focused on speciﬁc modelling traditions such as farm household models, have
examined related methodological domains (e.g., data analytics or OR–machine learning inte-
gration), or have covered particular geographical scopes (McGregor et al., 2001; Janssen &
Ittersum, 2007; White et al., 2011; Van Wijk et al., 2014; Argoubi et al., 2021; Ittmann,
2021; Orhan & Guajardo, 2022; Cock et al., 2023). However, to the best of our knowledge,

no review has explicitly focused on OR applications to smallholder agriculture in developing
countries while also offering an integrative perspective on how modelling choices, decision
contexts, and scales of analysis connect within smallholder decision-making processes.
In this context, the objective of this paper is twofold. First, it provides a structured review
of OR applications to smallholder agriculture in developing countries, analysing the literature
along multiple dimensions, including modelling scales, applied methods, decision objectives
and constraints, and the challenges addressed. Second, and more importantly, the paper
synthesizes the reviewed literature into an integrative conceptual framework that articulates
how OR has been positioned to support decision-making across scales and decision contexts
in smallholder agricultural systems. This framework links the determinants shaping research
scope and objectives, the corresponding methodological responses, and the mechanisms
through which research outputs are mediated and applied in practice.
The review focuses on studies applying OR techniques to smallholder farming systems,
with particular attention to farmers constrained by the need to meet subsistence requirements
while managing economic, social, and environmental trade-offs. By combining descriptive
analysis with conceptual integration, the paper aims to move beyond trend identiﬁcation and
contribute to a clearer understanding of the role of OR in supporting smallholder agriculture,
as well as to identify directions for future research and application.
The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 describes the review method-
ology. Section 3 outlines the documentary search process. Section 4 presents a descriptive
analysis of the literature. Section 5 examines OR applications under different criteria, includ-
ing modelling scales, methods, objectives, and challenges. Finally, Section 6 synthesizes the
ﬁndings through an integrative conceptual framework and discusses future perspectives for
extending the impact of OR-based decision support in smallholder agriculture.
2 Review methodology
The methodology adopted in this review follows a structured and transparent approach
inspired by the systematic review framework proposed by Tranﬁeld et al. (2003). This frame-
work emphasizes the explicit deﬁnition of the review scope, a systematic and reproducible
literature search, descriptive and thematic analysis, and the synthesis of ﬁndings to support
conceptual development and practical insight. The approach was adapted to accommodate the
diversity of modelling methods, decision contexts, and application domains characterizing
OR research applied to smallholder agriculture in developing countries.
The review methodology consists of four main stages: Documentary search, Descrip-
tive Analysis of the Literature, Literature Taxonomy, and Literature Analysis, Conceptual
Integration and Future Outlook. Table 1 summarizes the objectives of each stage, the method-
ological principles guiding them, and the corresponding sections of the paper. Stages two
to four follow a question-based approach to achieve their objectives, progressively moving
from descriptive analysis to critical synthesis and conceptual integration.
The documentary search
The documentary search aims to identify relevant research and select studies for inclusion in
the review. This process was structured around the deﬁnition of search and selection criteria
(steps a–c in Table 1), complemented by additional search procedures to ensure adequate
coverage of the literature (step d).

The descriptive analysis of literature
The descriptive analysis examines temporal, geographical, and disciplinary patterns in the
reviewed literature. In particular, it addresses where research has been conducted and applied,
which academic domains have employed OR methods to address agricultural challenges in
developing countries, how interest in these topics has evolved over time, and which general
trends can be observed regarding modelling scales and methodological choices (questions
Q1–Q4 in Table 1). This stage relies on descriptive statistics involving one or more variables,
such as publication year distributions and geographical distributions of authors and case
studies.
The literature taxonomy
A deeper analysis was conducted by classifying the selected references according to mod-
elling scales, applied OR techniques, objectives and constraints, and the types of challenges
addressed. This stage supports a structured comparison of modelling approaches and applica-
tion contexts, enabling the identiﬁcation of regularities and complementarities across studies.
The literature analysis, conceptual integration and future outlook
The ﬁnal stage focuses on synthesizing the results of the descriptive and thematic analyses to
derive broader insights. It addresses how OR applications can be conceptually integrated to
support decision-making in smallholder agricultural systems and identiﬁes opportunities to
extend the impact of OR-based decision support through methodological innovation, insti-
tutional mediation, and emerging technological developments (questions Q10a and Q10b in
Table 1). This stage leads to the development of an integrative conceptual framework and to
the discussion of future research perspectives.
3 The documentary search process
This review focuses on the application of OR to agriculture in developing countries from a
smallholder perspective. The rationale behind this choice is the authors’ perception that most
publications on sustainable agriculture emphasize industrial farming and economic aspects,
while giving less attention to the socio-economic and environmental challenges faced by
smallholder farmers.
To address this gap, a set of keywords was deﬁned to guide the search among peer-
reviewed papers published in English in international journals indexed in the Web of Science
and Scopus databases. Search queries were applied to titles, abstracts, and keywords. The
initial search employed the generic expression: “Operations Research” AND “Agriculture”
AND “Developing Countries”. In addition, several variations of this expression were con-
structed by replacing the original "OR" and "agriculture" related terms with commonly used
methodological and modelling concepts (Table 2). Studies focusing exclusively on modern,
large-scale commercial agriculture were excluded.
The search strategy followed an iterative and structured process. All expressions were
applied using Boolean operators to the same document ﬁelds (title, abstract, and keywords)
in both databases. The generic expression was ﬁrst used to identify a core set of publications.
Subsequently, two sets of variations were developed to broaden coverage while preserving
thematic relevance. In particular, the second set of variations explicitly incorporated the con-
cept of the "farm household" agricultural system, a deﬁning feature of smallholder agriculture
in developing countries, which in modelling terms may involve subsistence constraints, mar-
ket participation decisions, and household-level production–consumption linkages.

The reﬁnement of search expressions and inclusion criteria was guided by the objective
of capturing OR-based decision models relevant to smallholder farming systems. During the
screening stage, studies focusing on large-scale, industrial, or highly mechanized agricultural
systems were excluded. In addition to the database search, backward and forward citation
tracking was conducted on the selected papers to identify further relevant contributions.
Figure 1 summarizes the main stages of the documentary search process.
The search yielded 57 articles using the primary expression, 258 additional references
from the ﬁrst set of variations, and 51 from the second set. After removing duplicates, a total
of 358 articles were identiﬁed. Of these, 59 papers were retained following title, abstract, and
full-text inspection. The complementary citation search contributed an additional 30 papers.
As a result, the ﬁnal sample comprised 89 articles. The search was completed in July 2023.
No review articles were found that speciﬁcally address the application of OR to small-
holder agriculture in developing countries. A more general review by White et al. (2011)
compiled OR applications across several domains, including agriculture, within developing
country contexts. Reviews focusing on farm household models applied to developing coun-
tries, particularly in relation to food security and climate change, are presented in McGregor
et al. (2001); Janssen and Ittersum (2007); Van Wijk et al. (2014). Similarly, Orhan and Gua-
jardo (2022) reviewed the use of data analytics in developing countries and its contribution to
the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, including poverty alleviation and food
security. Two additional reviews examined OR applications in African countries (Ittmann,
2021; Argoubi et al., 2021), although agricultural applications were not their primary focus.
Finally, Cock et al. (2023) reviewed the integration of OR and machine learning in crop and
risk management, including selected case studies from developing countries.

5 Critical analysis under diﬀerent criteria
5.1 Research purposes
The reviewed papers apply OR models to a wide range of issues, including land suitability
analysis,optimalregionalplanning,agriculturalpolicydesignandevaluation,farm-levelopti-
mization, support for farmers’ market participation, and the analysis of interactions among
different components of agricultural systems. These applications are closely interconnected
and, in many cases, are combined within the same study.
Regional planning models aim to maximize the potential of available resources through
their optimal allocation in support of regional development objectives. Policy assessment–
identiﬁed as the most common application in the sample–focuses on evaluating the
effectiveness of strategies designed to promote resource reallocation and the adoption of
alternative technologies. Farm-level optimization, in turn, seeks to identify production and
resource-use strategies that align with farmers’ objectives while accounting for their con-
straints and available technologies.
The analysis of agricultural system dynamics supports the examination of trade-offs and
synergies among system components, as well as their sensitivity to changes in production
conditions, socio-economic contexts, and environmental factors. The insights derived from
these analyses can inform both planning and policy design, contributing to more effective
and context-sensitive decision-making.
5.2 Operations research methods
Among OR methods, linear programming (LP) was the most widely used technique, appear-
ing in 43 articles. Within this group, risk programming models based on LP, such as
minimization of total absolute deviations (MOTAD) and target MOTAD models, were also
applied. LP was followed by non-linear programming (NLP), featured in 11 papers; multiple-
objective evolutionary algorithms (MOEA), found in 7; and simulation (SIM), used in 6,
making these approaches the most frequently employed after LP. The sample also includes
other single-objective optimization techniques, such as mixed-integer linear programming
(MILP), integer linear programming (ILP), stochastic programming (SP), semi-sequential
programming (SSP), and dynamic programming (DP).
In addition to single-objective approaches, several multi-objective optimization meth-
ods were applied, including weighted goal programming (WGP), fuzzy goal programming
(FGP), multiple-goal linear programming (MGLP), interactive multiple-goal linear program-
ming (IMGLP), and multiple-objective linear programming (MOLP). Beyond mathematical
programming-based techniques, the reviewed papers also employ methods such as multi-
criteria decision analysis (MCDA), data envelopment analysis (DEA), and game theory (GT).
Furthermore, community operations research approaches, including robustness analysis (RA)
and the multi-level integrated planning and implementation (MIPI) process, are represented
in the sample. As shown in Figure 5, although linear programming remains the predominant
method, the literature exhibits a trend toward methodological diversiﬁcation over time.

5.3 Modelling scales
5.3.1 Macro-level or regional models
Macro-level models address agricultural systems covering large geographical areas, with
objectives that typically involve the aggregation and allocation of resources at the regional
level. The reviewed studies consider various administrative units, such as states, cantons,
districts, and countries, as well as agro-ecological zones, including river basins and irri-
gated landscapes. Table 3 provides an overview of macro-level model applications. The ﬁrst
column outlines the purpose of the models, including policy analysis, regional planning, land-
use analysis, and agricultural system analysis. The second column lists the policies, planning
objectives, or analytical focuses addressed, while the third column presents the applied tech-
niques. The fourth column reports the corresponding references. This structure is also used
in Tables 4 and 5, which summarize micro-level and multi-level models, respectively.

At the macro level, policy analysis and agricultural system analysis are the most com-
mon applications, with linear programming (LP) being the predominant technique. Policy
analysis is also addressed using non-linear programming (NLP) and dynamic programming
(DP). Regional planning problems are mainly approached through multi-objective tech-
niques, which enable the simultaneous consideration of socio-economic and environmental
goals. Among the 19 papers addressing agricultural issues at the macro scale, only Parikh and
Ramanathan (1999) applies a bio-economic model to track environmental emissions and soil
loss, and only Gohar et al. (2015) employs a multi-period model to examine the combined
effects of irrigation storage and water appropriation policies on agricultural production and
water balances over time.
5.3.2 Micro-level or smallholder models
Micro-scalemodelingfocusesonrepresentingsmallagriculturalsystems,suchasfarmhouse-
holds, communities, or villages. This level of analysis is the most frequently applied in the
sample (Figure 6) and shows an increasing trend over time. Among the 54 papers using a
micro-scale approach, 45 apply it at the farm level, 4 at the village level, 3 at the cooperative
level, and 2 at the community level. Mathematical programming farm household models also
constitute a key component of multi-scale approaches, discussed in the next subsection, where
higher-level models are generally constructed by aggregating micro-level optimizations.
At the micro scale, mathematical programming models are primarily used to simu-
late farmers’ production and consumption decisions, explicitly accounting for objectives,
constraints, and exogenous drivers. This modelling approach allows the analysis of how
socio-economic and environmental macro-level factors inﬂuence farm-level dynamics, as
well as the assessment of alternative technologies and management practices. Micro-scale
models enable an individualized representation of farmers, allowing heterogeneity to be
considered and facilitating the analysis of differentiated responses to environmental and
economic changes.
An important strength of micro-scale modeling is its ability to capture the non-separability
of production and consumption decisions within farm households, a deﬁning characteristic
of subsistence agriculture. As shown in Table 4, this representation capacity is exploited
in applications such as ex-ante policy analysis, which constitutes the most common use
of micro-scale models in the sample, as well as in studies of agricultural system dynamics.
Micro-scale models are also used to explore strategies for improving production and resource
use based on farmers’ objectives and constraints, and to evaluate the impacts of changes in
management and technology. In addition, they support efﬁciency analysis by identifying
potential management adjustments that could enhance farm performance.
Although linear programming (LP) remains the most frequently applied method at this
scale, micro-scale modeling exhibits a greater diversity of OR techniques than other mod-
elling scales. This methodological diversity allows a more comprehensive representation of
farmers’ decision-making processes and the challenges they face. For instance, adaptive deci-
sions under uncertainty are represented through multi-stage recourse stochastic models. This
diversiﬁcation is further accompanied by an increased use of bio-economic and multi-period
models.
5.3.3 Multi-level or mixed-scale models
This category includes 12 papers that combine micro- and macro-scale models. Table 5
summarizes the main applications of multi-level approaches. Several of these models are

bio-economic, non-separable, and/or multi-period in nature. Two broad types of modelling
strategies can be distinguished: two-layer frameworks that explicitly integrate regional and
farm-scale models, and approaches based exclusively on farm-level models that are aggre-
gated to perform upper-scale analyses.
Within the two-layer framework, two modelling conﬁgurations based on linear program-
ming are identiﬁed. In Huang et al. (2010), a regional LP model is combined with a farm-level
ILP model, whereas Roetter et al. (2007) integrate an IMGLP regional model with a LP farm
model. In both cases, large-scale models are used for regional planning or for exploring pol-
icy options aligned with regional objectives, while farm-scale models serve to assess policy
impacts at the individual farm level or to perform farmers’ portfolio analyses.
Approaches based on the aggregation of micro-level models are mainly applied to policy
analysis. In these frameworks, farmers’ heterogeneity is explicitly represented by simultane-
ously modelling different farmer types. The techniques employed in this context include
multi-agent system simulations based on mathematical programming (MP-MAS). Such
models are used to evaluate policies aimed at promoting the adoption of sustainable tech-
nologies, exploring production enhancement strategies, and supporting climate adaptation.
In these applications, farmers are represented as agents using MILP farm-level models that
are spatially linked to landscape units. This structure allows explicit simulation of agent–
environment interactions through biophysical model components. In addition, agent-to-agent
interactions are incorporated by introducing individual network thresholds, enabling the sim-
ulation of technology diffusion processes.
5.4 Objectives, constraints and output indicators
5.4.1 Objectives
In single-objective mathematical programming models, economic return is by far the most
commonly maximized objective, appearing in more than 75 percent of the papers across all
modelling scales. In several studies, this objective is interlinked with social or environmen-
tal aspects such as soil conservation, risk, and self-sufﬁciency. For instance, Shiferaw and
Holden (2000) maximize current net returns net of the present value of future income losses
caused by land productivity decline due to soil erosion, while Komarek and Msangi (2019)
maximize the sum of income and the value of food consumption from on-farm production.
Risk programming approaches are also applied in this context, as in Koide et al. (2021) and
Yu et al. (2022), where the objective is to minimize the sum of negative deviations from
expected income.
Beyond economic returns, food and nutrient-related objectives are considered in several
mono-objective models. For example, Wineman and Crawford (2017) maximize calorie pro-
duction, La Rovere et al. (2005) maximize a weighted sum of food production and income,
and Mtingele and O’Connor (2019) minimize deviations from a population mean calorie
diet. Nutritional considerations are further addressed in Maatman et al. (2002), who mini-
mize nutrient deﬁcits while maximizing net returns subject to subsistence requirements, and
in Rahman and Clarke (1991), who minimize the value of village food aid imports while
maximizing food export value once self-sufﬁciency is achieved.
In addition, some studies formulate utility-based objectives that combine income, con-
sumption of on-farm production, and investment decisions, as illustrated in Kruseman et al.
(1995); Chenoune et al. (2017); Wossen et al. (2014). The optimization of environmental
objectives is not found among the single objective models.

In multiple-objective models, economic returns are typically included, while also taking
into account a wide range of socio-economic and environmental objectives and goals. These
include regional employment (Bazaraa & Bouzaher, 1981; Sharma et al., 2006), food pro-
duction (Nidumolu et al., 2007; Roetter et al., 2007), and regional demand satisfaction and
foreign exchange expenditures (Bazaraa & Bouzaher, 1981). Environmental objectives are
more explicitly represented in this class of models, with soil nitrogen balance considered
in Senthilkumar et al. (2011); Kozicka et al. (2020); Toorop et al. (2020b), and soil organic
matter in Komarek and Msangi (2019); Toorop et al. (2020a,b).
Nutrient production and dietary requirements are also included as objectives in several
multi-objective formulations (Kowero et al., 2005; Komarek & Msangi, 2019; Toorop et
al., 2020a; Estrada-Carmona et al., 2020; Kozicka et al., 2020; Timler et al., 2020). Other
objectives addressed in these models include variance of crop revenues, erosion potential,
and crop diversity (Kozicka et al., 2020), water use (Toorop et al., 2020a), greenhouse gas
emissions (Paul et al., 2020), reduced use of fertilizers and biocides (Keramatzadeh et al.,
2011), and leisure time (Komarek & Msangi, 2019; Estrada-Carmona et al., 2020; Timler et
al., 2020).
5.4.2 Constraints
Financial constraints–such as limited capital, cash ﬂow, income, and discretionary spending–
are the most commonly represented restrictions in the reviewed models. These are typically
accompanied by land and labour availability constraints, which reﬂect intrinsic limitations of
agricultural production systems, although they are not always explicitly stated. In addition,
optimization is frequently conditioned on the fulﬁlment of consumption or nutritional sub-
sistence requirements. These requirements are either embedded in the objective function or
imposed as explicit constraints in several modelling approaches (Rahman & Clarke, 1991;
Schipper et al., 1995; Kaoneka & Solberg, 1997; Dorward, 1999; Shiferaw & Holden, 2000;
Glen & Tipper, 2001; Maatman et al., 2002; La Rovere et al., 2005; Baquedano & Sanders,
2006; Berger et al., 2006; Witcover et al., 2006; Laborte et al., 2007; Roetter et al., 2007;
Schreinemachers et al., 2007; Dolisca et al., 2009; Gerichhausen et al., 2009; Laborte et al.,
2009; Zingore et al., 2009; Baquedano et al., 2010; Keramatzadeh et al., 2011; Dhakal et
al., 2012; Gibreel et al., 2014; Louhichi & Paloma, 2014; Nedumaran et al., 2014; Quang
et al., 2014; Bidogeza et al., 2015; Tessema et al., 2015; Alary et al., 2016; Chenoune et
al., 2017; Ricome et al., 2017; Leonardo et al., 2018; Mtingele & O’Connor, 2019; Koide
et al., 2021). Beyond subsistence requirements, additional constraints are included to reﬂect
resource limitations or to operationalize speciﬁc policy or environmental goals.
Socio-economic constraints are also widely incorporated in the models. These include lim-
ited access to markets (Maatman et al., 2002; Witcover et al., 2006; Alary et al., 2016), proﬁt
equality requirements among cooperative members (Li et al., 2018), and restricted access to
credit or loans (Kruseman et al., 1995; Walters & Norton, 1988; Shiferaw & Holden, 2000;
Holden et al., 2004; Witcover et al., 2006; Laborte et al., 2007; Roetter et al., 2007; Laborte
et al., 2009; Gerichhausen et al., 2009; Berkhout et al., 2011; Bidogeza et al., 2015; Tessema
et al., 2015; Ricome et al., 2017). In several studies, off-farm employment opportunities also
appear as binding constraints affecting household decision-making (Bazaraa & Bouzaher,
1981; Kruseman et al., 1995; Schipper et al., 1995; Holden et al., 2004; Laborte et al., 2007;
Roetter et al., 2007; Dolisca et al., 2009; Laborte et al., 2009; Dhakal et al., 2012; Sharma et
al., 2006).
Environmental constraints are frequently introduced to account for natural resource lim-
itations and conservation objectives. Water availability is the most commonly represented

5.4.3 Output indicators
Besides objective function values and constraint levels, several papers report additional output
indicators to support the discussion of simulation results. These indicators enable the analysis
of synergies and trade-offs between different dimensions, such as planning or policy goals,

A wide range of socio-economic indicators is used in the reviewed papers. These include
farm household trading status (net buyer, net seller, or self-sufﬁcient) (Louhichi et al., 2020),

Other environmental indicators considered in the papers include water use (Nidumolu et

5.5 Challenges approached
5.5.1 Agriculture and rural development
Large- and multi-scale models are used to explore agriculture and rural development options,
with a focus on optimal regional planning and policy evaluation. Single-objective techniques,
including LP, ILP, and NLP, as well as multi-objective methods such as FGP, IMLP, and
MGLP, are applied to address development goals such as increasing income and proﬁts
(Schipper et al., 1995; Walters & Norton, 1988; Laborte et al., 2007; Nidumolu et al., 2007;
Roetter et al., 2007; Huang et al., 2010), generating employment opportunities (Bazaraa &
Bouzaher, 1981; Schipper et al., 1995; Nidumolu et al., 2007; Sharma et al., 2006), and
boosting food production (Nidumolu et al., 2007; Roetter et al., 2007).
Additional objectives include the satisfaction of regional product demand and the man-
agement of foreign exchange expenditures (Bazaraa & Bouzaher, 1981), as well as the
minimization of fertilizer and biocide use (Nidumolu et al., 2007; Roetter et al., 2007).
5.5.2 Food security
Papers in this category, 28 in total, focus on improving production, income, and/or nutrition
amongconstrainedpeasantsmallholders.ORmethodsaremainlyappliedtoexplorestrategies
at the farm level and to evaluate policies aimed at achieving these objectives. In addition,
OR techniques are used in regional planning and to examine the impacts of factors such as
poverty, population growth, and food prices on farm households. More than two-thirds of the
papers apply micro-level modelling, and more than half use LP models. Several studies also
employ NLP and MOEA methods, while other techniques–such as DP, MILP, RA, MCDA,
and DEA–are each applied in one paper.
A wide range of strategies is explored in these studies. These include nutrition-sensitive
agriculture through crop diversiﬁcation (Estrada-Carmona et al., 2020; Timler et al., 2020),
robustness analysis to enhance production (Murtaza & Thapa, 2017; Namen et al., 2009),
and the introduction of agroforestry systems to mitigate risks and net return losses associ-
ated with adverse climatic conditions (Babu & Rajasekaran, 1991). Other approaches focus
on alternative management practices and cultivation systems (Bogahawatte, 1984; Glen &
Tipper, 2001; Yu et al., 2022; Zingore et al., 2009), optimized nutritional planning based on
nutrient production (Chan et al., 2017), and farm redesign based on positive deviant farmers
(Toorop et al., 2020a).
Several studies evaluate policy interventions related to food security. These include the
conversion of lowlands into rice ﬁelds (Chenoune et al., 2017), as well as fertilizer subsidies,
food rationing, and trade liberalization measures (Rebelo, 1986). Policies promoting the
adoption of alternative practices or technologies–such as high-yield seeds, integrated pest
management, and efﬁcient fertilizer use–are assessed in Laborte et al. (2009); Louhichi and
Paloma (2014); Bidogeza et al. (2015).
Additional contributions address food security through the analysis of linkages between
population density and crop productivity (Komarek & Msangi, 2019), the impacts of food
prices and seasonal factors on rural household nutrition (Mtingele & O’Connor, 2019), the
evaluation of labour-saving and land-saving production activities (Leonardo et al., 2018), and
land suitability analysis for self-sufﬁciency food production (Habibie et al., 2021). Finally,
regional planning approaches focusing on agricultural development and improved water

5.5.3 Climate adaptation
Farmers’ adaptation strategies to uncertain rainfall and climate change, as well as policies
supporting these processes, are mostly addressed using micro-scale models. Of the 11 papers
in this category, seven apply farm-household models, while one paper each employs village-
, regional-, and multi-scale models. In addition, one study applies the MIPI process for
multi-level analysis. Unlike other thematic areas, LP is used less frequently in these studies,
appearing in only one paper. Instead, a variety of methods–including MOEA, NLP, DSP, SP,
and SIM–are used to represent aspects such as risk reduction (Koide et al., 2021; Maatman
et al., 2002; Ricome et al., 2017), climate change mitigation (Kozicka et al., 2020; Paul et
al., 2020), and multi-stage adaptive decision-making (Maatman et al., 2002; Robert et al.,

Several studies assess policies aimed at supporting adaptation. These include measures
promoting the adoption of sustainable agricultural practices through credit and subsidies
(Adji et al., 2022), irrigation water management policies (Robert et al., 2018a), weather-
index insurance schemes Ricome et al. (2017), and short-term credit to help farmers adapt
to intra-annual rainfall variability (Wossen et al., 2014).
Other contributions focus on farmers’ adaptive strategies under changing climatic con-
ditions. Two studies (Kozicka et al., 2020; Wineman & Crawford, 2017) simulate future
climate change scenarios for 2050 to analyse impacts on crop yields and farmers’ adapta-
tion responses, including crop choice and increased biodiversity. In addition, Maatman et al.
(2002) models farmers’ responses to uncertain rainfall, while Koide et al. (2021) and Wossen
et al. (2014) explore adaptation strategies such as improved cropping planning combined with
pond irrigation systems and shifts in planting dates, respectively. Finally, participatory cross-
level analyses and multi-level integration of policies and programs aimed at strengthening
smallholders’ adaptive capacity to climate change are addressed in Helfgott et al. (2023).
5.5.4 Sustainable use of natural resources
Severalpapersaddressstrategiesandpoliciesaimedatpromotingforestconservation,sustain-

objectives while seeking to balance the conservation of environmental resources with the
maintenance of agricultural productivity. Micro-scale LP models are the most commonly
used methodologies and are applied in several papers to analyse soil conservation (14
papers) and forest conservation (5 papers) policies. In addition, multi-scale approaches and
other methodologies–including MP-MAS, MOEA, MGLP, MILP, DP, and MCDA–are also
applied.
Beyond policy analysis, OR methods are used to explore alternative soil conservation
strategies and to analyse linkages between agricultural practices and soil nutrient dynamics.
Within this group, over three-quarters of the papers employ bio-economic models, and about
half use multi-period formulations, allowing the representation of farmer–environment inter-
actions over time. In addition to soil and forest conservation, sustainable water-use policies
and farm-level strategies are addressed in four papers applying both micro- and macro-scale
models, using OR techniques such as MOLP, MCDA, NLP, and LP combined with MGLP.

5.5.5 Access to markets
Micro-scale NLP and LP models are used to study the implications of improved access to off-
farm labour on household welfare and land management, as well as the beneﬁts of inventory
credit programs that facilitate fertilizer purchases, as analysed in Holden et al. (2004) and
Baquedano and Sanders (2006), respectively. In addition, Fuchigami et al. (2021) employ
a cooperative-level MILP model to develop a decision support system aimed at assisting
smallholder farmers in making decisions when participating in institutional markets through
non-competitive bidding mechanisms. At a larger scale, Pomareda and Samayoa (1979)
applies a large-scale LP model to examine how small traditional and large commercial farmers
respond to changes in maize price policy, particularly with respect to production technique
adoption and crop selection.
5.5.6 Other Challenges
OR techniques have also been applied to investigate a range of additional issues within the
sample. For instance, the interaction between rural energy and agriculture is examined using
macro-scale LP models in Parikh (1985); Parikh and Kramer (1985); Painuly et al. (1992,
1995); Parikh and Ramanathan (1999). The optimization of smallholder cooperative planning
and the distribution of proﬁts among members are addressed using LP combined with game
theory in Gerichhausen et al. (2009), and through agent-based modelling approaches in Li et
al. (2018). In addition, Gibreel et al. (2014) employ a set of village-level LP models to project
land-use change scenarios for sustainable development across an entire cluster study area.
Finally, Dorward (1999) evaluates modelling techniques such as DSP and SSP to represent
farmers’ responses to embedded risks under conditions of labour and land scarcity, as well
as under varying levels of access to credit.

6 Recapitulation, integrative framework and future outlook
6.1 Recapitulation
Papers in the sample address a wide range of challenges faced by rural farmers through
diverse strategies, operations research techniques, and modelling scales. OR methods are
applied to multiple purposes, including agricultural policy analysis, regional development
planning, farm-level optimization, and the study of rural agricultural systems, with the aim
of supporting smallholder farmers in key areas such as food security, adaptation to climate
variability, access to markets, and the sustainable use of natural resources.
Several trends emerge from the analysis of the 44-year publication sample. First, the
increasing number of publications across a broad range of journal types indicates that OR
methods have gained relevance as tools for addressing rural agriculture problems in devel-
oping countries. This growth is accompanied by a marked increase in the use of micro-scale
modelling approaches and by a diversiﬁcation of the OR methods employed.
The greater emphasis on micro-scale representation reﬂects a growing concern for the
differentiated evaluation of strategies targeting individual farms and their environments, as
well as for the explicit consideration of farmer heterogeneity. In parallel, the diversiﬁcation
of OR methods points to an effort to achieve more comprehensive representations of farmers’
decision-making processes, including their interactions with social, economic, and ecolog-
ical contexts, and to develop integrated approaches to address the multiple and interrelated
challenges they face. This representational capacity has been widely exploited in applications
such as policy analysis, which remains the most common purpose of the models reviewed.
Finally, since the second decade of the 2000s, there has been an increasing tendency to
simultaneously consider socio-economic and environmental dimensions, leading to a greater
use of bio-economic models. More than half of the studies employing such models were
published during this period, highlighting the importance of integrating multiple dimensions
of sustainability when exploring pathways to secure the livelihoods of rural smallholders.
6.2 An integrative conceptual framework for embedding OR in smallholder
decision-making
The bibliometric and thematic analyses conducted in this review reveal a research ﬁeld
that has expanded considerably in scope, methodological diversity, and application domains
over the past four decades. At the same time, the results point to a marked dispersion of
approaches across modelling scales, objectives, and decision contexts. While this diversity
reﬂects the richness of OR applications in smallholder agriculture, it also makes it challenging
to articulate how these contributions collectively support decision-making processes.
Building on the regularities identiﬁed in the reviewed literature, this section proposes
an integrative conceptual framework that synthesizes how OR has been positioned to sup-
port decision-making in smallholder agriculture in developing countries. Figure 7 provides
a schematic representation illustrating the relationships between research determinants,
methodological choices, and mediation mechanisms within smallholder agricultural systems.
It is structured around three core and interrelated dimensions: (i) determinants of research
scope and objectives, (ii) methodological response, and (iii) mediation and research output
application.
These dimensions correspond to the three subsections that follow (Sections 6.2.1–6.2.3),
which describe each component of the framework and clarify its role in embedding OR-based

6.2.1 Determinants of research scope and objectives
Smallholders operate in tightly constrained environments characterized by the non-
separability of production and consumption decisions, exposure to climatic and market risks,
and limited access to land, labour, capital, information, and technology. As a result, research
objectives extend beyond proﬁt maximization to encompass outcomes such as improved food
123

Annals of Operations Research
security, more stable incomes, reduced vulnerability to climate variability, and the sustainable
management of soil, water, and forest resources.
Research in this domain is conducted by a range of stakeholders, including academic
institutions, government agencies, policymakers, and national and international organizations
whoseagendasarelinkedtotheagriculturalandsocio-economicdevelopmentofsmallholders
in developing countries. In this context, OR methods are often embedded within broader
interdisciplinary research frameworks addressing complex development challenges.
The scope and objectives of research are further shaped by contextual factors such as
limited human and ﬁnancial resources, political and institutional conditions, insecurity, lim-
ited and non-standardized data availability, and cultural and social barriers. These conditions
inﬂuence not only what can be modelled, but also which decision problems are prioritised.
Within this setting, OR and optimization methods support the analysis of agricultural
system dynamics and inform decision-making at different, interrelated levels, ranging from
large-scale development planning and policy design to farm-level management. Research
may address one or several interconnected challenges–such as food security, sustainable
production, and climate adaptation–with the aim of improving environmental, social, and
economic dimensions of smallholder agricultural systems. The selection of research topics
reﬂectsboththeseverityofthechallengesfacedbysmallholdercommunitiesandthepriorities
and resources of national and international cooperation frameworks.
6.2.2 Methodological response
The methodological response can be understood as a coherent set of interrelated modelling
decisions that translate research concerns, goals, and contextual constraints into analytical
representations. These decisions include the choice of spatial and temporal scale of analysis,
the identiﬁcation of relevant decision-makers and intended users of model outputs, the selec-
tion of key characteristics of smallholder farming systems, and the representation of external
drivers affecting them.
The choice of modelling scale is closely linked to the primary users of research outputs.
Large-scale models are generally suited to institutional planners and policymakers analysing
regional development pathways, while micro-scale and multi-scale models offer a closer
representation of farmers’ realities and decision-making frameworks, making them relevant
for policy design, extension services, and, in some cases, farmers themselves. Multi-level
modelling platforms that integrate decisions from the farm to the regional level provide a
structured way to capture cross-scale interactions.
At the core of the methodological response are fundamental smallholder objectives, con-
straints, and decision problems, including proﬁt and subsistence goals; ﬁnancial, labour, and
land limitations; crop choice, input allocation, labour organization, and food consumption
decisions. Models may also incorporate adaptive management strategies under uncertainty,
as well as external drivers such as policies promoting technology adoption, resource conser-
vation, or livelihood diversiﬁcation.
Different OR techniques provide complementary ways to represent these problems. Linear
and non-linear programming are widely used for production optimization; multi-objective
approaches allow the explicit treatment of trade-offs between income, nutrition, and environ-
mental goals; stochastic and dynamic models capture risk and intertemporal adaptation; and
simulation, agent-based approaches, and bio-economic models support the representation of
interactions among heterogeneous farmers and between farming systems and ecosystems.
These methods can be viewed as modular components within a broader analytical architec-
ture.

Data availability and quality shape the methodological response. In contexts characterized
by weak data infrastructures, researchers rely on strategies such as simpliﬁed representations,
scenario-based analyses, participatory data collection, sensitivity analysis, and robustness-
oriented modelling approaches.
6.2.3 Mediation and research output application
Only a limited share of the reviewed studies present their models as decision support tools
intended for direct use by farmers or extension agents. Most outputs are designed to inform
researchers, policy makers, government institutions, and organizations involved in agricul-
tural development.
Mediationplaysacentralroleinlinkinganalyticalresultswithﬁeld-leveldecision-making.
Extension services, development organizations, and public institutions act as intermediaries
through which model outputs are interpreted, adapted, and communicated within speciﬁc
local contexts. Through these processes, research outputs interact with smallholder agricul-
tural systems, inﬂuencing economic, social, and environmental outcomes through changes
in practices, resource allocation, and adaptive strategies.
Mediation also introduces feedback mechanisms within the research process. Information
arising from implementation and use can inform research stakeholders about the relevance,
limitations, and practical implications of model-based analyses. Such feedback may highlight
data gaps, mismatches between model assumptions and observed decision practices, or unan-
ticipated system responses, thereby contributing to adjustments in data use and modelling
choices.
6.3 Future outlook
Future research on OR applications to smallholder agriculture in developing countries may
beneﬁt from the design of analytical frameworks that explicitly integrate multiple decision
levels and modelling scales. Strengthening the interaction between farm-level decision mod-
els and higher-level policy, planning, and institutional environments would allow a more
coherent representation of how individual decisions are shaped by, and in turn inﬂuence,
broader agricultural systems. Such integration is particularly relevant in smallholder con-
texts, where farm management, policy incentives, market conditions, and environmental
constraints are tightly interlinked.
Within this perspective, mediation remains a key element for embedding OR within
smallholder decision-making processes. As highlighted throughout the review, most OR
applications are not designed for direct use by farmers, but rather to inform researchers, pol-
icy makers, extension services, and development organizations. The effectiveness and impact
of OR-based analyses therefore depend on mediation mechanisms that translate analytical
outputs into locally meaningful and actionable knowledge. These mechanisms also enable
feedback from implementation and use, informing research stakeholders about data limita-
tions, mismatches between model assumptions and observed practices, and system responses
that may not be captured ex ante.
The current expansion of information and communication technologies (ICTs) and the
ongoing digitalization of agriculture provide new opportunities to strengthen both cross-scale
integration and mediation processes. Digital tools can facilitate data collection, information
sharing, and interaction between farmers, extension services, and institutions, thereby sup-
porting the embedding of OR-based insights within smallholder agricultural systems. Mobile

technologies, remote sensing, cloud computing, and geographical information systems can
enhance the accessibility and usability of analytical tools, while also enabling more dynamic
interactions between decision levels.
At the same time, the digitalization of agriculture in developing countries faces well-
documented challenges, including limited infrastructure, high technology costs, low levels of
digital literacy, weak regulatory frameworks, and uneven access to services. These constraints
raise the risk that digital transformation processes may exacerbate exclusion rather than foster
inclusion. Addressing these challenges requires coordinated investments in infrastructure,
institutional capacity, training, and governance, as well as careful consideration of the socio-
economic contexts in which digital tools are introduced. Importantly, digitalization pathways
are not starting from zero, as mobile phones and basic digital services are already widely
used in many rural areas, offering entry points for gradual and context-sensitive innovation.
Within this evolving context, an area that remains largely unexplored in the reviewed
literature, yet holds signiﬁcant potential, is the application of OR to precision or site-speciﬁc
agriculture in smallholder systems in developing countries. While precision agriculture has
traditionally been associated with capital-intensive and large-scale farming, recent techno-
logical developments open possibilities for adapting site-speciﬁc management approaches
to resource-constrained settings. When combined with OR methods, such approaches could
support more efﬁcient input allocation, improved soil and water management, enhanced yield
stability, and greater resilience to climate variability, directly contributing to food security,
sustainability, and climate adaptation objectives.
Overall, future research directions point toward OR applications that are increasingly
integrated across scales, mediated through institutional and digital channels, and attentive to
the constraints and heterogeneity of smallholder systems. Advancing along these directions
may enhance the relevance and impact of OR-based decision support in developing countries,
while contributing to more inclusive and sustainable agricultural development pathways.
