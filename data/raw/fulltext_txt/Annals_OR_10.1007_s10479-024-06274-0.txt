Abstract
The spread of renewable energy has been accelerated by investment in power generation
and transmission systems under environmental policy support such as a feed-in premium
(FIP) scheme. This study examines the decision-making of the transmission system operator
(TSO) and the power generation company (GENCO), where the TSO maximizes social sur-
plus by investing in transmission lines, and the GENCO maximizes its proﬁt by investing in
power generation facilities. Speciﬁcally, the TSO decides the investment timing, while the
GENCO decides the capacity. We develop a real options model to analyze the equilibrium
investment timing and capacity under uncertainties in a framework of game between TSO
and GENCO. We consider several scenarios in which the GENCO invests in non-renewable
energy (NRE); invests in renewable energy (RE) with FIP; and invests in RE with its instal-
lation cost reduction. Our results indicate that FIP and the installation cost reduction of
RE affect the equilibrium decision in a different manner. We ﬁnd that FIP tends to be more
welfare-enhancing than the reduction of RE installation cost when the degree of uncertainty is
larger. We also demonstrate that social surplus can be increased without FIP if the installation
cost of RE is reduced sufﬁciently in the future.

1 Introduction
As seen in the Paris Agreement, global warming is recognized as a serious threat to the
planet. The energy sector, especially the electric power sector, is a major contributor to the
greenhouse gas emissions that cause global warming due to their use of fossil fuels for power
generation. Thus, a shift from fossil fuels to renewable energy (RE) sources in the electric
power sector is expected to contribute to mitigating the impact of climate change. Many
countries have already begun to take steps to reduce the installation costs of RE, develop
power grids to accommodate RE, and expand policy options for further penetration of RE
sources.Inparticular,theimplementationofrenewableportfoliostandard(RPS),feed-intariff
(FIT), feed-in premium (FIP), and the installation cost reduction of RE through technological
innovation have promoted further investment in RE sources (Cheng et al., 2017).
Since the 1990s, the electric power industry has been restructured beginning with market
reforms mainly in Europe and the United States. The liberalization of electricity markets has
aimed at improving economic efﬁciency. Subsequently, the vertically integrated system, in
which all processes from power generation to delivery are consolidated and managed by a
single entity, has been unbundled to enhance fairness in the use of transmission infrastructure.
While competition has been introduced in the generation sector, the transmission sector
has been kept regulated due to its economies of scale. In an unbundled paradigm, various
restructuring efforts have been made to stimulate competition and promote efﬁciency in
electricity markets (Newbery, 2002; Künneke & Fens, 2007).
Under a vertically unbundled system, the power generation company (GENCO) and the
transmission system operator (TSO) make individual and independent investment decisions
to expand power generation and transmission facilities, taking account of each other’s actions,
which can be regarded as a game between those two parties. In addition, the GENCO and
TSO need to pay attention to uncertain factors such as electricity demand and prices when
making investment decisions in the electricity market. In this study, we envisage a situation
where the GENCO and TSO individually decide on investment in facilities considering each
other’s behavior in an unbundled system and under uncertainty. Speciﬁcally, we apply game
theory and the real options framework to the analysis of equilibrium outcomes.
The real options approach has been applied to decision-making problems under uncertain-
ties in many ﬁelds including the electric power sector (Dixit & Pindyck, 1994). Pringles et
al. (2014) evaluated the investment performance of the transmission system, suggesting that
transmission projects with ﬂexibility in investment, such as options to delay, have a higher
project value. Using a real options model, Fleten et al. (2011) analyzed investment decisions
for transmission capacity expansion between Norway and Germany, taking into account the
effect of the electricity price difference between the two countries. Likewise, Sereno and
Efthimiadis (2018) also studied the investment problem of the transmission line between two
neighboring countries using a real options approach under capacity constraints and incentive
schemes. On the other hand, many real options studies for the GENCO are also found in
the literature. For example, Bøckman et al. (2008) conducted a case study of the Norwegian
investment project for small hydropower plants to evaluate the optimal investment timing and
capacity under uncertainty. They derived the optimal investment rule based on a trigger price
in the setting of continuous design choices. However, most studies using the real options
approach have focused on the decision-making process of only one side, i.e., either only the
TSO or only the GENCO.
Moreover, many studies investigated decision-making problems in the electricity market
under environmental policies on the premise that a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions

that combines the real options framework and game theory was carried out by Zeng and
Chen (2019), who analyzed the optimal concession period of PV projects with the FIP and
subsidy scheme for RE investment in China. Their model was framed as a game between
the government and the private sector. However, to the best of our knowledge, no study has
applied both the real options approach and game theory to decision-making for investment
timing and capacity expansion of TSO and GENCO under environmental policies.
In this study, we unify the real options and game theory framework to analyze the TSO’s
and GENCO’s decision-making for investment in facilities facing demand uncertainty and
environmental policy of FIP. Our focus is on investment timing, capacity expansion, and
social surplus in equilibrium. Toward this end, several scenarios are compared focusing
on the occasions in which the GENCO invests in RE power generation: speciﬁcally, the
scenario with FIP for promoting RE and the scenario with the installation cost reduction
of RE through technological innovation. We demonstrate the difference in the impact of
FIP and installation cost reduction of RE on investment timing and capacity expansion in
equilibrium. FIP somewhat delays the investment timing but encourages capacity expansion
to a great extent, whereas the installation cost reduction of RE signiﬁcantly speeds up the
investment timing but its effect on capacity expansion is relatively small. We also show that
the magnitude of social surplus in each scenario varies depending on the degree of uncertainty.
We ﬁnd that FIP tends to be more welfare-enhancing than the reduction of RE installation
cost when the degree of uncertainty is greater. However, our result suggests that a sufﬁcient
reduction in RE installation cost, as low as the same level as the current installation cost of
thermal power generation, in the future may even make the FIP scheme redundant irrespective
of the degree of uncertainty.

2.1 Setup and scenarios
This study considers the electricity market consisting of separate GENCO and TSO under
vertical unbundling. We model GENCO’s and TSO’s decision-making problems with differ-
ent objectives. The GENCO aims to maximize its proﬁt from electricity sales as a private
company, while the TSO aims to maximize the social surplus as a public institution under
regulation.
We set up several scenarios to focus on the pressing need to promote investment in RE
for decarbonization. To compare the investment in non-renewable energy (NRE) with invest-
ment in RE, we consider two scenarios of capacity expansion - by NRE using LNG (NRE
scenario) and by RE using wind power (RE scenario). The RE scenario is further divided
into two scenarios, one with subsidies through FIP (RE-FIP scenario) and the other with
a reduction in the installation costs of RE (RE-Cost scenario). In recent years, the invest-
ment costs of RE have declined because of technological innovations and mass production.
Anticipating this trend, we envisage the RE-Cost scenario to examine the impact of poten-
tial future cost reductions on investment decisions. However, despite these progresses, the
current investment costs associated with RE still remain relatively high, necessitating policy
support for widespread adoption of RE. The FIP scheme aims to promote RE investment by
providing incentives through a premium added to the market price of electricity generated
from RE. Currently, many countries are transitioning from FIT to FIP, i.e., from a non-market
ﬁxed-price scheme to a more efﬁcient market-based scheme. In this context, we consider the
RE-FIP scenario to assess the impact of the FIP scheme on investment decisions. Figure 1
illustrates the difference and relationship of the scenarios, particularly, depicting how the
GENCO makes decisions under three scenarios, i.e., NRE, RE-Cost, and RE-FIP scenarios.
In all scenarios, the GENCO has an initial power generation capacity of q0 using LNG.
Similarly, the TSO has an initial transmission capacity of q0 that matches the generation
capacity. In the NRE scenario, further generation capacity of LNG is added to the GENCO’s
initial capacity q0 to become q1 (only LNG). In the RE scenario, the capacity to generate
wind power is added to the GENCO’s initial capacity q0 to become q1 (mix of LNG and
wind). After reaching the investment threshold of x, indicating the investment timing, the
TSO expands the transmission capacity from q0 to q1 according to the capacity determined
by the GENCO. Coherently, the GENCO invests in expanding its generation capacity from
q0 to q1, given the TSO’s investment threshold.

We assume that the TSO invests as soon as the investment threshold is reached, denoting
investment threshold as the investment timing in this study. Similarly, we assume that the
GENCO takes the TSO’s investment threshold as its own investment timing and invests as
soon as the investment threshold is reached. Since the construction of a transmission line
takes a considerable amount of time and costs, it is realistic to consider a setting in which
the TSO decides the investment timing taking account of generation capacity determined by
the GENCO. Note that a larger investment threshold means a delay in the investment timing,
where the TSO waits more for its investment.
The capacity q1 is obtained as a solution to the GENCO’s proﬁt maximization, while the
investment threshold x is derived from the TSO’s social surplus maximization using a value-
matching condition and a smooth-pasting condition. Our setup can be regarded as a game
between two players (Künneke & Fens, 2007; Pollitt, 2008). That is, the GENCO decides the
optimal capacity given the TSO’s investment timing, while as a public institution the TSO
determines the optimal timing for transmission investment given the GENCO’s action.

4.2 Best response function and equilibrium
As discussed earlier, the TSO and GENCO decide investment timings and capacity expansion
given each other’s action. Thus, we can regard x(q1) and q1(x) as the best response functions.
The results of the propositions in Sect.3 are illustrated in this section.
First, Fig. 3 shows the best response functions of the RE-FIP scenario with various FIP
levels while keeping other parameters ﬁxed at a baseline, i.e., N = M = 1000 and EW =
300, 000. The horizontal and vertical axes indicate the investment timing and the capacity,
respectively. The intersection of the best response functions represents the equilibrium of
investment timing and capacity. For all the ﬁgures in this section, we set σ = 0.1.
The case of FW = 0 in Fig. 3 can be seen as a baseline of the RE-FIP scenario, in which
the subsidy level is set at zero. At the same time, this case is also regarded as a baseline for
the RE-Cost scenario, i.e., FW = 0 and EW = 300, 000, which corresponds to the current
level of the installation cost of RE. Thus, the RE-FIP and RE-Cost scenarios share the same
baseline. As depicted in the base case of FW = 0, x(q1) and q1(x) are increasing functions of
each other’s variables, exhibiting the characteristics of strategic complements (Tirole, 1988).
Figure 3 demonstrates that increases in the FIP level, i.e., FW = 10 and 20, push up
the GENCO’s best response function q1(x), consistent with Proposition 1. It, in turn, shifts
the intersection point upwards and raises the equilibrium capacity compared to the base
case of FW = 0. On the other hand, although FIP has no direct effect on the TSO’s best
response function x(q1), the upward shift of q1(x) eventually moves the intersection point
in the upper-right direction with strategic complements, thereby delaying the equilibrium
investment timing. Therefore, the greater FIP level has the effect of increasing the capacity
but delaying the investment timing in equilibrium.
Next, Fig. 4 illustrates the RE-Cost scenario with EW = 100, 000, which is decreased
from the baseline of EW = 300, 000. Consistent with Proposition 2, the reduction in the
installation cost of RE shifts the GENCO’s best response function q1(x) upward, while
simultaneously shifting the TSO’s best response function x(q1) leftward. The equilibrium is
determined, depending on the magnitude of the effect of increasing the capacity and hastening
the investment timing through these shifts. The upward shift of q1(x) has the effect of raising
the capacity, and the leftward shift of x(q1) induces a reduction of the capacity, where q1(x)
and x(q1) are strategic complements. These two effects almost offset each other in our case

5 Conclusion
By applying a game theory framework and the real options approach, this study analyzed the
TSO’s and GENCO’s decision-making and resulting equilibria under demand uncertainty
and the FIP scheme in the transmission and generation sectors. We examined the investment
timing, capacity expansion, and social surplus, particularly focusing on the scenario with
FIP to promote RE (RE-FIP) and the scenario with the reduction in the installation cost
of RE (RE-Cost). We showed that FIP encourages capacity expansion to a large extent in
the RE-FIP scenario, whereas the installation cost reduction of RE signiﬁcantly hastens the
investment timing in the RE-Cost scenario. This indicates that FIP and the installation cost
reduction of RE have different impact on the equilibrium decision, and hence, the outcome
of social surplus. We demonstrated that the FIP scheme can be more welfare-enhancing than
the reduction of RE installation cost when the volatility or the degree of uncertainty is larger.
However, our result indicates that if the RE installation cost is reduced to the same level as the
current installation cost of thermal power generation due to further technological innovation
in the future, then FIP may becomes even redundant irrespective of the degree of uncertainty.
We suggest that policymakers decide whether to implement the FIP scheme by carefully
taking account of RE installation costs and market uncertainty. This could avoid unnecessary
public burdens while improving efﬁciency. Furthermore, a stimulus for active R&D to reduce
RE installation costs could ultimately enhance social welfare without policy support for RE.
Finally, possible directions for future research are listed below. Our model can be reﬁned
in future work by considering the impact of long-term market trends, such as demographic
shifts and technological advancements, and by incorporating more precise coefﬁcients of
the inverse demand function based on empirical data. Although this study focused on wind
power generation and FIP, analyses of other RE sources such as solar photovoltaic power

generation and other RE policies such as RPS are worth consideration for future research
topics. A leader-follower game between GENCO and TSO is also an avenue for future
research. Speciﬁcally, it is worth investigating proactive transmission planning within a game-
theoretic real options framework, where the TSO acts as the leader to proactively encourage
subsequent investments by the GENCO. In addition, extending the analytical framework,
such as increasing the number of players to reﬂect more realistic market settings, is another
area for future work.
