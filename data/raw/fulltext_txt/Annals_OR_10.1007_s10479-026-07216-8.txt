Abstract
This paper focuses on the assignment of attending physicians between the residents they
supervise and their own responsibilities. Unlike prior work that assumes patient care can
be interrupted at any time, we consider the more realistic and technically challenging sit-
uation when patient care is non-preemptive. Under the assumption that a holding cost is
incurred when residents and patients wait for a conference with the attending physician and
that rewards are earned whenever the attending physician completes a task (on his own or
with his residents), we completely characterize the allocation of the attending physician that
maximizes the long-run average proÔ¨Åt. Furthermore, we show that the optimality conditions
are simple thresholds on the holding cost. We also discuss how the optimal allocation of the
attending physician differs from those in systems with preemptions. In particular, we show
that the main difference occurs when the holding cost is high and there is no resident waiting
for a conference. In this case, the attending physician waits for a resident to be ready for
consultation in the non-preemptive case, whereas he works on his own responsibilities in the
preemptive model. We conclude with a study of various extensions of our attending physician
and residents interaction model and show that the structure of the optimal policy remains the
same in these more general settings.
1 Introduction
A resident physician, or resident, is a person who has a medical degree and practices medicine
under the supervision of fully licensed physicians, usually in a teaching hospital or clinic. At
medical schools, physicians learn a broad range of medical knowledge and basic clinical skills
while medical residency gives in-depth training within a speciÔ¨Åc branch of medicine. The
primary responsibility of a resident physician in a clinic or a hospital is patient care. In fact,
it is often the case that patients are Ô¨Årst examined by a resident physician who then consults
with the attending physician to reach a decision about the case. After this consultation, a care
plan for the patient may be prepared or the attending physician may want to examine the
patient again to conÔ¨Årm the Ô¨Åndings of the resident physician.

Since the attending physician works with several residents, examines patients on his own,
and has other responsibilities such as leadership, teaching, etc., resident physicians often do
not Ô¨Ånd the attending physician available and the patients may end up waiting for a long
time before their care plan can be formed. Hence, it is important to understand when an
attending physician should switch from his own responsibilities to confer with his resident
physician(s). When work prioritization is efÔ¨Åcient, the same clinical staff can serve a larger
number of patients at a lower cost. As a matter of fact, as discussed in Andrad√≥ttir et al.
(2021); Dobson et al. (2012), and their references, in the United States the Medicare program
makes explicit payments to teaching hospitals for a portion of the costs incurred due to the
training of the residents. However, the current payment rate for teaching health centers is far
from sufÔ¨Åcient and it is important to improve the operational efÔ¨Åciency in an effort to reduce
cost.
There is a vast amount of literature on the importance of attending physician and residents
interactions. For one of the earliest works, the reader is referred to Knudson et al. (1989).
Some more recent papers on this topic are Al Achkar (2020); Figg et al. (2023); Gupta et
al. (2016), and Thibodeau et al. (2010). But the literature on analytical models of attending
physician and residents interactions is scarce. The assignment of an attending physician to
tasks is related to synchronous resource allocation (see for example Andrad√≥ttir et al. 2022
and the references therein). However, to the best of our knowledge, there exist only two
analytical papers that speciÔ¨Åcally analyze the interaction between attending physician and
residents, namely (Dobson et al., 2012) and Andrad√≥ttir et al. (2021). For simplicity, both
of these papers focus on the case where service processes can be preempted. In particular,
while Andrad√≥ttir et al. (2021) consider the case without preemption and Dobson et al.
(2012) provide numerical examples comparing throughput performance for systems with
and without preemption, neither paper provides analytical results when service preemptions
are not allowed.
While preemptions greatly facilitate the analysis, it is not a reasonable assumption in a
healthcare setting from a practical point of view because service times in this case mostly
represent times spent with patients. Othman et al. (2015) and Othman and Hammadi (2017)
discuss the importance of non-preemptive service in pediatric emergency departments. Simi-
larly, Master et al. (2018) focus on non-preemptive scheduling of jobs motivated by managing
hospital operations in disaster scenarios. The main contribution of this paper is to study an
attending physician and resident interaction model that does not allow for preemptions. In
particular, our objective is to develop a more realistic model that will capture resident and
attending physician interactions in a healthcare facility with the objective of maximizing the
long-run average proÔ¨Åt. The exact analysis and control of the model without preemptions is
also signiÔ¨Åcantly more challenging than the one with preemptions because the corresponding
continuous-time Markov chain is no longer a birth-death process. Another contribution of
this paper is to understand if the optimal policies for systems with or without preemptions
differ. In particular, if the optimal policies are similar, this gives the assurance that in such
health care settings, one can gain useful insights from studying the simpler problem that has
preemptions. We will explicitly identify under what conditions the optimal policies differ
and when they are similar.
Dobson et al. (2012) consider a model where the patients are Ô¨Årst examined by the res-
idents, then the residents and the attending physician have a conference, and Ô¨Ånally, the
attending physician examines the patients on his own before forming a care plan. In their
model, the attending physician either idles, or confers with residents, or examines the patients
on his own, but has no other responsibilities of his own. Their objective is to determine
whether the attending physician should idle or consult with residents or examine the patients

on his own in order to maximize the throughput (minimize Ô¨Çow time), but Dobson et al.
(2012) limit their analysis to care facilities with at most two residents and a single attending
physician. They show that the attending physician never idles as long as there is a patient
ready for examination or a resident ready for consultation. We consider a model that both
includes throughput as a special case and also is more consistent with the goals of a teaching
health center which must simultaneously control operational cost while providing quality
(non-preemptive) medical care. Hence, we maximize the long-run average reward subject to
a penalty incurred when the patients wait for a conference between the attending physician
and his residents. Our analysis allows an arbitrary number of residents.
Andrad√≥ttir et al. (2021) study a problem similar to that considered in this paper except
that all tasks can be preempted (like Dobson et al. 2012), which may not be a reasonable
assumption in health care settings. We study a more realistic and technically more difÔ¨Åcult
model where all task times are non-preemptive. When preemptions are not allowed, not only
is the underlying continuous-time Markov chain no longer a birth-death process but also
the optimal policy has a more complicated structure because the attending physician may
wait for a resident to be ready for consultation rather than working on his own responsibil-
ities. Our analysis will demonstrate that in systems without preemptions the optimal policy
depends on the number of residents supervised by the attending physician, which is not case
in systems with preemptions. Moreover, when the holding cost is high, there is a signiÔ¨Åcant
difference between the optimal policies for systems with and without preemptions. We pro-
vide numerical examples in Section 6.1 illustrating that using the optimal policy for systems
with preemptions when preemptions are not allowed may yield a loss in long-run average
proÔ¨Åt as large as 929.5%.
In a related recent paper (though not in the context of residents and attending physicians),
Yu et al. (2023) consider a two-stage service system with abandonments and two types of
servers, namely subordinates who perform the Ô¨Årst-stage service and supervisors who have
their own responsibilities in addition to collaborating with the subordinates on the second-
stage service. Rewards are earned when Ô¨Årst- or second-stage service is completed and when
supervisors Ô¨Ånish one of their own responsibilities. Costs are incurred when impatient cus-
tomers abandon without completing the second-stage service and preemption of all services
is allowed. They determine how the supervisors should distribute their time between their
joint work with the subordinates and their own responsibilities to maximize the long-run
average reward. The current manuscript studies the interaction between supervisors and sub-
ordinates in a more realistic healthcare setting in which service preemptions are not allowed
and with holding costs to model the desirability of reducing patient wait times.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the model rigorously
and introduces the notation. In Section 3, we formulate the problem as a Markov decision
process. Section 4 is devoted to the characterization of the optimal policy in the most general
setting. Section 5 characterizes the optimal policy when there are certain restrictions on the
assignment of the attending physician. Section 6 discusses how the optimal policy without
preemptions differs from the one with preemptions. Section 7 focuses on various extensions
of our non-preemptive attending physician and residents interaction model. Finally, Section 8
concludes the paper. The proof of our main theorem and some long closed-form expressions
are given in appendices.

2 Model description
We assume that in addition to his own responsibilities, an attending physician is in charge
of N ‚â•1 resident physicians and that there are N examination rooms. Each patient is Ô¨Årst
examined by a resident and then the resident and the attending physician have a conference to
form a care plan for the patient. Since there are N examination rooms, the residents idle while
waiting to have a consultation with the attending physician after their initial examination of
the patients. This two-stage queueing system (which is depicted in Figure 1) can model the
interaction between the attending physician and his residents both when the conference takes
place in the presence of the patient and when the attending physician does not meet the
patient. We focus on the case when the amount of time that it takes a resident physician to
examine a patient is exponentially distributed with rate Œºr > 0, the conference time of the
resident and the attending physician is exponential with rate Œºc > 0, and the task times of the
attending physician on his own responsibilities are exponential with rate Œºa > 0. All service
times are independent of each other. The service times are also non-preemptive, which means
that the attending physician has to Ô¨Ånish his current task (conference with a resident or his
own responsibility) before switching to the next task and, similarly, the residents should
Ô¨Ånish conferences with the attending physician before switching to examining patients on
their own. Furthermore, there is an expected reward of rp ‚â•0 when a patient care plan is
formed, an expected reward of ra ‚â•0 when the attending physician Ô¨Ånishes one of his own
responsibilities, and an expected cost h ‚â•0 incurred by patients per unit time spent waiting
for the attending physician.

8 Conclusions
This paper focuses on an attending physician and residents interaction model that does not
allow preemptions of tasks. Though it is more realistic, this interaction model is signiÔ¨Åcantly
more challenging (to analyze) than the one with preemptions since the underlying stochastic
process is no longer a birth-death process.
We show that there could be only three policies that are optimal. If the holding cost is small
(h ‚â§ho), then the attending physician prioritizes his own responsibilities. If the holding cost
is intermediate (ho ‚â§h ‚â§hN), then the attending physician prioritizes his residents as long
as there is a resident ready for consultation. Finally, if the holding cost is large (h ‚â•hN), then
the attending physician consults with his residents at all times and idles when there are no
residents ready for consultation. Thus, in health care settings without service preemptions,
the optimal policy depends on the number of residents N which is different from systems
with preemptions. In particular, when h ‚â•hN, then the attending physician idles when
there are no patients ready for consultation in the non-preemptive case but works on his own
responsibilities in the preemptive case. We numerically demonstrate that using the optimal
attending physician assignment policy for systems with preemptions when preemptions are
not allowed may result in signiÔ¨Åcant losses in the long-run average proÔ¨Åt. These numerical
examples emphasize that when the holding cost is large, there is a substantial difference
between the two models.
We consider various extensions of our original attending physician and residents interac-
tion model, including a hybrid model where a certain percentage of the residents‚Äô patients
preempt the attending physician while he is working on his own responsibilities and a model
123

Annals of Operations Research
with an idling cost of the attending physician. In both these generalizations, the structure of
the optimal policy remains the same, but the expression of hN is different. This indicates that
our results can be applicable in more general health care settings.
As an immediate future research direction, we intend to consider healthcare settings with
different customer classes where some patients may only need care from the residents whereas
others may need the residents to consult with the attending physician.
