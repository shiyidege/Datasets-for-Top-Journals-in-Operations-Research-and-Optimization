Abstract
Nowadays, the need to reduce the industry’s energy demands means that green concerns must
be incorporated as performance indicators. In scheduling, this translates into decision makers
willing to sacriﬁce, to a certain point, production-related criteria, such as meeting delivery
deadlines, in favour of reducing energy consumption. The exact degree of compromise can be
difﬁcult to set a priori; instead, the expert may only be able to provide some vague production
goals that need to be achieved before dealing with energy savings. The decision maker’s task is
furthercomplicatedinmanyreal-worldproblemsduetothepresenceofuncertaintyinsomeof
the parameters. In this paper, we tackle a ﬂexible job shop scheduling problem with uncertain
task durations, minimising the total weighted tardiness while also considering the total energy
consumption. In this setting, when there is a clear hierarchy between multiple objectives, a
lexicographical goal programming approach seems natural but has the caveat of needing
well-deﬁned target values a priori. Therefore, we propose a new hierarchical multiobjective
adaptive strategy to work in a lexicographic goal programming setting where only vaguely
deﬁned goals are provided. This strategy is then embedded in a memetic algorithm. Extensive
experimentation shows that the proposed adaptive strategy is more suitable for the problem at
hand than a purely lexicographic and a traditional lexicographic goal programming strategy,
and it constitutes an excellent complement to Pareto-based methods.

1 Introduction
Scheduling is a classical manufacturing topic concerned with arranging the execution of a
set of operations on a set of resources under a set of constraints in order to achieve certain
objectives (Pinedo 2022). Solving such problems is essential in industrial applications (Han
et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2022; Wang et al. 2021).
One of the most important families of scheduling problems is the job shop (Çali¸s and
Bulkan, 2015) since its different variants can model many practical engineering and social
applications (Xiong et al., 2022). In particular, the ﬂexible job shop, which incorporates a
resource-allocation problem to the classical operation-sequencing problem, attracts special
attention due to its relevance in modern industrial problems (Dauzère-Pérès et al., 2024). As
many other problems in the scheduling family, the ﬂexible job shop is NP-hard (Lenstra et
al. 1977). This complexity poses a computational challenge and is the reason why most of
the proposed solving methods fall into the category of metaheuristics (Chaudhry and Khan
2016).
Most research devoted to scheduling assumes a deterministic setting. However, uncer-
tainty pervades real-life situations and adds a challenge to all industrial processes. Therefore,
uncertainty must be taken into account, so that obtained schedules are robust to withstand
possible deviations and production can handle unexpected events with minor issues (Bakon
et al. 2022). In García Gómez et al. (2023); Palacios et al. (2015) it is shown that modelling
uncertain processing times using fuzzy numbers allows achieving this aim, with the downside
of increased difﬁculty in solving the resulting problems.
Traditionally, scheduling problems have considered production-related objectives such as
reducing the makespan or the total weighted tardiness (Vela et al. 2020; García Gómez et
al. 2020). More recently, green objectives have gained relevance as the industry has to adapt
production to new laws and regulations that seek to reduce the ever-increasing demand for
energy (García Gómez et al. 2023; Shen et al. 2023; Ding et al. 2023; Rabet et al. 2024;
Komsiyah et al. 2018; Armasarwah 2025). Indeed, green objectives are framed within the
Sustainable Development Goals (United Nations 2015). In particular, one way to combat
climate change and its impacts (Goal 13) is through the development of sustainable and
innovative industrial processes (Goal 9), which lead to more sustainable consumption and
production patterns (Goal 12).
However, green objectives cannot be considered in isolation, as industry cannot sacriﬁce
proﬁt for energy efﬁciency at any cost (Kannan et al. 2023). For this reason, multiobjective
optimisation plays a paramount role in the green transition, and research is essential to ﬁnd
new methods that allow for a good balance between objectives (Cosmi et al. 2024). Literature
shows that research in this context is mostly focused on dominance-based approaches, both
in crisp Wei et al. (2021); Abedi et al. (2020); Li and Chen (2023) and uncertain Af¸sar et al.
(2022); Guo et al. (2024); Shukla et al. (2020); Zhou and Liu (2019); Wang et al. (2024); Wu
et al. (2025) settings. However, it is usual that production-oriented goals, such as meeting
delivery deadlines to improve customer satisfaction, and reducing energy consumption, are
not equally important to companies at all times and under all circumstances.
When integrating the goals or preferences of different stakeholders leads to establishing a
priority structure for the objectives, it is natural to resort to a lexicographic strategy (Xiao and
Konak 2015). However, a pure lexicographical model may focus too much on the ﬁrst objec-
tive and disregard the remaining ones. Typically, a decision maker might prefer to sacriﬁce
a small amount of quality in the main objective if that leads to a signiﬁcant improvement in
the rest. Lexicographic goal programming can be considered for these situations, but ﬁnding

appropriate target values for each objective usually depends on the speciﬁc domain and it
is not always easy for the decision maker to establish them a priori (Guggeri et al. 2023;
Yaghoobi et al. 2008).
Goal programming has been applied to a wide range of modern applications and is increas-

have paid little attention to the case of scheduling problems where priorities for objectives
exist. In González Rodríguez et al. (2010) the authors use a lexicographic goal program-
ming approach in a genetic algorithm to solve the fuzzy job shop problem with makespan,
maximum tardiness and maximum idleness minimisation. The same approach is followed
to solve the fuzzy open shop scheduling problem with a particle swarm algorithm minimis-
ing makespan and maximum tardiness in Palacios et al. (2015a) and makespan and ﬂexible
due-date satisfaction in Palacios et al. (2015b). More recently, a lexicographic optimisation
approach is adopted to minimise total quality loss and total tardiness in a parallel-machine
scheduling problem (Chen et al. 2023). To our knowledge, the only works using lexicographic
goal programming with green objectives can be found in Lei and Guo (2015), where a lexi-
cographic method is applied to the green job shop with interval processing times minimising
the makespan and the carbon footprint, and in Armasarwah (2025), where a multilevel lexi-
cographical analysis is proposed, as a ﬁrst solution, for a ﬂexible job shop considering total
energy consumption.
In multiobjective decision-making problems where the decision maker wants to have
ﬂexible targets for each objective, fuzzy goal programming allows modelling aspiration
levels using fuzzy numbers as target values and fuzzy relations (Yaghoobi et al. 2008). While
this formulation offers more ﬂexibility, it still requires the decision maker to provide target
values, albeit fuzzy.
In this work, we propose to take an alternative approach within lexicographical goal
programming by working with vaguely deﬁned goals. We consider the case when a decision
maker is not capable of providing clear targets but can instead provide tolerance levels for the
objectives, so a given a deviation from the optimum is tolerated for one objective if this allows
to improve a less prioritary one. Then, we propose a novel strategy that can be embedded in
any search method used to solve the resulting problem. This strategy uses adaptive targets,
which evolve during the search process by considering tolerance levels with respect to the
best solutions found so far in the search. The computational results show that the proposed
approach allows for a controlled and limited deterioration in the main objective in exchange
of expected signiﬁcant improvements in secondary objectives.
Speciﬁcally, in this paper, we will confront the fuzzy ﬂexible job shop minimising both
the total weighted tardiness and the total energy consumption in a hierarchical manner con-
sidering vague goals. The contributions are the following:
• The deﬁnition of a lexicographical goal programming problem that requires different
knowledge from the decision maker compared to classical goal programming. This is
done by substituting clear-cut goals represented by precise target values with vague
goals deﬁned in terms of tolerance thresholds with respect to ideal but unknown targets.
• A new lexicographic adaptive goal strategy that solves the proposed multiobjective prob-
lem by calculating dynamic targets based on the available knowledge at each step,
allowing the search algorithm to ﬁnd a trade-off between the objectives that correlates
better with the decision maker’s expectations.

• A memetic algorithm integrating the proposed strategy to solve the ﬂexible job shop prob-

and the energy consumption.
• An extensive experimental analysis on a varied set of instances of the proposed adaptive
goal strategy alongside solutions obtained by transforming the problem into a pure lexi-

2 The green fuzzy ﬂexible job shop scheduling problem
The ﬂexible job shop scheduling problem (FJSP) consists in scheduling a set O of operations
(also called tasks) in a set R of m resources (also called machines) subject to a set of con-
straints. Each operation o ∈O belongs to one job χ(o) in a set J of n jobs, so operations within
a job must be sequentially scheduled. The position of an operation o in its job’s sequence
will be denoted by η(o). There are also capacity constraints, by which every operation o ∈O
must be processed in one resource from a subset R(o) ⊆R. The operation’s processing time
p(o,r) depends on the resource r ∈R(o) and the operation requires the uninterrupted and
exclusive use of the resource for its whole processing time. Additionally, each job j ∈J has
a due date d( j) by which it should be completed and a weight w( j) indicating its priority.
In the energy-aware version of the problem, resources consume energy. We consider that
the energy consumption of each resource depends on two overlapping states of the resource:
on and active. Each resource r ∈R incurs a passive power consumption per time unit Pp(r)
when it is on. Additionally, when an operation o ∈O is being processed in a resource
r ∈R(o), this resource consumes an extra amount of energy per time unit, the active power
Pa(o,r) (García Gómez et al. 2023).

4 A novel multiobjective strategy
The lack of explicit target values in the lexicographical goal programming variant from
Equation (6) means that it is not immediate to solve it with existing methods. Clearly, if the
optimal value f ∗
i for each objective fi were known, then transforming vague targets into
speciﬁc ones (and vice versa) would be trivial. In the absence of such knowledge, we may
resort to using heuristic estimates hi of the optimal values f ∗
i , hi ≈f ∗
i , so the explicit target
value can be approximated as ti ≈(1 + αi)hi. The problem remains of how to provide
such heuristic estimates. Perhaps the most naïve approach is to take a trivial lower bound
hi = 0 for the optimal value. In practice, this would result in a lexicographical minimisation
problem. A second option is to obtain the heuristic value hi as the fi value for the solution
obtained by solving the problem with fi as a single objective or, equivalently, with fi as the
primary objective in a pure lexicographical setting.

5 Experimental results
To empirically evaluate our proposal, we shall present experimental results obtained with the
multiobjective memetic algorithm integrating the Lexicographic Adaptive Goal Program-
ming strategy, denoted LAGP henceforth. These results will be compared in Section 5.1 with
those obtained by the same multiobjective memetic algorithm using a pure lexicographical
criterion, denoted Lex, as well as using a lexicographical goal programming with preﬁxed
goals criterion, denoted LGP. For the latter, the goal ti for each objective function fi is set
as ti = (1 + αi)hi, where hi is the value obtained by Lex using fi as primary objective

5.2 Relationship with Pareto-optimality
For multiobjective optimisation, dominance-based algorithms are probably the most common
approach. They search for sets of non-dominated solutions that approximate Pareto-optimal
solutions. Pareto-optimal solutions are related to lexicographically optimal solutions; in fact,
a lexicographically optimal solution is always non-dominated and optimal solutions to a
lexicographical goal-programming problem are non-dominated solutions to the problem of
minimising deviations w.r.t. to target values (Ehrgott 2005). Therefore, one may argue that
optimal lexicographic solutions can also be obtained using common dominance-based tech-
niques. In this section, we run a series of experiments to test if our approach offers any
advantage with respect to using a dominance-based method. It is not frequent that authors
compare solutions of these different multiobjective approaches. We will follow the method-
ology proposed in Palacios et al. (2015a) to study the relationship between them. To this end,
we have modiﬁed the well-known NSGA-II to incorporate local search, so an intensiﬁcation
phase is also present and comparisons are fairer. Integrating a proper multiobjective trajectory
method is not trivial, so we have opted to randomly choose one of the objectives for each of
the generated offspring and, for that offspring and objective, use the same single objective
tabu search strategy as in LAGP.
For this experiment, we run both methods 10 times in each instance. Each run i of the
memetic version of NSGA-II provides a set of non-dominated solutions Si, i = 1, . . . , 10.
For LAGP, we used the results from the previous section. The set of 10 solutions obtained
when f1 = 

is denoted S(T W T ,T E).

5.3 Sensitivity analysis
In the experimental results presented so far, we have emulated a decision maker and, using
our expertise on the scheduling problem and the objective functions under consideration, we
have set the tolerance values that deﬁne the vague targets as αi = 0.05 when fi = 
T W T and
αi = 0.01 when fi = 
T E. However, it is natural to wonder how sensitive is the proposed
adaptive strategy LAGP to the tolerance levels provided by the decision maker.
To perform a sensitivity analysis, we have tested all possible combinations of α1 and
α2 where αi ∈{0.0, 0.01, 0.025, 0.05, 0.1}, i = 1, 2, for each of the two possible orders
between the 
T W T and the 
T E. Since we are running LAGP on 12 instances, this means
testing 600 cases in total. In the following, we will summarise the most important insights;
the detailed results for the 600 cases will be made available in the linked repository.
Those combinations where α1 = 0 have not been included in the following numerical
results, since in these cases the adaptive strategy (and thus the algorithm) is reduced to a pure
lexicographic comparison, regardless of α2, save for possible tie-breaks which, in any case,
cannot make a signiﬁcant difference.
As mentioned at the beginning of Section 5, since the optimal value f ∗
i for the objective
functions is unknown, it can be heuristically approximated hi by the value of fi in the solution
provided by Lex using fi as the primary objective. Thus, hi can be used as a reference value to
assess if the solutions obtained with LAGP satisfy the vague goal speciﬁed with the threshold
level αi. However, Lex is not a deterministic method, so a different result is obtained on
each run. In consequence, the reference values hi follow a distribution with mean μi and
standard deviation σi, for i = 1, . . . , G, and, given a tolerance level αi, the target values

6 Conclusions and future work
In this paper, we have presented a novel lexicographical vague goal programming approach
to tackle the fuzzy ﬂexible job shop problem considering the total weighted tardiness and
the total energy consumption. As an alternative to the traditional approach, this new multi-
objective problem avoids having to provide speciﬁc target values to deﬁne the goals. Instead,
we allow to deﬁne vague goals, so decision makers only need to provide tolerated deviations
from the optimal (unknown) values of the objective values. To deal directly with this novel
problem deﬁnition, we have proposed a new strategy that uses increasingly tight adaptive
target values to meet the decision maker’s expectations.

Annals of Operations Research

To assess the performance of this new multiobjective optimisation framework, we have
carried out a detailed experimental analysis that compares our strategy with the closest
existing approaches: a pure lexicographic and a lexicographical goal programming approach
with ﬁxed speciﬁc targets. The results demonstrate that our proposal yields promising results.
Also, we have related our results to those obtained by a dominance-based approach based
on NSGA-II capable of obtaining an approximation to the Pareto Front. Finally, to further
assess the behaviour of the proposed strategy, a detailed sensitivity analysis demonstrates the
consistency of the results across different tolerance values deﬁning the vague goals.
Although the presented multiobjective strategy has been presented for the green fuzzy
ﬂexible job shop problem, as it is the problem that motivated its deﬁnition, the approach is
generic and it can be used in other scenarios where there is a hierarchy between objective
functions but the desired target values for the goals associated to each objective cannot be
established explicitly a priori. Clearly, determining reasonable tolerance levels when deﬁning
the problem depends on the decision maker’s expertise, in the same way as providing exact
target values for traditional goal programming. By adding the possibility of having vague
goals, we provide the decision maker with a wider range of possibilities to model their
123

Annals of Operations Research
preferences. The choice of explicit or vague targets will depend on whatever option is better
suited to their expertise and the problem under consideration.
The proposed adaptive strategy has been used within a memetic algorithm, but in the
future it can be embedded in different solving methods. This, together with the possibility of
deﬁning vague goals for different multiobjective problems, opens a promising line of future
research.
