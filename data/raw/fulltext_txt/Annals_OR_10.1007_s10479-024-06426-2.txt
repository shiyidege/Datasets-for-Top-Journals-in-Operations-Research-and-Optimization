Abstract
As the economic environment becomes increasingly complex, enhancing supply chain
resilience is crucial for the operations and long-term development of enterprises. Real-world
supply chains, encompassing components such as goods, warehouses, and plants, often con-
tain complex network structures, making resilience analysis a challenging task. This paper
addresses this challenge from a network analysis perspective. We project the complex supply
chain network into single-mode, multi-layer networks focusing on plants and warehouses.
Utilizing a multi-layer community detection method, we identify local clusters within these
networks. By uncovering closely connected clusters, we reveal the ﬂexibility and redundancy
in production capabilities among different plants and warehouses. An empirical study using
real-world data demonstrates that multi-layer network clustering effectively uncovers indi-
rect capacity linkages between plants and warehouses. The ﬁndings from this community
detection are beneﬁcial for strategic capacity management, aiding enterprises in managing
supply shortages or sudden demand spikes.

1 Introduction
In today’s increasingly complex socio-economic environment, enterprises’ supply chains
face mounting threats, including catastrophic earthquakes, political instability, fuel shortages,
disease outbreaks, and acts of terrorism (Sodhi et al., 2012; Chen et al., 2013; Ho et al., 2015;
Son et al., 2021). A supply chain is a network of facilities that deliver plants or services from
producers to consumers (Ellram et al., 2004). Typically, real-world supply chains manifest
as complex logistics systems involving plants, warehouses, and distribution channels. For
enterprises, effective supply chain management aims to handle the ﬂow of goods efﬁciently
and sustainably (Mentzer et al., 2001). As the economic environment grows more volatile,
the likelihood of external shocks impacting supply chains also increases. Unforeseen “black
swan” events, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, can have devastating effects on supply chains.
When nodes or channels within the supply chain are incapacitated, the efﬁciency of the entire
production and transportation process can be compromised, leading to signiﬁcant economic
losses for enterprises, including declines in operating income, return on sales, return on assets
and stock market valuation (Hendricks & Singhal, 2003). Given the potentially sever impact
of supply chain disruptions, enhancing supply chain resilience, deﬁned as the capacity to
persist, adapt, or transform in the face of change (Wieland & Durach, 2021), is imperative.
The signiﬁcance of supply chain resilience analysis has been increasingly recognized.
Mandal (2012) have associated resilience with the capability to rapidly restore supply chains
to normal operations or enhance operational efﬁciency following disruptive events. Tuka-
muhabwa et al. (2015) have emphasized that a company’s ability to respond more effectively
to disturbances compared to its competitors can strengthen its market position. Consequently,
fostering a resilient supply chain is advantageous for both short-term survival and long-term
competitiveness. Gao et al. (2016) have advocated resilience as a crucial solution for supply
chains to navigate turbulent scenarios. Scholars have also proposed extending organizational
risk management practices to the realm of supply chains (Fan & Stevenson, 2018; Norrman
& Wieland, 2020). From a risk management perspective, the aim of resilience analysis lies in
identifying, assessing, controlling, and monitoring various sources of risk that could disrupt
supply chains. However, a supply chain comprises a complex network of interconnected orga-
nizations, transcending the sum of its parts and exhibiting dynamic characteristics. Hence,
there are fundamental disparities between organizational risk management and the analysis
of supply chain resilience. Davoudi et al. (2013) have argued that resilience analysis should
concentrate on the systemic attributes of supply chains rather than individual risk sources.
To systematically evaluate the resilience of a supply chain, this paper explores the network
structure within the chain and studies its characteristics through network analysis.
From a network perspective, a crucial mechanism for enhancing the resilience of a sup-
ply chain is the augmentation of redundancy. By incorporating redundant nodes, such as
duplicate manufacturing plants or surplus inventory within the supply chain, enterprises can
effectively navigate supply shortages or sudden spikes in demand through strategic allo-
cation of surplus resources (Maharjan & Kato, 2022). Maintaining spare capacity along
critical pathways within the supply chain is essential for cultivating resilience (Pavlov et
al., 2019). The duplication of capacity enables enterprises to implement contingency mea-
sures to uphold operations during instances of managerial failure. Moreover, redundancy
is expected to increase the ﬂexibility of the supply chain, allowing enterprises to expedite
resource deployment or preempt delays (Jüttner et al., 2003; Johnson et al., 2013; Kristianto
et al., 2014). However, increasing redundancy in a supply chain remains a challenging prob-
lem. Enterprise managers face several critical questions, such as which parts of the supply

chain need duplication and where capacity expansion is required. To provide decision-making
support for these concerns, we propose integrating community detection methods, widely
utilized for uncovering network structures, to enhance the analysis of supply chain resilience.
Our objective is to detect communities within a supply chain (e.g., groups comprise closely
interconnected plants and warehouses) and partition the supply chain into several densely
connected communities. We then study strategies for augmenting redundancy by investigat-
ing the node distributions within each community and identifying crucial nodes that bridge
different communities.
In multi-layer networks of complex systems like supply chains, community detection
presents unique challenges compared to single-layer networks (Huang et al., 2021). The pri-
mary objective is to uncover a shared community structure across multiple layers, identifying
groups of well-connected nodes. Various community detection methods originally developed
for single-layer networks have been adapted to accommodate the complexities of multi-layer
networks. For instance, the modularity metric, which evaluates the quality of divisions in
a single-layer network, has been extended to multi-layer modularity density, resulting in
modularity-based community detection methods for multi-layer networks (Ma et al., 2018).
Tagarelli et al. (2017) have proposed an ensemble-based approach to aggregate community
structures detected independently in each layer to form a comprehensive partition of all
nodes. Additionally, spectral clustering methods have been extended to multi-layer versions
to ensure consistent community detection in multi-layer networks (Lei et al., 2019). Further
research on this topic is available in (Kim & Lee, 2015; Huang et al., 2021; Ma & Nandy,
2023).
In this paper, we propose a comprehensive framework for analyzing the resilience of a
supply chain through community detection in multi-layer networks. We begin by modeling
the supply chain, which comprises various types of nodes, as a multi-mode network. To
explore the inherent community structures, we analyze different types of nodes separately,
projecting the multi-mode network into various multi-layer conﬁgurations, such as a multi-
layer network of plants. By applying community detection techniques to these multi-layer
networks, we can identify and understand critical local structures. This enables more effective
planning and management strategies to mitigate potential risks within the supply chain. The
aggregated analysis of multi-layer networks reveals global structures that may not be apparent
when examining individual layers separately. Identifying common structures across different
layers allows for more accurate inference results. For local communities, we examine the
distributions and diversities of each type of node, assessing the redundancy and ﬂexibility
of different parts of the supply chain. The proposed framework is applicable for general
supply chains. In regard to multiple relations among different objects within a supply chain,
we use a multi-layer network structure to model them and thus apply the proposed commu-
nity detection method. This framework aids enterprises in comprehensively considering the
signiﬁcance of various types of nodes in supply chain design and management. Ultimately,
it enhances the resilience and risk mitigation capabilities of the supply chain.

5 Conclusion
Analysis of supply chain resilience is crucial for enterprise production operations and long-
term development. In the current complex and volatile economic environment, it is essential
to quantify supply chain resilience in terms of ﬂexibility and redundancy. This approach
provides valuable insights for maintaining supply chain operations. This study addresses this
issue by conducting community detection within the supply chain network. We project the
complex supply chain network into single-mode, multi-layer networks focusing on plants and
plants. Through multi-layer community detection methods, we identify local units within the
network. These closely connected clusters help analyze capacity linkages between different
plants and potential coordination among plants. The results reveal the ﬂexibility and redun-
dancy in product manufacturing. Our empirical study, based on a real dataset, demonstrates
that applying multi-layer network clustering effectively uncovers indirect capacity linkages
between plants and warehouses. The community detection results are valuable for capacity
management, strategic allocation, and providing decision-making support for enterprises to
handle supply shortages or sudden spikes in demand.
For future research, we plan to test this method on additional supply chain datasets. We
will also integrate multi-layer network clustering with capacity forecasting to provide more
comprehensive references for analysis of supply chain resilience.

allocation of resources, even distribution of node loads, and effective risk dispersion. Such
an equilibrium enhances resource utilization, increases efﬁciency in the supply chain, and
improves system resilience. Conversely, Cluster 2 exhibits signiﬁcant imbalances in its net-
work structure. Plant nodes experience uneven loads, leading to an unbalanced distribution
of goods and warehouse nodes. Most goods nodes are clustered on one side of the network,
while warehouse nodes are concentrated on the other, failing to distribute evenly across all
plant nodes. This imbalance can create inefﬁciencies in resource utilization and compromise
the overall stability and resilience of the supply chain.

layer increased from 0.483 to 1.000. This indicates that plants within a cluster likely share
similar good types and production processes, promoting collaboration in addressing common
challenges and opportunities. Additionally, plants within a cluster can form a mutual support
network, enabling rapid assistance from other plants during times of difﬁculty or increased
demand. This collective support bolsters the stability and resilience of the plants within the
cluster.

(2) Redundancy Analysis of plants
A high similarity in the structure of goods produced by different plants indicates signiﬁcant
interchangeability in production lines, equipment, and processes. This interchangeability
allows other plants to easily take over the production tasks of a disrupted plant, swiftly
ﬁlling production gaps and ensuring the continuity of the supply chain. If a plant or ware-
house becomes inoperable due to unforeseen circumstances, other highly similar plants or
warehouses can quickly assume their functions, thereby mitigating the risk of supply chain
disruptions. This risk mitigation mechanism enhances the stability and reliability of the sup-
ply chain.
The Jaccard coefﬁcient is used to measure the correlation level between plants, indirectly
assessing each plant’s substitutability and determining the redundancy between the two clus-

production. Any production or inventory issues in a plant within Cluster 1 can be quickly
addressed by other plants. In contrast, although Cluster 2 maintains a high level of consis-
