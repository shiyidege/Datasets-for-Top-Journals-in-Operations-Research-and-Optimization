Abstract
In this study, we investigate the replenishment decisions for Ô¨Ārms that rely on multiple sources
of supplies, including regular forward contracts, option contracts, and the spot market. Our
research aims to tackle the challenges arising due to supplier disruptions and the volatility of
spot market prices that are correlated with these disruptions. We develop and solve multi-stage
stochastic programming models that incorporate demand and supplier disruption information
updates, while considering both risk-neutral and risk averse (CVaR) objectives. These mod-
els assist organizations with varying risk attitudes in achieving maximum performance by
optimally selecting a procurement portfolio based on the availability and quality of updated
information. Through analytical solutions and extensive numerical studies, our Ô¨Āndings offer
novel insights to organizations and policymakers, empowering them to enhance their sup-
ply chain resilience during critical supply disruption situations. Therefore, this research has
broader implications and aligns with the multiple sustainability objectives outlined in the
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

natural disasters, machine breakdowns, exchange rate volatility, terrorism, pandemics, and
more. Complex supply networks, coupled with industrial automation and decentralized man-
ufacturing increase the vulnerability of supply chains to disruptions (Rujeerapaiboon et al.,

In recent decades, the Ô¨Āelds of risk management have garnered noteworthy interest from
researchers and practitioners, supporting global efforts towards building a more resilient
supply chain ecosystem (Wu, 2016). These efforts led to supply chain risk assessment and
mitigation strategies to help companies streamline their operations (Choudhary et al., 2023).
One such strategy is diversiÔ¨Ācation Ô¨āexibility, which suggests having multiple sources of
supply to mitigate the risk of disruptions. In this study, we focus on two procurement modes:
the forward contract and the option contract. The adoption of option contracts has been
observed in various industries, including apparel, electronics, and fresh produce (Barnes-
Schuster et al., 2002; Eppen & Iyer, 1997; Nagali et al., 2008). The forward contract is less
expensive but has an extended procurement lead time and is subject to disruptions. The option
contract is more expensive but offers greater reliability with a much shorter lead time. In our
study, option contracts provide retailers with the opportunity to share the risks associated
with supply disruptions, demand Ô¨āuctuations, and price volatility with the suppliers.
In combination with forward and option contracts, Ô¨Ārms in various industries also seek
additional supplies from other resources. Spot markets have been actively employed as an
instrument to hedge supply‚Äďdemand inequity risks in the supply chains. While spot markets
offer products with negligible lead time, they come with higher and volatile prices to com-
pensate the extra Ô¨āexibility they offer. The joint usage of spot markets and capacity options
promotes the efÔ¨Ācient risk management for commodities such as metal and agricultural prod-
ucts. In addition to commodities, the use of option contracts together with the spot market is
also observed in various industries such as electronics and energy (Hou & Zhao, 2012; Kim,
2005). For example, Hewlett Packard (HP) invests in 50% forward contracts, 35% option
contracts, and leaves 15% to spot market purchases (Billington et al., 2002). Moreover, fast-
track suppliers, who may secure production for its customers in extremely short lead times,
may also serve as a spot market for various industries. Zhao et al. (2018) presents the case of
a trading company, SADL, from China offering design, merchandising, manufacturing, and
delivery service to apparel retail brands in Europe. SADL‚Äôs competitive strategy is to provide
production a very short lead time (e.g., two to three weeks) and a very small quantity (e.g.,
2‚Äď3 pieces). In return, this company receives a relatively high proÔ¨Āt margin and quick credit
payment.
In this study, we allow the supplier‚Äôs disruption status to inÔ¨āuence the spot market price
distribution. In practice, when the supplier is the primary provider of a speciÔ¨Āc part‚Äôs global
supply; disruptions at that supplier can substantially impact the spot market price and intro-
duce volatility in the market. Even if the supplier is not a key player, the supplier‚Äôs disruption
may be the indicator of a larger scale supply chain catastrophe signaling a joint supply and
demand shock. For example, recently the Covid-19 pandemic has contributed enormously to
market price instabilities in several industries. Semiconductor shortages led to price increases
in the bill of materials, which were then passed on to consumer products such as TVs or PCs
(Kaur, 2021; Knight, 2023; Mohammad et al., 2022). According to the Food Security Portal

Practitioners are often hesitant to fully adopt risk mitigation strategies such as excessive
capacity due to the need for expensive investments. The solution to avoid the inefÔ¨Āciency is
to invest in Ô¨āexibility, rather than redundancy in the supply chains. Organizations can exploit
the beneÔ¨Āts of the embedded Ô¨āexibility in the system by closely monitoring supply or demand
patterns and employing data in smart ways. Effective utilization of advance demand informa-
tion enhances the efÔ¨Āciency and resistance of production/inventory systems. Similarly, Ô¨Ārms
can employ various strategies to obtain more information about the supplier‚Äôs disruption
status before the scheduled shipment date. For example, the buyer may continuously track
shipments from the supplier to monitor if the earlier shipments are received on time and if
they are complete. This information enables the buyer to update the Ô¨Ārm‚Äôs assessment of the
disruption probability and make informed decisions. In our study, option contracts serve as a
reactive mechanism in response to updated information. Despite being more expensive, the
retailer beneÔ¨Āts from the ordering Ô¨āexibility provided by the reserved capacity, which can be
exercised in response to updated information about the supplier‚Äôs disruption status, demand
conditions, and spot market prices.
The supply chain procurement literature traditionally considers risk-neutral operating
conditions and use expected proÔ¨Āt measure to assess the overall value of the procurement
portfolio. On the other hand, maximizing the expected proÔ¨Āt in the long run may not always
be desirable from the practical perspective especially when demand and spot market price are
highly unpredictable and supply disruption risks are present. In such situations, minimizing a
downside risk measure could be more appropriate. To address this, our analysis incorporates
Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), a popular downside risk measure. By integrating CVaR
into our models, we can capture the differences in procurement strategies between risk averse
and risk-neutral decision makers, considering varying levels of information updates.

Covid-19 pandemic highlighted the vulnerability of the supply chains due to extended supply

2 Literature review
The literature review for this study can be classiÔ¨Āed into four streams. The Ô¨Ārst stream
provides an overview of the literature on supply chain disruptions and explores strategies to
enhance resilience. The second stream focuses on studies that consider multiple procurement
channels, such as multiple or unreliable suppliers, option contracts, or spot markets. The third
stream of literature examines the value of demand and supply information in replenishment
decisions. Finally, the fourth stream overviews the literature on sustainability efforts in supply
chain management in connection with the SDGs.
Several studies have investigated the impacts of disruptions and risk mitigation strate-
gies for supply chains. Hendricks and Singhal (2005) empirically study the effect of supply
chain disruptions on long-run stock price performance and equity risk of the Ô¨Ārm. Drawing
on the literature and the case studies from the industry, Sodhi and Tang (2012) summarize
modelling-based approaches to mitigate supply chain risk. Snyder et al. (2016) provide an
extensive review of OR/MS models for supply chain disruptions. More recently, Katsaliaki
et al. (2022) conduct a review on supply chain disruptions and risk mitigating strategies.
According to Lee (2004), supply chains that prioritize efÔ¨Āciency often deteriorate over time,
while companies that focus on agility, adaptability, and alignment gain a competitive edge.
Lee (2004) emphasizes the importance of Ô¨āexibility in building resilient and responsive sup-
ply chains. However, determining the optimal level of Ô¨āexibility to mitigate risks remains
challenging due to insufÔ¨Ācient data and elaborate cost‚ÄďbeneÔ¨Āt analysis. Tang and Tomlin
(2008) address this problem by presenting a uniÔ¨Āed framework and stylized models that
demonstrate the strategic value of implementing a risk reduction program with a relatively
low level of Ô¨āexibility. Simchi-Levi et al. (2018) investigate a hybrid strategy that combines
process Ô¨āexibility and Ô¨Ānished goods inventory to mitigate supply chain risks. They propose
a two-stage robust optimization model that considers inventory allocation before disruptions
and production quantities determination after disruptions. Mehmanchi et al. (2021) study
the worst-case performance of Ô¨āexibility designs under demand and supply uncertainties,
including disruptions. They introduce the Plant Cover Index under Disruptions (PCID) as a
measure for assessing required plant capacity after disruptions. More recently, Rujeerapai-
boon et al. (2023) investigate the resilience of long chains when there is a positive probability
of plant disruptions, proposing a simple greedy algorithm to describe the performance of the
chain under different disruption and demand scenarios.
The second stream of literature focuses on studies that investigate the use of multiple pro-
curement channels, including multiple/unreliable suppliers, option contracts, or spot markets.
Seifert et al. (2004) examine a single-period replenishment model and determine the opti-
mal order quantity for purchasing through option contracts and spot markets. Kleindorfer
and Wu (2005) study multiple unreliable suppliers through option contracts with a random
spot market price, assuming price and quantity dependent demand functions. Dada et al.
(2007) consider a Ô¨Ārm with multiple reliable and unreliable suppliers, proposing the "cost
Ô¨Ārst reliability second" strategy for risk-neutral supplier selection. We show that this insight

Several studies have focused on inventory management with demand information updates
or forecast revisions. We refer the readers to the detailed review and comments in the book
by Sethi et al. (2005) and the references therein. This approach is developed and used by
Gurnani and Tang (1999), Gallego and Ozer (2001), Sethi et al., (2001, 2003, 2004, 2005),
Karaesmen et al. (2004) and others. Eppen and Iyer (1997) consider a backup agreement

revised by the demand observed in the Ô¨Ārst stage and demonstrated that backup agreements
have a substantial impact on the expected proÔ¨Āt and the committed quantity. Barnes-Schuster
et al. (2002) develop analogous models, and their results are in line with Eppen and Iyer
(1997) in terms of the value of the demand information when using option contracts. Other
research on the option-related contracts that allow order adjustments after updating demand
information includes Brown and Lee (2003), Bassok and Anupindi (2008), Moinzadeh and
Nahmias (2000) and Huang et al. (2005). Guo et al. (2016) study a Ô¨Ārm that procures a product
from a regular supplier with supply disruption and yield risks and a backup supplier with
capacity reservation option. They Ô¨Ānd out that the value of the reservation option depends
on demand uncertainty and whether the reservation capacity is exercised before or after
demand realization. Zhao et al. (2018) study spot markets in a two-stage model to explore
the signiÔ¨Ācance of supply option contracts with both demand information updating and a
stochastic spot market. Our models differ from theirs in several aspects such as existence
of an unreliable supplier, risk averse decision maker and supplier disruption information
updates. We also explicitly model the correlation between the spot market price and the
disruption status of the supplier explicitly. Furthermore, most of the supply contract literature
with information updates considers risk neutral models except Buzacott et al. (2011) who
develop a newsvendor model for investigating risks of commitment‚Äďoption contracts with

determine the order quantity (qc). The primary supplier is relatively less expensive (with the

3.2 Risk averse approach
The decision models presented in Sect. 3.1 describe how the expected proÔ¨Āt function of the
Ô¨Ārm is maximized when procurement decisions are under consideration. On the other hand,
it is often undesirable to consider only the maximization of the expected proÔ¨Āt when the
decision maker also targets to avoid large Ô¨Ānancial losses. Our problem involves multiple,
uncertain elements (demand, supply disruptions and spot market price), the variance of the
resulting proÔ¨Āt may turn out to be quite large. We incorporate risk in our problem employing
the Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) as a risk measure. For a given signiÔ¨Ācance value,őĪ,
the Value-at-Risk, VaR, is equal to the largest value making sure that the probability of
obtaining a proÔ¨Āt less than VaR is lower than (1 ‚ąíőĪ). This means, the VaR is the őĪ-quantile
of the proÔ¨Āt distribution. CVaR is derived by taking a weighted average between the VaR
and losses exceeding the VaR. Although there are other risk approaches that can be applied
to supply chain optimization problems, such as VaR and mean‚Äďvariance, we chose CVaR
as it is proven to be coherent and consistent with the second order stochastic dominance
(see Rockafellar & Uryasev, 2000, 2002; Uryasev, 2000; Artzner et al., 1999; PÔ¨āug, 2000).
When the risk is accounted for multistage stochastic programming such as the model under
consideration; there is no natural or obvious way of modelling and measuring risk. The
difÔ¨Āculty in extending risk measures to the multistage setting has been discussed by several
studies (please see Homem-de-Mello & Pagnoncelli, 2016 and the references therein).

6 Summary of key results
This section summarizes key insights and managerial implications derived from the analytical
results and computational tests.
In the risk-neutral setting, we develop optimality conditions for various special cases
based on the information quality when the Ô¨Ārm utilizes the reserved capacity at the backup.
In addition to characterizing the exact ordering quantity at the unreliable supplier and the
reservation capacity at the backup supplier, we provide threshold values for the disruption
risk describing Ô¨Ārm‚Äôs preference towards to single or multi-sourcing strategies. We Ô¨Ānd
out that when there is no correlation, a risk neutral buyer never orders exclusively from
the backup supplier as the backup supplier is not cost efÔ¨Ācient. This result is known as
‚Äúcost Ô¨Ārst, reliability second‚ÄĚ in the unreliable supplier selection literature. We demonstrate
that increased correlation between spot market price and the disruption event enhances the
attractiveness of the backup supplier. In such cases, the Ô¨āexible but not efÔ¨Ācient backup
supplier becomes preferable and can be chosen as a single source. We believe that this is a
signiÔ¨Ācant result, contributing to the risk neutral procurement literature, as it highlights the
crucial role of the correlation between supply disruptions and the spot market price. Similarly,
we show that it is optimal to reserve more capacity at the backup supplier as the quality of
the disruption signal improves. We also validate our analytical Ô¨Āndings comparing them with
the numerical outputs obtained through our scenario-based modelling approach for the risk
neutral decision maker.
For risk averse Ô¨Ārms, increasing quality of supply disruption information and demand
information make the backup supplier more valuable, as the reserved capacity can be more
efÔ¨Āciently utilized in such cases. As the quality of disruption information improves, the buyer
tends to order higher quantities from both the primary supplier and the backup supplier. On
the other hand, as the quality of demand information improves, the buyer tends to decrease
the orders from the primary supplier and shift their orders to the backup supplier.
For extremely risk averse decision makers, the Ô¨Ārm exhibits a strong tendency to com-
pletely disregard the unreliable supplier as a sustainable supply source, irrespective of the
quality of disruption information. Hence, the anticipated impact of demand and disruption
information is reduced for risk averse decision makers compared to their risk-neutral counter-
parts. This leads to a reduced expected monetary value of demand and disruption information
for decision makers who are risk averse.

Our research supports the broader agenda of achieving the SDGs by 2030. In this section,
we summarize our contributions in this respect.
Our study provides practical decision models that can assist organizations in navigating
uncertain environments and making well-informed decisions. By considering elements such
as supply disruptions, volatile spot market prices, and option contracts, our models can
help organizations optimize their procurement processes, mitigate risks, and enhance overall
operational efÔ¨Āciency. Therefore, our research aligns well with SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation,
and Infrastructure).
By highlighting the role of Ô¨āexible options and correlation between supply disruptions
and spot market prices, we minimize possible overproduction (waste) and under production
(shortages). This would assist responsible procurement practices from both environmental

8 Conclusion
This study aims to improve the state of knowledge on the impact of forecast updating in a
supply chain, as Ô¨Ārms generally face a trade-off between the cost and the beneÔ¨Āt of gathering
market and supply information. To the best of our knowledge, in the existing supply chain
literature, the focus is generally on value of demand information for risk neutral decision
makers. In our study, we simultaneously focus on the value of supply disruption information
and demand information. Moreover, we incorporated risk aversion into our analysis. Further-
more, we study settings where the distribution of the random spot market price depends on
the unreliable supplier‚Äôs disruption status. Although the spot market price is often affected by
supply disruptions in practice, this is not a common assumption in most of the cited literature.
Despite the inherent complexity of the decision models, this study provides analytical
solutions for several special cases considering the risk neutral decision maker. Optimality
properties lead to notable insights on the ordering strategies for Ô¨Ārms. For example, we show
that the traditional ‚Äúcost Ô¨Ārst, reliability second‚ÄĚ result in the unreliable supplier selection
literature does not hold when there is correlation between the spot market price and the
supplier disruption. We believe that this is a signiÔ¨Ācant result, contributing to the risk neutral
procurement literature, as it highlights the crucial role of the correlation between supply
disruptions and the spot market price.
Moreover, we carry out an extensive numerical study to assess the value of information
and the effect of risk aversion on procurement decisions. For example, we establish that
the expected value of information is higher for the risk neutral decision maker compared
to the risk averse decision maker. This is a fundamental result for decision makers who are
evaluating options to collect more information before using the Ô¨āexible resource. Finally, the
consistency between the analytical results and the numerical tests strengthens the robustness
and reliability of the study‚Äôs conclusions.
By exploring procurement strategies that consider risk attitudes and information quality,
this study promotes sustainable supply chain management practices. Therefore, in addition
to its contributions to supply chain decision-making literature, this study aligns well with the
objectives of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).
In this study, we examine the value of information for the risk averse decision maker
with the CVaR objective. A future study may also consider alternative approaches for the
risk averse models. Utilizing a stochastic programming approach with uncertain disruption
scenarios raises a practical concern that recourse decisions may deviate signiÔ¨Ācantly from
the corresponding pre-determined Ô¨Ārst stage decisions, increasing system nervousness. In
this respect, future studies could focus on developing models with restricted recourse, for
example employing CVaR norm (see Pavlikov & Uryasev, 2014; Koca et al., 2021). We
