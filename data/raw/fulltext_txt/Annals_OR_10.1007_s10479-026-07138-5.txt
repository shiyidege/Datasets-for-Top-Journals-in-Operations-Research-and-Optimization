Abstract
Contextual or environmental variables usually play a major role in explaining relative efﬁ-
ciency differences in most empirical applications. Nevertheless, most studies focused on the
analysis of productivity changes over a period usually do not account for the inﬂuence of these
variables, especially those adopting a nonparametric approach. In this paper, we propose a
novel method to incorporate information about contextual variables in Malmquist productiv-
ity decompositions based on conditional nonparametric efﬁciency measures. The usefulness
of the proposed approach is illustrated on an empirical application using data about European
Union (EU) regions to investigate the extent to which the quality of governance inﬂuenced
the evolution of regional productivity in the years following the last ﬁnancial crisis. While
the index captures productivity change conditional on external factors, such as the quality
of governance in our empirical application, it does not allow for formal inference about the
marginal effect of these variables. Extending the methodology to address this limitation is
beyond the scope of the current paper but represents a promising direction for future research.
Keywords Data envelopment analysis · Efﬁciency · Productivity change · Contextual
variables · Malmquist indices
1 Introduction
When evaluating the performance of a set of production units or decision-making units
(DMUs), it is necessary to account for the fact that they usually operate under different
environmental conditions to make a fair assessment (Badin et al., 2014). Such operating
conditions refer to different concepts, such as external, environmental or contextual variables,
commonly known as variables Z in the efﬁciency literature. They represent factors that
are beyond the control of producers but may inﬂuence the production process. Therefore,

they play a key role in explaining relative efﬁciency differences across units (e.g., location
characteristics, weather conditions, type of ownership or quality indicators).
In view to their signiﬁcance, there are a host of proposals or alternative approaches in the
literature that account for the effect of such variables Z when estimating efﬁciency measures
of performance with nonparametric methods (Hughenin, 2015). Most of these methods aim
to model the effect of environmental factors on the production process and apply one-stage or
multi-stage approaches to capture the heterogeneity caused by these factors (Cordero et al.,
2008). Unfortunately, these approaches are often ﬂawed by some restrictive assumptions such
as the separability condition between the input–output space and the space of environmental
variables (Simar & Wilson, 2007, 2011). But there is another appealing option in the litera-
ture, namely, the conditional model developed by Daraio and Simar (2005, 2007a, 2007b).
This approach is based on the probabilistic formulation of the production process proposed
by Cazals et al. (2002). Within this approach, conditional efﬁciency measures are deﬁned
and estimated nonparametrically and DMUs are compared with others facing similar envi-
ronmental conditions. One of its main advantages is that it does not rely on the separability
condition, thus external variables can inﬂuence the shape of the production set and, there-
fore, the frontier. Moreover, if longitudinal data is available, this approach can be adapted to
a dynamic framework by including the time dimension as an additional conditional variable
(Mastromarco & Simar, 2015).
However, when researchers are interested in exploring what has occurred over a speciﬁc
period, the most frequent nonparametric approach applied for inter-temporal comparisons
is the Malmquist productivity index (MPI) (Caves et al., 1982). This tool can be deﬁned
as the geometric mean of two ratios of distance functions and compares the productivity of
different units over time. The MPI can be used to measure the change in productivity and
can be decomposed into a change in technical efﬁciency (EC) and a measure of change in
technology (TC). Therefore, this approach is widely used in the literature to assess the causes
of productivity changes in multiple frameworks such as banking (Asmild et al., 2014; Kevork
et al., 2017), healthcare (de Castro Lobo et al., 2010; Giuffrida, 1999), education (Essid et al.,
2014; Ouellette & Vierstraete, 2010), agricultural sector (Coelli & Rao, 2005; Rezitis, 2010)
or tax ofﬁces (Forsund, 2015; Fuentes & Lillo-Bañuls, 2015).
One of the main limitations of empirical studies using MPI to evaluate changes in produc-
tivity over time is that they do not usually account for the inﬂuence of variables Z. Therefore,
it is implicitly assumed that all the evaluated units operate in the same environment and also
that this environment does not change over time. This is highly unlikely in most cases. To
the best of our knowledge, the only exception to this is the alternative decomposition of the
traditional MPI proposed by Johnson and Ruggiero (2014). This alternative decomposition
includes an additional component (environmental harshness) reﬂecting the inﬂuence of con-
textual or environmental variables. Following this decomposition, it is possible to identify
and explain which part of EC and TC is inﬂuenced by the environment. Nevertheless, the
calculation of the MPI remains the same, and, therefore, it does not account for possible
changes in the environment of the units that may have an impact on the production process
are not considered in this calculation.
In this paper, we propose an alternative approach enabling the calculation of a new pro-
ductivity change index corrected for the inﬂuence of variables Z. We have named this new
approach the conditional Malmquist productivity index, since it relies on the calculation
of conditional nonparametric measures of performance. This approach has the advantage
of including environmental factors in the speciﬁcation of the data-generating process. It is,
therefore, possible to account for (and also measure) the effect of variables Z when calculating
productivity growth (or decline) over a period.

In order to illustrate the usefulness of this novel approach, we analyze the changes in
the regional-level productivity of EU-28 European regions in the years following the onset
of the ﬁnancial crisis. There is extensive recent evidence on the important role played by
institutions as determinants of regional productivity trajectories in Europe (Agostino et al.,
2020; Lasagni et al., 2015; Rodríguez-Pose & Ganau, 2022) and also for explaining economic
development (Annoni & Rubianes, 2016; Ketterer & Rodríguez-Pose, 2018; Peiró-Palomino,
2019; Rodríguez-Pose, 2013; Rodríguez-Pose & Garcilazo, 2015). Hence, we explore the
potential inﬂuence of the “quality of government” as a potential factor inﬂuencing regional
performance.
So far, most of the studies analyzing the determinants of regional productivity growth
have applied stochastic frontier analysis (SFA) (Tsionas & Michaelides, 2016; Otsuka, 2017;
Ramajo & Hewings, 2018; De Graff, 2020). However, SFA suffers from misspeciﬁcation
problems when the data-generating process is unknown, as is the usual case in applied stud-
ies. Likewise, there are studies applying nonparametric methods, such as data envelopment
analysis or Malmquist indices followed by a regression analysis (Schaffer et al., 2011; Zhang
et al., 2016). Unfortunately, these approaches are often ﬂawed by some restrictive and prior
assumptions (see Simar & Wilson, 2007, 2011 for details). To the best of our knowledge,
Ramajo et al. (2017) is the only previous study that has used a fully nonparametric approach
to account for the presence of heterogeneous conditions affecting regional-level efﬁcien-
cies, which were, in that particular case, represented by geographical externalities. The main
limitation of the above study is that it does not consider the possibility of production technol-
ogy having changed over the evaluated period. One of the main advantages of the approach
proposed in this paper is that it does allow us to detect whether there has been a change
in production technology by offering a broader decomposition of the different factors that
explain the changes in regional productivity levels.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the methodology.
Speciﬁcally, Sect. 2.1 deﬁnes efﬁciency measures based on distance functions and presents
the standard productivity decomposition of Malmquist indices. Section 2.2 describes different
approaches to deal with environmental variables in the estimation of efﬁciency measures.
Section 2.3 presents a new decomposition of Malmquist indices accounting for environmental
variables. In Sect. 3, we illustrate our decomposition with an application to EU regions.
Section 4 discusses our main ﬁndings, and Sect. 5 concludes the paper and provides future
research directions.

2.2 Dealing with contextual or environmental variables in efficiency measurement
When a production process is inﬂuenced by contextual or environmental factors, it is nec-
essary to account for them in the estimation of efﬁciency measures of performance. Several
approaches can be used for this purpose. They are divided primarily into the traditional, one-
stage and two-stage approaches and the more recent conditional nonparametric approach.
This section provides a brief review of these methods,1 with special emphasis on the advan-
tages of the conditional nonparametric approach to justify its use in this paper.
The one-stage approach developed by Banker and Morey (1986) is the simplest alterna-
tive for calculating efﬁciency measures incorporating the effect of external variables. This
1 See Badin et al. (2014) or Cordero et al. (2016) for a detailed review of such methods.

approach includes external factors in the model as an additional restriction in the formula-
tion of the standard DEA program, and thus they are involved in deﬁning the attainable set.
Although its use is very widespread, which is largely explained by the fact that it is included
in most DEA software packages (Barr, 2004), this approach presents some important draw-
backs that lead us to question its use. First, it requires some restrictive assumptions that
analysts cannot always foresee (Fried et al., 2002), like free disposability and convexity of
the attainable set, as well as prior speciﬁcation of the favorable or unfavorable effect of the
external factors on the production process. Second, the linear programs involved in deﬁn-
ing the corresponding efﬁciency scores depend on the assumption of returns to scale made
on external variables (Badin et al., 2014). Third, the discrimination power of this approach
decreases as the number of external variables included in the analysis increases, regardless
of the effect of those variables. Finally, efﬁciency scores estimated with this model might be
systematically biased. This occurs when DMUs in better operating environments are included
in the reference sets of DMUs operating in harsher environments (Ruggiero, 1996).
In another alternative, known as the two-stage approach, there is an initial stage in which
efﬁciency scores are estimated using data about inputs and outputs only. These scores are
then regressed on environmental variables using either Tobit regression or ordinary least
squares in the second stage (Hoff, 2007; Banker & Natarajan, 2008). This technique has also
been extensively applied by practitioners to account for external effects in the estimation
of efﬁciency measures. However, two inﬂuential papers by Simar and Wilson (2007, 2011)
demonstrated that efﬁciency estimates of the initial stage are serially correlated, and, conse-
quently, the error term in the second stage is correlated with the regressors, making standard
approaches to inference invalid. To address this problem, Simar and Wilson (2007) advocate
the use of the truncated regression model that explicitly considers the bounded domain of
the DEA efﬁciency estimates. They developed two different algorithms, based on the use
of bootstrap methods, to obtain consistent and unbiased estimates for the parameters of the
regression. The main utility of this approach is that it allows for valid inference in the second-
stage regression. However, it might not be a good option if we are interested in correcting
the initial efﬁciency scores to ﬁlter out the inﬂuence of the external variables. The fact is
that this approach is only meaningful if the separability condition between the input–output
space and the space of the environmental factors holds. This implies assuming that variables
Z have no inﬂuence on the shape of the attainable set and thus can only affect the probability
of being more or less efﬁcient. This is something that might be difﬁcult to assume in some
situations.2
Nowadays, the nonparametric conditional approach proposed by Daraio and Simar (2005,
2007a, 2007b) represents one of the most attractive options available in the literature to deal
with environmental variables. These authors extended the probabilistic formulation of the
production process introduced by Cazals et al. (2002), where the attainable set is interpreted
as the support of some unknown probability distribution deﬁned on the input–output space.

2.3 Measuring productivity change accounting for contextual variables
At this point, we should note that all the above explanations refer to how to deal with
environmental variables in a single period, i.e., when only cross-sectional data is available.
However, when longitudinal data are available, which is the focus of our study, researchers
may be interested in analyzing how the inﬂuence of Z variables may change over the period.
In this scenario, when the interest is focused on explaining the inﬂuence of Z variables on
inefﬁciency (implicitly assuming the existence of separability), the use of the panel boot-
strapping approach suggested by Du et al. (2018) is a very attractive option. This is basically
an extension of the two-stage approach proposed by Simar and Wilson (2007) to account
for possible technological change over time by estimating frontiers separately over different
years and adapting double bootstrapping accordingly. However, like the original two-stage
model, this method does not allow examining the possible inﬂuence of context variables on
the shape of the frontier(s).
To explore the possible inﬂuence of Z variables on the shape of the frontier in a dynamic

The approach introduced in Johnson and Ruggiero (2014) is inspiring, although, at the
same time, it does have some limitations. First, from a methodological point of view, their
approach is not deﬁned as a Malmquist productivity index affected by environmental con-
ditions. Without such a magnitude determined for each assessed unit in the data sample, it
is not possible to compare productivity change among DMUs, since it does not account for
the environment. Therefore, any comparison is unfair. For example, the same productivity
improvement detected for two different units cannot be interpreted as being equal for both
DMUs if one of them is operating in clearly negative environmental conditions. Under such
harmful conditions, the productivity growth observed for one of the units could be understood
as an outstanding achievement. In this regard, the performance of the two units could not be
put on the same level. Second, from a credibility perspective, the calculations proposed by
Johnson and Ruggiero (2014) rely on the estimation of efﬁciency scores using the one-stage
approach suggested by Banker and Morey (1986). Speciﬁcally, they identify the effect of the
variable Z using the ratio between the basic efﬁciency index (without Z) and the index calcu-
lated by including the variable Z based on the Banker and Morey (1986) model. As mentioned
above, the Banker and Morey model has some important drawbacks that lead us to question
the validity of the values obtained using the decomposition shown in Eq. (13). Finally, it is
also worth mentioning that Johnson and Ruggiero introduced their decomposition under the
CRS assumption (Färe et al., 1994), thus it is not possible to identify scale effects in their
approach. However, this limitation was subsequently addressed in two subsequent empirical
studies which proposed the extension of this method to a more general context characterized
by variable returns to scale (Blackburn et al., 2014; Brennan et al., 2014).

Malmquist index that can account for some units possibly having experienced a change (for
better or worse) in their operating environment over the period. We call this new index the con-
ditional Malmquist productivity index (CM). CM represents a new adapted/corrected index
that accounts for the inﬂuence of environmental variables. The derivation of the CM index
presented in this subsection follows directly by analogy with the classical Malmquist index
initially introduced by Caves et al. (1982) and subsequently decomposed by Ray and Desli

Additionally, it is worth mentioning that the conditional Malmquist productivity index
(CM) follows the same structure as the classical Malmquist index but incorporates informa-
tion about environmental variables through conditional distance functions. By construction,
these conditional measures are greater than or equal to their unconditional counterparts
(Daraio & Simar, 2005, 2007a, 2007b), so the difference between CM and M cannot be
interpreted mechanically as the impact of the environmental variable Z. To properly assess
whether this inﬂuence is positive or negative, a more rigorous approach is needed, such as
the two-stage procedure proposed by Badin et al. (2012). This framework evaluates the ratios
between conditional and unconditional measures using nonparametric inference techniques,
allowing the marginal effect of contextual factors to be identiﬁed. In this regard, our contribu-
tion should be viewed as a ﬁrst step in this procedure, i.e. the formulation and decomposition
of a conditional Malmquist index that incorporates environmental heterogeneity into produc-
tivity analysis. Extending the procedure proposed by Badin et al. (2012) to dynamic settings
is a promising avenue for future research, though it is beyond the scope of the present study.
Finally, we want to highlight that the interpretation of the conditional Malmquist index
and its decomposition relies on the assumption that the joint distributions of inputs, outputs,
conditioned on the contextual variable Z are independent across time periods. Speciﬁcally, for
each period t and t + 1, we estimate the production technologies independently, following the
probabilisticframeworkproposedbyCazalsetal.(2002)andDaraioandSimar(2005,2007a).
This implies that the joint distributions are treated as distinct, without imposing structural
assumptions regarding their temporal relationship. While this assumption is reasonable in
many practical applications – particularly when environmental factors are relatively stable
over time – we recognize that it may be a limitation if strong intertemporal dependencies
exist. Future research could explore methodologies that relax this assumption, potentially by
adopting dynamic conditional modelling approaches that incorporate temporal correlations
in the estimation process.

Although there is a consensus in the literature that QoG tends to be stable over time

year (EQI 2010) and the most recent wave available (EQI 2017). We believe that the proposed
method is well suited in this setting since it can account for these variations in the environment
when evaluating the changes in productivity between the two periods.
This is the information that we use in our empirical analysis to account for environmental
changes that may have affected the performance of the regions during the evaluation period.
Given that data on this EQI variable are only available for the years 2010 and 2017, we
assume that it is possible to consider that the quality reﬂected by the index in 2010 may
properly reﬂect the perceptions of citizens about the objective quality of government in the
initial year (2007) and that the EQI 2017 index represents the QoG perceived by citizens in
the ﬁnal year (2014). From our point of view, this argument makes sense since it is reasonable
to expect that citizens’ opinions on the quality of government should not be based solely on
the performance of the government in a single year, but on the trend followed in previous
years.

4 Results
In this section, we present and discuss the empirical results of applying the proposed method
to our dataset to estimate the productivity changes within European regions from 2007 to 2014
accounting for the quality of their governments (Z). We made all the estimations assuming
an output orientation and using the conditional DEA estimator deﬁned in Eq. (10). Since we
presume that regional performance might be affected by this external factor, the ﬁrst step of
our analysis is to check whether the separability condition is satisﬁed.11 For this purpose, we
apply the nonparametric test suggested by Daraio et al. (2018) and Simar and Wilson (2020)
for the two analyzed years (Table 3), and we formally reject the assumption of separability.12
Therefore, the use of the conditional Malmquist index (CM) makes sense in the context of
our study.
For the purposes of comparison, Table 4 provides a summary of the results for the whole
sample of regions, including the descriptive statistics (arithmetic mean, standard deviation,
median, minimum and maximum values) for the traditional Malmquist index (M) and the
proposed conditional Malmquist index (CM). Likewise, there is information about all the
components deﬁned in Eq. (16). This includes the three main components (conditional efﬁ-
ciency change –CEC–, conditional technical change –CTC– and conditional scale efﬁciency
change –CSEC–), and also the environmental change components (change in environmen-
tal harshness – CEH–, environmental technical change – ETC – and environmental scale
efﬁciency change – ESEC–).
First, we ﬁnd that there was a decrease in regional productivity levels throughout the
period. This is consistent with the productivity slowdown identiﬁed in earlier studies for a
similar period (e.g., Cette et al., 2016; Decker et al., 2017). Both the traditional Malmquist
index and the conditional Malmquist index that we propose detect this decline. However, it is
more pronounced when we incorporate the quality of regional government in the deﬁnition of
the index (CM < M). If we look at the different explanatory components of this productivity
change, the decrease can be attributed mainly to efﬁciency and technical changes, since scale
efﬁciency remained practically constant throughout the period. Likewise, we also ﬁnd that
there was a notable improvement in environmental harshness over the evaluated years.
A key ﬁnding that emerges from our application for the EU regions is that the level of
correlation between the values of both indexes (M and CM) is relatively low, as shown
in Table 5, which displays three different correlation coefﬁcients (Pearson’s r, Spearman’s
ρ and Kendall’s τ). To further explore this issue, Fig. 1 shows the density plots for both
indices. Likewise, Table 6 reports the results of applying the nonparametric test proposed by
Li et al. (2009)13 to explore whether the distribution of divergences between distributions
is signiﬁcant. According to the value of this test, we can reject the null hypothesis that the
distributions of the different indices can be considered as equal. This provides evidence that
the observed divergences are signiﬁcant. This suggests that, at least in the context of our study,
the consideration of environmental harshness (E) in the estimation of measures representing
productivity change might affect the result of the conducted empirical analysis. Actually, we
found that environmental change (CEH) is the component that explains a higher proportion
(6.4%) of the differences detected between the two indices.

Having found that there are notable differences between the proposed conditional and the
traditional Malmquist indices, we now explore the values of the different components of the
CM index with the aim of identifying the main drivers of productivity changes. Given the
large sample size, we decided to group the regions by country to conduct this analysis. We
are aware that this decision implies a certain loss of information on speciﬁc units, but we
believe that the main features that we intend to highlight can be better visualized in this way.
Table 8 shows the ranking of countries ordered according to the CM value together with the
values of the different components of this index.
We observe that only four countries (Poland, Romania, Slovak Republic and Latvia) have
experienced an improvement in their productivity levels (CM > 1), and this improvement was
driven mainly by a change in their efﬁciency levels (ranging from 7 to 10%). This outweighs
the slight decline in technical change (of around 5%). The variable Z values for all these
countries are negative, and, therefore, that they have to make an additional effort to improve
their efﬁciency levels to compensate for this adverse environment.
Looking at the countries placed at the bottom of the ranking, we also detect that most of
the decline in their productivity levels (more than 20% in some cases) is also explained by a
drop in efﬁciency levels that also exceeds the technological setback (again around 5%). This
situation may be due to the so-called productivity paradox concept, in the sense that some
regions might face adjustment costs due to the need to adapt to the use of new technology,
which may make them more inefﬁcient in the short term (Skevas & Lansink, 2014). Note also
that the values of the CEH component for most of these countries (Cyprus, Hungary, Slovenia,
Malta and Italy) are high, i.e., they experienced a signiﬁcant change in their environment,
so maybe these regions need some time to learn how to operate in this new framework. As
mentioned above, the traditional Malmquist index, which returns values very close to unity
in most cases, does not detect this change and even reﬂects notable growth in some cases,
such as for Malta.
In general terms, we observe that the change in environmental harshness (CEH) is the
environmental change component that mostly affects results, with values that reﬂect a growth
of more than 20% in some cases and a reduction of almost 10% in others. In contrast, there is
hardly any variation in the levels of environmental technical change (ETC), while the change
in scale efﬁciency (ESEC) improves for all countries over the analyzed period, only slightly
in most countries, albeit substantially in some (e.g., Finland, Bulgaria or Italy).
Finally, note that most countries with high values of the environmental variable (Z) have
CEH values that are very close to 1 (e.g., Luxembourg, United Kingdom, Finland, Belgium,
Netherlands or France). Our interpretation is that these countries have already reached such
a high level of institutional quality that this factor does not condition the evolution of their
productivity levels very much. In fact, the decline in productivity levels observed for the above
countries is mainly explained by technical change, although there is also a slight decline in
CEC in some cases (Finland, France or United Kingdom).
5 Concluding remarks
This paper proposed a novel method for incorporating information about contextual variables
in Malmquist productivity decompositions. This approach is based on conditional nonpara-
metric efﬁciency measures, in which information about these factors is considered in the
123

2372
Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2351–2388
estimation of frontiers. Thus, our proposed conditional Malmquist index is calculated dif-
ferently than the traditional Malmquist index, and the differences between the two can be
attributed to different factors related to the environment in which the evaluated units operate.
Moreover, this new approach can be adapted to different assumptions regarding the returns
to scale.
This new model was applied to assess changes in productivity for a sample of 270 Euro-
pean regions over the 2007–2014 period considering quality of government as a potential
environmental factor affecting the performance of the above regions. Our results showed that
the decomposition of productivity change signiﬁcantly dependent on whether this factor is
considered, and it is possible to detect notable differences between the results obtained using
our proposed index and those that would be obtained using the traditional Malmquist index.
Speciﬁcally, we identiﬁed that there was a generalized decline in productivity levels over
the analyzed period, and this decline is mainly explained by changes in the environmental
harshness for many regions.
The innovative tool that we propose offers an alternative way of analyzing changes in
productivity over a period. It can be used in multiple frameworks and may be particularly
useful when the context or environment in which the evaluated units operate is known to have
experienced signiﬁcant changes over time. This new index can be easily interpreted and may
provide useful information for analyzing performance both from a research perspective and
for policy design. Moreover, as a future research line, we consider that the proposed method
could be reﬁned using the latest advances on the probabilistic characterization of directional
distance functions and their robust versions introduced by Daraio and Simar (2014). This
would make it possible to handle undesirable outputs and make statistical inference possible
by using the appropriate bootstrap algorithms. Likewise, another promising extension would
consist of developing a rigorous statistical framework to assess the positive or negative
inﬂuence of contextual variables on productivity changes. This could be achieved by adapting
the two-stage approach proposed by Badin et al. (2012), or by relying on the more recent
and precise group-efﬁciency comparison methodology introduced by Badin et al. (2019),
which enables formal inference on conditional efﬁciency differences across subpopulations
operating under heterogeneous environments.
