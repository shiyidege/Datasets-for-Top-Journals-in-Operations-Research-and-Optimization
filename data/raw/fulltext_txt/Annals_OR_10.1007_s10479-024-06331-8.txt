Abstract
We study a general optimization problem in which coef’¼ücients in the objective are uncertain.
We use capacities (lower probabilities) to model such uncertainty. Two popular criteria in
imprecise probability, namely maximality and E-admissibility, are employed to compare
solutions. We characterize non-dominated solutions with respect to these criteria in terms
of well-known notions in multi-objective optimization. These characterizations are novel
and make it possible to derive several interesting results. Specially, for convex problems,
maximality and E-admissibility are equivalent for any capacities even though the set of
associated acts is not convex, and in case of 2-monotone capacities, ’¼ünding an arbitrary non-
dominated solution and checking if a given solution is non-dominated are both tractable.
For combinatorial problems, we show a general result: in case of 2-monotone capacities,
if the deterministic version of the problem can be solved in polynomial time, checking
E-admissibility can also be done in polynomial time. Lastly, for the matroid optimization
problem, more re’¼üned results are also obtained thanks to these characterizations, namely the
connectedness of E-admissible solutions and an outer approximation based on the greedy
algorithm for non-dominated solutions with respect to maximality.

1 Introduction
Uncertainty affects numerous real-world optimization problems. This paper investigates a
general optimization problem in which the vector of coef’¼ücients c in the objective are uncer-
tain.Weapproachtheproblemasadecisionmakingproblemwithinanuncertainenvironment,
where the decision maker (DM) is tasked with identifying best decisions (solutions).
In situations where an objective probabilistic information on coef’¼ücients is at hand, the
context is referred to as decision under risk, and the goal is to optimize the expected value of
the objective. Due to the lack of knowledge, such probabilistic information is typically not
available, and this case is referred to as decision making under uncertainty (DMUU). For
instance, in a particular case where the only available information is  ŌĆō the set of all possible
values of c, the robust optimization approach (Kouvelis & Yu, 1996) aims to select robust
solutions according to minimax and minimax regret criteria. Robust solutions maintain an
acceptable objective value across all possible realizations of c.
We can recast a DMUU problem into a risk problem by using a subjective probability
measure in the DMŌĆÖs mind to model the uncertainty of coef’¼ücients, thereby returning to the
optimization of the expected value. However, it has been shown in various situations that
probability theory is not suf’¼ücient for all aspects of epistemic uncertainty. Several alternative
approaches have been developed to supplement probability theory by allowing the use of non-
additive measures, namely capacities, to represent uncertainty. The most notable approaches
include possibility theory (Dubois & Prade, 1988), evidence theory (Shafer, 1976), and
imprecise probability theory (Walley, 1991) in which the considered capacities are possibility
measures, belief functions, and coherent lower probabilities, respectively. We refer readers
to these seminal works for a thorough treatment of these approaches as well as drawbacks of
probability theory. For a broader contemporary perspective in DMUU, we recommend the
recent survey (Keith & Ahner, 2021).
Inthispaper,weusecapacitiestomodeluncertainty.ViewedasDMUUproblems,common
criteria such as those reviewed in Denoeux (2019), Troffaes (2007) are used to compare
solutions and to select best, i.e., non-dominated solutions of the considered optimization
problems. In general, these criteria can be divided into the following two groups: the ones
that yield a set of equally preferred non-dominated solutions, and the ones that, in contrast
to the use of probability theory, yield a set of incomparable non-dominated solutions. For
criteria in the ’¼ürst group, ’¼ünding one non-dominated solution is usually suf’¼ücient. For criteria
in the second group, the DM may need to ’¼ünd several or even all non-dominated solutions.
Criteria in the ’¼ürst group have received much attention. For instance, the minimax,
minimax regret and OWA criteria have been extensively studied in robust combinatorial
optimization problems (e.g., see the surveys (Aissi et al., 2009; Kasperski & Zieli┬┤nski, 2016)
and references therein).
In optimization under evidential uncertainty, an extension of the minimax criterionŌĆōthe
generalized Hurwicz criterion which still is in the ’¼ürst groupŌĆōis explored in Helal et al.
(2018), Tedjini et al. (2021) for the vehicle routing problem with various uncertainty factors
such as demands, travel times, etc. Recent works (Goerigk et al., 2023; Vu et al., 2023) also
studied this criterion for general problems where uncertainty appeared only on coef’¼ücients
of objectives. In the latter work, we focused on a problem with a linear objective, and focal
sets of the considered belief function are Cartesian products of compact sets. In the former
work, authors investigated a problem with a general objective, and focal sets of the considered
belief function can be any discrete set.

On the other hand, only a few works have examined criteria in the second group when
applied to optimization, even though some of these criteria, especially maximality and E-
admissibility, are very popular in DMUU using imprecise probabilities. To our knowledge, the
use of maximality to compare solutions of optimization problems under severe uncertainty
was ’¼ürst proposed in Quaeghebeur et al. (2012) whose setting is very general because it
accounts for the case where uncertainty represented by coherent lower previsions can affect
the constraints. Beside the generalized Hurwicz criterion, our recent paper (Vu et al., 2023)
also considered four common criteria in the second group, namely strong dominance, weak
dominance, maximality and E-admissibility. Although the work (Vu et al., 2023) is less
general than (Quaeghebeur et al., 2012), it has a salient feature: it provided characterizations
for the non-dominated solutions with respect to these criteria in terms of well-known concepts
in optimization.
In this paper, we consider the same problem as in Goerigk et al. (2023). We also assume
that the set  of possible values of c is ’¼ünite. Such  is the so-called scenario set in robust
optimization (Kouvelis & Yu, 1996). However, in terms of representing uncertainty, we use
capacities to represent knowledge about the true value (but unknown) of c, which is more
general than that used in Goerigk et al. (2023). To address the above-mentioned disparity
in the utilization of two types of criteria in optimization, we devote this paper to studying
the two popular criteria who induce incomparable non-dominated solutions: maximality and
E-admissibility. Moreover, the choice of criteria is also in’¼éuenced by their properties and
computational considerations.
2 Paper organization and main contributions
This paper is organized as follows. Section3 presents necessary background material. In
Sect.4, we formally describe how the considered optimization problem can be viewed as a
DMUU problem and explain the choice of maximality and E-admissibility based on their
properties and computational aspects.
Since maximality and E-admissibility are rooted in imprecise probability, we advocate
for their characterizations through optimization lens, especially when they are applied in
optimization problems. In Sect.5, we provide such characterizations by employing well-
known concepts from multi-objective optimization. To our knowledge, our characterizations
are novel in the literature. Although they are quite straightforward, these characterizations
provide access to established results in optimization, and thus allow us to derive new inter-
esting ’¼ündings for maximality and E-admissibility. In there, we also state the main research
questions of the paper.
In Sect.6, we study convex optimization problems. In this case, we show that maximality
and E-admissibility are in fact equivalent for any capacities, even though the set of associ-
ated acts is not convex. Additionally, in case of 2-monotone capacities, we demonstrate the
tractability of ’¼ünding an arbitrary non-dominated solution and checking whether a given
solution is non-dominated.
In Sect. 7, we study 0-1 combinatorial optimization problems. In Sect. 7.1, for the general
case, we prove that in case of 2-monotone capacities, as long as the deterministic version of
the considered problem can be solved in polynomial time, checking E-admissibility can also
be done in polynomial time. In Sect.7.2, we consider a special yet important case, namely the
matroid optimization problem, and we uncover more re’¼üned results compared to the general
case.

3.3 Decision making under (severe) uncertainty
In decision making under uncertainty (DMUU), a decision-maker (DM) has to choose some
decisions from a range of possible alternatives. Depending on the context, each decision can

As mentioned in the Introduction, we devote to criteria that induce incomparable non-

8 Conclusion
We have studied a general optimization problem in which coef’¼ücients in the objective are
uncertain. We have used capacities (lower probabilities) to model such uncertainty. Two
popular criteria ŌĆō maximality and E-admissibility ŌĆō are employed to compare solutions. The
paperŌĆÖs setting is less general than the one in Quaeghebeur et al. (2012), which addresses
uncertainty represented by lower previsions that can affect constraints. However, this setting
allows us to maintain a key feature shared with our recent paper Vu et al. (2023), which
considers a more specialized setting: providing characterizations of non-dominated solutions
through established optimization concepts. Some key results to take away from the paper are:
ŌĆó We have shown that non-dominated solutions with respect to maximality and E-
admissibility are characterized in terms of weakly ef’¼ücient and supported ef’¼ücient
solutions, respectively. Thanks to this, we have obtained several interesting results con-
cerning convex problems, notably the equivalence of the two criteria and the tractability
of ’¼ünding arbitrary solutions.
ŌĆó Although the question of checking E-admissibility has already been studied in various
works (Kikuti et al., 2011; Utkin & Augustin, 2005; Denoeux, 2019), we have shown, for
the ’¼ürst time, the polynomial solvability of checking E-admissibility for polynomial-time
solvable combinatorial problems, which we have achieved using the ellipsoid method.
