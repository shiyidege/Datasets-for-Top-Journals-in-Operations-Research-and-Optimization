Abstract
This paper develops an integrated Bayesian-optimisation framework to assess and strengthen
regionalrecoverycapacityunderadverseconditions,combiningrecovery-riskmodellingwith
risk-aware policy design. To address the joint challenges of sustainability, uncertainty, and
performance reliability in complex systems, the study draws together several operations
research approaches, including Bayesian variable selection, spatial econometrics, and multi-
objective optimisation. The proposed framework combines a dynamic Bayesian shrinkage
model to identify the main drivers of recovery across time and space, a discrete-time hazard
component to estimate annual and cumulative recovery probabilities, and a policy module
that incorporates tail risk and probabilistic constraints through conditional Value-at-Risk and
chance-constrained programming. Applied to a large panel of European regions, the frame-
work identiﬁes structural vulnerabilities and supports the design of adaptive interventions
under both structural and distributional uncertainty. The results show that recovery reliability
is geographically differentiated and sensitive to the recovery horizon, while policy allocations
that account for broader structural disadvantage and adverse outcomes yield more balanced
and robust intervention proﬁles.

1 Introduction
The increasing complexity of socio-economic systems, together with the growing incidence
of large-scale disruptions, from pandemics to environmental disasters, has placed resilience
and reliability at the centre of sustainable development debates. As reﬂected in the Sustain-
able Development Goals (SDGs) and in parallel developments within operations research
(OR), the capacity of a system to absorb shocks, adapt to changing conditions, and recover
effectively is now widely recognised as a key determinant of long-run sustainability (Adida
& Perakis, 2010; Dupacova & Kopa, 2012; Fabozzi,Huang and Zhou, 2010; Gabrel,Murat
and Lei, 2013). This issue is particularly relevant at the regional level, where differences in
infrastructure, labour-market conditions, institutional quality, and productive structure often
translate into differentiated recovery paths and persistent territorial inequalities. Recent con-

In this context, OR offers a broad set of methods for the design, assessment, and opti-

stochastic optimisation, and decision-making under uncertainty are increasingly used to sup-
port policy design in complex environments (Bertsimas & Miši´c, 2016; Delage & Ye, 2010;
Gabrel,Murat and Thiele, 2014; Jiang & Xu, 2022; Paciﬁco, 2026). Yet their integration into
a coherent empirical framework is still less common, especially when the aim is to move
from structural explanation to forward-looking and risk-aware regional policy design. In par-
ticular, relatively few studies bring together Bayesian modelling, recovery forecasting, and
optimisation under uncertainty within a single framework that draws on empirical evidence
and supports policy design.
This paper contributes to this literature by developing a multi-part framework that links
Bayesian inference, adaptive covariate selection, recovery-risk modelling, and risk-aware
optimisation for resilience-oriented intervention design (Ben-Tal,Den Hertog,De Waege-
naere,Melenberg and Rennen, 2013; Gabrel,Murat and Remli, 2010; Gulpinar,Pachamanova
and Canakoglu, 2013; Roy, 2010). A dynamic spatial panel logit with hierarchical shrinkage
is ﬁrst used to identify the structural drivers of recovery and to screen the covariate space in a
data-adaptive way. The resulting reduced speciﬁcation is then used to estimate recovery risk
over time through a discrete-time Bayesian hazard model, delivering annual and cumulative
recovery probabilities at the regional level. These predictive outputs are ﬁnally translated into
policy-allocation problems under uncertainty through multi-objective optimisation, chance-
constrained programming, and conditional Value-at-Risk criteria.
The framework is intended to address a policy-relevant question: not only which structural
factors are associated with regional recovery, but also how recovery prospects evolve over
time, how they differ across territories, and how limited policy resources should be allo-
cated when expected gains, cost discipline, and protection against adverse outcomes must
all be considered jointly. More broadly, the paper contributes to a growing strand of OR
research that combines uncertainty quantiﬁcation, spatial complexity, and policy-oriented
analysis in the study of sustainability and territorial resilience (Fabozzi,Huang and Zhou,
2010; Kang,Wu,Sheng Ng and Su, 2023; Ribas,Hamacher and Street, 2010).
Empirically, the framework is applied to a panel of European regions observed over two
decades, combining structural indicators, spatial interdependence, and binary recovery out-
comes. Three main ﬁndings are in order. First, the Bayesian screening stage identiﬁes a
compact set of policy-relevant structural drivers, which are then used to construct a focused
recovery-risk model. Second, the hazard results show that recovery reliability is geograph-
ically differentiated and horizon-dependent: short-run recovery is more exposed to local
spillovers, while medium-run recovery highlights more persistent territorial differences.
Third, the optimisation results indicate that policy conclusions depend closely on how cost
and risk are deﬁned. In particular, moving from stricter cost-efﬁciency criteria to broader
structural cost measures and tail-risk control yields allocation proﬁles that remain targeted
while aligning more closely with territorial balance and medium-run resilience objectives.
The simulation evidence complements the empirical analysis. In the in-class design, which
reﬂects the type of networked recovery mechanism underlying the proposed framework, the
dynamic spatial speciﬁcation performs best on average in predictive terms. In out-of-class
designs, its performance remains broadly comparable to that of simpler alternatives, while
computationaldiagnostics remain stable throughout. Taken together, these results suggestthat

2 Dynamic spatial panel logit model
Consider a spatial panel with N units observed over T periods. Let yit ∈{0, 1} denote a
binary indicator equal to one if unit i is classiﬁed as recovered at time t, and zero otherwise.
Let Xit ∈RK denote the corresponding vector of covariates, and let W ∈RN×N denote a
spatial weights matrix describing the connectivity structure among units.
In the empirical application, W is constructed from NUTS-2 regional geometries using
a queen contiguity rule. Under this criterion, two regions are treated as neighbours if they
share either a common border or a common vertex. This choice is useful in the present setting
because it captures a broad notion of territorial adjacency and is well suited to contexts
in which recovery may diffuse not only through directly shared borders, but also through
geographically connected local systems. After deﬁning these neighbourhood links, the matrix
is row-standardised so that each row sums to one. As a result, the spatial lag can be interpreted
as an average of neighbouring outcomes rather than a raw count of adjacent regions, which
makes the spatial signal comparable across units with different numbers of neighbours. A
practical complication arises for geographically isolated regions that have no contiguous
neighbours under the queen rule. To avoid excluding such units from the spatial structure,
a nearest-neighbour correction is applied: isolated regions are linked to their closest region
in geographic space. This correction is intentionally minimal and is used only to ensure that
every region remains connected to the spatial network entering the estimation.
For estimation, the panel is organised in long format with O = N × T region-year
observations. More speciﬁcally, the data are stacked by unit ﬁrst and, within each unit, by
time. Under this unit-major ordering, the main objects entering the likelihood are written as

applied across the full coefﬁcient system.
In the baseline speciﬁcation, τglob is assigned a half-normal prior with scale τ0 = 0.5. This
choice is intended to provide moderate regularisation. A smaller scale would compress the
coefﬁcient paths more aggressively and could mask substantively relevant temporal variation,
whereas a substantially larger scale would weaken regularisation and make the model more
sensitive to short-lived ﬂuctuations. Setting τ0 = 0.5 therefore reﬂects a compromise between
ﬂexibility and stability: the coefﬁcient paths remain free to adjust over time, while purely
noisy movements are less likely to drive the selection results.
In substantive terms, this shrinkage structure helps separate effects that remain non-
negligible over time from those that are weak or unstable. Rather than assigning the same
status to all regressors, the model allows the data to determine which covariates retain system-
atic predictive content across periods and can therefore be used as inputs for the subsequent
hazard modelling and risk-aware policy optimisation stages.
2.1 Data organisation and preprocessing
The data are preprocessed in three steps before estimation. First, duplicate region-year entries,
when present, are collapsed so that each unit-year cell corresponds to a unique aligned
observation. This avoids duplication in the estimation sample and preserves consistency
between the original panel structure and its long-format representation.
Second, the set of candidate predictors is restricted to numeric covariates. Identiﬁers,
administrative ﬁelds, and variables that mechanically overlap with the outcome deﬁnition
are excluded. This restriction helps preserve the interpretability of the selected covariates
and avoids introducing regressors that are too closely tied to the deﬁnition of recovery itself.
Third, missing or non-ﬁnite covariate values are replaced through variable-wise mean
imputation prior to standardisation. For each covariate, the corresponding imputation share
is recorded. All retained predictors are then standardised using z-scores, and variables with
zero or non-ﬁnite variance are removed. Mean imputation is used here as a baseline treat-
ment that keeps the preprocessing step simple and avoids introducing an additional modelling
layer before estimation. In this way, the main speciﬁcation keeps the data treatment parsi-

screening device for isolating the covariates that carry the most persistent and interpretable
signal for the subsequent hazard modelling and risk-aware policy optimisation analysis.
3 Discrete-time bayesian hazard model

risk over time. Using the reduced covariate set obtained from the DSPL model in Section 2, a

drivers of recovery, the hazard model translates that information into a probability framework
suited to timing, reliability, and scenario analysis.

decision input. In this way, the empirical analysis moves from identifying which structural
factors matter to quantifying how strongly and how quickly recovery is expected to occur
under different regional conditions and policy scenarios.
4 Multi-objective optimisation framework under uncertainty
The ﬁnal step of the analysis translates the recovery probabilities produced by the hazard
model into a policy-allocation problem under uncertainty. The aim is not simply to rank
regions by expected recovery prospects, but to identify intervention proﬁles that balance
recovery gains, cost discipline, and protection against adverse outcomes. In this way, the
empirical framework moves from prediction to decision support.
Let x denote a vector of policy allocations across regions or policy targets, subject to
non-negativity and resource limits. For any feasible allocation, the hazard model delivers
recovery probabilities and cumulative recovery proﬁles that can be used to construct policy
objectives. In the present setting, the optimisation problem is designed to reﬂect a common
policy trade-off: higher expected recovery gains are desirable, but they may come at the cost
of higher expenditure, greater concentration of resources, or larger exposure to unfavourable
scenarios.

θ can be interpreted as the minimum acceptable recovery standard that an allocation should
satisfy under the chosen reliability requirement.
This formulation is kept deliberately broad because, in the empirical application, it serves
more than one purpose. In the ﬁrst part of the analysis, the optimisation is used to trace a Pareto
frontierbetweenexpectedrecoverygainsandalternativecostmeasures.Inpracticalterms,this
makes it possible to compare allocations that perform well on average with allocations that are
less costly or more balanced. The resulting trade-off is explored numerically through the Non-
dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II), which is well suited to approximating
efﬁcient sets when the decision problem is nonlinear and potentially high-dimensional.
Robustness is addressed more explicitly in the next part of the analysis. Chance-
constrained programming (CCP) is used to rule out allocations that fail to meet a minimum
recovery requirement with sufﬁcient probability. This is useful when policy design must avoid
solutions that look attractive on average but become unsatisfactory under less favourable
conditions. The corresponding threshold parameter can therefore be read as a minimum
acceptable policy standard rather than as a purely technical restriction. In the baseline CCP
speciﬁcation, a concentration constraint is also imposed, such that no single region can receive
more than 5% of the total allocation, that is, xi ≤0.05 for all i. The purpose of this bound
is to avoid excessively concentrated corner solutions while preserving the logic of a strongly
targeted minimum-cost benchmark.
A complementary role is played by Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR). Whereas the
expected-loss component focuses on average performance, CVaR focuses on the adverse
tail of the outcome distribution. At conﬁdence level α, it summarises the mean loss in the
worst-performing fraction of scenarios. In the present setting, this is useful because regional
policy is rarely concerned only with average gains. It is also concerned with avoiding allo-
cations that leave some territories systematically exposed when recovery conditions turn
unfavourable. In the CVaR speciﬁcation, the same allocation shares xi are subject to a tighter
upper bound, such that xi ≤0.02 for all i. This tighter upper bound is intended to match the
more protective role of the CVaR criterion, which gives greater weight to adverse outcomes
and therefore is more consistent with a less concentrated allocation proﬁle.

5 Data description
The empirical analysis examines regional patterns of recovery and resilience using a panel
dataset covering the period 2012–2023. After data alignment and harmonisation, the ﬁnal
sample forms a balanced panel with 280 units observed over 12 annual periods, yielding a total
of 3360 unit-year observations. The analysis focuses on how socio-economic, demographic,
environmental, and productivity-related conditions are associated with the probability of
recovery after a shock, with particular attention to temporal dynamics and cross-regional
heterogeneity.
The dataset includes 18 indicators describing structural regional conditions. These com-
prise labour market variables, such as unemployment, employment in high-technology
sectors, nominal labour productivity, and the share of the active population; demographic
variables, including population size, population change, life expectancy, infant mortality, fer-
tility, and deaths; environmental pressure indicators, such as greenhouse gas emissions per
capita; and measures of capital accumulation and economic performance, including gross
ﬁxed capital formation per capita, GDP per capita, productivity, and total factor productivity.
Migration dynamics are also captured through net migration. All variables are drawn from
the ISTAT and ARDECO databases, ensuring consistency and comparability across units and
over time.
The dependent variable in the recovery model is the binary indicator success, equal to
1 if recovery occurs within one year and 0 otherwise. When not directly available in the raw
data,thisindicatorisconstructedfrom recovery_time,whichrecordsthenumberofyears
required for a unit to recover following a shock. The dataset also reports recovery_year,

6 Estimation results and dynamic driver identiﬁcation
This section reports the main empirical ﬁndings from the baseline DSPL speciﬁcation esti-
mated on the full 2012-2023 sample. The discussion focuses on three questions. First, is there
evidence of a meaningful spatial component in regional recovery? Second, which covariates
remain sufﬁciently relevant over time to enter the reduced policy-oriented set? Third, how
large are their effects when translated onto the probability scale?
6.1 Full-sample estimates and screening outcomes
Table 1 reports posterior summaries for the main structural parameters of the baseline DSPL
model. The spatial dependence parameter is clearly positive, with posterior mean ˆρ = 0.896
and a 90% posterior interval [0.795, 0.947]. In substantive terms, this indicates that recovery
is more likely when neighbouring regions recorded stronger recovery in the previous year.
The estimated spatial effect is therefore consistent with diffusion, coordination, or spillover
dynamics rather than with isolated regional adjustment.
The posterior mean of the global shrinkage parameter is moderate (ˆτglob = 0.440), which
is consistent with the role assigned to the prior in Section 2: coefﬁcient paths are regularised,
but not forced into excessive smoothness. The posterior mean of σβ[1] is comparatively
small, again suggesting that time variation is present but not mainly driven by short-lived
ﬂuctuations. The shrinkage control parameter c2 remains more weakly identiﬁed, as expected,
but all ˆR values are essentially equal to one and effective sample sizes are large. Detailed
trace and density diagnostics are reported in the Supplementary Material.

6.2 Dynamic effects on the probability scale
Posterior relevance alone does not show how much a selected covariate changes the proba-
bility of recovery in practical terms. For this reason, the selected drivers are also evaluated
on the probability scale. Table 2 reports average posterior effects over time for the retained
covariates, combining last-year relevance with mean marginal effects and mean changes in
predicted recovery probability for a one-standard-deviation increase in the covariate.
Two patterns stand out. First, the strongest average negative association is found for
nlab_prod, whose mean change in recovery probability is about −2.105 percentage points
for a one-standard-deviation increase. Second, the clearest positive association is found for
hrst, with an average increase of about 1.278 percentage points. By contrast, most of
the remaining covariates have much smaller average probability effects. This should not
be read as evidence that they do not matter. In several cases, their contribution lies less in
producing a large average shift in recovery probability and more in remaining relevant over
time, becoming more informative in particular periods, or helping to characterise the broader
structural conditions under which recovery becomes more or less likely.
In policy terms, this distinction matters. Some indicators are associated with clearer
directional shifts in recovery probability, whereas others are more useful for identifying the
structural conditions under which recovery becomes more or less likely. The results there-
fore support a differentiated interpretation of regional resilience policy. Measures linked to
labour productivity and skills appear more strongly connected to the direction of recovery
prospects, while other selected covariates help identify the broader environment in which
recovery becomes easier or harder.

6.3 Prospective validation and parsimony
The full-sample results are complemented by a prospective validation based on one-step-
ahead prediction for 2023. The train-test design and its implementation are described in the
Supplementary Material; here the focus is on what the comparison implies for the trade-off
between predictive performance and interpretability.
Table 3 reports the 2023 Brier and log scores for four speciﬁcations: the full model with
all 18 covariates, the baseline reduced model with 15 retained covariates, a lower correlation
cut-off, and a collapsed-by-cluster alternative. The Brier score measures the average squared
gap between realised outcomes and predicted probabilities, whereas the log score measures
the predictive likelihood assigned to the realised outcomes and is therefore more sensitive to
overconﬁdent errors. The collapsed-by-cluster speciﬁcation is included as a stricter redun-

retained covariate. The full model performs best in purely predictive terms, but the gap relative
to the reduced speciﬁcations is small. The baseline reduced model increases the Brier score
only from 0.0025 to 0.0026 and the log score from 0.0502 to 0.0511. The lower-correlation
alternative performs almost identically, while the collapsed-by-cluster speciﬁcation coincides
numerically with the baseline reduced model in the reported results.
This comparison is important for policy use. The reduced speciﬁcation gives up very
little predictive performance while delivering a more interpretable and more manageable set
of drivers. When the objective is not only to predict recovery, but also to identify a set of
variables that can inform regional intervention design, this trade-off is favourable.

7 Recovery risk, forecasting, and spatial reliability patterns
This section reports the main empirical results from the discrete-time hazard model. The
focus is on three questions. First, how broad is the effective recovery risk set under alternative
recovery horizons? Second, what spatial patterns emerge in annual and cumulative recovery
probabilities? Third, what do the forward-looking policy scenarios imply for short-run and
medium-run recovery prospects across regions?
The discussion is organised accordingly. The ﬁrst part summarises the empirical risk
set and the baseline spatial reliability benchmark. The second part examines the spatial
distribution of recovery hazards and cumulative recovery probabilities. The third part turns
to forward-looking projections and policy scenarios. The ﬁnal part assesses how sensitive
these conclusions are to the choice of recovery horizon.
7.1 Risk-set structure and baseline spatial reliability
Table 4 summarises the effective risk set under the one-, two-, and three-year recovery
horizons. The full panel contains 3,360 region-year observations in each case, but only a
subset belongs to active shock episodes in which recovery is relevant. Under the baseline
one-year horizon, 536 observations are at risk, corresponding to about 16.0% of the panel,
with 173 observed recoveries. The at-risk share rises to 19.1% under the two-year horizon
and to 20.1% under the three-year horizon, as longer horizons naturally keep regions in the
recovery window for more years. Importantly, no event is ever recorded outside the risk set,
which indicates that the deﬁnition of the risk set is applied consistently throughout.
This feature is important for interpretation. The hazard model is not averaging over all
region-years without distinction. Instead, it focuses on those periods in which recovery is
actually being determined and in which policy support is most relevant. In substantive terms,
this makes the resulting probabilities easier to interpret as indicators of recovery reliability
rather than as generic transition frequencies.
Table 4 also reports the baseline Spatial At-risk Index (SAI), deﬁned as the product
of median |ρ| and the average lagged neighbouring failure state within the anchor-year
risk set. The index is highest under the one-year horizon, with a median value of 0.828,
and declines sharply to 0.520 under the two-year horizon and 0.184 under the three-year
horizon. This pattern is informative from a policy perspective. When recovery is evaluated
over a short horizon, neighbouring unresolved distress remains strongly associated with local
recovery prospects. As the horizon is extended, that immediate spatial pressure becomes less
pronounced, suggesting that short-run recovery is more exposed to local spillovers than
medium-run adjustment.

7.2 Spatial distribution of recovery hazards and cumulative probabilities
Figure 4 reports the geographical distribution of estimated annual recovery hazards and
cumulative recovery probabilities under the baseline one-year horizon for the ﬁrst two years
in which the risk set is active. The upper row shows the annual hazard, conditional on still

episode.
Two main patterns emerge. First, recovery conditions differ across space from the early
stages. Both the annual hazard and the cumulative measure display marked variation across
European regions, which indicates that recovery reliability is not simply a matter of common
macro timing. It differs materially across territories. Second, the cumulative measure is
smoother and more persistent than the annual hazard, as expected. The annual hazard is useful
for identifying where recovery is immediately more or less likely, whereas the cumulative
measure highlights how quickly recovery prospects accumulate over the risk episode.
For policy interpretation, the distinction between these two panels is useful. A region may
face a modest annual hazard in a given year and still retain a relatively favourable cumulative
outlook if recovery probabilities build up gradually over time. Conversely, low cumulative
recovery probabilities point to territories where weak year-by-year recovery prospects persist
rather than being quickly reversed. This is precisely the type of information that matters when
distinguishing between temporary stress and more persistent recovery weaknesses.

may favour one group of territories, while a strategy assessed over the medium-run may
prioritise another. The choice of horizon is therefore not a minor technical detail. It changes
both the estimated effectiveness of policy levers and the territorial ranking of where those
levers appear to matter most.
Taken together, the hazard results point to a clear empirical conclusion. Recovery relia-
bility is geographically differentiated, accumulates at different speeds across territories, and
responds differently depending on whether policy success is assessed over one, two, or three
years. For short-run crisis management, the one-year horizon is informative because it high-
lights where immediate recovery remains difﬁcult. For strategic regional policy, however, the
two- and three-year horizons appear more relevant, because they capture slower but more
persistent improvement channels. This distinction will matter directly in the optimisation
stage, where policy allocations are evaluated not only by expected gains but also by their
robustness across competing objectives and risk criteria.
8 Optimising resilience allocation under uncertainty
The ﬁnal part of the analysis turns the recovery forecasts into an allocation problem under
uncertainty. The objective is not simply to identify where recovery is expected to be stronger,
but to determine how limited policy resources should be directed when decision-makers must
balance expected gains, cost considerations, and risk exposure. In the present application, the
beneﬁt term is deﬁned as the projected increase in cumulative recovery probability between
2023 and 2026. This forward-looking gain is then combined with alternative cost criteria
to evaluate how the geography of optimal intervention changes when policy gives relatively
greater weight to environmental cost or to broader structural vulnerability. This type of trade-
off is increasingly recognised in recent operations research on resilience, where efﬁciency,

risk protection, and sustainability are treated as jointly relevant design criteria rather than as
isolated objectives (Ataburo,Ampong and Essuman, 2024; Govindan, 2025).

the observed 2023 value of ghg_pc, and therefore provides an environmental benchmark for
cost-efﬁciency. The second is based on a composite Sustainable Development Goal (SDG)
cost built from the selected structural indicators in the 2023 cross-section.

benchmark is unlikely to be enough for cohesion-oriented policy, because a portfolio that
is optimal in strict cost terms may still be too selective in territorial terms. This makes the
comparison with broader criteria, and especially with the CVaR extension discussed next,
particularly relevant.
8.3 Risk-aware reallocation through CVaR
The ﬁnal part places greater emphasis on the risk dimension by moving from a simple chance
constraint to a CVaR-based formulation. The policy intuition is straightforward. Rather than
asking only whether a recovery target is met with sufﬁcient probability, the CVaR criterion
also accounts for adverse outcomes in the lower end of the distribution. In other words, it
gives greater weight to avoiding unfavourable realisations, not only to satisfying the average
reliability condition. The use of risk-averse criteria in this contextis also in line with recentOR
contributions that incorporate CVaR-based objectives into resilient allocation and portfolio
decisions under disruption risk (Merzifonluoglu, 2026).

acceptable territorial distribution of resources.
From a policy perspective, this comparison highlights the main ﬁndings of the analysis.
Resilience allocation depends not only on expected gains, but also on how policy deﬁnes
cost and how much weight it places on protection against adverse outcomes. A framework
based only on cost-efﬁciency can be informative, but it is too limited for cohesion-oriented
intervention. By contrast, introducing broader structural cost criteria and explicit tail-risk
control yields allocations that remain well targeted while becoming more consistent with
territorial balance and medium-run resilience objectives.
9 Simulation study
The simulation design considers three distinct data-generating processes. The main design is
a network-based cascade-and-recovery data-generating process, which is the relevant in-class
case for the present study. In this setting, units may enter failure states, remain exposed to
neighbours’ failures, and subsequently recover, with recovery probabilities shaped by both
123

Annals of Operations Research
local characteristics and network conditions. This is the setting that most closely matches
the empirical hazard structure discussed in Section 3. Two further generators are used as
robustness checks. The ﬁrst is a more standard dynamic binary process with persistence and
spatial interaction. The second is an agent-based investment-success mechanism in which
the outcome is driven by investment decisions and neighbouring exposure. These two out-
of-class cases are included to check whether the proposed model remains usable when the
underlying mechanism differs from the one it is designed to represent.
Across all generators, the proposed Bayesian dynamic spatial logit (DSPL) is compared
with three simpler alternatives: a dynamic logit without spatial exposure (DL), a reduced
network-hazard logit with simpler coefﬁcient structure (NHL), and a reduced static logit
benchmark (ARL) for the agent-based model (ABM). Predictive performance is evaluated
out of sample on the ﬁnal period. In line with the underlying methodological logic of the
analysis, the emphasis is placed on predictive discrimination and calibration rather than on
recovering every latent parameter of the simulated system. Results are summarised using
mean log score and mean Area Under the receiver operating characteristic Curve (AUC)
across replications, where AUC is useful because it measures how well the model discrimi-
nates between successful and unsuccessful outcomes independently of any single probability
threshold. Computational reliability is assessed through standard sampler diagnostics. Full
implementation details are reported in the Supplementary Material.
Table 15 reports predictive performance across the three generators. The main result is
clearest in the in-class design, namely the network cascade-and-recovery generator. In this
setting, the proposed model records both the lowest average log score (0.608) and the highest
average AUC (0.687) among the four competitors. The numerical margins are moderate rather
than large, but they are aligned across both metrics and point in the same direction. The main
ﬁnding is that the proposed methodology shows its clearest advantage in the in-class design,
since that setting most closely reﬂects the empirical recovery mechanism of interest.
The two out-of-class designs lead to a different conclusion. Under the system-dynamics
generator, simpler alternatives perform slightly better on average, while under the agent-
based generator all four methods produce very similar results. The main ﬁndings therefore
indicate that the proposed model is not intended as a uniformly dominant speciﬁcation across
all binary-response settings. Its advantage emerges most clearly when the underlying process
is close to the recovery mechanism studied in this framework, while outside that setting it
remains competitive and computationally stable.
Figure 13 focuses on the in-class case. The proposed model performs best on the gen-
erator that most closely reﬂects the proposed framework. This ﬁnding highlights that the
additional modelling structure is most useful where recovery depends on evolving exposure
to neighbouring failure and recovery patterns, rather than only on static correlation or generic
persistence.
Table 16 reports the main sampler diagnostics. The main diagnostic results are in order.
Across all generators and all competing models, no divergent transitions are observed. The
maximum ˆR values remain close to one throughout, and effective sample sizes are con-
sistently satisfactory. Overall, the proposed model is not computationally weaker than the
simpler alternatives. In some cases, especially under the out-of-class generators, the reduced
speciﬁcations yield lower minimum effective sample sizes than the proposed model. These
results support the computational reliability of the proposed speciﬁcation in moderate sim-
ulation settings and reinforce its practical suitability for an empirical framework that aims
to combine dynamic recovery modelling, spatial dependence, and policy-oriented decision
support.

10 Concluding remarks
This study developed a uniﬁed Bayesian-optimisation framework to analyse and support
regional resilience under systemic shocks. By combining dynamic Bayesian covariate screen-
ing, discrete-time recovery-risk modelling, and risk-aware optimisation through CVaR and
CCP criteria, the framework provides an integrated basis for policy design under uncertainty.
The empirical ﬁndings point to clear geographical and temporal differences in regional
recovery. Recovery reliability is not distributed uniformly across space, nor does it evolve at
the same speed across territories or recovery horizons. This suggests that regional intervention
cannot be based on a single average proﬁle. The optimisation results highlight these ﬁndings
by showing that policy allocations depend not only on expected gains, but also on how cost
and adverse outcomes are deﬁned. In particular, broader structural cost criteria and explicit
tail-risk control lead to allocation proﬁles that remain focused while aligning more closely
with territorial balance and medium-run resilience objectives.
From a methodological perspective, the framework brings together work in operations
research on Bayesian learning, recovery-risk analysis, and policy optimisation under uncer-
tainty. More generally, it contributes to ongoing efforts to develop integrated approaches for
complex socio-economic systems in which sustainability, uncertainty, and decision support
need to be considered jointly.
Future research may extend this framework to real-time monitoring settings, incorporate
endogenous policy feedbacks, and adapt the approach to other application domains such as
climate resilience, urban infrastructure, and health systems.

DSPL covariates. The workﬂow takes as inputs the aligned panel, the selected covariate
set, the spatial weights matrix, the recovery horizon h, and the baseline shrinkage scale
τ0. Shock episodes are ﬁrst converted into at-risk recovery windows, after which recovery-
event indicators, failure states, and lagged spatial failure terms are constructed. Estimation
is then carried out in Stan by HMC, and the resulting posterior draws are used to compute
region-year recovery hazards and cumulative recovery probabilities over each active recovery
window. Algorithm 2 summarises the full workﬂow.
