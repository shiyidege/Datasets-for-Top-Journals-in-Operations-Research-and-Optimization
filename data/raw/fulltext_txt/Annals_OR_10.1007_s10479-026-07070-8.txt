Abstract
The increasing complexity of global supply chain networks, driven by economic integration,
has heightened their susceptibility to cascading failures triggered by various disruptions.
Existing studies on risk propagation and response strategies in supply chain networks often
model only single failure modes, neglect ﬁrm-speciﬁc heterogeneity in deﬁning failure
characteristics, and treat recovery strategies as discrete interventions rather than continu-
ous adaptive behaviors across varying operational states. To address these shortcomings,
this study proposes an extended load-capacity model that integrates the coexistence and
interaction of overload and underload failure modes, deﬁnes nonlinear functions to cap-
ture ﬁrm-heterogeneous failure characteristics, and develops a continuous uniﬁed framework
comprising four distinct node states that integrates proactive and reactive risk recovery strate-
gies for adaptive and risk states. By employing the Barabási–Albert model to construct supply
chain networks of varying density and simulating both random and targeted disruptions,
the results demonstrate that: (1) ﬁrms require a comprehensive risk management framework
incorporating forward-looking risk warning mechanisms and dual-track parallel risk response
systems, with effective implementation of adaptive recovery strategies focusing on three key
dimensions under the equilibrium principle; (2) at the network level, the implementation
of adaptive recovery strategies should follow differentiation principles across two critical
dimensions—strategic prioritization of core ﬁrms and management of inter-ﬁrm capability
disparities. These insights advance both theoretical understanding and practical approaches
for designing resilient supply chain networks capable of preventing and mitigating cascading
disruptions.

1 Introduction
The accelerating integration of global markets and the increasing complexity of economic
activities have fundamentally transformed supply chain systems from traditional linear struc-
tures into intricate, multi-tiered networks comprising suppliers, manufacturers, and retailers
(Cheng et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2022). While this evolution has enabled greater efﬁciency
and reach, it has also intensiﬁed the complex interdependencies within supply chain net-
works, heightening their vulnerability to supply risks and sudden disruptions (Chakraborty
et al., 2024; Cottrell et al., 2019).
Recent global events have clearly illustrated these vulnerabilities. The 2011 Tohoku
earthquake in Japan compelled Toyota to shut down 12 factories, triggering widespread
disruptions across the global automotive industry’s supply chain and generating substan-
tial losses (Carvalho et al., 2021; Matsuo, 2015). Similarly, the Russia-Ukraine conﬂict that
erupted in 2022 produced far-reaching consequences for global supply chains. When LEONI,
the world’s leading manufacturer of automotive wiring harnesses, suspended operations at
its Ukrainian and Russian facilities, the resulting shortage of crucial raw materials forced
prominent automakers such as Volkswagen and Audi to temporarily cease production. This
situation exempliﬁes the cascading impact of localized disruptions on global industrial oper-
ations (Ren et al., 2024; Srai et al., 2023).
Critically, risks affecting speciﬁc ﬁrms can propagate through complex network connec-
tions to impact seemingly unrelated organizations, potentially destabilizing entire supply
chain networks through cascading failures and causing substantial economic losses (Inoue &
Todo, 2019; Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024a). This phenomenon underscores the urgent
need to understand risk propagation pathways and develop appropriate risk response strate-
gies to strengthen supply chain network resilience and ensure operational stability.
Driven by the critical nature of cascading failures in supply chain networks, research
in this area has expanded signiﬁcantly. Existing studies typically model these networks by
representing ﬁrms as nodes and business relationships as edges, facilitating the analysis of
material, capital, and information ﬂows. Within this analytical framework, the load-capacity
model has emerged as a particularly valuable tool, where node load represents a ﬁrm’s logis-
tic volume and capacity reﬂects its maximum sustainable operational scale. This approach
effectively captures ﬁrms’ ability to manage both excess inventory and material shortages.
The model and its variants have been instrumental in examining cascading failure effects,
particularly in characterizing two primary failure modes: (1) upstream ﬁrm overload due
to delayed product distribution, and (2) downstream ﬁrm underload stemming from raw
material deﬁcits. For example, Shi et al., (2021b) investigated network ripple effects using a

load-capacity model focused on overload-induced cascading failures, demonstrating that col-
laborative capacity sharing among adjacent ﬁrms can boost supply chain resilience without
substantial cost increases. Taking a different perspective, Mu et al. (2024) analyzed failure
probabilities under overload conditions in multilayer supply chain networks, highlighting
how node redundancy capacity serves as a safeguard against network collapse.
However, existing studies on cascading failures and risk response strategies reveal three
signiﬁcant limitations. First, the prevalent focus on single-mode failure analysis (Fu et al.,
2024;Zhangetal.,2024d)oversimpliﬁesthesimultaneousoccurrenceandinteractionofover-
load and underload conditions, limiting our understanding of supply chain network dynamics.
Second, existing models oversimplify failure thresholds and probabilities as linear functions
of node degree, initial load, or capacity (Mu et al., 2024; Zhong & Liu, 2024; Zhou et al.,
2025), neglecting crucial ﬁrm-speciﬁc attributes, and thereby undermining the representation
of heterogeneous failure conditions. This simpliﬁcation fails to differentiate between ﬁrms
with the same structural position but distinct operational robustness. Third, most existing
studies on the temporal scope and integration of risk response strategies focus on a single
strategy, either reactive or proactive (Nibbrig et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2021b). While a limited
number of studies have explored the integration of both strategies, they oversimplify the con-
tinuous behavioral adaptations linked to ﬂuctuating load levels or expectations (Gurbuz et al.,
2023; Zhao et al., 2019), failing to adequately capture ﬁrms’ continuous adaptive behaviors
across different stress levels.
To address these research gaps, this study examines three questions: (1) How can cascad-
ing failure processes be modeled to account for concurrent overload and underload failure
modes? (2) How can failure characteristics be deﬁned beyond linear assumptions, in a way
that captures ﬁrm heterogeneity? (3) What uniﬁed framework can effectively integrate proac-
tive and reactive risk response strategies while encompassing continuous recovery behavioral
states of network nodes? The study employs the Barabási–Albert (BA) model–consistent with
the scale-free topologies observed in real-world supply chain networks (Fessina et al., 2024;
Pal & Swami, 2022; Wiedmer & Grifﬁs, 2021) to generate two supply chain networks of
varying densities and develops an enhanced load-capacity model considering overload and
underload failure modes to analyze cascading failures under both random and targeted shock
scenarios. This model introduces a four-state ﬁrm categorization (normal, adaptive, risk, and
failed states) while incorporating heterogeneous failure parameters based on ﬁrm charac-
teristics. The analysis explores the combinatorial effect of two recovery strategies in order
to enhance network resilience. First, adaptive recovery strategies are implemented during
the adaptive state, where ﬁrms adjust operations in advance based on predictive indicators
to sense emerging vulnerabilities. Second, risk recovery strategies are applied during the
risk state, where reactive measures are taken after their operational loads exceed critical
thresholds, representing classical ex-post resilience mechanisms.
The study makes three primary contributions: (1) It provides a comprehensive treatment of
both overload and underload failure modes, overcoming potential distortions in supply chain
network risk assessments caused by focusing on single failure mode analysis in previous
research; (2) It explicitly incorporates ﬁrm heterogeneity into the speciﬁcation of failure char-
acteristics in cascading processes, deﬁning nonlinear functions of initial load, node strength,
and adjustment parameters, thereby moving beyond uniform deﬁnitions in existing studies
to better reﬂect the diverse operational characteristics of ﬁrms; (3) It develops a uniﬁed
continuous framework that integrates four node states with proactive and reactive recovery
strategies, thus addressing the limitation of previous studies in which recovery behaviors are
triggered at speciﬁc discrete time points or by speciﬁc events.

2 Literature review
2.1 Complex supply chain networks
Supply chains comprise intricate webs of ﬁrm relationships that form sophisticated network
structures. This complexity has made complex network theory an essential analytical frame-
work for understanding supply chain networks and establishing theoretical foundations for
supply chain risk management. Empirical research has conﬁrmed that supply chain networks
demonstrate distinctive scale-free characteristics, including short path lengths, high cluster-
ing coefﬁcients, and power-law connectivity distribution (Fessina et al., 2024; Wiedmer &
Grifﬁs, 2021). The BA model generates scale-free networks through growth and preferential
attachment mechanisms, with node degree following a power-law distribution that aligns
with the characteristics of real supply chain networks (Barabasi & Albert, 1999; Hearnshaw
& Wilson, 2013). This model effectively captures the “rich-get-richer” dynamic in supply
chains (Pal & Swami, 2022). In contrast, as a foundational network model, the Erd˝os–Rényi
(ER) random network exhibits a uniform degree distribution (Erd˝os & Rényi, 1959). The
Watts–Strogatz (WS) small-world network model maintains a degree distribution that approx-
imates a Poisson distribution despite desirable properties such as high clustering coefﬁcients
and short average path lengths (Watts Duncan & Strogatz Steven, 1998). These characteristic
limits the capacity of ER and WS models to capture the hub dominance observed in actual
supply chain networks. Therefore, the BA model is widely employed in simulating supply
chain networks and their dynamic propagation patterns under cascading failures, particularly
in examining node degree, local preference, heterogeneity, price, and cost relationships (Vaid
et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). Furthermore, studies have demonstrated that, compared with
the ER network model and the WS network model, the BA network model exhibits stronger
reliability, responsiveness, and persistence, as well as a shorter recovery time when respond-
ing to random shocks (Habibi et al., 2023). Such investigations into both static and dynamic
structural properties have signiﬁcantly advanced our understanding of supply chain networks
and spurred further research in this ﬁeld.
The past decade has witnessed unprecedented disruptions to global supply chains, stem-
ming from geopolitical and economic conﬂicts, as well as public emergencies including the
COVID-19 pandemic and climate-related disasters. These challenges have catalyzed research
across multiple dimensions: risk propagation analysis (Brusset et al., 2022; Scarpin et al.,
2022), development of recovery strategies (Mu et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2019), and assess-
ment of structural robustness and resilience (Habibi et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2019). Robustness
evaluates, from a static structural perspective, a network’s capacity to maintain its systemic
functions when subjected to internal and external risk shocks (Han et al., 2025; Wang et al.,
2025). The associated strategic mechanisms emphasize structural optimization aimed at min-
imizing the probability of systemic collapse during the pre-disruption network design phase.

In contrast, resilience encompasses the dynamic process of withstanding disruptions, adapt-
ing during shocks, and recovering post-failure to restore or even enhance system functionality
(Munir et al., 2024). Corresponding strategic mechanisms focus on rerouting ﬂows through
real-time responses and post-disruption recovery processes to achieve faster recovery times
and potentially stronger systemic functions (Ivanov, 2024a; Moatari-Kazerouni et al., 2025;
Raj et al., 2022; Tabatabaei, 2025). Notable contributions in this ﬁeld include the spatiotem-
poral dynamic model developed by Brusset et al. (2022), which integrated epidemic modeling
with material ﬂow analysis to reveal ripple effects in supply networks. Similarly, Huang et al.
(2024) developed a comprehensive resilience assessment framework that evaluates accessi-
bility, connectivity, and network efﬁciency through a dual-layer network model, applying it
to China’s electric vehicle supply chain.
These studies collectively underscore the critical role of complex network theory in
advancing supply chain risk management research. By providing a robust theoretical founda-
tion for understanding the intricate interactions and decision-making processes within supply
chain networks, complex network theory offers an effective analytical tool for examining risk
propagation dynamics and a scientiﬁc basis for optimizing risk mitigation strategies.
2.2 Load-capacity models in supply chain networks
Building on the vital role of complex network theory in supply chain research, this section
examines risk propagation and recovery strategies. Within complex systems, nodes and edges
transcendsimplephysicalentitiesorﬂows,encompassingdynamicstatetransfersandmultidi-
mensional risk propagation. Three primary modeling approaches have emerged: Independent
Cascade (IC) models (Guo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), OPA (ORNL-PSERC-Alaska) mod-
els (Carreras et al., 2020; Park et al., 2022), and load-capacity models (Zhang et al., 2024a;
Zhu et al., 2023). These three models differ markedly in application domains and analyti-
cal focus. The IC model describes the information propagation process and suits scenarios
where an individual’s behavior is inﬂuenced by a single other individual, such as the spread of
rumors and gossip. The OPA model, conversely, emphasizes the physical characteristics and
operational constraints of power grids, capturing dynamic behaviors including line tripping
and voltage collapse. The load-capacity model simulates network dynamics based on the load
and capacity characteristics of individual nodes. Cascading failures in supply chain networks
typically involve complex interactions among multiple nodes and the impact of node failures
throughout the entire network. Both the IC model and the OPA model are inadequate for
capturing the mechanisms of risk transmission induced by enterprises’ production and sales
activities when modeling cascading failures in supply chain networks. The comparative anal-
ysis demonstrates that the load-capacity model serves as a robust analytical tool, extensively
utilized for simulating the dynamics of supply chain networks.
Building on the classical load-capacity model, studies of supply chain networks have
extended the framework in four major directions: the relationship between initial load and
capacity, failure modes, failure conditions, and failure recovery mechanisms. The ﬁrst exten-
sion focuses on the fundamental relationship between initial load and capacity. Early studies
considered node degree as a measure of load and set node capacity as a linear increasing
function of initial load (Chen et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022). However, as understanding of
real-world network complexity deepened, this linear paradigm proved insufﬁcient. Recogni-
tion of nonlinear relationships led to more sophisticated models, as demonstrated by Chen
et al. (2024) and Zhang et al., (2024a), who developed hybrid frameworks incorporating both
linear and nonlinear components. This advancement provided a more reﬁned characterization

of node capacity functions. Despite these improvements, current models still inadequately
address the heterogeneity in node strength and its implications for overall network perfor-
mance.
The second extension addresses failure modes in supply chain networks. Traditional
research has predominantly focused on the overload failure mode, where order volumes
exceed maximum production capability (Wang et al., 2021; Zhang, et al., 2024c). A signiﬁ-
cant advancement came with Yang et al. (2021)’s introduction of underload failure analysis,
which revealed crucial insights into supply chain network vulnerability in Europe. Build-
ing on this foundation, Liu et al. (2022) developed an underload cascading failure model to
investigate supply chains’ adaptive capabilities through reorganization in dynamic environ-
ments. However, real-world observations suggest that ﬁrm failures often trigger simultaneous
effects: upstream cargo accumulation (overload failure) and downstream material shortages
(underload failure). This reality underscores the necessity for integrated models incorporating
both failure modes.
The third extension focuses on failure conditions, which are examined through two pri-
mary perspectives: failure thresholds and failure probabilities. Failure thresholds, used to
identify operational interruptions for ﬁrms, are generally deﬁned in two ways: critical failure
thresholds and load redistribution failure thresholds. Shi et al., (2021b) innovatively deﬁned
the critical failure threshold as an aggregate of a ﬁrm’s total capacity and collaborative addi-
tional capacity. Parallel developments in load redistribution failure threshold research have
maintained a linear relationship with node load or capacity (Liu et al., 2022; Qi et al., 2020;
Zhou et al., 2025). A signiﬁcant theoretical advancement emerged with the introduction of
failure probability metrics, acknowledging that not all overloaded nodes fail immediately.
This probabilistic approach, exempliﬁed by the piecewise capacity function with a linear seg-
ment deﬁned by Mu et al. (2024), offers new insights into network behavior under slight load
ﬂuctuations. However, current deﬁnitions of thresholds and probabilities remain predomi-
nantly focused on the linear relationship with node degree, capacity, or load, overlooking
other crucial ﬁrm-speciﬁc attributes such as a ﬁrm’s adaptive ﬂexibility and undermining the
representation of heterogeneous failure conditions.
The fourth extension to the classical load-capacity model addresses recovery strategies,
encompassing load redistribution mechanisms, response timing, and strategy selection. In
terms of load redistribution, Qi et al. (2020) advocated for prioritizing nodes with high
redundant capacity, while subsequent studies proposed proportional redistribution based on
original load or business relationship strength (Fu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Regarding
response timing, existing load-capacity model-based extensions have primarily concentrated
on reactive recovery strategies, such as strengthening existing relationships with partners
having redundant capacity (Mu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2021b; Wang & Zhang, 2018). How-
ever, comparatively little attention has been given to proactive interventions. Nibbrig et al.
(2025) emphasized the design of adaptive resilience strategies for preemptive risk mitigation.
While load-capacity model-based studies rarely integrate proactive and reactive approaches,
research not grounded in this model has demonstrated the value of combining both strategies.
Pavlov et al. (2018) stressed the necessity of considering both mechanisms when estimating
supply chain resilience, and Pavlov et al. (2022) proposed integrated frameworks opera-
tionalizing this concept. Some modeling studies have illustrated practical paths: Zhao et al.
(2019) developed an agent-based model that builds on reactive strategies of seeking alter-
native suppliers, while proactively identifying the highest-risk ﬁrms at least two hops away
from the focal ﬁrm, removing them, and selecting their direct competitors as replacements to
reconﬁgure the network; Gurbuz et al. (2023) employed simulation to analyze how small and
123

medium-sized enterprises respond to major disruptions through supply chain network conﬁg-
uration, recommending increased ﬂexibility via geographically distributed partnerships, with
actions triggered by speciﬁc discrete time events. Although previous studies have provided
valuable insights for integrating proactive and reactive recovery strategies, the load-capacity
model and its extensions possess distinct advantages in characterizing cascading failure
processes. Therefore, it is essential to address the current limitations of proactive strategy
research within the load-capacity modeling framework.
In terms of strategy selection, load-capacity model-based extensions have primarily
focused on load redistribution recovery strategies based on existing connections and recovery
strategies optimized by adding new connections. A model prioritizing load distribution to
cooperative ﬁrms with larger redundant capacity, proposed by Li et al. (2022) suggested that
strengthening existing connections is often the optimal choice, as establishing new connec-
tions can be costly. Fu et al. (2024) further explored two-stage recovery strategies combining
the strengthening of existing business connections and the establishment of new links.
While these studies collectively conﬁrm the effectiveness of diverse recovery strate-
gies, signiﬁcant limitations remain. Load-capacity model-based approaches, which typically
deﬁne recovery strategies as behaviors triggered at speciﬁc discrete time points or events,
often oversimplify the continuous behaviors under various node states within certain load
ranges. Moreover, existing extensions have provided limited exploration of proactive strate-
gies and their integration with reactive measures in relation to ﬁrm state dynamics. This,
coupled with the neglect of node heterogeneity in strategy design, constitutes a critical gap
in current understanding.
2.3 Research gaps
The preceding literature review demonstrates how complex network theory has established a
solidtheoreticalfoundationforunderstandingriskpropagationinsupplychainnetworks.This
theoretical framework provides an effective analytical tool for examining complex interactive
behaviors and decision-making processes within supply chain networks while offering a
scientiﬁcbasisforstrategyoptimization.Giventhisstudy’sfocusonload-capacitymodels,the
comparative analysis presented in Table 1 extends to other models, with discussions limited
to their recovery strategies, and reveals three signiﬁcant limitations in existing research.
The ﬁrst major limitation concerns the treatment of failure modes. Most existing studies
consider only a single type of node failure, such as overload failure or underload failure,
thereby neglecting the coupled dynamics between them. This simpliﬁcation fails to capture
the intricate real-world scenarios where ﬁrms simultaneously face capacity bottlenecks and
demand shortfalls within supply chain networks. To address this limitation, the present study
develops an integrated framework encompassing both overload and underload failure modes
to simulate their concurrent evolution.
The second limitation stems from oversimpliﬁed heterogeneous failure characteristics.
Existing models typically deﬁne failure thresholds and probabilities as linear functions of
node degree, initial load, or capacity, neglecting crucial ﬁrm-speciﬁc attributes (e.g., size and
adaptive ﬂexibility). These formulations fail to distinguish between ﬁrms with identical struc-
tural positions but different operational robustness (e.g., a well-managed vs. poorly-managed
supplier with the same degree). To overcome this limitation, the present study deﬁnes them
as the nonlinear functions of initial load, node strength, and adjustment parameters, enabling
more realistic representation of heterogeneous network environments.

3 Methods
3.1 Network construction and structural metrics
Supply chain networks exhibit directed and weighted properties, characterized by the ﬂow
of diverse commodities with varying volumes from upstream to downstream ﬁrms. Drawing
upon complex network theory, this study constructs a directed and weighted network model,
represented as G(V, E, W, A). Speciﬁcally, G denotes the supply chain network, and the set
of nodes V = {vi} represents all participating ﬁrms. E = {ei j} is the set of edges, where ei j
represents a commodity ﬂow from ﬁrm vi to vj. W = {wi j} is the weight matrix for the supply
chain network, with the element wij representing the volume of commodities sold from vi to
vj and reﬂecting the business relationship strength between the two ﬁrms. A = {ai j} is the
adjacency matrix, where the element aij = 1 indicates a directed business relationship from
vi to vj; otherwise,aij = 0.
Access to inter-ﬁrm business data faces signiﬁcant practical limitations, including con-
ﬁdentiality concerns, access restrictions, and data integration difﬁculties (Ducharme et al.,
2024). To investigate risk propagation and response strategies in supply chain networks, this
study employs simulated networks while maintaining the generalizability of the ﬁndings.
Previous studies have demonstrated that supply chain networks exhibit scale-free structural
characteristics (Huang et al., 2024), wherein most nodes tend to form links with a few hub
nodes (Xie et al., 2023), and that the node degree follows a power-law distribution (Hearn-
shaw & Wilson, 2013). The BA network model, proposed by Barabasi and Albert (1999),
explains the formation mechanism of scale-free networks, which follow growth patterns and
preferential attachment. Leveraging these theoretical foundations, this study employs the
following evolution algorithm to generate directed and weighted supply chain networks:

3.2 Improved load capacity model
Previous studies have typically categorized the status of nodes in supply chain networks into
three states: normal, risk, and failure (Mu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Zhong & Liu,
2024). The normal state indicates that a ﬁrm can effectively manage various uncertainties
and conduct business activities, with its load within the production and supply constraints

referred to as node load capacity. When the load exceeds the upper limit of the overload
capacity or falls below the lower limit of the underload capacity, the node transitions from
the normal state to the risk state of overload or underload, respectively. Firms in the risk
state operate abnormally in production or sales: inventory accumulation indicates overload
(a demand-side excess problem), while raw material shortages signal underload (a supply-
side scarcity problem). With the deterioration of the operational issues, ﬁrms with node loads
exceeding the upper overload limit or falling below the lower underload limit cannot maintain
their abnormal operating state, ultimately transitioning to a failure state.
During normal operations, ﬁrms face two scenarios: maintaining existing operational
strategies unchanged, or adjusting strategies within their individual abilities to preemptively
respond to potential risk shocks. The latter scenario, involving load capacity range adjust-
ments, has not been fully explored in previous studies. Therefore, this study proposes an
improved load capacity model with four node states, aiming to more accurately depict the
statetransitionprocess,loadredistributionmechanism,andcascadingfailureprocessofnodes

among the four node states is deﬁned by dividing the existing normal operation state into two
distinct states: normal and adaptive. Speciﬁcally, when ﬁrms under normal operations proac-
tively adjust their operational strategies (i.e., adopt adaptive recovery strategies) to address
potential risk challenges in advance, they transition from a normal state to an adaptive state.

validated by Spieske et al. (2022), instead of immediately entering a failure state (Mu et al.,
2024). To illustrate the load bounds triggering state changes, several deﬁnitions are proposed

3.2.2 Load redistribution mechanism
In the supply chain network, the failure of a node leads to load redistribution to maintain the
balance and normal operation of the system, a phenomenon empirically observed by Carvalho
et al. (2021). As illustrated in Fig. 2, the failure of node vi at time t leads to the redistribution
of loads on the corresponding edges ehi, eh’i, eij, and eij’, and thus the upstream nodes vh,

upstream ﬁrms may face the challenge of excess inventory and need to re-evaluate and adjust

need to seek additional supply from other operating suppliers due to supply shortages.

3.3 Risk response strategies of firms
In complex economic and social systems, ﬁrms are exposed to shock impacts from various
known and potentially anticipated risks and must adaptively adjust their operational strate-
gies to navigate the continuously evolving environment and maintain normal operations.
Firms in the risk state must implement risk recovery strategies to transition from abnormal
operations to normal operations (Fu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2024; Shi et al.,
2021b). It is noteworthy that, even though ﬁrms can maintain normal operations in adaptive
states, they have already perceived the load change trends brought about by indirect shocks,
necessitating the implementation of adaptive recovery strategies. Therefore, to enable ﬂexi-
ble responses to risks, this study proposes a comprehensive strategy framework for ﬁrms in
the supply chain network, including adaptive recovery strategies and risk recovery strategies.
At its core, the framework emphasizes adaptive features rooted in a system’s inherent adapt-
ability, addressing uncertainty through adaptiveness and ﬂexibility rather than relying solely
on redundancy. This design corresponds to the adaptation-based framework for supply chain
resilience proposed by Ivanov (2025). Unlike traditional reactive recovery strategies, which
are post-disruption responses triggered by actual load variations (Laari et al., 2024), adap-
tive recovery strategies are characterized by anticipatory actions based on forward-looking
risk assessment and manifest as ex-ante strategic positioning rather than mere operational
ﬂexibility (Ivanov, 2025; Ivanov & Dolgui, 2025).
3.3.1 Risk recovery strategies
When ﬁrms in the supply chain network enter an overload or underload risk state, they seek
assistance from other ﬁrms to resume normal operations. Existing partners are prioritized over
ﬁrms without direct business relationships for capacity expansion due to established trust,
shorter response times, and lower cooperation costs (Li et al., 2024). Thus, ﬁrms in overload
risk states collaborate with downstream partners to alleviate pressure, avoiding bankruptcy
caused by product accumulation, sudden surges in inventory pressure, or cash ﬂow dis-
ruptions. Conversely, when facing underload risk, ﬁrms seek help from upstream partners to
procure additional raw materials or semi-ﬁnished products, aiming to prevent production line
shutdowns and subsequent consequences such as delayed deliveries, substantial liquidated
damages, loss of market share, or even bankruptcy. Notably, when selecting from existing
partners, ﬁrms prioritize those with substantial redundant capacity (Mu et al., 2024; Zhang
et al., 2024a). Redundant capacity includes resources such as spare labor, backup machinery,
and extra inventory, quantiﬁed by the difference between a ﬁrm’s capacity upper limit and
real-time load (Wang & Zhang, 2018). Speciﬁcally, this study uses the difference between
the adaptive overload upper limit and real-time load for quantiﬁcation.

3.4 Cascading failure process
By incorporating the node state transition process, load redistribution mechanisms, and risk
response strategies of enterprises deﬁned in Sect. 3.1–3.3, the cascading failure process,
empirically validated by Moetz et al. (2019), can be systematically executed through the
following steps, as illustrated in Fig. 4. Pseudocode is presented in the Section S3 of the
Supplementary Information.
Step 1: Initialization. At time t = 0, the supply chain network is initialized by setting
nodes, edges, node initial loads, capacities, and other relevant parameters.
Step 2: Implementation of shocks. At time t + 1, a speciﬁc network node is subjected to
shocks, causing it to fail based on the set parameters. In particular, two shock scenarios are
considered: random shocks and targeted shocks. In the scenario of random shocks, nodes
to be shocked are selected randomly with uniform probability. In the scenario of targeted
shocks, nodes are targeted based on their degree centrality, prioritizing those with a higher
node degree.
Step 3: Load redistribution. The failed nodes’ loads are redistributed to their upstream and
downstream neighboring nodes according to the predeﬁned redistribution mechanisms.
Step 4: Updating neighboring node states. The states of nodes are identiﬁed by comparing
their updated loads with their respective capacity thresholds. Based on the failure probability,
the nodes in the overload or underload state implement risk recovery strategies; nodes in the
failed state lead to a subsequent cascading iteration, which returns to Step 3.
Step 5: Recovery assessment. A) The outcomes of nodes implementing risk recovery
strategies are assessed. If recovery is successful, the process continues to update loads for
remaining nodes; if unsuccessful, the process returns to Step 3 with current load values for
cascading continuation. B) The states of nodes within a two-hop distance from the shocks
are identiﬁed. For nodes in adaptive states, the adaptive recovery strategies are implemented
to adjust their capacity limits. The load of the remaining nodes is updated in the next time
period.
Step 6: Stopping iteration. The cascading failure process stops when all nodes have failed
or no more nodes fail.

et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021) and to reﬂect the network resilience to shocks. The metric is

4 Simulations and results
To ensure the generalizability of the results despite limited access to real supply chain data,
this study employs simulated networks. Following established research demonstrating scale-
free characteristics of supply chains (Hearnshaw & Wilson, 2013), the BA network model
(Barabasi & Albert, 1999) is introduced in Sect. 3.1 to simulate cascading failures.
Using two simulated BA networks, this study examines cascading failure processes under
two realistic shock scenarios: random and targeted shocks. A comparative analysis of network
resilience under targeted shocks is provided across three conditions: (1) no recovery, (2) risk
recovery strategies only, and (3) combined adaptive and risk recovery strategies. Additionally,
the effects of six parameters identiﬁed in the model proposed in Sect. 3 on supply chain
network resilience is investigated. To minimize potential biases from any single network,
simulations for each set of parameter settings are performed 20 times independently. Each
simulation is conducted on a distinct network with the same density. The reported results
represent the average of these 20 outcomes, thereby ensuring that the ﬁndings reﬂect the
inherent properties of networks rather than the idiosyncratic characteristics of a speciﬁc
random instance.

4.4.1 Adjustment parameter for initial load (α)
To investigate the sensitivity of network resilience to parameters under targeted shocks, this

5 Discussion and implications
5.1 Forging an integrated adaptive-reactive risk management framework
The simulation results highlight the necessity of establishing a comprehensive risk manage-
ment framework for ﬁrms, incorporating both forward-looking risk warning mechanisms and
dual-track parallel risk response mechanisms. As illustrated in Fig. 9, the synergistic applica-
tion of adaptive and risk recovery strategies proposed in this study signiﬁcantly strengthens
supply chain network resilience, particularly in dense networks. Firms should conduct proac-
tive, indirect risk analyses and establish ﬂexible, adjustable operating frameworks for daily
operations, enabling preemptive operational strategy adjustments that enhance adaptability
to potential risks. Simultaneously, ﬁrms should foster strategic alignment in resource shar-
ing, information exchange, and complementary capabilities by establishing stable, long-term
collaborative mechanisms with upstream and downstream supply chain partners. This align-
ment facilitates rapid resource mobilization across partner networks when confronting direct
risks, supporting effective risk recovery strategies. The integration of proactive adaptation and
reactive measures enables ﬁrms to prevent and address operational risks while demonstrating
resilience during shocks, aligning with the ﬁndings of Ivanov (2024b).
5.2 Emphasizing the impact of initial network characteristics on strategies
The initial characteristics of the supply chain network, particularly load distribution balance
and ﬁrm capacity redundancy levels, play crucial roles in the effectiveness of comprehen-
sive recovery strategies. As shown in Fig. 12, increasing parameter α exacerbates load
imbalance among nodes, thereby reducing network resilience, particularly in sparse net-
works. These ﬁndings underscore the importance of balanced load distribution in mitigating
systemic risks arising from excessive load concentration. Policy-makers and industry asso-
ciations should employ policy tools to promote balanced ﬁrm development within supply
chain networks while pursuing industrial efﬁciency. Concurrently, ﬁrms, especially those in
core positions, should develop ecological platforms and strengthen partnerships to enhance
network resilience while pursuing their own growth, thereby achieving long-term strategic
value.
Furthermore, an increase in parameter β signiﬁcantly expands the range of normal opera-
tional loads for ﬁrms, thereby improving overall network resilience, as illustrated in Fig. 14.
However, the marginal effect of increasing capacity redundancy on network resilience
123

Annals of Operations Research
depends signiﬁcantly on network structure: in dense networks, the beneﬁts diminish over
time, whereas in sparse networks, the impact follows a nonlinear pattern, initially increasing
before declining. This distinction offers dual insights into risk management practices. Firms
should enhance capacity redundancy ﬂexibility through dynamic production scale adjust-
ments and optimized inventory buffer allocation during normal operations while minimizing
efﬁciency losses caused by excessive redundancy in dense networks. Policy-makers, on the
other hand, should offer targeted support for critical ﬁrms to expand capacity redundancy in
sparse networks. This approach enables the striking of an optimal balance between enhancing
risk prevention capabilities and resource investment.
5.3 Implementing adaptive recovery strategies with differentiation principles
from network-level perspective
From a network-level perspective, the implementation of adaptive recovery strategies should
follow differentiation principles across two critical dimensions: strategic targeting of core
ﬁrms and management of inter-ﬁrm capability differentials. As demonstrated in Fig. 10,
implementing adaptive recovery strategies universally across nodes proves suboptimal. Net-
work resilience achieves optimization when these strategies are implemented by the top 5%
of ﬁrms with the highest node degrees. Figure 11 further illustrates that the marginal beneﬁts
to network resilience diminish as implementation extends to nodes with decreasing degrees.
Consequently, under resource constraints, industry associations and policy administrators
should focus support on high-degree core ﬁrms for adaptive recovery strategy adoption.
This approach leverages the regulatory inﬂuence of core ﬁrms while avoiding the "fallacy of
composition" that could arise from widespread implementation.
Moreover, the adjustment parameter for node strength ε demonstrates a non-linear rela-
tionship between node strength and adaptation/risk tolerance capabilities. As illustrated in
Fig. 17, increasing ε values amplify the heterogeneity in failure probability and adaptive
state probability across ﬁrms, resulting in network resilience that initially strengthens before
deteriorating. Therefore, industry associations and policy administrators should provide cal-
ibrated support to core ﬁrms to moderately optimize the regulatory and tolerance capability
differentials between high-strength and low-strength ﬁrms and foster complementary rela-
tionships between core and non-core ﬁrms, consistent with the recommendations of Zhu
et al. (2023). This approach leverages the leadership potential of high-strength ﬁrms and
prevents the adverse effects of the competitive dynamics arising from the adaptive recovery
strategies implemented universally across all ﬁrms. However, careful monitoring is essential
to prevent excessive capability gaps that could compromise low-strength ﬁrms’ self-recovery
capabilities and overall network stability.
5.4 Implementing adaptive recovery strategy under the equilibrium principle
from a firm-level perspective
From a ﬁrm-level perspective, the effective implementation of adaptive recovery strategies
requires focus on three key dimensions under the equilibrium principle: sensitivity to poten-
tial risks, network density, and adjustment of capacity limits. Network density signiﬁcantly
moderates strategy effectiveness. As illustrated in Fig. 16, identical adaptive recovery strate-
gies yield varying outcomes across networks of different densities. This necessitates that

ﬁrms formulate strategies based on precise network environment assessments and imple-
ment differentiated approaches aligned with network structural characteristics, consistent
with the ﬁndings of Zhang et al., (2024a) and Ma et al. (2025).
Firms should maintain moderate sensitivity to potentialrisks, especially in dense networks.
Increases in parameter γ expand the load range for ﬁrms in adaptive states, enhancing risk
sensitivity and promoting operational strategy adjustments over steady-state maintenance. In
dense networks, excessive sensitivity to potential risks may trigger frequent operational strat-
egy adjustments, initiating large-scale load redistribution that undermine network resilience.
Conversely, in sparse networks, the high sensitivity and rapid adjustment capabilities of core
ﬁrms are essential for network stability. These ﬁndings also underscore the critical role of
industry associations and policy-makers in providing market guidance to help ﬁrms optimize
the balance between operational stability and adaptive ﬂexibility.
Regarding the intensity of adaptive recovery, parameter τ, representing ﬁrms’ capacity
to adjust capacity limits through mechanisms such as facility leasing and production expan-
sion, exhibits a non-linear relationship with network resilience, as illustrated in Fig. 18. As τ
increases, network resilience initially strengthens before deteriorating. This pattern indicates
that ﬁrms should carefully calibrate the intensity of adjustments when enhancing adaptive
recovery capabilities to avoid excessive changes that may undermine resource allocation efﬁ-
ciency and network resilience, aligning with the suggestions of Wang et al. (2024). Notably,
numerous factors inﬂuence τ. Therefore, in practical management, a systematic empirical
framework is essential: multi-source data collection combined with multi-level comprehen-
sive evaluation methods should be employed to thoroughly assess and precisely estimate
the parameter range, thereby determining a reasonable interval. This approach facilitates the
characterization of the redundancy costs and their resulting resilience beneﬁts.
5.5 Enhancing firm tolerance to abnormal operating conditions
Enhancing ﬁrm tolerance to abnormal operating conditions is crucial for the effective imple-
mentation of risk recovery strategies. Figure 15 reveals that increasing parameter δ effectively
reduces the failure probability of ﬁrms and improves their recovery potential from risk
states, thereby signiﬁcantly enhancing network resilience. Based on these ﬁndings, ﬁrms
should adopt multiple measures to strengthen their tolerance to abnormal operating condi-
tions, including implementing ﬂexible resource allocation strategies, enhancing supply chain
ﬂexibility, and establishing robust emergency response mechanisms, aligning with the rec-
ommendations of Bussieweke et al. (2024).
Meanwhile, industry associations and policy-makers need to establish scientiﬁc evaluation
standard systems to help ﬁrms objectively assess and accurately predict their performance
underabnormalconditions.Furthermore,buildingindustrycooperationplatformsisessential,
as they will help organize and guide other ﬁrms within the industry to provide collaborative
support to those in risk states. Through such multi-dimensional collaborative mechanisms,
not only can the risk tolerance of individual ﬁrms be enhanced, but the overall resilience of
the supply chain network can also be improved, thereby achieving sustainable development
of the industry, consistent with the ﬁndings of Zhao et al. (2019).

6 Conclusion and future work
This study addresses the critical issue of cascading failures in supply chain networks, which
can lead to widespread disruptions and substantial economic losses. By reﬁning the clas-
siﬁcation of ﬁrm states and incorporating both underload and overload failure modes, the
proposed extended load-capacity model captures the complex dynamics of risk propagation
in supply chain networks. Moreover, the integration of proactive adaptive recovery strate-
gies and reactive risk recovery strategies provides a comprehensive framework for ﬁrms to
enhance their resilience against various disruptions.
Through extensive simulations of supply chain networks with different structural char-
acteristics under random and targeted shock scenarios, this study uncovers several key
ﬁndings. First, the synergistic application of adaptive and risk recovery strategies signiﬁ-
cantly improves supply chain network resilience, particularly in dense networks. Second, the
initial network characteristics, such as load distribution balance and ﬁrm capacity redundancy
levels, play crucial roles in determining the effectiveness of the proposed strategies. Third, the
implementation of adaptive recovery strategies should follow differentiation principles based
on the strategic importance of ﬁrms and their capability differentials. Fourth, ﬁrms should
carefully calibrate their sensitivity to potential risks, consider network density, and adjust
capacity limits to optimize the effectiveness of adaptive recovery strategies. Finally, enhanc-
ing ﬁrm tolerance to abnormal operating conditions is vital for the successful implementation
of risk recovery strategies.
These ﬁndings offer valuable insights and practical implications for ﬁrms, policy-makers,
and industry associations seeking to enhance supply chain resilience. By establishing com-
prehensive risk management frameworks, optimizing network characteristics, strategically
implementingadaptiveandriskrecoverystrategies,andstrengtheningﬁrmtolerancetoabnor-
malconditions,stakeholderscansigniﬁcantlyimprovetheresilienceofsupplychainnetworks
against cascading failures.
Thisstudyhasseverallimitationsthatalsosuggestpromisingdirectionsforfutureresearch.
First, although the proposed model extends beyond extreme overload failure situations by
incorporating buffer strategies analogous to queuing mechanisms through adaptive load
adjustment, it lacks explicit conﬁgurations for queuing buffers. This omission may increase
the impact of individual node failures during the dynamic process, potentially resulting in
more conservative mitigation strategies. Therefore, the current model serves as a foundational
framework for future studies to explicitly incorporate queuing dynamics, thereby enabling
a more comprehensive response to both normal operational ﬂuctuations and signiﬁcant risk
shocks. Second, while redundancy costs are implicitly considered through adjustment param-
eter τ, explicitly modeling these costs remains an important direction for subsequent research
to enhance resilience assessment. Third, empirical validation is limited by the availability of
actual business data, largely due to conﬁdentiality constraints around inter-ﬁrm transactions
and the long temporal horizon of cascading failures. Upon gaining access to commercial
data, future research should prioritize reﬁning feedback regulatory mechanisms driven by
demand-side changes within complex load-capacity models and estimating optimal param-
eters for implementing recovery strategies. Additionally, case study analyses could further
strengthen model validation. Addressing these limitations will advance the understanding
and management of complex supply chain networks in an era of increasing disruptions.
