Abstract
Lot scheduling problems are a significant focus in scheduling theory due to their broad ap­
plications and effect on operational efficiency. Traditionally, research in this field assumes 
knowledgeable scheduler and truthful agents. However, this study delves into a more real­
istic scenario where these assumptions are challenged, proposing a game theory approach 
to handle the complexities arising from incomplete information and strategic behavior. We 
examine lot scheduling scenarios with uniform capacities for lots comprising orders of 
varying sizes. Departing from the conventional paradigm, we introduce the concept of a 
scheduler with limited information and agents prone to providing misleading information 
for personal gain. We investigate five fundamental objective functions in lot scheduling: 
(i) minimizing the completion time of the last job exiting the system, (ii) minimizing the 
total completion time, (iii) minimizing the total weighted completion time, (iv) minimiz­
ing the number of tardy orders, and (v) minimizing the total weighted number of tardy 
orders. Notably, we show that problems (i) to (iv) can be efficiently solved in polynomial 
time, while problem (v) is solvable in pseudo-polynomial time. Our research advances 
the understanding of decentralized scheduling, where agents’ behavior and information 
gaps affect decision-making. It underscores the need to address real-world complexities in 
scheduling theory and provides insights for designing adaptable algorithms.

1  Introduction
In the intricate domain of scheduling theory, allocating resources and sequencing tasks 
represent a pivotal area of study with profound implications across various sectors. Effi­
cient scheduling plays a foundational role in optimizing operational processes. Moreover, 
efficient scheduling is a linchpin for enhancing productivity, minimizing costs, and meet­
ing stringent deadlines. The significance of scheduling theory transcends its mere applica­
tion in manufacturing and production environments, extending its influence into diverse 
domains such as transportation logistics, healthcare systems, information technology, and 
project management. Recent advancements explore increasingly complex scheduling set­
tings, including multi-agent environments with competing objectives (Xu et al., 2023), and 
dynamic, partially observable systems in uncertain domains, where reinforcement learning 
strategies such as Deep Recurrent Q-Learning are employed to construct adaptive schedul­
ing policies (Rangel-Martinez & Ricardez-Sandoval, 2024). As the heartbeat of organiza­
tional efficiency, an adept understanding of scheduling theory empowers decision-makers 
to navigate complex resource allocation challenges, mitigate bottlenecks, and design robust 
systems capable of adapting to dynamic demands.
A specific class of scheduling theory is the lot scheduling problems in which orders are 
grouped into lots, and decisions are made regarding when each lot should be processed 
and on which machines. Lot scheduling is a crucial component of production planning and 
control, aiming to strike a balance between achieving high efficiency and meeting customer 
demands. In lot scheduling, an added challenge arises from the possibility of splitting orders 
across multiple lots or machines, a constraint layered onto the standard considerations of 
regular scheduling, which include machine capacities, processing times, due dates, and spe­
cific requirements of the production process. Recent research has introduced polynomial-
time algorithms for identifying all Pareto-optimal schedules in bicriteria lot scheduling 
problems with splitable jobs on single parallel-batch machines (Shen et al., 2025), while 
mixed-integer linear programming approaches have also been proposed to jointly minimize 
maximum lateness and total tardiness in green logistics-inspired lot scheduling environ­
ments (Liu et al., 2024). From the perspective of production continuity, the method of group­
ing a specified quantity of products into a single batch for production is referred to as Lot 
Production. This approach is particularly effective in scenarios where demand is insufficient 
for continuous production, yet not so low as to justify producing items on a per-order basis. 
When considering this production method in terms of order processing, it is applicable to 
both Make-to-Stock (MTS) and Make-to-Order (MTO) systems. However, from the product 
type and quantity standpoint, Lot Production is classified as medium-mix, medium-volume 
production. In such cases, enhancing the operational availability of machinery and facilities 
is essential due to the intermittent nature of batch production. Consequently, less frequent 
logistics operations are required, often resulting in larger lot sizes, potentially leading to 
excessive inventory. Therefore, a critical challenge in Lot Production lies in minimizing lot 
sizes and optimizing production efficiency by reducing preparation times.
In contrast to conventional challenges in scheduling theory, where the scheduler pos­
sesses complete knowledge of the entire set of orders and associated constraints, this study 
introduces a novel approach by exploring decentralized scheduling environments where 
the scheduler lacks complete information (referred to as being ‘ignorant’) and agents may 
intentionally provide misleading information. Importantly, our model does not assume

or permit the scheduler to act dishonestly; rather, the scheduler is unaware of key order-
specific parameters and must rely on the information provided by the agents, who may 
behave strategically. For example, in large-scale manufacturing systems, individual produc­
tion managers may withhold or distort information about their line’s capabilities to secure 
favorable scheduling slots, leading to inefficiencies. Similarly, suppliers might exaggerate 
lead times to prioritize their orders in global supply chain management, creating a decentral­
ized scheduling problem rife with information asymmetry and strategic behavior. Another 
instance can be found in project management within IT, where various teams may provide 
conflicting information to push their tasks ahead, further complicating scheduling. In such 
distributed scheduling, multiple autonomous agents, each representing different entities or 
tasks, make decisions independently based on local information without centralized coordi­
nation or access to the global state of the system. This decentralized nature introduces chal­
lenges such as information asymmetry and strategic behavior among agents. Agents may 
misrepresent their preferences, capabilities, or constraints to influence scheduling decisions 
in their favor, leading to inefficiencies or unfair outcomes in the overall scheduling process. 
Moreover, the scheduler may lack complete visibility into the actions and intentions of 
individual agents, further complicating the task of coordinating schedules optimally. Recent 
works have explored both cooperative and noncooperative decentralized models to address 
these challenges. For example, (Alon & Anily, 2023) analyze parallel machines schedul­
ing game where multiple manufacturers collaborate under a cooperative game framework, 
proving the existence of a stable cost allocation within the core that supports coalition sta­
bility in distributed production environments. (Xiong et al., 2024) develop a consensus-
based decentralized scheduling algorithm for energy trading in seaport virtual power plants, 
demonstrating how privacy-preserving optimization can enhance operational efficiency 
across independently managed agents. Similarly, (Xu et al., 2024) propose a model-assisted 
multi-agent reinforcement learning method to coordinate decision-making across bidding, 
re-dispatching, and disaggregation stages in decentralized virtual power plant operations. 
Addressing these challenges requires the development of robust mechanisms and algo­
rithms that can effectively handle incomplete or unreliable information, incentivize truthful 
reporting by agents, and ensure fairness and efficiency in scheduling outcomes. Techniques 
such as mechanism design and game theory play crucial roles in designing decentralized 
scheduling algorithms that can navigate the complexities of information asymmetry and 
strategic behavior among autonomous agents (Luo et al., 2021; Heydenreich et al., 2007).
Our study, thus, introduces a paradigm shift by constraining the scheduler’s information 
capabilities. Here, we posit a scenario wherein the scheduler’s awareness of various orders 
and their constraints is limited. Specifically, the only means by which the scheduler gains 
knowledge about diverse orders is through direct communication with the agents. Further­
more, we depart from the conventional assumption that all agents, including the scheduler, 
adhere to truth-telling practices; our model allows agents to provide inaccurate information 
intentionally. The scheduler’s need to rely on information the agents convey presents novel 
and intriguing challenges that have yet to be thoroughly investigated. This departure from 
the traditional model opens avenues for exploring the dynamics of knowledge exchange and 
the implications of potential misinformation in the scheduling process.
This research makes significant mathematical contributions by introducing a mechanism 
that incentivizes truthfulness among agents, ensuring that honest reporting becomes a domi­
nant strategy. To the best of our knowledge, no one has applied the Vickrey-Clarke-Groves

(VCG) mechanism in the field of lot scheduling. Additionally, we analyze five fundamental 
objective functions in scheduling theory, demonstrating that four can be efficiently solved 
in polynomial time, while the fifth is solvable in pseudo-polynomial time. This research not 
only advances the understanding of decentralized scheduling but also provides a foundation 
for future exploration in addressing real-world complexities, such as incomplete informa­

nal ideas that enhance the field’s understanding of decentralized scheduling, where agents’ 
behavior and information gaps significantly affect decision-making.
We aim to address several specific research questions in this study: How do decentral­
ized scheduling environments, where agents possess local information and may strategi­
cally manipulate it, affect scheduling outcomes? What mechanisms and algorithms can 
effectively handle incomplete or unreliable information in distributed scheduling? How 
can truthfulness be incentivized among agents in decentralized scheduling environments? 
What are the implications of misinformation in scheduling processes, and how can they be 
mitigated?
To address those questions and build upon the above-mentioned fundamental assump­
tions, this paper introduces the rationale behind agents intentionally supplying false informa­
tion to the scheduler when given a chance. Following this, a set of scheduling mechanisms 
is introduced, illustrating that, under well-defined system rules, honest disclosure becomes 
a prevailing strategy for the agents. Additionally, an examination is conducted on each 
mechanism’s associated price of anarchy and stability. To substantiate the effectiveness of 
the proposed strategy, a compilation of numerical examples is provided, showcasing the 
resilience and efficiency of the suggested mechanisms.
The main novel contributions of this paper are threefold. First, it introduces a new 
paradigm in scheduling theory by modeling a setting in which the scheduler has limited 
knowledge and agents may intentionally misreport information-a scenario reflective of 
real-world strategic behavior and information asymmetry. Second, it applies the Vickrey-
Clarke-Groves (VCG) mechanism to the lot scheduling domain for the first time, devel­
oping strategy-proof mechanisms across five foundational scheduling objectives. Third, 
it provides formal proofs and algorithmic solutions demonstrating that these mechanisms 
incentivize truthful reporting and lead to socially optimal outcomes. These contributions 
advance both the theoretical foundations and practical applicability of decentralized sched­
uling under strategic manipulation.
The paper is organized as follows: Sect. 2 reviews the relevant work. Section 3 presents 
our model. Section 4 addresses the naïve cases. In Sect. 5, we provide and analyze several 
scheduling mechanisms. In Sect. 6, we discuss the issues of the truthfulness of agents, the 
price of anarchy, and the price of stability. Section 7 provides numerical examples demon­
strating an agent’s incentive to lie to the scheduler. The last section, Sect. 8, has the conclu­
sion and ideas for future research.

2.1  Lot scheduling
The first relevant paper on the setting of lot scheduling studied here is that of (Hou et 
al., 2014). They researched the problem of minimizing total completion time on a single 
machine, given (i) identical lots (both in terms of size and processing time) and (ii) allow­
ing splitting, i.e., a setting in which an order can be split between two consecutive lots. 
They proved that sorting the orders according to the known Shortest Processing Time (SPT) 
policy is optimal. (Zhang et al., 2019) extended the problem to minimize the total weighted 
completion time and total weighted discounted completion time. Given that order splitting 
is allowed, they proved that the Weighted Shortest Processing Time (WSPT) first policy is 
optimal for both cases. (Mor 2021) studied lot scheduling with position-dependent lot pro­
cessing times. The scheduling measure was minimum total weighted completion time and 
three special cases: minimum makespan, minimum total completion time, and minimum 
linear of their combination. All problems were shown to have polynomial time solutions. 
(Mor & Mosheiov, 2021) studied lot scheduling with Just-In-Time objectives, assuming 
the common due date and common due window are decision variables. Polynomial-time 
dynamic programming algorithms were introduced when the scheduling measures consist 
of order earliness and tardiness, and the due-date (-window) cost and order-splitting are 
allowed. (Mor et al., 2021) investigated lot scheduling problems with the option of order 
rejection, assuming an upper bound on the maximal permitted rejection cost. Pseudo-poly­
nomial dynamic programming algorithms were introduced for three NP-hard problems: 
minimum makespan, minimum total completion time, and minimum total weighted comple­
tion time. (Chen et al., 2022) extended the setting that (Zhang et al., 2019) studied to the 
case that lots may have different capacities. They proved that when minimizing the total 
weighted completion time and total weighted discounted completion time, WSPT remains 
the optimal policy. (Biber et al., 2023) studied lot scheduling involving completion time 
problems, with consecutive or parallel splitting models, on identical parallel machines and 
provided exact and linear-time approximation algorithms.
Researchers and practitioners in operations research and production management explore 
different algorithms, heuristics, and optimization models to address the complexities of lot 
scheduling problems and improve the overall performance of manufacturing systems, e.g., 
(Yang et al., 2017) focused on the same setting as (Hou et al., 2014), assuming that splitting 
is not allowed. They proved that this problem is NP-hard in the strong sense and introduced 
and tested a binary integer programming model and four heuristics. The heuristic based 
on the binary integer program with a (reasonable) bound on the running time was proved 
to provide the best results. (Zheng & Jin, 2019) returned to the objective function of mini­
mum total completion time with no splitting. They introduced a new heuristic and showed 
numerically that it performs better than the heuristics proposed so far. (Mor et al., 2020) 
considered the measure of a minimum number of tardy orders (with order splitting), a mini­

mum weighted number of tardy orders (assuming order splitting), and a minimum number 
of tardy orders (assuming no order splitting). The first problem was shown to be polynomi­
ally solvable; the second was proved to be NP-hard, and a pseudo-polynomial algorithm 
was introduced and tested. The third was proved to be strongly NP-hard, and a heuristic

and Earliest Due Date first (EDD), respectively.
2.2  Game theory
Game theory is a formal analytical and conceptual framework equipped with mathematical 
tools, enabling studying complex interactions (Fudenberg & Tirole, 1991). More specifi­
cally, it is a branch within artificial intelligence that provides the necessary tools for analyz­
ing and navigating situations in which players or agents make interdependent decisions. 
This interdependence forces players to consider others’ potential strategies during their 
decision-making process. Game theory encompasses two primary branches: cooperative 
(Ravula et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021) and non-cooperative (Alkoby et al., 2017; Marden 
et al., 2010). Cooperative game theory involves a group of agents collaborating to achieve 
a shared objective. In contrast, non-cooperative game theory delves into competitive social 
interactions characterized by winners and losers, exemplified by scenarios like the pris­
oner’s dilemma.
The field of game theory has been widely explored and enriched by a multitude of stud­
ies across various domains, providing valuable insights into strategic decision-making and 
interactions among rational agents. The foundational work of (Von Neumann, 1947) has 
provided a solid basis for comprehending both cooperative and non-cooperative games. In 
the domain of repeated games, the work of (Aumann & Shapley, 1994), has been influential 
in understanding the dynamics of repeated interactions among rational players. Further­
more, Schelling’s work (Schelling, 1958) has significantly contributed to examining strate­
gic interactions and introducing the concept of focal points within game situations.
Game theory has found applications in various fields, and its relevance extends to eco­
nomics (Roth & Wilson, 2019; Nash, 1950), political science (Wolford, 2019), biology 
(Axelrod & Hamilton, 1981; McNamara & Leimar, 2020), psychology (Battigalli & Duf­
wenberg, 2020), and computer science (Hirschprung & Alkoby, 2022; Roughgarden, 2010). 
Recently, game theory has become a central tool in designing and analyzing communication 
systems (Mkiramweni et al., 2019; Yan et al., 2018).
2.3  Game theory in the context of scheduling theory
The intersection of game theory and scheduling theory has become a fertile ground for 
research, offering a nuanced perspective on decision-making processes in complex, dynamic 
environments. Game theory has found application in scheduling scenarios where entities 
interact and compete for resources or influence outcomes. The strategic interactions among 
agents in a scheduling context often involve conflicting objectives, requiring a delicate bal­
ance between cooperation and competition. Integrating game theory into scheduling theory

Annals of Operations Research
provides a robust analytical foundation for understanding decision-making dynamics in 
systems characterized by interdependencies and resource constraints. This interdisciplinary 
research enhances our theoretical understanding of strategic decision-making in schedul­
ing and holds promise for informing practical applications across diverse domains such as 
manufacturing, transportation, and project management.
 (Ahmad et al., 2008) addresses the issue of power-aware task scheduling on hetero­
geneous and homogeneous multi-core processor architectures, a challenge not adequately 
addressed by conventional methodologies. Employing game theory, they conceptualize the 
problem as a cooperative game and convert it into a max-max-min problem. This transfor­
mation enables the generation of solutions characterized by swift turnaround times. (Li et 
al., 2012) tackles the multi-objective integrated process planning and scheduling (IPPS) 
problem. Utilizing Nash equilibrium to handle multiple objectives, they present a compel­
ling range of results that underscore the effectiveness of their developed approach. The work 
of (Yang et al., 2020) used game theory to simplify the complex problem of task scheduling 
in the cloud computing domain.
The most relevant work to ours is the study conducted by (Kovalyov & Pesch, 2014), 
which introduces a game mechanism for single-machine sequencing, specifically address­
ing scenarios with zero risk. The authors assumed that a group of non-cooperating clients 
(agents) compete on the usage of a shared resource, i.e., a single machine. Each agent is 
inspired to move their job to an earlier position in the sequence. The agents are assumed to 
avoid making a risky decision that can decrease their utility function. The studied problems 
were minimizing the total weighted completion time and the weighted number of tardy jobs, 
and a game mechanism was devised in which no agent has an incentive for claiming false 
costs. In this paper, we extend their work to the setting of lot scheduling and investigate 
several additional target functions considered the most basic functions of scheduling theory.

As opposed to traditional problems in scheduling theory in which the scheduler is a 
know-it-all entity that owns the knowledge regarding the entire set of orders to be performed 
and their constraints, we assume that the scheduler’s knowledge is limited in this study. In 
our research, we assume that the only way for the scheduler to know about the different 
orders, specifically their constraints (e.g., their due date), is to receive it directly from the 
agents. It is important to clarify that the scheduler is not modeled as a strategic or dishonest 
participant; rather, it is an uninformed decision-maker whose role is purely reactive to the 
information provided by the agents. Additionally, we relax the traditional assumption that 
all agents (including the scheduler) are truth-telling, i.e., agents in our model are allowed 
to lie. The fact that the knowledge needs to be brought to the scheduler through the agents 
themselves and that the information they provide is not necessarily correct raises new and 
exciting questions that have yet to be addressed. It might not be beneficial for the agents to 
tell the truth regarding characterizing their orders. This is inapplicable regarding the size of 
the orders, as the agents expect the delivery of their exact size of orders, and the scheduler 
must know the actual size to schedule it in the relevant lot properly. However, when it comes 
to its desired due date or order urgency (i.e., order weight, denoted as wj), in many cases, 
an agent will be interested in providing the scheduler with a different due date than its actual 
one in an attempt to avoid tardiness. Moreover, the scheduler himself might find it beneficial 
to lie regarding its processing time or the lot capacity.
Considering all the above assumptions, in this study, we aim to provide a strategy-proof 
mechanism, i.e., where all agents’ dominant strategy is to provide the scheduler with accu­
rate information (due date and weight). We consider the five scheduling theory fundamental 
problems, which are: (i) minimizing the completion time of the last job to leave the system, 
also known as makespan, which is given by Cmax; (ii) minimizing the sum of completion 
time, which is given by ∑Cj; (iii) minimizing the sum of weighted completion time, which 
is given by ∑wjCj; (iv) minimizing the number of tardy orders, which is given by ∑Uj; 
and (v) minimizing the weighted number of tardy orders, which is given by ∑wjUj.

4  The Naïve cases
Since, in our model, the scheduler is ignorant, the agent might be tempted to provide it with 
false information, e.g., a higher weight or a more urgent due date, to influence the scheduler 
to schedule their job earlier in the allocation. This section offers several numeric examples 
demonstrating how an agent can gain from lying to the scheduler. Note that this paper con­
siders the case in which orders can be split, i.e., if a part of the order is processed, it can be 
delivered even if the rest is not completed. If no splitting is allowed, the problem transforms 
into a bin packing problem, i.e., a strongly NP-complete problem.
Two exceptions in which the agent will not be interested in lying to the scheduler, even 
for the naïve case, are our first and second problems, i.e., Cmax and ∑Cj. In these cases, 
the sole input parameter is the order size, and offering incorrect sizes would be detrimental. 
Therefore, we will focus exclusively on addressing problems P3–P5 throughout this paper. 
Despite problems P1 and P2 typically requiring agents to communicate truthfully even in 
the naïve scenario, we have included them in our scope for this paper to provide a thorough 
solution to the five foundational scheduling core problems.

5  Formal analysis
The following section will provide and analyze several strategy-proof scheduling mecha­
nisms. Our set of solutions heavily relies on the Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG) mechanism. 
The VCG mechanism is a well-known mechanism used in mechanism design theory to 
achieve desirable outcomes in various settings, including auctions and resource allocation 
problems. It consists of three fundamental components: Vickrey auction, Clarke tax, and 
Groves mechanism. The Vickrey component, represented by the Vickrey auction, ensures 
that participants bid truthfully by making them pay only the externality they impose on oth­
ers, encouraging truthful valuation revelation. The Groves component incentivizes honest 
reporting by maximizing individual utility or minimizing individual costs when participants 
disclose their private information, promoting truthful revelation of preferences. Meanwhile, 
the Clarke component, incorporated by the Clarke tax concept, imposes a hypothetical pen­
alty on deviations from the socially optimal outcome, further reinforcing truthful behavior 
by discouraging strategic deviations. These components encourage participants to disclose 
accurate information, leading to more efficient outcomes in auctions, resource allocation, or

8  Conclusions and future work
This paper studies a special case of lot scheduling where the scheduler is not a know-it-all 
entity, and the agents are allowed to lie regarding the characteristics of their orders. In the 
paper, we reviewed the five most basic objective functions in scheduling theory, which are: 
Cmax, ∑Cj, ∑wjCj, ∑Uj, and ∑wjUj.
After demonstrating that for the naïve case, there are many incentives for the agents 
to lie, both regarding their job’s weight and their due date, we presented a strategy-proof 
mechanism that, if used by the scheduler, can prevent the agents from lying. Formal proofs 
were also presented, and a set of numerical examples demonstrated the superiority of our 
mechanism in incentivizing agents to tell the truth.
Overall, our analysis has shown that game-theoretic models can help address several 
challenges that might arise in real-life scenarios of scheduling problems, such as incomplete 
information, strategic behavior, and uncertainty. By incorporating strategic considerations 
into scheduling decisions, we can improve resource allocation’s efficiency and effectiveness 
while promoting fairness and reducing potential conflicts and disputes.
Furthermore, our findings suggest that the success of game-theoretic approaches in 
scheduling problems depends on several factors, including the level of competition among 
agents, the degree of uncertainty in the environment, and the availability of information. 
Future research should explore these factors further and develop more sophisticated game-
theoretic models that can accommodate a more comprehensive range of broader scheduling 
scenarios and decision-making contexts, e.g., studying the case where splitting is forbidden 
is an exciting direction for future research.
