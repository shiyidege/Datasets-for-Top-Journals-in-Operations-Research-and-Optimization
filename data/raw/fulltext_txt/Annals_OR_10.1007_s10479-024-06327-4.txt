Abstract
Outranking methods were developed for solving Multi-Criteria Decision-Making (MCDM)
problems with a ﬁnite set of alternatives. In this paper, we propose a comprehensive frame-
work that extends the application of outranking methods for solving MCDM problems with
continuous sets of alternatives. This is achieved through the integration of multi-objective
optimization methods and outranking methods. The derived Three-phases integration pro-
cedure is illustrated by the integration of the Weighted Sum method for continuous MCDM
problems and the outranking Preference Ranking Organization METHod for Enrichment
Evaluations (PROMETHEE) method. Moreover, the proposed framework incorporates the
use of the Principal Component Analysis (CPA) to address the problem of selecting a Pareto
optimal solution when the phase of application of the outranking method generates a large
set of ‘best’ Pareto optimal solutions. The application potential of the procedure is illustrated
by a multi-dimensional ﬁnancial portfolio selection problem.

Preferences Modelling

criteria is called Multiple Criteria Decision Making (MCDM). The formal study of MCDM
goes back to the works of the economists Edgeworth (1881) and Pareto (1897). However, the
real start of intensive research and application of MCDM occurred in the 60 s of the twentieth
century. Nowadays, MCDM is applied in almost all sectors of human activities such as
management, ﬁnance, engineering, sustainable development, and medicine, just to mention
a few (Greco et al., 2016; Mardani et al. 2015; Ramanathan et al., 2017; Zopounidis and
Doumpos, 2017; Ben Abdelaziz, et al. 2007). Interestingly, right at the inception of MCDM,
scholars noticed that there was a dichotomy. MCDM developed in two distinct categories,
Multiple Criteria Decision Aid (MCDA) and Multiple Objective Programming (MOP). We
will shortly refer to this dichotomy as MCDA/MOP. Any decision-making process with a
ﬁnite or inﬁnite set A of alternatives (decisions, options, course of actions, solutions, etc.)
with at least two criteria and at least one Decision Maker (DM) can be the subject of MCDA.
MCDA focuses on helping DM in ranking or sorting alternatives of set A.
Mathematical Programming (MP) is one of the most developed paradigms of operations
research as it deals with the decision-making process on how to use limited resources to
maximize proﬁt or minimize costs. Powerful and effective methods of solving MP were
developed such as the simplex method, Newton’s method, descent methods, and interior
point methods. It has been applied in a large spectrum of areas such as ﬁnance, health care,
industry, economics, production, and operations management. However, considering a single
objective function that aggregates all the criteria involved in the decision-making process is
a restriction that may lead to decisions that do not necessarily reﬂect the preferences of the
DM, especially when the criteria are conﬂicting or measured in different units. Therefore,
MOP came as a natural extension of MP. A MOP problem consists of at least two objective
functions to be minimized or maximized subject to some constraints expressed in the form of
equalities and/or inequalities. The concept of solution of MOP is Pareto optimality (Pareto,
1897).
Although both MCDA and MOP were developed to solve MCDM situations, they are
fundamentally different. In MOP, the set of alternatives, being deﬁned by inequalities and
equalities on a continuous set, is generally a continuous set, therefore, MCDA methods do
not apply to MOP as it is not possible to enumerate a continuum number of alternatives.
Conversely, MOP methods do not apply to MCDA as they require analytic properties such
as continuity, differentiability, and convexity which cannot be deﬁned on a discrete set of
alternatives, be it ﬁnite or inﬁnite countable. Another difference is that MCDA outranking
methods are based on pairwise comparison. They use and incorporate the DM’s preferences
to construct a binary relation, aRb, on the set of alternatives A so that the alternatives could
be ranked (outranked). In MOP, a set of Pareto optimal solutions is computed and presented
to the DM to select his/her decision from. As the set of Pareto optimal solutions is generally
large (often continuous), some MOP methods proceed by local search presenting parts of the
Pareto frontier to the DM. These fundamental differences in methodology between the two
approaches made them develop almost independently from the very beginning and continue
to do so. Let us brieﬂy see how the two approaches evolved.
After the publication of the method ELECTRE I (Roy, 1980) which deals with the problem
of ranking alternatives, MCDA evolved in four directions. The ﬁrst direction is the extension
of ELECTRE I to ranking and sorting methods that aggregate DM’s preferences into a
binary relation for pairwise comparison of alternatives. ELECTRE II-IV, ELECTRE-TRI,
and PROMETHEE are the most popular in this class of methods. In the second direction,
many methods were introduced using the multi-attribute utility theory which is derived from
the traditional utility theory. To each alternative a in A, a value u(a) is associated. This
value is generally the result of an aggregation procedure such as the weighted sum of the

marginal utilities of the criteria for a. The alternatives are then ranked based on the utility
function u(.). AHP, TOPSIS, and MACBET are the most well-known methods based on this
approach. The third direction is the development of interactive methods that alternate periods
of interaction with the DM and computation periods. An initial set of solutions is presented to
the DM, who provides his/her preferences, then a new set of solutions based on these updated
preferences is generated and presented to him/her, and so on. The process ends when the DM
is satisﬁed, that is, the stopping criterion in these methods is subjective. The last direction
in which MCDA evolved is the incorporation of uncertainty related to cognitive aspects or
external factors in the models and methods. Thus, random (stochastic), fuzzy, fuzzy-random,
rough, etc. MCDA models were developed (Doumpos et al., 2019).
MOP evolved in seven main directions. The ﬁrst direction is the extension of the traditional
optimization methods to ﬁnd (weak) Pareto solutions of linear, nonlinear, parametric, bilevel,
and non-smooth MOP problems with a continuous set of alternatives. The main approach
in this direction is scalarization which transforms the multiple criteria into a single crite-
rion. Non-scalarization methods were also developed. The second direction consists of the
development of methods of approximation of the set of (weak) Pareto optimal solutions of
continuous MOP using polyhedral sets, functions, curves, rectangles, etc. The third direction
is the investigation of Multi-Objective Combinatorial Optimization (MOCO) problems which
are linear MOP problems with a discrete binary set of alternatives and integer-valued objec-
tive functions. The fourth direction is the introduction and development of Goal Programming
(GP) (Charnes & Cooper, 1961) which is based on the satisﬁcing principle of Simon (1955).
In the GP model, the DM aims to obtain solutions that minimize the deviations between
the achievement and the aspiration levels for each objective (criterion). The ﬁfth direction
is the development of interactive methods for solving MOP with continuous variables. The
sixth direction is the incorporation of different types of uncertainty, such as randomness and
fuzziness (Ben Abdelaziz, 2012; Muñoz and Ben Abdelaziz, 2012). The last direction is
the use of evolutionary algorithms to generate a representative of the set of Pareto optimal
solutions. Evolutionary algorithms are general-purpose optimization heuristics inspired by
natural evolution. They do not require analytical conditions such as continuity and differen-
tiability of the objective functions and can handle problems with almost no constraints. Thus,
they can solve complex problems that cannot be solved by methods inspired by traditional
optimization methods. Some very recent developments are presented in (Doumpos et al.,
2019). In Ben Abdelaziz et al. (2018) Neural Networks, the Weighted Sum method, and the
Particle Swarm method are used to solve an MCDM problem with unknown input (criteria)
functions to determine an optimal structure for sustainable SMEs.
This short and non-exhaustive chronological review shows that for a few decades, MCDA
and MOP evolved separately as depicted in Fig. 1.
However, this MCDA/MOP dichotomy came to an end in 2009. (De Smet et al., 2009) is
the ﬁrst work to use an outranking method, namely PROMETHEE, for solving continuous
MOP. Recently, Rao et al. (2019) and Chai et al. (2021) used PROMETHEE in a MOP.
These two works are implemented in two phases. In the ﬁrst phase, a set of Pareto optimal
solutions is generated, and then PROMETHEE is applied to select a subset of it. These
works are similar in the sense that in the ﬁrst phase, they use evolutionary algorithms to
ﬁnd an approximation of the Pareto Frontier (the non-dominated solution set). However, the
three works did not address the problem of selecting a Pareto optimal solution from the set
generated by PROMETHEE when it is large. For instance, in (De Smet et al., 2009) the
implementation of PROMETHEE in the second phase generated 20 “best” solutions. The
authors suggested that the selection of a solution needs to be discussed with the decision-
maker, no method is proposed to select a solution. As selecting a solution from a set of 20

From the above review, we can draw two conclusions. 1) The existing works on using
MCDA methods for solving continuous MOP problems use metaheuristics to ﬁnd an approxi-
mation of the set of Pareto optimal solutions and they are peculiar to applications. The general
integrating view that any pair of MOP-MCDA methods can be used to solve a given contin-
uous MOP problem is not mentioned, and its beneﬁts and implications are not discussed. 2)
No method or procedure systematically addresses the problem of selecting a Pareto optimal
solution when the MCDA method generates a large set of Pareto optimal solutions.
The contribution of this paper is twofold. 1) It presents the general view that any MOP-
MCDA pair can be used to solve a continuous MOP problem. The practical and theoretical
implications of this view are discussed. The MOP-MCDA integration is presented in the form
of a three-phase operational procedure. 2) A method based on Principal Component Analysis
(PCA) (Lay, 2003) is proposed to help the DM select a solution when in Phase II the set of
Pareto optimal solutions generated by the MCDA method is large. These contributions are
illustrated by a ﬁnancial portfolio selection using the integration of the MOP Weighted Sum
method and the MCDA outranking PROMETHEE method through a hypothetical example

where PROMETHHEE produces a large set of “best” solutions. We have chosen these two

Integrating these two seemingly independent paradigms of MCDM will enhance them and
create a synergy that helps DMs reach better quality decisions. In a sense, the two approaches
are complementary. Precisely, MCDA can be used in post-MOP analysis by systematically
incorporating and integrating the DMs’ preferences in the decision-making process, which in
return opens the possibility of application of MCDA to continuous MOP problems. Indeed, in
general, a continuous MOP problem has a continuous set of Pareto optimal solutions. MOP
methods are used to generate a ﬁnite set of such solutions that is presented to the DM to
choose from without systematically incorporating his/her preferences. Selecting a solution
becomes even harder in decision-making situations involving a large number of variables
and constraints, and many criteria as the number of Pareto optimal solutions to be considered
can be large. The use of MCDA becomes relevant and even a necessity to reach good quality
decisions.
From an application perspective, with the expansion of MCDM application scope to
complex problems such as public economics and policy, communication networks, big
data analysis, sustainable development, ﬁnancial engineering, engineering design, medicine,
astronomy, and quantitative psychology as stated by (Greco et al., 2016), both paradigms
are needed for MCDM to succeed. In most applications, there is an objective stage, where
MOP techniques are appropriate, and there is a subjective stage, where human judgment
and preferences come into play. These human aspects can be incorporated systematically by
MCDA. Thus, a two-stage approach to MCDM problems, MOP-MCDA, would be far more
effective and successful than the dichotomic approach, MCDA/MOP. Moreover, linking the
two approaches generates a new general MCDM framework that can present new challenges
and require the introduction of new concepts that can lead to further theoretical development
of MCDM.
The integration of the two Paradigms can be done in the following way. Given a MOP
problem, in the ﬁrst phase, a MOP method is applied to generate a set of Pareto optimal
solutions. Then, in the second phase, the DM expresses his/her preferences, which are incor-
porated by an MCDA outranking method. Finally, the DM applies the outranking method
to the whole set of Pareto optimal solutions to select a solution. For instance, the Reference
Point method (Wierzbicki, 1980b) can be used to generate a ﬁnite set of “best” Pareto-optimal
solutions by varying the reference point; then, by using the outranking method ELECTRE
III, the solutions of this ﬁnite set are ranked according to the DM’s preferences. Thus, this
integration process yields an improved method over the two initial methods in the sense that
the outranking method is extended to the case of continuous MOP problems, while the MOP
method acquires the ability to systematically and explicitly integrate the DM’s preferences.
The rest of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the proposed integration
process and outlines the PROMETHEE and Weighted Sum methods. Section 3 presents this
integration process as a three-phase operational procedure, which is illustrated through a
problem related to multi-criteria ﬁnancial portfolio selection problem. Section 4 explains
and illustrates PCA application in Phase III to the problem of selecting a Pareto optimal
solution from the set of the “best” Pareto optimal solutions generated by the application of
PROMETHEE in Phase II. The last section summarizes the main contributions of the paper
and provides some research directions.

2.2 The MCDA and MOP Integration
On the one hand, as mentioned in the previous section, in MOP the DM is interested in
selecting Pareto optimal solutions. As the set of Pareto optimal solutions of a continuous
123

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 346:1187–1215
1193
MOP problem, also called the Pareto frontier, is generally inﬁnite continuous, selecting
a Pareto optimal solution can be a challenging task for the DM. Therefore, analysts use
different approaches to select a ﬁnite subset of the Pareto frontier to be presented to the
DM to help him/her select a solution. The most used approaches are scalarization and the
determination of a subset that represents or approximates the Pareto frontier. It has been
pointed out in the literature that the DM may have difﬁculties in determining the scalarization
parameters (Greco et al., 2016). Further, presenting a representative set of the Pareto frontier
may not be effective if this set is large. Even if this set is small, the DM would like to rank
the proposed solutions to select the best by specifying his/her preferences. The interactive
methods (Miettinen, 1998) help the DM to move from one Pareto optimal solution to another
by making some compromises (concessions) among the considered criteria. Based on the
DM’s compromises, a new Pareto optimal solution is generated and proposed to the DM. The
process continues until the DM accepts the current solution. Thus, these methods consider one
type of preference information, the classiﬁcation of criteria into three classes: the criteria that
he/she is ready to give up some value, the ones that he/she wants to keep their achievement
at the same level, and the criteria that he/she wants to increase. In practice, the DM may
want/need to consider other preference concepts or principles in selecting a solution, for
instance, a fuzzy preference. Moreover, he/she may have difﬁculties in specifying the values
by which a criterion is decreased to allow others to increase, that is, reaching compromises
among the criteria may be a hard task for the DM.
Moreover, the Pareto frontier may be very large which often occurs in complex decision-
making situations, exploring this set by the classiﬁcation approach may be practically
impossible, tedious, and time-consuming. As the stopping condition is subjective, the DM
may stop the process without exploring all the regions of the Pareto frontier.
Similar comments can be made to MOCO methods because generally, the set of Pareto
optimal solutions is very large, which often occurs in complex real-world problems. Present-
ing this set to the DM for selecting a solution is not practical. A local analysis may not be
helpful if the number of regions to be considered is large.
From the foregoing, we can conclude that MOP and MOCO methods lack complete
and systematic incorporation of the DM’s preferences. Therefore, MCDA is appropriate and
even often necessary in post-MOP/MOCO analysis to help DM reach decisions that represent
better his/her preferences, especially, in complex real-world MCDM problems.
On the other hand, MCDA methods fundamentally and exclusively apply to MCDM prob-
lems with a discrete set of alternatives X (Hwang & Yoon, 1981). They have the essential
property of incorporating the preferences of the DM systematically. The preference informa-
tion is used to construct a preference relation that allows ranking of the alternatives. However,
their main drawback is that they do not apply to a major class of MCDM problems, the con-
tinuous MOP because it is not possible to rank a continuous set of alternatives X. Thus,
when a DM expresses speciﬁc and complex preferences in MCDM with a continuous set
of alternatives, the analyst may feel frustrated because it is not possible to solve it in a way
that incorporates the expressed preferences systematically, that is, using an MCDA method.
Noticing that if we look at the problem from another angle by considering that in an MOP
problem, the DM can express his/her preferences at two stages: before an MOP method is
applied, that is, before the ﬁnite set of Pareto optimal solutions is computed and after this
set is computed. In this work, to remove the dichotomy MOP/MCDA and free our minds,
we propose to apply MCDA a posterior (after a MOP method is applied) instead of a priori
(before MOP is applied). Thus, we make it possible to apply MCDA to MOP problems by
introducing the MOP-MCDA framework, thereby, extending MCDA methods to continu-
ous MOP problems, which will tremendously enlarge the former scope of application. In

set of alternatives. In the next section, we present the MOP-MCDA integration framework
in a procedure form for application purposes.
2.3 The integration process
From the previous discussions, the complementarity between the two paradigms of MCDA
can be considerably beneﬁcial for the DM and this can be achieved by integrating them
as MOP-MCDA. The question is how this integration can be made. In this subsection, we
present the MOP-MCDA framework for operationally solving continuous MOP problems.
It consists of three phases, each of which can be completed in some steps. In Phase I, a ﬁnite
set of Pareto optimal solutions is generated by using some MOP method. In Phase II, the
preferences of the DM are incorporated into the model by an appropriate MCDA method that
is used to rank the solutions of the ﬁnite set generated in Phase I. In case the set of “best”
Pareto optimal solutions generated by the MCDA method is large and the DM cannot select
one from it, Phase III is required, where the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is applied
to rank the criteria, and then select a solution.

6.3 Illustrating the integration process
Portfolio selection is one of the areas where Pareto optimal solution selection is intensively
used to select the best portfolio to be adopted (Masmoudi and Ben Abdelaziz, 2017; Ben
Abdelaziz et al. (2007). Four main methods are used to select a portfolio, the Weighted Sum
Method, the ε- Constraint Method, Goal Programming, and Compromise Programming.
These methods are generally used to generate only one Pareto optimal solution that partially
reﬂects the preference of the DM in the sense of the principle the method is based on. The
Weighted Sum is used when the DM provides a weight for each criterion that represents well
the relative importance. As mentioned earlier, the DM may have difﬁculties in establishing the
weights in the Weighted Sum Method. GoalProgramming is based on the satisﬁcing principle,
it is used when the DM can provide desired goals related to the decision-making criteria. In ε-
Constraint method, the DM provides a threshold to be satisﬁed for all criteria. Compromise
Programming is based on the principle that the more achievement is the better. It is used
when the DM does not provide preferences over the criteria; it ﬁnds the closest Pareto optimal
solution to the ideal point. Thus, using these methods to generate a Pareto optimal portfolio,
the DM has no possibility and freedom to express other aspects of his/her preferences, such
as indifference and preference among criteria or alternatives. These limitations may lead to
undesired or unsatisfactory solutions.

11 Phase III. dealing with the case where the outranking method
generates a large number of “Best” solutions
To reach good quality Pareto optimal solutions using the proposed integration approach, a
representative sample of the Pareto optimal frontier is needed. Therefore, a large sample
of Pareto optimal solutions is required in Phase I. Generally, In Phase II, when applying
PROMETHEE or any other outranking method, a large set of “best” solutions is generated.
Then the decision-maker may not be able to select a decision from this set. Selecting one
solution randomly may not be the best way as variation within this large set can be signiﬁcant.
It is a considerable drawback of this approach. This problem has been reported in (De Smet
et al., 2009), the authors considered an initial sample of 600 Pareto optimal solutions, then
using PROMETHEE, they obtained 20 “best” Pareto optimal solutions. It will be difﬁcult
for the DMs to select a solution from 20 Pareto optimal solutions. There is a feeling that the
problem is not completely solved and the objective of helping the DMs is not satisfactorily
achieved! There is a need for some extra effort to reduce the set of “best” Pareto optimal
solutions to a reasonable cardinality to enable the DMs to select a ﬁnal “best” optimal solution
comfortably based on their objectives and knowledge. We propose an approach to solve this
problem based on PCA.

13 Concluding remarks
MCDA methods are exclusively designed to deal with MCDM problems with ﬁnite sets of
alternatives, while MOP methods are designed to deal with continuous MCDM problems.
It has been shown that the paradigms MCDA and MOP are complementary. The basic idea
is to look at a continuous MOP problem as a three-phase problem in terms of its resolution.
In Phase I, an MOP method is applied to the initial continuous MOP problem, which gen-
erates a representative ﬁnite set of Pareto optimal solutions. In Phase II, the DM selects a
Pareto optimal solution from this set. With this approach, it becomes possible to integrate
both paradigms by applying MCDA in Phase II as a ﬁnite set of Pareto optimal solutions is
available to the DM. If in Phase II a large set of “best” Pareto optimal solutions is generated
by the MCDA method, Phase III is necessary. In Phase III, PCA is applied to select a solution.
This MOP-MCDA integration signiﬁcantly improves both; it generalizes MCDA to contin-
uous MOP problems and makes it possible for the DM to incorporate his/her preferences
systematically via MCDA and thus reach better quality decisions.
The three-phase integration process was illustrated by the MCDA outranking
PROMETHEE method and the Weighted Sum method for continuous MOP problems. An
application to a multiple-criteria portfolio selection is presented. The proposed procedure
opens new research opportunities such as.
i)
exploring other pairs of methods (multi-objective optimization–outranking), such as the
Reference Point methods (Wierzbicki, 1980a, 1980b) and ELECTRE methods (Roy,
1990),
ii)
extending the proposed procedure to fuzzy and stochastic MCDM problems, and
iii) applying the proposed procedure to real-world MCDM decision-making situations.
