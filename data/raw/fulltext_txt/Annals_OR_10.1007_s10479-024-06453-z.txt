Abstract
With the rapid development of digital technologies, responsible AI has become a critical 
focus for ensuring ethical and socially conscious advancements in business and opera­
tions management. The integration of responsible AI practices in business groups’ digital 
transformations is essential to mitigate potential risks and maximize the positive impact on 
operational efficiency, supply chain performance, and subsidiary performance. This study 
aims to examine the consequences and mechanisms through which responsible group digi­
talization influences business group’s operation management, as manifested in subsidiary 
performance within the context of the digital economy. Analyzing data from 202 affiliated 
subsidiaries, we examine the role of HRM collaboration and technological turbulence in 
facilitating group digitalization. This study enriches the operations management literature 
and expands the application of ethical and responsible AI practices in digitalization by 
investigating the relationship between business group digitalization and business opera­
tions. Furthermore, this study provides practical implications pertaining to how ethical 
and responsible practices can guide group digital transformations, business operations and 
enhance the performance of subsidiaries.

1  Introduction
Responsible AI has emerged as a crucial focus in the era of digital transformation, neces­
sitating ethical considerations and socially responsible practices (Brown et al., 2024; Budh­
war et al., 2023; Charles et al., 2023; Choudhuri et al., 2023; Chowdhury et al., 2023; Raisch 
& Krakowski, 2021). Indeed, the rapid development of digitalization, driven by technolo­
gies such as AI, cloud computing, blockchains and others, has revolutionized economic 
growth and high-quality societal development (Charles et al., 2023; Emrouznejad et al., 
2023; Gabsi, 2024; Lanzolla et al., 2020). The digitalization process driven by AI introduces 
a paradigm change in business and operation management, prompting new understanding of 
how digitalization creates firm value (Lui et al., 2022; Nambisan et al., 2017), revolutionizes 
business process (Amato et al., 2024) and reshapes organizational operations (Blichfeldt & 
Faullant, 2021; Hanelt et al., 2021; Heredia et al., 2022; Peng & Tao, 2022).
Despite the increasing attention to explore the relationship between digitalization and 
firm operations, there is limited research on how these initiatives at the business group level 
influence firm value and operational performance. Business groups are a predominant orga­
nizational structure in both developed and developing economies (Banalieva & Dhanaraj, 
2019; Dau et al., 2021; Faccio & O’Brien, 2021; Kim et al., 2021). The limited attention to 
the context of business groups regarding responsible AI and business operations is concern­
ing, particularly given their potential role in digital transformation. Specifically, within the 
context of business groups, subsidiaries serve as operating units (Lamin, 2013; Meyer et 
al., 2020). While business groups may embrace digital transformation, its implementation 
and impact on subsidiary performance is not always straightforward. As business groups 
commonly share resources, expertise, and capabilities among their member companies, fos­
tering collaboration and integrating various business functions, including human resources 
and supply chain, are important to gain a competitive advantage (Khanna, 2000). Therefore, 
investigating the implications of responsible group digitization and mechanisms on business 
group operation has the potential to enrich the responsible AI domain and provide theoreti­
cal and practical guidance for business groups in navigating their digital transformations, 
while simultaneously mitigating potential risks and maximizing positive outcomes for busi­
ness groups’ operation management.
Accordingly, this research aims to investigate how business group digitalization influ­
ences business group operations and improves the performance of their subsidiaries in the 
evolving context of the digital economy. The key research question is whether and how 
group digitalization affects the performance of subsidiaries. The dynamic capabilities view 
states that firms can shape, configure, and reconfigure their resources and capabilities to 
react to changes in markets or technology (Teece et al., 1997). Firms’ competencies lie in 
their adeptness at strategically deploying their resource base to effectively address dynamic 
and evolving environments through managerial and organizational processes. Among these 
processes, HRM practices, identified as the sources of competitive advantages (Shaw et 
al., 2013), play an important role in organizational coordination, learning, and transforma­
tion. Accordingly, the success of the digital transformation process requires adequate and 
appropriate communication and exchange with the human capital within the business group, 
particularly through effective HRM practices. In this context, HRM collaboration can be 
considered a foundation, contributing to core competitiveness and sustainable competitive

advantage (Grimpe et al., 2023). Therefore, we draw upon the dynamic capabilities view to 
explore the role of parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration in this relationship.
The dynamic capabilities view emphasizes the context-specific nature of organiza­

develop and adapt their capabilities in response to market changes (Schilke et al., 2018; 
Sullivan & Fosso Wamba, 2024). Our study proposes that certain environmental conditions 
influence how business group digitalization affects parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration. 
More specifically, we argue that technological turbulence, as an environmental condition, 
influences the degree to which group digitalization affects parent-subsidiary HRM collabo­

subsidiary performance. Additionally, we posit that technological turbulence moderates the 
effect of group digitalization on HRM collaboration.
This study contributes to the existing operations management literature in three key 
areas. Firstly, it adds an interdisciplinary lens to complement the operations management 
literature with an HRM angle within an emerging context (i.e., digitalization). By explor­
ing the relationship between business group digitalization and subsidiary performance, 
this study reveals the importance role of collaboration and cooperation in value creation 
and capability enhancement. In this direction, it aligns with recent research in operations 
management (e.g., Enang et al., 2023) to further move from techno-centric Industry 4.0 to 
value-centric Industry 5.0. Secondly, it uncovers the specific mechanisms through which 
business group digitalization influences subsidiary performance, particularly highlighting 
the mediating role of parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration. Thirdly, it provides a more 
nuanced understanding of and guidance for digitalization management in business groups 
by analyzing the impact of group digitalization, parent-subsidiary HR collaboration, and 
technological turbulence on subsidiary performance. These findings offer valuable insights 
for organizations navigating the complexities of digital transformation and implementing 
responsible AI adoption within business groups.

2  Theoretical foundation and hypotheses development
2.1  Dynamic capabilities view and literature review
The dynamic capabilities view, conceptualized by Teece et al. (1997), establishes a theo­
retical foundation for understanding organizational competencies in resource orchestration 
and environmental adaptation. In their 1997 groundbreaking research, Teece and colleagues 
proposed that a firm’s competitive advantages are rooted in its dynamic capabilities, which 
are defined as “the firm’s ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and exter­
nal competencies to address rapidly changing environments” (Teece et al., 1997, p. 516). 
Research has demonstrated that dynamic capabilities are instrumental in both achieving and 
sustaining competitive advantage (Nambisan et al., 2017). Further theoretical development 
has enhanced our understanding of dynamic capabilities’ characteristics, encompassing 
market responsiveness, resource base reconfiguration (Barreto, 2010; Schilke et al., 2018), 
and engaging in sensing and seizing and reconfiguring activities (Teece, 2007).
Over the last decade, artificial intelligence has pervaded the functioning of many orga­
nizations around the world. The rise of digitalization, characterized by the profound use 
of data analytics and artificial intelligence, has not only revolutionized business practices 
but also compelled organizations to focus on more sophisticated operational approaches 
(Nambisan et al., 2017). The emergence of digitalization raises a critical question: how 
can firms effectively use these technological advancements to enhance their competitive 
advantage and operational performance? Previous studies have demonstrated multiple of 
digitalization’s impact, including strategy formulation (Matt et al., 2015), decision-making 
enhancement (Gupta et al., 2023; Jussupow et al., 2021) and value creation (Arias-Pérez et 
al., 2021; Blichfeldt & Faullant, 2021; He et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2022). Among various 
organizational contexts, business groups present a particularly compelling case, where digi­
tal transformation requires fundamental changes in subsidiary operation management and 
value creation processes (Grimpe et al., 2023). Despite the strategic importance of group 
operation management in the digital era, our understanding of how group-level digitaliza­
tion influences subsidiary operations remains notably limited.
Business groups evolve to overcome market imperfections or institutional voids across 
product, labor, and capital markets in their domestic market (Khanna & Palepu, 2000a). The 
dynamic capabilities view suggests that firms need to adjust and reconfigure their resource 
base as markets and technologies evolve (Teece, 2007) and these capabilities help orga­
nizations create, deploy, and protect intangible assets that support superior long-run busi­
ness performance. As the core of firms’ dynamic capabilities, managerial and organizational 
processes involve the ways in which companies’ routines are structured to align and merge 
activities, facilitate knowledge acquisition through learning, and adapt as required to keep 
up with evolving market conditions (Teece et al., 1997). Among these organizational pro­
cesses, HRM practices, which are identified as the sources of competitive advantages (Fest­
ing & Eidems, 2011; Shaw et al., 2013), play an important role in promoting organizational 
coordination, learning, and transformation. Therefore, this study employs the dynamic 
capabilities view to explore how digitalization influences operational efficiency and effec­
tiveness within business groups, examining both the mediating role of HR collaboration and 
the moderating role of technology turbulence.

2.2  Business group digitalization and subsidiary performance
Khanna and Rivkin defined a business group as “firms which though legally independent, 
are bound together by a constellation of formal and informal ties and are accustomed to 
taking coordinated action” (2001: 47). Firms affiliated with business groups use them as 
efficient capital and labor internal markets (Khanna & Palepu, 2000b) as well as effective 
vehicles to enrich the flows of tacit and formal information among the group partners, lead­
ing to better resource allocation, reduction of uncertainty, contract improvement, and oppor­
tunity identification (Granovetter, 1995; Guillen, 2000). The term digitalization describes 
the use of digital technologies or artificial intelligence in organizations for multiple pur­
poses and functions (Grimpe et al., 2023; Von Krogh, 2018). Digitalization is enabled by the 
electronic interconnectedness that networks provide between organizations via the Internet 
or internally via intranets (Ritter & Pedersen, 2020), and it affects many firms by offering 
new ways of increasing efficiency or creating new products. This study conceptualizes busi­
ness group digitalization as digital technology applications in business groups, including the 
application of strategy, production processes, management, and so on. Dynamic capabilities 
in businesses reflect their ability to acquire, integrate, and reallocate internal and external 
resources, as well as respond to changes in the external environment (Teece et al., 1997). 
Drawing from the dynamic capabilities view, we argue that business groups’ digitalization 
can enhance subsidiaries’ operational performance.
Firstly, the characteristics of digital technology, such as connectivity, facilitate timely 
information communication and data analysis, reducing parent-subsidiary information 
asymmetry and enabling rapid adaptation to organizational changes. Digital technology 
also breaks through temporal and spatial limitations, expanding the scope of exchange and 
communication between parent companies and subsidiaries. These technological advances 
promote knowledge and resource management (Lyytinen et al., 2016), facilitate external 
resources acquisition, and enhance organizations’ ability to sense opportunities, respond to 
threats, and adapt to changes.
Second, digitalization can facilitate communication and collaboration between parent 
company and subsidiaries. The application of information technology promotes inter-firm 
information sharing, reducing communication and transaction costs (Adamides & Karaca­
pilidis, 2020).Through digital transformation, subsidiaries tightly integrate acquired data 
resources with other production factors, accelerating the digital adoption in areas such as 
research and development, procurement, production, design, and inventory. This process 
establishes data resources as a crucial tool for obtaining market information. Simultane­
ously, digital transformation streamlines data transmission channels throughout the entire 
supply chain, optimizing resource allocation among subsidiaries, enabling accurate match­
ing of market supply and demand, and enhancing production efficiency.
Third, digitalization enables subsidiaries to better understand business group strategy 
and market structures, thereby enhancing their ability to target customer needs. The group’s 
digitalization facilitates real-time and transparent management, research and developments, 
and other operational activities within the group, reducing potential errors and improving 
operational efficiency. Digitalization of data-based processes not only reduces production 
costs but also enhance consumer engagement, thus contributing to overall operational effi­
ciency (Hagiu & Wright, 2020). From A dynamic capabilities view, business groups achieve 
resource reallocation through digital transformation, gradually reshaping their processes,

edge domains and successfully convert insights into valuable products and services at the 
subsidiary level. Based on these arguments, we propose:
Hypothesis 1  Business group digitalization is positively associated with subsidiary 
performance.
2.3  Business group digitalization and parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration
Human resource management could be a significant source of competitive advantage (Shaw 
et al., 2013). Human capital represents “firm-specific assets that are difficult if not impos­
sible to imitate” (Teece et al., 1997, p. 516), serving as the driving force of multiple capabili­
ties (Hsu & Wang, 2012), and contribute to sustainable competitive advantage (Barney & 
Wright, 1998). In general, HRM collaboration emphasizes the value of working in groups 
or teams to maximize the distinctive knowledge possessed by groups members (Lepak & 
Snell, 1999, 2002). In the collaborative system, training programs aim to develop essential 
teamwork abilities for selection procedures. Furthermore, recruitment, evaluation and com­
pensation processes always comply with group criteria (Lepak & Snell, 1999).
HRM collaboration in business groups facilitates the generation of valuable and firm-
specific knowledge through effective member group interaction. Developing HRM col­
laboration helps a subsidiary to access the human capital resource of the business group. 
Therefore, HRM collaboration is critical for disseminating specialized knowledge through­
out the business groups. Following prior studies, collaboration-oriented HRM systems 
emphasize building relationships with external stakeholders and developing shared activi­
ties (e.g., training programs) with external actors such as business partners and consultants 
(e.g. business partners, and consultants) (Zhou et al., 2013). This reflects a broader defini­
tion of HRM to include not only internal human capital, but also external human capital that 
could be of value to the organization. We define parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration as the 
systematic sharing of human resource functions between parent company and subsidiaries, 
including a series of practical measures such as job design, recruitment, training, assess­
ment, and compensation management.
Digitization can improve parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration for several reasons. First, 
digital technology plays a pivotal role in promoting the development of comprehensive 
capabilities that provide smooth cross-functional cooperation and integration (Chen et al., 
2017). Consequently, the application of digital technology can break down geographical 
and cross-organizational barriers, enable timely sharing of HRM-related information, and 
reduce repetitive work in the collaboration between the parent company and subsidiaries. 
Second, selective staffing procedures, extensive training, and competitive compensation 
denote significant expenditures and diverse levels of cost-effectiveness. Digital connectiv­
ity reduces the associated training, socialization, and monitoring costs, enabling human 
resources to be more mobile within the business group.
In practice, digitalization enhances subsidiary participation in recruitment plans and 
ensures accurate plan formulation through timely information exchange. During the recruit­
ment process, information technology facilitates real-time communication between parent 
company and subsidiaries, allowing for immediate adjustments to recruitment strategies 
and training plans based on market conditions. The implementation of group digitalization

Hypothesis 2  Business group digitalization is positively associated with parent-subsidiary 
HRM collaboration.
2.4  Parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration and subsidiary performance
Human resources can shape firms’ human capital resources, including the knowledge, skills, 
and abilities of their employees (Collins & Smith, 2006; Jiang et al., 2012; Zhao & Chad­
wick, 2014), which play a critical role in enhancing organizational performance (Jiang et al., 
2012). Research provides evidence that organizations that synchronize their human resource 
management practices are more likely to achieve superior performance (Chadwick et al., 
2015; Shaw et al., 2013). Dynamic capabilities serve to build, integrate, and reconfigure 
assets, thereby affecting resources, practices, and employees, which subsequently influence 
organizational performance. Based on the dynamic capabilities view, we argue that parent-
subsidiary HRM collaboration can improve subsidiary performance.
Parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration is conducive to communicating business group 
strategies and core values. The reciprocal relationship between a parent company and its 
subsidiaries reduces opportunistic behavior of both parties in the cooperation process (Kim 
& Lui, 2015). This collaboration involves harmonizing HRM practices and policies to 
ensure consistency across the business group while allowing for necessary adjustments. 
From the dynamic capabilities view, HRM collaboration enables subsidiaries to build, inte­
grate, and reconfigure existing HR resources and practices. As suggested by Chadwick and 
Flinchbaugh (2021), HRM dynamic capabilities can potentially explain firm differences in 
adapting to disruptive environments. Furthermore, HRM resources and practices determine 
whether specific HRM dynamic capabilities will emerge in the organization (Apascaritei & 
Elvira, 2022).
First, collaboration between parent company and subsidiaries is fostered through com­
munication, knowledge sharing, and information exchange, which facilitates the transfer 
of knowledge and resources within business groups. The transfer of knowledge in the field 
of HRM stands out as one of the most intricate aspects in multinational corporations or 
business groups (Sveiby & Simons, 2002). Through this process, knowledge and resources 
flow the subsidiary and the group network, enhancing intercompany trust and collaboration 
relationships. This exchange accelerates the flow of social capital and company resources, 
thereby increasing the likelihood of enhancing the subsidiary’s competitive advantage. Busi­
ness group can benefit from both tangible and intangible resource sharing (Kim et al., 2021). 
Prior studies have indicated that business groups can improve their competitive advantage 
by sharing resources among their affiliates, including intangible resources and technology 
(Chang & Hong, 2000).
Second, HRM practices that focus on the acquisition, development, and motivation of 
employees are an important source of competitive advantages for organizations (Apascaritei 
& Elvira, 2022; Barney & Wright, 1998; Chow et al., 2008; Jiang et al., 2012). The HR 
system can be a unique source of sustained competitive advantage, particularly when its 
components demonstrate high internal and external fit (Becker & Gerhart, 1996). HRM col­

laboration encompasses both internal and external dimensions. Internally, capabilities asso­
ciated with social capital relate to employee networks among peers and across hierarchical 
levels and functions. Externally, social integration capability facilitates the management of 
inter-organizational knowledge-sharing networks, engagement with target audiences, and 
connections with key institutional labor market agents. Parent-subsidiary HR collaboration 
improves heterogeneous resources and intangible resources, enabling subsidiaries to develop 
their competitive advantages (Oehmichen & Puck, 2016) and create synergistic value (Van 
Beers & Zand, 2014). Organizations can develop employee skills, knowledge, and capabili­
ties by aligning strategic objectives with HRM practices, thereby enhancing operational and

capabilities, ultimately contributing to sustain competitive advantage (Kim et al., 2021).
Third, the formation of strategic teams through HRM practices provides firms with flex­
ibility when these teams align with organizational goals and are configured based on team 
members’ expertise, complementarity, and potential for reconfiguration (Bell et al., 2018). 
The dynamic capabilities view suggests that human capital development enables firms to 
navigate uncertain, complex, and volatile environments, while systematically building orga­
nizational capabilities that enhance firm performance (Fu et al., 2017). For example, HRM 
practices promoting informal cooperation and knowledge sharing help increase collabora­
tion at the unit level. This process of social consolidation is both dynamic and iterative as 
social relationships develop over time (Schenkel & Teigland, 2017). The effectiveness of 
these processes is further enhanced by developing a diverse and integrated workforce, which 
strengthens both market access capabilities and operational performance (Roberson et al., 
2017). Business groups may alleviate shortages by functioning as internal labor markets, 
investing in training and development, and dispatching scarce talent to needy subsidiaries 
(Khanna & Palepu, 2000b). For example, training can promote and engender common val­
ues across the core company and subsidiaries, and ensures that all subsidiaries work towards 
a common organizational goal and approaches (Fenton-O’Creevy et al., 2008). Therefore, 
based on these theoretical arguments, the following hypothesis is proposed:
Hypothesis 3  Parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration is positively associated with subsidiary 
performance.
Integrating the reasoning for the above two hypotheses, we further argue that business group 
digitalization can have an implication for subsidiary performance via the mediation of the 
parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration. The HR function and operational capabilities of 
HRM departments enable the daily management and coordination of individuals (Minbaeva, 
2018). Drawing from dynamic capabilities view (Teece et al., 1997), which emphasizes an 
organization’s ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure competencies, parent-subsidiary 
HR collaboration serves as a crucial mechanism facilitates resource orchestration and capa­
bility transfer across the organizational network. By leveraging the business group digita­

lization through HR collaboration, the parent company can facilitate the transfer of digital 
knowledge, skills, and best practices to its subsidiaries (Minbaeva et al., 2014). This collab­

Hypothesis 4  Parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration mediates the relationship between

2.5  The moderating role of technological turbulence
With the advancement of technology and artificial intelligence, organizations today oper­
ate in an increasingly dynamic environment. Because organizational adaptability is a key 
determinant of competitive advantage (Halevi et al., 2015), environmental factors may be 
significant contextual determinants of organizational operations and development. Tech­
nological turbulence, defined as “the amount and unpredictability of change in technolo­
gies” (Slater & Narver, 1994, p. 51), relates to the perceived rate of technological change 
(Jin et al., 2022). It has become a pervasive feature of the modern business environment. 
Technological turbulence has become a pervasive feature of the modern business environ­
ment, creating increasingly competitive market conditions. In these highly competitive 
environments, competitors’ action become increasingly unpredictable. Digitally oriented 
firms demonstrate high sensitivity to technological evolution and actively pursue advanced 
technological capabilities (Feliciano-Cestero et al., 2023). The dynamic capabilities view 
emphasizes organizations’ ability to address rapidly changing environments (Teece et al., 
1997). We thus hypothesize that the relationship between business group digitalization and 
HRM collaboration may be affected by technological turbulence.
First, in highly turbulent technological environments, the impact of business group digi­
talization on parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration is likely to be amplified. This amplifi­
cation occurs because rapid technological changes necessitate more frequent and efficient 
communication and knowledge sharing between parent and subsidiary HR departments for 
rapid adaptation. By providing digital technology in promoting cross-functional cooperation 
and integration, business group digitalization can enhance HRM collaboration. High levels 
of technological turbulence can further amplify this positive relationship. When techno­
logical turbulence is high, new and improved advanced technology may constantly emerge 
(Gupta et al., 2023; Sullivan & Fosso Wamba, 2024). For example, the development of 
internet platforms has made it possible for businesses to deal with the collaboration between 
affiliated company, giving them access to a greater amount of resource flow and knowl­

edge transfer through frequent communication and coordination across parent-subsidiary. 
Moreover, by using artificial intelligence, firms can revolutionize their managerial prac­
tices for performance analyses and developing novel insights (Sullivan & Fosso Wamba, 
2024). Consequently, these advanced technologies amplify the relationship between busi­
ness group digitalization and HRM collaboration.
Second, in turbulent environments, business group digitalization enables more flexible 
and agile HR practices that can be rapidly adapted and implemented across subsidiaries. 
This enhanced agility strengthens an enhance HR collaboration by facilitating rapid adjust­
ments to changing technological landscapes (Teece et al., 2016). A significant advantage 
of business groups lies in their unique synergy through “internalization” (Lamin, 2013), 
whereby they minimize transaction costs by internalizing operations within the organiza­
tion. In turbulent environments particularly, member companies increasingly rely on internal 
collaboration (Sandford et al., 2022) for identifying and leveraging valuable information. As 
business groups undergo digital transformation, they enhance their capacity to share tech­
nological knowledge with subsidiaries, fostering a culture of continuous learning and adap­
tive HR practices. This can result in more effective HR practices and stronger alignment 
between parent and subsidiary HR operations. Consequently, business group digitalization 
exerts a stronger influence on HRM collaboration in turbulence technology environments 
compared to stable ones.
Thus, based on the above arguments, we hypothesize that technological turbulence 
positively moderate the relationship between business group digitalization and HRM col­
laboration. When the technology is highly instability, technological turbulence positively 
moderates the relationship between business group digitalization and HRM collaboration. 
Thus, we propose,
Hypothesis 5  Technological turbulence positively moderates the relationship between busi­
ness group digitalization and parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration. That is, at higher levels 
of technological turbulence, the association between group digitalization and parent-subsid­
iary HRM collaboration is higher (vs. lower).
3  Methodology
3.1  Sample and procedure
We collected data from subsidiaries of business groups operating in China. Top manage­
ment team members were invited to participate in the survey, with assured confidentiality of 
their responses. To reduce the potential common method bias, we employed a multi-wave 
data collection approach. In the first wave, we administered questionnaire to top managers, 
and asked them to rate the degree of digital development of the business group and their 
own demographics. Also, in this wave, we asked top managers to report their perceptions of 
technological turbulence, and asked them to answer the firms’ characteristic. Next, a second 
questionnaire was distributed to the subsidiary, with subsidiary HRM directors evaluating 
questions related to the degree of collaboration between the parent company and subsidiary 
HRM. Third, another questionnaire was distributed to the subsidiaries’ financial directors 
(CFOs) to investigate the subsidiary’s performance. After eliminating incomplete or invalid

questionnaires, the final questionnaires consisted of 202 matched subsidiaries with com­
plete data across all three waves.
3.2  Measurements
In order to ensure the validity of the survey, we searched for items in measures that had 
been used in earlier research. As the measures were derived mainly from western literature 
in English, a back-translation technique (Brislin, 1980) was deployed to develop the survey 
in Chinese with the assistance of two independent researchers. All ratings were made via a 
5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree).
3.2.1  Business group digitalization
We used five items from Wang et al. (2013) to measure business group digitalization. Top 
managers were asked to indicate the degree to which the statements accurately described 
their business group’s digitalization. The sample item is: “To what extent does your group 
apply information technology (IT) to support the relationship with suppliers (establish close 
relationship with suppliers, quality monitoring, monitoring delivery relationship)”. The 
Cronbach’s alpha for this measure is 0.933.
3.2.2  Human resource management collaboration
HRM collaboration emphasizes reaching out to, and building relationships with external 
stakeholders and developing HRM activities such as shared training programs with actors 
(Zhou et al., 2013). We adopted the 9-item scale from (Chen & Liu, 2010) to measure HRM 
collaboration between parent company and subsidiary. Subsidiary’s HR rated this. The reli­
ability coefficient of this scale is 0.802. Sample items include “the participation of subsidiar­
ies in the formulation of HR planning is high” and “the middle managers of subsidiaries are 
evaluated by the parent company before they are hired”.
3.2.3  Technological turbulence
We measured technological turbulence using the 4-item scale developed by Terawatanavong 
et al. (2011). Subsidiaries’ top managers evaluated the extent of change and the instability of 
the external technology. A sample item was “Technological changes provide big opportuni­
ties in our industry”. Cronbach’s reliability coefficient alpha value was 0.837.
3.2.4  Firm performance
Following prior studies (Rai et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2003), we measured firm performance 
by six items, regarding the firm’s performance relative to other firms in its industry in terms 
of profit, sales, market share, growth of assets, and competitive positioning in the firm’s 
industry. The reliability coefficient is 0.895.

3.2.5  Control variables
In this study, the subsidiary age, centralization, subsidiary status, and subsidiary devel­
opment stage were used as control variables, which as indicated in prior research might 
have an implication for subsidiary performance (Iona et al., 2013; Mahmood et al., 2017). 
We control for subsidiary age as the number of years from the establishment of the firm, 
because longer-established subsidiaries may own more resources (Mahmood et al., 2017). 
Centralization is a dummy variable with 1 if the subsidiary is highly centralized by the par­
ent company, and 0 otherwise. Subsidiary status refers to the status of a subsidiary in the 
business group network. The subsidiary development stage was measured by asking “the 
development stage of the company’s main business”, which is divided into five stages. We 
also control the regional attributes.

4.3  Tests of the hypotheses

4.3.1  Test of moderation
To examine the moderation hypothesis (Hypothesis 5), we carried out regression analy­
ses. The variables used as components of interaction terms were standard-centered to mini­
mize any multicollinearity between the interaction terms and their components (Aiken et 
al., 1991). As shown in Table 3, the interaction between business group digitalization and 
technological turbulence was significant in predicting HRM collaboration (M3, β = 0.153, 
p < 0.05). In addition, we conducted simple slope tests to illustrate the moderating effect of 
technological turbulence. Figure 2 shows this interaction pattern. Consistent with Hypoth­
esis 5, at a higher level of technological turbulence, the positive relationship between group 
digitalization and subsidiary performance is stronger (β = 0.365, p < 0.001) versus weaker 
(β = 0.155, p < 0.05). Therefore, Hypothesis 5 is supported.
5  Discussion
This study expands the available knowledge at the intersection of business groups opera­
tions and digital transformation. Although many researchers in strategic management and 
operations management have has explored the impact of digitalization on organization and 
management (Guo et al., 2023; Holmström et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2022), 
the specific effects of digitalization on operational efficiency within the context of business 
group has received limited attention. Therefore, this study addresses that gap in understand­
ing how business group digitalization unfolds, particularly concerning digitalization driving 
by responsible AI in operations management. Drawing on the dynamic capabilities view, our 
research has explored the positive association of business group digitalization on subsidiary 
performance and demonstrated the significant meditating the role of parent-subsidiary HRM 
collaboration. Furthermore, the findings underscore the crucial role of technological turbu­
lence in shaping the influence of business group digitalization. Specifically, higher levels of 
technological turbulence are found to enhance the impact of business group digitalization on 
parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration, thus providing deeper insights into the implications 
of digitalization at the business group level. By examining these relationships, our study 
provides a nuanced understanding of digitalization at the business group level, emphasizing

5.1  Theoretical implications
Our study, as underpinned by the theoretical insights from dynamic capabilities view, 
unfolds the mechanism of digitalization influences subsidiary development within business 
group. We highlight three important theoretical contributions of this research.
First, this study enriches the operations management literature by adopting an interdisci­
plinary lens to investigate the nexus between business group operation, digitalization and, 
HRM. We also introduce a novel context (i.e., business group operation), by exploring the 
roles of business group digitalization on subsidiary performance. Against the backdrop of 
the increasing importance of digital transformation in operation management, research has 
started to investigate the influence of digital technologies on business operational efficiency 
or firm performance (Holmström et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2023). However, although it has 
been suggested that the rapid development of digital technologies influences the economy 
and organizations (Von Krogh, 2018), few studies have attempted to empirically assess the 
influence of business group digitalization on subsidiary development. This study advances 
theoretical understanding by demonstrating how responsible AI practices can be integrated 
into business group operations, specifically showing how the responsible deployment of AI-
driven digitalization transforms traditional operational processes within business groups. 
Our empirical findings demonstrate that when business groups commit to responsible AI 
practices in operations management, their subsidiaries can better adapt to digital transfor­
mation while simultaneously enhancing performance and maintaining ethical standards.
Second, this research reveals the mechanisms through which business group digitali­
zation affects subsidiary performance, with particular emphasis on how parent-subsidiary 
HRM collaboration mediates this relationship. This finding aligns with the suggestion of 
Meyer et al. (2020) that human capital plays a crucial role in influencing subsidiary profit­
ability and survival. In this direction, this study advances the debate to an emerging context 
under which subsidiaries may benefit from technological change in their parent companies. 
We demonstrate that HRM collaboration is not only essential for knowledge transfer and 
coordination but also for embedding responsible AI practices within the business group’s 
supply chain. This nexus with HRM also helps to shift the techno-centric Industry 4.0 to a 
value-oriented Industry 5.0 (Leng et al., 2022; Modgil et al., 2023).
Third, this study advances our understanding of technological turbulence’s moderating 
role by examining how it affects responsible AI implementation in business group digitali­
zation. Specifically, we offer insight into the moderating role of technological turbulence on 
the relationship between business group digitalization and parent-subsidiary HRM collabo­
ration. In high technological turbulence environments, the implementation of responsible 
AI practices becomes particularly crucial as organizations must balance rapid technologi­
cal advancement with ethical considerations. Our findings reveal that subsidiaries under 
a higher technological turbulence are better able to help the subsidiary understand and 
implement group digitalization strategies and consequently improve its HRM collaboration 
between the parent company and subsidiary. We extend operations management field by 
examining how contextual factors (e.g., technological turbulence) influence the develop­
ment of responsible AI practices within complex organizational structures, particularly in

5.2  Practical implications
Our finding demonstrates that for businesses to enhance operation efficiency, it is important 
to integrate human resource management (i.e., HRM) and digital transformation into the 
business operation. This is necessary not just because of the discrete impact of each of these 
elements on operation management, but also due to the synergetic effect to result in superior 
operational outcomes across the business group. This overarching finding yields several 
specific practical implications.
First, our research provides business group leaders with a framework for implementing 
digital transformation strategies. Our findings empirically demonstrate that group digitaliza­
tion can improve subsidiary value creation and performance outcomes. We emphasize that 
successful digitalization transcends the simple adoption of digital tools; rather, it necessi­
tates a fundamental organizational transformation that revolutionizes operational paradigms 
and business processes. Business groups must embed responsible and ethical digital trans­
formation initiatives into their overarching strategic vision, ensuring alignment with both 
technological advancement and organizational values. This strategic integration requires 
organizations to proactively adapt to and reshape both their internal operations and external 
business environment, while maintaining clear and consistent communication about their 
digital transformation vision. To ensure successful implementation, business group lead­
ers should develop and execute targeted communication strategies that effectively convey 
the value proposition of digitalization to subsidiaries, explicitly demonstrating how their 
active participation advances collective group objectives and creates sustainable competi­
tive advantages.
Second, our research provides practical approaches for leveraging digitalization and 
responsible AI adoption to enhance collaboration and knowledge sharing among subsid­
iaries. These insights enable operations managers to design and implement more effective 
cross-functional teams and processes across subsidiary boundaries. Our findings demon­
strate that enhanced human resource management collaboration during the digitalization 
process significantly strengthens business groups’ competitive advantages. Specifically, 
digitalization catalyzes improved parent-subsidiary HR collaboration, which in turn drives 
superior subsidiary performance. In implementing digitalization initiatives, managers 
should integrate responsible AI practices with strategic HRM approaches to attract, recruit, 
and retain specialized talent for operations and supply chain management in this emerging 
era of AI-driven transformation. Specifically, they can utilize AI - powered platforms for 
targeted recruitment, designing attractive compensation and benefits packages, and provid­
ing personalized training and development opportunities. For another example, managers 
can design effective performance evaluation systems and incentive mechanisms to stimulate 
employee motivation and creativity, fostering the successful implementation of digitaliza­
tion within subsidiary operations and management.
Additionally, our research reveals that when the subsidiary in a dynamic technology 
environment, it significantly enhances the effect of group digitalization on parent-subsidiary

HR collaboration. In response to this finding, organizations should continuously calibrate 
their digitalization strategies to align with environmental changes. Particularly in envi­

responsible AI with enhanced monitoring systems and ethical consideration that can adapt 
to fast-changing technology circumstances.
Finally, our research findings have policy implications. The research reveals a strong 
positive correlation between business group digitalization and subsidiary operational per­
formance, providing evidence for policymakers to optimize resources allocation in facili­
tating business group digital transformation. Consistent with the related literature (Hao et 
al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023), policymakers could offer support through two 
primary channels: direct financial assistance such as fiscal subsidies to help business groups 
advance their digitalization initiatives, and indirect institutional support such as technical 
advisory services and professional consultation programs to enhance subsidiaries capabili­
ties in group-wide digital transformation. Furthermore, policymakers need to recognize the 
complexity and uncertainty that business groups face during their digitalization process, 
particularly regarding the challenges in parent-subsidiary collaboration. Consequently, we 
advocate for the establishment of support mechanisms calibrated to different technological 
contexts, aimed at mitigating digitalization barriers and fostering parent-subsidiary collabo­
ration. Such policy interventions would contribute to fostering an institutional environment 
conducive to sustainable digital transformation and organizational value creation in busi­
ness group contexts.
5.3  Limitations
We acknowledge that despite our efforts to enhance the validity and reliability of study find­
ings, there are some limitations. First, although we collected data through a multi-phase, 
multi-source approach to minimize common method bias, we acknowledge the limitations 
of the questionnaire design in establishing causal relationships. Therefore, we encourage 
future studies to employ longitudinal research designs or quasi-experimental designs to bet­
ter establish stronger causal relationships. Second, we acknowledge the relatively small 
sample size in our study. However, we also highlight the challenges associated with col­
lecting matched data on business groups and their subsidiaries, particularly due to the 
hierarchical complexity of business group structures and the sensitive nature of strategic 
decision-making information. The inclusion of both headquarters and subsidiary executives 
in our data enables us to empirically explore the intricate relationships between parent and 
subsidiary firms in the context of digital transformation. The scale of our sample is consistent 
with other studies in the fields of business group research and strategic management (e.g., 
Liu et al., 2022; Ray et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2021). Thus, we believe that the quality of the 
matched data provides valuable insights into business group digitalization and subsidiary 
operational performance. For instance, whether and how supply chain management within 
business groups can play a role in the context of digitalization and this direction has not 
been fully investigated. Future research can further explore how to design and implement 
responsible AI systems for supply chain management within business groups, addressing 
issues such as data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the explainability of AI-driven decisions.

5.4  Conclusion
The evolving digitalization era has brought new opportunities for operation management 
research, as a growing number of firms are adopting digital transformation practices in their 
operation. Drawing on dynamic capabilities view, this study provides a well-grounded and 
practical understanding of how digitalization in business groups can enhance subsidiary 
performance through the mechanism of parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration. Moreover, it 
sheds light on the role of technological turbulence in moderating the relationship between 
business group digitalization and parent-subsidiary HRM collaboration. Our study offers a 
novel explanation of why, how, and when business group digitalization can have an impli­
cation for subsidiary performance. As businesses navigate this digital shift, our findings 
emphasize the importance of strategically aligning digital initiatives with human capital 
practices, emphasizing not just technological adoption but also the ethical and human-cen­
tric aspects of digital transformation. It offers theoretical insights and managerial implica­
tions in the context of the responsible AI and digital economy but also stimulates further 
research in the domain of business groups.
