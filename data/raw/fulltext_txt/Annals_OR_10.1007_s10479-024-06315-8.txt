Abstract
Textual data analysis is critical for monitoring changing themes over time. To overcome
challenges posed by data richness, graph theory emerges as a tool for investigating word-
topic associations. We present an approach to clustering co-occurrence word networks that
prioritises network similarity quantiﬁcation over time. Addressing theoretical and network
geometrical constraints, a statistical framework for manifold data analysis facilitates the
grouping of semantic networks, partitioning the observed time frame into periods, and identi-
fying dominant topics in each period via tensor decomposition. The analysis of Brexit-related
tweets demonstrates the efﬁcacy of modern methods for identifying social media patterns on
public discourse.
Keywords Semantic networks · Graph embedding · Tensor decomposition · Social media

1 Introduction
The signiﬁcance of semantic network analysis has greatly increased in the present era, which
is characterised by an abundance of text-based information. The rise of social media platforms
and other technological advancements has greatly expanded the availability of data, making
it crucial to efﬁciently convert unstructured textual data into valuable knowledge. Within this
context, semantic networks serve as a valuable instrument for categorising and discerning
patterns and structures within the extensive volume of data, enabling us to acquire a more
profound comprehension of the meanings inherent in our information-driven society (Segev,
2021a).
Various theories, models, methods, and practical applications have emerged regarding the
development and use of semantic networks. In fact, the review conducted by de Barros Pereira
et al. (2022) points to the existence of a plurality of “semantic network” deﬁnitions. In broader
terms, a semantic network is a graph that includes nodes, which represent semantic entities
such as units of language (e.g., words, sentences, or concepts) and edges, which indicate the
relationships between the nodes. This structure enables the representation and analysis of
associations and dependencies among various concepts or elements within a given domain.
The associations between nodes represent semantic similarities or semantic relatedness and
can be derived from ontologies or can be estimated through corpora analysis based on Natural
Language Processing techniques (Harispe et al., 2015). Semantic networks can be used to
represent computational ontologies, which are “means to formally model the structure of a
system, i.e., the relevant entities and relations that emerge from its observation” (Guarino
et al., 2009). In this study, we utilise a different interpretation of semantic networks (Drieger,
2013), which relies on empirical textual data rather than structured systems for representing
knowledge. Our methodology takes advantage of this framework to build networks using
empirical textual data. In this framework, links are measures of semantic relatedness (Harispe
et al., 2015) within the textual corpus. In this context, there are several ways to build the links
in the semantic graph, including patterns of co-occurrence (Dyer & Kolic, 2020; Ferret,
2004; Palchykov et al., 2021; Stuart & Botella, 2009; Szumlanski & Gomez, 2010; Türker
& Sulak, 2018) or semantic and statistical similarities (Song & Jang, 2023; Türker & Sulak,
2018; Veremyev et al., 2019). In the literature, corpus-based semantic measures are referred
to as distributional measures to emphasise that they are based on the distributional hypothesis,
which states that words occurring in similar contexts tend to be semantically close (Harispe
et al., 2015).
Research focusing on the application of semantic network analysis to extract information
from unstructured textual data falls in the ﬁeld of Textual Network Analysis (Segev, 2020),
also deﬁned as Network Text Analysis (Diesner & Carley, 2005), an approach that combines
text mining techniques and network analysis to examine the structural properties and textual
content of large-scale textual data. Textual network analysis (TNA) lies at the intersection
between content analysis and network analysis: TNA allows for the exploration of textual
patterns and relationships through the lens of network structure, leading to the generation
of meaning (Segev, 2021b). Using network metrics and community detection methods on
textual data simpliﬁes the identiﬁcation of hidden thematic clusters, important terms, and
structure of the conversation (Segev, 2020). Therefore, TNA has been utilised to reveal the
underlying patterns that relate to different subjects and domains, especially considering the
digital realm. In fact, social media platforms are particularly useful for social scientists as
they provide extensive textual corpora that offer valuable insights into virtual conversations.

Within the domain of online debates, numerous studies investigate political matters, cov-
ering a wide range of subjects that include political and electoral campaigns (Chung & Kim,
2019; Mussi Reyero et al., 2021; Park et la., 2020; Radicioni et al., 2021), as well as social
movements and protests (Asher et al., 2019; Choi & Park, 2023; Mikhaylova & Gradosel-
skaya, 2021; Suitner et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2019). Some analyses take
into account the temporal aspect of the online debate. The study conducted by Gargiulo
et al. (2015) involved the reconstruction of daily networks to analyse the evolution of dis-
cussion topics related to the American anti-capitalist movement Occupy Wall Street. Lee
and Park (2019) applied temporal Semantic Network Analysis to examine the emergence of
gender-related topics in Korean online media.
Alignedwiththisareaofresearch,theprimaryaimofourstudyistoexaminethecapacityof
TNA to analyse online debates while considering their temporal development. With a speciﬁc
focus on political debate discourse analysis, our aim is to uncover and gain an understanding
of the underlying themes that emerge over time, without imposing any temporal-dependent
structure. Our hypothesis is that observing the emergence as well as the growth of these
themes over time may enable us to discern latent patterns and trends in the development and
transformation of political discourse. When provided with a corpus of textual data that spans a
substantial period of time, TNA can serve as a valuable tool for identifying temporal changes
in the underlying themes within speciﬁc time frames. To this end, we rely on clustering tech-
niques for graph data as a methodological tool to properly group different semantic networks
observed over time. Once clusters of semantic networks, representing different conﬁguration
of the online debate have been identiﬁed, we can use topic modelling methodologies to iden-
tify the debated themes and capture their dynamics over time. As a case study, we focus on
the online discourse surrounding the Brexit process, speciﬁcally within the ﬁnal year leading
up to the United Kingdom’s ultimate departure from the European Union.
In the following sections, we will provide a detailed description of the proposed approach.
Section2 introduces the proposed analytical framework for the analysis of temporal semantic
networks. Speciﬁcally, Sect.2.1 outlines the methodological aspects of the graph clustering
procedure. Following this, Sect.2.2 discusses the application of tensor decomposition for
efﬁcient topic detection within multiple semantic networks. The application of our framework
in analysing the dynamics of the Brexit debate is covered in Sect.3. Additional results are
included in Supplementary Material. Section4 concludes the paper summarising key ﬁndings
and their implications.

2.1 Graph clustering
To identify clusters of semantic networks, we employ a model-based clustering framework
with Gaussian densities (Gaussian Mixtures Model). In model-based clustering, data is
assumed to be generated from a mixture of underlying probability distributions, each repre-
senting a different cluster. In this framework we utilize the Expectation–Maximization (EM)
algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977) to estimate the parameters of the mixture model and opti-
mize cluster assignments (see e.g. Fraley & Raftery, 2002 for a comprehensive discussion
of EM algorithm in model-based clustering). The applicability of this clustering approach
to networked data is not immediate, as graphs do not conform to Euclidean geometry. Nev-
ertheless, networks may exhibit associations with speciﬁc natural subsets of the Euclidean
space (Ginestet et al., 2017). In order to represent graphs in an Euclidean space where the EM
algorithm can be applied straightforwardly, we consider the framework proposed by Severn
et al. (2022) for extrinsic statistical analysis of samples of networks.

2.2 Topic detection
Our analysis centres on the identiﬁcation of the community structure that underlies the distinct
group-speciﬁc graphs in order to gain insight into the debated themes. Simultaneously, we
investigate the temporal collocation of the distinct semantic communities. This temporal
collocation is analysed in relation to the temporal dimension of the grouped graphs. It is
crucial to mention that grouping the graphs observed at various times might not guarantee
the temporal proximity of the topics’ evolution over time.
To explore the predominant themes within the semantic networks, we organise the
mean adjacency matrices { ¯A(c)}c=1,...,C, reconstructed from the mean Laplacian matrices
{ ¯L(c)}c=1,...,C obtained as detailed in Sect.2.1, into a 3-way n × n × C array T . This tensor,
reﬂecting the absence of inter-layer edges, embodies the structure of a multiplex network.
Several approaches can be explored to succinctly capture the insights derived from the
reconstructed tensor (see Morup, 2011; Subhadeep & Yuguo, 2020, and referencesthere
in). Here we adopt the so-called Canonical Polyadic (CP) decomposition (Kiers, 2000),
also known as CANDECOMP (Carroll & Chang, 1970) or PARAFAC (Harshman, 1970),
which offers a reliable methodology to perform the decomposition of the T tensor into a
sum of simpler components. The CP decomposition method helps us ﬁnd different network
communities that represent debate topics, along with the activation that goes with them over
the clusters that have been found (Gauvin et al., 2014).

3 Analysing the online Brexit debate: a TNA case study
To explore how TNA can be applied to analyse online debates while considering their evolu-
tion over time, we focus on the discourse surrounding Brexit in close proximity to the UK’s
planned withdrawal from the EU. Speciﬁcally, we examine a corpus of Brexit-related tweets
shared on Twitter. First, we describe the process of data collection and the construction of
semantic networks (3.1). Following this, we illustrate how graphs’ embedding, dimension-
ality reduction, and clustering techniques are used to group these textual networks based
on semantic similarity, resulting in a partition of periods within the observed time-frame
(3.2). Finally, we demonstrate how non-negative matrix factorisation is applied to identify
key topics and track their progression across different groups over time (3.3). A ﬂowchart
summarising the analysis steps is presented in the Supplementary Material.
3.1 Data collection and graph construction
We utilised Twitter’s Streaming Application Programming Interface (API) to collect tweets
written in English containing the term ‘Brexit’ in the text or as a hashtag. The temporal
window for data collection spans from December 31, 2018, to February 9, 2020, covering
a total of 406 days. Our sample includes 135,607,216 tweets, of which 33,430,376 are pure
tweets (i.e., tweets that are no retweets) that we used to build the semantic networks.
123

Annals of Operations Research
We developed a Python script to perform the screening and cleaning process (tokenisation;
lowercase conversion; special characters, URL, and mentions removal) of text documents
in order to extract the relevant content and remove any unwanted nuisance terms. The
nltk.word_tokenize function in the NLTK Python package (Bird et al., 2009) was employed
for tokenisation. The dataset contains 2,148,421 unique tokens; about 65% of these distinct
tokens occur only once within the entire corpus and around 90% are used less than 10 times
in the entire corpus.
For the analyses, the tokens with less than 5000 occurrences in the corpus have been pruned
and the stop words removed. The stop words list contains a set of words that have no inherent
useful information, either because they do not have any meaning (prepositions, coordinating
conjunctions, determines, etc..) or because they are too frequent. Stop words create problems
in identiﬁcation of key concepts from textual sources when they are not removed due to their
overwhelming presence. The stop word list for our analysis contains also the word Brexit,
used as search term and thus presents in all the tweets. The ﬁnal vocabulary consists of
2204 tokens. Utilising Python scripts in conjunction with the Numpy library, 406 adjacency
matrices A(t), each possessing dimensions of 2204×2204, have been generated. Each matrix
serves to delineate a distinct day, with its rows and columns signifying the elements of the
vocabulary. The individual entries within each matrix denote the frequency of co-occurrence
of two words within the tweets posted on the corresponding day. To mitigate the impact of
daily tweet volume variability, the entries in A(t) are scaled based on the total number of
tweets posted on the corresponding day t.

To be more precise, nodes with a high degree centrality represent words that frequently appear
together with other words, indicating their importance in conveying shared semantic meaning.
Nodes with high closeness centrality indicate words that have a short average distance to other
words in the network. Words with high betweenness centrality serve as connectors between
distinct word groups in the semantic network. Examining these centrality measures offers
useful insights into the individual contributions of certain words to the overall structure and
connectivity of semantic networks linked to each cluster.

3.3 The topics of the Brexit debate on Twitter and their temporal collocation
Given the multiplex constituted by the 10 mean adjacency matrices and organised into the
20204 tensor T , we apply the CP decomposition as described in section 4 ﬁxing the number
of topics to 20. The analysis was conducted within the MATLAB environment, utilising the
Tensor Toolbox by Bader et al. (2023).
Finding the best low rank approximation of a tensor is often a challenging task. Although
for matrices the best approximation is obtained by considering the ﬁrst leading factors of
the SVD, this property does not hold for higher dimensional tensors. This fact implies that
lower rank approximations of a tensor are not a factor of higher approximations, and so all
the components of a rank-R must be found simultaneously. Moreover, the best low rank
approximation of a tensor T may not even exist for special cases referred as degeneracies
(see e.g. Kolda & Bader, 2009). To overcome these issues different heuristics have been
proposed. One possibility is to identify R as the smallest number of rank-one tensors needed
to approximate the speciﬁed tensor T with an arbitrarily small non-zero error ||E||. Another
well established proposal is the CORCONDIA criteria by Bro and Kiers (2003), a consistency
diagnostic employed to compare approximations with different number of components.
In our application we impose R = 20 in order to guarantee both interpretability of results
and consistency with previous topic analysis on the same dataset (del Gobbo et al., 2021).

4 Discussion and conclusions
Our research aimed to explore the potential of TNA in discovering the intricacies of online
debates, speciﬁcally focusing on the discourse temporal evolution. Our primary objective
was to uncover latent themes emerging over time, avoiding the imposition of rigid temporal
structures. By combining clustering technique to group similar semantic networks and a topic
detection procedure based on tensor spectral decomposition, we were able to identify patterns
and trends in the development of online discourses.
In particular, we have considered a general framework for the statistical analysis of graph
Laplacians as proposed in Severn et al. (2022) where the Procrustes size-and-shape metric
123

Annals of Operations Research
Fig. 9 Topic 9: most relevant words, activity level and cluster temporal collocation
is exploited to analyse a given set of graphs. Indeed, given the non Euclidean nature of
graph Laplacians it is essential to consider suitable metrics to conduct statistical analyses
effectively. To properly taking into account the dimension of the space Ln of symmetric
graph Laplacian matrices we perform a PCA in a given tangent euclidean space Tν(Mn).
This stage aids in determining the optimal relational data partition by utilising a predeﬁned set
of principal components as arguments of a mixture of normal distributions. We then represent
each cluster with its estimated centroid obtained as the sample mean using the Procrustes
size-and-shape distance as discussed in Sect. 2.1. By adopting this method, textual networks
sharing similar semantic meaning can be grouped together, leading to a partitioning of the
observed time-frame into distinct periods. Notably, this choice is made without relying on
any assumptions regarding the temporal dynamics/correlation leading to a partition purely
based on semantic similarities. Finally, since each centroid can be considered as an icon to
represent the overall online debate in a given time frame we perform a non-negative matrix
factorisation to retrieve driving topics and their activity across different groups.
To put our methodology into practice, we applied our proposed framework to analyse
the discourse surrounding Brexit on Twitter during the last year on the UK’s planned with-
drawal from the EU. This comprehensive approach revealed how topics emerge, evolve, and
eventually dissipate over time. To facilitate the visualisation of the rich results of the pro-
posed framework we provide the reader interpretable graphical representations leveraging
also on standard network metrics. In essence, our study contributes to the ﬁeld by presenting a
robust and versatile framework that not only facilitates researchers but also provides a deeper
understanding of the temporal dynamic inherent in online debates.
