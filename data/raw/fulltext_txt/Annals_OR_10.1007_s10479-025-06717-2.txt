Abstract
In this paper, we re-examine 166 previously identified asset pricing characteristics and 
their ability to successfully predict stock returns. We use Explainable Artificial Intelli­
gence to rank these return predictors based on their importance in various asset pricing 
model settings. Our findings suggest that ensemble and deep learning-based models have 
an advantage in providing generalized predictions across different return measures. Using 
SHapley Additive exPlanations, we also find that momentum and trading-based features 
possess higher predictive power in estimating asset returns. The long-short portfolio analy­
sis reveals that key return predictors exhibit substantial economic significance, reflected 
in the large differences in out-of-sample R2. These findings remain robust across various 
models and persist even after controlling for characteristics-based predictors.

FinTech · SHAP
1  Introduction
The use of machine learning (ML) in finance, particularly in asset pricing, has surged rap­
idly in recent years. This growth is primarily due to the ability of these approaches to handle 
large datasets with complex non-linear interactions. Compared to traditional econometric 
approaches, ML models are better equipped to capture multi-way predictor interactions and 
identify non-linear effects. Several empirical studies show that ML, particularly deep learn­
ing (DL) models, can significantly enhance asset return predictability and portfolio perfor­
mance (Gu et al., 2020, 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Leippold et al., 2022; Uddin & Yu, 2020).
The black-box nature of ML models presents significant challenges in interpreting their 
performance and understanding their predictions. In finance, both practitioners and poli­
cymakers are often interested in understanding the contribution of various predictors to

a model’s overall performance. Although some ML models, such as decision trees, offer 
built-in predictor importance, they are far from ideal and often provide limited information 
on model explainability. In DL models, a large number of non-linear interactions makes it 
difficult to estimate which predictors contribute the most to return predictability and under 
what conditions. Therefore, creating explainable DL models, along with achieving perfor­
mance improvements, is essential.
Highly sophisticated ML models are often complex and opaque, leading to increased 
interest in model explainability within the Artificial Intelligence (AI) research community. 
Researchers are focused on understanding the underlying mechanisms of these algorithms 
and ensuring that their predictions can be effectively applied in practice. Several frame­
works have been developed to address these challenges and improve model explainability. 
One of the most widely used frameworks for explaining model predictability is SHapley 
Additive exPlanations (SHAP), developed by Lundberg and Lee (2017) based on Shap­
ley values (Shapley & Shubik, 1954). SHAP offers an intuitive framework for introduc­
ing model explainability in this rapidly growing field of empirical asset pricing via ML. It 
enables us to address fundamental questions, such as how to interpret complex black-box 
models and identify the characteristics or features that play the most significant role in 
explaining returns. SHAP assigns each feature an importance score based on the marginal 
contribution it makes in predicting the outcome variable. Feature importance is expressed 
in the units of the dependent variable, allowing for clear and efficient interpretation. In 
addition to local explainability, SHAP offers generalized global explainability of features, 
providing a comprehensive view of how they interact and contribute to the final predictions 
of any ML model.
In this paper, we apply the SHAP framework to various ML models to identify key 
characteristics that significantly contribute to predicting cross-sectional asset returns. We 
construct ML models using 166 previously identified asset pricing predictors along with 
12 macroeconomic factors. These ML models vary in complexity, ranging from simple lin­
ear regression (LR) to non-linear ensemble models, such as Random Forests (RF), Extra 
Tree Regressor (XTR), and Extreme Gradient Boosting Tree (XGB) to more complex Deep 
Neural Networks (DNN) with multiple hidden layers. We also examine the performance 
variations of these models under different characteristic controls, including the market fac­
tor, Fama and French (1993)’s three-factor model, and Carhart (1997)’s four-factor model.
The primary motivation for using SHAP as the main measure of explainability is its 
capacity to capture non-linear interactions and interdependencies among predictors– an 
essential characteristic of financial data. This capability affords several advantages over 
other ad hoc methods for calculating feature importance among asset pricing predictors 
(Chen et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2020). First, the feature importance evaluation method pro­
posed by Chen et al. (2024) relies on the specific structure of a no-arbitrage constraint-based 
generative adversarial network. In contrast, SHAP is model-agnostic and can be applied to 
any ML model, enhancing its versatility. Second, unlike the iterative retraining required 
by the feature importance calculation method proposed by Gu et al. (2020), in which the 
model must be refitted for each feature, SHAP generally requires only a single model fit. 
As a result, SHAP offers considerable computational efficiency. Finally, gradient-based 
methods for assessing feature importance in neural network models, such as Sadhwani et

This assumption is often invalid for complex financial datasets, where variables frequently

on such assumptions and allocates feature importance consistently across various feature

To ensure robustness, we employ two additional methods to assess explainability. The 
first is an iterative feature importance approach based on Gu et al. (2020). In this method,

feature values. We then analyze the variation in R2

difference in R2

relevance to the predictive performance of the ML model.
Our work revealed several interesting findings. First, we observe that boosting algorithms 
provide better out-of-sample performance. The ability of these models to learn sequentially 
from their residuals makes them a better choice when tuned adequately using grid search. 
Second, we find that many predictors are interoperable when predicting various excess 
returns. Momentum and trading-based features consistently rank high in feature importance 
across various methods. Third, we observed that the feature importance derived from SHAP 
exhibits slight differences compared to that obtained through the LOOM method. Features 
such as beta (market beta) and roaq (quarterly return on assets) that were ranked important 
by the SHAP approach, deemed unimportant according to LOOM. However, the portfolio 
analysis shows that the most important predictors identified by SHAP also exhibit signifi­
cant long-short portfolio R2
OOS highlighting the economic significance of these predictors. 
Finally, we found that the results are consistent when controlling for market, size, value, 
and momentum. After controlling for the market, some market-related factors, such as beta, 
became less significant; however, we did not observe any notable reduction in the impor­
tance of momentum-related factors, even after introducing the Carhart (1997) momentum 
factor as a control.
The contributions of this paper are multi-fold. First, we conducted a comprehensive anal­
ysis of an extensive feature set and provided a list of open-source features that exhibit high 
predictive power when coupled with specific ML algorithms. The paper offers explanations 
for the selection of models and features, along with a holistic comparison of performance 
across various algorithms. These findings can be used to develop custom-built ML models 
with fewer, yet important, predictors without sacrificing performance. Second, we highlight 
the use of the SHAP’s model explainability framework to generate explainability at both the 
global model and local prediction level. We find that SHAP is less computationally inten­
sive than LOOM. The latter approach requires fitting a new model for each feature, which 
becomes highly challenging as the feature space grows, particularly with the addition of 
interaction terms with macroeconomic factors. Third, through interaction term and depen­
dency analysis, we demonstrate how feature interactions affect model performance. These

This work also makes a significant contribution to the literature on the application of 
ML in asset pricing, especially in the area of explainable AI. Earlier studies have employed 
firm-specific characteristics and macroeconomic predictors as inputs for various ML mod­
els, including Auto-encoders (Gu et al., 2021; Uddin & Yu, 2020) and Multi-layer Per­

(Atkins et al., 2018) and social media (Houlihan & Creamer, 2021), to gauge market senti­

models have been applied to predict pricing dynamics in other asset classes, including 
options (Goudenege et al., 2020; Eckstein et al., 2021), mortgage-backed securities (Deku

ing higher predictive accuracy.
However, recently there has been a growing shift in focus from improving prediction 
accuracy to enhancing model explainability (Zhang et al., 2023; Del Vitto et al., 2023; 
Jabeur et al., 2024). Explainable AI techniques, such as AutoML and SHAP along with 
gradient-based methods as seen in Sadhwani et al. (2021); Horel and Giesecke (2020) have 
been successfully applied in fields like real-estate, healthcare (Leung et al., 2022), engi­
neering (Nyawa et al., 2023), and transportation (Veran et al., 2020) to analyze algorithmic 
fairness and model trustworthiness. Despite the increasing application of ML in finance, the 
use of explainable AI, particularly in asset pricing, remains limited. This paper addresses 
this gap by providing insights into feature and model selection processes in empirical asset 
pricing using ML, thus offering enhanced explainability.

2  Data and methodology
2.1  Data
In this paper, we use the open-source cross-sectional asset pricing data from Chen and Zim­
mermann (2022). This dataset comprises monthly data from December 1925 to June 2022, 
incorporating 210 predictor variables that have been extensively documented in the litera­
ture as significant predictors of cross-sectional asset returns.1
We perform some essential data preprocessing before feeding the data into the ML mod­
els. Several firms and predictors exhibit a significant number of missing values, particu­
larly in the earlier years. To address this issue, we limit the sample period to January 2000 
through July 2019 for our main experiment. Second, among all existing firms and indicators,

we retain only those with more than 50% non-missing observations. Third, we eliminate

forward-filling when a firm has a non-missing observation in the previous time period; 
otherwise, we impute using the class median for that feature. After preprocessing, the final

In addition to firm-specific predictors, we also incorporate global macroeconomic fac­
tors as inputs to our ML models. Following Gu et al. (2020), we include 12 macroeco­
nomic characteristics identified by Welch and Goyal (2008) in our feature set. These macro 
characteristics are dividend-price ratio (dp), dividend yield (dy), earnings-price ratio (ep), 
dividend payout ratio (de), equity risk premium volatility (rvol), and many others.2 How­
ever, unlike Gu et al. (2020), rather than using all raw macroeconomic features, we follow 
Ludvigson and Ng (2009) by calculating latent factors from macroeconomic characteristics. 
To calculate these latent factors, we perform principal component analysis (PCA) on the 
macro characteristics and select the top three components (henceforth referred to as PC1, 
PC2, and PC3), as their cumulative explained variance is 99.9%. We then use these latent 
factors to compute interaction terms with the firm-specific predictors, which serve as input 
features. This approach provides two key benefits. First, it helps to avoid multicollinearity 
in the macroeconomic data. Second, it allows us to retain a more compact feature space, as 
the use of interaction terms can increase the feature space exponentially.
Ultimately, the total number of covariates, zi,t, is 166 × (3 + 1) = 664; comprising 166 
firm-specific characteristics and three latent macroeconomic factors. The interaction vari­
ables are named according to the latent components that interact with the firm character­
istics to create the final feature set. For instance, when PC2 interacts with the firm feature 
MaxRet (Maximum return over a month), the resulting feature is designated as MaxRet_2.
For all ML models, we use the next month’s return as the dependent variable. In line 
with existing asset pricing literature, we employ both excess return and abnormal return as 
measures of return. Excess return is calculated by subtracting the Treasury bill rate from 
the log return. Abnormal return over the market (w/Rm) is obtained by estimating market 
model parameters for each firm using a 60-month rolling window, with a minimum require­
ment of 30 months.
To enable a comprehensive comparison across models and their identified predictors, 
we conduct multiple robustness tests. These include extending the data timeline (January 
1990 to July 2019) and performing a moving window analysis with alternative time inter­
vals. Additionally, we experiment with randomly split train-test datasets to evaluate the 
stability of our results across different time periods and training configurations. Finally, we 
assess each model’s performance on abnormal returns derived from the Fama-French three-
factor model (FF3), which incorporates size and value factors alongside the market factor, 
and the Carhart four-factor model (FF4), which adds momentum factor to FF3.3 Abnormal 
returns from FF3 and FF4 are calculated similarly to market abnormal returns, employing a 
60-month rolling window with a minimum requirement of 30 months.

asset pricing. Following the empirical design established by Gu et al. (2020), we construct 
these ML models. The future excess return of an asset can be defined as an additive error 
model:

Here, the function g∗(·) indicates the conditional expected return that uses the predictors 
zi,t of firm i at time t to estimate the excess return ri,t+1 of next month t + 1. For estimat­
ing g∗(·), we consider Linear Regression (LR), Random Forest (RF) (Ho, 1995), Extra 
Tree Regressor (XTR) (Akbari et al., 2021), Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGB) (Chen & 
Guestrin, 2016), and Deep Neural Network (DNN) with 1 to 5 hidden layers.4
We select these specific models because they represent a broad spectrum of ML tech­
niques, ranging from simple ensemble models, such as RF, to more complex DNN. This 
diversity in methodologies allows us to leverage their unique strengths and capabilities, 
thereby providing a robust foundation for predictive modeling in cross-sectional asset pric­
ing. The selected models offer a well-balanced approach between explainability (e.g., RF) 
and complexity (e.g., DNN). By integrating models of varying complexity, we effectively 
address the trade-off between accuracy and explainability, fostering a more comprehensive 
understanding of asset pricing dynamics. In addition, these models are widely utilized as 
benchmarks in contemporary asset pricing literature (Chen et al., 2024; Uddin et al., 2023), 
with studies such as Gu et al. (2020), Gu et al. (2021), and Leippold et al. (2022) demon­
strating their superiority over linear models in predicting cross-sectional asset returns. In 
the following sections, we provide detailed descriptions and implementation specifics for 
these models.

model complexity is high, necessitating careful tuning of hyperparameters such as learning 
rate, tree depth, and regularization parameters to achieve optimal performance. Its flexibility 
and scalability make it a popular choice for large-scale datasets. For our study, we use the

2.3  SHapley additive exPlanations (SHAP)

The importance of each feature in SHAP is expressed in the units of the dependent vari­
able, ensuring that the assessment of feature importance reflects their contributions to predic­
tions when considered alongside other features. In addition to providing local explainability, 
SHAP offers a generalized global perspective on feature importance by averaging across 
samples after accounting for absolute differences. This approach delivers a comprehensive 
understanding of how features interact and contribute to the final prediction.
While Shapley values provide valuable insights into feature contributions, the computa­
tion of these values is computationally intensive, particularly for high-dimensional data. 
This complexity arises from the necessity to evaluate all possible feature combinations, 
which makes the approach less feasible for very large datasets. Nevertheless, Shapley val­
ues explain the extent to which each feature contributes to predicting a specific instance and 
how this contribution deviates from the average prediction across the dataset. By quantify­
ing feature importance in this manner, SHAP enhances explainability and fosters a deeper 
understanding of the model’s decision-making process.
2.4  Evaluation framework
To evaluate the performance of ML models, we divided the data into train and test sub-
samples on a temporal scale. Records between 2000 and 2017 were put in the training sub-
sample to train the ML models. Data from 2018 and 2019 were included in the test sample. 
The purpose of dividing the data on a temporal scale is to avoid the problem of information 
leakage, a common issue in time-series prediction. This study does not incorporate a valida­
tion set, which is typically employed for hyperparameter tuning, as the primary objective is 
not to achieve the most accurate predictions. Instead, we focus on demonstrating the appli­
cation of SHAP and a diverse array of commonly utilized ML models to enhance model 
explainability. This approach allows us to emphasize the interpretative capabilities of these 
models, thereby contributing to the broader understanding of their functionalities within the 
context of predictive analytics.

objective of this paper, we have opted to maintain a simple network structure.
3.2  Predictors importance using SHAP
As we documented in subsection 3.1, ML models are more effective in predicting cross-
sectional asset returns. We now shift our focus to our main objective - identifying the sig­
nificance of predictors. To accomplish this, we employ the SHAP framework (Lundberg & 
Lee, 2017), which measures the average marginal contribution of a feature to a model’s out­
come across all possible feature combinations. SHAP achieves this by iteratively creating 
data samples, holding a subset of features constant, and recording the difference between 
the observed prediction and the average prediction. The global feature importance is then 
calculated by considering all possible combinations of features and aggregating them into a 
single metric representing the mean of these differences.

4  Robustness test
4.1  Robustness tests with alternative return measures
In this section, we evaluate the robustness of our findings across multiple settings. First, 
we assess the robustness of the outcome variables by using two additional measures of 
abnormal returns. Specifically, we apply abnormal returns from the Fama and French (1993) 
three-factor model (w/FF3), which incorporates size and value factors alongside the market 
factor, and abnormal returns from the Carhart (1997) four-factor model (w/FF4), which adds 
momentum to the FF3 factors.
The result of the robustness test with these two abnormal returns is reported in Table 5. 
The overall ranking of the models remains consistent with the main analysis reported in 
Table 2. In both the FF3 and FF4 controls, nonlinear ensemble methods such as XTR, RF, 
and XGB outperform other models. Additionally, the DNN outperforms the shallow NN, 
further solidifying their superior predictive capacity in cross-sectional asset return. We also 
observe that there is an overall decline in performance after introducing these characteris­
tics control. After introducing size and value as controls (Panel A), the R2
OOS on test data 
dropped by 25, 22, and 19 basis points for XTR, XGB, and RF, respectively, compared to 
the market control model reported in Table 2 Panel B. Furthermore, after incorporating 
momentum (Panel B) to FF3, the R2
OOS declined by an additional 33, 15, and 13 basis 
points for XTR, XGB, and RF, respectively. A similar pattern is observed in the DNN mod­
els. Compared to only market control, after controlling for FF3, the R2
OOS declined by 26 
and 14 basis points for NN4 and NN5, respectively. When we added momentum control, 
the R2
OOS further declined by 15 and 17 basis points from the FF3 model. This general 
decline in performance is expected, as the four factors– market, size, value, and momen­
tum– are widely regarded as the most dominant predictors of cross-sectional asset returns.

5  Conclusion
With the growing application of ML in finance, the demand for more explainability of these 
algorithms is also gaining rapid traction. Especially in the domain of asset pricing, it is 
imperative to understand the contribution of various predictors in stock return estimation 
and portfolio construction. This paper is an initial step in this regard. In this paper, we per­
form a comprehensive analysis of widely used asset pricing predictors through the optics of 
ML. The findings of this study reinforce our understanding of the importance of momentum 
and trading frictions in asset pricing. The results remain consistent even after controlling 
for multiple characteristics-based predictors and employing different approaches for robust­
ness testing. The implications of these findings are manifold. They will assist researchers in 
narrowing down the search space for selecting optimal predictors and focusing on the most 
significant ones. Additionally, these findings can aid investors in developing profitable port­
