Abstract
We focus on the problem of ﬁnding the greatest element of the intersection of max-closed
convex sets. For this purpose, we analyze the famous method of cyclic projections and show
that, if this method is suitably initialized and applied to max-closed convex sets, it converges
to the greatest element of their intersection. Moreover, we propose another projection method,
called the decreasing projection, which turns out both theoretically and practically preferable
to Euclidean projections in this particular case. Next, we argue that several known algorithms,
such as Bellman-Ford and Floyd-Warshall algorithms for shortest paths or Gauss-Seidel
variant of value iteration in Markov decision processes, can be interpreted as special cases
of iteratively applying decreasing projections onto certain max-closed convex sets. Finally,
we link decreasing projections (and thus also the aforementioned algorithms) to bounds
consistency in constraint programming.
Keywords Euclidean projection · Max-closed set · Max-closed polyhedron · Shortest paths
1 Introduction
Max-closed (and analogously min-closed) sets ﬁnd many practical applications, e.g., in con-
straint programming (Narboni, 2014; Jeavons & Cooper, 1995), temporal planning (Kong et
al., 2018), network design (Subramani, 2007), program veriﬁcation, or econometrics (Sub-
ramani & Worthington, 2011; Chandrasekaran & Subramani, 2013). The deﬁning property
of a max-closed set is that it is closed w.r.t. taking component-wise maxima (all concepts
will be formally deﬁned later in Sect. 1.1). In this paper, we focus on ﬁnding the greatest
element of the intersection of max-closed closed convex sets. For this purpose, we restrict
our attention to projection methods since the greatest element of the intersection turns out to
be the projection of any upper bound on the intersection (i.e., a point component-wise greater
than or equal to any point in the intersection1).

More generally, the problem of ﬁnding a point belonging to the intersection of a family
of closed convex sets is well-studied and has practical applications in different areas, such as
image reconstruction, approximation theory, control theory, or signal processing (see, e.g.,
Bauschke and Borwein, 1996; Combettes, 1997 and references therein). The famous method
of cyclic projections (Bauschke & Borwein, 1996; Combettes, 1997; Deutsch & Hundal,
2006), also known as POCS (Sezan, 1992), ﬁnds such a point by sequentially projecting a
current point onto the individual convex sets. However, except for special cases (e.g., when
the sets are subspaces (von Neumann, 1950; Halperin, 1962)) or unless other algorithms
are used (e.g., Dykstra’s algorithm (Boyle & Dykstra, 1986; Gaffke & Mathar, 1989)), it is
known that the resulting sequence need not converge to the projection of the initial point onto
the intersection, but only to some point in the intersection (Gaffke & Mathar, 1989; Escalante
& Raydan, 2011). In the following, we show that if all the sets are in addition max-closed
and the cyclic projections algorithm is initialized to an upper bound on the intersection, the
obtained sequence converges towards the projection of the initial point onto the intersection,
which turns out to be the greatest element of the intersection.
Moreover, we analyze a special kind of projection, called the decreasing projection, which
can also ﬁnd such a point and is even preferable to Euclidean projection in this case.
Next, we pay particular attention to polyhedra deﬁned by max-closed linear inequalities,
which frequently appear in the literature (Chandrasekaran & Subramani, 2013; Van Maaren
& Dang, 2002). For such max-closed sets, we show that the decreasing projections can be
computed in a simple and efﬁcient manner and that several algorithms are equivalent to
sequentially applying decreasing projections. These are, e.g., the Bellman-Ford and Floyd-
Warshall algorithms for computing shortest paths (which allows us to interpret these shortest-
path algorithms as projection algorithms that ﬁnd the greatest element of a certain max-closed
polyhedron) or a variant of value iteration for Markov decision processes. Finally, we relate
our results to bounds consistency from the ﬁeld of constraint programming.
1.1 Preliminaries and notation
In the following, we abbreviate, for any n ∈N, [n] = {1, . . . , n}. As usual, we denote the
components of a vector x ∈Rn by x j, j ∈[n]. A sequence of vectors is indexed by a
superscript (e.g., as in x1, x2, . . .) and xk
j is the j-th component of k-th vector. In contrast, a
sequence of sets is indexed by subscripts (e.g., S1, S2, . . .) to avoid confusion with the n-ary
Cartesian product (as in Rn).
For x1, x2 ∈Rn, y = max{x1, x2} ∈Rn is the vector composed of component-wise
maximal elements of x1 and x2, i.e., y j = max{x1
j , x2
j } for all j ∈[n].
A set S ⊆Rn is max-closed if, for all x1, x2 ∈S, max{x1, x2} ∈S (Van Maaren & Dang,
2002; Narboni, 2014). Obviously, the intersection of max-closed sets is again a max-closed
set.
Vector x ∈Rn is an upper bound on a set S ⊆Rn if x ≥x′ for all x′ ∈S (Van Maaren
& Dang, 2002) where the comparison is meant component-wise, i.e., x ≥x′ if and only if
x j ≥x′
j holds for all j ∈[n].
Finally, x ∈S is the greatest element of set S if x ≥x′ holds for all x′ ∈S (Subramani
& Worthington, 2011; Chandrasekaran & Subramani, 2013). Not every set has a greatest
element, but if a set has a greatest element, then it is unique. The greatest element of S can
be equivalently characterized as the point in S which is an upper bound on S.
To comment on the relations among these concepts, not every set with a greatest element
needs to be max-closed (Gabbay, 1976). Conversely, a max-closed set need not have a greatest

2.2 Decreasing projection
Let S ⊆Rn be a closed set and x ∈Rn be such that there exists x∗∈S satisfying x∗≤x.
If S is max-closed or convex (or both), we deﬁne the decreasing projection of x onto S,
denoted by ρ(x, S), to be the unique optimal solution of the problem
min{∥x −x′∥| x′ ∈S, x′ ≤x}.
(7)
The following proposition ensures that the element ρ(x, S) is well-deﬁned.
Proposition 1 Let S ⊆Rn be a closed set, x∗∈S, and x ∈Rn be such that x∗≤x. If S is
max-closed or convex, then the optimal solution of (7) is unique. Moreover, if S is max-closed,
the optimal solution is the greatest element of the set {x′ ∈S | x′ ≤x}.
Proof If S is max-closed, then the feasible set of (7) is closed, max-closed, and non-empty.
Together with the fact that x is an upper bound on the feasible set, this yields that the feasible
set has a greatest element y. This greatest element is the unique optimal solution of (7) since,
for any x′ feasible for (7), x′ ≤y ≤x.
If S is convex, then the feasible set of (7) is closed, convex, and non-empty. Consequently,
ρ(x, S) = π(x, {x′ ∈S | x′ ≤x}).
⊓⊔

set if it is max-closed, but, in any case, we need to ensure that the set S contains an element
that is component-wise less than or equal to the point to be projected.

4.3 Markov decision processes and value iteration
In this part, we focus on Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) and show that a variant of
the value iteration algorithm can be also interpreted as sequentially computing decreasing
projections. An MDP can be formulated either as maximization of reward (Feinberg &
Shwartz, 2012) or minimization of cost (Altman, 1999; Kushner & Kleinman, 1971). To ﬁt
our framework, we will present MDP as a minimization problem. A ﬁnite MDP is deﬁned
by:
• a ﬁnite set S of states,
• a ﬁnite set A of actions so that, for each s ∈S, the non-empty set A(s) ⊆A contains the
actions available at state s,
• transition probabilities p(s′|s, a) for each s, s′ ∈S and a ∈A(s), i.e., p(s′|s, a) is the
probability of transitioning into state s′ if action a is taken in state s, and
• a cost function that assigns the cost c(s, a) ∈R to each s ∈S and a ∈A(s).
The MDP goes as follows. First, the process is in some (possibly pre-deﬁned, but gener-
ally arbitrary) initial state s1 ∈S. Next, it proceeds iteratively for each time step t ∈N:
being in state st, we choose a single action at ∈A(st) from the set of available actions,
incur cost c(st, at), and transition to state st+1 which is randomly sampled according to the
probability distribution p(·|st, at).
The task is to ﬁnd a policy, i.e., a way of choosing actions in states so that an objective
function (typically, the expected value of aggregated costs) is minimized. There exist several
settingswhere,e.g.,thepolicyisdeterministicorrandomized(i.e.,whetheranactionischosen
deterministically or according to a probability distribution), stationary or non-stationary (i.e.,
whether it depends on the time step t), the process can be ﬁnite or inﬁnite, and the objective
can be the expected value of the total, average, or discounted cost (Feinberg & Shwartz,
2012).
In the following, we focus on the case of inﬁnite-horizon MDPs where we seek a deter-
ministic stationary policy q : S →A such that q(s) ∈A(s) for all s ∈S that minimizes the

6 Conclusion and discussion
We analyzed two cyclic projection methods for ﬁnding the greatest element of the intersection
of max-closed convex sets. One of them is based on Euclidean projections whereas the other
14 Note, this follows from Proposition 4 (i.e, (max D1, . . . , max Dn) is a solution of the CSP) and the fact

on decreasing projections, which we newly deﬁned. While both require initialization to an
upper bound on the intersection, we found that the latter kind of projection is preferable in
this particular setting, which was proven theoretically and was conﬁrmed by our experiments.
For the former one, this yields a new special case where it is capable of ﬁnding the projection
of the initial point onto the intersection of the sets.
Next, we paid particular attention to max-closed sets deﬁned by linear inequalities and
equalities and argued that several existing algorithms can be interpreted as sequential appli-
cation of decreasing projections onto suitably deﬁned sets.
Finally, we linked decreasing projections to bounds consistency in constraint program-
ming. Although solving systems of max-closed constraints by enforcing certain local
consistency levels was explored before (Jeavons & Cooper, 1995) and has practical applica-
tions (Kong et al., 2018), we discussed that all of the listed algorithms, such as the well-known
Bellman-Ford and Ford-Fulkerson algorithms, can be also interpreted in this way.
