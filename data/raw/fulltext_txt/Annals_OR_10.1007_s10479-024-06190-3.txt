Abstract
The Production Routing Problem (PRP) seeks optimal production and distribution planning
that minimises costs and fulÔ¨Āls customer orders. Yet, existing literature often overlooks
the potential impact on proÔ¨Ātability. Achieving optimal proÔ¨Āt does not necessarily imply
meeting all customer orders. The cost-to-proÔ¨Āt ratio should be considered when serving
customer orders, as there are circumstances where it might be more proÔ¨Ātable to cancel or
backorder certain orders. Thus, this paper proposes, for the Ô¨Ārst time, a novel extension of
PRP that maximises proÔ¨Āt where demand is price-sensitive and allows order cancellation and
backorders under service level targets. From on-Ô¨Āeld observations, price is inherently subject
to uncertainty; thus, we propose a robust mathematical model for the problem that optimises
the worst-case proÔ¨Āt. To solve the problem, the paper proposes a decomposition algorithm
that splits the problem into a master problem and a set of subproblems, enhanced by valid
inequalities and warming up lower bounds to alleviate the model complexity. Through a
series of computational tests, we prove the ability of the proposed algorithm to tighten the
optimality gaps and alleviate computational time. An additional economic study is conducted

1 Introduction
Small and medium-sized manufacturing companies are faced with Ô¨Āerce competition and
highly demanding customers. This environment creates high and persistent pressure on those
Ô¨Ārms to operate and grow. To endure competition, Ô¨Ārms focus their efforts on rising incomes
and catching growing market shares. Since these Ô¨Ārms are called to master their operational
expenses, they tend to manage several links of their supply chain, including production,
storage, and distribution, through their own decision-making processes. In the literature,
several studies show that it is proÔ¨Ātable for Ô¨Ārms to jointly monitor production and distribution
decisions in one integrated decision-making framework known as the Production Routing
Problem (PRP) (Adulyasak et al., 2015b; Chandra, 1993; Torkaman et al., 2020).
The majority of the proposed models for PRP have focused on proposing optimal joint
production and distribution planning under cost minimisation objectives (Adulyasak et al.,
2015b); however, the associated generated proÔ¨Āt has not been discussed. In fact, customers
nowadays are selective and demanding, and they often order small batches of items. The
volatility and quantity of their needs challenge production and distribution systems as they
are a source of high expenses that reduce the desired proÔ¨Āt (Torkaman et al., 2020). In the
literature, the only related paper to this work is the one by Torkaman et al. (2020). The
authors studied PRP with pricing, considering an endogenous and price-dependent demand
where all customers are served regardless of the generated proÔ¨Āt from each customer. In this
paper, the considered PRP focuses on the total generated proÔ¨Āt, where serving all customers
is not mandatory. The studied version of PRP is referred to as Production Routing Problem
with ProÔ¨Āt (PRPP). The idea is inspired by the well-known Vehicle Routing Problem with
ProÔ¨Āt (VRPP), where proÔ¨Āt is considered when constructing customer-serving routes. The
way proÔ¨Āt is perceived differs in three different versions of VRPP: ProÔ¨Ātable Tour Prob-
lems (PTPs), Team Orienteering Problems (TOPs), and Prize-Collecting Routing Problems
(PCRPs). For a comprehensive review of these models, readers are referred to Ahmadi-Javid
et al. (2018), Amiri and Salari (2019), Archetti et al. (2017), Buluc et al. (2022), El-Hajj
et al. (2016), Gansterer et al. (2017), K√∂hler et al. (2023), Pamukcu and Balcik (2020), and
Vidal et al. (2016). The version of PRPP studied in this paper is formulated based on the PTP
extension of VRPP.
Since the proposed problem is targeting proÔ¨Āt maximisation, some customer orders might
not be served if they are not proÔ¨Ātable or not reachable by the optimal vehicle routes. However,
these orders might be accepted to be backordered for upcoming time periods. Considering
these features, the resulting proposed model is referred to as PRPP with backordering. It is
worthwhile to mention that the Vendor Managed Inventory policy is not applied to this PRPP
version; however, accepting, rejecting, and backordering customers‚Äô orders makes production
and distribution integration Ô¨Ānancially rewarding within the studied model.
Order rejection might induce customer dissatisfaction and may lead to losing potential
market share in the medium and long term. Yet, customer satisfaction and service level
maximisation are becoming two of the main drivers of competitive markets. When faced with

stiff competition, tangible pressure is put on companies to increase the service level even when
operational and distribution costs are high (Bulh√Ķes et al., 2018). This situation brings about
two conÔ¨āicting goals: proÔ¨Āt versus service level maximisation. This paper proposes, for the
Ô¨Ārst time, a PRP with proÔ¨Āt and backordering under a service level constraint. Since offering a
competitive service level to customers is an inherent challenge, this new PRP conÔ¨Āguration is
managerially impactful as it allows decision-makers to express their optimisation preferences
and choose to Ô¨Ānd a trade-off between proÔ¨Āt and service level maximisation. In this study,
we investigate how service level constraints have a very tightening impact on proÔ¨Āt and how
they might inÔ¨āuence planning.
In the proposed model, demand is price-sensitive, and the products are sold at a Ô¨Āxed
price, referred to as the mill price. In addition, by adopting a spatial pricing policy, the
customers‚Äô proximity and accessibility to the factory deÔ¨Āne their corresponding retail prices,
which comprise the mill price and an additional cost determined as a percentage of the mill
price, called delivery cost. The percentage used to calculate the delivery cost from the mill
price is referred to as the markup level. The markup level allows for the calculation of the
total price of a customer order. In the proposed model, the mill price is input data, while
the markup level applied to a customer order is a decision variable. In realistic cases, for
some products, mill prices are not known exactly at the beginning of the planning horizon.
The proposed model Ô¨Āts a wide variety of business applications where mill price uncertainty
is frequent. These businesses include gas companies, agri-food supply chains, e-commence
and online market places, energy and utilities distribution, food and beverage distribution,
and retail distribution (Zhang et al., 2019).
Since the mill price is considered uncertain, we introduce uncertainty in the model formu-
lation by distinguishing between (here-and-wait) decisions taken before the exact value of the
mill price is known and (wait-and-see) decisions, which are taken when the mill price value
is revealed. In the proposed model, the demand value is determined based on the price. Thus,
the model consequently addresses demand uncertainty. In the literature, uncertainty has been
handled using reactive, proactive, and proactive/reactive approaches. Combining proactivity
with reactivity is the most realistic way to tackle uncertainty, as it has been proven in the
literature (Rodrigues & Agra, 2022). The methods used in the literature are mainly stochas-
tic programming, robust optimisation, and fuzzy programming. The most suitable method to
tackle the uncertainty of the mill price is robust optimisation, since the scenarios‚Äô occurrences
cannot be predicted via a known distribution. In addition, when robust optimisation is per-
formed via a worst-case scenario, it will avoid (re)adjusting the already taken decisions when
a perturbation happens, while stochastic and fuzzy programming do not prevent schedules
from being adjusted, which might lead to time and proÔ¨Āt loss.
In this paper, we propose a robust mixed integer linear programming model for the PRPP
with backordering and service level, whose objective function consists of maximising the
worst-case value that can be attained for proÔ¨Āt and thus determining the worst-case price
scenario. The MILP deterministic version for PRPP with backordering and service level
integrates different decisions that are mainly related to the VRP and lot-sizing problem LSP.
Thus, when considering robustness in the same model, the number of decision variables
increases in parallel with the number of considered scenarios. Thus, exact methods embed-
ded within most commercial solvers can easily get trapped, even for small instances; this
complexity constrains the usage of these solvers. Therefore, we propose a decomposition
algorithm alleviated with novel accelerating procedures. The decomposition algorithm iter-
ates between a master problem and a set of sub-problems. The algorithm is enhanced with
novel valid inequalities that effectively improve optimality gaps by strengthening its linear

problem, we seek to understand the impact of service level on proÔ¨Āt and PRP costs. Addi-
tionally, we aim to explain the proÔ¨Āt Ô¨āuctuation under different parameter settings and also
how the service level threshold and the length of the planning horizon inÔ¨āuence the rejection

The remainder of the present paper is structured as follows. In Sect. 2, we review the
related literature. The mathematical formulation is described in Sect. 3, followed by a detailed
presentation of the solution methodology in Sect. 4. The results of the computational study
are reported in Sect. 5, followed by highlights of the managerial insights. Finally, we sum up
and draw conclusions to potentially extend the present contribution.
2 Literature review
Recently, PRP has attracted signiÔ¨Ācant attention from the academic community since efÔ¨Ācient
andpracticalsolutionstothisproblemarebeingrequestedbycompanies.Foracomprehensive
literature review on PRP, readers are referred to Adulyasak et al. (2015a) and other recent
studies (Avci & Yildiz, 2019; Brahimi & Aouam, 2016; Dayarian & Desaulniers, 2019; Qiu
et al., 2017; Russell, 2017; SolyalńĪ & S√ľral, 2017; Zhang et al., 2017). The present paper
addresses PRP with proÔ¨Āt (PRPP), considering backordering and service level with price
uncertainty. Therefore, we will focus on reviewing the literature related to these areas.
InPRP-relatedliterature,variousobjectivefunctionsandmetricshavebeenexplored.Most
of these performance indicators are focused on minimising expenses by reducing total travel
distance, number of vehicles, length of the tours, and production and inventory costs with
waiting times (Avci & Yildiz, 2019; Brahimi & Aouam, 2016; Dayarian & Desaulniers, 2019;
Qiu et al., 2017; Russell, 2017; SolyalńĪ & S√ľral, 2017; Zhang et al., 2017). ProÔ¨Āt maximisation
in PRP, however, has not been studied widely in the literature. In contrast, several articles
consider proÔ¨Āt-oriented models (e.g., Ahmadi-Javid & Ghandali, 2014; Berger et al., 2017;
Fern√°ndez et al., 2007; He et al., 2016; Pelegr√≠n et al., 2012; Vogel, 2011). In these models,
proÔ¨Āt maximisation is considered in VRP from the order acceptance perspective, where
cancelling certain customer orders can generate higher proÔ¨Āts for the facility (HanaÔ¨Āet al.,
2020; Lera-Romero & Miranda-Bront, 2021; Speranza et al., 2014; Stavropoulou et al., 2019;
Sun et al., 2018; Vidal et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2019).
The present paper is the Ô¨Ārst to explore a proÔ¨Āt-oriented PRP with an order acceptance
feature. We assume that fulÔ¨Ālling all orders is not mandatory, particularly when the serving
cost of a customer exceeds the generated proÔ¨Āt. Consequently, the proposed model may cancel
or backorder customer orders if serving cost does not cover the corresponding expected proÔ¨Āt.
However, it may frequently happen that this strategy impacts the service level. Given this
situation, a conÔ¨āict arises between maximising proÔ¨Āt and increasing service levels. To address
this, we formulated the proposed model to Ô¨Ānd a tradeoff between these two objectives. In the
PRP literature, service level maximisation has not been considered, but it has been addressed
in close problems such as VRP (Bulh√Ķes et al., 2018; Campelo et al., 2019) and IRP (Rahimi
et al., 2017). To enhance service levels and avoid completely rejecting customer orders, the
proposed model also includes backordering options as a way to avoid completely rejecting
customer orders. In PRP literature, this option has been considered by Brahimi and Aouam

Uncertainty is an inherent challenge for production and routing systems. It mainly arises
from common aspects such as demand, price, travel times, energy consumption, and cus-
tomers‚Äô locations. In the literature, uncertainty has been handled in the sub-problems of
PRP using different approaches. For lot-sizing problems, readers are referred to the works
by Aghamohammadi-Bosjin et al. (2020), Alem et al. (2018), Curcio et al. (2018), Hu and
Hu (2018), Mohammadi et al. (2020), Mohammadi (2020), and Zouadi et al. (2015, 2016,
2018). Regarding VRP, uncertainty has been handled using different approaches. Many works
use robust optimisation frameworks. Pelletier et al. (2019) addressed the electric VRP with
energy consumption uncertainty via a robust optimisation with a two-phase heuristic method.
A robust optimisation model with uncertainty sets was also proposed by Hu et al. (2018) for
a VRP to handle the case when demand and travel time are uncertain with a two-stage
algorithm. Two robust bi-objective models were proposed for a VRP with scheduling for
perishable products under uncertainty about the travel time and the freshness-life of the
products by Rahbari et al. (2019). Uncertainty in travel times was also studied by Wang et al.
(2019), who proposed a robustness approach known as ‚ÄúElastic p-Robustness‚ÄĚ and a memetic
algorithm. Munari et al. (2019) proposed a robust VRP, which they solved using compact
formulations and a branch-price-and-cut method. A fuzzy optimisation model was proposed
by Zhang et al. (2020) for an electric VRP considering the uncertainty of the service time,
the battery energy consumption, and the travel time.

tainty. To the best of our knowledge, the only work that tackles PRP with uncertainty is the
contribution by Adulyasak et al. (2015b). The authors proposed a mathematical formulation,
which they solved using a branch-and-cut algorithm. A Benders‚Äô decomposition approach
was proposed to handle the large number of scenarios under demand uncertainty. In this
paper, we extend the well-known traditional PRP to a new version with proÔ¨Āt PRPP with
backordering, service level, and uncertain prices. The contributions of the present paper are

and service level maximisation and to study how targeted service levels impact proÔ¨Āt.
‚ÄĘ Given the tangible uncertainty in price, we propose a robust model for PRPP with backo-
rdering and service levels. The model seeks to optimise the worst-case scenario.
‚ÄĘ To solve the new model and deal with the large number of scenarios involved in practice,
we propose a novel decomposition algorithm enhanced with new procedures, including
valid inequalities and lower bounds designed speciÔ¨Ācally for the proposed problem.

terms of solution quality and CPU time. Additionally, we show the value-added of each

3 Mathematical formulation
In this section, we propose the mathematical model for PRPP with backordering and service
level in both robust and non-robust versions. For both versions, the assumptions presented
in the sequel are applied. The additional assumptions that reÔ¨āect the difference between the
two formulations will be highlighted in Sect. 3.2. To formulate the problem addressed in this
paper, we consider a production plant that delivers multiple products to serve a set of identiÔ¨Āed
customers. Each delivered product has a mill price, from which we get the wholesale price by
adding a delivery fee as a percentage (markup level) of the mill price (Eqs. 1, 2). We consider
that this percentage has different values, and we need to decide which value to apply for
each customer order. Customer demand is price-sensitive and depends on the markup level
applied to customer orders.
At the manufacturing site, we assume that production, setup, and inventory costs are
known and invariant over time, and that the produced quantities are completely stored in the
incapacitated warehouse of the plant. The company has a Ô¨Ānite number of clients who place
orders over the planning horizon. At each time period, a customer can place several orders;
each order corresponds to a different product. The facility reserves the right to accept or refuse
these orders. If the customer‚Äôs order for a product is accepted, the company decides whether
to deliver the quantity required on time or to backorder it. In both cases, for each product,
split delivery is not permitted. Delivery of products is ensured by a set of homogeneous and
capacitated vehicles, and each customer can only be served by one vehicle route.
Since not all orders are accepted and not all of them are served on time, we deÔ¨Āne the
company‚Äôs service level as the percentage of orders that have been served on time from the
total placed orders. This percentage is an input to the model; thus, the production, backorder-
ing, rejection, routing, and pricing decisions are taken with the aim of maximising the proÔ¨Āt
without falling below the predetermined service level target.

4 Robust decomposition algorithm
Initially, the robust version of the proposed mathematical model has been tested in CPLEX
and Gurobi solvers using Branch and Cut (B&C), but they could not efÔ¨Āciently solve all
instances, as will be detailed in Sect. 5. In fact, the deterministic version is an integration of
a VRP with production decisions, which is known to be NP-Hard. Thus, the robust version
becomes even harder to solve because of the number of uncertain scenarios. To overcome
this, we propose in this paper to decompose the robust model and then solve it using a novel
algorithm that avoids exploring all the scenarios.
The algorithm consists of separating the robust model into a master problem and a set
of sub-problems. The master problem is deÔ¨Āned by the objective function (24) and the set
of constraints (25)‚Äď(44). At each iteration of the algorithm, the master problem is solved
by considering a subset of scenarios ‚Ä≤ of the original subset . In the Ô¨Ārst iteration, the
master problem is solved with a sub-set ‚Ä≤ that contains only the original scenario. In the
next iterations, the remaining scenarios are added to the subset ‚Ä≤ and the master problem
is solved again with the updated subset ‚Ä≤. The strength of the proposed decomposition
algorithm is that not all scenarios are added during the algorithm‚Äôs execution, but just some
of them. Choosing which scenarios to add to the subset ‚Ä≤ is done based on the results of
solving the sub-problems.
In each iteration, the master problem gives all PRP decisions, including routing and pro-
duction ones. We keep the Ô¨Ārst-stage decisions deÔ¨Āned by the routing and backordering
decisions to feed a set of sub-problems. For every scenario that is not included in ‚Ä≤, a
sub-problem is solved by taking as inputs the Ô¨Ārst-stage decisions of the master problem and
seeking production and inventory decisions that suit the corresponding scenario. The objec-
tive function of the set of sub-problems is expressed as in Eq. (48), subject to the constraints

The sub-problem is a min‚Äďmax optimisation problem. Min‚Äďmax problems are not con-
vertible to a single optimisation problem; thus, we solve each inner model separately. A
maximisation problem is solved for every scenario ŌČ‚Ä≤ that was not included in the master
problem. When all the sub-problems are solved, we choose the scenario that gives a proÔ¨Āt
that is lower than the proÔ¨Āt given by the master problem solved in the same iteration. Then,
we add this scenario to the subset of scenarios ‚Ä≤ used to solve the master problem again. In
the case that the sub-problems did not Ô¨Ānd any potential scenarios, the solution of the master
problem is considered optimal (see Fig. 1). The algorithm considers different scenarios, and
through several iterations, it converges towards the worst-case scenario, which ensures that

the Ô¨Ārst-stage decisions remain robust even when the worst-case scenario occurs. The algo-
rithm stops when no remaining scenario can lead to a worst-case proÔ¨Āt. Thus, this proves that
the solution obtained at the end is robust, and the scenario corresponding to that solution is
the one that leads to the worst proÔ¨Āt. It is worth recalling that, for the proposed decomposition
algorithm, it is mandatory to solve the master problem to optimality just at the last iteration
(refer to Chargui et al., 2023a, 2023b; Rodrigues & Agra, 2021).
Preliminary tests showed that even after decomposition, it is still time-consuming to solve
the problem by using the proposed algorithm framework in its basic form. Thus, we enhance
the proposed algorithm with speed-up procedures that consist of valid inequalities and warm-
up lower bounds.
4.1 Procedure to optimally solve sub-problems
At every iteration of the decomposition algorithm, several sub-problems need to be solved
to achieve optimality. As the number of sub-problems depends on the size of the uncertainty
set, it becomes harder to solve them exactly. In the decomposition algorithm, the decisions
to be taken in the sub-problem concern production and inventory quantities based on routing
and pricing decisions. Since we deal with an incapacitated production system, a dynamic
Fig. 2 Steps of the decomposition algorithm with the improvement procedures
123

Annals of Operations Research
programming procedure is applied to obtain the optimal decisions. In the literature, it has
been proven that there exists an optimal solution to each sub-problem in which It Qt = 0
for all t ‚ąą{1, . . . , T }, and it is found via the dynamic programming algorithm proposed
by Wagner and Whitin (1958). Every sub-problem of the decomposition algorithm can be
represented as a shortest path formulation on an acyclic-oriented graph where the objective
function is the negative value of the proÔ¨Āt, as we are dealing with a maximisation problem.
Then, using the dynamic programming algorithm, an optimal solution is found for every
sub-problem.
4.2 Lower bounds procedure for the master problem
To speed up the resolution of each master problem, we propose a method to Ô¨Ānd efÔ¨Ācient lower
bounds to start the B&C algorithm in a constructive way. At the beginning, pricing decisions
are made. For each customer, markup levels are assigned during the planning horizon as
follows. For each time period and every markup level, we calculate the product of the price
and the demand associated with that markup level, and then we choose the markup level that
gives the best revenue. In this constructive procedure, based on the value of the service level
target, we randomly select some orders to reject and backorder without falling below the
service level target. Orders coming from distant customers with lower revenue are rejected.
We reject as much as we can, unless we violate the service level constraint. Once the pricing,
rejection, and backordering decisions are taken, we move on to the routing decisions and use
efÔ¨Ācient heuristics from the literature (for more details, readers are referred to the libraries of
Gro√ęr et al. (2010) and Liu et al. (2023)). These libraries are so far considered the most up-to-
date local search moves for the speciÔ¨Āc VRP version that is considered in our formulation. It
has been used in similar papers in the literature and proved high performance (Liu et al. 2023).
Once routing decisions are made, we use the proposed dynamic programming algorithm
described in Sect. 4.1 to make optimal production and inventory decisions. Finally, a lower
bound for the master problem is found. The described procedure is repeated each time the
master problem needs to be solved within the decomposition algorithm. Starting with this
lower bound, the master problem is solved using the B&C embedded within CPLEX and
Gurobi solvers. Figure 2 illustrates the steps of the decomposition algorithm, including all
the improvement procedures mentioned above.

5.2 Computational results
To evaluate the performance of the decomposition algorithm (EDA) with the proposed
enhancement procedures, we conducted a comparison study as follows. At the beginning, we
implemented the robust model to solve the instances in both CPLEX and Gurobi (abbreviated
in the following tables as GR) to compare them in terms of efÔ¨Āciency. Then, we used the
decomposition algorithm without strengthening procedures (version EDA). Next, we tested
the enhanced versions of the algorithm to evaluate the improvement. To highlight the value of
the strengthening techniques implemented within the decomposition, each instance is solved
several times for each version.
To further evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithm, we implemented a parallel
computing-based metaheuristic from the literature (Yan, 2018) to solve the problem. This
123

Annals of Operations Research
technique is based on an Ant Colony Optimisation algorithm (ACO). The choice of parallel
ACO was based on its efÔ¨Āciency when it comes to solving problems that are similar to the one
addressed in this paper. To the case of our problem, we adapted local search techniques from
Yan (2018) in addition to the libraries that we mentioned in Sect. 4 to optimise the vehicle
routing decisions. Regarding the way of implementing parallel computing, our approach of
implementation is inspired from the works of Amirteimoori et al. (2022) and Amirteimoori
et al. (2023), where the authors thoroughly explained the details of how to use the multi-
processing package of Python. In the following results, we refer to this benchmark algorithm
as PA, which consists of solving the problem using only parallel ACO without implementing
EDA. This algorithm (PA) will be compared with EDA, as well as with all its improved
versions, as detailed below. We set the same time limit for PA and EDA versions in order to
guarantee a reliable comparison.
The Ô¨Ārst improvement technique (EDA + PA) consists of integrating the parallel ACO to
start the decomposition algorithm with high-quality, feasible solutions. The second improve-
ment (EDA + PA + VI) consists of strengthening the mathematical model with valid
inequalities, while the third improvement (EDA + PA + VI + LB) implements the lower
bound technique described in Sect. 4. For each method, we report the average of optimality
gaps, which is calculated over all instances of each sub-category. To execute the different
versions of the EDA, we set a time limit of one hour. Each time the master problem is solved,
the upper and lower bounds are reported. After that, when no scenario can be added to the
master problem, the instance is then solved with the small set of scenarios left. If the optimal
solution of the master problem is found within the time limit, the solution is optimal; other-
wise, the current worst scenario and an upper bound to the worst-case proÔ¨Āt are saved (refer
to Fig. 1).
From the results reported in Tables 3, 4, and 5 and the graphs in Figs. 3, 4, and 5, we notice
that the EDA is much more efÔ¨Ācient than CPLEX and Gurobi for all categories of instances.
When comparing the two commercial solvers, we Ô¨Ānd that Gurobi is faster than CPLEX
based on the tightened optimality gaps found. In addition, the strengthening procedures,
including the parallel ACO, lower bound, and valid inequalities, have signiÔ¨Ācantly improved
the optimality gaps. The implementation of the metaheuristic PA was useful in reducing the
initial optimality gaps. In fact, we can see that it provides better optimality gaps compared
to the basic version of EDA. However, the enhanced versions of EDA (strengthened with the
proposed technics including lower bounds and valid inequalities) provide better results than
PA. We can also notice that optimality gaps become smaller when the service level target
is greater. This means that it becomes easier to solve instances when service level targets
are tight. In fact, for example, when the service level is already Ô¨Āxed at 100%, the decision
about rejection or backordering will be neglected. However, when the service level decreases,
rejection and backordering options become more important, which increases the complexity.
The details about optimality gaps for time sub-categories within the same category of the
number of customers are given in Table 5 and represented in the graph of Fig. 5. Within the
same category of the number of customers, we notice that optimality gaps increase with the
number of time periods. For small instances, the valid inequalities and parallel ACO added
to the EDA helped obtain the optimal solutions within an average CPU time of 3 min. Also,
when the lower bounds are included, the CPU time is improved to an average of 58 s.
For medium instances, including strengthening techniques helped the decomposition
accelerate the search and improve the convergence, which is perceptible through optimality
gaps. Optimal solutions could not be found for the case of medium and large instances within
a time limit of 1 h as the decomposition algorithm involves solving a VRP, which is NP-hard.

6 Conclusion
The contribution of this paper presents signiÔ¨Ācant insights from theoretical and practical
viewpoints. It not only proposes a novel model and method to address the existing gap
in the related literature but also provides the directions for promising applications with its
managerial implications.
6.1 Theoretical contributions
In this paper, we introduced a novel version of PRPP with backordering and order accep-
tance subject to service level constraints. This extension aims to investigate the trade-off
between proÔ¨Āt and service level maximisation and study how the variability in service level
targets inÔ¨āuences proÔ¨Āt. Thus, we proposed a robust mathematical model that optimises the
worst-case scenario among several scenarios of price values. To solve this model and handle

the large number of scenarios, we proposed a novel decomposition algorithm enhanced with
strengthening procedures, including valid inequalities and lower bounds, designed speciÔ¨Ā-
cally for the proposed model. The efÔ¨Āciency of the decomposition algorithm has been proven
via a diversiÔ¨Āed experimental study. Moreover, we conducted a series of economic analyses
that showed the sensitiveness of proÔ¨Āt to the variation in the model parameters. Finally, we
also highlighted why a robust version of the proposed model outperforms a deterministic
model.
6.2 Managerial implications
This study introduces a proÔ¨Āt oriented PRP with backordering and service level, offering a
practical approach to enhance proÔ¨Āt margins for small and medium companies. By presenting
anovelmodelandalgorithm,thestudyaimstooptimiseproductionanddistributionprocesses,
with a special emphasis on balancing backordering decisions and maintaining the desired
service levels, allowing companies to attain cost savings and enhanced proÔ¨Āt potential without
the need of huge investments in expanding capacity. The proposed algorithm serves as a
decision-support tool, ensuring a robust integration of production and routing decisions with
backordering and service level constraints. This robustness adapts to Ô¨āuctuations of prices
and demand, enabling operational efÔ¨Āciency even when facing uncertain scenarios. The study
outlines the value-added of robust decision-making over traditional non-robust approaches
through computational experiments. Moreover, it explores how the value derived from this
robustness varies with changes in key parameters such as the price, distribution costs, and
service level requirements. This insight allows practitioners to Ô¨Āne-tune their operational
decisions to maximise proÔ¨Āts and get the potential beneÔ¨Āts of the robust approach, proving
the algorithm‚Äôs adaptability and its potential to signiÔ¨Ācantly impact the planning.
6.3 Limitations and recommendations
This study has some limitations that provide potential directions and recommendations for
future research. For instance, the model could be extended to include time windows, new
pricing strategies, and other VRP variables. Additionally, uncertainty affects not only prices
but also production system reliability, as unexpected machine shutdowns and corrective
maintenance can hinder the plant‚Äôs ability to produce the planned quantities. Delivery times
also face uncertainty due to trafÔ¨Āc and mechanical issues. Future work could incorporate
these uncertainty factors into the proposed model and adapt the algorithm to address these
new aspects of uncertainty. Moreover, multi-echelon PRP could be considered, integrating
practical features such as multi-user warehouses and energy constraints prices.
