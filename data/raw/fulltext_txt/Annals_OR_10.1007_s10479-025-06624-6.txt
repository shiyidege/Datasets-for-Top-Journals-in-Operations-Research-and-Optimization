Abstract
This manuscript develops a spatially continuous temperature model for pricing weather
derivatives. We formulate temperature at any speciÔ¨Āc point as a function of its longitude
and latitude coordinates. Using satellite climate data, we learn this function using deep neu-
ral networks and use it to model and forecast temperature. More speciÔ¨Ācally, our model allows
for describing temperature dynamics at any point within the zone of interest, even when there
is no available temperature data at that speciÔ¨Āc location. This approach enhances our abil-
ity to evaluate climate risk with greater precision across different regions. Furthermore, we
design a neural network architecture that maintains model explainability, which is crucial for
promoting the use of these methods in real-life applications. Through numerical experiments
with NASA-MERRA-2 satellite data, we illustrate the model‚Äôs application in pricing Heating
Degree Day (HDD) derivatives. Additionally, we explore potential extensions of the model
to improve forecasting accuracy further.

1 Introduction
TheimpactofweatheronvariousbusinessactivitiesissigniÔ¨Ācant.Manybusinesses,including
energy producers and consumers, supermarket chains, the leisure industry, and the agri-
cultural sector, are exposed to weather-related risks. This substantial impact underlines
the critical necessity to hedge the risk associated with weather. Among these strategies,
weather derivatives emerge as a powerful tool, enabling market participants to effectively
mitigate the adverse effects of weather variability on their operational efÔ¨Āciencies and Ô¨Ānan-
cial performance. The payoffs of weather derivatives may be linked to various underlying
weather-related variables such as temperature, rainfall, humidity, or snowfall (Groll et al.,
2016). Especially, the majority, around 98‚Äď99%, of weather derivatives currently traded are
linked to a temperature index. These contracts typically utilise temperature-based metrics like
heating degree days (HDD) and cooling degree days (CDD), which measure deviations from
a speciÔ¨Āed base temperature (Huang et al., 2008). So, having a good model for temperature
evolution is the backbone of effective weather derivative pricing. An accurate temperature
model ensures that the derivatives provide effective protection against these risks and facil-
itate Ô¨Ānancial institutions managing a portfolio of weather derivatives, in understanding the
correlations and variances in temperature patterns. Moreover, properly assessing the risk
associated with temperature Ô¨āuctuations ensures that the derivatives are neither overpriced
nor underpriced, leading to fair market pricing. Finally, accurate models can analyse his-
torical temperature data to identify trends and patterns. This historical insight is essential
for making informed predictions about future temperature behaviour. As climate change
impacts temperature patterns, having a dynamic and accurate model helps in adapting the
pricing strategies of weather derivatives to new realities.
However, deriving an accurate model for daily temperature is a complex process. Observed
temperatures show seasonality in the mean, variance, distribution, and autocorrelations, as
well as long memory in the autocorrelations. It is common practice to model daily temper-
atures using continuous Ornstein-Uhlenbeck processes. Once the process is estimated, one
can value any contingent claim by taking the expectation of the discounted future payoff.
Unfortunately, also the problems of pricing and hedging of weather derivatives is a difÔ¨Ācult
one. Firstly, if the underlying is identiÔ¨Āed as being the temperature index or the degree-days,
the HDD calls and puts are Asian options. Secondly, the weather market is incomplete in the
sense that the underlying assets cannot be stored or traded (Berhane et al., 2021). This market
discrepancy renders arbitrage pricing mechanisms ineffective, as weather derivatives cannot
be efÔ¨Āciently replicated with other Ô¨Ānancial instruments (Campbell & Diebold, 2005). In
an incomplete market, it is not always possible to replicate the payoff of a given contingent
claim by a controlled portfolio of basic securities. Consequently, the classical Black Scholes
Merton pricing approach based on no-arbitrage theory may not apply directly in all cases. In
such cases, Monte Carlo simulations are often used.
In the Ô¨Āeld of weather derivatives evaluation, various models have been proposed to cap-
ture the complex dynamics of temperature variations and their impact on Ô¨Ānancial contracts.
One notable contribution is the model proposed by Alaton et al. (2002), which considered a
mean-reverting process for the temperature dynamics in which the variance is constant in each
month. However, this model may not fully capture all the features of temperature Ô¨āuctuations
over time. Additionally, Brody et al. (2002) introduced a fractional model with a fractional
Brownian motion driving the stochastics. While this model offers a potentially more realistic
representation of temperature dynamics, its derivations become complex, particularly when
considering the time dynamics of different futures products. Nevertheless, the fractional

which may inÔ¨āuence the pricing of weather derivatives. Furthermore, Benth and Ň†altytňôe-
Benth (2005) presents a stochastic modelling framework for temperature variations tailored
towards weather derivatives pricing. Their approach considers the mean-reverting nature
of temperature processes, capturing both short-term Ô¨āuctuations and long-term trends. This
framework provides a foundation for understanding temperature dynamics and their implica-
tions for derivative pricing. In Benth and Ň†altytňôe-Benth (2007), they improve the modelling

(2008), the authors propose a model that incorporates the time-dependent speed of mean
reversion in the context of weather derivatives pricing. In Castellano et al. (2020) the authors
introduce a temperature index, which is deÔ¨Āned as the weighted mean of daily average tem-
peratures measured in different locations. In Wang et al. (2015) authors propose a feasible
weather derivative pricing model using daily average temperature data; in Ta¬łstan and Hay-
favi (2017) authors apply a methodology similar to that of Benth and Ň†altytňôe-Benth (2005)
to model daily average temperatures across various regions. Notably, their model features
a dual-noise structure, with one component driven by a Brownian process and the other
by compound Poisson processes, allowing for a more precise representation of temperature
dynamics. Most recently, in Tong and Liu (2020) a time-changed Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process
is introduced: this process integrates seasonal drift and volatility components. From an empir-
ical point of view, some noteworthy contributions are given in Huang et al. (2018), where
authors propose a method based on long-memory modelling to price a weather derivative
considering global warming; BemŇ° and Aydin inBemŇ° and Aydin (2021), critically analyse
the various steps in determining the price of a temperature derivative, such as the choice of
the indicator, the use of simulations, and the pricing strategy. Finally the papers (Mart√≠nez
Salgueiro & Tarrazon-Rodon, 2019; Bobrikov√°, 2022) focus on aspects concerning the design
of weather derivatives with respect to speciÔ¨Āc markets, namely tourism and agriculture.
Among all, the model proposed by Alaton et al. (2002) stands out due to its balance
between complexity and practicality, offering a comprehensive representation of temperature
dynamics while maintaining computational feasibility. This model strikes a suitable trade-
off, providing sufÔ¨Ācient Ô¨āexibility to capture temperature Ô¨āuctuations over time without
introducing excessive complexity that may hinder implementation and interpretation.
One of the main aspects of dealing with weather derivatives is choosing the adequate
geographical area for which the contract is written. In this sense, a weather derivative could
be poorly effective in hedging weather risk in an area far from the contract or with peculiar
micro-climate conditions, such as coastal or mountain areas (Brockett et al., 2005; Manfredo
& Richards, 2009). This drawback is known in the literature as geographical or spatial basis
risk (Rohrer, 2004). Geographic basis risk not only interferes with the correct valuation of
the derivative and its effectiveness but also generates mistrust in the tool, which tends to be
used less as the distance from the reference weather station increases (East, 2005; Simmons
et al., 2007). Furthermore, the correct speciÔ¨Ācation of the model strongly depends on the
covered geographical area, as shown by Schiller et al. (2012).
In the literature, different basis risk mitigation portfolio strategies have been proposed.
The outdated solutions are the use of the OTC market or derivatives with multiple underlings,
such as basis derivatives, underwritten on the difference between two prices, i.e. two weather
stations in a given time. While mitigating the basis risk, both solutions are affected by a non-
negligible credit risk (Brockett et al., 2005). A possible portfolio strategy is to use different
derivatives of multiple nearby weather stations (Woodard & Garcia, 2008; Norton et al.,
2012; Ritter et al., 2013; Mart√≠nez Salgueiro & Tarrazon-Rodon, 2019). However, it should
be noted that geographic distance does not necessarily affect basis risk negatively due to the

complex dependency structure between weather and geographical conditions. In this sense,
D‚ÄôAversa et al. (2023) propose a non-parametric optimisation strategy of Italian basis risk
for weather derivatives. In recent years, the use of satellite data has improved the reliability
of index-based insurance to cover adverse climate-related events, but encouraging results can
also be obtained in weather derivatives pricing (M√∂llmann et al., 2018).
With reference to Italian weather heterogeneity, Olper et al. (2021) analysed the effect
of weather variables on economic growth by exploiting a panel of 110 provinces observed
between 1980 and 2014, showing that effects of climate risk on agricultural business have
highly uneven effects across geographical areas. Mysiak (2018) proposes a climate change
index for the regions of Italy based on the EURO-CORDEX project, showing different hazard,
exposureandvulnerabilitylevelsdependingonthegeographicalareas.Inotherwords,therisk
assessment of a large non-homogeneous geographical location, such as Italy, which extends
over about ten degrees of latitude and presents morphological differences such as coastal and
upland areas, requires a careful evaluation of the derivatives coverage of climate risk, which
may be affected by strong basis risk. For this reason, using data that can approximate the risk
based on area-speciÔ¨Āc climatic characteristics mitigates the geographical basis risk.
This manuscript presents a spatial continuous model for temperature modelling and fore-
casting. In our framework, temperature is represented as a function of longitude and latitude
coordinates, deÔ¨Āned as real numbers. This method effectively captures the heterogeneity of
risk across different areas. Our model allows for describing temperature dynamics at any
point within the zone of interest, even when there is no available temperature data at that
speciÔ¨Āc location. Using satellite data, we introduce a calibration scheme based on neural
networks, since, in a spatially continuous setting, the parameters are functions that require
approximation. Despite this complexity, we maintain model explainability, which is crucial
for real-world applications, by retaining the structure of the Alaton model at the output layer.
We illustrate the model‚Äôs application using NASA-MERRA-2 data. Numerical experiments
show the model‚Äôs ability to accurately describe the temperature dynamics and effectively
learn the morphological features of the area of interest. Monte Carlo simulations are then
applied to price weather derivatives, such as options based on HDD. Additionally, we discuss
how the model can be easily extended to other temperature model speciÔ¨Ācations to enhance
forecasting accuracy.
The paper is organized as follows: in Sect. 2, we deÔ¨Āne weather derivative contracts, in
Sect. 3, the Neural Network architecture we use in our evaluation is introduced. Section4
describes our starting point temperature model which is the one proposed in Alaton et al.
(2002). In Sect. 5 we state our proposed spatially continuous model for the daily temperature
and in Sect.6 numerical results and performance of the proposed model are discussed. Finally,
in Sect. 7 one of the possible extensions is illustrated, and Sect.8 concludes the paper.

management strategy to mitigate the risks associated with adverse or unexpected weather
conditions. A weather derivative is based on measures such as rainfall, temperature, humid-
ity, or snowfall, so the underlying asset of a weather derivative has no intrinsic value and
cannot be stored or traded. However, the weather must be quantiÔ¨Āed to be incorporated into
the weather derivative and this is achieved by introducing temperature, rainfall, precipitation,
or snowfall indices as underlying assets. The list of traded contracts in the weather derivatives

3 Neural networks
Since our approach is based on the use of deep neural networks, in this section, we will
provide a brief description. Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are sophisticated computational
models composed of interconnected neurons arranged in multiple layers. These layers apply
nonlinear transformations to their inputs, allowing the model to capture complex relationships
in the data. DNNs are Ô¨Ātted using training algorithms which iteratively adjust the weights
of the connections based on prediction errors to enhance accuracy. Several types of neural
network layers exist and many successful applications of DNNs in Ô¨Ānance appear in the
literature, see for instance (Chen et al., 2014; Sirignano, 2019; Horvath et al., 2021). They
have also been utilized for the calibration of stochastic models, as discussed in Liu et al.
(2019); Scognamiglio (2022); Corsaro et al. (2024). In the following, we consider the Fully-
Connected Network layer and the Embedding layer, since they are the blocks composing the
network architecture used in this paper.

3.2 Embedding layer
An Embedding Network (EN) layer is designed to acquire a low-dimensional representation
of categorical variable levels. Let qL ‚ąąN denote the hyperparameter determining the size
of the embedding. The categorical variable levels are transformed into a real-valued qL-

learned space reÔ¨āect the similarity of levels concerning the target variable: closely related
levels exhibit small Euclidean distances, while signiÔ¨Ācantly different categories display larger
distances.

The point-wise method discussed above can only model temperature dynamics at speciÔ¨Āc
geographical points where data is available. This method ignores any information about
the temperature measured in the surrounding area, neglecting spatial dependencies among
temperatures in that zone. This inefÔ¨Ācient use of available information is especially apparent
when dealing with satellite data, as we model the temperature of each pixel individually
without considering the temperatures of neighbouring pixels. To address this limitation, we
propose an alternative approach.
5 A spatially-continuous neural network temperature model
Our idea is to deÔ¨Āne the longitude and latitude coordinates u and v on discrete sets and
treat the parameters as continuous functions of these coordinates. This approach allows
for modelling temperature at any real-valued pair of longitude and latitude coordinates. By
doing so, we can evaluate climate risk with greater precision across different regions within
the considered zone. This method improves upon the pixel-wise approach by capturing the
heterogeneity of risk across different areas rather than relying on generalized data from
discrete pixels. Furthermore, by introducing an optimisation scheme in which the model is
calibrated only once for all points in the zone of interest, we exploit spatial dependencies
in temperature dynamics and promote more efÔ¨Ācient use of available data. We deÔ¨Āne this
model as a Spatially-Continuous Neural Network Temperature model (SCNNT) because it
uses neural networks to express the parameters as a function of the data. Additionally, since
neural networks are only used to learn the parameters of the model introduced in Alaton
et al. (2002), we maintain the model explainability. This is crucial for promoting the safe
introduction of neural network models in real-life applications.

6.4 Monte Carlo simulation
We now want to investigate the impact of our model on the evaluation of weather derivative
contracts, that is the expected value of its discounted future payoffs. We consider an HDD
call option whose payoff is deÔ¨Āned in Eq. (2).
Geographical risk results from the distance between the company that wishes to hedge and
the station at which the weather measurement takes place. Currently, no weather derivatives

8 Conclusions
This manuscript introduces a spatially continuous temperature model for weather derivative
pricing. Our idea is to deÔ¨Āne temperature as a function of longitude and latitude, allowing
for the modelling and forecasting of its dynamics at any real-valued pair of coordinates. By
doing so, we can evaluate climate risk with greater precision across different regions within
the considered zone. This method improves upon the pixel-wise approach by capturing the
heterogeneity of risk across different areas. Furthermore, by introducing an optimisation
scheme where the model is calibrated only once for all points in the zone of interest, this
approach exploits spatial dependencies in temperature dynamics and promotes more efÔ¨Ācient
use of available data. The calibration process leverages advanced neural network techniques,
enabling the model to approximate the complex functional relationships between temperature
and geographical coordinates. First, this approach ensures that the model can adapt to varying
climatic conditions and provide accurate predictions across the entire area of interest. On the
other side, since the network architecture we use replicates on the output layer of the structure
of the model proposed in Alaton et al. (2002), we are able to preserve explainability, which
is crucial for promoting the safe use of neural networks in real-life applications.
To demonstrate the effectiveness of our model, we conduct numerical experiments using
NASA-MERRA-2 satellite data, focusing on a rectangular area of 500 pixels that includes
the Italian mainland and surrounding sea. The Italian territory is characterized by signiÔ¨Ācant
latitudinal extension and pronounced morphological differences, such as coastal, mountain-
ous, and continental areas, with notable variations within these categories (e.g., the altitude
difference between the Alps and the Apennines or the seabed depth differences between the
Tyrrhenian and Adriatic seas). Our results show that the SCNNT model effectively captures
the morphological characteristics of Italy and the resulting variations in temperature model
parameters, providing a higher level of detail compared to pixel data. Additionally, we illus-
trate the application of our model to the pricing of Heating Degree Day (HDD) options.
Numerical results reveal the heterogeneity in contract prices based on different geographi-
cal points, highlighting the importance of considering varying risk exposures across different
areas. This underscores the model‚Äôs capability to account for localized climatic risks, thereby
enhancing the precision and reliability of weather derivative pricing. We also present a possi-
ble extension of the proposed model where some parameters vary over the different months
to show its Ô¨āexibility. In this case, we are able to obtain notable improvements in terms of
forecasting accuracy.
Future research will proceed in several directions. First, we plan to incorporate altitude
into our model, as it is a crucial factor in analysing temperature data. Second, we aim to
introduce a dynamic model for temperature volatility, allowing us to account for its evolution
over time. Finally, we intend to explore neural network architectures speciÔ¨Ācally designed
for processing sequential data, such as long short-term memory networks, which would be
particularly useful for leveraging the time series structure of temperature data.
