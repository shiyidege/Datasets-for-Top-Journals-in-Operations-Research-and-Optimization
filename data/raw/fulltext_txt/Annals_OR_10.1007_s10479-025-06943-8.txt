Abstract
The current solutions for weight determination of conflicting stakeholder groups partici­
pating in group decisions apply subjective weight allocation to create the overall priority. 
This paper offers a mathematical solution for the weight allocation by preference vector-
distance minimization using the Euclidean metric. The proposed technique has been tested 
by numerical examples and a real-world case study on an automated vehicle implementa­
tion survey. All results show that from the aspect of rank concordance, measured by Ken­
dall W, Spearman’s Rho, and Garuti indices, the new method outperforms the equal weight 
and the multi-actor multi-criteria method. The proposed weight allocation technique can 
be beneficial for theoreticians and practitioners of multi-criteria decision-making.

1  Introduction
Reaching an acceptable consensus among the decision-makers has always been a primary 
objective in Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) models (Dong & Saaty, 2014). Consensus 
ensures the sustainability of the final decision and the actions taken based on that, since the 
dissatisfaction is mitigated if all decision-makers are considered and they more or less agree 
on the final outcome (Amenta et al., 2021).
1 3

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 357:837–857
However, in theoretical and real-world decision-support we often face problems in which 
conflicting groups of evaluators are involved (Hua et al., 2023) and their incentive, knowl­
edge on the problem and motivation are so different that it is very difficult to reach the 
agreement (Amenta et al., 2021).
Basically, the overall priority is reached by assigning weights (Battulga & Dhakal, 2024) 
to the different sub-groups (stakeholders) of the total evaluator pattern, and the preferences 
of each sub-group are considered by its own weight in the final decision. It is easy to see 
that the values of these weights have a serious impact on the final result, the more the 
stakeholder preferences differ, the bigger influence of the assigned weights can be detected 
(Gidage & Bhide, 2024; Brouwers et al., 2025).
Consequently, assigning weights to the participating sub-groups in the evaluation pro­
cess is a key element and a subject of debates in decision sciences. A straightforward solu­
tion to decide on the assigned weights to the stakeholder groups of a decision is to conduct 
the debate on the stakeholder weights themselves. This is the core idea of the Multi-Actor 
Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA) method created by Macharis et al. (2009). This is a well-
proven and widely spread procedure that provides a framework to address the complexity 
of multiple stakeholder decision-making and strives to reach a final consensus among the 
involved groups (Huang et al., 2025) by assigning those weights to the sub-groups that are 
the outcomes of a negotiation process. After collecting and aggregating the preferences 
of the participants in the survey of the decision-making problem, in MAMCA, a session 
is dedicated for the negotiation of the representatives of each stakeholder group on the 
determination of the stakeholder weights in the final decision, which can be considered as a 
democratic aspect (Perera & Thompson, 2021; Huang et al., 2024).
Even though there are evident merits of the MAMCA method, e.g. stakeholders have the 
possibility to select their own objectives and criteria, or the open negotiation phase among 
the involved groups (Blad et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2024), some elements of the procedure, 
especially determining the stakeholders’ weights subjectively can be biased (Zhang et al., 
2024) and might lead to an overall false decision. Dominance of a stakeholder group might 
occur during this type of conversation or the under-represented group might obtain less 
weight which might refer to a group with less information on the problem (Huang et al., 
2025).
Subjectivity should be avoided in assigning weights to the sub-groups of the evaluators 
to avoid this bias.
In the literature, some objective methods exist to tackle the stakeholder group weight 
determination. Many of them are connected to large-scale decision-making (Ming & Huang, 
2021) because of the high number of participants and their sub-group segmentation.
Consensus Reaching Process (CRP) is often utilized in large-scale decision problems 
(Labella et al., 2018). Since all CRP-s are requiring iterative participation from the evalua­
tors, the problem of time costs and willingness of the stakeholders arise. Moreover, (Liang et 
al., 2024) explicitly stated that it is impractical in case of large-scale decision-making to 
require adjustments from the evaluators to reach an acceptable consensus. Consequently, all 
consensus creation methods that do not need further actions from the decision-makers have 
merit from theoretical and practical aspects as well.
Some consensus creating methods can be found in the literature that use the optimiza­
tion aspect of minimizing distance among decision-maker preferences, however, all of them 
refer to fuzzy environment. Liang et al. (2024) proposed a procedure capable of ordinal and

cardinal consensus creation for incomplete fuzzy preference relations. Zhang and Li (2022) 
connected consensus creation with keeping individual consistency, however, the authors 
applied minimal adjustments in the individual evaluations to reach an acceptable consensus. 
Another interesting endeavour for consensus creation is the work of Li et al. (2022), who 
proposed consistency control while adjusting the scores with assistance in a hesitant fuzzy 
environment. Apart from the obvious benefits of optimization and distance-based consensus 
creation with keeping the individual consistency of the responses, all these papers require 
some adjustments of the evaluations to reach the acceptable final decision.
Based on the arguments above, the question might arise how could we eliminate the pos­
sibly biased element from the MAMCA methodology and substitute it with a more objec­
tive, mathematically proven procedure without requiring adjustments from the evaluators to 
determine the weights of the stakeholders with the objective of reaching a consensus. The 
suggested method should complete the following preconditions.
 
●
Any of the stakeholder groups should not be discriminated and excluded from the final 
decision.
 
●
No further adjustments from the evaluators are required.
 
●
A consensual solution is needed so the approximation of the diverse preferences and 
scoring is the aim to reach the consensus.
 
●
The sum of the allocated weights is 1 considering the group decision-making theoretical 
and practical recommendations.
 
●
The method should produce a feasible and objective solution in every group decision-
making problem.
 
●
The suggested method should outperform the existing benchmark techniques for group 
weight determination from an important aspect of consensus creation and this perfor­
mance must be measurable.
 
●
Especially the equal weight case and the MAMCA weight determination case must be 
tested to prove the relevance of the new method.
Thus, the aim of this paper is to present a new methodology for stakeholder weight determi­
nation that completes all the above mentioned criteria and fill in the existing research gaps:
 
●
Finding a consensus creation method that does not utilize subjective judgments on sub-
group weights.
 
●
Introducing an optimization-based procedure that does not require adjustments of the 
individual scorings or evaluations.
 
●
Presenting a model that is not restricted to fuzzy environment and does not apply fuzzy 
aggregators.
 
●
Amends the MAMCA methodology by addressing its weakest phase, the negotiation on 
the stakeholder weights.
In the following we will describe in detail the concept of our suggested technique, provide 
some numerical examples to prove its efficiency and a case study that applied the MAMCA 
procedure and the new method as well. The basic methodology, the Group Analytic Hier­
archy Process (GAHP) is only relevant for deriving the group weights from the pairwise 
comparison matrices stakeholder-wise but our suggested technique can also be applied more

2  Methodology
2.1  Fundamentals of AHP for m stakeholder groups
AHP applies pairwise comparisons to explore the relation of criteria or alternatives con­
structing the decision hierarchy of the problem to be investigated. These comparisons are 
included in Pairwise Comparison Matrices (PCM-s) which are positive (aij > 0 for ∀i and 
∀j) and reciprocal (aij = 1/aji for ∀i and ∀j). The arbitrary element of a PCM, aij shows 
how many times item i is more important/less important from the decision’s aspect than 
item j. Consequently, for the elements of the main diagonal aii = 1. The general structure 
of a consistent, theoretical Pairwise Comparison Matrix (PCM)

Consistency means the perfect transitivity of the elements of the matrix aik = aij · ajk 
for ∀i, j, k, where 1 ≤i, j, k ≤n. In decision problems, the evaluators are asked to fill 
only the entries above the main diagonal due to the reciprocity in the matrix. Since the 
experiential matrices (gained by the scoring of the decision-makers) are most likely not 
completely consistent, we measure if the inconsistency of the evaluation is tolerable or not 
(Saaty, 1977). We measure the consistency by Saaty’s Consistency Ratio, the threshold is 
0.1 which can be increased for larger PCM-s (over the size 5 × 5) up to 0.3. All experiential 
matrices that do not fulfil the CR criterion are excluded from the analysis.

In 2022 Hamadneh et al. (2022) conducted a stakeholder viewpoint analysis by using the 
MAMCA method on the perspectives of the Autonomous Vehicle (AV) industry.

The researchers surveyed four involved groups all connected to AV-s, the users, the leg­
islators, the operators, and the manufacturers. The participants evaluated objectives and 
criteria through pairwise comparisons, followed the framework of MAMCA, and the weight 
scores were derived by a modified AHP method, by the Parsimonious AHP (PAHP). The 
PAHP reduced the number of pairwise comparisons but contained the consistency check 
procedure and the final outcome were the normalized preference vectors just as in AHP.
Altogether 50 participants evaluated the survey questionnaire with acceptable inconsis­
tency, 30 users, 6 legislators, 8 operators, and 6 manufacturers. Within each stakeholder 
group, the individual evaluations were aggregated by the conventional Group AHP method.
Note that the MAMCA procedure allows the stakeholders to identify their own objec­
tives and criteria related to the main goal of the survey. However, there can be common 
objectives or criteria that are evaluated by many or all of the stakeholders. In the real-world 
example demonstrated in this section, six criteria were evaluated by all stakeholder groups: 
cyber security, data privacy, cost of travel, travel time, accessibility, and safety of travel. 
These criteria were pairwise compared based on the question: ‘In your opinion how much 
more important is criterion ‘a’ compared to criterion ‘b’ in the implementation of AV-s in 
the near future?’
As a part of the MAMCA procedure, the participants had to assign weights to the stake­
holder groups to create the final ranking of the objectives and criteria. Finally, 57 evaluators 
agreed in the following weights for the stakeholders: Users 0.4, Legislators 0.31, Operators 
0.16, Manufacturers 0.13. Note that the MAMCA weights are also normalized and sum to 1 
just as in the case of our proposed method.

5  Conclusion
Determining the weights of the stakeholders should not only be based on subjective evalua­
tions because it is really difficult to allocate weights to more or less conflicting groups with­
out the risk of biased final decisions. In the current paper, we proposed the weight allocation 
by a vector-distance minimization process using the Euclidean distance metric.
The proposed method satisfies the conditions of being non-discriminative to any of the 
stakeholder groups, the final solution can be considered consensual due to the approxima­
tion nature of the technique, the weights sum up to 1, and the method is feasible and objec­
tive, moreover, does not require adjustments from the evaluators.
1 3
852

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 357:837–857
The numerical examples conducted on generated matrices with appropriate consistency 
and the real-world case study based on real evaluations both indicated that the proposed 
method is more efficient than the equal weight or the MAMCA methods in terms of rank 
concordance with the stakeholder prioritization.
Kendall W index, the Spearman’s Rho method and Garuti index were utilized to demon­
strate the better rank-correlation and vector compatibility among each stakeholder and the 
overall preference vector.
Utilizing our proposed technique, the MAMCA methodology can be amended by the 
objective stakeholder weight determination which could substantially contribute to the 
improvement of the procedure by mitigating the risk of false weight determination.
As a limitation, we emphasize that more experiments are necessary to examine the effi­
ciency of the proposed method, i.e. simulations might be beneficial to conduct large-scale 
calculations on the rank concordance of the calculated optimized weight technique. Even 
though in real-world decision-making the number of (conflicting) stakeholder groups are 
limited to four or five, it would be interesting to test the proposed technique on more groups 
and examine its efficiency.
As a remark for further research, the boundaries of AHP could be relaxed and other multi-
criteria decision-making methods could also be considered that provide normalized prefer­
ence vectors. Different metrics could be a subject of investigation, especially the Aitchison 
or Manhattan distances might be tested if they could produce better rank-correlation than 
the Euclidean distance. Also, the examination of the proposed method in fuzzy environment 
can be interesting and can provide a rich field to discover for the theoreticians and practitio­
ners of decision-making.
