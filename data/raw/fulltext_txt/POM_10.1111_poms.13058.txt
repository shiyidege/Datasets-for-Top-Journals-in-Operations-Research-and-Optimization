Abstract 
In this paper, we study a multi-period stochastic perishable inventory system with multiple de­
mand classes that require products of di erent ages. The frm orders the product with a positive 
leadtime and sells it to multiple demand classes, each only accepting products with remaining 
lifetime longer than a threshold. In each period, after demand realization, the frm decides how 
to allocate the on-hand inventory to di erent demand classes with di erent backorder or lost-
sale cost. At the end of each period, the frm can dispose inventory of any age. We formulate 
this problem as a Markov decision process and characterize the optimal ordering, allocation, 
and disposal policies. When unfulflled demand is backlogged, we show that the optimal order 
quantity is decreasing in the inventory levels and is more sensitive to the inventory level of 
fresher products, the optimal allocation policy is a sequential rationing policy, and the optimal 
disposal policy is characterized by a set of thresholds. For the lost-sale case, we show that 
the optimal allocation and disposal policies have the same structure but the optimal ordering 
policy may be di erent. Based on the structure of the optimal policy, we develop an eÿcient 
heuristic that is at most 4% away from the optimal cost in our numerical examples. Using 
numerical studies, we show that the ordering and allocation policies are close to optimal even 
if the frm cannot intentionally dispose products. Moreover, ignoring the di erence between 
demand classes and using a simple allocation policy (e.g., FIFO) can signifcantly increase the 
total cost. We examine how the frm can improve the control of perishable items and show that 
the beneft of decreasing the leadtime is more signifcant than that of increasing the lifetime 
of the products or that of decreasing the acceptance threshold of the demand. The analysis is 
extended to systems with age dependent disposal cost and stochastic supply. 
Keywords: Perishable inventory systems; multiple demand classes; stochastic optimal control; 
demand fulfllment and rationing; multimodularity

Introduction 
The control of perishable products is becoming increasingly important in supply chain management. 
Mismanagement of perishable products can lead to signifcant loss of proft for companies in the 
grocery retailing industry and signifcant spoilage of drugs in hospitals. For example, according to 
Buzby et al. (2014), the estimated total value of food loss at the retail level in the United States 
was $53.8 billion in 2010. The 2011 National Blood Collection and Utilization Survey reported 
that 4.3% of all components of blood processed for transfusion were outdated in 2011 in the USA. 
Recognizing the economic and social importance of perishable products, researchers have conducted 
substantial work on perishable inventory problems. However, the perishable inventory problem with 
multiple demand classes with di erent requirements for the age of products, which is the focus of 
this paper, has attracted only limited attention. 
Suppliers of perishable products usually face several classes of customers who have their own 
requirements. 
For example, consider a processed meat producer facing several customer types 
including a retailer and a fast food restaurant. 
At the retailer store, the meat products may 
remain on the shelf for several days before being sold and customers want to ensure that they 
can use the products for a period of time. Thus, the retailer expects to receive fresh products 
to ensure there is enough time till the units are expired. However, the restaurant may not be as 
sensitive to the age of products as the retailer since the products are used to make food for direct 
consumption, and as a results, it may accept products with a shorter remaining lifetime. Given 
the di erent customers’ requirements, the supplier may provide price incentives to customers and 
charge di erent prices depending on the remaining shelf life to ensure the older units are sold 
before being expired. In addition, at the end of each day, the supplier may donate the surplus of 
its stock to a local food bank, where products will be consumed fast. Another example is a blood 
product supply chain where hospitals need fresh blood products for specifc patients or operations. 
For example, patients with a major bleeding caused by trauma or surgery can be transfused with 
platelets of any age, while patients with a platelet function disorder, such as leukemia, need to be 
transfused with fresh platelets that are stocked for at most 3 days (see, e.g., Haijema et al. 2005 and 
Deniz et al. 2010). Similarly, suppliers of many other perishable items, such as dairy, fowers, and 
pharmaceutical drugs, may deliver products of di erent ages to retailers in di erent geographical

locations. Retailers located far from the supplier usually require products with longer remaining 
lifetime since the transit time is longer. Local retailers may accept products that are relatively 
older. 
Motivated by the above examples, in this paper, we examine how ordering and allocation of 
products with di erent ages to di erent demand classes a ect the total cost of the system. Specif­
ically, we consider the joint ordering, allocation, and disposal policy for a multi-period stochastic 
perishable inventory system. The frm orders the product from a supplier that has ample capacity 
and delivers the ordered quantities after a fxed leadtime, so that the frm receives products with 
remaining lifetime n. The frm sells the product to multiple demand classes, each only accepting 
products with remaining lifetime longer than a threshold. In each period, after demand realization, 
the frm decides how to allocate the on-hand inventory to the di erent demand classes. Unfulflled 
demand is backordered and di erent demand classes may have di erent backorder costs. At the 
end of each period, the frm can dispose inventory of any age. The total cost we consider includes 
holding, shortage, and disposal over the entire planning horizon. We formulate this problem as a 
Markov decision process and show that the optimal cost function is multimodular. This allows us 
to derive the optimal ordering, allocation, and disposal policies. 
In this paper, we make the following key contributions: 
(1) As far as we know, our paper is the frst to examine the optimal joint ordering, allocation, 
and disposal decision for a perishable inventory system with multiple demand classes that require 
products of di erent ages. Previous literature that studies perishable inventory systems with multi­
ple demand classes usually focuses on identifying e ective heuristics for a certain type of policy. For 
example, Haijema et al. (2005, 2007, 2009) use simulation to construct several simple heuristics for 
the ordering policy that are nearly optimal under a given allocation and disposal policies. Civelek 
et al. (2015) propose a critical level (or protection level) allocation policy under some practical 
replenishment policies and show that this policy leads to superior performance compared to exist­
ing heuristics. However, none of them examine the optimal joint ordering, allocation, and disposal 
policy or propose heuristics for the joint policy. Our paper flls in this gap in the literature. 
We characterize the structure of the optimal ordering, allocation, and disposal policies for the 
case with general m(≤ n) demand classes. In particular, we show that, as expected, the optimal 
order quantity is decreasing in the inventory levels and is more sensitive to the inventory of the 
2

 Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 
fresher products. We establish that the optimal allocation policy is a sequential rationing allocation 
policy: frst satisfy the demand with the highest priority, then the demand with the second highest 
priority, and so on, such that while fulflling each demand class, it is optimal to ration fresher 
products to certain thresholds for future demand with higher priority, and use the rest to fulfll 
demand of that class as much as possible following the First-In First-Out (FIFO) policy. We fnd 
that the optimal disposal policy is characterized by n − 1 thresholds, where each threshold specifes 
the reservation level for products with a particular remaining lifetime. At the end of each period, 
only if the inventory levels are larger than the thresholds, it is optimal to dispose the surplus. 
We note that Chen et al. (2014) study the joint pricing and inventory control problem for 
perishable products with a single demand class. In that paper the focus is on characterization 
of the joint inventory and pricing decisions. Subject to the assumption that each demand class 
accepts products of all ages, they claim that the results of the single demand class model can 
be extended to models with multiple demand classes. In contrast, in this paper, we assume that 
di erent demand classes require products of di erent ages. Consequently, the optimal allocation 
policies are di erent. For example, Chen et al. (2014) indicate that “for any given total amount 
allocation, it is always optimal to meet the demands to the maximum extent from the highest 
priority to the lowest priority”1 and thus the optimal allocation policy can be characterized by this 
single amount. This is not true in our model. Instead, we show the optimal allocation policy is 
a sequential rationing policy. Moreover, as we discuss below, the approach in Chen et al. (2014) 
cannot be applied in our model. 
(2) We show that the structure of the optimal policy is robust with respect to di erent assump­
tions and the optimal ordering and allocation policies are close to optimal even if the frm cannot 
intentionally dispose products. Moreover, we show that it is important to consider the di erence in 
the requirements on the age of acceptable products between demand classes. Ignoring this fact and 
using simple policies (e.g., FIFO) can signifcantly increase the total cost. This also suggests that 
the heuristics proposed in Haijema et al. (2005, 2007, 2009) may perform poorly when compared to 
the optimal joint policy. Based on the structure of the optimal joint policy, we develop an e ective 
and computationally eÿcient heuristic which is at most 4% away from the optimal cost in our 
numerical examples.

(3) We examine how the frm can improve the control of perishable items. Clearly, the frm can 
reduce its total cost and the number of products disposed by decreasing the leadtime, increasing 
the lifetime of the products, or increasing customers’ willingness to accept older products. We 
show that, among the above three strategies, decreasing leadtime is the most eÿcient (with a 
potential cost beneft of 20% in our numerical examples). Increasing the lifetime is more eÿcient 
than increasing customers’ willingness to accept older products. 
(4) We also make a contribution from the analysis perspective. Characterizing the structure of 
the optimal policy and deriving the comparative statics of the optimal thresholds and the optimal 
order quantity rely on the property of “multimodularity”. This concept (or an equivalent concept 
“L♮-convexity”) has been used to characterize the structure of the optimal policy for many inven­
tory systems; see, for example, Zipkin (2008), Huh and Janakiraman (2010), Chen et al. (2014), 
and Li and Yu (2014). In these papers, to show that the optimal value function is multimodular 
or L♮-convex, the idea is to frst show that multimodularity or L♮-convexity is preserved for con­
straints with a certain structure and then change the constraints in the original dynamic program 
to new ones that have this structure. However, when there are multiple demand classes that re­
quire products of di erent ages, this approach does not work. Therefore, we use the defnition of 
multimodularity to show that after state transformation, the new function is submodular. This 
requires showing that submodularity is preserved under minimization with a non-lattice constraint, 
which is not trivial. 
Literature Review 
There is a large body of literature on inventory problems with perishable products. Broadly speak­
ing, there are two streams in this literature, periodic review and continuous review; see the recent 
reviews by Nahmias (1982, 2011) focusing on periodic review, Baron (2010) focusing on continuous 
review, and Karaesmen et al. (2011) that includes both. Our paper belongs to the periodic review 
stream and we next focus on it. 
In a setting with zero leadtime and lifetime of two periods, Nahmias and Pierskalla (1973) frst 
study the optimal ordering policy for a perishable inventory system with a single stochastic demand 
class. Then, Nahmias (1975) and Fries (1975) characterize the structure of the optimal policy for the 
4 
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 

 Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 
general lifetime problem with zero leadtime, in backorder models and lost-sales models, respectively. 
They show that the optimal ordering quantity depends on the levels of on-hand inventory of di erent 
ages. Because of the complexity and the state dependency, implementation of the optimal policy 
is diÿcult. Therefore, several simple and e ective heuristic policies were proposed and examined, 
for example, NIS policy (Brodheim et al. 1975), TIS policy (Nahmias 1976, 1977; Nandakumar 
and Morton 1993; Cooper 2001; Haijema 2011), proportional-balancing policy (Chao et al. 2015), 
dual-balancing policy (Chao et al. 2015; Zhang et al. 2016), and truncated-balancing policy (Zhang 
et al. 2016), among others. All these papers make two common assumptions: there is a single class 
of demand and only the excess inventory that expires at the end of each period is disposed. 
Recently, there is a growing body of literature on perishable inventory systems that allows 
inventory of any age to be salvaged. Xue et al. (2012) study a perishable inventory model where 
the surplus of stock can be sold (i.e., disposed) in a secondary market with some salvage value. 
They characterize the structure of the optimal policy and then propose a heuristic. By employing 
the concept of multimodularity, Li and Yu (2014) revisit the same problem and show that the 
optimal policy is monotone with bounded sensitivity. Chen et al. (2014) consider the joint pricing 
and inventory control problem for this model and allow for positive lead time. Haijema (2014) 
numerically investigates cost reductions that can be achieved by an optimal stock-age dependent 
ordering, issuing, or disposal policy. Li et al. (2016) study the problem of joint replenishment and 
clearance sales of perishable goods under a Last-In First-Out (LIFO) issuing rule and show that the 
optimal policy for clearance sales is characterized by two thresholds for each age group of inventory. 
All the papers discussed focus on a single demand class. 
There is limited literature that studies perishable inventory systems with multiple demand 
classes. Much of the literature either considers a single period problem (see, for example, Ishii 
1993; Ishii and Nose 1996) or focuses on identifying e ective heuristics for a certain type of policy 
(see, for example, Haijema et al. 2005, 2009; Deniz et al. 2010; Civelek et al. 2015). None of them 
examine the optimal joint ordering, allocation, and disposal policy or propose heuristics for the 
joint policy. Our paper flls in this gap in the literature. 
Finally, our work is related to the literature on inventory rationing and capacity management 
with multiple capacity types and multiple demand classes. Topkis (1968) considers the allocation 
of non-perishable inventory to multiple demand classes in a multi-period setting and shows that 
5 
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 

3 
 Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 
a static rationing policy is optimal. Shumsky and Zhang (2009) consider a multi-period capacity 
allocation model with single-step upgrading. They show that it is optimal to use any available 
capacity to satisfy same-class demand, and then upgrade customers until capacity reaches a pro­
tection limit. Yu et al. (2015) extends this model to allow general upgrading and show that a 
sequential upgrading policy is optimal. A common assumption in this literature is that there is 
no replenishment opportunity during the selling season. However, our model allows the frm to 
replenish inventory in each period. 
Model with Two Demand Classes 
We consider a single item multi-period stochastic inventory control problem where a frm serves two 
demand classes. The supplier has ample capacity and delivers the order quantities after a leadtime 
L so that orders placed in period t arrive in period t+L. The planning horizon consists of T +L ≥ 1 
discrete time periods. Time t = 1 is the frst period and time t = T + L is the last period. The 
product is perishable and has a lifetime of n + L periods. Thus, products have remaining lifetime 
of n periods when the frm receives them. We say a product is at age j if it has remaining lifetime 
of n + L − j periods. In the rest of the paper, we simply use products with lifetime i to denote 
products that have remaining lifetime of i periods and we only use this terminology (remaining 
lifetime).

Managerial Implications 
In this section, we provide more managerial insights into our problem. We restrict ourselves to the 
case with two demand classes. The results for cases with n demand classes are similar. We consider 
20 
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 

 
 
 Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3191819 
two important managerial questions in the control of perishable items. 
1. How robust and e ective is the optimal control? 
We consider this question in terms of: (i) How robust is the optimal policy we derive to other 
settings, including settings with an infnite planning horizon, settings with lostsales, and settings 
with no intentional disposal? (ii) How signifcant is the cost advantage of the optimal policy in 
comparison to a simple heuristic and what part of the control, ordering, rationing, or disposing, is 
more important? (iii) Does the curse of dimensionality for dynamic programming makes calculating 
the optimal policy too time consuming in practice? 
From our results we fnd that (a) the structure of the optimal policy is robust to di erent 
assumptions and the optimal ordering and allocation policies are close to optimal even if the frm 
cannot dispose products; (b) ignoring di erence between demand classes and using simple policies 
can signifcantly increase the total cost. We also develop an e ective and computationally-eÿcient 
heuristic which is at most 5% away from the optimal in our numerical examples. 
2. How can the frm improve the control of perishables? 
We consider this question by examining the impact of the leadtime, the lifetime, and the demand 
class. Clearly, the frm can reduce its inventory control costs by (a) increasing the products’ lifetime, 
(b) decreasing the products’ leadtime, and (c) increasing customers’ willingness to accept older 
products. We show that the beneft of decreasing the leadtime is more signifcant than the beneft 
of increasing the lifetime of the products and the beneft of increasing the lifetime of the products 
is more signifcant than the beneft of decreasing the acceptance threshold of the demand.

In this subsection, we answer the frst question: how robust and e ective is the optimal policy? We

First, our results can be extended to the case with non-stationary parameters, with any l1, . . . , ln,

also hold in the setting with an infnite planning horizon under the expected total discounted cost

and stochastic, our results continue to hold.

Conclusion 
In this paper, we presented a new model for perishable systems with multiple demand classes 
that require products of di erent ages. We characterized the structure of the optimal ordering, 
allocation, and disposal policies. 
We showed that the optimal order quantity is decreasing in 
the inventory levels and is more sensitive to the inventory level of fresher products, the optimal 
allocation policy is a sequential rationing policy, and the optimal disposal policy is characterized 
by n − 1 thresholds. We show that the optimal policy is e ective and robust. We examined how 
the frm can improve the control of perishable items and showed that the beneft of decreasing the 
leadtime is more signifcant than the beneft of increasing the lifetime of the products or the beneft 
of decreasing the acceptance threshold of the demand. We also developed an e ective heuristic 
which is at most 4% away from the optimal cost in our numerical examples. 
There are several possible avenues for future research. It would be useful to incorporate the 
pricing decision into our problem. Since there are multiple demand classes with di erent require­
ments on the age of acceptable products, it would be of interest to investigate how to set prices for 
di erence demand classes. It would also be interesting to consider a two-echelon perishable inven­
tory system with a warehouse and several distribution centers, where each center requires products 
of a suÿcient remaining shelf life. It would then be useful to examine how to order products for the 
central warehouse and how to allocation products of di erent ages to di erent distribution centers.
