Abstract
This paper introduces a comprehensive framework for detecting and conceptualizing hetero-
geneity in data envelopment analysis (DEA), aligning with the microeconomic production
theory. Despite DEA’s signiﬁcant advantage in evaluating DMUs based on their efﬁciency
without assuming a speciﬁc functional form of technology, it critically relies on the com-
parability of these units. We address the persistent issue in DEA modeling that stems from
the assumption of homogeneity among DMUs, which is often untested. We propose a novel
methodological approach that serves as a testing framework for heterogeneity, predicated on
minimal assumptions about data randomness. This framework provides a means to examine
the biases introduced by technological disparities among DMUs and offers an approach for
practitioners to ensure the validity of DEA modeling across diverse technological settings.
This approach not only uncovers biases from technological differences but also serves as a
preliminary step to enhance methods like clustering, aiding practitioners in verifying DEA’s
applicability across varied technologies.

1 Introduction
This paper provides a framework to conceptualize and test heterogeneity in data envelopment
analysis (DEA) taking into account the principles of production theory of microeconomics.
DEA is a method used to rank a set of decision-making units (DMUs) according to their
efﬁciency performance (Banker et al., 1984; Charnes et al., 1978; Emrouznejad & Yang,
2018). The method is grounded in the concept of the production possibility frontier (PPF)
that represents the combination of outputs attainable with a speciﬁc set of inputs, contingent
upon the currently available technology. One of the key advantages of DEA is its ability
to evaluate the relative efﬁciency of DMUs by analyzing their input–output relationships,
regardless of any speciﬁc functional form underlying the technology. While DEA does not
rely on any particular technology, it does operate under the fundamental assumption that

DMUs are comparable (Banker et al., 1984; Cooper et al., 2007). This assumption ensures
that the efﬁciency comparisons made across DMUs are meaningful and that they share a
common production technology, allowing their input–output relationships to be evaluated
using a single production possibility frontier (PPF). This pivotal assumption must be fulﬁlled
to ensure a meaningful evaluation of the relative performance of DMUs, and subsequent
analyses such as benchmarking.
In light of this assumption, one ongoing issue concerning DEA modeling revolves around
determining whether DMUs are sufﬁciently homogenous or similar to ensure the underlying
assumption of DEA is met (Cook & Seiford, 2009). This has prompted the development
of two primary practical solutions in the literature, which involve either clustering DMUs
into homogeneous groups or incorporating additional constraints into the DEA formulation
(see Sect. 2). While these solutions present novel insights into performance measurement,
the concept of comparable DMUs lacks a solid theoretical basis. This is primarily because
the very essence of DMU comparison is rooted in the observation of distinct performances.
Hence, modeling heterogeneity in DEA, instantly raises questions regarding which DMUs
are truly comparable, the extent to which dissimilarities are permissible, and the conditions
under which DEA modeling remains valid across varying degrees of dissimilarity.
In this paper, we assert that dissimilarities among DMUs pose a signiﬁcant bias in per-
formance measurement when stemming from the utilization of different technologies by
DMUs. In particular, this situation necessitates comparisons that rely on distinct PPFs, effec-
tively reﬂecting the technological disparities among the DMUs. To illustrate this concept,
we revisit production theory in microeconomics and examine the potential bias that can arise
in DEA modeling when DMUs utilize different production technologies, especially when
DEA fails to account for these disparities. Our theoretical discussion is complemented by
a testing framework designed to detect heterogeneity in DEA modeling, which operates on
minimal assumptions regarding the randomness present in the data. The proposed framework
is intended to serve as a preliminary step in DEA analysis, aimed at detecting and addressing
technological heterogeneity among DMUs before any performance measurements are com-
puted. By identifying these disparities early, the method helps to remove biases that could
otherwise distort DEA performance evaluations, ensuring that subsequent comparisons are
conducted on a more homogeneous basis.

modeling heterogeneity in Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA). In Sect. 3, we apply produc-

for testing the hypothesis of homogeneity, and Sect. 5 presents an empirical application of
the proposed testing framework. Sections 6 and 7 provide the discussion and conclusions

2 Background
In the realm of Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA), heterogeneity refers to the variation in the
production processes or environmental conditions across the decision-making units (DMUs)
being evaluated (Halkos & Tsionas, 2019). DEA is designed to assess the relative efﬁciencies
of DMUs that convert multiple inputs into outputs (Azizi, 2014; Bowlin, 1998), but when
these units operate under varied conditions—be it technological, scale, or environmental
differences—this can lead to an unfair or distorted efﬁciency comparison (Banker et al., 2004;
Paradi & Zhu, 2013; Wang et al., 2011; Yu & Chen, 2020). The importance of comparability

among DMUs operating under similar technological conditions is recognized as vital for
accuratelyevaluatingtheirinput–outputrelationshipsusingacommonPPF.ThePPFservesas
the boundary of a technology set, deﬁning the maximum feasible output that can be achieved
given a set of inputs. Different groups of ﬁrms (or DMUs) may have distinct frontiers due
to variations in technological conditions under which they operate (O’Donnell et al., 2008).
Thus, technological heterogeneity acknowledges that DMUs may not be operating on a
common production frontier due to these differences (Ahikiriza et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
2018). Ignoring heterogeneity can lead to inaccurate efﬁciency scores (Geffersa, 2022).
To account for heterogeneity in DEA, two main approaches are commonly employed
(Banker et al., 1984; Chen et al., 2020; Podinovski, 2004): weight restrictions (Zhu, 2022)
and clustering along with meta-frontier analysis (Battese & Rao, 2002; Cook & Zhu, 2007;
O’Donnell et al., 2008). Weight restrictions in DEA are a form of a priori information that
can be incorporated into the analysis to reﬂect the decision-makers’ preferences or to mirror
market conditions (Tracy & Chen, 2005). These restrictions serve as bounds on the input
and output weights, ensuring that the DEA model does not give undue importance to any
particular DMU characteristic that could distort efﬁciency scores (Lins et al., 2007; Pedraja-
Chaparro et al., 1997). This method is especially crucial when evaluating units that are not
directly comparable due to their operational differences. According to Allen et al. (1997),
weight restrictions can be applied in various forms, such as assurance region (AR) or cone
ratio (CR) restrictions. These methods effectively narrow down the weight ﬂexibility in the
DEA model, ensuring that the calculated efﬁciencies are not a result of unrealistic weight
distributions. For instance, an AR approach sets bounds based on the proportionality between
inputs or outputs, while CR approaches restrict weights to a speciﬁc range, reﬂecting relative
importance or market valuations. Weight restrictions can help address the issue of non-
homogeneity by ensuring comparability among DMUs (Podinovski, 1999; Cook et al., 2015).
This is particularly relevant when DMUs operate in different contexts or when there is a need
to evaluate across diverse groups. The restrictions can be applied when reliable a priori
information is available, as they can be designed to align with the operational realities of
DMUs. However, in scenarios where such information is lacking, the imposition of weight
restrictions becomes a subjective exercise, potentially leading to arbitrary and misleading
efﬁciency assessments. As Cooper et al. (2000) argue, without solid a priori information,
weight restrictions may be based on speculative or biased judgments, which can unjustly
penalize certain DMUs or unduly beneﬁt others.
Another approach to address non-homogeneity in DEA is the use of clustering, which
entails the categorization of DMUs into subsets based on shared operational attributes prior
to applying DEA (Cook et al., 2007; Triantis et al., 2010). This method ensures that efﬁciency
assessments are conducted within homogenous clusters, enhancing the comparability of
DMUs by aligning them with peers operating under similar conditions. The rationale is that
DMUs within a cluster share a common production environment and technology, making
intra-cluster efﬁciency comparisons valid (Najadat et al., 2020). The use of clustering is
particularly advantageous in multi-environmental or multi-technology contexts, as it can
create homogenous sub-sets of DMUs that are comparable. Cook and Zhu (2007) highlight
that the key to effective clustering is the selection of relevant characteristics that reﬂect
operational similarities. These characteristics can range from input and output measures to
external environmental factors. Upon establishing clusters, DEA can be applied within each
group. However, while clustering allows for a more tailored analysis within groups, it does not
facilitate a direct efﬁciency comparison across them. This limitation is where meta-frontier
analysis becomes applicable (Battese et al., 2004). The meta-frontier is derived from the
notion that while DMUs may be operating under different conditions and technologies, there

The validity of clustering as an approach rests on solid evidence that substantiates the
existence of non-homogeneity among DMUs. More speciﬁcally, when clustering is applied,
DMUs are grouped based on shared characteristics, with the aim of achieving homogeneity
within clusters and heterogeneity between them. This approach naturally leads to different
efﬁciency frontiers for each cluster, reﬂecting the unique operational context of the grouped
units. Intra-cluster analysis allows for the comparison of DMUs against a frontier com-
posed of similar units, thereby yielding results that are internally consistent and comparable.
Conversely, within-cluster results are expected to diverge signiﬁcantly from those of other
clusters due to the deliberate segregation of DMUs based on distinct operational environ-
ments and technologies. The differentiation in results between and within clusters is a direct
consequence of the clustering process. It underscores the importance of having a robust evi-
dentiary basis for assuming non-homogeneity. Without such evidence, clustering could be
arbitrary, potentially leading to misleading conclusions about efﬁciency. As Cooper et al.
(2000) articulate, the presumption of non-homogeneity should be grounded in empirical
realities, such as variations in production conditions, technological disparities, or differing
regulatory frameworks that affect the operations of DMUs.
In this paper, we introduce a testing framework designed to identify heterogeneity issues
before the implementation of the above-mentioned methods. This framework can be viewed
as a complementary step to existing approaches, offering an empirical basis for the need to
accountfortechnologicalheterogeneityinDEAmodeling.Thetestingframeworkproposedin
this paper is part of a broader category of methodologies designed to address the heterogeneity
issue in DEA, with notable contributions from Daraio et al. (2018) and Kneip et al. (2016).
While Daraio et al. (2018) explored the separability condition and proposed a method
to test for environmental heterogeneity in DEA, they approached it from a different angle.
Separability condition testing primarily investigates whether environmental variables affect
only the inefﬁciency distribution among DMUs or also inﬂuence the production frontier itself.
In this context, environmental variables refer to factors that are neither inputs nor outputs,
typically beyond the manager’s control, yet they may impact the production process. In
contrast, our discussion in this paper extends beyond the scope of environmental variables
as a third class of variables. Technological disparities among DMUs may be inﬂuenced by
environmental variables, but these disparities can also exist and be detected independently of
them. Speciﬁcally, even if the impact of environmental variables on the production frontier
is rejected, it does not necessarily mean that all DMUs operate under a single, common
frontier. Thus, we argue in this paper that technological heterogeneity must be considered a
distinct and crucial factor when evaluating the comparability of DMUs in DEA, beyond just
the inﬂuence of environmental variables.
In another study, Kneip et al. (2016) propose a method to test whether mean efﬁciency
levels differ between groups of DMUs, which can indicate underlying differences in technol-
ogy or operational conditions. Their approach focuses on signiﬁcant differences in efﬁciency
means across groups. In contrast, our proposed framework examines the distribution of slack
values, acknowledging that while efﬁciency scores reﬂect overall performance, they may not
distinguish between different types of inefﬁciencies. Two DMUs might have similar efﬁ-
ciency scores, yet one could have signiﬁcant slacks in certain inputs or outputs, indicating
disparities in their respective PPFs not captured by efﬁciency scores. By testing whether

Here, the term technological heterogeneity refers to variations in the underlying technology
across DMUs. Technological heterogeneity results in varying capabilities among DMUs to
transform inputs into outputs, leading to distinct data generating processes. To quantify the
divergence in these processes based on observed data, various metrics can be employed. One
well-established measure is the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD), a statistical tool used
to assess the similarity between two probability distributions (Menéndez et al., 1997). JSD
operates by computing the average of the Kullback–Leibler Divergence (KLD) between each
distribution and the mixture distribution of the two.1
This approach allows for a comprehensive comparison that captures both the similarities
and differences between the underlying data generating processes. While JSD is not a direct
test for heterogeneity within the DEA framework, it is a powerful metric for quantifying
the extent of heterogeneity post-simulation using observed data. By utilizing JSD, we can
effectively demonstrate the severity of technological heterogeneity captured in the simulation
based on the observed distributions, independent of any prior knowledge about the underly-
ing generation mechanism. Mathematically, the Jensen-Shannon Divergence is expressed as
follows,

4.1 Statistical analysis of heterogeneity
The Kolmogorov–Smirnov (K–S) test stands out in statistical analysis as a non-parametric
method for evaluating the distributional properties of a dataset (Kolmogorov, 1933; Smirnov,
1939). Unlike many traditional tests, it does not rely on assumptions regarding the underlying
distributional form of the data, making it a powerful tool for empirical research. Origi-
nally devised for univariate distributions, the K–S test has been extended to accommodate
multivariate data. The multivariate K–S test measures the distance between the empirical
distribution function of the sample and the cumulative distribution function of the reference
distribution. In our analysis, we choose a uniform distribution as the reference. This choice
is based on the assumption that, in the absence of heterogeneity, slack values would be
uniformly distributed across DMUs. The uniform distribution serves as a neutral baseline,
allowing us to detect signiﬁcant deviations that indicate heterogeneity. The null hypothesis
states that the sample is drawn from the reference distribution. A signiﬁcant K–S statistic
indicates that the sample distribution function deviates from the reference, suggesting that
the sample may come from a different distribution.
One of the principal strengths of the K–S test is its ﬂexibility regarding distributional
assumptions.Whileitdoesrequireaspeciﬁedreferencedistribution—inthiscase,theuniform
distribution—it does not necessitate that the data conform to normality, exponentiality, or
any other speciﬁc distribution. This distinguishes it from tests such as the t-test or chi-square
test, which assume normality and independence of observations. Moreover, the K–S test is
scale and location invariant, meaning it treats all continuous distributions equally regardless
of their mean and variance. This characteristic is crucial when comparing datasets across
different scales or units of measurement. When compared to distribution-dependent methods
like the Anderson–Darling test or Shapiro–Wilk test, which are more powerful for detecting
departures from normality, the K–S test provides a more generalized approach suitable for
our study’s objectives.
The K–S test lies in its measurement of the largest distance between the empirical distribu-
tion function (EDF) of the observed data and the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a
reference distribution, or between two empirical distributions. For a given sample of n obser-
vations, the empirical distribution function Fn(χ) at a point χ is deﬁned as the proportion of
sample points less than or equal to χ. Mathematically, it is represented as,

5 Empirical application
The effectiveness of the proposed heterogeneity testing framework in DEA is best illustrated
through a practical application. In this section, we apply our framework to the study conducted
byHenriquesetal.(2018),whichevaluatestheefﬁciencyof37Brazilianbanksovertheperiod
from 2012 to 2016. This empirical setting is particularly well-suited for testing our framework
due to the diversity of the banks analyzed, ranging from large national institutions to smaller
regional entities.
The Brazilian banking sector presents a compelling case for examining technological
heterogeneity. Previous studies, including Henriques et al. (2018), have highlighted the sig-
niﬁcant differences in technological processes across these banks. Larger banks could often
operate with more advanced technologies since they have the requisite resources whereas
smaller banks typically could use less technologically advanced technologies. These dif-
ferences suggest the presence of underlying technological disparities that, if not properly
accounted for, could lead to biased efﬁciency analyses in DEA.
To address these potential biases, we utilize the heterogeneity testing framework as a
preliminary step before further DEA modeling. Our goal is to identify whether these banks,
when assessed together, exhibit technological homogeneity or heterogeneity. This analysis
not only tests the validity of the DEA models used by Henriques et al. (2018) but also
reﬁnes the approach by integrating insights derived from the heterogeneity framework. By
systematically applying K–S test to the slack values derived from the DEA, we assess whether
the assumption of homogeneity holds or if distinct technological proﬁles exist among the
banks in the study.
Henriques et al. (2018), speciﬁed three inputs—ﬁxed assets, total deposits, personnel
expenses—and one output, total loans, to evaluate the performance of Brazilian banks using
standard DEA models (CCR and BCC). To apply the testing framework, we follow the
input–output conﬁguration speciﬁed in the paper and given the assumed differences in the
technologies of the banks in the sample, we focused primarily on the BCC model. This
model accommodates variable returns to scale, allowing us to capture the nuanced efﬁciency
dynamics between larger and smaller banks. Each bank-year combination was treated as a
separate DMU, resulting in multiple observations per bank across the period from 2012 to
2016.

distributional characteristics of these values. The slack values, which represent the inefﬁcien-

the targeted output on the efﬁcient frontier and the actual output of a DMU. Then we tested
the distribution against various reference distributions to determine whether they followed a
uniform or normal distribution. The K–S test was applied in the following steps,

6 Discussion
In this section, we expand our discussion to address the critical aspects of technological het-
erogeneity within the DEA framework, focusing on both the theoretical underpinnings and
practical applications of our proposed methodology. We ﬁrst examine the rationale behind
the homogeneity assumption, particularly its relevance and validity in the context of DEA.
123

1554
Annals of Operations Research (2025) 351:1537–1558
Following this, we explore the practical implications of detecting technological heterogene-
ity, emphasizing the importance of this preliminary analysis step in ensuring accurate and
meaningful efﬁciency evaluations. Finally, we introduce an extension of our testing frame-
work to multi-input and multi-output scenarios, incorporating advanced statistical methods
like the multivariate Kolmogorov–Smirnov test to enhance the framework’s applicability in
more complex, multidimensional data environments.
The assumption that DMUs exhibit identical output distribution for a given input level
is central to our analysis, particularly when investigating technological heterogeneity. This
assumption is pivotal to our approach, yet it is not excessively stringent. There are several
convincing reasons to support its likelihood. Namely, this assumption is in line with a fun-
damental principle of efﬁciency analysis, which often is assumed in relative performance
measurement frameworks that DMUs have the inherent capability to reach equivalent levels
of output with the same input mix. Hence, our assumption is not deviating from conven-
tional practice in DEA modeling. Furthermore, the assumption is empirically justiﬁed in
many industrial contexts where standardized production processes and regulatory guidelines
ensure that output levels are consistent across DMUs when provided with similar inputs.
Lastly, in competitive markets, the market pressure tends to homogenize technologies and
practices, as ﬁrms adopt the most effective methods to reduce costs and increase output. Over
time, one would expect a convergence in the distribution of outputs for a given input level as
DMUs either improve their processes or exit the market. Thus, in a market where competition
is a signiﬁcant force, the assumption of identical output distribution for equivalent inputs is
not unreasonable.
The second issue to address is the course of action if evidence of technological hetero-
geneity is detected within the DEA framework. The method proposed in this paper is intended
as a preliminary analysis step, designed to detect technological heterogeneity speciﬁcally in
DEA modeling. By identifying technological disparities, the framework helps ensure that
DEA performance evaluations are conducted on a more homogeneous basis. If heterogeneity
is detected, relying on a single production possibility frontier (PPF) to compare all DMUs
may lead to erroneous conclusions. In such cases, it becomes necessary to discern groups of
DMUs that are operating under similar technological conditions using additional information.
One approach to achieve this is through cluster analysis, which takes into account auxiliary
factors, such as environmental or contextual variables, to categorize DMUs. By grouping
DMUs based on these factors, one can perform DEA separately within each cluster. This
method allows for a more tailored analysis that accurately reﬂects the performance of DMUs
in relation to peers that share comparable technological environments. Additionally, DEA
can still be applied to the entire sample as a whole to gain an overview of the broad efﬁciency
landscape, but with the understanding that this global analysis may mask underlying hetero-
geneity. This dual-level approach—ﬁrst conducting a preliminary heterogeneity analysis and
then analyzing both within clusters and across the entire sample—provides a comprehensive
view of efﬁciency while accounting for technological diversity.
While the empirical application in this paper has primarily focused on single input or
output scenarios, the proposed testing framework is equally applicable to cases involving
multiple inputs and outputs. In a multi-output context, the framework can be extended by
considering each output as a separate dimension in the analysis. The underlying principle
remains consistent—if technological heterogeneity exists, it will manifest as discrepancies in
thedistributionsofoutputslacksacrossdifferentDMUs,evenwhenaccountingforthevarious
inputs. In extending the proposed heterogeneity testing framework to multi-input and multi-
output scenarios, it is essential to consider methodologies capable of handling the complexity
of joint distributions across multiple dimensions. The multivariate Kolmogorov–Smirnov
123

1555
(K–S) test, as developed by Fasano and Franceschini (1987), provides a suitable extension
to the univariate K–S test for this purpose. To integrate the multivariate K–S test into our
existing DEA heterogeneity testing framework, we propose the following steps:
1. After obtaining the DEA-estimated slack values for each output dimension, these values
can be combined into a multivariate data set representing the joint distribution of inefﬁ-
ciencies across all outputs. Similarly, if analyzing inputs, the joint distribution of input
slacks can be constructed.
2. The multivariate K–S test can then be applied to this joint distribution to assess whether
the slack values across multiple dimensions deviate signiﬁcantly from the expected distri-
bution under the null hypothesis of homogeneity. A signiﬁcant result would indicate the
presence of technological heterogeneity affecting the outputs (or inputs) simultaneously.4
3. By utilizing the multivariate K–S test, we gain the ability to detect and quantify discrep-
ancies in the joint distribution of slacks that might not be apparent when examining each
dimension independently. This method allows us to capture more complex forms of het-
erogeneity that arise from the interaction between multiple outputs (or inputs), offering
a more nuanced understanding of the inefﬁciencies within the DEA framework.
By integrating the multivariate K–S test into our heterogeneity testing framework, we
can effectively assess whether heterogeneity exists in production technologies across mul-
tiple outputs. This enhancement strengthens the framework’s ability to detect technological
disparities within DEA models, especially in complex, multi-dimensional input–output con-
ﬁgurations. It not only improves robustness and comprehensiveness but also provides deeper
insights into efﬁciency measurement dynamics. By addressing both theoretical and practical
challenges, our framework offers a versatile approach for ensuring the validity of DEA-based
performance analyses across diverse technological contexts.
7 Conclusion
Throughout this paper, we have explored the challenges associated with technological
heterogeneitywithinDEAmodeling.WebridgedthegapbetweenDEAandtheinherentprob-
abilistic structure discernible within datasets. The probabilistic nature of data is frequently
overlooked in DEA modeling, largely due to the assumption that DEA’s non-parametric
approach is inherently protected from the probabilistic assumptions typically required in
statistical analyses. Our discussion illuminated the often-neglected probabilistic character-
istics of data in DEA modeling, emphasizing that despite DEA’s non-parametric nature, the
data itself could indeed inform us about the suitability of DEA modeling by making min-
imal assumptions about the randomness embedded in the data. Moreover, we proposed a
methodological approach to verify the validity of DEA modeling, speciﬁcally addressing
the issue of heterogeneity. This practical framework serves as a diagnostic tool, allowing
practitioners to test for and ultimately ensure the integrity of DEA applications in the face of
diverse technological contexts. In summary, our contribution lies in highlighting the proba-
bilistic dimensions of DEA and presenting a viable solution to test the effects of technological
heterogeneity, thereby enhancing the robustness of DEA modeling in empirical research.

and presenting a viable solution to test the effects of technological heterogeneity, thereby
enhancing the robustness of DEA modeling in empirical research. By addressing the iden-
tiﬁed limitations and exploring new avenues for research, we believe this framework can
signiﬁcantly advance the ﬁeld of efﬁciency analysis.
