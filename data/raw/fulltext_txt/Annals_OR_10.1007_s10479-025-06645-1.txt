Abstract
Society currently experiences a growing interest in renting as an alternative to exclusive
ownership (Fota et al., 2019; Yeganeh, 2021). This paper supports rental companies in this
transition by providing a method to evaluate rental system occupancy. The method considers
time-dependent systems in which customers rent substitutes during stockouts. Speci’¼ücally,
the paper provides a queueing model based on a continuous-time Markov chain for sys-
tems where rentals occur according to Poisson processes and rental times follow phase-type
distributions. Additionally, the paper provides a heuristic optimization algorithm to mini-
mize inventory capacity while maintaining an upper stockout probability limit. The paper
evaluates these methods through numerical experiments, including a company case. The
results indicate that the queueing model adequately re’¼éects the occupancy distributions of
the investigated items, and that the optimization algorithm minimizes the inventory capacity
to near-optimality. In the company case, these methods reduce the inventory capacity while
simultaneously improving the maximum stockout probability.
Keywords Queueing ┬Ę Markov chain ┬Ę Phase-type ┬Ę Rental
1 Introduction
Renting is an established concept in the society and provides a popular alternative to exclusive
ownership (Fota et al., 2019; Yeganeh, 2021). The bene’¼üts of renting include the ability to
serve the same number of customers with fewer resources by pooling and sharing resources.
With an increasing access to data and computational resources, rental companies can now
optimize their inventories more effectively (Vassakis et al., 2018; Favaretto et al., 2023). This
paper takes advantage of these opportunities by providing a method for turning data on arrival
rates and rental times into occupancy estimates, focusing on cases where customers can rent
substitutes during stockouts. Additionally, the paper provides an approach for optimizing the
inventory capacity.

The academic literature covers roughly 60 years of research on inventory replenishment
and rental services (Wagner & Whitin, 1958; Tainiter, 1964; Jalali & Nieuwenhuyse, 2015;
Ma et al., 2019). In more recent literature, Benjaafar and Hu (2020) investigate various
sharing economies and derive three distinct types: The ’¼ürst is Peer-to-peer Resource Sharing
(PRS), where the owner rents a single item to a non-owner, the second is On-demand Service
Platforms (OSPs), where individual workers perform time-sensitive services, and the third is
On-demand Rental Networks (ORNs), where a company provides on-demand rentals of items
from several inventories to a large number of customers. Benjaafar and Hu (2020) emphasize
that queueing theory is a convenient tool for describing the dynamics in rental systems and
note that the appropriate modeling structure depends on the speci’¼üc rental system. OSPs
typically involve a random capacity since workers enter the platform voluntarily (Ibrahim,
2018; Dong & Ibrahim, 2020). Conversely, the ORNs involve a constant capacity for the
whole system, whereas the capacity alternates in the individual queues. For instance, in car
rental companies, a car might return to another vendor than the car was rented from. Finally,
the PRS systems can be modeled using a single queue of constant capacity.
The academic literature contains a recurrent number of applications of queueing theory
differing in model structure and scope. Tainiter (1964) conducted the ’¼ürst study describing
a rental system using queueing theory and suggested to model the occupancy of items as a
queue with customer loss and derive the pro’¼üt of the system using the model. Researchers
studied several similar cases in the following years. For instance, both Savin et al. (2005)
and Gans and Savin (2007) investigated the problem of allocating capacity and optimizing
pro’¼üt for rental systems with two classes of customers. Speci’¼ücally, Gans and Savin used
a Markov decision process to decide on an action for each customer as they arrive at the
queue. In addition, Papier and Thonemann (2008) described the occupancy of rail cars as a
model with customer loss and derived the systemŌĆÖs pro’¼üt from the model. Inspired by the
increasing interest in vehicle-sharing platforms, George and Xia (2011) derived a closed
queueing network model for the occupancy of vehicles. Subsequently, the authors derived
an approach for ’¼ünding the optimal ’¼éeet size. In a slightly altered setting, Choi and Yoon
(2016), Chakravarthy (2020) and Sivakumar et al. (2020) studied a problem they denoted the
queueing-inventory system, where service-completions result in a loss of servers.
There seem to be abundant studies applying queueing theory to the rental industry, but
many of them assume stationary customer behavior. In fact, this literature review uncovered
only two studies accounting for time-dependent behavior. Papier and Thonemann (2008)
evaluated the time-dependent behavior using the average stationary approximation method,
andEpsteinetal.(2020)usedaRunge-Kuttamethod.Naturally,thewiderliteratureonapplied
queueing theory contains several examples of time-dependent behavior, such as the analyses
conducted by Izady and Worthington (2011) and Singh et al. (2021), and the investigations
of hospital settings conducted by Bekker and Bruin (2010), Brummelen et al. (2018), and
Andersen et al. (2019).
Returning to the rental industry, only Papier and Thonemann (2008) consider item substi-
tution, and only three studies on item substitution exist in the wider literature on logistics and
manufacturing (Ahiska et al., 2017; Walt & Bean, 2022; Mahmuto╦śgullari & Yaman, 2023). A
recent study by Mahmuto╦śgullari and Yaman (2023) solved the problem of supplying retailers
using mixed integer programming. In the study, retailers sell two product types, where one
is of high quality, the other of low quality, and only the high-quality product can be used as
a substitute.

2 Problem description
This paper considers rental companies renting out items from an inventory, where the items
return to the same inventory, such as household appliances, clothing, construction machines,
and so on. Customers arrive and perform rental requests with random inter-arrival times. On
arrival, a customer requests a single item, and if available, the customer rents and removes
the item from the inventory for a random duration. When the rental period ends, the customer
returns the item to the inventory. This paper considers rental companies serving customers
on a ’¼ürst-come-’¼ürst-served basis, as well as companies where the inventory capacity is
suf’¼üciently large that maintenance can be conducted ahead of the next rental. Furthermore,
if an arriving customer requests an item during a stockout, the customer will attempt to rent a
substitute with a known probability (cf. Figure 1). In this case, several substitutes might exist,
and the preferences depend on the availability of all items in the inventory. If the customer
decides not to rent a substitute, or the selected substitute is out of stock, the customer will
leave the system.
Finally, demand ’¼éuctuations, such as seasonal behavior, causes the customer arrival rate
and rental time to ’¼éuctuate over time. Thus, this paper views the system as a time-dependent
queueing system with customer relocation, where the items correspond to the servers. Sec-
tions 2.1-2.2 elaborate on the formal details.

4 Optimization approach
Rental companies might be confronted with the challenge of estimating the correct capacity
by accounting for various aspects of their business. For instance, rental companies can acquire
too many or too few items leading to unnecessarily high holding costs or insuf’¼ücient customer
service. Thus, this paper employs the CTMC (cf. Section 3) in a heuristic optimization

6.1 Description of data
The company Nethire, a rental service for contractors in the Danish construction industry,
provided the data. Nethire allows contractors to rent equipment and machines from a network
of various vendors. The investigated case focuses on rentals of scissor lifts from a single
vendor from the Copenhagen area. A scissor lift is a mobile platform for lifting workers and
equipment to high elevations. It consists of a platform supported by interlocking metal arms
that can extend and retract to raise or lower the platform. This case considered scissor lifts
of 8 and 12 meters. Customers preferring the 12-meter lift could not use a lift of 8 meters as
a substitute since the 8-meter lift was not tall enough. Conversely, customers preferring the
8-meter lift could solve their tasks using the 12-meter lift since the lifts could be lowered.
As a result, the scissor lifts only substituted in one direction.
The provided dataset covered transactions of two years, from the 1st of January 2018 up
to and including the 31st of December 2019. Each transaction contained the rental date, the
return date, and the type of scissor lift. In addition, the company provided information about
the vendorŌĆÖs historic capacities. In practice, a rental company would employ a forecast of
the demand incorporating analyses of seasonal behaviors. Sections 6.2-6.3 considered the
historic two-year period as the planning horizon since the case focuses on validating the
adequacy of the CTMC and optimization algorithm.

7 Conclusion
This paper proposes a set of methods for evaluating and optimizing an inventory considering
a time-dependent rental system with substitutable items. The paper assumes that customers
arriveaccordingtotime-dependentPoissonprocessesandfollowphase-typedistributedrental
times. Based on a CTMC, the paper evaluates the time-dependent behavior by segmenting
the planning horizon and employing the uniformization method. This approach results in an
occupancy distribution for each item. Moreover, the paper provides a heuristic optimization
algorithm that minimizes the total capacities in the planning horizon but ensures the stockout
probabilities do not violate an upper limit.
The numerical experiments in Section 5 showed no signi’¼ücant difference between the
CTMC and a discrete event simulation of the rental system, indicating that the implementation
adequately evaluates the occupancy distributions of the CTMC. Similarly, there was no
difference between the solutions obtained by the optimization algorithm and those obtained
by complete enumeration or simulated annealing. Thus, the experiments indicated that the
algorithm leads to near-optimal solutions. Finally, Section 6 showed that by employing the
algorithm, a vendor could reduce the inventory capacities and simultaneously the maximum
stockout probabilities, improving customer service. Altogether, Sections 5-6 indicate that the
proposed methods can be useful for rental companies aiming to reduce their inventory.
Future research should focus on reducing the computational memory requirements of the
CTMC model. This can be achieved by exploiting the numerical advantages of PH distri-
butions, such as conditions where the representation reduces to the exponential distribution
