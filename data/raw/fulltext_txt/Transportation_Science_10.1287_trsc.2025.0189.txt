We focus on a logistics service provider (LSP) that organizes the inbound logistics for an original equipment manufacturer
(OEM) using just-in-time production. The LSP conducts periodic milk-runs, i.e., tours visiting a subset of suppliers on
a fixed route to collect shipments bound for the OEM. While the milk-run routes and rough schedules are fixed well in
advance, the exact shipment due dates at the OEM only become known once the production sequence has been finalized.
During daily operation, the given milk-runs are executed such that shipments are picked up from the suppliers and
delivered to the OEM ideally exactly just-in-time. However, the production sequence on the assembly lines at the OEM
often changes on the day of execution, which alters shipment due dates at the OEM and makes it necessary to anticipate
potential disturbances when assigning shipments to milk-runs. We formulate a two-stage stochastic program, where, in
the first stage, shipments are assigned to specific milk-runs and, in the second stage, when alterations to the production
sequence are revealed, arrival times are adjusted and shipments are express-delivered if necessary. The assignment induces
earliness-tardiness costs depending on the uncertain due date of each shipment at the OEM, and express deliveries induce
fixed costs. Besides an extensive-form mixed-integer linear program (MILP), we develop a solution procedure based on
the integer 𝐿-shaped method, enriched with valid inequalities and cuts derived from partial Benders decomposition and a
subproblem relaxation. In a computational study on realistic test data, we show that the proposed 𝐿-shaped method solves
62 out of 70 instances to optimality. From a managerial perspective, we show that express deliveries are an effective way
of mitigating uncertainty in the production sequence, as are overlapping milk-run routes, which are not yet widely used in
practice but are relatively easy to implement.
Key words: stochastic programming, assignment problem, just-in-time, L-shaped method

Introduction
Just-in-time (JIT) logistics plays an important role in many manufacturing industries, prime among them
the automotive one (Lai and Cheng 2009). Striving for the zero-inventory ideal, in a JIT system, parts
and components should only be transported from one stage of the supply chain to the next when these
parts are required by the subsequent stage. Just-in-time logistics is pervasive especially in the automotive

industry, where it was pioneered by Toyota in the 1950s. To illustrate the practical implications from the
perspective of a logistics provider, for instance Pfenning Logistics (2025), a German company specializing
in supply chain and contract logistics, report delivering several tens of thousands of parts per day using about
160 towing vehicles and 230 trailers and semi-trailers to supply an automotive plant. In this context, we
consider the problem faced by a logistics service provider (LSP) transporting shipments from suppliers to an
original equipment manufacturer (OEM), while accounting for uncertain production sequences at the latter.
Specifically, while most extant literature addresses either only the routing decision or the purely operational
(scheduling) level, we focus on the interplay between the tactical and operational planning levels.
To enable a reliable and smooth flow of parts, the so-called heijunka principle in the parlance of the
Toyota production system is employed. According to this principle, regular milk-runs connecting suppliers
to the OEM plant are usually planned by a logistics service provider (LSP) on a tactical time frame, e.g., one
month in advance (Chuah and Yingling 2005). A milk-run consists of an ordered subset of supplier visits,
which we call a route, as well as an OEM arrival time window. As milk-runs are planned before knowing the
exact production sequence at the OEM, they are planned according to average demand rates, constituting a
routing problem based on average demands. Based on these rates, the milk-runs are set and communicated
to the suppliers, who expect reliable, periodic pick-ups. Milk-runs usually follow the common frequency
routing principle (Chuah and Yingling 2005), whereby routes are repeated on a given day with different
OEM arrival time windows, each corresponding to a milk-run.
The OEM generally establishes its production plan about three to four days prior to execution (Emde
and Gendreau 2017). This plan dictates the required shipments, which are initially located at geographically
dispersed suppliers. Especially, every assembled product at the OEM may require different shipments (parts),
each shipment originating from a unique supplier. The production plan is communicated to the LSP, who,
upon receiving the OEM production plan, assigns shipments to its milk-runs, which have been established
previously at a tactical level. While data availability and quality in automotive supply chains is very high,
using industry-standard electronic data exchange protocols (see, e.g., Verband der Automobilindustrie 2020),
the production plan rarely precisely coincides with the average demand rates used in designing the milk-runs.
Moreover, during daily operation, the OEM’s production sequence may be perturbed due to unforeseen
disturbances. For example, in car manufacturing, the paint shop is a frequent source of delays (Lübben,
Pries, and Sikora 2023). The daily disturbances in the production directly impact the shipment due dates
(i.e., the time of day at which shipments are due at the OEM), albeit not the total demand. OEMs tend to
minimize the impact of such disturbances by penalizing LSPs for unpunctual deliveries with respect to the
perturbed production sequence.
We consider the operational optimization problem faced by the LSP in the previously described setting.

for the other shipments are established. We call the overall resulting problem the Stochastic Just-In-Time

the tactical level is provided in Figure 1. Our contributions are fivefold.
• We formalize the problem of assigning shipments to given milk-runs in a JIT logistics system, antici-
pating disturbances in the shipment due dates. Extant literature on the topic only considers simplified
problem versions, where only direct trips (as opposed to milk-runs) in a deterministic environment are
considered (e.g., Boysen, Briskorn, and Emde 2016).
• We capture the inherent uncertainty of the problem via a two-stage stochastic program. We propose a
tailored exact solution algorithm based on the integer 𝐿-shaped method (Laporte and Louveaux 1993),
which we strengthen via a number of algorithmic components. These include tailored valid inequalities,
optimality cuts, a partial Benders decomposition strategy, and lower bounds.
• Furthermore, we propose an extensive formulation that is strengthened by two sets of valid inequalities,
allowing the immediate application of default solvers at least to small instances.
• We evaluate the added value of the algorithmic components of our integer 𝐿-shaped method through
a comprehensive set of experiments. We show that the integer 𝐿-shaped method provides optimal
solutions for 62 out of 70 problem instances within a 60-minute computational time limit, whereas the
extensive formulation MILP only solves 38 instances to optimality.
• We provide several managerial insights. We examine the impact of milk-run frequency and express-
delivery cost. Furthermore, we explore the effect of relaxing a widespread industry practice where each
supplier is on a single milk-run route. Lastly, we evaluate the effect of express-delivery costs.
The remainder of this article is organized as follows. We review the relevant literature in Section 2. We
derive the formal problem definition in Section 3. In Section 4 we introduce our integer 𝐿-shaped method.
We present our computational experiments in Section 5 and, lastly, our conclusions in Section 6.
2.
Literature review
We structure this section in the following manner. In Section 2.1, we review the literature on applications
related to SJITMRA, with a focus on how milk-runs are established prior to assigning shipments to them. In
Section 2.2, we survey problems bearing similarity with SJITMRA. In Section 2.3, we focus on assignment
problems with similar properties to those of SJITMRA.
2.1.
Related applications
As stated in the introduction, SJITMRA is motivated by shipment logistics in the automotive industry. The
recourse cost in our problem is caused by uncertainties in the OEM’s production sequence, which are a
result of disturbances such as machine breakdowns or high rework rates. Such disturbances may require
resequencing the production plan. Lübben, Pries, and Sikora (2023) discuss such problems in the context of
automotive assembly lines. Generally speaking, inbound logistics between suppliers and an OEM may be

organized by way of cross-docks, distribution centers, milk-runs, and point-to-point (or direct) deliveries.
Meyer and Amberg (2018) consider the transport concept selection, which means deciding between point-
to-point delivery, milk-runs, and area forwarding. The latter includes forwarding the shipments to LSP hubs.
In this context, Ranjbaran, Kashan, and Kazemi (2020) focus on pallet logistics, while accounting for reverse
flow direct deliveries and milk-runs.
An antecedent problem to SJITMRA consists of optimizing milk-run routes. Chuah and Yingling (2005)
study a problem where parts are directly shipped from the suppliers to a single OEM via milk-runs. They
consider a so-called common frequency routing model, which entails that each supplier is repeatedly served
by milk-runs consisting of the same route but with different OEM time windows that follow a given frequency,
which is a common policy in practice. Ohlmann, Fry, and Thomas (2008) consider generalized frequency
routing, where the visit frequency may differ from one supplier to another. Similar problem settings to
Chuah and Yingling (2005) are explored by, e.g., Natarajarathinam, Stacey, and Sox (2012), under the title
of inbound inventory routing problem. Dong and Turnquist (2015) allow different predefined frequencies for
supplier visits. Chitsaz, Cordeau, and Jans (2020) solve the assembly routing problem, which simultaneously
deals with production, inventory, and transportation decisions. Following the principle of standardized work
from lean production, Bertazzi et al. (2020) consider the cyclic inventory routing problem, assuming a
constant production rate for the final products, which allows synchronization with the suppliers.
The previously mentioned problems deal with the tactical problem of optimizing milk-run routes. These
serve as input to SJITMRA, which deals with assigning shipments to them. SJITMRA may consider periodic
milk-runs as well as arbitrary routes and frequencies, which is taken into account by Mao et al. (2020), who
look at milk-run routing in combination with progress-lanes, which buffer the supplies required for a certain
production period. Similarly, Güner, Murat, and Chinnam (2017) propose dynamic routing for milk-runs
that reacts to congestion, while Novaes et al. (2017) focus on minimizing the effect of tardiness caused by
suppliers. This is done by adjusting the tardiness tolerance level.
2.2.
Related problem characteristics
SJITMRA is applicable in supply chains following the JIT principle. Consequently, we aim at reducing the
inventory and shortage cost, i.e., earliness and tardiness of the shipments at the OEM. JIT scheduling has a
long tradition in academic research (e.g., Hall and Posner 1991, Hall, Kubiak, and Sethi 1991). However, in
addition to JIT principles, SJITMRA incorporates decisions pertaining to express deliveries. In the routing
literature, this is similar to outsourcing decisions treated in the vehicle routing problem with private fleet
and common carrier. Such problems are studied under various cost structures considering a single planning
period (e.g., Gahm, Brabänder, and Tuma 2017) or several planning periods (e.g., Ceschia, Di Gaspero,
and Schaerf 2011). While elaborate outsourcing cost structures may be considered (e.g., Baller et al. 2020),
in SJITMRA we assume that express-delivery costs are dependent on the shipments. This simple structure
allows modeling costs that are commensurate with shipment size or the supplier’s distance from the OEM.

2.3.
Related generalized assignment problems
From a modeling perspective, the generalized assignment problem (GAP) bears similarity to SJITMRA. The
scope of GAP is to assign tasks to a certain number of agents such that the tasks do not exceed the agents’
capacity. As such, the GAP is similar to the first stage of SJITMRA, i.e., the assignment of shipments to
milk-runs. However, GAPs lack the scheduling component and consequently do not enable accounting for
earliness and tardiness costs. As these costs are pivotal in our objective function, the methods put forward for
GAPs are not directly applicable to SJITMRA. Nonetheless, in what follows, we survey GAPs with supply
chain modeling elements or stochastic features that we deem relevant to SJITMRA.
The GAP is relevant to a large variety of applications. Consequently, it has a long research tradition,
going beyond its golden anniversary in 2005 (Pentico 2007). The GAP appears in the context of supply
chain resilience (Radhakrishnan, Harris, and Kamarthi 2018), which is somewhat related to SJITMRA. For
example, Mazzola and Neebe (2012) deal with a GAP for supply chain capacity planning. The authors assign
tasks to agents in time periods and allow reassigning tasks to other agents within consecutive periods. The
dynamic GAP by Kogan and Shtub (1997) is a continuous-time optimal control model that considers both
quadratic inventory costs for earliness and shortage cost for tardiness besides linear processing costs of the
tasks. Kilic, Durmusoglu, and Baskak (2012) classify different assignment problems for in-plant milk-runs,
which are, however, only peripherally related to SJITMRA, which deals with inbound logistics.
Stochastic versions of the GAP have mainly focused on uncertainty related to demands, capacity, or
which jobs to eventually perform (Öncan 2007). Albareda-Sambola and Fernández (2000) consider uncer-
tain Bernoulli-distributed demands. Toktas, Yen, and Zabinsky (2006) address capacity uncertainty. Sarin,
Sherali, and Kim (2014) consider a stochastic generalized assignment machine scheduling problem in which
the processing times of jobs are assumed to be random variables. Finally, uncertainty regarding whether to
perform a task or not is discussed by Albareda-Sambola, van der Vlerk, and Fernández (2006). However,
unlike all GAP models in the literature, SJITMRA has an overarching scheduling component, which is due to
the earliness-tardiness cost of the shipments. Since, from a modeling perspective, stochastic GAP is the most

3.
Problem description
SJITMRA receives milk-runs as input. We recall that a milk-run is defined as an ordered subset of supplier
visits, coupled with an OEM arrival time window. On an operational level, the production plan is commu-
nicated to the LSP by the OEM, typically three to four days before its execution. At this time, the first-stage
problem of SJITMRA is solved, which consists of assigning the shipments to the given set of milk-runs. The
LSP communicates to the suppliers what shipments will be picked up on what milk-run. Then, unforeseen
changes in the production sequence may occur on the execution day of the production plan. Such uncer-
tain changes may affect the due dates of shipments at the OEM, and hence possibly necessitate recourse
actions. The second-stage problem of SJITMRA establishes which shipments are to be express-delivered,
and reschedules the arrival times at the OEM of the milk-runs with the remaining shipments. We assume
that all times in SJITMRA, including due dates, are integer multiples of the takt time (work cycle) whose
length is typically around 90 seconds in the automotive industry (Emde and Schneider 2018).
Formally, SJITMRA consists of assigning a given set 𝐼= {1, . . . , 𝑚} of shipments to a set 𝐽= {1, . . . , 𝑛}
of given milk-runs. Additionally, milk-run 𝑗∈𝐽is to arrive at the OEM within its predetermined hard time
window [𝑎𝑗, 𝑏𝑗], lest it interfere with the previous or subsequent scheduled milk-run. Moreover, the trucks
executing the milk-runs each have a capacity of 𝐻∈N+, and shipment 𝑖∈𝐼is associated with a capacity
requirement of ℎ𝑖∈N+. We denote by 𝐼𝑗⊆𝐼the subset of shipments that can potentially be picked up on
milk-run 𝑗∈𝐽. Subset 𝐼𝑗is derived from the pre-established subset of suppliers that are visited in milk-run 𝑗.
Note that a shipment may refer to a single part, or it may be a whole box or even a pallet of parts. We simply
refer to one atomic package that needs to be picked up from a supplier to go to the OEM as a shipment.
We assume that a discrete set of scenarios Ω describes shipments’ due date distributions. In the second
stage of SJITMRA, we consider that due dates are revealed for each shipment at the beginning of the day.

Structure of the second-stage problem

An integer 𝐿-shaped algorithm for SJITMRA
We solve our problem with the integer 𝐿-shaped algorithm of Laporte and Louveaux (1993). It is an extension
of the 𝐿-shaped method of Van Slyke and Wets (1969) for continuous stochastic programs, which itself is
an application of Benders decomposition to stochastic programs. We refer the reader to Rahmaniani et al.
(2017) for an overview of Benders decomposition.
The integer 𝐿-shaped method can deal with stochastic mixed-integer programming models with complete
recourse, and is particularly suitable for SJITMRA, since this problem decomposes naturally by stage and
scenario. The other category of exact methods employed for two-stage stochastic mixed-integer programming
models in routing applications is branch-price-and-cut (BPC) (e.g., Florio et al. 2021). However, BPCs
tend to work best on problems with a relatively small support and tight capacity limits (Hoogendoorn and
Spliet 2023).These two conditions generally do not hold for realistic SJITMRA instances. First, the number
of potential production sequence permutations, while finite, can be large. Second, shipments tend to be
small relative to the truck capacity. The basic idea of our integer 𝐿-shaped method is to solve the first-stage
problem where the expected recourse cost E𝝃[Q(𝒙, 𝝃)] in objective function (11) is replaced with a set of
non-negative continuous variables 𝜃𝑗, standing for the expected recourse cost incurred by milk-run 𝑗in the
optimal solution. Consequently, the master problem is

5.
Computational study
In this section, we investigate the computational performance of our integer 𝐿-shaped algorithm and use it
to gain numerical insight into the problem and the solution characteristics. First, we define the benchmark
instances and the computational environment in Section 5.1. Using these test data, in Section 5.2, we
analyze the contribution of the different algorithmic components of our solution method. In Section 5.3, we
benchmark the performance of our algorithm against the extensive formulation proposed in Section 3.1.
In addition to technical aspects, we use our solution method to gain managerial insights. In Section 5.4,
we evaluate the impact of uncertainty via classic stochastic programming measures. We then examine the
impact of the time window lengths, the wide-spread practice of common frequency routing, and the impact
of express-delivery cost in Section 5.5.
5.1.
Benchmark instances and computational environment
In total, we generate 70 instances. The details of the generation scheme are in online appendix C; we provide
an outline here. The test data can first be distinguished by the number of shipments. In the thirty small
instances labeled shipment120, 𝑚= 120 shipments are required at the OEM, and in the thirty large instances
(labeled shipment400), 𝑚= 400 shipments must be delivered to the OEM. The shipments are provided by
either 24 (shipment120) or 48 (shipment400) suppliers.
The due dates of the shipments are derived from the production sequence at the OEM, which is subject
to uncertainty because it may be shaken up by unforeseen disturbances. We consider |Ω| = 200 different
production sequences (that is, scenarios) with equal probability 𝑝𝜔=
1
|Ω| . We derive these production

The costs are drawn from uniform distributions. Specifically, 𝛼𝑖∼U [17, 23], 𝛽𝑖∼U [68, 92], and
𝐾𝑖∼U [3400, 4600], ∀𝑖∈𝐼, which yields realistic proportions in that earliness is cheaper than tardiness,
which in turn is cheaper (to a point) than express delivery; specifically, on average, an express delivery
makes sense if a shipment is more than 60 TU early or more than 15 TU late. The weight of each type of
part is defined based on the uniform distribution U [50, 100]. The truck capacity is set to 𝐻= 800. Hence,
we can load up to between eight and 16 shipments on a truck.
The shipments are assigned randomly to the suppliers. Several suppliers are served by the same milk-run,
which arrives at the OEM within a certain time window. We assume that the same routes are repeated
multiple times within the planning horizon at different times, that is, multiple milk runs serve the same route
at different times, and that each supplier is on exactly one route, which is typical industrial practice, referred
to as common frequency routing. This structure is illustrated in Figures 1a and 2a. The milk-run arrival

without exception, leading to between 78 and 99 and between 270 and 408 milk-runs for the shipment120-
CRS and shipment400-CRS instances, respectively. Second, in the reduced route set (RRS) instances, we
tighten the transport capacity to the bare minimum by deleting milk-runs from the CRS instances so that the
total capacity of the remaining milk-runs is just enough to fit all shipments, yielding between 11 and 13 and
between 38 and 47 milk-runs for the shipment120-RRS and shipment400-RRS instances, respectively. Finally,
the mixed route set (MRS) instances comprise exactly 𝑛= 40 and 𝑛= 133 milk-runs for the shipment120-

appendix C). All instances are publicly available from https://github.com/jbaals/SJITMRA.

integer 𝐿-shaped approach benefits from tighter cuts in the CRS instances, where there fewer shipments on

method. Applying the VI strongly improves the performance of the extensive-form MILP on the MRS and
RRS instances, but this comes at the cost of poorer performance in case of larger instances (CRS with 400
shipments), where using the MILP with VI often does not even allow finding a feasible solution within the
time and memory limits. Especially when a lot of milk-runs need to be handled, the number of constraints
grows, which leads to issues in the preprocessing step before exploring the root node.

known solution for all instances in our tests. This means that the gap to BKS is 0.00% across the board

includes detailed results for the computation times. In conclusion, the integer 𝐿-shaped method performs
well even on larger instances with |Ω| = 200 scenarios and 𝑚= 400 shipments, which the extensive-form

Conclusions
In this paper, we define, model, and solve SJITMRA. The problem is motivated by the inbound logistics
process in the automotive industry. In SJITMRA, we apply JIT production principles and common (or
generalized) frequency routing in an uncertain environment. We focus on OEM production sequence changes.
SJITMRA captures the aspect of assigning shipments to predetermined milk-runs such that shipments arrive
as punctually as possible while avoiding express deliveries whenever possible. We describe this problem
as a stochastic programming model based on the stochastic GAP. To solve SJITMRA, we propose an
extensive-form MILP and a solution procedure based on the integer 𝐿-shaped method.
The extensive-form MILP is capable of solving SJITMRA to optimality only for a small share of the
instances. The integer 𝐿-shaped method runs faster than the extensive-form MILP on the vast majority of
instances. The integer 𝐿-shaped method can also solve all large instances in our test set to optimality or
at least near-optimality. Moreover, we test the components of the integer 𝐿-shaped method, demonstrating
their substantial positive effect on the quality of the bounds and the computation time.
From a practical perspective, we show that it is important to align the milk-run frequency with the available
information on the due dates at the OEM. If the milk-runs are too frequent, the LSP can only adapt the arrival
time at the OEM within a small range. If milk-runs are too infrequent, many shipments with different due
dates at the OEM need to be aggregated on the same run. In both cases, the total shipment costs increase.
Furthermore, in current industrial practice, suppliers are often only assigned to one milk-run within a given
time window. By incorporating at least some suppliers in several milk-runs with the same time window, we

show that planning flexibility is increased and thus total shipment costs are lower. The express-delivery costs
per shipment have a strong effect on the total express-delivery and shipment costs and the decision about
the transportation mode (i.e., milk-run or express delivery). In our computations, a drastic decrease in the
express-delivery costs per shipment is necessary to make this mode a viable option to compensate for the
lack of knowledge about the true due dates. This helps explain why many OEMs go to great lengths to stick
to the planned production sequence, even at the expense of maintaining costly buffers.
We formulated the planning problem from the perspective of a LSP taking into account uncertainty
in the due dates provided by the OEM. We identify several directions for future research. First, traffic
congestion while operating the milk-run routes may be a challenge for the LSP, especially after promising
fixed time windows for the arrival at the OEM. Second, express deliveries and resequencing at the OEM
affect the suppliers, too, by disrupting their own production or provisioning sequences. Therefore, it would be
interesting to explore how the suppliers’ picking and production operations deal with such scenarios. Third,
we assume that the decision about express deliveries is made before the routes are operated. Some parts
may be unexpectedly required with a shorter notice period. This could be modeled as a sequential decision
problem. Alternatively, while rarely a problem in the automotive industry, other JIT manufacturing industries
may struggle with data availability and may hence benefit from a (distributionally) robust optimization
approach.

does there exist a partition {𝐵1, . . . , 𝐵𝑘} of 𝑂satisfying Í

We transform an instance of BinPacking to an instance of SJITMRA by introducing a shipment 𝑖∈𝐼for each item
𝑖∈𝑂, set the capacity requirement for each item to ℎ𝑖:= 𝑠𝑖, the express-shipment cost to any arbitrary positive value
𝐾𝑖> 0, and the earliness-tardiness penalties to 𝛼𝑖= 𝛽𝑖:= 0, ∀𝑖∈𝐼. The vehicle capacity is 𝐻:= 𝐶. The remaining

Appendix C:
Instance generation
The generation of benchmark instances follows the structure of Boysen and Emde (2014), who generate demand data for
parts and products following a real-world automotive production process. The general idea is illustrated in the example
in Section 3.2. The goal is to produce a given set of ordered products on an assembly line (or in a flow shop). The
OEM requires certain parts for each product. Each part is a single shipment from a certain supplier. When these parts
are required at the OEM plant depends on the production sequence, which is subject to unexpected alterations. Several
suppliers are served by the same milk-run, which arrives at the OEM within a certain time window. We assume that the
same routes are repeated multiple times within the planning horizon at different times, i.e., multiple milk-runs serve
the same route at different times, and that each supplier is on exactly one route, which is typical industrial practice,
referred to as common frequency routing. This structure is illustrated in Figures 1a and 2a.
In total, we generate 70 instances. They can first be distinguished by the number of products. In the 40 small
instances labeled shipment120, 20 products are assembled out of six parts each, and in the thirty large instances (labeled
shipment400), 40 products require the assembly of ten parts each. In total, we get 𝑚= 120 shipments in the shipment120
instances and 𝑚= 400 shipments in the shipment400 instances. The parts are provided by either 24 (shipment120) or
48 (shipment400) suppliers.
Every phase of the production process requires a single part. The duration varies uniformly between 5 and 30 TU
from phase to phase, but a given phase has the same duration for all products, as is typical in flow-line production.
Thus, given a production sequence [𝜌1, . . . , 𝜌𝑃⟩in some scenario 𝜔, where 𝑃∈20,40 is the number of products to be
produced, the due date 𝜉𝜔

The source of uncertainty is the production sequence, which may be shaken up by unforeseen disturbances (e.g.,
rework of some workpieces) and hence affect the due dates of the parts. We consider |Ω| = 200 different production
sequences with 𝑝𝜔=

sequence that was originally communicated to the supply chain partners, that is, [𝜌1, . . . , 𝜌𝑃⟩. We generate the scenarios
by perturbing this initial order. Specifically, we randomly postpone up to two products by up to three positions in the
smaller shipment120 instances and up to four products by up to six positions in the larger shipment400 instances. Once
a product is postponed the products behind it in the original sequence are shifted forward by one production cycle
to an earlier position in the production sequence. Thus, those items have an earlier due date compared to the initial
production sequence because the subsequent product(s) take the position of the postponed one, i.e., there are no vacant
production cycles. This scenario generation approach emulates real-world sequence perturbations in which a product
is pulled out of the production line due to some issue and then reinserted once the issue is fixed. The initial sequence
and the perturbed sequences correspond to one scenario each.
The costs are drawn from uniform distributions. Specifically, 𝛼𝑖∼U [17,23], 𝛽𝑖∼U [68,92], and 𝐾𝑖∼
U [3400,4600], ∀𝑖∈𝐼. Hence, early arrivals are always cheaper than late arrivals, given that earliness cost correspond
mostly to storage cost, which tend to be lower than the potential penalties caused by delays, which run the risk of, in
the worst case, the production line starving for parts. Meanwhile, express-delivery cost are higher still and are only
worthwhile if they help avoid significant unpunctuality. In practice, the costs of delays are (significantly) higher than
earliness costs (Boysen et al. 2015, Baals, Emde, and Turkensteen 2023). Consequently, an express delivery is more

likely if a shipment is expected to be late. The weight of each part type is defined based on the uniform distribution
U [50,100]. The truck capacity is set to 𝐻= 800. Hence, we can load up to between eight and 16 shipments on a truck.
Each shipment (i.e., part) is assigned randomly to one of the 24 or 48 suppliers, where we ensure that each supplier
is associated with at least one shipment. The milk-run arrival times at the OEM have non-overlapping time windows

We generate three and six routes for the shipment120 instances and the shipment400, respectively. We generate the
respective number of routes, either three or six, by solving a compatible capacitated vehicle routing problem with a
distance minimization objective function. As such, each supplier is assigned to a single route, and each route is repeated
in each non-overlapping OEM time window. Consequently, since there are 𝑐non-overlapping time windows, in the
shipment120 instances, there are 3 · 𝑐milk-runs in total; in the shipment400 instances, there are 6 · 𝑐milk-runs in total.
We call this a complete milk-run route set (CRS).
For the shipment120 instances, the CRS contains between 78 and 99 milk-runs per instance, depending on parameter
𝑐. For the shipment400 instances, the CRS contains between 270 and 408 milk-runs. To test how the solution methods
react to tighter capacities, we create a second set of instances, which we dub reduced milk-run routes set (RRS).
These instances are exact copies of the CRS instances, except that we cull some of the milk-runs. We only leave a
minimal subset of milk-runs in the set, while ensuring that for each shipment, there is at least one milk-run left that can
theoretically deliver the shipment to the OEM on time in at least one scenario without resorting to express delivery.
Moreover, the total vehicle capacity of the milk-runs in the RRS must be sufficient for the total capacity requirement
of the shipments. By doing this, we reduce the number of milk-runs in the shipment120-RRS instances to between 11
and 13 milk-runs and in the shipment400-RRS instances to between 38 and 47 milk-runs, i.e., by a factor of about 7.5.
The RRS offers a minimum number of milk-runs during the planning horizon, while at the same time guaranteeing
that each shipment has at least a chance of arriving at the OEM without requiring express delivery.
We finally create another set of milk-runs respectively for the shipment120 and shipment400 instances. They contain
a mixed milk-run route set (MRS), consisting of all the milk-runs from the corresponding RRS instance and a random
selection of milk-runs from the corresponding CRS instance that are distinct from those already included in the RRS
instance. The milk-run sets of the shipment120-MRS instances comprise 𝑛= 40 milk-runs and the shipment400-MRS
instances 𝑛= 133 milk-runs. In summary, the CRS instances include regular, frequent milk-runs to all suppliers, the
RRS instances contain only the minimum number of milk-runs to still enable punctual service in at least some scenarios,
and the MRS set is somewhere between these extremes.
