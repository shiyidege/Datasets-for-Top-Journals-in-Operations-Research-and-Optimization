Abstract
We consider the impact of counterparty risk on the pricing of ﬁnancial derivatives contracts
on energy commodities. We model the counterparty credit risk exposure within the Heath–
Jarrow–Morton framework (Heath et al. Econ J Econ Soc. 6: 77–105, 1992), allowing the
counterparty credit spread curve to evolve according to its own volatility and to the correlation
between the risk-free interest rate and the credit spread. We focus on the case in which the
underlying swap contract is the spark spread. We evaluate the counterparty credit valuation
adjustment (CVA) of the swap fair price. In doing so, we take into consideration all relevant
correlations, namely the correlation between the electricity and natural gas return processes
and between these and the credit spread. Finally, we show how in our framework CVA varies
with electricity and natural gas price volatilities, with the volatility of the credit spread, and
as a function of the correlation between the spark spread and the counterparty credit spread.
Keywords Spark spread · CVA · Commodity swap

the correlation between the electricity and natural gas return processes and between these

and the mean reversion of electricity and gas prices, which is reﬂected in the seasonality and
the mean reversion of their forward prices. We focus on the analysis of counterparty risk
implications for the contract fair value rather than for hedging policies. For this reason, we
use a fully arbitrage free and ﬁne-tuned risk neutral valuation approach and our processes
are calibrated to liquid market information on forward curves.
We specialize this approach for the pricing of swaps on the spark spread, though it can be
used to price a wider set of derivatives on the spark spread and also, with relatively minor
adaptations, for other types of commodity swap contracts. We apply our model to price swaps

energy market disruptions, where the interplay of energy prices and counterparty risk has
had severe ﬁnancial consequences.

2 Deﬁnitions, preliminaries and notation
2.1 Spark spread and spark spread swap
The spark spread is a common metric for the proﬁtability of natural gas-ﬁred electric genera-
tors. A producer of electric power must purchase natural gas and enters contracts for the sale
of the electricity produced. The spark spread is the difference between the price received by
for the electricity produced and the cost of the natural gas needed to produce that electricity.
The proportion in which natural gas is purchased and electricity is sold depends on the heat
rate (or heat rate index) of the production process under consideration.

and electricity is sold, the spark spread can be deﬁned as follows:

daily prices for natural gas and electricity power at various regional trading points.
Given its deﬁnition, the spark spread can be seen as the payoff of a portfolio being com-

appropriate ratio which depends on the heat rate index of the production process, as well

Derivatives on the spark spread offer utilities, generators, marketers, and market makers
the ability to lock in a margin on current and future generation. The price risk to which
the energy producer is exposed on both markets can be hedged by entering a spark spread
swap contract. By purchasing a swap contract effectively both parties ﬁx the spark spread,

the production of electricity. The fair price of a ﬁnancial derivatives with these properties,
allowing the counterparty to be subject to default, is the crux of the investigation undertaken
in this study.

2.3 Forward contracts on commodities
A forward contract on a commodity is an agreement to buy a speciﬁed commodity at a certain
future time T for a certain price K, known as the delivery price. Let t be the trading date and
valuation time, t < T . One of the two parties of a forward contract assumes a long position,
and its payoff at maturity time T is given by the spot price of the commodity at time T , ST ,
minus the delivery price K. That is, the payoff is ST −K. The no-arbitrage price at time t
of the contract is given by the risk neutral (i.e. under the standard risk neutral measure P)
expected value of the discounted payoff at time t:

3 Electricity and natural gas price processes
3.1 Electricity power price modelling
In the past two decades, liberalization and deregulation in the electricity market have deter-
mined new price dynamics. Electricity prices, determined by supply and demand ﬂuctuations,
are affected by the physical characteristics of electrical power, which is a ﬂow commodity
with very limited storage. The difﬁculty of storing electricity implies that it must be generated
nearly exactly at the time when it is needed for consumption. Therefore, with the exception
of hydropower, the supply is nearly completely inelastic to price changes. Consequently,
electricity prices exhibit high seasonality, in the sense of seasonal bouts of volatility, which
can be extremely high at times of electricity scarcity and, therefore, of high electricity prices.

4 Risk-neutral valuation of the counterparty credit risk exposure in the
HJM framework
It is known that the choice of an appropriate model for interest rates is crucial for pricing
accuracy, as shown by Recchioni and Tedeschi (2017), and for modeling of credit risk and its
transmission among counterparties (Grilli et al., 2017). To estimate the counterparty credit
risk exposure arising under the swap contract, we model the forward instantaneous risk-free
rate and the credit spread within the HJM framework (Heath et al. (1992)).
Doing so offers two main advantages. The ﬁrst advantage is that, in the formulation of the
spot rate process, the market price of interest rate risk drops out by being incorporated into
the Wiener process under the risk neutral measure. Therefore, when the model is calibrated
to the initial yield curve, it is taken into due account without the need to be estimated.
This is an advantage because of the difﬁculty of estimating the market price of risk from
either historical or survey data. This difﬁculty is well known (e.g., Cochrane (2005)) and
reinforced by mounting evidence that yields are non-stationary (e.g., they might contain a
drift, as emphasized by Favero and Fernandez-Fuertes (2023)). The second advantage of

well speciﬁed. For natural gas, we use a test based on the Variance Ratio to make inferences
on whether it exhibits mean-reverting dynamic behavior. The variance ratio can be used to
test the null hypothesis that long-run variance is proportional to short-run variance in the
same way in which long-run (log-)returns, calculated over a period of length n × τ, are the
sum of short-run (log-)returns, calculated over consecutive periods of length τ. If this null is
rejected, it means that returns are predictable (i.e., either positively or negatively correlated).
In turn, since it is well known that for returns we can rule out a stochastic trend (i.e., a random
walk), rejection of the null of the test implies mean-reversion. Looking at Panel B of Fig.4
in Appendix, we observe that the variance ratio function is decreasing and always less than
one, implying that we can indeed reject the null of this test and, therefore, pointing to clearly
mean-reverting behaviour of the natural gas price dynamics.5

electricity power price volatility, natural gas price volatility and credit spread volatility. We
do so to permit an appreciation of these volatilities on CR-CVA. In Table 6 we show the

the payer may agree to pay to its counterparty. The adjustment to the strike price is given
by the estimated CVA and is needed to take into account the estimated counterparty credit
risk exposure of the payer. To permit an assessment of the accuracy of the evaluation, we
also report the standard error for each estimate. The reported results show that the CVA is
very sensitive to the volatility of the electricity power price process for any value of the
correlation, whereas it is sensitive to the volatility of the gas price and of the credit spread
when the correlation is large in absolute value, especially when it is positive, in the case of
natural gas volatility.
These results demonstrate that the CVA depends to an important extent not only on the
volatilities of the gas and electricity prices and of the credit spread process, but also on their
correlations. We see that, as the spark spread tends to move in line with credit spreads, higher
credit spreads will lead to higher spark spread values, and the value of the swap increases.
The size of the CVA hence depends on the precise value of the volatility and correlation
dynamics parameters, that cannot be easily subsumed by simple multipliers. Finally, looking
at the standard errors of the CVA simulations, it can be observed that the accuracy of the
CVA estimates increases if we consider volatility multiplied by 0.5 and 1 instead of 1.5 and
2. In fact, the standard error is signiﬁcant at least at the 5-th decimal place in the case of
electricity and credit spread volatilities and at the 4-th for the natural gas volatility.

6 Conclusions
In this paper, we have proposed a framework for pricing swaps on the spark spread, an impor-
tant and widely use instance of energy market derivatives contract, allowing for counterparty
risk exposure arising when only the counterparty can default.

Our modelling approach is applicable to a relatively broad class of commodity swaps,
though our focus and exempliﬁcative application has been on evaluating the counterparty
risk exposure arising from swaps on the spark spread.
To evaluate the swaps under default risk, we have developed a novel CVA modelling
strategy. To this end, we have applied and extended the framework of Brigo et al. (2008) by
modelling the credit spread and the interest rate curves using the HJM (Heath et al. (1992))
approach, and we have applied it to evaluate the counterparty risk exposure arising from
positions in swaps on the spark spread. In doing so, we have taken into consideration all
relevant correlations, namely the correlation between the electricity and natural gas return
processes and between these and the credit spread.
Wehaveprovidedanexempliﬁcationofourapproachbasedondataoncontractsexchanged
on the Italian derivatives market.
In our approach, the quantiﬁcation of the CVA can be seen as either a byproduct of the
valuation of the spark spread swaps under default risk or it can become the goal of the
analysis. That is, one possible application is to solve for the CVA. We leave the assessment
of the empirical ﬁt of the CVA under our mode to CVAs implied in traded contract prices
data for future work.
The ﬁnancial implications of our ﬁndings extend beyond theoretical interest, as they pro-
vide actionable insights for market practitioners. The demonstrated sensitivity of CVA to
various volatilities and correlations underscores the necessity of incorporating such factors
into derivative pricing and risk assessment frameworks. This approach can support more accu-
rate valuation of energy derivatives, helping stakeholders manage ﬁnancial exposure more
effectively. Moreover, our framework has broader applicability, enabling ﬁnancial institu-
tions to better navigate the economic challenges associated with counterparty credit risk in
commodity markets.
