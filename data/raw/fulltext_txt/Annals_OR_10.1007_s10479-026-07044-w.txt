Abstract
Convoy movement problem (CMP) involves the routing and scheduling of military con-
voys during war time, peacekeeping, or related operations. Although this study focuses on
wartime CMP, the underlying methodologies have potential relevance for peacetime logis-
tics and civilian emergency mobility planning. Wartime convoy routing is subject to stringent
strategic constraints such as no road crossing, no halts enroute, and maintaining minimum
headway distances. Existing literature faces challenges related to scalability, computational
complexity, and limited use of search heuristics, highlighting the need for faster and reli-
able solution approaches. This study proposes 2 methodologies: (1) a heuristic based on the
breadth ﬁrst search [BFS] principal principle and (2) a Simulated Annealing [SA] meta-
heuristic algorithm. Two categories of instances [CAT1 and CAT2] are generated using
realistic parameters derived from minimum and maximum arc densities. The methodolo-
gies are tested on 70 hypothetical cases across both categories, and results are analyzed. Both
approaches solve the problem within reasonable computation times. On average, the BFS
heuristics yields solutions 6.20% from optimal for CAT1 and 19.43% for CAT2. SA What
forms comparatively better, with deviations of 3.86% for CAT1 and 5.78% for CAT2. Overall,
the ﬁndings indicate that BFS and SA each provide strong performance for speciﬁc classes of
CMP instances when compared with optimal solutions obtained through conventional global
optimization techniques.

1 Introduction
During war time operations, military troops require a continuous ﬂow of supplies, Supported
and protected by groups of vehicles moving together, commonly known as convoys. A typical
convoy may consist of six to three hundred vehicles, spaced 50 to 100 m apart during transit
(Ram Kumar & Narendran, 2011). The scale and structure of convoys are illustrated by the
64-km military convoy deployed by Russia in 2022 during its advance towards Kiev Ukraine.1
Accurate scheduling of convoy movements is critical as timely delivery of military supplies
often determines mission success. However, planning and coordinating such movements in
combat environments is highly challenging. For instance, during the late 1990s, a NATO
military campaign in the Balkans required extensive time and coordination efforts to move a
single convoy into Kosovo (Cummings, 1999).
The Convoy Movement Problem (CMP) involves the strategic planning and coordination
of military convoys, focusing on determining the most efﬁcient routes and schedules for
transportation between designated origin–destination pairs. It is fundamentally a complex
network routing problem that demands careful consideration of operational constraints and
strategic objectives (Tuson & Harrison, 2005). Although traditionally studied in wartime
contexts, the underlying principles of CMP have signiﬁcant crossover potential for peace-
time and civilian applications. Similar routing and scheduling challenges arise in disaster
relief operations large scale humanitarian logistics and the movement of civilian emergency
response ﬂeets, were timely and coordinated movement is equally critical.
CMP becomes particularly relevant when convoys must be transported across a network
withoutencountering conﬂicts enroute. A conﬂictarises when two convoys attemptto traverse
the same edge or intersect at a node simultaneously (Alturki & Lee, 2024). Each convoy has a
deﬁnedorigin–destinationpairandmovesthrougharestrictednetwork,maintainingregulated
spacing between vehicles. Ensuring these temporal and spatial separations is essential to
reduce congestion, avoid exposure to threats, and maintain operational ﬂow.
Routing and scheduling convoys under such constraints are inherently complex CMP’s
are widely recognized as NP hard combinational optimization problems requiring effec-
tive planning of paths and schedules while respecting operational restrictions (Gopalan,
2015; Mokhtar et al., 2020). Each convoy must travel from its origin to its destination, often
under speed limits, time window constraints, and mission critical deadlines (Sadeghnejad-
Barkousaraie et al., 2017). Additional constraints include avoiding interaction between
convoys, such as overtaking, crossing at nodes, or simultaneous occupation of road seg-
ments (Gopalan & Narayanaswamy, 2009; Maniamkot et al., 2022; Mokhtar et al., 2020;
Thomas et al., 2015). This time space conﬂicts substantially increase the computational difﬁ-
culty of the problem, necessitating advanced algorithmic approaches for timely and accurate
solutions.
ThescholarlyliteratureonCMPincludesvariousmathematicalandalgorithmictechniques
designed to address these challenges classical approaches include Lagrangian relaxation
and Mixed Integer Linear Programming (Ezhil & Rajendran, 2020; Ju & Chung, 2023).
Building on these researchers have experimented with distributed agent models (Thomas
et al., 2015) and hybrid algorithms that combine mathematical programming with heuristic
components (Sadeghnejad-Barkousaraie et al., 2017). However, these methods often face
limitationsrelatedtoscalabilityasmanystruggletogeneratesolutionsfornetworksexceeding
100 nodes. They may also require relaxing certain constraints or involve computationally

2 Literature review
‘Military mobility models’ are network ﬂow algorithms that are created for military convoy
routing. The CMP is a major concern within the large class of military mobility models.
Strategic military movement models have been the subject of numerous academic litera-
ture (McKinzie & Wesley Barnes, 2004). It is to be noted that consistent attempts have been
made to increase the interconnectedness of the models, the usage of sophisticated computer
models for instance (Gray & Vantsevich, 2015) has become essential, and the inability of the
existing algorithms to solve huge practical problems has demonstrated a pressing need for
efﬁcient algorithms (Chardaire et al., 2005). Thus, there is a need for more efﬁcient algo-
rithms to solve CMP with practical constraints in the meantime and have less computational
complexity.
The literature search was conducted using the Scopus database, employing the keyword
“convoy movement problem.” To ensure relevance and academic rigor, the search was limited
to articles published in English, categorized under the subject areas of Business Management
and Mathematics. Furthermore, only journal articles were included in the review, and these
were ﬁltered to select publications appearing exclusively in Q1, Q2, and Q3 quartile-ranked
journals. This approach ensured that the analysis was based on high-quality and credible
scholarly sources.

Based on the scenario, CMP can be classiﬁed in two ways i.e., wartime and peacetime.
Once a convoy is formed, it departs and travels from the source as a single unit. During
wartime, convoys cannot halt enroute; however, peacetime convoys can halt during their travel
from source to destination. The wartime convoy movement proposes to generate a clash-free
plan to move large convoys with constraints on time and space (Thomas et al., 2015), and
the peacetime convoy movement proposes to minimize trafﬁc disruptions (Sadeghnejad-
Barkousaraie et al., 2017). The peacetime CMP also has civilian applications like routing
and scheduling trains, aircraft landings, and automated guided vehicle routing (Maniamkot
et al., 2022).
Based on speed, CMP can be classiﬁed into constant and variable speed. A constant speed
CMP aims to maintain a constant inter-vehicular distance throughout the journey (Chehardoli
& Ghasemi, 2022) however, a variable-speed convoy adjusts the speed of vehicles based on
prevailing trafﬁc conditions (Grumert et al., 2015; Li et al., 2018). Based on the situation,
CMP can be classiﬁed into static and dynamic. While a static situation CMP refers to a
situation where military convoys are scheduled and routed in a way that doesn’t allow for
interactions, such as passing through a node or passing one another, a dynamic situation CMP
is the process of routing and scheduling military convoys considering real-time changes and
constraints (Gopalan, 2015; Maniamkot et al., 2022; Mokhtar et al., 2020).
CMP can also be classiﬁed based on the objectives, modes of transportation, number of
transporters, and convoy size. Based on objectives, CMP can be classiﬁed into single and
multiple objective problems. The extant literature primarily consists of studies that seek to
optimize one objective function i.e., minimizing the total travel time or minimizing the travel
span. However, there could be other objectives like minimizing the cost, risk, and delay and
maximizing the use of assets (Ram Kumar & Narendran, 2010). Different modes of trans-
portation are employed while moving military convoys from one place to another, depending
upon the connectivity and the terrain traversed. Accordingly, CMPs are classiﬁed into single
and multiple modes of transport. Most CMPs employ a single mode of transportation i.e.,

road or rail, while certain conditions demand movement by multiple modes (Ram Kumar
& Narendran, 2010). Further, based on the number of transporters available, CMP can be
classiﬁed into limited and unlimited transporters. Limited transporters indicate restrictions
on the obtainability and use of transportation assets, while unlimited transporters indicate
abundant availability of transportation assets (Ram Kumar & Narendran, 2010). Lastly, based
on the convoy length, CMP can be classiﬁed into particle and non-particle convoys. Convoys
are modelled as particles when the length of the edge is large compared to convoy lengths.
In dense networks with short edge traversal times, the length of a convoy plays a crucial
role in coordinating operations. The length of the convoy must be speciﬁcally considered to
solve such challenges. It is assumed that convoys have zero length when they are treated as
particles (Ram Kumar & Narendran, 2010).
CMP can also be classiﬁed based on the solution procedure adopted, viz., classical opti-
mization techniques and heuristics. Approaches such as branch-and-bound algorithms are
based on classical optimization techniques generate reliable solutions and lower bounds for
CMP. On the other hand, heuristics and meta-heuristics approach like Genetic Algorithm
(GA), Simulated Annealing (SA), and Tabu Search (TS) generate near-optimal solutions
quickly (Ram Kumar & Narendran, 2010).
2.2 Algorithms used to solve a CMP
Schank et al. (1991) and McKinzie and Barnes (2004) examined numerous military mobility
models. They summarize every model, including information on its operation and its pros and
cons for military movement applications. The authors noted that over the last two decades,
a concentrated effort has been made to increase the accuracy and interconnection of these
models. Due to the complexity of military logistics, sophisticated computer models for anal-
ysis are used. The current models either use simple, inefﬁcient, naïve techniques or laborious
classical optimization procedures.
A simpliﬁed version of the CMP was presented by Chardaire et al. (2005) using an integer
programming framework based on the idea of a time–space network. Lagrangian relax-
ation was used, and a modiﬁed Dijkstra’s method was employed to assess the dual function
(Dijkstra, 2022). Although the authors accepted that there are constraints to crossing and
overtaking, they removed them from their model to simplify it. They stated that numerous
real-world scenario issues were successfully solved using their technique, and the outcomes
were judged to be promising.
Bovet et al. (1991) considered the convoy scheduling problem and solved the problem of
scheduling the movements of a collection of convoys along the same road to reach different
destinations. They considered the no-crossing constraint and speciﬁed the time window for
the convoy to depart from its origin. The authors also show the equivalence of the CMP to a
one-processor sequencing problem with due dates and ready times, which is NP-complete.
Two formulations for the problem, one based on integer programming and the other based on
graphs, were developed. A Tabu search algorithm was also proposed to solve the problem.
Lee et al. (1996) developed three approaches to solving a CMP: a branch and bound
algorithm, a Genetic algorithm and a hybrid approach combining branch and bound and
genetic algorithm. Every suggested method was examined using both ﬁctitious data and
actual situations. It can be inferred from the authors’ conclusion that the convoy routes can
be made better by implementing start delays. They highlighted that it is technically viable to
permit delays at speciﬁc intermediary nodes and that these delays might improve the quality
of the solutions.

in either case, computational complexity was considered a major challenge in solving CMP.
A CMP under static and dynamic situations can be solved using mathematical approaches
like Lagrangian Relaxation and hybridized algorithms. However, a major challenge associ-
ated with the techniques is the complex computation demanding the need for more heuristic
methods (Gopalan, 2015; Maniamkot et al., 2022; Mokhtar et al., 2020).
The review highlights that a CMP with fewer constraints, such as movement during
peacetime, can be simulated using algorithms that require minimal computation. However,
simulating a CMP with multiple and rigid constraints, such as wartime movement or the
movement of trains and autonomous vehicles, requires computationally intensive mathemat-
ical models. The following table compares the existing literature in the domain with the
current work and differentiates it in a systematic manner.
In summary, the literature shows that existing methods rely on complex algorithms that are
computationally demanding, and in some cases, strategic constraints were relaxed to obtain
solutions. Prior studies also did not report average computational times or evaluate heuristic
accuracy, creating a clear performance gap. This highlights the need for a simpler heuristic
that reduces computational complexity while retaining all strategic constraints. The present
study addresses this by simulating wartime convoy movement using a heuristic based on
Breadth-First Search and Simulated Annealing, offering effective solutions with reasonable
computation.
In this study, a war-time CMP is considered. The objective is to minimize the total move-
ment time for all the convoys subject to the constraints that two convoys do not cross each
other, and no convoy stops enroute till it reaches its destination.
3 Problem description and heuristic methodologies
The war-time CMP is deﬁned as follows: Let G = (V, E) be a digraph, V is the set of vertices
and E is the set of edges in a route network G. Let set U, be the convoys to be moved.
Associated with each convoy is a set of convoy speciﬁc characteristics such as the origin and
destination nodes, and nose to tail distance parameters. The convoys are strictly not allowed
to cross each other. The objective of the problem is to minimize the total movement time
for all convoys from source to destination. Two approaches are proposed for solving the

war-time CMP: Breadth First Search (BFS) heuristic and a Simulated Annealing (SA) search
algorithm.
The two approaches are discussed in detail in this section. Before going into the details
of the proposed solution methodologies, conﬂict resolution methods in the heuristics are
discussed ﬁrst.
Two kinds of conﬂicts might occur: (Case 1) opposite direction conﬂict (Case 2) same
direction conﬂict.
Case 1: Consider an arc and two convoys (C1 and C2) are competing to traverse along
the same arc in opposite direction (see Fig. 1). Assume that convoy C1 reaches E before C2
reaches E’. Convoy C1 is allowed to traverse the arc EE’. Convoy C2 is not allowed to travel
on the arc E’E. A second shortest path from the source of convoy C2 to the destination of
convoy C2 is determined. C2 is allowed to traverse through the alternative path.
Case 2: Consider an arc and two convoys (C1 and C2) are competing to traverse along the
same arc in same direction (see Fig. 2). Assume that convoy C1 reaches E before C2 reaches
E. Resolve the conﬂict based on the ﬁrst-in-ﬁrst-out rule. Convoy C1 is allowed to traverse
the arc EE’. If the convoy C2 has the minimum headway to traverse in arc EE’, then allow
C2 to travel in the arc. Else, travel of C2 in EE’ is forbidden. The second shortest path for
C2 is determined and C2 traverses through the alternative path.

3.1 Breadth-first search heuristic

heuristic, a BFS search algorithm is used to ﬁrst determine the shortest paths for each convoy
in the network. BFS heuristic is terminated once the destination vertex is reached. This early
termination is used as the time and space complexity of the BFS search increases with number
of vertices and edges in the network. This termination condition helps us to get quick and
good solutions.

3.2 Simulated annealing (SA)
SA has been successfully used also for routing by Osman (1993) and Lin et al. (2009). In this
study, a SA heuristic procedure is developed to determine the best possible shortest paths for
the convoys in the given network. The steps in SA are detailed below:
Step 1. For each convoy, generate a random initial solution to give as an input to the SA algo-
rithm (see Sect. 3.2.1). Evaluate the objective function using the decoding procedure
discussed (Sect. 3.2.2).
Step 2. Get the initial solution string and start the SA algorithm (Sect. 3.2.4). See Sect. 3.2.3
on the parameter settings for initial parameter settings for SA.
Step 3. Perform the swap perturbation operation as deﬁned and accept or discard the per-
turbed solution based on acceptance probabilities as deﬁned in Sect. 3.2.3.
Step 4. Terminate the algorithm, when the number of iterations reaches the stopping cri-
terion. Return to the shortest path of the convoys. Decode the shortest path and
evaluate the objective function corresponding to the shortest path.
Step 5. Calculate the arrival times of the convoy at each node of its shortest path determined
by SA.
Step 6. Determine if there are any conﬂicts between the convoys; If there is a conﬂict, resolve
them using conﬂict (); In the conﬂict () function, the shortest paths are calculated
by repeating steps 1 to 5 (i.e., using the SA procedure). Repeat until all the conﬂicts
are resolved.

5 Discussion and conclusions
CMP is NP Hard and solving it using the exact method requires huge computational effort. In
thisstudy,awar-timeconvoymovementproblemisstudied.Twosolutionmethodologieswere
proposed: a Heuristics based on BFS and a Simulated Annealing algorithm. The contribution
of this work lies in the development of the SA algorithm and the BFS heuristic to solve
the CMP. The validity of the proposed methodologies was tested with hypothetical datasets.
Both the proposed methodologies solve the problem in reasonable computation time. The
proposed BFS heuristic provides good solutions for CAT1 problems, whereas SA algorithm
produces good solutions for both CAT1 and CAT2 problems, when compared to the optimal
solutions obtained from CPLEX.

6 Limitations and future work
Although the study offers valuable insights into solving the wartime convoy movement prob-
lem using BFS heuristics and simulated annealing several limitations must be acknowledged.
While the study introduces a new and different method for a very speciﬁc problem, it does
not account for cyclical time patterns particularly the fact that convoy operations typically
occur during night to minimize exposure. This omission is signiﬁcant as incorporating day-
night cycles, camping breaks, multi-driver rotations, and driver rest periods would greatly
enhance the operational realism. The model also assumes continuous uninterrupted move-
ment and does not consider practical constraints such as refueling, maintenance related halts
or speciﬁed start and end time windows for convoy movement.
Additionally, the study treats convoys as homogeneous entities and relies on deterministic
travel times leaving out critical real-world uncertainties such as mechanical failures, weather
disruptions, road closures, or hostile threats. The networks used are entirely hypothetical
which limits the generalizability of the ﬁndings to real military terrains with geographic
complexities.
Further the conﬂict resolution strategy focuses largely on rerouting and does not explore
practical alternatives such as phased departures or controlled waiting at safe locations. These
limitations highlight opportunities for future work including integrating richer operational
constraints, modeling uncertainty and exploring more advanced heuristics and metaheuristics
such as genetic algorithm and Ant colony optimization.
Despite these issues, the ﬁndings of the current study remain highly useful and is expected
to stimulate further research aimed at validating extending or adopting these methods. Schol-
ars may also examine the performance of the proposed heuristics on larger and more complex
networks with varied characteristics. Moreover, the approaches present in this study can
be directly adapted for peacetime convoy movement planning and humanitarian logistics,
thereby broadening the practical relevance of the proposed methodologies.
