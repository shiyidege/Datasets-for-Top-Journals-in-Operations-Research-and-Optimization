Abstract
This paper presents a novel multi-objetive methodology for optimizing ﬁnancial investment
decisions. To do this, we integrate machine learning (ML) techniques into a Black–Litter-
man (BL) model, using conditional value at risk (CVaR) as a risk measure within a socially
responsible investment framework. The proposed approach employs ML models, includ-
ing long short-term memory, random forest, artiﬁcial neural networks, gated recurrent unit,
and autoregressive integrated moving average (ARIMA), to forecast asset returns. These
predictions are aggregated through a model based on historical performance, and they are
incorporated into a Black–Litterman–CVaR model (BL–ML–CVaR). Environmental, social,
and governance (ESG) criteria are used to construct portfolios that align ﬁnancial returns with
sustainable investment practices. Results demonstrate that the BL–ML–CVaR portfolio offers
higher returns and balanced risk compared to traditional models, with the ESG-integrated
version achieving competitive performance while adhering to sustainability goals. This study
provides valuable insights into how ML-driven models can enhance investment strategies in
some emerging sectors facing environmental challenges, offering a path toward more sus-
tainable and responsible ﬁnancial practices.

1 Introduction
Sustainable ﬁnance refers to the integration of environmental, social, and governance (ESG)
considerations into ﬁnancial decision-making in the investment world. The aim of this
approach is not only to achieve ﬁnancial returns but also to address long-term sustainability
challenges such as climate change, social inequality, and resource scarcity (Billio et al., 2024).
By embedding ESG principles into their investment strategies, companies and investors seek
to ensure that their activities promote a more sustainable and equitable future.
In recent years, sustainable ﬁnance has grown signiﬁcantly as more organizations rec-
ognize the importance of aligning ﬁnancial objectives with broader social goals. This trend

Sustainable ﬁnance encompasses various investment strategies, including green bonds,
impact investing, thematic investing, and socially responsible investing (SRI) (Tavanti, 2023).
Green bonds are intended to ﬁnance projects that have quantiﬁable and positive environ-
mental outcomes, such as renewable energy developments or infrastructure that promotes
energy efﬁciency (Marín-Rodríguez et al., 2023). Impact investing involves directing capi-
tal to projects or companies whose goal is to generate a measurable social or environmental
impact in addition to economic beneﬁts, with a strong emphasis on addressing speciﬁc global
challenges, such as poverty or sustainability (Busch et al., 2021). Thematic investing, on the
other hand, focuses on capitalizing on long-term trends or themes, such as technology or cli-
mate change, with the primary goal of generating ﬁnancial returns. Similarly, SRI involves
screening investments based on speciﬁc ESG criteria to exclude or favor certain industries or
companies based on their adherence to ethical and sustainable practices (Bilbao-Terol et al.,
2018).
One of the key drivers of sustainable ﬁnance is the recognition that ESG factors can
directly affect ﬁnancial performance (Xiao et al., 2023). Studies have shown that companies
with strong ESG practices tend to exhibit better risk management, more stable long-term
performance, and a stronger reputation. As a result, sustainable ﬁnance is no longer seen as a
niche but rather as a critical component of modern ﬁnancial systems, appealing to a wide array
of stakeholders, including institutional investors, governments, and individuals who seek to
align their values with their ﬁnancial goals. Governments and regulatory bodies worldwide
are also playing an active role in promoting sustainable ﬁnance by setting frameworks,
creating incentives, and mandating the disclosure of ESG-related information. Initiatives
like the European Union’s Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation and the Task Force
on Climate-related Financial Disclosures are examples of efforts to encourage transparency
and accountability in the ﬁnancial sector’s impact on the environment and society (Braasch
& Velte, 2023; Hummel & Jobst, 2024; Kalu et al., 2024).
Our research narrows its focus to sustainability criteria aligned with the United Nations
(UN) Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) number 6: "Ensure access to water and sanitation
for all." According to the UN, water is essential for sustainable development, crucial for
socioeconomic progress, energy production, food security, and ecosystem health. Water also
plays a pivotal role in climate change adaptation, being a critical link between human needs
and environmental sustainability. Moreover, the UN emphasizes the importance of ensuring
access to clean and private sanitation, particularly for women and girls.

As the water market evolves to meet growing demand from agriculture, industry, and
public utilities, it presents opportunities for investors seeking to align their portfolios with
sustainable practices. Investors can choose to support companies developing water-efﬁcient
technologies, water utilities committed to sustainability, or environmental ﬁrms focused on
water puriﬁcation and distribution. In this study, we propose a methodology for optimizing
investments in the water sector. By investing in the water ﬁnancial market, investors can
contribute to solving one of the world’s most pressing sustainability challenges.
The Black–Litterman (BL) model, introduced by Fisher Black and Robert Litterman in
1990 at Goldman Sachs, is one of the most popular frameworks for asset allocation in modern
ﬁnance. By combining market equilibrium with investors’ subjective views on asset returns,
the BL model offers a more ﬂexible approach compared to the Mean–Variance (MV) model
proposed by Markowitz (1952). This ﬂexibility, coupled with its ability to address some of the
limitations of the MV model, has led to its widespread adoption in the investment industry.
This paper explores the potential of machine learning (ML) techniques to enhance the
BL model by integrating data-driven views. This research contributes to the literature on
asset allocation by demonstrating how ML can enhance the BL model and develop investment
strategies aligned with sustainability goals.
Our approach integrates ML to predict the absolute prices of water-related assets, then
aggregates these predictions using a method that adjusts weights based on the accuracy of the
previous week’s forecasts. This is combined with market equilibrium through the BL model
to create a portfolio that balances expected proﬁtability with risk. The risk is measured by
Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR); CVaR takes into account the average losses that exceed the
VaR threshold, providing a more comprehensive, robust, and conservative measure of risk.
By incorporating CVaR into the portfolio construction process, the resulting framework is
formulated as a multi-objective optimization problem that simultaneously balances expected
returns, downside risk, and sustainability-oriented investment objectives. This leads to the
development of a Black–Litterman–machine learning–CVaR (BL–ML–CVaR) model that
integrates price forecasts with a rigorous risk management structure for asset allocation.
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: a review of relevant literature is
presented in Sect. 2; Sect. 3 details the methodology; Sect. 4 delves into an Empirical case;
Sect. 5 discusses the results; and Sect. 6 presents the main conclusions.
2 Literature review
A wide range of studies have employed statistical, ML, and hybrid techniques for ﬁnancial
time-series forecasting and portfolio optimization. Traditional models such as the ARIMA
have been widely applied due to their simplicity and interpretability. For instance, Ariyo et al.
(2014) forecast Nokia stock prices using ARIMA, while Khanderwal and Mohanty (2021)
apply it to three sectors of the NSE index, Khan and Alghulaiakh (2020) predict Netﬂix
stock prices. However, despite their ease of implementation, ARIMA models are limited by
their linear structure and inability to capture the nonlinear dependencies, and sudden regime
changes typical of ﬁnancial markets.
To overcome these limitations, researchers have adopted ML models capable of learning
complex patterns and nonlinear relationships in ﬁnancial data. Selvin et al. (2017) employ
convolutional neural networks (CNN) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) for stock price
prediction. Kompella and Chakravarthy Chilukuri (2020) apply Random Forests for stock
marketforecasting.Sunnyetal.(2020)andLinetal.(2021)showthatlongshort-termmemory

data, reducing forecasting error. Gupta et al. (2022) further enhance predictive performance
through data augmentation in a GRU-based StockNet model, applied to the Indian CNX-Nifty
market. Illa et al. (2022) evaluate Random Forest and Support Vector Machine (SVM) models

of LSTM networks with the ensemble stability of Random Forests, achieving high predictive

Collectively, these studies highlight how ML and hybrid approaches outperform tradi-
tional statistical models in terms of accuracy, adaptability, and robustness. They can process
larger datasets, capture nonlinear dynamics, and quickly adapt to changing ﬁnancial condi-
tions—beneﬁts essential to portfolio management and asset allocation.
The BL model (Black & Litterman, 1990) is an investment methodology that combines
the beneﬁts of Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) with the incorporation of subjective investor
views. Recent studies explore the integration of ML techniques into the BL model to generate
investor views and optimize portfolios. Min et al. (2021) propose a BL model that incorporates
views generated by Random Forest algorithms. Punyaleadtip et al. (2024) use RNN and
support vector regression (SVR) models to estimate investor views for the BL model. Their
approach is tested in both emerging and developed markets, consistently outperforming buy-
and-hold strategies. Duraj and Yu (2023) also contribute to the literature by introducing
an end-to-end version of the BL model that jointly optimizes view generation and portfolio
allocation using deep learning. This integration of ML with portfolio optimization techniques
demonstrates considerable potential.
While the BL model traditionally relies on variance as a risk measure, some authors have
proposed integrating alternative metrics, such as Value at Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value
at Risk (CVaR), to better capture tail risk. VaR, introduced by Morgan (1987), assesses the
maximum expected loss at a given conﬁdence level but provides no information about losses
beyond that threshold. To overcome this limitation, Rockafellar and Uryasev (2000, 2002)
proposed an optimization-based approach to compute and minimize CVaR, which represents
the average losses that exceed VaR, offering a more conservative and comprehensive measure
of downside risk. This makes CVaR particularly suitable for modern portfolio management.
Meucci (2005, 2006) extends the BL model to non-normal markets using copula and
opinion pooling (COP) approaches. He provides a general recipe for implementing the
COP approach, which allows for views on non-normal market distributions. This exten-
sion addresses the limitations of the traditional BL model that assumes normality, making the
model more applicable to real-world markets characterized by asymmetry and fat tails. Jia and
Gao (2016) integrate stock volatility information from options data into the BL model using
a mean-CVaR optimization model. Their approach updates expected returns and covariance
matrices through inverse optimization and moment matching techniques. Pang and Karan
(2018) derive a closed-form solution for a BL–CVaR model under multivariate elliptical dis-
tributions, simplifying optimization while maintaining accuracy. Sahamkhadam et al., (2018,
2022) introduce the copula-based BL (CBL) model, incorporating tail dependence through
vine copulas, and demonstrate its superiority in minimizing CVaR and maximizing Sharpe
ratios. Teplova et al. (2023) extend this idea by combining ARMA-GARCH-copula-based
expected returns with Regular Vine (R-vine) copulas in a BL–CVaR framework, achieving
robust risk–return trade-offs under asymmetric dependencies.

Recent work has explored deep learning–based approaches for portfolio optimization. For
example, Cui et al. (2024) propose a multi-period portfolio model based on deep reinforce-
ment learning that selects among predeﬁned trading strategies rather than directly optimizing
asset weights. This line of research illustrates how modern deep learning techniques can be
applied to portfolio allocation problems.
In parallel, integrating ESG criteria into portfolio optimization has gained growing
academic and practical interest. Schmidt (2020) extends the classical MV framework by
including ESG scores to align investment portfolios with sustainability objectives. Using
MSCI ESG ratings applied to the Dow Jones constituents, the study shows that sustainabil-
ity integration can preserve performance while improving ethical alignment. Alessandrini
and Jondeau (2020) propose a strategy that maximizes ESG quality while maintaining bal-
anced sectoral and regional exposures, avoiding concentration biases common in traditional
screening. Their approach yields risk-adjusted returns comparable to or higher than conven-
tional benchmarks across multiple markets. De Spiegeleer et al. (2021) analyze ESG and
greenhouse gas emissions data across the STOXX Europe 600 and Russell 1000 indices,
revealing that ESG inclusion can maintain or enhance portfolio performance across different
risk proﬁles.
Recent works integrate ML with ESG portfolio construction. Sokolov et al. (2021) intro-
duce an innovative automated method for ESG portfolio construction by employing ML and
the BL framework. Their approach uses news data to generate ESG-related views, which
are then incorporated into portfolio optimization. The growing inclusion of sustainability
and responsible investment criteria has further reinforced the relevance of multi-objective
optimization approaches, which are well established in the portfolio selection literature.
Bilbao-Terol et al. (2023) employ a data envelopment analysis model that integrates both
ﬁnancial and ESG criteria to assess the overall efﬁciency of mutual funds. This model was
applied to a dataset of 144 French mutual funds, including 31 classiﬁed as SRI funds. Varmaz
et al. (2024) propose a multi-index model-based approach to portfolio optimization as a more
ﬂexible and computationally efﬁcient alternative to full covariance matrix estimation, par-
ticularly for incorporating ESG considerations. Additionally, Garcia-Bernabeu et al. (2024)
use a multi-objective genetic algorithm to construct mean–variance–ESG efﬁcient surfaces
and deliver investor portfolio recommendations, optimizing risk, return, and sustainability
dimensions. These studies conﬁrm that responsible investing can align with strong ﬁnancial
performance when advanced optimization techniques are employed.
Within the sustainable investment context, the water sector has received increasing atten-
tion due to its relevance to SDG6. Tularam and Reza (2016) analyze the relationship between
idiosyncratic risk and returns for four water-focused ETFs from 2007 to 2015 using a Markov
switching model. Rompotis (2016) evaluates U.S. water ETFs that invest in water-related
companies marketed as socially responsible. Díaz-Mendoza and Pardo (2023) demonstrate
that water ETFs can act as diversiﬁers in both North American and European stock markets,
hedges for European ﬁxed-income markets, and safe-haven investments for assets related to
the North American ﬁxed-income index and for US and European volatility indices. Reza
et al. (2022) employ the DCC-GARCH model to investigate the dynamic relationships and
volatility spillovers among Asian water indices, thereby highlighting the beneﬁts of port-
folio diversiﬁcation. Together, these studies underscore the potential of innovative ﬁnancial
models to channel investment toward water resource sustainability.
Despite these signiﬁcant advances, most existing studies treat ML-based forecasting, BL
portfolio optimization, CVaR risk assessment, and ESG integration as separate streams of
research. The lack of a uniﬁed framework limits the translation of these innovations into
practical, sustainable investment strategies. This study addresses this gap by proposing a

3 Methodology
In this paper, we construct investment portfolios incorporating ESG criteria in a BL model
that integrates data-driven views obtained through ML techniques and CVaR as the risk
measure (BL–ML–CVaR).
We follow a structured three-stage methodology (see Fig. 1). First, we forecast stock prices
for the upcoming week using various time series forecasting techniques. Then, we aggregate
the forecasts, taking into account their reliability from the previous week. The second stage
involves adjusting ESG scores based on PT. Finally, in the third stage, we apply the BL model,
replacing traditional investor views with our predictions and incorporating CVaR as a risk

3.2 Transforming ESG scores based on Prospect Theory
A company’s sustainability practices and ethical impact are assessed through a composite
score based on ratings on environmental (E), social (S), and governance (G) disclosures. This
score is derived from indicators spanning three key pillars. The environmental pillar includes
indicators on energy use, waste management, pollution control, natural resource conser-
vation, and animal welfare. The social pillar includes indicators on business relationships,
charitable giving, volunteer work, and employee health and safety. Finally, the governance
pillar encompasses indicators that reﬂect corporate governance practices, such as conﬂict of
interest policies, executive compensation, board diversity, and management structure. ESG
rating agencies such as MSCI, Sustainalytics, S&P Global, and Reﬁnitiv (now part of LSEG
Data & Analytics since 2021) provide investors and stakeholders with detailed assessments
of companies’ environmental, social, and governance performance. These scores help guide
their responsible investment decisions, allowing them to focus on companies that align with
sustainable and ethical values.
Although ESG scores provide a measure of sustainability performance, they may not
fully consider how investors perceive risks and opportunities. According to PT (Kahneman
& Tversky, 1979), people are more sensitive to losses than gains, meaning that negative ESG
outcomes (such as environmental disasters or governance failures) could weigh more heavily
in decision-making than positive ESG outcomes. This asymmetric sensitivity to losses and
gains is visually represented in the PT value function (see Fig. 2).
We apply a methodology based on PT to adjust the original ESG scores, motivated by
PT’s ability to alleviate the issue of compensatory effects in cases where poor performance
could otherwise be offset by good performance. Speciﬁcally, we use a value function which
incorporates risk and loss aversion (Bilbao-Terol et al., 2016; Kahneman & Tversky, 1979).
This approach allows for more accurate differentiation between companies with varying
ESG performance levels by assigning greater penalization to bad scores, ensuring that poor
performance has a more substantial impact and is not completely offset by good scores. The

3.3.4 Using a copula for modeling the dependence structure of the assets
Unlike traditional correlation measures, copulas capture complex dependencies that may not
be linear, making them particularly useful for ﬁnancial modeling where asset returns can
exhibit tail dependencies, meaning extreme events in one asset might be more likely to occur
alongside extreme events in others. A copula is a statistical tool used to model the depen-
dence structure between random variables, allowing for the construction of a multivariate
distribution from individual (univariate) marginal distributions (Frees & Valdez, 1998).

4 Empirical case
This section presents the empirical results obtained from applying the BL–ML–CVaR model
with ESG criteria to the water investment sector. The analysis focuses on comparing the
performance of different portfolio strategies, evaluating their returns, risk proﬁles, and the
impact of ESG integration. By examining portfolios constructed under varying risk and sus-
tainability thresholds, the results illustrate how the proposed methodology enables investors
to achieve a balance between ﬁnancial objectives and responsible investment practices. These
ﬁndings highlight the potential of advanced optimization frameworks to support sustainable
portfolio management in sectors facing signiﬁcant environmental challenges.
To evaluate the proposed BL–ML–CVaR model, we use 55 stocks from the ETF iShares
Global Water UCITS ETF, which perfectly replicates the S&P Global Water index. Our
data set covers the period from 01/07/2019 to 30/06/2024, incorporating weekly closing
prices on Mondays. It is important to note that this timeframe includes the onset of the
Gaza conﬂict (07/10/23). This period is chosen because the window contains heterogeneous
regimes (post-COVID normalization, a mid-cycle sideways phase, and the October 2023
Gaza shock), which is crucial for stress-testing risk-aware portfolios in the water sector.
The following companies have been excluded: Pennon Group PLC (3PN0.DU) and Veralto
Corporation (VLTO) due to only having data available for the last few months. The data,
including closing prices, are obtained from the Yahoo Finance database.
A descriptive analysis of the weekly returns of the selected stocks is presented in Table
A1 in the Appendix. On average, the assets exhibit a mean return of 16.4% with a standard
deviation of 0.36, indicating the overall level of variability observed in the sample. The mean
minimum weekly return reaches –21%, while the mean maximum weekly return attains 66%,
reﬂecting the range of extreme ﬂuctuations experienced by the stocks during the 2019–2024
period. These statistics provide a concise overview of the return behaviour of the constituents
oftheS&PGlobalWaterindexandestablishtheempiricalbasisforthesubsequentapplication
of BL–ML–CVaR portfolio optimization. In the forecasting stage, the models are estimated
using a rolling window of the previous 30 weeks of price data to predict the following
week’s prices. This rolling-window procedure allows the models to continuously update
their parameters using the most recent information.
In this paper, ESG scores assessing companies’ performance across the Environmental
(E), Social (S), and Governance (G) pillars are obtained from LSEG Data & Analytics, with
data collected as of July 2024. This rating agency compiles ESG information from more
than 630 indicators across public sources, including annual reports, sustainability reports,
regulatory documents, and corporate websites. From these indicators, the most relevant for
each sector are selected and grouped into 10 categories, divided across the three pillars. Each
category is weighted by its relevance to the company’s industry. The scores are calculated
using a percentile methodology, comparing the company’s performance with its peers in the
same industry and region. These scores range from 0, indicating poor ESG performance,

4.1 Prediction results
We forecast prices for the upcoming week using the previous 30 weeks of data, employing
the following techniques: LSTM, Random Forests, GRU, RNN, and ARIMA. A grid search
process is utilized to optimize hyperparameters for the LSTM, GRU, and RNN models. The
tuning process includes varying the number of units per layer across three sets of values:
[64, 128, 256], [32, 64, 128], and [16, 32, 64] for each layer, respectively. Dropout rates are
systematically adjusted, ranging from 0.0 to 0.2 for the ﬁrst two layers and from 0.0 to 0.1 for
the third layer. Additionally, batch sizes of 16 and 32 are evaluated to determine their impact
on model performance. Next, we utilize Min–Max scaling to normalize the data, ensuring
that the values are bounded between 0 and 1. We use 70% of the dataset (1 July 2019–26
December 2022) as the training set, 10% serves as the validation set (2 January 2023–26
June 2023), and the remaining 20% (3 July 2023–1 July 2024) functions as the test set.
In ML, it is common practice to allocate most of the available data to training while reserv-
ing separate validation and test sets to ensure reliable hyperparameter tuning and unbiased
performance evaluation. Recent studies have adopted a 70% / 10% / 20% split for train-
ing, validation, and testing. This conﬁguration is widely regarded as a practical and standard
choice for evaluating model performance on evolving datasets while preserving a sufﬁciently
large and independent test set (Bichri et al., 2024; Dileo et al., 2025).

4.4 Computational complexity
The computational cost of the proposed framework is primarily driven by the machine learn-
ing–based forecasting stage and the subsequent portfolio optimization. While the training
of ML models entails higher computational costs, these processes are performed asyn-
chronously. Portfolio optimization is carried out synchronously and formulated as a convex
optimization problem, whose computational complexity scales polynomially with the number
of assets and simulated scenarios, ensuring tractability for practical portfolio sizes.
The framework is implemented in Python 3.11. Black–Litterman return estimations and
portfolio construction are performed using the PyPortfolioOpt library. Portfolio optimization
is formulated and solved using CVXPY, relying on a convex optimization solver (ECOS).
Asset return volatility is modelled using a GARCH(1,1) process estimated using the arch
package. The multivariate Student’s t copula was ﬁtted via maximum likelihood, resulting in
an estimated degrees of freedom parameter ranging between 3 and 6.

6 Conclusions
This study introduces an innovative methodology for optimizing sustainable investments
by integrating ML techniques into a BL model that employs CVaR and requires adherence
to certain ESG levels. The proposed approach uses ML models, including LSTM, Ran-
dom Forest, ANN, GRU, and ARIMA, to forecast asset prices. These predictions are then
aggregated based on historical performance and incorporated into a Black–Litterman–CVaR
model (BL–ML–CVaR). The inclusion of CVaR as a risk measure allows for a more conser-
vative assessment of potential losses. Additionally, incorporating ESG data ensures that the
constructed portfolios align ﬁnancial returns with sustainable investment practices.

Annals of Operations Research
The proposed model has been applied to the water sector. The results show that the
BL–ML–CVaR portfolio outperforms traditional benchmarks, delivering higher returns while
maintaining moderate risk. Furthermore, integrating ESG criteria into the investment process
demonstrates that achieving respectable returns without compromising sustainability and
social responsibility is possible. The study’s ﬁndings suggest that the proposed model can
serve as a valuable tool for investors seeking to balance proﬁtability with ethical investment
practices.
The incremental contributions of the main components of the proposed BL–ML–C-
VaR–ESG framework can be summarized as follows. First, the incorporation of machine-
learning–based perspectives enhance the return forecasts by capturing nonlinear information
that the classic BL model cannot. In addition, aggregating individual machine learning mod-
els yields more robust predictive signals than any single speciﬁcation. In the absence of this
component, the resulting portfolio would likely exhibit lower adaptability to complex return
dynamics. Second, the PT transformation mitigates potential offset effects by asymmetrically
penalizing assets with low ESG scores. Without this adjustment, the effectiveness of the ESG
constraints would be reduced, potentially allowing portfolios that satisfy the aggregate sus-
tainability thresholds while still including assets with signiﬁcant environmental, social, or
governance weaknesses. Third, the adoption of copula-based CVaR models enhances the
representation of tail dependence and extreme downside risk. If this component were omit-
ted, downside risk could be underestimated, leading to portfolios that are less resilient under
extreme market conditions. Overall, the joint integration of these three elements enables the
proposed framework to simultaneously balance proﬁtability, risk management, and sustain-
ability considerations.
Future research may extend this analysis by applying the proposed framework to other
sectors or geographic regions facing signiﬁcant environmental challenges. Such extensions
could provide broader insights into the performance of machine learning–driven investment
strategies across different contexts and contribute to the development of more generaliz-
able portfolio models. In addition, the present study does not incorporate transaction costs,
which represent an important practical consideration and should be addressed in future work.
Additionally, artiﬁcial intelligence techniques may be employed to analyze investors’ risk
proﬁles and ESG preference structures. Natural language processing methods—such as sen-
timent analysis, and transformer-based embedding models— could be applied to textual
disclosures, surveys, or investor communications to infer granular behavioral patterns and
sustainability preferences. Integrating such personalized preference modeling into the frame-
work would enable more tailored portfolio recommendations and strengthen the alignment
between sustainable investment strategies and individual investor objectives.
Overall, the integration of machine learning techniques in sustainable investment with
the Black–Litterman model and the CVaR risk measure provides a ﬂexible framework for
incorporating predictive information, downside risk control, and ESG considerations into
portfolio optimization. By combining these elements, the proposed approach contributes to
the advancement of more sustainable and responsible investment strategies, particularly in
sectors where environmental challenges play a central role in ﬁnancial decision-making.
