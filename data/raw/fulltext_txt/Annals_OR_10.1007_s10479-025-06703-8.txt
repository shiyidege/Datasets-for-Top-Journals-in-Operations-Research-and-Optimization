Abstract
A constrained Markov decision process (CMDP) approach is developed to construct re­
sponse-adaptive procedures in clinical trials with binary outcomes. The resulting CMDP 
class of Bayesian response-adaptive procedures can target a certain objective, such as maxi­
mizing expected treatment outcomes, while using constraints to control other operating char­
acteristics such as the type I error rate. The constraints can be formulated under different 
priors, enforcing control of trial operating statistics given that the parameters lie in a specific 
part of the parameter space (e.g., following from a statistical hypothesis). A solution method 
is developed to find an optimal policy, while we propose a computationally efficient method 
based on a cutting plane algorithm that uses backward recursion at every iteration, yield­
ing a feasible policy accompanied by a respective optimality gap. In three applications, we 
impose constraints on the Bayesian average (i.e., a priori expected) type I error rate, power, 
and mean-squared error, and a constraint on prior robustness. Comparison of the constructed 
CMDP procedures with the constrained randomized dynamic programming procedure shows 
a stronger frequentist type I error control and similar performance in the other operating 
characteristics when constraining the Bayesian average type I error rate, power, and mean-
squared error, whereas CMDP additionally shows substantial outperformance in terms of 
expected treatment outcomes when only constraining the type I error rate and power. The 
third application shows that the CMDP approach provides a previously unexplored way to 
construct response-adaptive procedures with a desired degree of robustness to prior mis­
specification in terms of expected treatment outcomes.
Keywords  Bayesian optimisation · Power constraints · Type I error control · Mean-
squared error control · Prior misspecification control · Adaptive treatment allocation

1  Introduction
The current gold standard for assessing the efficacy of an experimental treatment is the ran­
domized controlled trial (RCT) (Bhatt, 2010). Historically, participants enrolled in an RCT 
are randomized to each treatment, where the probability of allocation is independent of the 
history of outcomes in the trial. This approach induces a high quality of statistical operating 
characteristics (OCs) such as low variance of the treatment effect estimator or high power 
for statistical tests, making it possible to make statements on how the experimental treatment 
will perform for a new patient, not considered in the trial (extrapolation), which is important 
in settings with large patient populations (Palmer, 1993). Furthermore, independence of the 
outcomes and allocations ensures comparability of treatment groups and robustness to time 
trends. A downside of this approach is that a large part of the patients included in the trial 
might obtain inferior treatment. For a two-arm trial with equal allocation probabilities, this 
could concern half the patients, while for a multi-arm trial, this percentage can be even higher. 
The effect of this depends on the severity and nature of the illness. For instance, the effect 
is greater when the illness is very severe. Also, for rare diseases, a large part of the patients 
having the disease will be included in the trial, and in this setting extrapolation of trial results 
might be considered less important. In such cases, it could be desirable to make a different 
trade-off between individual ethics and collective ethics (Heilig and Weijerb, 2005).
An alternative, aiming to make such a trade-off, is a response-adaptive  (RA) proce­
dure (see, e.g., Antognini & Giovagnoli, Rosenberger (1996); Rosenberger, 2015; Villar & 
Jacko, 2022). The general idea behind an RA procedure, introduced in Thompson (1933), 
is to sequentially base the allocation of the next arriving participant to a treatment on the 
current history of outcomes and allocations in the trial (trial history), aiming to reach a cer­
tain objective, e.g., high expected treatment outcomes or a high quality of statistical OCs. 
RA procedures have been proposed where the allocation probability is strictly between zero 
and one for all trial histories, or where the allocation is deterministic for at least one trial 
history. An RA procedure in the former class is termed a response-adaptive randomiza­
tion (RAR) procedure, while an RA procedure in the latter class is termed a deterministic 
response-adaptive (DRA) procedure. A clinical trial using an RA procedure will from now 
on be termed an RA design, while a design where the treatment allocations and outcomes 
are independent will be termed a non-adaptive (NA) design. In recent years, RA procedures 
have found a growing number of applications in clinical trials (Berry and Viele, 2023), 
where most of these applications involved RAR procedures. The US FDA has encouraged to 
consider multiple possibilities for statistical trial design, including RAR procedures in US 
FDA (2019), while this guidance document also highlights the controversies of RA proce­
dures (see Robertson, Lee, López-Kolkovska, & Villar 2023, for an overview and comment).
The current paper focuses on optimisation-based Bayesian RA (BRA) procedures. Opti­
misation-based BRA procedures focus on the exploration-exploitation trade-off, which, in 
a clinical trial, corresponds to the aim of learning which treatment is best while simultane­
ously allocating the highest amount of patients to the best treatment. For a recent literature 
review see Williamson (2020, Chapter 2).
BRA procedures are mainly classified in three categories in the literature:
 
●
Index-based approaches
 
For an index-based BRA procedure, an index value is independently determined based

on the data collected for each treatment group, and the treatment with the highest in­
dex value has the highest probability of being allocated to the next patient by the RA 
procedure. Examples are DRA procedures such as the Gittins index procedure (Gittins, 
1979), Bayes-UCB procedure (Kaufmann et al., 2012), and RAR procedures such as 
semi-randomized index-based approaches (Bather, 1981).
 
●
Thompson sampling (and modifications)
 
For Thompson sampling (introduced in Thompson, 1933) the treatment with the highest 
posterior probability of having the highest expected outcome has the highest probability 
of being allocated to the next patient. A modification of Thompson sampling has been 
proposed in Thall and Wathen (2007) which induces a smaller variance in the allocation 
probabilities. See Du et al. (2018) for other modifications.
 
●
Markov decision process (MDP) approaches
 
MDP procedures, introduced in Bradt et al. (1956), equal an optimal policy (i.e., op­
timal solution) found for an MDP. MDP procedures sequentially allocate treatment to 
trial participants to maximize a given objective function, e.g., the expected treatment 
outcomes up to a fixed horizon, given a prior distribution on the parameters of the 
model. The MDP approach will be the main focus of the current paper.
Out of the three optimisation-based BRA procedures, the MDP approach is the most com­
putationally intensive, as shown in Villar et al. (2015). As (modifications of) Thompson 
sampling can directly be applied in blocked randomized designs, the second approach is 
currently the most common approach when implementing RA procedures in practice (Berry 
and Viele, 2023), while the other two approaches have the potential to show the highest 
expected treatment outcomes (see, e.g., Villar et al., 2015; Williamson, Jacko, Villar, & 
Jaki, 2017)
The MDP approach is a natural optimisation method for the setting of a clinical trial, as 
every outcome is weighted equally and the finite trial size is taken into account (Hardwick, 
1995). A variety of different MDP procedures have been proposed in the literature, showing 
the flexibility of this approach. In Berry and Eick (1995), an MDP procedure was introduced 
which optimises, under a Bayesian model, the expected outcomes of participants in the trial 
as well as the expected outcomes of a finite number of patients which are all allocated to 
one treatment after the trial is completed. This procedure hence makes an explicit trade-
off between individual and collective ethics. While RAR procedures can be constructed 
based on index-based BRA procedures (Bather, 1981), the randomization component is not 
taken into account in the optimisation. Cheng and Berry (2007) introduced an MDP RAR 
procedure where an optimal RA procedure was determined based on randomized treatment 
allocations. Williamson (2020) considered extensions of this approach, including penaliza­
tion of a large imbalance in treatment group sizes, delays in outcomes, and random arrivals. 
Hardwick (1995) introduced a multi-objective MDP procedure, where the two objectives 
revolved around expected treatment outcomes and best treatment selection. In Merrell et 
al. (2022) an MDP procedure was formulated for block-wise allocation, optimising the 
block sizes, number of blocks, and treatment allocations for blocks, where in the objec­
tive a trade-off is made between the expected number of successes and OCs. Yi and Wang 
(2023) formulated a frequentist infinite-horizon MDP for allocation in a clinical trial with 
general outcomes and showed that the allocation ratio converges, ensuring the validity of 
likelihood-based tests.

The current paper introduces a novel class of constrained MDP (CMDP) procedures. 
The class of CMDP procedures consists of policies maximizing an expected sum of rewards 
under one expectation operator given that several different sums of rewards under other 
expectation operators are bounded by specific values. The latter property makes that our 
CMDP formulation is different from the CMDP formulation in Altman (1999). Our CMDP 
formulation can be used to construct policies maximizing the expected sum of rewards 
under one prior distribution, while constraining the expected sum of costs under other prior 
distributions following from, e.g., protection of expected treatment successes to prior mis­
specification or different statistical hypotheses on the parameter values. In addition to the 
optimal feasible policies for a CMDP, this paper also refers to the feasible policies found 
by our proposed approximate solution method (detailed in Sect. 3.3) as CMDP procedures, 
seen to consistently result in near-optimal feasible policies in the current paper. As in Cheng 
and Berry (2007) and Williamson et al. (2017), the degree of randomization for a CMDP 
procedure can be enforced through a maximum randomized allocation rate. Different CMDP 
procedures can be constructed by using different expectation operators, reward functions, 
constraints, and different maximum randomized allocation rates.
In comparison to previous methods from the literature, the CMDP approach can be con­
sidered a more natural way to directly impose a desired behaviour for an RA procedure while 
ensuring that, given the imposed constraints, the obtained procedure is optimal. An advan­
tage over penalized or multi-objective approaches, such as the ones proposed in Hardwick 
(1995), Williamson et al. (2017), and Merrell et al. (2022), is that, beforehand, the effect of 
adding constraints is known, namely shrinking the set of feasible policies, whereas this is 
not the case when adding a penalty term. Furthermore, the use of a constrained instead of a 
multi-objective approach circumvents the issue that each objective should be defined on a 
comparable scale.
Throughout the current paper we make use of an efficient implementation of backward 
recursion and forward calculations based on the ideas outlined in Jacko (2019), where we 
use a conservation law for the states, use a storage mapping function to store values effi­
ciently, and overwrite elements of the value function not used further in the algorithm. This 
makes it possible to compute the CMDP procedures considered in this paper in a relatively 
short amount of time. Furthermore, this makes it possible to compute the values of clinical 
trial OCs directly instead of approximating them by simulation, avoiding Monte Carlo error.
The current paper mainly evaluates RA procedures using frequentist OCs  (e.g., 
type I error rate or power) as these measures are often required by regulators, are well-
known in the medical literature, and are often used to evaluate RAR procedures (Ventz and 
Trippa, 2015; Robertson et al., 2023). Directly controlling frequentist OCs is difficult in 
general, as frequentist OCs often depend on unknown parameters. Instead, the CMDP-T 
and CMDP-E procedures considered in this paper aim to maximize the expected sum of 
treatment successes while ensuring a weaker form of OC control using constraints on the 
expectation of frequentist OCs under a prior distribution, i.e., Bayesian average OCs. The 
OC control can be strengthened by partitioning the parameter space and defining constraints 
based on prior distributions on each subset, or by adding constraints for specific parameter 
values through point priors.
Historically, Bayesian design methods used utility functions based on loss functions or 
on Shannon information, while nowadays it is not rare to find Bayesian design methods 
based on frequentist OCs (Giovagnoli, 2021). Design methods based on the integration of

frequentist OCs with respect to Bayesian priors are often termed Bayesian design methods, 
and hence we also refer to the CMDP approach as a Bayesian method. When the analysis of 
data coming from such designs is a frequentist data analysis, this would make for a hybrid 
(or Bayesian-frequentist) clinical trial overall. Although not considered in this paper, the 
CMDP also allows for fully Bayesian constraints based, e.g., on posterior variance.
The current paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces the model of a binary 
two-arm response-adaptive clinical trial, as well as relevant OCs. Section  3 introduces 
CMDP procedures, gives their properties, and provides algorithmic methods for determin­
ing CMDP procedures. Section 4 evaluates CMDP procedures targeting maximum expected 
treatment outcomes under constraints on the Bayesian average type I error rate, power, and 
mean-squared error, and robustness to prior misspecification. Section 5 concludes the paper 
and gives directions for future research.
2  Model and operating characteristics

3  Constrained Markov decision processes
In this section, we introduce the class of CMDP procedures. While any Bayesian average 
OC from Sect. 2 can be used in the objective, the CMDP procedures as formulated in this 
section and evaluated in the remainder of the paper specifically aim to maximize expected 
treatment outcomes. The class of CMDP procedures is based on constrained Bayesian opti­
misation, where each CMDP RA procedure is either an optimal feasible policy for a CMDP 
or a feasible policy found by our proposed solution method (Sect. 3.3). Trial participants 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
are sequentially allocated treatment, where the probability of allocating a treatment is deter­
mined based on a trade-off between exploration and exploitation and the resulting distribu­
tion over states should also be such that certain constraints are satisfied. It is allowed to 
formulate the constraints under a different transition structure. This makes it possible to, 
e.g., perform optimisation under a model that does not assume any prior information on θ, 
while a kernel for the type I error rate constraint would restrict θC = θD, and a kernel for the 
power constraint could, e.g., assume |θD −θC| ≥ξ for some ξ ∈(0, 1).

Example 1: Control of type I error rate and power  A downside of RA procedures is that reli­
able statistical inference can be complicated due to an imbalance and variance over treat­
ment group sizes, as well as dependence in the outcomes induced by the RA procedure, 
which can lead to a reduction of power and/or type I error rate inflation in comparison to a 
NA design (Robertson et al., 2023). To test a certain hypothesis on the difference θD −θC 
after collecting data using the CMDP procedure, the constraints (6) can be specified such 
that the Bayesian average type I error rate and power remain under control. This idea has 
similarities with Bayesian sample size determination, where Bayesian average type I error 
rate and power are used to determine the sample size of a clinical trial based on historical 
data (Brown et al., 1987).

Algorithm 1  Cutting plane algorithm for calculating V
Remark 1  Algorithm 1 calls backward recursion in every iteration of the while loop to deter­
mine L(λ) and calls forward recursion |K| times to determine ∇L(λ). Hence Algorithm 1 
is less computationally tractable than standard backward recursion.
Standard backward recursion cannot be used to solve the CMDP problem. First, the con­
straints (6) are formulated using value functions of an optimal policy starting from the initial 
state x0, hence it would not be possible to check whether a decision at, e.g., state n −1 
would lead to violation of the constraints in backward recursion. To do that, one would need 
to know the distribution over states Xn−1 which depends on the policy up to n −1, which 
is at that point unknown (i.e., what is to be solved in the subsequent steps).
Second, as the constraints are stated in terms of a functional (e.g., rejection rate, expecta­
tion) of the distribution over end-states, violation of the constraint cannot be attributed to 
a single action, i.e., the order in which the state-action pairs are chosen would determine 
which action would violate the constraint. This issue can be solved by joint optimization of 
the actions at each decision epoch using, e.g., an LP, but this would already make the algo­
rithm different from the usual backward recursion algorithm.
Several backward recursion-based algorithms for CMDPs have been proposed in the 
literature. In Piunovskiy and Mao (2000) a backward recursion algorithm based on an exten­
sion of the state space was proposed for CMDPs, where each extended state consists of a 
probability distribution on X paired with the expectations in (6). This solution only works 
for problems with smaller state spaces and hence would not be computationally tractable 
for the applications considered in this paper. Other backward recursion-based approaches 
for CMDPs, which are also approximation techniques, are given in Chamie et al. (2019) 
and Satija et al. (2020).

π(x) = 1/2 for all x ∈X.
 
●
Dynamic Programming (DP):
 
This is the RA procedure π found from the unconstrained version of the CMDP problem 
when taking p = 1.00.
 
●
Constrained Randomized Dynamic Programming (CRDP):
 
This is the RA procedure proposed in Williamson et al. (2017) that follows from the un­
constrained version of the CMDP problem () when taking p = 0.9 and adding a penalty 
equal to −n to the objective whenever mina na(xn) < 0.15n.
 
●
Thompson sampling (TS):
 
For this RA procedure, we have π(xt) = Π(θC ≥θD | Xt = xt), i.e., the probability 
of assigning the next patient to the control treatment equals the posterior probability 
that θC ≥θD.
The code for calculation of the CMDP procedures according to Algorithm 1 and code for 
calculation of the OCs uses an efficient implementation of backward recursion and forward 
calculations based on Jacko (2019), i.e., uses the conservation law for the states, a storage 
mapping function, and overwrites elements of the value function in backward recursion and 
probability distribution in forward recursion that are not used further in the algorithm. All 
experiments are performed in Julia 1.9.0 on a laptop with Intel® CoreTM i7-9750 H CPU 
with 2.60GHz clock speed and 16 GB RAM. The absolute tolerance ϵtol for Algorithm 1 is 
set to 10−9 for each evaluation. To obtain a feasible policy in each considered scenario, we 
set φ = 0.01. For the evaluation, we let θD ∈{0.00, 0.01 . . . , 1.00} while fixing θC = 0.5. 
In the numerical evaluation, we set the significance level α to 0.1, in agreement with the 
comparison in Williamson et al. (2017). For all CMDP procedures, we set p = 0.95. Assum­
ing the uniform prior, the allocation probabilities for TS were calculated using adaptive 
Gaussian quadrature (absolute tolerance 10−3).

5  Discussion
The current paper introduced the class of constrained Markov decision process (CMDP) 
response-adaptive procedures. The constraints for the Markov decision process facilitate the 
construction of response-adaptive procedures with good operating characteristics (OCs) 
while prioritizing one OC in the trial (e.g., expected treatment outcomes) by putting it in 
the objective.
Three applications were presented in the current paper. In the first application, con­
straints on the Bayesian average (i.e., a priori expected) type I error rate and power were 
formulated, and the resulting (CMDP-T) response-adaptive procedures were shown to yield 
comparable power to the constrained randomized dynamic programming response-adaptive 
procedure introduced in Williamson et al. (2017) while inducing higher expected treatment 
outcomes and stronger type I error control. Furthermore, it was shown that deterministic 
versions of the CMDP-T procedures also had the desired power properties, while inducing 
higher expected treatment outcomes than the randomized CMDP-T procedures and con­
strained randomized dynamic programming. A deterministic procedure might have certain 
non-desirable properties, e.g., can lead to allocation biases, but it is nonetheless curious 
that deterministic response-adaptive procedures with good power properties could be con­
structed. A topic of future research for CMDP-T procedures could be to evaluate the perfor­
mance of CMDP-T procedures for other tests such as tests for superiority or non-inferiority.
In the second application, we constructed two CMDP procedures by formulating con­
straints on the Bayesian average mean-squared error (MSE) in addition to the type I error 
rate and power constraints. The resulting procedures, CMDP-E1 and CMDP-E2, showed 
low MSE and a similar type I error rate and power behaviour as seen for the CMDP-T proce­
dures. Here, the priors under the constraints were designed such that the constraint reflected 
the average behaviour of the response-adaptive procedure on a specific part of the parameter 
space. The CMDP-E1 procedure showed higher expected treatment outcomes than the non-
adaptive equal allocation procedure while having similar MSE and power. The CMDP-E2 
procedure showed OCs similar to constrained randomized dynamic programming while 
showing stronger type I error control and outperformance in expected treatment outcomes 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
for a trial size of 200 participants. These results also indicate a high-quality performance 
and general applicability of the constrained randomized dynamic programming procedure.
In the third application, CMDP procedures, termed CMDP-R procedures, were devel­
oped for prior robustness, where the objective is to maximize expected treatment outcomes 
under an informative prior, with the restriction that the expected treatment outcomes should 
also be sufficiently high under a less informative prior on the success probabilities. It was 
shown that the constraint parameter ξ could be tuned to target a desired level of robustness 
to prior misspecification. Furthermore, in our evaluation, policies could be generated with 
expected total rewards having a relative difference of within 1% with the optimal value 
under both priors. To the best of our knowledge, ours is the first approach to incorporate 
robustness of total expected treatment successes to prior misspecification directly into the 
design of the response-adaptive procedure.
Other applications might also be of interest to explore. For instance, in line with recent 
literature on higher moments and tail bounds of the regret distribution for multi-armed ban­
dits (Fan and Glynn, 2023), it could be possible to define constraints on moments and tails 
of the number of suboptimal decisions made by the Markov decision process procedure, 
ensuring that the performance of the response-adaptive procedure in terms of expected treat­
ment outcomes is consistent over different data sets. The constraints could also be used to 
constrain the allocations to both treatment groups to a minimum amount with high probabil­
ity, as is the purpose of the penalty term in constrained randomized dynamic programming, 
and differences in OCs between such a constrained approach and constrained randomized 
dynamic programming could be investigated.
In Application 2 (where the focus was on the MSE), it was seen that to obtain a good 
performance in all OCs, the parameters in the constraints had to be chosen in a specific 
manner. This is mainly because the constraints are Bayesian, hence they only guarantee 
a certain behaviour when averaged over a prior. To automatically obtain good frequentist 
OCs, it would be interesting to explore approaches more in line with robust optimisation, 
or approaches that add a second layer of optimisation. Bias reduction techniques, e.g., those 
listed in Bowden and Trippa (2017) could also be explored. It would also be interesting to 
consider the performance of CMDP procedures based on fully Bayesian constraints con­
sidering e.g., the posterior probability of superiority of a treatment at the end of the trial, 
posterior variance or a Bayes factor (Reyes and Ghosh, 2013; US FDA, 2020), which would 
make for a fully Bayesian clinical trial. Constraints on the OCs of such test procedures can 
readily be included in the CMDP procedure, while they leverage prior data and could hence 
lead to more efficiency than Fisher’s exact test, which is only based on the within-trial 
information.
In this paper, we proposed a cutting plane algorithm based on Lagrange multipliers and 
backward recursion to calculate CMDP response-adaptive procedures. In our applications, 
we found that this algorithm yields a policy with a small relative optimality gap in all cases. 
However, there is no guarantee that this will always be the case. Namely, in settings where 
the amount of constraints is larger in comparison to the number of state-action pairs, this 
method might result in a policy with a larger optimality gap. Future research could focus 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
on alternative solution methods that find approximately optimal solutions for the CMDP in 
different ways, e.g., by using the linear programming formulation more directly.
For the sake of simplicity and tractability, the current paper considers a trial with two 
arms and binary outcomes. It would be interesting to extend the approach to more general 
settings, such as multiple trial stages, outcomes, and arms. As the computational effort for 
calculating CMDP procedures would quickly increase in such settings, it would be inter­
esting to investigate whether approaches such as approximate dynamic programming or 
reinforcement learning could be (successfully) applied. It would furthermore be interesting 
to consider whether the CMDP framework and results such as Lemma 1 could be extended 
to different outcome types. One could think of a generalization of the model in Sect. 2 
along the lines in Yi and Wang (2023), where a clinical trial with general outcome types 
was described using a Markov decision process. Furthermore, the assumption underlying 
the applicability of response-adaptive procedures is that the inter-arrival times of trial par­
ticipants are longer than the follow-up times. If this is not the case, the CMDP procedure 
needs to be able to deal with delayed responses. In Williamson et al. (2021), the robustness 
of constrained randomized dynamic programming to delayed responses was demonstrated. 
It would be interesting to investigate whether the same result can be shown for the CMDP 
procedure. Lastly, as blocked response-adaptive procedures are often preferred in clinical 
trials (Merrell et al., 2022), it would furthermore be interesting to incorporate blocked allo­
cation in the CMDP approach.
The current paper provides theoretical results and a method for finding an optimal policy 
for a class of finite-horizon CMDPs that is more general than usually considered in the 
literature, as a different expectation operator, corresponding to a different prior belief, can 
be used in each constraint. Such optimisation problems arise naturally in settings where 
multiple hypotheses are considered for the treatment effect, e.g., when considering Bayes­
ian average type I error rate and power constraints. It is possible to also apply Lemma 1 
to multi-objective Markov decision processes, where each objective is formulated under a 
different prior. In principle, Markov decision process response-adaptive procedures follow 
from a partially observable Markov decision framework, and hence it would be interesting 
to investigate whether there are other settings involving partial observability where the solu­
tion approach can be applied.
