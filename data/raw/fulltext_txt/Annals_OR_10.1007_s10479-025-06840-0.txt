Abstract
The article analyzes, which is novel in the literature, combined aimed and unaimed as 
reported by Lanchester (Aircraft in warfare: the dawn of the fourth arm, Tiptree Con­
stable and Co. Ltd., London, 1916) warfare with variable kill rates. Each group possesses 
nine kinds of military equipment, i.e. planes, helicopters, artillery, tanks, armored per­
sonnel carriers, guided missiles, manpowered handheld offensive weapons, submarines, 
and air defense systems. Each group’s artillery, tanks and armored personnel carriers are 
attacked by the opposing group’s artillery in unaimed as reported by Lanchester (Aircraft 
in warfare: the dawn of the fourth arm, Tiptree Constable and Co. Ltd., London, 1916) 
warfare, and also attacked by the opposing group’s other eight kinds of military equip­
ment in aimed as reported by Lanchester (Aircraft in warfare: the dawn of the fourth arm, 
Tiptree Constable and Co. Ltd., London, 1916) warfare. Simulations show how one 5% 
stronger group gradually eliminates the opposing group. If the first group lacks planes and 
helicopters, it loses most of its military equipment. It degrades especially the opposing 
group’s artillery, tanks, armored personnel carriers and submarines. It degrades partly the 
opposing group’s planes, helicopters, guided missiles. It eliminates the opposing group’s 
manpowered handheld offensive weapons. This article is useful for military analysts, bud­
get allocators, and others composing groups where multiple kinds of military equipment 
impact each other multifariously. Recommendations include prioritizing air equipment for 
dominance, with limitations noted for modern factors like drones and munitions.

1  Introduction
The article analyzes Lanchester (1916) warfare between two groups. Each group has nine 
kinds of military equipment, i.e. planes, helicopters, artillery, tanks, armored personnel 
carriers, guided missiles, manpowered handheld offensive weapons, submarines, and air 
defense systems. Each group’s artillery attacks the opposing group’s artillery, tanks, and 
armored personnel carriers in unaimed Lanchester (1916) warfare where each group’s loss 
rate is proportional to the product of the two groups’ strengths of their interacting military 
equipment. The other eight kinds of military equipment attacks the opposing group’s mili­
tary equipment in aimed Lanchester (1916) warfare where each group’s loss rate is propor­
tional to the opposing group’s strengths of its military equipment.
This article’s research question is to model warfare between two groups which both 
are equipped with the nine kinds of military equipment, and determine the implications of 
this modeling. Each kind of military equipment is designed to attack and defend against a 
subset of the opposing group’s military equipment. That causes warfare impact in multi­
farious directions which are scrutinized. Whereas Lanchester (1916) assumes constant kill 
rates, this article assumes variable kill rates where one group’s military equipment of a 
certain kind also gets degraded proportionally to the strength of its own military equipment 
of that kind relative to the strengths of its own military equipment of all kinds. The kill 
rates account for both the tempo of combat and how forcefully the combat proceeds. For 
example, the tempo of air combat is roughly an order of magnitude faster than surface com­
bat (the aircraft sweeping in and out over the battle). Furthermore, surface combat is itself 
an order of magnitude faster than submarine warfare (anti-submarine warfare commonly 
known as extremely slow warfare). The hypothesis tested is that variable kill rates, account­
ing for combat tempo and proportional degradation, yield non-linear outcomes where small 
initial asymmetries (e.g., 5%) amplify over time, leading to extinction of weaker forces. 
This hypothesis is supported by the simulations, which demonstrate how initial conditions 
drive qualitative shifts in warfare dynamics, such as air inferiority causing ground losses, 
generalizing beyond specific parameters.
Lanchester (1916) theory dominates the warfare literature. Wrigge et al. (1995) review 
and classify 730 references of Lanchester (1916) theory. Engel (1954) and Weiss (1966) 
illustrate how World War II impacts warfare research. Morse and Kimball (1951) introduce 
stochastic analysis, expanded by Parkhideh and Gafarian (1996), Hausken and Moxnes 
(2000, 2002, 2005), and others. Morse and Kimball (1951, p. 70), Ventisel (1964, pp. 183–
184) and Leffingwell (1987) criticize Lanchester (1916) theory e.g. for being unsatisfactory 
for small groups. Kress et al. (2018) generalize Lanchester (1916) warfare to three warring 
groups.
Taylor (1983) analyzes Lanchester (1916) warfare analytically and through Monte Carlo 
simulations. He criticizes the use of firepower scores1 which may fail to measure the accom­
plishment of unit missions, may ignore essential factors, and may relate inappropriately to 
the physical combat processes. Stockfish (1975, p. 90) argues that firepower scores may

The primary contribution is methodological: extending Lanchester (1916) theory to 
combined aimed and unaimed fire with variable kill rates across nine kinds of equipment, 
enabling simulation of multifarious interactions without assuming constant rates or simple 
aggregation. This addresses gaps in prior work (e.g., MacKay (2009)) by incorporating 
tempo differences (e.g., faster air combat) and providing a framework for testing hypotheses 
on equipment composition, though parameter values are illustrative rather than empirically 
estimated.
Analyses of aimed, unaimed and asymmetric Lanchester (1916) warfare with variable 
separable kill rates for heterogenous groups assuming random target allocation have been 
conducted by MacKay (2009). He shows the optimality of eliminating the opposing group’s 
various kinds of military equipment successively. Liu et al. (2012) provide related analyses, 
further developed by MacKay (2012). Kalloniatis et al. (2021) generalize to networked 
Lanchester (1916) warfare. They consider warfare between dispersed elements and how 
each group strikes a balance between maximizing the damage on the opposing group and 
minimizing one’s own damage, implying transitions between defeat, stalemate and victory.
The nine kinds of military equipment are assumed to be mutually exclusive, jointly 
exhaustive, and to cover the most relevant kinds of military equipment. The nine kinds 
may operate differently. For example, helicopters may be considered as facing off directly 
against other forces, while planes will likely sweep in and out, replaced with other planes 
sweeping in and out. Guided missiles are broadly defined to include ballistic missiles and 
anti-tank guided missiles, but not intercontinental ballistic missiles.2 Air defense systems 
are broadly defined to include antisatellite systems. Examples are Nike, Hawk, and L70. 
Planes, helicopters and guided missiles are assumed to attack the platforms and infrastruc­
tures associated with air defense systems. Manpowered handheld offensive weapons are 
assumed operated by individual humans, e.g. FIM-92 Stinger which can attack planes, 
and M72 which cannot attack planes. Submarines are included for exhaustiveness, with 
extremely low weight in the examples, to ensure that air, land, and sea are incorporated into 
the model.3

These nine kinds of military equipment are traditionally operated by humans, but can in 
future warfare increasingly be operated by robots and artificial intelligence. Combat armor 
and military manpower are excluded since they are assumed present in individual humans 
operating manpowered handheld offensive weapons. Armored vehicles and autonomous 
military vehicles are excluded since they are used mainly for transport. Biological and 
chemical weapons are excluded since they are of a different nature. They can be released 
by any of the nine kinds of military equipment analyzed in this article, and they can be 
defended against by some or most of the nine kinds of military equipment, and can also be 
defended against by other kinds of equipment not modeled in this article, e.g. by humans 
moving to underground bunkers or shelters.

2  The model
Consider two groups B and R at war. Group Z, Z = B, R, has nine kinds of military equip­
ment expressed with strength Zi (t) ≥0, i = P, H, A, T, C, G, M, S, D, at time t ≥0 
where P expresses planes, H expresses helicopters, A expresses artillery, T expresses 
tanks, C expresses armored personnel carriers, G expresses guided missiles, M expresses 
manpowered handheld offensive weapons, S expresses submarines, and D expresses air 
defense systems. This level of granularity—nine mutually exclusive kinds—strikes a bal­
ance between abstraction (e.g., lumped aimed/unaimed categories) and hyper-detail (e.g., 
sub-kinds of tanks), allowing tractable differential equations while capturing key interac­
tions like air-ground asymmetries. It avoids over-complexity that would obscure qualitative 
insights, such as in stylized two-kind models, and enables extension to real conflicts without 
simulating every variant (e.g., Russian tanks in Ukraine).
2.1  Aimed and unaimed Lanchester (1916) warfare
The model uses differential equations to describe the rate of strength loss for each group’s 
kinds of equipment, with notation Bi (t) and Rj (t) representing the strengths of group B’s 
and group R’s equipment of kind i or j at time t (e.g., P for planes, A for artillery). Equa­
tions (1), (2), (3), and (4) govern these dynamics: Eq. (1) models basic aimed Lanchester 
warfare with constant kill rates, applied in this model to handheld offensive weapons M 
where each group’s M is attacked only by the opposing group’s M due to their specific 
targeting (Sect. 2.2.7). Equation (2) extends this to multiple kinds with variable kill rates, 
(3) models unaimed artillery fire, and (4) combines aimed and unaimed fire against artillery, 
tanks, and armored personnel carriers. Kill rates (raji, rdji, baij, bdij) and target alloca­
tion parameters (ρdji, γdij) reflect combat effectiveness and prioritization, respectively, with 
subscripts indicating attack (a) or defense (d) roles.

2.2  Nine kinds of military warfare
2.2.1  Plane warfare
Equation (2) applies for plane warfare where group Z’s planes P are attacked by the oppos­
ing group’s military equipment k = P, H, G, M, S, D, i.e. all its nine kinds except artillery 
A, tanks T, and armored personnel carriers C which cannot be used to attack planes P, 
Z = B, R.
2.2.2  Helicopter warfare
Equation (2) analogously applies for helicopter warfare where group Z’s helicopters H are 
attacked by the opposing group’s military equipment k = P, H, G, M, S, D, i.e. all its nine 
kinds except artillery A, tanks T, and armored personnel carriers C which cannot be used 
to attack helicopters H, Z = B, R.
2.2.3  Artillery warfare
Equation (4) applies for artillery warfare where group Z’s artillery A are attacked by the 
opposing group’s artillery A and military equipment k = P, H, T, C, S; Z = B, R.
2.2.4  Tanks warfare
Equation (4) also applies for tanks warfare where group Z’s tanks T are attacked by the 
opposing group’s artillery A and military equipment k = P, H, T, C, G, M, S; Z = B, R.

4  Doctrinal recommendations
From a doctrinal perspective, the simulations recommend prioritizing investments in air 
superiority (planes and helicopters) for asymmetric advantages, as their absence leads 
to rapid degradation of ground equipment like artillery and tanks. In budget-constrained 
scenarios, allocating resources to guided missiles and air defense systems can mitigate air 
threats, while over-reliance on manpowered handheld weapons may only succeed against 
similarly limited opponents. Policymakers should thus balance portfolios toward high-
tempo military equipment (e.g., air units) to exploit variable kill rates and achieve decisive 
victories.

5  Limitations and future research
This model excludes modern elements like drones, electronic warfare, long-distance preci­
sion artillery, and munitions stockpile dynamics to maintain tractability and focus on gen­
eralizable interactions among nine core kinds of equipment. These elements, while critical 
in conflicts like Ukraine, introduce additional complexity (e.g., detection probabilities, 
stockpile depletion rates) that require separate state variables or stochastic modeling, which 
are proposed as future extensions. This scoping enables a clear methodological advance in 
combining aimed and unaimed Lanchester (1916) warfare with variable kill rates, providing 
a baseline for incorporating such factors.
The firepower scores used to derive kill rates (Table 1) are based on conventional esti­
mates from military operations research (e.g., 30 for planes/helicopters, 40 for artillery; 
Taylor (1983)), adjusted for relative effectiveness (e.g., divided by 10 for unaimed artillery, 
300 for air-to-submarine impacts). These scores are illustrative, not empirically validated, 
due to the complexity of real-world combat variables (e.g., terrain, training, technology). 
The baseline scenario (Fig. 1) establishes outcomes with group B’s 5% strength advantage, 
while sensitivity analyses (Figs. 2, 3 and 4) mitigate this by testing variations in initial 
strengths: Fig. 2 increases group B’s artillery by 20%, Fig. 3 removes group R’s planes and 
helicopters, and Fig. 4 triples group R’s air defenses under air inferiority. These variations 
demonstrate how outcomes shift, but future work could refine scores using historical data 
or field tests (e.g., via Training and Doctrine Command (TRADOC) simulations; Chapman 
(1991)).
Limitations include omitting explicit modeling of target acquisition (e.g., sensors and 
decoys), post-fire mobility (e.g., 'scoot' tactics after artillery reveals positions), communica­
tion trade-offs (e.g., electronic signals risking detection vs. dispersal benefits), force con­
centration challenges (e.g., satellite/drone vulnerabilities in massing tanks), and munitions 
stockpiles (e.g., depletion of expensive aimed rounds). These could be addressed in exten­
sions by adding state variables for detection probabilities or stockpile dynamics (Hausken &

1016

Zhuang, 2011; Levitin & Hausken, 2009, 2013), potentially shifting outcomes in precision-
era warfare away from World War I-inspired Lanchester assumptions.
Parameters, including kill rates derived from illustrative firepower scores, are not empiri­
cally validated here and may vary by context. Future work could compare this model to 
aggregated ones (e.g., 3–5 types) or field-test it against historical data (e.g., TRADOC 
simulations, Chapman (1991)) to assess the value of nine-type granularity. Kill rates could 
be calibrated using datasets from conflicts like Ukraine, such as open-source equipment 
loss records from Oryx (2025) or TRADOC running estimates. While not optimized like 
dynamic control models, the descriptive Eqs. (1), (2), (3), (4) provide a baseline for such 
extensions.
Future research may generalize to more than two groups, may split each of the nine 
kinds of military equipment into subkinds (e.g. different kinds of planes), and may include 
other kinds of warfare such as biological or chemical warfare. Modern elements from con­
flicts like Ukraine, such as drones for reconnaissance and strikes, electronic warfare against 
drones, long-distance precision artillery, and jamming of guided systems, could be incor­
porated as additional kinds of equipment or modifiers to kill rates (e.g., reducing aimed fire 
effectiveness via jamming). The costs of the various kinds of military equipment may be 
estimated and evaluated against military success for each kind of equipment. Reinforce­
ment of the various kinds of military equipment may be modeled. Stochastic analyses may 
be developed where the initial conditions, kill rates, reinforcement, weather, etc. are drawn 
probabilistically. The firepower scores may be verified empirically by assessing the kill 
rates, endurance, survivability, etc.of each kind of military equipment, possibly accounting 
for costs of purchase and maintenance of military equipment. The model may be supported 
empirically more broadly with real world warfare.
6  Conclusion
Warfare between two groups is analyzed. Each group possesses nine kinds of military equip­
ment intended to be mutually exclusive and jointly exhaustive, i.e. planes, helicopters, artil­
lery, tanks, armored personnel carriers, guided missiles, manpowered handheld offensive 
weapons, submarines, and air defense systems. The article distinguishes between unaimed 
artillery Lanchester (1916) warfare and aimed Lanchester (1916) warfare for the other eight 
kinds of military equipment. Aimed Lanchester (1916) warfare where each group possesses 
multiple kinds of military equipment accounts for variable kill rates. One group’s military 
equipment of a certain kind gets degraded proportionally to the opposing group’s kill rate 
and strength, but also proportionally to the strength of its own military equipment of that 
kind relative to the strengths of its own military equipment of all kinds. The article general­
izes, which is novel in the literature, to combined aimed and unaimed Lanchester (1916) 
warfare. Each group’s military equipment of three possible kinds, i.e. artillery, tanks and 
armored personnel carriers, is assumed to be attacked by the opposing group’s artillery in 
unaimed Lanchester (1916) warfare, and also attacked by the opposing group’s other eight 
kinds of military equipment in aimed Lanchester (1916) warfare.

This article’s focus on the interaction of nine kinds of military equipment for two groups 
is applicable for military analysts, policy makers, military budget allocaters, and others 
tasked to analyze or compose the relative strengths of the nine kinds of military equipment 
for one or two groups. Rather than estimating ad hoc, according to historical precedent, 
qualitative judgments, or expert advise, the simulation procedure may reveal indirect quan­
tifiable impacts of the various kinds of military equipment of one group on another group.
