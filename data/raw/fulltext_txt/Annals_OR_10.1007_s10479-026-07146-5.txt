Abstract
In this paper, we address a group decision-making context within multi-attribute utility/value
theory (MAUT/MAVT) under partial or incomplete information. The problem involves objec-
tives of markedly different natures and multiple decision-makers (DMs), each possessing
expertise in speciﬁc subsets of objectives. We propose a hierarchical elicitation process that
integrates multiple weighting methods. Within this framework, DMs contribute exclusively
to the elicitation of the objectives for which they hold expertise and may choose the weighting
method that best aligns with the type and amount of information they are able or willing to
provide. This design further reduces the cognitive burden on DMs, particularly given that each
elicitation step focuses on a limited set of objectives–typically of similar nature–within the
relevant level and branch of the objective hierarchy, rather than across the entire criteria set.
The proposed hierarchical weighting approach has been implemented in a web-based decision
support system (WEB-MAUT-DSS) and is illustrated through a real-world decision-making
problem: the restoration of aquatic ecosystems contaminated by radionuclides. Monte Carlo
simulation techniques are employed to assess its performance across a range of scenarios.

has been widely used to address complex real-world decision-making problems. An additive
multi-attribute utility model is used to evaluate the alternatives under consideration since it
is considered to be a valid approach in most real decision-making problems (von Winterfeldt
& Edwards, 1973; Raiffa, 1982; Stewart, 1996), where decision-makers (DMs) have to elicit
weights representing trade offs between the objectives under consideration.
In this context, different weighting methods are available in the literature. These methods
can be categorized as subjective and objective approaches (Tzeng et al., 1998; Da Silva et al.,
2021). Objective approaches determine the criteria weights on the basis of the information
contained in a decision-making matrix by means of mathematical models, neglecting the
DM’s opinion, whereas subjective approaches incorporate the DM’s opinion and intuition
into the elicitation process. Most of the weighting methods in the literature account for
the DM’s cognitive preferences. In this paper, we will also consider subjective weighting
methods.
Subjective weighting methods vary in terms of the information that the DM is asked to
provide throughout the elicitation process. It is often not easy to elicit precise weights, since
the DM may ﬁnd it difﬁcult to provide the requested information (some weighting methods
are too cognitively demanding, and the number of questions that the DM is required to answer
may increase when there are a lot of criteria under consideration).
On top of this, it may be impossible to exactly predict the performance, possibly derived
from statistical methods, of the alternatives under consideration. Moreover, the DM may
provide imprecise information when assessing their preferences–that is, in the evaluation
of component utilities representing the DM’s preferences regarding the possible attribute
values, and in the elicitation of weights that reﬂect objective trade-offs. This situation, is
usually referred to as decision-making with imprecise information, incomplete or partial
information (Rios-Insua & French, 1991), which has been widely addressed in the literature
focusing mainly on the weight elicitation process (Riabacke & Danielson, 2012; Aguayo et
al., 2014; Sarabando & Dias, 2009; ¸Sahin, 2021; Lakmayer & Danielson, 2023a) as well as
in the context of group decision-making (Dias & Climaco, 2005; Frej et al., 2019).
In this context, uncertainty about the alternative performances, as well as imprecision
concerning the quantiﬁcation of the DM’s preferences are incorporated to the decision-
making process. Accordingly, families of component utility functions or utility intervals for
discrete attribute values are derived from the component utility assessment, while weight
intervals are obtained from the weight elicitation process.
Besides, ordinal information could be incorporated into the quantiﬁcation of the DM’s
preferences, i.e., rankings of alternatives arranged in descending order of performance from
the best to the worst could be provided for some criteria (Sarabando & Dias, 2010), together
with a ranking of importance of criteria/attributes, arranged in descending order of importance
from the best to the worst, respectively.
Many weighting methods are available in the literature using ordinal information (Chergui
& Jiménez-Martín, 2024), and some of them account for additional information about the
strength of the differences between the ranked criteria in different forms, including a ranking
of differences, a semantic scale, precise/imprecise cardinal information, ratio-based methods,
and preference statements. However, most of the weighting methods are directly applied on
the whole criteria set. This could be drawback for the following reasons.
In general, the amount of information requested in weighting methods is sensitive to the
number of criteria, which makes these methods more demanding and stressful for the DM.
For instance, in weighting methods in which the DM is required to answer probability-based
questions–such as in the tradeoff-based weighting method (Jiménez et al., 2003)–the number
and complexity of these questions increase with the number of criteria, as further explained

some cases, be a complex task for the DM when the number of criteria is large.
Inthispaper,wefocusoncomplexgroupdecision-makingproblemswithinMAUT/MAVT
with partial information that involve multidisciplinary impacts/criteria (social, economic,
environmental...) that is, different types of very specialized criteria (Jiménez et al., 2003,
2007; Jiménez-Martín et al., 2016; Araújo et al., 2022). In these cases, there will not be a
single DM who is an expert in all disciplines. Instead, we will have a group of DMs, each
of whom is an expert/specialist in a particular discipline. Then, each DM should take part
in the weight elicitation for the criteria in which he/she specializes rather than for the whole
criteria set.
This is possible if weights are elicited hierarchically, that is, trade offs between the objec-
tives under consideration are computed at the different levels and branches of the objective
hierarchy with the intervention of the DM that has expertise in the objectives under consid-
eration. Then, the attribute weights used in the additive model to evaluate the alternatives
would be computed by multiplying the local weights in the paths from the corresponding
lowest-level objective (in which the attribute is established) to the overall objective.
Moreover, different weighting methods could be available for the DMs, which they would
decide to use depending the information that they are asked and are willing/able to provide,
i.e., some DMs could provide ordinal information, whereas others could provide weight
intervals directly, or use other methods (trade-offs,...).
This proposed hierarchical elicitation process has been implemented in WEB-MAUT-
DSS (https://vps155.cesvima.upm.es/web-maut-dss/), an improved web-based version of
the generic multi-attribute analysis (GMAA) system currently under development (Jiménez
et al., 2003; Jimnez et al., 2006). WEB-MAUT-DSS is a decision support system based on
the DA methodology with an additive multi-attribute utility model in which weights are
hierarchically elicited using different weighting methods.
A direct assignment and a method based on trade-offs are available in WEB-MAUT-
DSS. In both cases, it outputs weight intervals, since the DM is allowed to provide imprecise
information. However, the use of the method based on trade-offs is only appropriate for weight
elicitation at lower levels of the objective hierarchy since the complexity of the probability
questions that the DM is required to answer increases in proportion to the number of attributes
involved.
As an alternative, WEB-MAUT-DSS incorporates new weighting methods based on ordi-
nal information in addition to direct assignment. To do this, a review of weighting methods
based on ordinal information and comparative analyses on their performance in the litera-
ture was performed in (Chergui & Jiménez-Martín, 2024) to identify the most appropriate
weighting methods for incorporation into the WEB-MAUT-DSS. Note that it includes not
only weighting methods that consider ordinal information on weights (Danielson, 2014),
but also weighting methods accounting for additional information about the strength of the
differences between the ranked criteria in different forms, including a ranking of differ-
ences (Aguayo et al., 2014), a semantic scale (Danielson & Ekenberg, 2017), and cardinal
information (Riabacke et al., 2009; Danielson et al., 2014; Larsson et al., 2015).
We illustrate the hierarchical approach combining weighting methods within a real multi-
disciplinary decision-making context that we addressed in several European projects in which
we took part: the restoration of aquatic ecosystems contaminated by radionuclides based on
a multicriteria perspective. Different universities participated in these projects, where the
corresponding research group was composed of specialists in different disciplines including
environmental modelling, social impacts, radiological issues, and economic impacts.

Besides, it is clear that the hierarchical combination of weighting methods is perfectly
suited to in a multi-disciplinary group decision-making context and facilitates the weight
elicitation process for DMs. This, however, raises the question of what effect the use of a
hierarchical versus a non-hierarchical elicitation process may have on the results (ranking of
alternatives) for decision making generally and not only multi-disciplinary group decision
making. We perform a comparison analysis based on Monte Carlo simulation techniques to
analyse the performance of weighting methods based on ordinal information applied both
hierarchically and directly on the whole criteria set.
The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 describes weighting methods available in
WEB-MAUT-DSS, including a direct assignment and methods based on trade-offs and ordi-

provided in Section 5.
2 The hierarchical weighting procedure in the WEB-MAUT-DSS system
In WEB-MAUT-DSS, a direct assignment or a method based on trade-offs to elicit weights
can be used at any level or branch of the objective hierarchy, as in the GMAA system. In
both cases, this results in weight intervals.
For direct assignment, the DM is asked to provide weight intervals for the objectives under
consideration. Then, a normalization process based on the DM’s responses is performed
automatically, leading to the corresponding average normalized weights and normalized
weight intervals.
Inthemethodbasedontrade-offs(Jiménezetal.,2003),theDMisgivenseveralprobability
questions and is asked to provide a probability interval such that he/she does not have a clear
preference regarding a gamble and sure performances. The number of probability questions
matches up with the number of objectives whose weights are being elicited and the dimension
of the gamble depends on the number of attributes stemming from these objectives. Thus, the
DM will face more complex gambles when the weight elicitation corresponds to objectives
at the higher levels of the hierarchy (more attributes would be involved).
As an alternative, new weighting methods based on ordinal information have been incor-
porated into WEB-MAUT-DSS. Moreover, weighting methods accounting for additional
information about the strength of the differences between the ranked criteria have been also
added to the system, including a ranking of differences, the use of a semantic scale, and
cardinal information.
Different weighting methods based on ordinal information can be found in the literature,
mainly surrogate weighting methods and methods based on the notions of pairwise and
absolute dominance.
In surrogate weighting methods (SWMs), a weight vector is selected from a set of admis-
sible weights to represent the set, which is then used to evaluate the alternatives on the basis
of MAVT/MAUT.
SWMs are reviewed in Chergui and Jiménez-Martín (2024), including their associated
mathematical expressions and a comparison analysis performed in the literature to ana-
lyze their performance. The conclusion is that it is not possible to identify the best SWM.

If additional information on the ranking of criteria is available by means of a ranking
of differences, then either the minimizing squared deviations from extreme points (MSD)
method (Ahn, 2017), the modiﬁcation of classical decision rules or dominance measuring

stemming from the objectives under consideration precisely to mitigate this negative effect.
3 Restoring an aquatic ecosystem contaminated by radionuclides
In this section, the hierarchical weighting method is illustrated using WEB-MAUT-DSS
in a complex multidisciplinary decision-making problem: the restoration of upper Heimdal
lake (Øvre Heimdalsvatn), located in Oppland county (Norway), contaminated by radionu-
clides. This problem was previously analyzed using the GMAA system (Jiménez et al., 2003;
Mateos et al., 2006) but without using ordinal information (and the possibility of additional
information) in the weight elicitation.
Although the lake was contaminated with a fallout of 130kBq/m2 of 137Cs after the
Chernobyl accident, which required no countermeasures, some restoration strategies were
analyzed with the objective of testing the GMAA system.

ﬁelds took part in the restoration of the lake ecosystems contaminated by radionuclides in
the context of several EU-framework projects (Appelgren et al., 1996; Monte et al., 1999),

In the above section, the proposed hierarchical weighting method is illustrated using WEB-
MAUT-DSS for the restoration of upper Heimdal lake, contaminated by radionuclides. The
conclusion reached for this example is that the results are practically unchanged when ordinal
information is incorporated to the hierarchical weight elicitation process.
The question now arises of whether the results would be affected if weighting methods
based on ordinal information were applied non-hierarchically and, if so, to what extent.

4.1 Simulation design
In the simulation process, we consider several scenarios accounting for different numbers
of alternatives m and objective hierarchies. Two parameters are used to deﬁne objective
hierarchies, the number of levels l and the maximum number of branches per node b. A high
number of instances are generated for each speciﬁc scenario.
Three alternative probability distributions – a uniform, normal and exponential distribution
– can be used to randomly generate weights, representing different situations. In the uniform
distribution, the magnitude of the values of the generated weight vectors may be similar or
different, whereas weights of similar magnitude will be obtained when the normal distribution
is used and very different weights in terms of magnitude are generated from the exponential
distribution. We could consider the uniform distribution as a general case and the normal and
exponential distributions as particular cases.
The constraint that the weights sum to 1 is incorporated into the simulation process, which
has been extensively discussed in the literature. As demonstrated in Danielson and Ekenberg
(2017), the effectiveness and reliability of surrogate weighting methods, such as the rank
order centroid (ROC) method, depend heavily on this constraint. Ignoring the sum-to-one
requirement can lead to distorted rankings and biased decision outcomes. This issue has
also been highlighted by (Kunsch & Ishizaka, 2019), which emphasizes emphasizes that
neglecting the summation constraint can lead to misleading or biased results in multi-criteria
decision analysis. Moreover, when the weights are required to sum to unity, a careful proce-
dure for generating them is essential. Simple normalization of randomly generated weights,
for instance, introduces a bias toward producing more similar weights, potentially distorting
the intended variability in the decision-making process. The impact of such normalization
and the associated bias has been discussed in classical sensitivity analysis literature, includ-
ing (Butler, 1994), and has been extensively studied in the context of stochastic multicriteria
acceptability analysis (SMAA) by Tervonen and colleagues (Tervonen & Figueira, 2008;
Tervonen et al., 2013), where the generation and evaluation of weights are carefully designed
to respect the summation constraint while preserving appropriate diversity among criteria.
The default parameter values in the R package are m ∈{5, 10, 15, 25}, l ∈{3, 4} and
b ∈{2, 3, 4, 5}, using the uniform probability distribution and 10,000 random instances
generated per scenario. Note that l = 1 corresponds to the overall objective, whereas l = 2
represents a ﬂat hierarchy.
Two quality measures are considered to analyze the performance of the methods under
comparison: the hit ratio and Kendall’s τ. The hit ratio represents the proportion of the 10,000

r j, j = 1, ..., n. Weights are then normalized, leading to the TRUE attribute weight vector,
wTRUE. For this, in the case of the uniform distribution, we select n −1 independent random
numbers from a uniform distribution on (0, 1), and rank these numbers. Suppose the ranked
numbers are 1 ≥rn−1 ≥... ≥r2 ≥r1 > 0. The differences between consecutive ranked
numbers are then used as the target weights wn = rn−1, wn−1 = rn−2 −rn−1, ..., w1 = r1.
The resulting weights will sum 1 and be uniformly distributed (Jia et al., 1998). The SUM1
normalization (Alfares & Duffuaa, 2016) is applied when considering normal and exponential
distributions.
A ﬁltering process could be applied in order to discard all unnatural weight vector (vectors
with very high/low values) when uniform and normal distributions are used.
No ﬁlter process is used by default in the simulation package. Alternatively, a threshold
value can be speciﬁed, meaning that if the distance between the largest and the second largest
generated weights exceeds this threshold, then the weight vector is resampled. The default
threshold value is 0.2, as originally proposed in Lakmayer and Danielson (2023a), and this
parameter can be modiﬁed if the user deems it appropriate. Finally, the weak and strong
ﬁlters proposed in Danielson and Ekenberg (2016) could be used, in which the following
conditions must be satisﬁed
(weak ﬁlter) 0.05/n ≤wi ≤0.7 + 0.3/n,

where n is the number of criteria under consideration.
Next, the additive model is used to evaluate the alternatives under consideration on the
basis of U′ and wTRUE to derive a ranking of the alternatives, the TRUE ranking.
Secondly (in green), ordinal information is obtained from the TRUE attribute weight
vector, and the sum reciprocal (SR) method is used to compute the surrogate weights, wSR.
Then, the additive model is used again to derive a ranking of the alternatives: the SR ranking.
Note that the SR ranking corresponds to a non-hierarchical weighting process based on
ordinal information.
Next (in red), TRUE weights are used to compute the weights at the different levels and
branches of the hierarchy. To do this, we start at the lowest level of the hierarchy and moves
upwards assigning as node weights the sum of the weights of their children. Then, starting
at the highest level (the overall objective), we work our way down, transforming the weights
of the children of a node into ordinal information and applying the SR method to obtain the
local surrogate weights. The attribute weights, wHSR, are then obtained by multiplying the
local surrogate weights along the path from that attribute to the overall objective. Then, the
additive model is used to derive the HSR ranking. Thus, the HSR ranking corresponds to a
hierarchical weighting process based on ordinal information.
Finally (in blue), the TRUE ranking is compared against the SR and the HSR rankings
across the 10,000 instances executed for the scenario under consideration to analyze their
performance based on the hit ratio and Kendall’s τ measures.

trade-offs and ordinal information (with the option of providing additional information about
the strength of the differences between the ranked criteria by means of cardinal information,
a ranking of differences and the use of a semantic scale).
The hierarchical weight elicitation process has been implemented in WEB-MAUT-DSS,

Thus, we recommend the proposed hierarchical weight elicitation process since it clearly
makes weight elicitation easier for DMs, since a smaller number of objectives are considered
at each leaf and level of the hierarchy, and the DMs can select the weighting method according
to the type and amount of information they are willing or able to provide. This also makes
the system particularly suitable for the speciﬁc group decision-making context addressed in
the paper.
Finally, it is important to note that although hierarchical weight elicitation has been intro-
duced in this paper within a speciﬁc group decision-making context, it can also be applied by
a single decision-maker in other contexts, taking advantage of the various weighting methods
provided by the system.
Although MAUT/MAVT provides a normative framework for evaluating alternatives
across multiple criteria and assumes that preferences are structurally invariant: the orga-
nization of attributes, whether arranged in a hierarchy with multiple levels or presented in a
ﬂat list, should not affect the resulting judgments or rankings (Keeney & Raiffa, 1982; von
123

Annals of Operations Research
Winterfeldt & Edwards, 1986), a large body of behavioral decision research demonstrates
that real DMs deviate substantially from this assumption.
The most inﬂuential ﬁnding is split bias, the tendency to assign greater importance to
an attribute when it is subdivided into multiple sub-attributes without substantive meaning
(Weber et al., 1988). This effect has been consistently conﬁrmed across weighting methods,
decision contexts, and populations (Fischer, 1995; Pöyhönen & Hämäläinen, 2001). Sev-
eral cognitive mechanisms have been proposed to explain split bias (Lichtenstein & Slovic,
2006; Salo, 1997), which jointly explain why split bias is large, stable, and pervasive across
applications.
Research on hierarchical structures in MAUT shows systematic biases: DMs fail to cor-
rectly integrate local and branch-level weights into coherent global ratings, often using
additive or heuristic rather than multiplicative aggregation (Fischer, 1995; von Winterfeldt &
Edwards,1986).Hierarchicaldepthalsodistortsattention,leadingtopersistentoverweighting
of lowest-level objectives and under-weighting of intermediate or upper-level nodes (Fischer,
1995; Pöyhönen & Hämäläinen, 2001).
Research on ﬂat attribute structures generally shows higher consistency in weight elicita-
tion, lower cognitive load, and reduced susceptibility to structural distortions. However, when
many attributes are included, ﬂat structures face issues of non-attendance and information
overload (Louviere et al., 2000). Thus, while they mitigate some hierarchical biases, they
introduce new challenges, leaving a persistent trade-off between conceptual clarity (hierar-
chy) and cognitive tractability (ﬂat structures).
Behavioral researchers and MCDA experts recommend avoiding unnecessary attribute
subdivision, as this produces predictable distortions in weights.
As future research lines, we propose to use the ordinal information provided in the weight
elicitation process for the computation of non-dominated and potentially optimal alternatives
in WEB-MAUT-DSS and the incorporation of additional weighting methods that require
different information from the DM than the methods already available in WEB-MAUT-DSS.
