Abstract
Ensemble learning algorithms show good forecasting performances for ﬁnancial distress in
many studies. Despite considering the feature selection and feature importance procedures,
most overlook imbalanced data handling. This study proposes the Easyensemble method
based on undersampling and combines it with ensemble learning models to predict ﬁnancial
distress. The results show that Easyensemble sampling presents better forecasting perfor-
mance than SMOTE sampling. We subsequently conduct Permutation Importance (PIMP),
Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE), and partial dependence plots, and the experimental
results show that the feature selection procedure can effectively reduce the number of indi-
cators without affecting the prediction accuracy, improve the prediction efﬁciency as well as
save processing time. In addition, the indicators from proﬁtability, cash ﬂow, solvency, and
structural ratios are essential in predicting ﬁnancial distress.
Keywords Financial distress · Easyensemble sampling · Ensemble learning · SMOTE
1 Introduction
The prediction of ﬁnancial distress is to distinguish ﬁrms at risk of ﬁnancial distress, which
can measure the ﬁrms’ solvency (Qian et al., 2022). Even ﬁrms with impeccable operating
mechanisms may fail ﬁnancially and collapse due to the unstable economic environment and
ﬁerce competition. Therefore, the development of the ﬁrm, the interests of many investors,
and the structure of the capital market are related to the possibility of effectively and quickly
anticipating the ﬁnancial distress of listed ﬁrms (Sun & Li, 2008).

Early research on predicting ﬁnancial distress included statistical approaches including
univariate analysis, multiple discriminant analysis (MDA), Logistic regression (LR), and
others (Altman et al., 1994; Qian et al., 2022; Sun & Li, 2008). While these methods use
historical samples to create diagnostic models, they do not learn dynamically by induction
from new data which greatly affects the accuracy of predictions (Sun & Li, 2008). In practice,
these statistical approaches frequently break some assumptions. The assumption of multi-
variate normality of independent variables, for example, restricts the predictive accuracy of
the models (Begley et al., 1996). Machine learning has begun to arise and grow fast in recent
years, with the advent of information technology, amid the awkward backdrop of excessive
data and weak understanding. Machine learning, unlike traditional statistical approaches, can
automatically extract knowledge from training samples and does not assume a certain data
distribution. For ﬁnancial distress prediction, ANN (Elhoseny et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024;
Odom & Sharda, 1990), SVM (Ding et al., 2008; Shin et al., 2005; Van Gestel et al., 2006),
and ensemble learning approaches including random forests (RF) (Barboza et al., 2017), and
gradient boosting decision trees (GBDT) (Wyrovek & Kluza, 2018), Bagging and Adaptive
Boosting(AdaBoost) (Alfaro et al., 2008; Qian et al., 2022), Extreme Gradient Boosting
(XGBoost) (Son et al., 2019), are very popular and achieve good prediction performances.
Existing research on ﬁnancial distress forecasting has signiﬁcant limitations, particularly
in handling imbalanced data. Primarily, the data used for prediction are imbalanced, with
far fewer distressed ﬁrms compared to normal ones. Many studies focus solely on machine
learning methods, neglecting the issue of imbalanced data. Kotsiantis et al. (2006) demon-
strate that preprocessing imbalanced data enhances algorithm performance and improves
prediction accuracy. Sun et al. (2014) and Sun et al. (2020) combine the synthetic minor-
ity over-sampling technique (SMOTE) with the Bagging ensemble learning algorithm for
ﬁnancial distress prediction, achieving strong results. However, while SMOTE is a popular
oversampling method, the study does not conﬁrm its suitability for ﬁnancial distress forecast-
ing. Additionally, the large number of features in ﬁnancial distress prediction includes many
that are irrelevant, making feature selection a critical step in data preprocessing. Although
Qian et al. (2022) enhance feature selection methods for ﬁnancial distress forecasting and
integrate them with common machine learning techniques, their study overlooks the issue
of imbalanced data. Additionally, existing methods for predicting ﬁnancial distress are gen-
erally limited to conventional classiﬁcation methods, such as LR (Ohlson, 1980). With the
development of big data, the role of neural networks and ensemble learning in predicting
ﬁnancial distress cannot be overlooked. Especially with data that has undergone imbalanced
sampling, the comparison of these models’ predictive performance is worth in-depth inves-
tigation.
Our study enhances existing ﬁnancial distress prediction methods by addressing the
aforementioned limitations and focusing on Chinese-listed ﬁrms. First, we preprocess the
imbalanced data using the SMOTE oversampling method and the Easyensemble undersam-
pling method. Next, we apply XGBoost, AdaBoost, GBDT, RF, SVM, LR, and DNN to
the balanced data to predict the ﬁnancial condition of these ﬁrms. Finally, we conduct
feature selection using Permutation Importance (PIMP) and Recursive Feature Elimina-
tion (RFE), alongside feature importance and partial dependence analysis for the ﬁnancial
indicators. This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the literature review of
ﬁnancial distress prediction. Section 3 provides the introduction of benchmark models,
the methods for feature selection, feature importance analysis, and evaluation indicators.
Section 4 shows the data and experimental procedure. Section 5 shows the results of our
experiments and the comparison of different models. Section 6 shows the conclusion of this
study.

2 Literature review
2.1 Forecasting financial distress
Statistical methods are used in early ﬁnancial distress forecasting. Speciﬁcally, the single
variable discriminant model is proposed by Beaver (1966) and the multivariate discriminant
analysis (MDA) model is presented by Altman (1968), while Ohlson (1980) presents the
logistic regression (LR) model, as the popular statistical methods in forecasting ﬁnancial
distress. Although statistical models have beneﬁts like a more straightforward structure,
fewer parameters, and shorter training times, they are constrained by statistical hypotheses
like the normal distribution, equal covariance matrices for independent variables, and the
absence of multi-collinearity among independent variables (Sun et al., 2020).
Many studies have some progress in combining machine learning methods with the pre-
diction of ﬁnancial distress. In contrast to conventional statistical methods, machine learning
can automatically extract knowledge from training samples and does not presume a certain
data distribution. In particular, ANN, SVM, and ensemble learning methods are heavily used
for ﬁnancial distress prediction.
Frydman et al. (1985) ﬁrst present the Recursive Partitioning Algorithm (RPA), which
belongs to the decision tree using a computerized, nonparametric technique for ﬁnancial
analysis. Sung et al. (1999) invented the technique for editing the decision trees, produc-
ing an interpretable output using the genetic algorithm, discriminant analysis, and neural
networks. Zhou et al. (2017) combine decision trees with the feature selection method to
improve the performance of forecasting ﬁnancial conditions. Odom and Sharda (1990) ﬁrst
apply the ANN, as well as MDA as a comparison, to a variety of bankrupt and solvent
US ﬁrms using the ﬁnancial measures as inputs to the neural networks, which shows the
ANN achieves better results than MDA in classiﬁcation accuracy. Jan (2021) and Elhoseny
et al. (2022) show that ANN achieves good results in the ﬁeld of ﬁnance, especially in
forecasting ﬁnancial distress. The Support vector machine (SVM) is also widely discussed
by scholars in predicting ﬁnancial distress, which is proposed by Vapnik (1999). Tay and
Cao (2001) introduce SVM to ﬁnancial time-series forecasting ﬁrst and then Van Gestel
et al. (2006) and Shin et al. (2005) present that SVM performs better than other models
in bankruptcy forecasting. Ding et al. (2008) show that the SVM model outperforms tra-
ditional statistical methods and back propagation neural networks. A robust model with
high prediction accuracy is provided for predicting the ﬁnancial distress of Chinese listed
ﬁrms.
Recently, ensemble learning algorithms emerged as the popular method for generating
forecasting ﬁnancial distress models. Barboza et al. (2017) show ensemble algorithms such
as bagging, boosting, and RF outperform other traditional machine learning methods (MDA,
LR, SVM) with higher accuracy for predicting bankruptcy, default events, and credit risk
management in North American ﬁrms from 1985 to 2013. Mselmi et al. (2017) combine
partial least squares (PLS) with SVM to propose the PLS-SVM to forecast ﬁnancial distress.
Their study uses PLS’s capacity for handling multi-collinearity among predictors and SVM’s
capacity for capturing latent variable nonlinear relationships and shows a higher prediction
accuracy than traditional methods such as PLS, LR, ANN, and SVM. Sun et al. (2014) inte-
grate the synthetic minority over-sampling technique (SMOTE) with the Bagging ensemble
learning algorithm, and SVM is used as the basic classiﬁer (SMOTE-Bagging-SVM) which
outperforms other traditional methods for imbalanced ﬁnancial distress prediction. Given the

3.1.2 Ensemble learning
Dasarathy and Sheela (1979) ﬁrst introduce the idea of ensemble learning, which has been
able to successfully overcome various limitations of the single machine learning model in
recent years. Ensemble learning can be mainly divided into Boosting and Bootstrap Aggre-
gation (Bagging) (Breiman, 1996; Freund & Schapire, 1997; Hansen & Salamon, 1990). The
Boosting algorithm is an ensemble strategy that converts a weak learner into a strong learner,
which can signiﬁcantly improve prediction accuracy. In this case, a weak learner tends to
perform only slightly better than a random guess, for example, the classiﬁcation accuracy
is slightly more than 50% (Hastie et al., 2009; Meir & Rätsch, 2003; Schapire, 2003). The
bagging algorithm is one of the earliest integrated learning algorithms. It is simple in struc-
ture but superior in performance. It generates new training subsets by randomly changing the
distribution of the training set, then trains individual learners with different training subsets
separately, and ﬁnally sets them into a whole (Breiman, 1996). In our study, random forest
(RF) from Bagging and Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost), Gradient Boosting Decision Tree
(GBDT), and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) from Boosting are used to conduct
experiments.

erated, each of which can be used to build a decision tree model. Decision trees divide the
feature space into smaller and smaller non-overlapping sections, which can better capture
the nonlinear mapping connection between the response and the feature. A tree diagram

3.2 Sampling algorithms for imbalanced data
An imbalanced data set refers to a data set in which the number of samples in each class
differs signiﬁcantly. In the case of a binary classiﬁcation problem, for example, if the number
of samples in the positive category is much larger than the number of samples in the negative
category, the data, in this case, is called imbalanced data. According to Sun et al. (2020),
the samples of ﬁnancial distress forecasting is the imbalance class, in which the number of
samples in the ﬁnancial normal (majority class) is much larger than the ﬁnancial distress
samples (minority class). Imbalanced data is usually handled in two ways: oversampling and
undersampling. Oversampling is a random sampling from a few classes of samples to add
new samples while undersampling is a random selection of a small number of samples from
the majority class samples, and then merging the original minority class samples as the new
training data set.
3.2.1 SMOTE (oversampling)
In the oversampling method, we take the synthetic minority oversampling technique
(SMOTE) method for sampling. The principle of the SMOTE is to analyze and simulate
the samples of a few categories and add artiﬁcially generated new samples to the data set
based on the K-nearest neighbor algorithm so that the categories in the original data are no
longer seriously imbalanced (Chawla et al., 2002). SMOTE works by selecting samples that
are close to each other in the feature space, drawing a line between the samples in the feature
space, and then selecting a new point along this line as a sample. Fernandez et al. (2018) show
SMOTE makes progress in combining with machine learning methods to tackle imbalanced
data.
3.2.2 Easyensemble (undersampling)
Liu et al. (2008) propose the Easyensemble algorithm based on the principle of undersam-
pling, which integrates Bagging and AdaBoost methods. The principle is that each sample
is ﬁrst sampled using the Bagging sampling method for the majority class (the class with
a larger number of samples) so that the number of samples is equal to the number of sam-
ples from the minority class. Subsequently, the sample set obtained from the majority class
sampling is combined with all the samples from the minority class sample set for AdaBoost
model training. Finally, all the AdaBoost models derived from each training are ensembled.

3.3 Bayesian hyperparameter optimization
Probabilistic models have become a mainstream approach in the ﬁeld of artiﬁcial intelligence
and machine learning (Ghahramani, 2015). Ghahramani (2015) points out that Bayesian opti-
mization as a probabilistic model is one of the most advanced and promising techniques in
the ﬁeld of probabilistic machine learning and artiﬁcial intelligence. Bayesian optimization
is a very effective global optimization algorithm to ﬁnd the global optimal solution. Bayesian
optimization effectively solves the classical machine-intelligence problem in sequential deci-
sion theory: ﬁnding the next evaluation position based on the information obtained for an
unknown objective function f , and thus reaching the optimal solution as fast as possible (Jones
et al., 1998). To make the model training results more accurate, Bayesian optimization is used
in this study to obtain the hyperparameters.
3.4 Permutation importance (PIMP)
In recent years, effective estimators of feature importance for complicated or non-parametric
models such as tree-based models with Gini importance (GI) have been developed. Tree-
based models with GI such as RF are biased in favor of features with a large number of
categories. Altmann et al. (2010) propose a corrected feature importance measure: Permuta-
tion Importance (PIMP) to correct the feature importance bias, which is based on repeated
permutations of the result vector used to estimate the distribution of assessed importance
for each variable in a non-informative setting. PIMP can directly output the importance of
features in the training of the model, and calculate the impact on the overall prediction effect.
PIMP is based on a “permutation test” to detect the importance of features, which can be
used only after the model is completed. More speciﬁcally, PIMP changes the arrangement

3.5 Recursive feature elimination (RFE)
RFE is one of the most commonly used algorithms for feature selection (Guyon et al., 2002).
It works by searching a subset of features starting from all features in the training dataset and
successfully removing features until the desired number is retained. It is achieved by ﬁtting
the given algorithmic models and then ranking the features by importance so that discarding
the least important features and reﬁtting the model. The selection process is repeated until a
speciﬁc number of features are retained and the speciﬁc steps are shown below:
Algorithm RFE (Step1–5):
S1:ImportthetrainingsetincludingalltheinputfeaturesX0 andoutputlabelsY0.Initialize
a subset of features S = [1, 2, …,m] and the rankings of features R = [].
S2: Train a base model based on the training set and calculate the prediction accuracy.
S3: Calculate the feature importance and rank them. Obtain the least important feature f
and exclude it from the feature subset S = S—S(f). Update the ranking of features R = [S(f),
R].
S4: Repeat steps 2 and 3 until the feature subsetS is empty.
S5: Output the ranking of features R.

ﬁrms usually take special treatment (marked as ST ﬁrms) due to abnormal ﬁnancial conditions
as an indication that a ﬁrm is in ﬁnancial distress (Chen et al., 2006). In this study, ﬁrms
marked as ST are considered as ﬁrms in ﬁnancial distress, and ﬁrms not labeled as ST are
considered as ﬁrms in ﬁnancial normalcy. According to the relevant system of disclosure of
annual reports of listed ﬁrms in China, the disclosure time of annual reports of listed ﬁrms
is within four months from the end of each ﬁscal year, so the disclosure time of the ﬁrm
being ST in year T and the time of releasing the annual report of T-1 is almost in the same
time.1 Since ST ﬁrms are subject to special labeled in year T based on the ﬁnancial reports
published in year T-1. More speciﬁcally, the ﬁnancial report in year T-1 itself determines
whether the ﬁrm is labeled as ST or not, and there is a certain causal relationship between the
two. Therefore, there is no practical sense to construct a ﬁnancial distress prediction model
using the data in year T-1. Therefore, this study uses T-2, T-3, and T-4 data for the selection
of ﬁnancial distress prediction time which is shown in Fig. 1.
This study collects all listed ﬁrms in China from 2008 to 2021 on the CSMAR database.
China implemented a new accounting standard in 2007, so our sample data starts from 2008
to make the experimental results more reliable. Excluding the samples with incomplete data,
the remaining sample size in T-2, T-3, and T-4 is shown in Table 2. The data for T-4, T-3, and
T-2 starts from 2008, respectively, and the data for T ends in 2021.

4.3 Experimental procedure
As shown in Fig. 2, our research process mainly includes a. comparison of results before
and after processing of imbalanced data; b. comparison of results before and after feature
selection; contribution analysis of 80 features. The benchmark models involve XGBoost,
AdaBoost, GBDT, RF, SVM, LR, and DNN. Finally, 8 evaluation metrics commonly used
in classiﬁcation problems namely ACC, Precision, F1, Recall, Speciﬁcity, AUC, H-measure,
and KS, are conducted to evaluate the models with the best prediction performance. This
research ﬁrst collected historical ﬁnancial-related indicators data to form the original data
set then they will be divided into the training set and test set according to 9:1, which are used
for subsequent model training and testing, respectively. Our model can be more useful in
practice as it is trained on the training set’s data and then uses the test set’s error to determine
the ﬁnal model’s generalization error in a practical setting.

5.2.2 Easyensemble
Liu et al. (2008) present the Easyensemble algorithm, which is based on undersampling and
incorporates the Bagging and AdaBoost approaches. Easyensemble sampling is to use the
Bagging sampling method to sample classes with a large number of samples (ﬁnancially
normal ﬁrms) so that the number of samples is equal to the number of samples from a few
classes (ﬁnancially distressed ﬁrms). Since the samples after SMOTE and Easyensemble are
both balanced data, to save training cost and time, the benchmark models after Easyensemble
sampling are directly trained using the hyperparameters in Table 7. Compared with the results
in Table 7, the values of Recall in our baseline models from the whole sight, which reﬂects
the predictive performance of ﬁnancially distressed ﬁrms, become bigger. This demonstrates
the improved ability of the benchmark models to predict ﬁnancially distressed ﬁrms when
combined with the Easyensemble sampling compared to SMOTE sampling. In terms of
evaluating the overall prediction performance, the value of ACC has a small decrease, while
AUC, H-measure, and KS all increase (Table 8).
We can conclude that the prediction power of the benchmark models for ﬁnancially dis-
tressed ﬁrms is much improved after combining with Easyensemble, which subsequently
leads to the misidentiﬁcation of some ﬁnancially normal ﬁrms as ﬁnancially distressed ﬁrms.
The unbalanced number of ﬁnancially distressed and normal ﬁrms in the test sample leads to
a decrease in ACC. Overall, the predictive power of the benchmark models after Easyensem-
ble sampling is greatly improved. XGboost, Adaboost, GBDT, and RF models still present
better prediction performance than the other three baseline models. Sun et al. (2020) dis-
covered the importance of handling imbalanced data for predicting ﬁnancial distress and
found that the Adaboost-SVM model combined with SMOTE sampling demonstrated good
performance in predicting ﬁnancial distress. Sun et al. (2014) also discovered the important
role of SMOTE in handling imbalanced data for predicting ﬁnancial distress, and their study
results demonstrated that the combination of SVM and SMOTE, without ensemble learning,
showed good performance in predicting ﬁnancial distress. However, their study’s dataset was
limited to data before 2016, and they did not explore data lagging by more than two years. Our
results complement their ﬁndings by showing that, compared to SMOTE sampling, ensemble
learning models, not limited to AdaBoost-SVM and SVM, combined with easy ensemble
sampling, perform better in predicting ﬁnancial distress.

5.4 Feature selection
The number of initial ﬁnancial features is 80, some of which are redundant and may not be
useful in improving forecasting accuracy. Given this, the RFE method is employed to select
our ﬁnancial indicators to save calculation costs and calculation time. The RFE method is an
effectivefeatureselectionapproachformachinelearningmodelsbasedonfeatureimportance.
RFE needs to be combined with the base model to rank the features for selection. The choices
of the base model in the currently available studies mainly include SVM (Duan et al., 2005;
Rtayli & Enneya, 2020; Tang et al., 2007), RF (Chen et al., 2018; Elavarasan et al., 2020),
XGBoost (Priscilla & Prabha, 2021; Sikander et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021). After testing,
RF as the base model combined with RFE to form RF-RFE presented better prediction
performances in this study, so RF-RFE is employed in our experiment for feature selection.
The indicators are selected by RFE through tests as shown in Table 9, where the bolded
features make an effect on the prediction for T-2, T-3, and T-4 at the same time. Table 10 shows
the prediction accuracy of our benchmark models after RFE selection under Easyensemble.
In forecasting ﬁnancial distress for T-2, and T-3, the number of ﬁnancial indicators left after
selecting is 30, and for T-4, the number of ﬁnancial indicators left is 40. By testing, the
prediction accuracy using the indicators in Table 9 is close to the prediction accuracy using
80 ﬁnancial indicators, and the prediction accuracy of some ensemble learning models is even
higher. However, in general, the improvement of forecast accuracy by indicator selection is
not obvious, and can only play the role of saving forecasting costs.

5.6 Partial dependence plots
The PIMP method can assess feature importance but cannot capture the marginal effects
between features and the predicted outcome. Qian et al. (2022) use Partial Dependence Plots
(PDP) to illustrate the relationship between ﬁnancial distress and indicators for Chinese
listed ﬁrms. Figure 5a–c show PDPs for the top nine important features for T-2, T-3, and
T-4, respectively, after applying PIMP with XGBoost as the base model. Notably, Fig. 5a,
F21 in Fig. 5b, and F64 in Fig. 5c exhibit trends that signiﬁcantly differ from other features,
indicating inconsistent univariate relationships with the predicted outcomes. Additionally,
the steep curves of F7 and F64 in Fig. 5a; F6 and F12 in Fig. 5b; F64 and F79 in Fig. 5c
suggest a strong dependence on the predicted outcomes.
5.7 Excluding the effects of COVID-19
As is well known, since the COVID-19 pandemic occurred from the end of 2019 to the
beginning of 2020, we excluded the data from 2020 and 2021 from the original sample and
conducted the prediction again to make our research results more robust. All our ensemble
learning models: XGBoost, AdaBoost, GBDT, and RF use the hyperparameters shown in
Table 7 and incorporate Easyensemble sampling. All prediction results are presented in
Table 11. Compared with the results in Table 8, ACC, Recall, and Speciﬁcity have not changed
signiﬁcantly,indicatingthatafterexcludingtheimpactofthepandemic,theensemblelearning
model combined with easy ensemble sampling still shows good predictive performance in
forecasting ﬁnancial distress.
6 Conclusion
Many studies, such as Zieba et al. (2016) and Qian et al. (2022), demonstrate that ensem-
ble learning algorithms perform well in predicting ﬁnancial distress. However, while they
address feature importance and selection, most overlook the issue of handling imbalanced
data. This study builds on existing research in ﬁnancial distress forecasting by comparing two
methods for handling imbalanced data—SMOTE and Easyensemble—and analyzing feature
selection and importance. The key ﬁndings are: (1) Models using Easyensemble outperform
those using SMOTE in prediction accuracy; (2) Ensemble learning algorithms deliver more
accurate predictions than SVM, LR, and DNN; (3) Feature selection reduces the number of
indicators without sacriﬁcing prediction accuracy, improving efﬁciency and reducing com-
putation time; (4) Proﬁtability, cash ﬂow, solvency, and structural ratios are identiﬁed as
critical factors for predicting ﬁnancial distress through feature importance ranking (PIMP)
and partial dependence plots.

The combination of Easyensemble and ensemble learning models has proven effective in

be considered for use in other scenarios. Furthermore, among the selected machine learn-
ing prediction models, ensemble models demonstrated better performance, indicating that
ﬁnancial distress prediction should not be conﬁned to conventional methods. This offers new
approaches and insights for the application of AI in the ﬁeld of ﬁnancial distress prediction.
Research on integrating imbalanced data processing with machine learning is still evolv-
ing, particularly in the emerging ﬁeld of AI applications in corporate ﬁnance, especially for
binary forecasting. Future studies could explore whether the imbalanced data treatments dis-
cussed here are effective for publicly listed ﬁrms in other countries. Additionally, it would
be valuable to test whether our proposed techniques apply to other binary forecasting sce-
narios, such as disease prediction in medical contexts. Due to space limitations, this study
focuses solely on prediction results using Easyensemble and SMOTE with undersampling
and oversampling, leaving other sampling methods unexplored. Further research is expected
to improve the interpretability of ﬁnancial distress predictions, reﬁne current algorithms, and
potentially introduce new, highly interpretable prediction techniques.
