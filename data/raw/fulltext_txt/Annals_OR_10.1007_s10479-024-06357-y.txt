Abstract
Multi-criteria Decision Making (MCDM) involves evaluating alternatives based on a com-
prehensive set of conﬂicting criteria, often requiring the involvement of varied decision
makers. This has led to the emergence of stakeholder-based multi-criteria group decision-
making (MCGDM) frameworks. However, traditional MCGDM frequently overlooks the
interactions and trade-offs among different actors and criteria. The Multi-actor Multi-criteria
Analysis (MAMCA), developed in 2000, provides a transparent decision-making process
explicitly considering these interrelationships. It allows diverse stakeholder groups to repre-
sent their priorities, thereby enhancing their understanding of their own and others’ positions.
Over the past two decades, MAMCA has seen a signiﬁcant rise in popularity and has been
widely applied in diverse contexts, proving valuable as both a decision-making and stake-
holder engagement tool. However, our analysis of publications on cases in which MAMCA
was applied over the years shows that considerable variation exists in the overall process
approach, contingent upon the speciﬁc goals and context. To address these variations, this
paper proposes a modularized MAMCA structure, complemented by systematic application
guidelines, to aid future users in navigating the process steps and identifying the most suitable
methods for each step. Additionally, future research directions are suggested for potential
enhancements to the MAMCA framework by integrating varied methodologies.

1 Introduction
The process of decision-making in complex situations where multiple, conﬂicting criteria
exist is referred to as Multi-criteria Decision Making (MCDM) (Zavadskas et al., 2014).
This method involves evaluating and comparing alternatives based on a comprehensive set
of criteria, including both quantitative and qualitative factors, to arrive at a well-informed
decision. Despite its widespread application in some domains, it is necessary to involve
more than one decision maker (DM) to appraise the possible alternatives in the interest
of, for example, diverse perspectives, increased acceptance of decision, and reduced bias
(Bonner et al. 2002; Thabrew et al. 2009). Different group decision-making extended MCDM
methods were presented in the eighties and nineties and multi-criteria group decision-making
(MCGDM) frameworks have been proposed (Saaty & Peniwati, 2013). MCGDM engages
a group of DMs in the decision-making process, thereby incorporating a wider range of
perspectives, knowledge, and values into the evaluation of alternatives. Among them, the
stakeholder-based MCGDM plays an important role in public and social decision-making
(Banville et al. 1998). The stakeholders can be deﬁned as individuals or interest groups that
have vested interests in the outcome of a particular issue or the decision being considered
(Freeman et al. 2010). They may directly or indirectly impact or be impacted by the decision
and can hold both positive and negative perspectives. In public and social decision-making,
it is necessary to involve different stakeholders to obtain their support and also incorporate
their viewpoints, which facilitates a comprehensive consideration of diverse perspectives and
promotes outcomes that are equitable, inclusive, and socially responsible (Dean, 2022).
However, even with the incorporation of multiple stakeholders, the interactions and trade-
offs between different actors and criteria are often overlooked in MCGDM. Multi-actor Multi-
criteria Analysis (MAMCA) was developed in 2000 by Cathy Macharis to explicitly address
these interrelationships and to provide a comprehensive and transparent decision-making
process for complex, multi-actor problems (Macharis, 2000, 2005). MAMCA considers
the perspectives of stakeholders and the trade-offs between conﬂicting criteria. Different
stakeholder groups can be involved in the decision-making and have unique criteria sets that
can represent their priorities (Macharis et al. 2009). It enables discussion among stakeholder
groups after the evaluation, which makes them better understand the position of themselves
and others. MAMCA has been widely researched and developed over the past 20 years,
resulting in signiﬁcant advancements and improvements in the method. It was ﬁrst presented
to support the decision in the transportation sector, but its application has expanded to a
wide range of areas, including environmental and sustainability management, public policy,
etc. As it has the active involvement of stakeholders at its core, MAMCA is increasingly
not only used for purely decision-making purposes, but also as a stakeholder engagement
tool, and hence a sustainability-enhancing tool. The MAMCA software was developed and
has been continuously reﬁned to support real-world decision-making challenges (Huang,
Burgherr, and Macharis 2024). It has been implemented across various countries globally
(See Fig. 1 and Subsect. 3.1). The growth in interest and application of MAMCA has led to
the development of new approaches and techniques, which have increased its versatility and
robustness in decision-making problems.
With its growing body of literature and diverse applications, MAMCA has the potential
to further improve and transform the decision-making and participation process in various
ﬁelds. Within this review paper we analyze the way the framework has been used in the
past. In response, we propose a comprehensive guideline that takes into account the various
approaches used by previous researchers and that provides a systematic framework for its

2 MAMCA framework - what it is and how it works
The original objective of MAMCA is to help actors understand the preferences and priorities
of all relevant stakeholders, and to identify and evaluate different alternative solutions for
which a consensus can be reached (Macharis & Bernardini, 2015). It is a decision-support
framework with ’stakeholder involvement’ as a keyword.
The process of MAMCA typically involves the following steps, and normally requires
the help from facilitators/moderators to structure the decision-support model (Macharis et
al. 2009):
1. Problem identiﬁcation and alternative deﬁnition. In this step, the problem is identiﬁed
and the possible alternative solutions to address the problem are deﬁned. The focus is on
identifying the decision context. The consideration of a wide range of alternatives can
help to ensure that the ﬁnal decision is well-informed and robust.
2. Stakeholder analysis. Facilitators need to identify the stakeholders who may be affected
by the decision and analyze their interests, needs, priorities, etc. This step helps to ensure
that the decision-making process takes into account the perspectives of those who are most
likely to inﬂuence or be inﬂuenced by the deployment of the decision (Freeman, 1984).
Facilitators are neutral actors who coordinate but have no decision-making power. Their
123

Annals of Operations Research
main role is to guide participants through the MAMCA process steps without inﬂuencing
the content of the discussions.
3. Criteria identiﬁcation. In this step, for each stakeholder criteria need to be identiﬁed that
will be used for the evaluation of the alternatives. These criteria need to be relevant to
the problem and should reﬂect the interests and needs of the stakeholders identiﬁed in
the previous step. They should be speciﬁc and measurable to ensure that the evaluation
process is objective and transparent. The most relevant criteria are selected for every
stakeholder and weights are elicited that reﬂect their importance.
4. Criteria indicators building. Indicators are deﬁned to quantify the different selected crite-
ria, and are used to evaluate the performance of various alternatives. The indicators can
be either qualitative or quantitative.
5. Stakeholder overall analyses. This step is the core step in MAMCA, where stakehold-
ers evaluate the alternatives based on their criteria. It can be regarded as a standalone
MCDM evaluation step where the stakeholders do not know the preferences of the others.
In this step, alternatives are appraised using the chosen MCDM method, incorporating
the weights elicited in step 3. Different MCDM methods can be used, like for example
analytic hierarchy process (AHP) (Vaidya & Kumar, 2006), preference ranking organi-
zation method for enrichment evaluations (PROMETHEE) (Brans & De Smet, 2016),
etc., which enable the stakeholders to rank the alternatives and thereby generate a set
of performance scores for each criterion. These performance scores can then be used to
determine the optimal decision alternative.
6. Results discussion. This step comprises the discussion of the stakeholders to seek consen-
sus, which can be supported by conducting a thorough sensitivity analysis. This analysis
provides insight into the stability of the rankings with respect to the applied criteria
weights, which is particularly relevant in the stakeholder layer of MAMCA. By conduct-
ing a sensitivity analysis of these criteria, the decision-maker can better understand the
barriers to reaching a consensus and evaluate the magnitude of dissent. This analysis is
a promising starting point for facilitating a ﬁnal decision and successful implementation,
providing essential information to the decision-makers on the challenges they face.
7. Implementation. Implementation paths for the chosen alternative are developed, taking
into account the information gathered in the previous steps. These paths could include
modifying assessment results or proposing new alternatives, depending on the analy-
sis results. By examining stakeholder interests closely, feedback loops are created that
emphasize the strong stakeholder focus in all steps of the MAMCA process. Iteration of
the MAMCA process is a key aspect of the methodology.
The aforementioned steps are illustrated in Fig.2.
Compared to other multi-criteria group decision making (MCGDM) frameworks,
MAMCA has several similarities, but also distinct differences. Its structure is similar to
common MCGDM frameworks as outlined by Kabak and Ervural (2017). The generic con-
ceptual framework for MCGDM unfolds through three sequential stages: structuring stage,
assessment stage, and selection/ranking stage. Initially, the framework lays the groundwork
by identifying decision goals and establishing a committee of decision-makers (DMs), while
potentially assigning weights to DMs to reﬂect their expertise and inﬂuence. This foun-
dation facilitates the creation of a decision matrix encompassing alternatives, criteria, and
performance values. The assessment stage then diverges into two paths: criteria-based assess-
ment, where DMs employ either agreed or individual criteria sets, and alternative-based
assessment, focusing on direct evaluations of alternatives through pairwise comparisons for
situations with unclear criteria or dynamic decision environments. The process leads to the

selection/ranking stage, where collective preference orderings are derived from aggregated
DM evaluations. This stage also includes a consensus reaching process (CRP) to harmonize
DM preferences, ultimately guiding the decision-making body toward a deﬁnitive choice
among alternatives (Zhang et al. 2019).
Within the generic conceptual framework for MCGDM, the initial stages of MAMCA
(steps 1–2) correspond to the structuring phase, steps 3–5 correspond to the evaluation
phase, and steps 6–7 correspond to the selection/ranking phase. However, MAMCA intro-
duces unique elements that increase its ﬂexibility. A primary difference lies in MAMCA’s
high regard for stakeholder autonomy; stakeholders are empowered to introduce criteria that
reﬂect their interests and to evaluate alternatives based on personal preferences. Unlike other
frameworks, MAMCA does not aggregate scores from different stakeholders but instead
encourages a collaborative discourse to explore varied perspectives and work towards a
consensus solution. The following provides a comprehensive description of the distinctions
between MAMCA and the stages outlined in the generic conceptual framework of MCGDM:
1. In the structuring phase, traditional approaches typically assign weights to DMs or stake-
holders to reﬂect their inﬂuence or reliability, taking into account differences in expertise,
background, or knowledge (Boix-Cots et al. 2023). This process accounts for different
impacts on the overall result. MAMCA differs in that it promotes a more egalitarian
approach to decision-making by rejecting stakeholder rating and weighting. MAMCA
is ﬁrst and foremost stakeholder-oriented, and is aimed at giving all relevant groups of
actors an equal voice in the process, regardless of their power, background, size, or even
appearance of representation.
2. During the evaluation phase, stakeholders in MAMCA are allowed to apply their own
evaluation criteria, providing greater ﬂexibility than the generic frameworks, where DMs

3 Analysis of MAMCA cases
To thoroughly analyze MAMCA cases, we embarked on a comprehensive collection of all
papers related to MAMCA applications. In order to ensure an all-encompassing and unbiased
selection of relevant MAMCA literature, a systematic search procedure was employed. This
process focused on including English-language publications such as articles, book chapters,
and conference papers from peer-reviewed sources that were relevant to MAMCA case stud-
ies. The structure of the search string is illustrated in Fig.3. The searches were conducted
using Elsevier’s Scopus database1 and subsequently analyzed through a structured screening
process. The ﬁrst stage of this process entailed utilizing the Scopus database, renowned for its
extensive coverage of peer-reviewed scientiﬁc literature and advanced interface that enables
in-depth analysis and data export (Harzing and Alakangas2017; Moed et al. 2016). Our search

application. Representation from 5 different continents suggests that geographical location
hasn’t been a signiﬁcant limiting factor for users applying the methodology.

3.3 Research domain (What)

topics (see Fig.5). Out of the 66 studies 45 center around transportation solutions. However,

domains, and that the ﬁeld of study is not considered a key impediment by users applying
the methodology.

time and effort must be invested to ensure that this particular stakeholder group’s perspectives
are thoroughly considered and integrated (Renn et al. 2013). Involving citizens typically leads
to an increase in the number of representatives, aligning with MAMCA’s objectives. The aim
is not merely to include public opinions but to comprehensively account for their prefer-
ences and empower them as genuine decision-makers in the evaluation process. Engaging
citizens as active participants fosters a more inclusive decision-making process, which can
potentially lead to greater acceptance and satisfaction with the outcomes (Cooper & Wood,
1974; Fagence, 2014). Moreover, citizen involvement can contribute valuable local knowl-
edge, diverse perspectives, and innovative ideas to the process, resulting in more robust and
well-informed solutions (Stivers, 1990; Callahan, 2007; Chopyak & Levesque, 2002). Thus,
expanding the role of citizens in MAMCA is not only beneﬁcial for the decision-making
result but also helps create a more engaged and informed decision-making process.

application, AHP was predominantly used in many cases. However, as time progressed and
the ﬁeld evolved, a wider variety of methods have been employed. One reason for this shift
could be the increasing awareness and understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of
various methods in different decision-making contexts. For instance, C5 and C7 provide a
comprehensive comparison and selection of MCDM methods in MAMCA. As researchers
and practitioners gained more experience with MAMCA, they may have recognized the need
to explore and adopt alternative techniques that could be better suited to address speciﬁc
challenges, stakeholder preferences, and problem complexities. This evolution in method
selection reﬂects the ongoing growth and maturation in MAMCA evaluation.
As mentioned before, MAMCA allows the inclusion of different weight elicitation meth-
ods and MCDM methods. The other methods that haven’t been implemented in the previous
cases can potentially be applied in the future. The ﬂexibility of the method choices in
MAMCA encourages a more customized and context-sensitive approach to decision-making,
fostering more effective outcomes. By allowing facilitators to tailor the process to the unique
needs and characteristics of each case, MAMCA supports more informed and robust decision-
making that can better address the diverse challenges and complexities faced in various ﬁelds
and industries.
3.7 Purpose of use (Why)
MAMCA is a useful tool to reach a consensus decision with different stakeholders or at least
highlight the conﬂicts between them, but that is not the only added value the methodology
can bring. MAMCA can be employed as an integral part of a stakeholder participation
process, with a differentiated use depending on the goal of the engagement. Based on the
‘Wheel of Participation’ framework of Davidson (1998), which has its roots in the ‘Ladder of
Participation’ (Arnstein, 1969), four main goals can be identiﬁed, that all imply a speciﬁc way
of using MAMCA. In each of these, stakeholders are given a different role in the engagement
process and can have diverse incentives to participate. In the following sections, we focus on
the four big categories of the wheel of participation (see Fig.7).
3.7.1 Information
Since the different steps of MAMCA give a detailed overview of various project aspects
(such as potential solutions, involved actors, and stakeholder criteria) the methodology can
be used as a tool to raise the knowledge of stakeholders on the project topic. By going through
the whole process, which can for example be set up as a test run instead of a real decision-
making initiative, participants learn about all elements that come into play and the different
points of view that will have to be taken into account for the ﬁnal decision. By bringing all
relevant actors together in a MAMCA workshop information can be more easily provided
and questions answered.

MAMCA can also be used for general education purposes in classes. It is a useful way to
let students discuss the possible impacts of certain measures and it adds an element of fun
through roleplay. A slide desk was created that can be used for classes, and the students can
use the MAMCA software online.
A goal of MAMCA that is gaining popularity is creating mutual understanding of each
other’s viewpoints between the stakeholder groups, where a group discussion is held on
the results of each step. Often misconceptions exist about other actors’ objectives, and a
lack of understanding also results in less willingness to work on compromise solutions. By
giving stakeholders a better insight into each other’s motivations a foundation for further
engagement is built. That is why the method can be called an empathy creator.
3.7.2 Consultation
By providing the opportunity to gather stakeholder criteria, having them weighed according
to importance by the stakeholders themselves, and letting them evaluate different poten-
tial solutions, a wide range of useful information is gathered through a MAMCA process
that can serve as input for the ﬁnal decision-making. Stakeholders get to voice their needs
and wants directly, which often yields information that could otherwise be overlooked by
researchers or decision-makers, and a more complete overview is created of all elements that
are necessary to take into account. This ‘consultation’ objective also includes the testing of
the stakeholder support for various alternative solutions. Through the evaluation step and the
resulting multi-actor view useful information can be gathered on essential boundary condi-
tions and stakeholder preferences, so alternative solutions can be developed that align better
with the demands of everyone involved.

3.7.3 Participation
Various steps of MAMCA allow for greater stakeholder input and participation. By allowing
actors a greater active input throughout the whole decision-making process, greater support
for the end result can be built. Participation efforts give stakeholders the opportunity to co-
design a solution that ﬁts their needs, and to better accept concessions that must potentially
be made. Another variant of the MAMCA was developed, namely COMCA standing for
“Competence Based Multi Criteria Analysis”. The reason behind this development is the
often very complex institutional setting of the governmental stakeholder. In order to better
capture the complexity of governance levels, this methodology starts with the question: Who
has the competence in this matter? In order words, once we come to a decision, who will be
able to also implement it (maybe in collaboration with others)? The method has been applied
to the problem of a congested rail track in the middle of Brussels and several alternatives
were analyzed (te Boveldt et al. 2018).
3.7.4 Empowerment
To fully empower stakeholders by means of the MAMCA process, not only the co-creation
of the alternative solutions and shared responsibility over the content and management of the
other MAMCA steps should be central. Stakeholders should also be given greater input in
the ﬁnal decision-making as well as in the development of the implementation plan. When
stakeholders decide themselves on the solution to be deployed, and are responsible for this
implementation, full mutual agreement is essential. Depending on the scale of the project, this
often requires permanent cooperation and discussion platforms and engagement structures
to be set up, to guarantee continued follow-up and successful supported implementation.
3.7.5 Engagement objectives in the studied cases
The goal of the MAMCA process, and subsequently the way the MAMCA methodology
was applied, differs between the cases described in the papers. In the majority of the cases
the initiative served as an ex-ante evaluation to clarify the advantages and disadvantages of
choice situations and to deliver input to come to a stakeholder-supported implementation
decision. Actively gathering direct input from stakeholders was an overall central focus.
Figure8 provides an overview of the engagement objectives in the studied cases.
From the ‘Wheel of Participation’ categorization of Davidson, ‘Consultation’ therefore is
the most prevalent objective of the applied MAMCA processes. In all of the studied cases
gathering information from the participants was an aim of the process. Most often this was
done by inquiring the different stakeholders about their relevant criteria and by letting them
attribute an importance score to these criteria. The evaluation of the proposed solutions
(MAMCA step 5, described in all papers except for C10, C23, and C43) is also considered

C47 the evaluation output was used for comparison with an MCA sustainability assessment,

3.8 Applied MAMCA steps (Why)
Depending on the aim of the process, MAMCA is used in different ways. Not all steps are
relevant to achieve the set (participation) goal. When analyzing which of the 7 MAMCA
steps were applied in the cases described in the papers, it is noteworthy that in 23 of them, the
MAMCA case was a ﬁctional case study to test the methodology or had a conceptual thematic
at its base without a concrete implementation goal. Step 7 (implementation) was therefore
redundant. In the other cases, however, step 7 is also almost never performed. Only C12 and
C14 mention the implementation of the result, with C15, C44, C51, and C55 already having
one of the alternatives implemented before or in parallel with the MAMCA exercise. This
makes it hard to assess the effect the engagement process has had on the actual result in terms
of for example mitigating measures. In C37 deployment recommendations are developed for
each alternative, based on the individual evaluation results.

4 An adapted guideline based on learned experiences
The analysis results of the previous section suggest that the MAMCA framework lends itself
to a highly adaptable group decision-making process. It achieves this through the potential
applicability across diverse domains, the integration of various weight elicitation and MCDM
methods, and the ability to accommodate varied ways of participation and diverse numbers
and types of participants. Because of its inherent ﬂexibility and broad usage options, a need
for a structured guideline exists. This guideline should serve to provide DMs, facilitators, and
researchers with a clear roadmap on how to effectively implement the MAMCA framework,
navigate its complexities, and harness its full potential in varied application scenarios. Based
on the insights derived from the case study review in the previous section, we have pinpointed
several critical considerations for designing this guideline:
1. The increasing number of geographical locations (see Subsect. 3.1) and research domains
in which MAMCA is used (see Subsect. 3.3), as well as the growing signiﬁcance placed
on broad participation, encompassing a diverse array of stakeholders in terms of both
number and type 3.5 show a growing variety of potential use cases. To ensure that
MAMCA is used in a way that suits the complexity of each case best, there is a need for
more detailed guidelines on the various possible approaches and the purpose they can
serve.
2. Examination of the usage purposes of MAMCA, as detailed in Subsect. 3.7, reveals that
stakeholder engagement varies across different MAMCA phases, related to the process
goal. Although the sequence of the steps can vary across case studies, overall MAMCA
stages can be identiﬁed for which the order of application is the same in all cases. This
observation underscores the feasibility of implementing MAMCA in a modular fashion.
3. The study in Subsect. 3.8 of the applied MAMCA steps shows that not all of them are
performed in every case. By developing a more ﬂexible modular MAMCA framework,
it becomes more clear for the users that the steps can indeed be approached in different
ways, and what speciﬁc purposes they can fulﬁll.
4. The variation in uses and approaches of the MAMCA process steps identiﬁed in Sub-
sects. 3.7 and 3.8 demonstrate the value of comprehensive guidelines that lead users
through the use purposes of MAMCA, providing them with insights into how the method-
ological steps can be navigated across diverse circumstances.

5. Although the fundamental steps of MAMCA should remain consistent at a high level,
a clearer description of each step is necessary to improve practitioners’ comprehension
and application of the process.
Therefore, to assist users in effectively applying MAMCA across diverse scenarios, and
to highlight the adaptable formats in which MAMCA can be implemented, we have devised
a systematic guideline. This is complemented by a modularized MAMCA framework that
incorporates the methodologies outlined in the previous section. This comprehensive guide
aims to streamline the decision-making process and enhance the adaptability of MAMCA,
enabling users to tailor the approach to their speciﬁc needs and challenges (see Fig.9).
4.1 Modularized MAMCA framework
As previously mentioned, MAMCA emphasizes ﬂexibility, allowing for the implemen-
tation of various steps in different orders to address unique case requirements without
strictly adhering to a conventional framework order. With this in mind, we seek to further
enhance MAMCA’s adaptability by modularizing its steps. This modular approach fosters
an even more ﬂexible and customizable engagement and decision-making process, enabling
researchers and practitioners to tailor MAMCA to their speciﬁc needs while ensuring com-
prehensive and systematic evaluations across diverse scenarios. By reﬁning the structure
and augmenting MAMCA’s ﬂexibility, we strive to facilitate more effective and informed
123

Annals of Operations Research
decision-making that aligns with the multifaceted needs of stakeholders and the complex-
ities of various decision-making contexts. The new modularized MAMCA framework is
comprised of three stages: (I) Evaluation Structuring Stage, (II) Appraisal Stage, and (III)
Implementation Preparation Stage. Typically, researchers and practitioners should imple-
ment MAMCA stage by stage, following the prescribed order. However, the sequence of
steps within each stage may vary, and some steps might be optional. Within the (I) Evalu-
ation Structuring Stage, there are (A) Alternative Deﬁnition, (B) Stakeholder Analysis, and
(C) Criteria Deﬁnition; within the (II) Appraisal Stage, there are (D) Criteria Indicator Build-
ing and (E) MCGDM s; and within the (III) Implementation preparation Stage, there are (F)
Round-table discussion and (G) Revision, action plan and validation. In the following sub-
sections, we present an overview of the steps within each stage, offering a comprehensive
understanding of the modularized MAMCA framework and its implementation:
4.1.1 Evaluation structuring stage
The evaluation structuring stage serves as the foundation of the MAMCA process. Upon iden-
tifying the problem at hand, this initial stage involves deﬁning the alternatives, stakeholders,
and stakeholders’ criteria.
• (A) Alternative Deﬁnition: In this step, the alternatives are identiﬁed and clearly deﬁned.
These alternatives represent the various possible solutions or options being considered
in the decision-making process.
• (B) Stakeholder Analysis: The stakeholder analysis step involves identifying and
understanding the key stakeholders who are affected by or have an interest in the decision-
making process.
• (C) Criteria Deﬁnition: In this step, the criteria for evaluating the alternatives are deﬁned
based on the decision context (and input from stakeholders).
4.1.2 Appraisal stage
The appraisal stage is a crucial part of the modularized MAMCA framework, where the
criteria weight elicitation and the assessment of the deﬁned alternatives take place. This
stage builds upon the foundation laid in the evaluation structuring stage and involves the
systematic evaluation of alternatives based on the criteria and preferences of the stakeholder
groups.
• (D) Criteria Indicator Building: In this step, measurable indicators are developed for
each criterion established in the evaluation structuring stage. During this stage, data for
quantitative criteria indicators are also gathered. This step ensures that the necessary
information is obtained for step (E).
• (E) MCGDMs: The MCGDMs step involves eliciting the criteria weights and evaluating
the alternatives based on the criteria. In this step, the representatives from each stake-
holder group participate in a group decision-making process, i.e., MCGDM. Thus, while
representatives within the same stakeholder group share a common set of criteria, differ-
ent stakeholder groups may have different criteria sets. This is in line with the essence
of Multi-actor Multi-criteria Analysis (MAMCA), which integrates various stakeholder
groups into a uniﬁed framework, allowing for a series of MCGDMs.
123

Annals of Operations Research
4.1.3 Implementation preparation stage
The ﬁnalization stage marks the concluding phase of the modularized MAMCA framework.
Building on the outcomes of the evaluation structuring and appraisal stages, this stage focuses
on consolidating the results and validation the chosen decision.
• (F) Round-table Discussion: In this step, the goal is to jointly discuss the evaluation
results with all stakeholder groups, to identify the aspects that are essential to achieve a
generally supported end result. Sensitivity analysis and a consensus reaching model can
be applied.
• (G) Revision, action plan and Validation: This step, instead of directly implementing the
chosen decision, focuses on reviewing and revising the evaluation results and alternatives
as needed to enable stakeholders to reach a consensus. This process may involve reﬁning
the alternatives, re-evaluating speciﬁc aspects, or repeating the evaluation with adjusted
parameters. In tandem with the revision process, an action plan is developed to outline the
steps needed to implement the chosen alternative. If stakeholders are not satisﬁed with
the revised alternatives, they may opt to redo the entire MAMCA process, thus creating
a loop until a satisfactory consensus is achieved.
The objective of this stage is to ensure that the ﬁnal decision is well-informed, robust, and
meets the needs and expectations of all stakeholders involved.
4.2 Engagement methods in MAMCA
In this section, we introduce various methodologies for involving stakeholders that can be
implemented within the different stages of the modularized MAMCA framework to enhance
its adaptability and effectiveness. Engagement methods such as workshops and surveys can be
employed to facilitate stakeholder involvement throughout the process. These methodologies
can be incorporated into speciﬁc stages or applied across multiple stages, as needed.
For Stage (I) Evaluation Structuring Stage, stakeholders can be involved via a workshop
and/or a survey. Workshops can be employed to encourage interactive discussions and brain-
storming, while surveys can be used to gather broader input from various stakeholder groups
to ensure a comprehensive understanding of potential future developments. In this stage,
several methodologies can be implemented, such as:
• Scenario planning: By creating multiple alternative scenarios, stakeholders can explore
the potential impacts of their decisions and better understand the factors that may
inﬂuence the success or failure of their choices. This approach can help inform the
development of robust alternatives that are more resilient to future uncertainties (Tori et
al. 2023).
• Criteria preprocessing: This method streamlines a stakeholder-based process of deﬁning
important criteria, by identifying and prioritizing the most relevant criteria per stake-
holder group, ensuring that the most signiﬁcant factors are considered when evaluating
alternatives (Huang et al. 2023).
• Co-creation and Co-design: Co-creation and co-design actively involve stakeholders in
the process of deﬁning alternatives and criteria, fostering collaboration and shared own-
ership of the decision-making process ( De Koning et al. 2016). These approaches can
enhance the relevance and acceptance of the ﬁnal decision, as stakeholders are more
likely to support a solution they helped develop (Pappers et al., 2021).
123

Annals of Operations Research
Stage (II) Appraisal Stage can involve stakeholders through a workshop and/or survey. If
mass participation is employed across Stages (I) and (II), surveys can be utilized to collect
input from a larger group of stakeholders, ensuring a more comprehensive and inclusive
decision-making process (Huang et al. 2021). By involving a larger number of stakeholders,
facilitators gain a broader understanding of stakeholder preferences and priorities, lead-
ing to more informed and inclusive decisions. On the other hand, the MCDGM step is
typically carried out by engaging stakeholders through workshops. Stakeholders can partic-
ipate in discussions and activities to identify and prioritize criteria, to appraise alternatives.
If an additional sustainable evaluation framework is incorporated in the Appraisal Stage,
experts can help assess alternatives based on social, environmental, and economic dimen-
sions of sustainability (Keseru, Bulckaen, and Macharis 2016b). By considering these factors,
decision-makers and stakeholders can better understand the long-term implications of their
choices and promote more sustainable and responsible decision-making.
Across Stages (II) and (III), Monte Carlo analysis can be implemented to account for
uncertainties and variability in the decision-making process, providing a more robust and
reliable evaluation of alternatives and the potential impacts of the ﬁnal decision (Baudry et
al. 2018b; Huang et al. 2023).
In Stage (III) Preparation Implementation Stage, fostering discussions among stakehold-
ers is crucial; therefore, workshops serve as an effective means to involve them. Interactive
workshops can be structured to encourage open communication, negotiation, and compro-
mise among stakeholders, helping them achieve consensus on the optimal alternative to
implement. During this stage, consensus-reaching models can be employed to further sup-
port stakeholders in reaching an agreement on their preferred choice (Huang et al. 2021).
Additionally, adapted alternatives can be co-created, based on the evaluation and consensus-
building discussion results.
4.3 Approach of the MAMCA steps, related to the engagement goal
Depending on the objective of the MAMCA application, the various steps can be approached
in different ways. The three steps of Stage (I), as well as step (E) MCGDM of Stage (II)
are essential to perform in every context where the method is applied. The other steps are
optional, and the approach can differ, related to the envisioned engagement goal.
As mentioned before, the objective ‘information’ can take three forms:
• The MAMCA workshop is used to provide more detailed information on the topic to
the participating stakeholders. In this case it is working as a knowledge-building and an
awareness-building tool;
• MAMCA is used for educational purposes, where applicants learn to apply the method in
a mock set-up. They take on the role of various stakeholders, to gain insight into various
elements and points of view that are relevant to the topic in scope;
• Themethodisusedtocreatemutualunderstandingamongstakeholders.Bybringingthem
all together in a workshop setting, and giving them the time to explain their objectives
and needs to each other, mutual insight is created into the various stakes that are at play.
This raises understanding, empathy, and the willingness to make compromises for the
mutual good.
In all three cases, when ‘information’ is the only engagement objective, the project initiator
deﬁnes the alternatives beforehand (A). The same goes for the stakeholder analysis (B), where
it is the initiator who identiﬁes the stakeholders that are relevant to involve. In an educational
context, the MAMCA participants don’t represent themselves, but a pre-deﬁned stakeholder

Annals of Operations Research
group through roleplay. For the criteria selection (C) a list of potential criteria is provided
by the initiator. This can be either a ﬁxed list for each stakeholder group (compiled through
literature research or prior experience), or a longlist from which each stakeholder group can
select a limited amount of personally most relevant criteria. A detailed discussion of the
selection results among the stakeholders (and of the later criteria weighting results) is added
in cases where the goal is creating mutual understanding. For the criteria indicators building
step (D) expert input is provided and explained for insight-gaining purposes. The evalua-
tion (E) is done based on expert as well as stakeholder input in the information-provision
and educational cases. When the goal is to create mutual understanding the evaluation can
be done by the stakeholders, not based on actual measurement information, but through a
personal assessment of the impact of the different alternatives on their criteria, explaining
their evaluations to each other. By additionally letting participants take on different roles they
become more understanding of the criteria of other parties as well as the necessary trade-offs.
Caution is needed with this approach as the perception of the actors might differ from reality
or from scientiﬁc research results. Expert evaluation support can be useful since it allows
providing feedback to the participants when their idea strongly differs from reality. By ﬁrst
letting the stakeholders do the evaluation by themselves, and then repeating the exercise with
provided expert insight on how alternatives score on certain indicators, both participants and
project initiators learn about potential misconceptions that might live among the stakehold-
ers. When expert input is not available, it is therefore important that it is highlighted at the
start that the aim of the process is creating mutual insight, and not realistic evaluation or
actual decision-making.
In all cases, Stage (III) contains a consensus-building discussion on the evaluation results,
and potentially the adaptation of the initial alternatives based on the discussion outcome.
Although this stage can also be disregarded when the objective is purely knowledge-building
on the topic.
The goal ‘consultation’ can take two forms:
• MAMCA is used by the project initiators to gain information on stakeholder motivations;
• The method is used for testing the appraisal of and support for particular pre-deﬁned
alternatives.
In the ﬁrst case only the stakeholder analysis (B), criteria selection (C) and criteria weight-
ing (E) are essential. The results of these steps give an insight into the main project objectives
of each of the relevant stakeholders, which can for example be used in the development of
alternatives. In the second case, all steps need to be applied, with pre-determined alternatives
that are the subject of the support test (A). Stage (III) could be disregarded if the aim is to
determine the support of individual stakeholder groups. It is however advised to include the
consensus-building discussion step (F) in the process since the results give a better insight into
the overall support for certain alternatives, the potential ﬂexibility in stakeholders’ points of
view, and the willingness to compromise. Step (G), under the form of the co-creation of new
alternatives or an implementation plan, can also provide useful input on necessary boundary
conditions that potentially increase support.
When MAMCA is applied speciﬁcally with ’participation’ as a goal in mind, additional
actionscanbetakenineachofthevariousstepstoenhancestakeholderinvolvement.Thealter-
natives (A) can be co-created or tweaked together with the stakeholders in a design charette
instead of provided ready-made by the project initiator. Stakeholders can be determined (B)
through the snowball method (Biernacki & Waldorf, 1981), in which a ﬁrst selection is made
by the initiator, and these stakeholders decide themselves on potentially relevant other actors
until everyone involved agrees on the ﬁnal list. For the criteria determination (C), the stake-

holders themselves can develop their own criteria, assisted by experts in the ﬁeld, instead
of selecting them from a pre-set list. This can be done individually as well as through joint
exercises in a workshop. The weighting of the criteria and the evaluation (E and D) can be
done by the stakeholders themselves in a joint workshop, followed by a consensus-building
discussion (F) and the co-creation of adapted alternatives (G). Stage (III) is designed specif-
ically for participation purposes since it is aimed at the formation of compromises through
group discussions on individual evaluation results. An important prerequisite is the presence
of a neutral moderator that can make sure every participant gets the same opportunity to
contribute to the discussion and equal volume of all voices is guaranteed. As inclusiveness is
a key principle of MAMCA, there is no weight attributed to the stakeholders, and the input
of every participant is equally important.
When the objective is ‘empowerment’ all additional participative efforts for the different
MAMCA steps can be applied too, supplemented with the option to let the stakeholders
themselves make the ﬁnal decision on the alternative that will be deployed, and letting them
co-create an implementation plan (G). The goal here is to let the stakeholders take more
control over the process and give them responsibility over the decision-making, assisted and
not directed by experts, since they will also be the ones in charge of the deployment.
5 Future research suggestions
Over the past 20 years, MAMCA as a stakeholder-based group decision-support framework,
has addressed various decision-making challenges across diverse domains. Owing to its
ﬂexible structure, practitioners and researchers have applied MAMCA in a multitude of
ways. To better support the utilization of MAMCA in different scenarios, we propose a
formal guideline for its implementation. Additionally, based on the insights gained from
existing use cases, we recommend further research directions centered around the 5 W’s and
H.
5.1 What
5.1.1 More focus on sustainability in decision-making
Through its inclusive nature, with a focus on the active participation of all relevant stakeholder
groups without importance weighting of the voices involved (C6, C7, C9, C11, C14, C15,
C22, C37), MAMCA intrinsically has the potential to assist in more sustainable decision-
making. Where normative evaluation methods such as a Cost-Beneﬁt Analysis (CBA) or a
multi-criteria sustainability analysis identify quantiﬁable sustainability elements as the basis
for a ‘calculated’ sustainability evaluation, a participative method such as MAMCA offers
the opportunity to include the topic of sustainability in the decision-making process under a
different form. Future research on deepened stakeholder management within MAMCA (see
Subsect. 5.3.2), and an examination of what role the results of a normative sustainability
evaluation could play in the stage (III) Implementation Preparation Stage of MAMCA, can
further enhance the methodology as a sustainable decision-making tool.
123

Annals of Operations Research
5.1.2 Strengthening the connection with transition management
A just transition is only possible with stakeholder involvement and especially citizen involve-
ment (e.g., C1, C4, C6, C12). The combination of MAMCA with transition management
proved very fruitful in strengthening the practical stakeholder involvement in the transition
(Opricovic 1998; Lode et al. 2021), and can be further explored with variating use cases.
5.2 Why
5.2.1 MAMCA as an accelerator hub
MAMCA can be used as part of a larger change process. The factor 8 process is a process
of change, starting with creating awareness, followed by identifying solutions at different
level (Macharis, 2022). Actor involvement, possible by means of MAMCA, allows then to see
what will be the impacts of these solutions on the objectives of the actors that are inﬂuenced
or can be inﬂuenced. The participatory evaluation process allows to come to better and better
supported solutions that enable to accelerate the transition.
5.2.2 MAMCA as a tool for different engagement purposes
As indicated in the analysis, MAMCA is used for various purposes: from a tool to provide
information and enhance knowledge to an instrument to empower participants in a co-creation
process (see Subsect. 3.7). Depending on the goal, the way the different steps are approached
often varies. To create a better insight into the full potential of MAMCA as an engagement
instrument, and further detail the potential approaches of each step in relation to the process
purpose, more research is needed. This type of study has been conducted within the context
of energy community setups (Heuninckx et al. 2023), but not for all other domains in which
MAMCA has been or could be used. Future studies could therefore focus speciﬁcally on
various step applications, to further enrich and deepen the MAMCA framework with more
detailed examples and approach support for its users. This could for example lead to system-
atic guidelines with suggestions on how to approach each of the MAMCA steps, contingent
upon its use purpose.
5.3 Who
5.3.1 Meeting the needs of a diverse number of stakeholders and different stakeholder
types
As a stakeholder-oriented decision-support framework, MAMCA is designed to uncover the
diverse perspectives of stakeholders with varying interests. Still, it can be further developed
to better be employed in both small-scale group decision-making scenarios and large-scale
group decision-making scenarios (see Subsect. 3.5). Small-scale group decision-making usu-
ally involves a group of DMs or experts working together to identify a ﬁnal solution for
implementation, aiming for accurate performance values based on their expertise. Integrat-
ing Delphi rounds within the MAMCA process can potentially yield more robust results
(Tavana et al. 1993).
123

Annals of Operations Research
While we generally recommend against aggregating scores from different stakeholder
groups into a single uniﬁed metric, certain contexts, such as enterprise evaluation, necessitate
a ﬁnal assessment for the alternatives. In these instances, a weighted decision-maker (DM)
approach proves beneﬁcial. This method tailors the evaluation process by assigning weights
to the DMs based on their expertise and decision-making authority, enhancing the accuracy
and relevance of the uniﬁed ranking of alternatives (Kunsch & Brans, 2019).
In large-scale contexts, which often occur in public and social decision-making processes
involving citizen participation, it is crucial to gain a comprehensive understanding of public
priorities. Building upon the mass-participation concept proposed in MAMCA (Huang et
al. 2021), the framework can be further developed to explore stakeholder heterogeneity in
various ways.
5.3.2 Deepening stakeholder management
MCGDM methods in general focus on the integration of various stakeholder perspectives
into the decision-making process. The term ‘stakeholder’ however is closely linked to proﬁt
motivation and does not always fully cover the broad range of relevant actor views. To
progress towards more sustainable and democratic governance, shifting away from proﬁt-
centric approaches, it is important to deepen stakeholder management. For MAMCA, a
proposed future pathway to study in this regard is to broaden the involved actors and adopt
the principle of ‘rightsholders’. By expanding process participation with representatives of
for example ‘the next generation’ or nature and incorporating the perspective of rightsholders
in the process instead of only stakeholders, an additional sustainability level could potentially
be added to the decision-making.
5.4 How
5.5 Examining various MCDM methods for optimal application in diverse scenarios
Different MCDM methods can be incorporated into the MAMCA software to enhance its
usability (Huang et al. 2020; Huang, Burgherr, and Macharis 2024) (see 3.6). This integration
allows for the comprehensive application of different decision-making techniques within one
platform, simplifying the decision-making process and increasing its efﬁciency.
However, not every method is suited for each case and in a MAMCA setting. Several
MCDM methods have already been incorporated into MAMCA, showcasing its ﬂexibility
in combining different weight elicitation and alternative performance evaluation techniques.
However, these methods only represent a small portion of the available MCDM methods.
In future research, it would be valuable to explore and examine a broader range of MCDM
methods to determine their potential applicability in speciﬁc cases. For instance, the ELEC-
TRE method can be particularly helpful in outranking alternatives without compensation,
which could prove interesting in cases where stakeholders are seeking a consensus solution
(Figueira et al. 2016). Examining the TOPSIS (Lai et al. 1994) and VIKOR (Mardani et al.
2016) methods is also interesting, as these methods, along with ELECTRE, PROMETHEE,
and AHP, are often discussed collectively for their potential applicability (Opricovic, 1998).
However, the feasibility of implementing these methods ultimately depends on the speciﬁc
case at hand. By investigating the various methods, researchers can gain a better understand-
ing of the factors that inﬂuence their successful application in different decision-making
scenarios and further enhance the adaptability of the MAMCA framework.

5.5.1 Incorporating Fuzzy logic to consider stakeholders’ ambiguity
The integration of fuzzy logic into the MAMCA framework provides a promising avenue
for further investigation. Fuzzy logic can account for the inherent uncertainties and impreci-
sion in stakeholder preferences and decision-making processes. It allows preferences to be
modeled on a continuum rather than in binary terms, reﬂecting the complex, often subjective
nature of human judgment. Several fuzzy MCDM methods already exist, such as fuzzy-AHP
(FAHP) (Liu et al. 2020) and fuzzy-PROMETHEE (Senvar et al. 2014). The application of
fuzzy logic in group decision-making enables the modeling of complex decision-making
scenarios where stakeholder opinions are not merely ’for’ or ’against’ but are represented on
a spectrum of agreement. This approach acknowledges and quantiﬁes the degrees of uncer-
tainty and subjectivity in stakeholder inputs, facilitating a more comprehensive and realistic
analysis of collective preferences. By integrating fuzzy logic, the MAMCA framework can
become more robust and adaptable to real-world scenarios, where uncertainty and ambiguity
often play signiﬁcant roles.
5.5.2 Going a level deeper than criteria: what are the underlying values?
In general, to determine important assessment criteria in the evaluation structuring stage (I),
all stakeholders are asked about their objectives for the project in scope. In the appraisal stage
(II), an easier-to-measure/assess indicator is coupled with each of these objectives. We think
that for sustainability questions, it would be good to go a level deeper, i.e. asking about the
underlying values. What is for them important? By inquiring about the fundamental values
that inform stakeholders’ objectives, By doing so we would like to transcend traditional
assessment criteria and illuminate the core principles guiding stakeholders’ perspectives,
thereby fostering a more holistic understanding of their motivations and priorities.
By identifying the actors’ values that make them set their goals instead of listing these
goals, you dive a level deeper and will get to an even more transparent discussion.
5.5.3 Integrating domain-speciﬁc techniques for applications in different domains
Given MAMCA’s applicability across diverse domains, integrating domain-speciﬁc tech-
niques can signiﬁcantly enhance its effectiveness. For instance, in urban planning decision-
making, incorporating Geographic Information Systems (GIS) can offer valuable insights
by considering the geographical context of the alternatives, enriching the information avail-
able for decision-making (e.g., C2). Similarly, within the ﬁeld of mobility decision-making,
employing agent-based modeling (ABM) allows for the simulation of scenarios, generating
more robust and comprehensive data for evaluating alternatives (Huang et al. 2023, 2024).
In environmental management decision-making, applying Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to
evaluate the environmental impacts of alternatives throughout their life cycles can provide

a comprehensive view of the ecological consequences of each option. In healthcare sec-
tor, utilizing Evidence-Based Medicine (EBM) practices to weigh the outcomes of different
healthcare interventions allows stakeholders to make informed decisions based on clinical
evidence. These tailored approaches not only facilitate the decision-making process but also
ensure that recommendations are grounded in detailed, domain-relevant analysis. More in
general we would like to welcome the integration of decision support systems that can be
used for inputs in the MAMCA analysis.
6 Conclusion
A comprehensive review of literature since 2010 reveals diverse applications of the MAMCA
framework across various contexts and methodologies, signifying its broadening geograph-
ical reach and application across a range of research domains. Besides its primary role in
decision-making, MAMCA also emerges as a signiﬁcant stakeholder engagement tool. The
selection of methods and tools for each stage in the MAMCA process often varies depend-
ing on the deﬁned goals and context, leading to substantial variability in the overall process
approach.
In response to these variations, this study introduces a modularized MAMCA structure,
supplemented by systematic guidelines outlining potential application strategies. This pro-
posed structure aims to streamline navigation through the different process steps for future
users, simultaneously assisting them in identifying the methods best suited to each stage.
Additionally, we delve into possible future research directions, focusing on the potential for
evolving the MAMCA framework through the integration of diverse methodologies.
