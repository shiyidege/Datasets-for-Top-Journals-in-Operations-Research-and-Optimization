Abstract
The Israeli Queue is a batch service polling system where a single server attends to multiple
queues based on seniority. Each arriving customer belongs to one of several classes. Upon
arrival, a customer either joins an existing queue for their class or initiates a new queue if they
are the ﬁrst of their class to arrive. Customers from the class with the most senior member are
served together as a batch, with the service time remaining constant regardless of the batch
size. This service model is found in applications like advanced elevator systems and on-
demand shared mobility, where passengers heading to the same destination can share a ride.
However, in many real-world scenarios, the vehicle capacities are small and constraining,
which calls for a deeper exploration of the Israeli queue with a capacitated server (IQCS). In
this paper, we formally deﬁne the IQCS and address the challenges of creating a mathemati-
cally tractable model to represent it. To approximate the IQCS, we develop a quasi-birth-death
process and derive approximations for key performance measures. To validate our approach,
we implement a simulation model and use it to compare the IQCS, the approximate model,
and the original Israeli Queue. Our results across various scenarios demonstrate the accuracy
of the approximate model. Nonetheless, the presence of a remaining gap underscores the
ongoing challenge of precisely and efﬁciently modeling the IQCS, posing an open question
for the research community.

In this work, we focus on online station based ride-sharing services. In such services, trips
are provided from/to a given set of stations and information regarding the passengers’ travel
requests is revealed online, as they arrive in one of the system’s stations. Particularly, when
passengers arrive at a station, they notify the system regarding their desired destination. Then,
they are either instantaneously assigned to a vehicle parked in the station or are asked to wait
for a vehicle assignment. In other words, they queue for a vehicle. This type of setting exists
in several mobility services such as station based ride pooling (Stiglic et al., 2015; Fielbaum
et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2021), Personal rapid transit (PRT) (Andréasson, 2005; Lees-Miller
et al., 2009), elevator systems (Nikovski and Brand, 2004; Hanif and Mohammad, 2023) and
last mile transit services (Repoux et al., 2021).
In assessing the performance of mobility services, we look at two key indicators: the
time passengers spend waiting at their origin stations and the number of individuals wait-
ing. While historical data provides insight into past system performance, exploring different
setups requires dynamic stochastic models. This study speciﬁcally zooms in on the number
of waiting passengers at a speciﬁc location, like the ground ﬂoor of a high-rise (for elevator
systems) or a busy transit station (applicable to ride-pooling and PRT). Our approach hinges
on the straightforward application of Little’s law to deduce average waiting times. By inde-
pendently analyzing each station, one can derive a system-wide estimate of the number of
waiting passengers and their average waiting times.
Modeling the dynamics of a queue under ride-sharing policies is not straightforward. For
example, a simple and yet effective ride-sharing policy may be to group passengers queued in
a station, based on their destinations. Thus, when a vehicle is assigned to a certain destination,
the system may prioritize passengers heading to the same destination over other passengers
that may have been waiting longer in the station.
Several studies have applied classical queueing models in order to approximate the wait-
ing time in shared mobility services such as elevator systems, see e.g. Lee et al. (2009);
Kahraman and Gosavi (2011) and PRT systems, see Lees-Miller et al. (2009); Lees-Miller
(2016). These queueing models are based on a ﬁrst come ﬁrst served regime which is suitable
in scenarios where each arriving request is served separately, whether the request represents a
single passenger or a group of passengers. However, these models fail to accurately represent
the dynamics of mobility services that apply ride-sharing. That is, cases where several pas-
sengers arriving at different times and sometimes at different stations may be served together.
In particular, passengers are not necessarily served according to their order of arrival, but
according to the compatibility of their requested destinations. To better approximate the wait-
ing times in such cases a different approach is needed, such as polling systems with batch
service, especially the so-called ’Israeli Queue’, originally deﬁned in Boxma et al. (2008).
Israeli queue represents a seniority-based multi-queue polling system with a single server
and batch service. In particular, the next queue to be served by the server is the one with
the most senior customer (the customer who has been waiting for the longest time among
all present customers). This model was extended by multiple following studies. Perel and
Yechiali (2013) studied a Markovian single-server preemptive priority queue, in which the
high priority customers form a regular M/M/1 queue, while low priority customers form an
unlimited-size batch service Israeli queue with at most N groups, with a Geometric group-
joining policy. Perel and Yechiali (2014a) extended the Israeli queue model to the case where
there is no bound on the number of different groups that can be present simultaneously in the

All of the above-mentioned studies of the Israeli Queue assumed an inﬁnite-size batch
service. That is, when the server begins serving a new group, having the most senior customer
in the queue, it is assumed to serve at the same time all other customers in the queue belong-
ing to that group. However, the vehicles in most ride-sharing services (cars, elevators) are
typically characterized by small passenger capacities. Hence, this assumption may become
impractical in many cases. To answer this gap in the literature, we deﬁne and analyze in this
paper the Israeli Queue with a capacitated server (IQCS). This queue operates according to
the Israeli queue regime. However, in contrast to previous studies, only a limited number of
customers can be served in a single service time. While this limitation aligns with real-life
scenarios to a greater extent, it adds complexity to the modeling and to the analysis of the
corresponding queueing system.
Recently, Ananthanarayanan et al. (2022) studied an elevator system operating under the
restrictions of COVID-19 and social distancing requirement. The model in Ananthanarayanan
et al. (2022) considers N elevators, each with a capacity of C passengers. In one scenario,
arriving passengers were grouped into the same elevator if they shared the same destination
ﬂoor (a practice known as cohorting). In a second setting, the authors suggest a queue splitting
policy, which creates different queues to different groups of nearby ﬂoors. That is, in both
settings, passengers are served in a batch, to different destinations (ﬂoors), with limited batch
size. However, the system dynamics and the performance measures in Ananthanarayanan et
al. (2022) predominantly determined through simulation. In contrast, our current paper offers
a comprehensive analytical solution for the proposed models, in addition to simulation results.
The contributions of this paper are as follows. First, we formally deﬁne the IQCS, and
present the complexities involved in formulating an accurate mathematical model that con-
siders all of the IQCS characteristics. Second, we present and analyze an alternative queueing
model which approximates the IQCS. Third, we create an ad-hoc simulation model and use
it to numerically compare several variants of the approximate model to the IQCS. The results
of such numerical analysis allow us to underline settings in which each variant is favorable. In
addition, we compare these queues to the Israeli queue and standard ﬁrst-come-ﬁrst served
regime. The results highlight the importance of applying the Israeli queue regime while
modeling ride-sharing services and further emphasizes the impact of capacity limitations.
The remaining of this paper is organized as follows: in Sect.2 we introduce the IQCS
and discuss modeling challenges. In Sect.3, we formulate the approximate queuing model,
and analyze it in Sect.4 via Matrix Geometric methods. In Section 5 we present a numerical
study and ﬁnally, is Sect.6, we provide concluding remarks.
2 The Israeli queue with a capacitated server
Consider a shuttle service system, in which each shuttle has the capacity to accommodate
up to C passengers who are all headed to one of N different destinations. Both C and N

are predetermined ﬁnite constants. Passengers arrive at the system according to a Poisson
process with rate λ. Once they arrive, they board a shuttle destined for location i (where
i = 1, 2, ..., N) with equal probability of 1
N . A single dispatcher (server) releases the shuttles,
according to the seniority of the ﬁrst passenger to enter the shuttle. That is, the shuttle
dispatched next is the one that carries the passenger who has been waiting the longest. Note
that also non-full shuttles may be dispatched, according to the seniority-based service policy.
Dispatching time is Exponentially distributed with rate μ. It is important to emphasize that
the current model extends the Israeli queue regime, originally presented in Boxma et al.
(2008), by introducing the constraint of a ﬁnite capacity for each batch being served. That
is, at most C customers (passengers) of the same group (destination) can be served within a
single service duration.
Although we assume Poisson arrivals and Exponential service times, the service regime
based on the passenger’s seniority introduces an intricate challenge when endeavoring to
formulate the model as a mathematically tractable one. The modeling difﬁculty arises from
the necessity to monitor not only the number of occupied (non-empty) shuttles, but also the
number of passengers in each shuttle, as well as to account for the sequence of passenger
arrivals and their seniority within the system.
Hence, the task of formulating and analyzing the IQCS is unresolved, and we leave it as
an open question. Consequently, the next section introduces an alternative model for approx-
imating the IQCS, presenting three variations. This alternative model can be formulated as
a QBD, it is mathematically tractable and can be studied using Matrix Geometric Analysis
(MGA). The effectiveness of the approximation model is evaluated and detailed in Sect.5.
3 An approximate model for the IQCS
3.1 Model description
Consider a shuttle service system, serving passengers traveling to only one of N different
destinations. Each shuttle can carry (serve) at most C passengers in a single ride, where C is a
known ﬁnite constant. Passengers arrive at the system according to a Poisson process with rate
λ, and each customer is of type (destination) i, i = 1, 2, ..., N with probability 1
N . A single
dispatcher (server) releases the shuttles, where dispatching time is Exponentially distributed
with rate μ. Passengers enter the waiting shuttles according to their destinations (each shuttle
is dispatched to a single destination). Once a shuttle is full (i.e. with C passengers), it waits in a
queue with all other full shuttles. The dispatcher sends the full shuttles to their corresponding
destinations according to FCFS order. If there are no full shuttles, then the next shuttle to
be sent by the server is chosen according to one of the 3 following policies: (1) Shuttle
with highest number of passengers (SHNP), i.e., the shuttle having the largest amount of
passengers on it, where in case of a tie between several shuttles, one of them is chosen
randomly; (2) Random shuttle (RS), i.e., a shuttle is chosen randomly among all non-empty
shuttles; (3) Random passenger (RP), where a passenger is chosen randomly, and his/her
shuttle is the next one to be dispatched by the server.
Note that the suggested approximation simpliﬁes the complexity of the IQCS model, while
still capturing its essential dynamics and maintaining key characteristics, i.e. the number of
destinations N, the capacity of a shuttle C, and arrival and service rates. In fact, by prioritizing
full shuttles (as in the approximation), the system effectively models the seniority of passen-
gers. Passengers in a full shuttle likely include those who have been waiting for a longer time.
123

Annals of Operations Research
Thus, dispatching full shuttles ﬁrst serves as a proxy for seniority-based service. Although
the approximation model does not directly track the arrival times of individual passengers (as
the actual seniority-based rule would), the strategy of dispatching full shuttles ﬁrst achieves
a similar effect because passengers in these shuttles are likely to have higher waiting times.
Furthermore, the policy of sending full shuttles ﬁrst is analytically more tractable, than track-
ing each passenger’s arrival time and maintaining a true seniority order. It provides a practical
approximation to a seniority-based policy while reducing the computational and analytical
complexities. Though sending full shuttles ﬁrst approximates the seniority effect, it is not
a perfect representation. There could be scenarios where a non-full shuttle has passengers
who have been waiting longer than some passengers in a full shuttle. However, the numerical
results presented in Sect.5 demonstrate that the approximation closely aligns with the IQCS
model.
We formulate the resulting queueing system as a QBD process, and provide an analytic
solution for its steady-state behavior. Furthermore, using simulation, as well as exact analysis,
we compare between various performance measures according to the three suggested policies.

5 Numerical study
In this section, we present the outcomes derived from a simulation-based numerical exper-
iment. This experiment serves a dual objective. Primarily, it aims to assess the precision
of the approximate model for the IQCS, necessitating a thorough comparison between the
IQCS and the approximate model within a shared modeling framework. Secondly, it aims to
examine the impact of the server capacity on system performance, speciﬁcally, the number
of passengers in the system. This analysis is instrumental in highlighting the criticality of
representing server capacity, particularly when it exerts a constraining impact on the system.
The event-based simulation model we have implemented incorporates various setting char-
acteristics as input parameters, including the passenger arrival rate (λ), the shuttle dispatching
rate (μ), the capacity of the shuttles (C), the number of different destinations (N) and the
shuttle selection policy. Appendix B provides a high-level overview of the simulation model
and brieﬂy discusses key implementation and computational aspects. It’s important to note
that setting the shuttle capacity to inﬁnity effectively simulates the Israeli Queue. To reduce
the variance of the simulation results, we run each setting over a sequence of 10 million
passenger arrivals. Additionally, to ensure a fair comparison between different settings, we
employ the common random numbers technique to further reduce variance in the simulation
outcomes.

a model, customers arrive at the system according to a Poisson process with rate λ. A single
server can process partial batches up to a maximum size of C. This implies that the server
attends to customers in groups of size C, unless the number of customers in the system is
less than C. In the latter case, the server initiates service for those customers, while new
arrivals immediately join service, until reaching the limit of C. The entire batch completes
its service, independent of the batch’s size, as well as its member’s entry time into service.

6 Conclusions
In this paper we have introduced the Israeli Queue with a Capacitated Server as a frame-
work for modeling the dynamics of a single station within ridesharing services, such as
elevator systems and ride-pooling, where shuttles operate with ﬁnite and binding capacity.
Accurately capturing the dynamics of an individual station holds the potential to facilitate a
comprehensive system analysis through decomposition into multiple stations. Thus, a closed
analytical analysis of the IQCS holds the potential for various applications. We present an
approximate model for this queueing regime, distinguishing it from the IQCS by its priori-
tization of full shuttles as opposed to the IQCS’s focus on the shuttle with the most senior
passenger. Additionally, we explore three shuttle selection policies applicable when no full
shuttles are awaiting service. These policies involve selecting the shuttle with the highest
number of waiting passengers, opting for a random shuttle, or choosing a random passenger
and serving their shuttle. We conduct an analytical analysis of the approximate model using
the matrix geometric method. In addition, in order to compare the approximate model to
the IQCS under the same modeling framework, we have implemented an ad-hoc simulation
model. The results of the two models are compared and are shown to be almost identical,
thus validating the accuracy of the simulation model.
UponcomparingtheapproximatemodelwiththeIQCS,itbecomesevidentthattherandom
shuttleselectionpoliciesofferamoreaccuraterepresentationoftheIQCS’sperformance.This
can be explained by the fact that systematically selecting the shuttles that are most occupied
deviates too much from the IQCS. Recalling that in the IQCS, in some cases, the shuttles
with the most senior passenger do not necessarily harbor the highest number of passengers
on-board. Overall, the approximate model is shown to be quite accurate in representing the
IQCS. However, the remaining gap is not negligible. The challenge of precisely and efﬁciently
123

Annals of Operations Research
modeling the IQCS is still an open question. By presenting the model and its approximations,
we aim to highlight this analytical challenge within the Operations Research community. Our
intent is to encourage additional research on this distinctive queueing model, recognizing its
potential applicability across various domains.

Appendix B Simulation model
To replicate the dynamics of the IQCS and the proposed approximations, we have developed
an event-based simulation framework. This appendix provides a high-level overview of the
model and brieﬂy discusses key implementation and computational aspects.
The event-based simulation framework handles two types of events: passenger arrivals and
service completions (shuttle dispatches). The simulation takes as input the shuttle capacity,
number of classes (destinations), arrival rate, and service (dispatch) rate. An array of lists,
corresponding to the number of destinations, is initialized, with each list maintaining a time-
ordered record of passenger arrival times for a speciﬁc destination.
123

Annals of Operations Research
When a passenger arrival event occurs, the passenger’s destination is drawn based on a
given distribution. In this paper, we focus on equal-sized classes, using a uniform distribution
to assign passengers to destinations. The passenger’s arrival time is then appended to the
corresponding destination list. After that, an inter-arrival time is drawn, and a new arrival
event is added to the event list.
For service completion events, the destination to which a shuttle is dispatched is deter-
mined. In the IQCS model, the destination is selected based on the earliest arrival time across
all class lists. In the approximation models, if any class has more passengers waiting than
the shuttle’s capacity, a destination is randomly selected from this group. If no destination
exceeds the shuttle capacity, the selection follows the approximation policy (SHNP, RS, RP).
Once a destination is chosen, if it has fewer passengers than the shuttle capacity, the entire
list is cleared. Otherwise, the ﬁrst shuttle-capacity entries are removed. Finally, the dispatch
duration for the next shuttle is drawn, and a new service completion event is added to the
event list.
The simulation tracks the total waiting time of all passengers and the total number of
passengers served. At the end of the simulation, these values are used to calculate key perfor-
mance metrics, such as the average waiting time, the average number of passengers waiting,
and the average number of shuttles waiting.
Since the queuing model provides a steady-state estimation, we adopt a non-terminating
simulation approach. Speciﬁcally, a single long run of the simulation is used to generate
evaluations. The system starts with no passengers waiting, so to minimize bias from initial
conditions, we deﬁne a warm-up period and begin accumulating data only after this period
ends. In the experiments presented, the simulation was terminated after 10 million passenger
arrivals, with the ﬁrst 10,000 arrivals excluded to allow for warm-up.
The simulation was implemented in MATLAB and run on an Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-8550U
CPU @ 1.80 GHz with 24 GB of RAM. While no special emphasis was placed on optimizing
run times, for the N = 4, C = 4 settings with 10 million passenger arrivals, the simulation
took between 2 and 8min per run. This time could be signiﬁcantly reduced using a faster
programming language or fewer passenger arrivals. In fact, with 1 million passenger arrivals,
we observed similar results, with each setting running in just a few seconds to less than a
minute. The MATLAB code is available from the authors upon request.
