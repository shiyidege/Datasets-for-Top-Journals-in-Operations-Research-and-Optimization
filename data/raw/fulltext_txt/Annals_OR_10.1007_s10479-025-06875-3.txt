Abstract
We consider a two-stage distributionally robust simple integer recourse (DR-SIR) model 
with a mean-MAD ambiguity set. By leveraging infinite-dimensional linear programming 
duality and complementary slackness, we identify the worst-case distribution for every 
first-stage decision. These distributions turn out to be discrete with at most three realiza­
tions. Consequently, we are able to derive an expression for the worst-case expected cost 
function, which we denote as the DR-SIR function. This expression depends on the first-
stage decision, the value of the mean absolute deviation, and several other conditions. In 
particular, depending on the specific case, the DR-SIR function is discontinuous, but may 
be linear or hyperbolic on parts of its domain. Numerical experiments show that the DR-
SIR function is typically larger for larger values of the mean absolute deviation. Moreover, 
we find that DRO may have a significant convexifying effect on the second-stage value 
function, in particular for medium values of the mean absolute deviation.
Keywords  Distributionally robust optimization · Stochastic integer programming · Two-
stage recourse model · Mean-MAD ambiguity set

When the ambiguity set P consists of only a single probability distribution, meaning that 
the exact distribution of the random parameters ξ is known, then our optimization problem 
(1) reduces to a standard two-stage stochastic mixed-integer program (SMIP), see, e.g., 
Birge and Louveaux (2011), Shapiro et al. (2021), and Klein Haneveld et al. (2020). Such 
problems have many applications in fields such as energy (Nowak & Römisch, 2000), trans­
portation (Barbarosoǧlu & Arda, 2004), and healthcare (Zhang et al., 2021). In many such 
applications, however, it is not realistic to assume that the probability distribution is exactly 
known due to limited data. In such cases, we can use DRO instead, where such approach 
does not minimize the expected total cost, but the worst-case expected costs over all poten­
tially true probability distributions, modelled by the ambiguity set P. Several types of ambi­
guity sets exist, including, e.g., (i) moment-based ambiguity sets (Shapiro & Ahmed, 2004; 
Yu & Shen, 2022), (ii) ambiguity sets based on the Wassertein distance (Kuhn et al., 2019) 
and (iii) based on ϕ-divergences (Bayraksan & Love, 2015).
When the second-stage problem contains only continuous decision variables, then the 
second-stage value function v(ξ, x) is known to be convex and the corresponding DRO 
problem is therefore computationally tractable. In contrast, when the second-stage problem 
also contains integer decision variables, then significant computational challenges remain. 
Previous work on two-stage mixed-integer DRO problems include Duque et al. (2022), who 
study general optimal transport distances and propose a computationally tractable algo­
rithm, and Bansal et al. (2018), who presents a decomposition algorithm for a two-stage 
distributionally robust mixed integer program. Moreover, Hanasusanto et al. (2016) study 
two-stage distributionally robust binary programs with polyhedral moment information for 
which they find that the problems can be reformulated as mixed-integer programs of poly­
nomial size if only the cost parameters are uncertain. Delage and Saif (2022) shows that a 
randomized strategy may be beneficial for two-stage mixed-integer DRO problems. Such 
a randomized strategy works since the second-stage mixed-integer value function is non-
convex. There are extensive studies that focus on convex approximations of the second-
stage cost function. One of the contributions of Hanasusanto and Kuhn (2018) is to present 
an approximation for two-stage distributionally robust linear program if the ambiguity set 
constitutes a 2-Wasserstein ball centered at a discrete distribution. Similarly in stochastic 
integer programming, there are extensive studies that focus on convex approximation for 
the second-stage problem, e.g., Romeijnders et al. (2016) and van Beesten and Romeijnders 
(2020). Finally, van Beesten et al. (2024) introduces a pragmatic approach to mixed-integer 
DRO which guarantees that the resulting model is convex.
Many of the aforementioned studies take an algorithmic approach to solve mixed-integer 
DRO, but do not study the impact that the interaction of having both DRO and integer deci­

Stochastic integer programs have a wide range of applications. For example, Kim and

Motivated by the practicality of mixed-integer DRO problems, we investigate the inter­
action between DRO and integer deicision variables for SIR models. In particular, the one-
sided version of such models is obtained by setting W in (2) equal to the identity matrix, and 
Y = Zn2

into properties and algorithms of stochastic integer programs, see, e.g., Louveaux and van 
der Vlerk (1993) and Klein Haneveld et al. (2006). In this paper we aim to do the same for 
DR-SIR models. In particular, we extend the work of Xie and Ahmed (2018), who inves­
tigate DR-SIR models where the ambiguity set contains all probability distributions with 
known support and mean, by assuming that in addition also the mean-absolute deviation 
(MAD) is known.
MAD is a widely used dispersion measure in the field of optimization (Ben-Tal & Hoch­
man, 1972) and has several computational advantages over other dispersion measures such 
as the standard deviation, see, e.g., Postek et al. (2018) and Postek et al. (2019). By adding 
information about the MADs of the random parameters to our ambiguity set in the model, 
we obtain a less conservative model than Xie and Ahmed (2018). Moreover, we numerically 
show that in specific cases, the DR-SIR function under the mean-MAD ambiguity set has a 
significant convexifying effect on the non-convex second-stage value function.
It turns out that adding the MAD to the ambiguity set leads to a much wider range 
of optimal worst-case distributions than in Xie and Ahmed (2018). Nevertheless, exploit­
ing infinite-dimensional LP duality and complementary slackness, we are able to derive all 
worst-case distributions analytically, and consequently we obtain an analytic expression for 
the DR-SIR function, i.e., the worst-case expected second-stage costs. These worst-case 
distributions are all discrete, with at most three possible outcomes. Specifically, they are 
three-point distributions that may vary depending on the first-stage decision and the value 
of the MAD. Using this, we show that the DR-SIR function is discontinuous, but may be 
linear or hyperbolic on parts of its domain.
Summarizing, our main contributions are as follows.
 
●
We derive an exact analytical expression for DR-SIR functions with a mean-MAD am­
biguity set, and we derive the corresponding worst-case probability distributions.
 
●
We prove that the worst-case probability distributions corresponding to DR-SIR func­
tions under a mean-MAD ambiguity set are at most three-point distributions.
 
●
We show that as the MAD increases, the probability mass of the worst-case distribution 
increases at its end points and decreases on its intermediate point.

4  Conclusion
We consider a two-stage distributionally robust simple integer recourse (DR-SIR) model 
with a mean-MAD ambiguity set. Using infinite-dimensional linear programming duality 
and complementary slackness, we are able to determine optimal primal and dual solutions 
for the inner supremization problem where we optimize over all probability distributions 
with a given support, mean, and MAD. The worst-case probability distributions that we 
obtain turn out to be three-point distributions for which the realization may be different 
depending on the value of the first-stage decision. Consequently, we are able to derive an 
expression for the worst-case expected cost function, which we denote as the DR-SIR func­
tion. This expression depends on the first-stage decision, the value of the mean absolute 
deviation, and several other conditions. In particular, the DR-SIR function is discontinuous, 
but may be linear or hyperbolic on parts of its domain. Numerical experiments show that 
the DR-SIR function is typically larger for larger values of the mean absolute deviation. 
Moreover, we find that DRO may have a significant convexifying effect on the second-stage 
value function, in particular for medium values of the mean absolute deviation.
Future research directions include analyzing the DR-SIR function for different types of ambi­
guity sets, which for example may include more or different types of generalized moments.

DRO problems, with DR-SIR problems with mean-MAD ambiguity sets and medium values of 
the MAD as special case.
