Abstract
This paper analyzes how the brand company chooses to outsource customer acquisition and
retention to the service supplier who is either risk neutral or averse when the customers
can not only compare the marketing efforts on acquisition and retention but also have a
probability not to respond to any effort. We examine four outsourcing strategies: Acquisi-
tion Outsource, Retention Outsource, Two-Service Combined Outsource and Two-Service
Separated Outsource. For each outsourcing strategy, we establish the incentive contracts,
determine the resulted marketing efforts, and evaluate the optimal expected proÔ¨Āt for the
brand company. Our main results reveal the signiÔ¨Ācant impacts of both the customer effort
comparison and the service provider‚Äôs risk preference. Customer effort comparison may help
the brand company to offer better incentives to the service supplier without hurting his own
proÔ¨Ātability. The service supplier‚Äôs risk preference impacts on the brand company‚Äôs choice
of outsourcing strategy. Through numerical study, we also examine how ignoring customer
efforts comparison affects the brand company‚Äôs proÔ¨Ātability and the service supplier‚Äôs utility,
as well as the combined inÔ¨āuence of cost efÔ¨Āciency and customer comparison efforts on the
brand company.

1 Introduction
In the e-Commerce market era, the Internet forces many companies to charge nearly the same
price for similar or identical products (Boyaci & Gallego, 2004). As a result, the competition
among companies has shifted from product price to supply chain management centered on
customer service (Ellram, 1991). Acquiring new customers and retaining old customers are
twomajorcomponentsofcustomerservicesinmarketingactivities,buthowtobalancethetwo
is always a challenge (Furman et al., 2021). Outsourcing customer acquisition and retention
to service suppliers based on supply chain management can help companies save operation
costs and improve efÔ¨Āciency. Though outsourcing can help companies operate customer
service at a lower cost, there are some operational risks in supply chain management. For
example, Dell‚Äôs decision to outsource customer retention to India was considered a failure
because the accent of the service suppliers led to a decline in customer satisfaction even as
market share rose.
In addition to outsourcing risk in service supply chain, the efÔ¨Āciency of customer acqui-
sition and retention is also affected by consumer effort comparison, which means that
consumers may compare the marketing efforts of customer acquisition and customer retention
before purchasing. To attract new customers, credit card companies often use incentives such
as cash back and no annual card fee. However, similar incentives are not typically extended
to existing customers, who may even face higher fees. This disparity can lead customers to
feel undervalued, prompting them to switch to other credit card providers, especially when
their annual fees are due for renewal. Similarly, new users of software subscription ser-
vices frequently receive discounts, free trials, or a free Ô¨Ārst month as promotional offers.
However, once the subscription period ends, existing customers rarely receive comparable
renewal incentives. This difference may cause customers to perceive a decline in the com-
pany‚Äôs attention to their needs, thereby diminishing their interest in continuing using the
service. However, little research has been done to accurately capture the presence of effort
comparison. With the popularity of social media, it is now easier than ever for consumers to
discover differentiated discounts that companies offer to customer groups (Newman et al.,
2019). New customers are often offered lower prices, such as in cable companies or banks.
But for customers who already have been served by the providers, these promotions will
create an unsatisfying reputation. In fact, customer acquisition affects not only potential cus-
tomers but also existing customers (Lhoest-Snoeck et al., 2014); customer acquisition and
retention are not two separate activities that inÔ¨āuence the other‚Äôs output (Thomas, 2001). The
conÔ¨āict between customer acquisition and retention caused by effort comparison becomes a
challenge given customer service is outsourced to third-party service suppliers.
In order to better reconcile the conÔ¨āict between customer acquisition and customer reten-
tion caused by consumer effort comparison, it is necessary to take effort comparison into
account in the structure of service supply chain. In this research, we develop four models
with effort comparison to represent different service supply chain structures, namely, acquisi-
tion outsource to one supplier (AO), retention outsource to one supplier (RO), and two-service
combined outsource to one supplier (TCO) and two-service separated outsource to two sup-
pliers (TSO). Furthermore, using no outsourcing (NO) as a reference, we assess whether the
brand company can beneÔ¨Āt from outsourcing. Consumer effort comparison will affect the
demand function of new and old customers. Since the risk attitude will inÔ¨āuence marketing
effort input, we consider a service supplier is risk neutral or risk averse, respectively. This
paper expands the research scope of the trade-off between customer acquisition and customer
retention, and provides answers to the following research questions: (a) how does customer

effort comparison affect the operations of the brand company and the service supplier under
different outsourcing strategies? (b) which outsourcing strategy is the best, given customer

outsourcing always beneÔ¨Ācial to the brand company compared to not outsource? Would the
situation change if the service supplier has an effort cost advantage? and (d) can the brand
company and the service supplier ignore the customer effort comparison?

2 Literature review
In early research, Slade (1996) demonstrates that conÔ¨āicts may exist between customer
acquisition and retention. Thomas (2001) proposes a model approach that connects customer
acquisitionwithretention.ParkandFader(2004)empiricallyexploretheinteractionsbetween
customer acquisition and retention, referring to them as ‚Äėlinked propensities‚Äô. Lhoest-Snoeck
et al. (2014) examine the impact of acquisition activities with price promotions on existing
customer‚Äôs retention intentions and customer long-term value. Some studies discuss the
link of customer acquisition and retention from the perspective of resource balance (e.g.,
Ovchinnikov et al., 2014; Williams, 2015; Af√®che et al., 2017). Based on the work of Blattberg
and Deighton (BD), Tsao (2013) introduces market share growth to explore the link between
customeracquisitionandretention.KooshaandAlbadvi(2015)developamodelforallocation
of marketing budgets to customer acquisition and retention spending based on both decision
calculus. However, existing literatures about the link are mainly discussed within the brand
company itself but little research handles it in a principal-agent context. Dong et al. (2011)
are the Ô¨Ārst to use a principal-agent framework to investigate interactions between customer
acquisition and retention, and capture the negative effect of acquisition on retention. Shi
et al. (2021) studied Ô¨Ārms‚Äô incentive design decisions when outsourcing online marketing
and operations to professional external and internal service providers within a principal-agent
framework. Wang et al. (2022) extended the principal-agent incentive model by considering
the two-stage decision-making of customer acquisition and retention, exploring the scenarios
where agents are risk-neutral and risk-averse respectively. However, Shi et al. (2021) and
Wang et al. (2022) focused on incentive mechanism design, while overlooking the conÔ¨āicting
relationship between customer acquisition and retention.
Another stream of research employs a trade-off model to study strategies of customer
acquisition and retention, which mainly involves marketing spending strategy (e.g., Pfeifer,
2005; Ditkowski, 2008; Homburg, 2009; Williams, 2015). King et al. (2016) establish a
dynamic model based on the cash constraint of total expected expenditure to study the allo-
cation of resources in acquisition and retention. Rhouma and Zaccour (2018), differently,
propose a new dynamic model‚ÄĒthe diffusion model, for subscription services and estimate
the parameters of the model using the market data of two well-known companies in the
telecommunications industry. However, their focus is on centralization instead of decentral-
ization, i.e. the principal-agent model. Dong et al. (2011) investigate how the brand company
make a decision between implementing the acquisition task itself (i.e. direct selling) and
transferring it to the service supplier (i.e. delegation). The most recent literature on customer

which explores the interaction behavior of two kinds of customers (new and returning) in
a preferential attachment network. Also, a small number of papers address human resource

The third relevant stream of literature is on incentive compensation. Economic literature
has long established that incentive mechanisms are effective tools for mitigating the moral
hazard problem (e.g., Baker et al., 1988; Beatty & Zajac, 1994). The existing research can
be broadly divided into two categories: incentive mechanism design (e.g., DeHoratius &
Raman, 2007; Jerath et al., 2017) and sales force compensation (e.g., Jain, 2012). In recent
years, a growing body of work has revisited the classic problem of designing linear contracts
under uncertainty and behavioral heterogeneity. For example, Yu and Kong (2020) shows that
linear contracts remain optimal when Ô¨Ārms face ambiguity about the agent‚Äôs effort-output
relationship, providing a robust foundation for their widespread use in practice. Similarly,
Kambhampati et al. (2025) analytically prove that linear contracts are robust even when the
principal knows only a subset of the agent‚Äôs possible actions, reafÔ¨Ārming their theoretical sim-
plicity and practical effectiveness. Complementing this line of research, Wang et al., (2025a,
2025b) study standardized linear incentive contracts under agent heterogeneity, considering
differences in ability, cost of effort, and risk aversion. They Ô¨Ānd that contract effectiveness
depends strongly on aligning the slope of the linear incentive with agent characteristics.
Gonz√°lez-Jim√©nez (2024) further extend this discussion by examining how agents‚Äô distorted
perception of risk probabilities inÔ¨āuences optimal contract design, showing that introduc-
ing controlled risk into compensation may improve motivation and effort provision. Beyond
individual incentives, Claveria-Mayol et al. (2024) explore peer effects in team-based envi-
ronments, identifying how optimal wage schemes depend on organizational structure and
the interdependence of agents‚Äô performance. Given that multiple scholars have demonstrated
the robustness of linear contracts, our paper continues to adopt linear contracts and explores
outsourcing strategies and incentive mechanism design by considering the impact of the
customer comparison effect.
3 The model
We use a principle-agent model to study how the brand company should outsource customer
acquisition and retention, the two major tasks of the customer service. The brand company
that manufactures products is the principle with neutral risk preference. The customer service
supplier is the agent who can be either risk neutral or risk averse. In this principal-agent
framework setting, the brand company may pick up one of four strategies:
(I)
Acquisition Outsource (AO) strategy. The brand company only addresses the customer
retention issue and let the service supplier target customer acquisition.
(II)
Retention Outsource (RO) strategy. The brand company is in charge of acquiring new
customers, while the service supplier controls customer retention activities.
(III)
Two-Service Combined Outsource (TCO) strategy. The brand company hires one ser-
vice supplier to attract new customers and manage customer retention.
123

Annals of Operations Research
(IV) Two-Service Separated Outsource (TSO) strategy. The brand company hires two ser-
vice suppliers and let one do the customer acquisition and the other do the customer
retention.
We also consider a base setting where the brand company runs the acquisition and retention
activities by itself. This is named as No Outsource (NO) strategy.
3.1 Customer demand function
The brand company‚Äôs customers pool consists of three groups: the existing loyal customers
who have bought multiple times and will buy again if no external impact added; the emerging
customers who have bought once and are likely to be cultivated the loyal customers; and the
potential customers who have not bought before but may become the emerging customers
due to some acquisition efforts. Without acquisition or retention efforts, the brand company‚Äôs
customer demand is completely determined by the existing loyal customers, who have the
size of n. However, the acquisition efforts and the retention efforts will cause two different
adjustments.
The Ô¨Ārst one is the newly acquired customers from the potential customers. We name
it the acquisition demand Da, which is driven by the acquisition effort ea. However, the
outcomes of such effort are not guaranteed. For simplicity, it is written as Da  ea + ŌĶa,
where ŌĶa is a normally distributed random variable, with mean of 0 and standard deviation of
ŌÉa, representing the uncertainties in the effort results (Giri et al., 2021; Hauser et al., 1994;
Zheng et al., 2022).
The second variant is caused by the retention efforts er and its relativity to the acquisition
efforts (ea ‚ąíer). The existing customers (loyal and emerging) may view such efforts dis-
crepancy (ea ‚ąíer) as a sign of how the brand company valuates the customer loyalty. The
superior retention effort (i.e.,ea ‚ąíer < 0) enhances the loyalty of the previous customers,
while the over-emphasized acquisition activities (i.e.,ea ‚ąíer > 0) discourages them from
buying again. We model the retention demand as Dr  er ‚ąíőĽ(ea ‚ąíer) + ŌĶr, where őĽ is a

7 Conclusion
In this study, we propose a one-period model consisting of a brand company and a service
supplier to investigate how the brand company adjusts its service outsourcing strategy to
absorb the effort comparison effect. By introducing direct interactions between customer
acquisition and retention in our model, we speciÔ¨Ācally examine four outsource scenarios:
Acquisition Outsource (AO), Retention Outsource (RO), Two-Service Combined Outsource
(TCO) and Two-Service Separated Outsource (TSO). Moreover, we present a case in which
the service supplier is risk averse in addition to the traditional risk-neutral setting.
Our main Ô¨Āndings indicate that the customer effort comparison effect and the service sup-
plier‚Äôs risk preference signiÔ¨Ācantly inÔ¨āuence operations and decision-making. First, under

Second, the optimal outsourcing strategy is inÔ¨āuenced by risk preference. When the sup-
