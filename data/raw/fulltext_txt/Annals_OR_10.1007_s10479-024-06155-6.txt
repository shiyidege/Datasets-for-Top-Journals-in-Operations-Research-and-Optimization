Abstract
The need to obtain ﬁnancial funds to pursue public utility investments implies the generation
of Public-Private Partnership (PPP) projects. The PPP framework can involve risk-sharing
mechanisms between public administration and private sector to encourage private investors
to fund these projects. However, these risk-sharing mechanisms, such as Minimum Revenue
Guarantee or Revenue Cap (RC), could generate opportunistic behaviors. For this reason,
we can set this problem as a game in which government and private investors would act as
players. This paper proposes a mathematical model to evaluate the PPP projects through a
combination of Game Theory (GT) and Real Options Approach (ROA). The ROA is needed
to price the uncertainty that affects PPP investments, and the GT captures the strategic
interactions between public and private sectors. A case study on a wastewater treatment
project is developed to apply the model we proposed.
Keywords PPP projects · Game theory · Risk-sharing mechanisms · Strategic interactions ·

the same time, to keep control over the public project (Walker and Smith, 1995). A quite
widespread form to implement the PPP is the Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT) approach, which

affected by various uncertainties, such as political, economic, and social unstable conditions

Thus, the PPP framework can involve risk-sharing mechanisms between public administra-
tion and private ﬁrms to encourage private investors to fund these projects. Pursuing a PPP
project requires huge capital that could discourage the private sector from investing. Using a
concession agreement implies that the government would transfer some project risks to the
concessionaire (private sector). To mitigate the risks for private investors, the government
can adopt subsidies, guarantees, or other incentive schemes (Klein, 1997). Some examples
of risk-sharing mechanisms to protect the private sector are the non-competition rights and
the Minimum Revenue Guarantee (MRG). The non-competition right implies that the gov-
ernment ensures the absence of similar projects in the same area in the future that can be
anticipated (Liu et al., 2014). The MRG consists in ensuring a minimum amount of revenues
for private investors by the government if the net revenues go below the level of minimum
guaranteed net revenues (Chiara et al., 2007).
An example of a risk-sharing mechanism to protect the public sector is the Revenue Cap
(RC). The RC is the option held by the government, thanks to which it can obtain a part of the
excess revenues of the private sector when the latter exceeds the expected ones (Wang and
Liu, 2015). This allows to prevent natural monopoly situations that can harm public interest
(Wang and Liu, 2015).
The risk-sharing mechanisms in PPP projects are adequately evaluated through the Real
Options Approach (ROA). The ROA is a methodology able to include managerial ﬂexibility
in the project by considering the investment decision as “options” (Trigeorgis, 1993). This
approach is used to evaluate uncertain projects that would otherwise be difﬁcult to evaluate
with classical methods. The uncertainty can be mitigated by allowing the investor to change
investment decisions during the project’s lifetime and by including operational ﬂexibility or
optionality in the project valuation. In fact, the classical methods such as the Net Present
Value could lead to a wrong investment decision because it does not include the optionality
in the project valuation. In this regard, Ross (1995) showed that the NPV could lead the
investor to reject an investment when it should be accepted or accept an investment when it
should be accepted. For example, a valuation methodology that ignores managerial ﬂexibility
could give negative pricing, and including managerial ﬂexibility can turn positive. Thus, the
negative results of a valuation model without ﬂexibility open the way to looking for the
needed ﬂexibility to undertake the project, designing the appropriate ﬁnancial options, and
identifying potential writers. For this reason, some projects characterized by operational
ﬂexibility need to be priced through the ROA.
It is quite useful to price the MRG and RC through the ROA since these can be viewed as
options to be exercised respectively for private investors and government. Concerning this
point, Myers (1987) and Trigeorgis and Mason (1987) explained that the ROA is recom-
mended to price investments with strategic options. For example, Carbonara et al. (2014)
developed a ROA to model the MRG by adopting the concept of fairness to design the MRG.
Ashuri et al. (2012) used the ROA to evaluate the MRG and toll revenue cap options relating
to BOT highway projects. Speciﬁcally, they considered both options as compound options
using the risk-neutral valuation. Liu and Cheah (2009) focus on applying the ROA to evalu-

The risk-sharing mechanisms, such as Minimum Revenue Guarantee (MRG) or Rev-
enue Cap (RC), could involve opportunistic behavior by private ﬁrms (Liu et al., 2016).
For instance, in the case of the RC, if the excess revenues permitted for the private sector
is too low, they can decide to maintain the revenues under the cap to avoid sharing excess
revenues with the government (Wang and Liu, 2015). Again, in the case of the MRG, the
private investor could adopt poor management performance on purpose to obtain the MRG
(Di Bari 2021 and Wang et al. 2019). On the other hand, the government could raise/lower
the thresholds of MRG and RC depending on the will to raise/lower the possibility of sub-
sidizing the private sector. Thus, the involvement of opportunistic behaviors allows the set
of PPP projects as a game where the players are government and private investors. To solve
the possible moral hazard problem of private investors, Wang et al. (2019) developed a game
theory model to encourage the private sector to avoid a bad attitude towards the government.
Medda (2007) applied the game theory to analyze the risk allocation between the public and
private sectors modeled as a bargaining process. The author modeled this bargaining process
as a ﬁnal offer arbitration game.
Therefore, combining ROA with Game Theory (GT) to evaluate the PPP projects under
risk-sharing mechanisms is necessary. This is because the ROA allows the price of the man-
agerial ﬂexibility to exercise the MRG and the RC, and, through the GT, we can include
the strategic interactions between the public and private sectors. Previous studies combined
ROA with GT (Chevalier Roignant and Trigeorgis 2011 and Di Bari and Villani 2022) to
obtain a new methodology called Option Games (OG). In this way, the OG approach would
include the managerial ﬂexibility that characterizes some investments (through the ROA) and
the strategic interactions (through GT). For example, the study of Di Bari and Villani (2022)
combines the GT with ROA to price a broadband project. Their methodology is developed in
discrete time through the binomial approach and combined with a Stackelberg competition.
The novelty of this paper is propose a mathematical model based on the combination of
GT and ROA organized in a new manner to support investment decisions in PPP projects.
Speciﬁcally, we develop a mathematical model based on ROA to evaluate PPP projects
under MRG and RC mechanisms by considering the strategic interactions between public
and private sectors in exercising the MRG and RC options. We consider an updated version
of simple ROA, the compound ROA, to include the sequential nature of PPP projects that
can be organized in a phased manner. In fact, previous studies showed that the compound
options logic is useful to price multi-stage projects (Biancardi et al., 2023). The payoffs
calculated through the compound ROA have been embedded in a matrix game to consider
the strategic behavior of government and private ﬁrms that act as two players. Differently from
the study of Di Bari and Villani (2022), the ROA is developed in a continuous time by letting
evolves underlying assets through a standard Brownian motion. Other relevant differences of
this work compared to the Di Bari and Villani (2022) are the application ﬁeld and the game
structure. In fact, this work develops a mathematical model to price PPP projects under MRG
and RC that differs completely from the study of Di Bari and Villani (2022) that applied the
OG approach to price broadband projects. Moreover, differently from Di Bari and Villani
(2022) that used a Stackelberg game, this work determines the Nash equilibrium in a game
in which players act simultaneously. We apply the methodology to evaluate a wastewater
treatment plant project. The wastewater treatment plant projects are often pursued through

results conﬁrm what (Di Bari, 2021) found in his work, where he provided a tool to evaluate
a PPP project under MRG without considering the government and private sector as players.
Di Bari (2021) conﬁrms that adopting the moral hazard for the private sector represents
an unproﬁtable choice. On the government side, the best strategy for the government is to
keep the thresholds of MRG and RC relatively low. In fact, this could represent an efﬁcient
strategy to discourage the private sector from adopting opportunistic behavior. This article
is organized as follows: Sect.2 describes the basic model setting of MRG and RC; Sect.3
provides the PPP project valuation under risks sharing mechanisms; Sect.4 sets the static
options game between private investor and government; Sect.5 provides a case study to
apply our mathematical model; Sect.6 discusses the conclusive remarks.
2 Risk-sharing mechanisms setting: minimum revenue guarantee and
revenue cap
The PPP projects involve risk-sharing mechanisms between the public and private sectors
to encourage the private sector to ﬁnance these projects and to protect the public sector
from situations that could harm public interest, such as natural monopoly. Since the risk-
sharing mechanisms in the PPP context can generate opportunistic behaviors, we consider
the government and private sector as two players. Focusing on Minimum Revenue Guarantee
(MRG) and Revenue Cap (RC), we provide a mathematical model to evaluate PPP projects
through a revisited Options Game approach adapted for PPP characteristics.
The MRG is a support mechanism through which government subsidizes the private ﬁrms
if the actual revenues are lower than expected revenues (Ashuri et al. 2012; Wang and Liu,
2015 & Di Bari 2021). In the ROA, it is quite common to consider a unique parameter
that represents the sum of the discounted cash ﬂows of the expected revenues acting as
an underlying asset (Armada et al., 2007). For this reason, we use the present value of the
discounted expected cash inﬂows (AR0), and we ﬁx a minimum threshold of the present value
of revenues (E Rmin) below which the private could request the government to be subsidized
for a percentage X ∈(0, 1). For the sake of simplicity, we deﬁne X as the percentage of both
risk-sharing mechanisms (RC and MRG). This means that X represents the percentage that
should be obtained by the private sector in the case of MRG and the percentage that should
be obtained by the government in the case of RC. Now, we proceed by setting the MRG.
This option allows for the mitigation of the risk of ﬁnancial losses. For the computation
of the European option involved in our model, we focus on the valuation of MRG and RC
at maturity T . Assuming that ARt can change during the time t ∈[t0, T ], we model the
minimum revenue guarantee M RGT for government as:

3 The PPP project valuation under risk-sharing mechanisms

projects. It is important to explain that the revenues under risk-sharing mechanisms occur in
the operating stage once the PPP investment to construct/pursue the project is made. We can
identify three sequential investments. The ﬁrst investment (K0) allows initiating the project,
including administrative and land acquisition costs. The second investment (K1) is needed to
create/build the project. The third investment (K2) is required to pursue operation activity. The
private ﬁrm makes these three investments respectively at time t0, t1 and T . These investments

5 Case study
In this section, we propose a case study to apply our model. We consider the involvement
of the PPP in creating a wastewater treatment plant; water puriﬁcation represents a relevant
activity to preserve nature since this process removes the pollutants from the wastewater.
Water pollution damages aquatic organisms and human health. Implementing wastewater
treatment projects is necessary to mitigate the negative effects of water pollution and improve
water quality. Since wastewater treatment represents an activity of public interest, the gov-
ernment can give a concession for creating a wastewater treatment plant to the private sector.
Yu et al. (2021) explained that the wastewater treatment projects would ﬁt well for the PPP
context since they require high costs, skilled workers, and a high-quality management team.
The private sector would be able to provide efﬁcient operation performance, and at the same
time, it could share the risks related to the high costs with the public sector. The usefulness
of the PPP mechanism in the wastewater ﬁeld is also conﬁrmed by Yang et al. (2017) and
Tang et al. (2021). Speciﬁcally, Yang et al. (2017) showed the feasibility of the PPP model

5.3 Discussion
As shown in Table 4, the Nash equilibrium solution consists of adopting the best possible
management performance for private investors and keeping the government’s RC and MRG
thresholds as low as possible. This means that the model suggests that investors should
avoid opportunistic behavior. In contrast, the government should not incentivize the private
sector too much to prevent the latter from practicing opportunistic behavior. In this sense,
the results are is in line with the study of Wang et al. (2019). They applied a game theory
model to analyze the mechanisms encouraging investors to cooperate with the government.
Their ﬁndings explained that to inﬂuence efﬁciently the investors’ willingness to cooperate
with the government, they should adopt incentive mechanisms. Although the mathematical
setting of our paper is different from the study of Wang et al. (2019), both aim to ﬁnd a
solution to minimize the possibility of opportunistic behavior. Adopting a good performance
increases the proﬁts for the private sector, in addition to pursuing economic beneﬁts for
the government. These results conﬁrm what was discovered by Di Bari (2021) that showed
that, for private ﬁrms, the project value without moral hazard is higher than that one with
opportunistic behavior.
Regarding the ROA, the results suggest that the private sector should accept the PPP project
since its value is positive πp = 313 160 e. This valuation embeds the uncertainty, the risk-
sharingmechanisms,andpossiblestrategicinteractionsthatcharacterizethePPPprojects.For
government the project value is πg = 126 640 e. This result suggests that the government
should accept the project since it is economically feasible. Moreover, developing a PPP
project allows the government to achieve social goals. In fact, the government’s goal should
not be to make proﬁts but to improve the social well-being of the inhabitants. Concerning
this point, Liu et al. (2016) explained this aspect widely in their work. They discussed that
the goals of the government and private sector are different: the ﬁrst aims to maximize public
interest through PPP investments, and the second aims to pursue the interest of maximizing
proﬁt returns. The proposed methodology would support the PPP investment decision for the
public and private sectors.
In fact, on the one hand, the private sector could use this mathematical model to decide
whether to invest or not in PPP projects based on the ﬁnal project valuation and on the
will to make a certain level of proﬁts. On the other hand, the government could use this
mathematical tool to decide whether to subsidize or not the PPP project based on the budget
constraints that affect the public budget and to mitigate the risks of opportunistic behavior of

the private sector. This approach can be used by the government to highlight the importance
of avoiding the opportunistic behavior of private investors to maximize the project value
under risk-sharing mechanisms. In this way, our model could encourage the best practice in
the PPP context.

private and public sectors.
Figure 1showsthatallpayoffsoftheprivatesectorgoupbyincreasingσ,t1, AR0.Conversely,

increment of the private payoffs as a consequence of increasing AR0 is not linear, and
sometimes, it presents some changes in growth rate. The payoff trend becomes ﬂatter where
AR0 evolution crosses the thresholds. So, the private sector should share excess revenues with
the government or be compensated for a huge ﬁnancial loss. The private payoffs are positively
related to γ in the absence of opportunistic behavior of the private ﬁrms and negatively related
to γ when the opportunistic behavior exists. Thus, the private sector should be discouraged
from keeping the revenues quite low to avoid sharing excess revenues with the public sector.
For speciﬁc γ values, we note that the private payoffs change by generating some peaks.
Fig.2 shows that the payoffs of government are positively related to AR0, σ for any strategic
action. By increasing the value of γ , the payoffs of government go up when private ﬁrms
do not adopt opportunistic behavior. Conversely, if the private sector practices opportunistic
behavior, the payoffs of government decreases when γ increases. Finally, when government
decides to lower the thresholds to avoid to incentivize too much the private sector, its payoffs
values go up by increasing ω. Conversely, when the government decides to keep the thresholds
very high to incentivize the private sector, the value of payoffs decreases. This phenomenon
can be explained by the fact that, by lowering the thresholds, the government reduces the
possibility of compensating the private sector through the MRG and increases the possibility
of sharing private excess revenues through the RC.
6 Conclusions
This work aims to develop a mathematical model to support investment decisions in PPP
projects. The PPP projects involve risk-sharing mechanisms that can generate opportunistic
behavior. In this paper, we price a PPP project that includes the Minimum Revenue Guarantee
(MRG) and Revenue Cap (RC). We believe that the private sector could intentionally keep
revenues low to avoid sharing extra proﬁts with the government (in the RC case). We also
considerthepossibilitythatthepublicdecision-makercouldkeeptheMRGandRCthresholds
very low to minimize the possibility of subsidizing the private sector in the MRG case and
maximize the possibility of obtaining extra proﬁts in the RC case. In this way, the losses
deriving from potential opportunistic behavior would be minimized.
To model this situation, we combine the Real Options methodology with the Game theory
to apply a methodology called the Options Game approach. On the one hand, the Real Options
methodology allows the embedding of the typical uncertainty of PPP project performance
in the project valuation through the managerial ﬂexibility assessment. On the other hand,
the game theory allows for strategic interactions between the private and public sectors by
considering them as players.

Private investors should accept the project if implementing this Options Game approach
results positively. At the same time, the government should decide to subsidize the private
sector by considering some strategic measures.
