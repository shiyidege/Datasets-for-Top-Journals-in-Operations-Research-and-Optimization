Abstract
This study develops models that predict banks’ stock price crash risk using novel machine
learning techniques. A key element of our approach is that we retrieve textual information
from ECB presidents’ speeches. To this end, we employ quarter-bank level data and various
measures for stock price crash risk, ensuring the robustness of our ﬁndings. First, we ﬁnd that
the machine learning models can generally perform better than the simple regressions. Next,
our results also suggest that textual information from the ECB president’s speeches has signif-
icant predictive power. Finally, when we jointly use textual information and macro-ﬁnancial
variables as inputs, the performance of our models is substantially increased compared to
models using a single type of input. Our empirical ﬁndings provide signiﬁcant policy implica-
tions for investors and policymakers as they can help regulators assess the ﬁnancial system’s
stability and identify any potential systemic risks, allowing them to take proactive measures
to prevent or mitigate a ﬁnancial crisis.

1 Introduction
A stock price crash risk is a possibility that a stock’s price will experience a sudden and
large decline, typically due to various reasons like market turbulence, economic downturn,
company-speciﬁc events, etc. (Jin & Myers, 2006). This paper aims to provide a novel
empirical analysis of the predictive power of textual information as an early warning signal
of banking crises in terms of banks’ stock price crash risk.
The stock price crash risk literature is introduced by Jin and Myers (2006) and has attracted
signiﬁcant academic attention since then. The essence of their theory lies in the information
asymmetry between investors and managers regarding the ﬁrm’s cash ﬂow and value, which

bad news. However, the accumulation of such news cannot continue indeﬁnitely, simulta-

Many stock price crash risk aspects have been explored in the literature, such as ﬁnancial

In general, economic sentiment can have a signiﬁcant impact on stock price crash risk, as
it refers to the general outlook and conﬁdence of consumers, businesses, and investors in the
economy and its prospects. When economic sentiment is positive, it can boost consumer and
business spending, which can lead to higher stock prices and signiﬁcantly affect stock price
crash risk. Therefore, economic sentiment is an important factor to consider when assessing
stock price crash risk. Notably, we offer evidence that textual information can signiﬁcantly
improve the prediction of bank crash risk. Second, instead of using econometric techniques to
perform our task, we utilize several machine learning models, which have several advantages
over traditional econometric methodologies (Mai et al., 2019). Hence, any improvement in
prediction accuracy is important, considering that there is still no consensus regarding the
development of an accurate method to predict crash risk.
In contrast to previous studies examining the determinants of the ﬁrm- or bank-speciﬁc
stock price crash risk, we are the ﬁrst who perform a forecasting exercise for the bank-speciﬁc
stock price crash risk. The investigation of stock price crash risk for banks is generally
considered more important than ﬁrms for several reasons. First, banks play a crucial role in
the ﬁnancial system and a stock price crash in a bank could lead to a ﬁnancial crisis and have
a cascading effect on the broader economy. Banks also typically use leverage, meaning they
borrow money to ﬁnance their operations and investments, which magniﬁes their potential
losses in a market downturn. Unlike other ﬁrms, banks are backed by deposit insurance which
makes them more attractive to customers, but also means that in case of a stock price crash,
the government might have to bail them out to protect depositors. Bank opacity is inversely
related to the disclosure of information, as the level of bank opacity decreases with the quality
of disclosure (Flannery et al., 2013; Jiang et al., 2016; Zheng, 2020). Therefore, the stock
price crash risk for banks is seen as more signiﬁcant than that of ﬁrms and requires more
attention and consideration.
We employ quarter-bank level data and various measures for stock price crash risk ensuring
the robustness of our ﬁndings. First, we ﬁnd that the machine learning models can generally
perform better than the simple regressions. Next, our results also suggest that textual infor-
mation from the ECB president’s speeches has signiﬁcant predictive power. Finally, when
we jointly use textual information and macro-ﬁnancial variables as inputs, the performance
of our models is substantially increased compared to models using a single type of input.
Forecasting banks’ stock price crash risk with textual features is even more important
because it can help investors make informed investment decisions, by providing a measure of
the potential risk associated with investing in bank stocks. Additionally, it can help regulators
assess the stability of the ﬁnancial system and identify any potential systemic risks, allowing
them to take proactive measures to prevent or mitigate a ﬁnancial crisis. Moreover, forecasting
stock price crash risk for banks based on textualinformation can also provide valuable insights
for bank managers and executives, allowing them to identify potential risks and implement

2 Literature review
The possibility of a stock market crash has lately acquired attention, with several variables
being investigated. In this section, we list the variables that prior research has examined.
The literature on the danger of a stock market fall is introduced by Jin and Myers’ (2006)
paper, which has subsequently gained considerable academic interest. The authors refer to
the knowledge asymmetry between investors and management about a company’s cash ﬂow
and value as opaqueness, which is central to their thesis. To safeguard their careers, managers
are incentivized to conceal unpleasant news. However, the buildup of such material cannot
continue indeﬁnitely, nor can all unpleasant news be immediately released. Consequently,
there is a precipitous decline in stock values, and investors experience huge losses. Similarly,
other publications examine the impact of ﬁnancial reporting and corporate disclosures. For
instance, Hutton et al. (2009) add to the determinant of opacity, but Kim et al. (2019) ﬁnd
that the probability of a crash decreases as the complexity of 10-K reports decreases.
According to earlier research, the capital market encourages managers to hold negative
information, raising the possibility of collapse risk. For instance, Callen and Fang (2015)
establish that short interest in stocks is a strong indicator of collapse risk because short sellers
may identify adverse news stockpiling, prompting them to short the stocks. Additionally,
Chang et al. demonstrate a statistically signiﬁcant and positive correlation between stock
liquidity and collapse risk.
Another body of research focuses on the CEO traits that impact crash risk. Kim et al.
(2016), for instance, establish that CEO overconﬁdence increases crash risk. Additionally,
younger CEOs tend to raise the danger of a future stock market meltdown (Andreou et al.,
2017). Also, CEOs with early-life disaster experiences lead to more incidents of crash risk,
as Chen et al. report.
Internal company governance signiﬁcantly inﬂuences the probability of a stock market
fall. Chen et al. (2017) demonstrate that the quality of internal control appears to reduce the
probability of future crash risk. According to Xu et al., excessive rewards at China’s state-
owned ﬁrms give managers with incentives to conceal bad news, resulting in a high crash
risk. An and Zhang (2013), on the other hand, emphasize the mitigating effect of devoted
institutional ownership on crash risk. Similarly, Callen and Fang demonstrate the negative
relationship between institutional investor stability and collapse risk.

Likewise, Bhargava et al. (2017) describe the moderating effect of anti-takeover regulations

Another body of research focuses on informal institutional structures that greatly increase

Even though the number of studies that study the association between accident risk and
attitude is very small, there are a few noteworthy papers. There are a few research stud-

ﬁrm-speciﬁc investor mood and the chance of a stock price drop. Yin and Tian (2017) also
demonstrate a favorable correlation between investor sentiment and crash risk. Wen et al.
(2019) assert that equities with strong retail investor attention have a reduced chance of future
price crash risk, but Anastasiou et al. (2021) argue that crisis emotion has a favorable inﬂu-
ence on the price crash risk of cryptocurrencies. Anastasiou et al. (2021) record the positive
inﬂuence of crisis sentiment on the price crash risk of cryptocurrencies, whereas Tzomakas
et al. (2023) show that banks’ stock price crash risk is likewise affected by a number of crisis
sentiment proxies in the euro area.
As can be observed from the literature cited above, the main aim of the relevant research in
the ﬁeld of stock price crashes is to provide investors with indicators or proxies that can assist
them in identifying negative news stockpiling behavior in order to protect their portfolios
from crashes. However, all of the aforementioned studies concentrate on causal inference by
examining correlations within samples. To our knowledge, no study has examined the out-of-
sample predictability of stock price crashes at the bank level. Notably, the only closely related
study to ours is of Reichmann and Reichmann (2022), who examined the predictability of
ﬁrm-speciﬁc stock price crashes using modern machine learning techniques. However, as far
as we know, this is the ﬁrst study building models to predict price crashes of bank stocks
using machine learning techniques, accompanied by textual features.

3.2 Textual methodology
3.2.1 Textual sources and pre-processing
First, we gather all the ECB president’s speeches from the ECB website from February
2008 to November 2020 on the condition that they are in the English language. This yields a
sample of 351 speeches. Second, after the retrieval of speeches, we must transform the textual
data into numerical information before inserting them as inputs to our predictive models. It
is called pre-processing and is very important in textual analysis as it could inﬂuence the
overall outcome regarding the predictive contribution of textual features (Jegadeesh & Wu,
2013; Nassirtoussi et al., 2014).
To do so, we proceed to a two-fold analysis in order to improve the quality of textual data.
The ﬁrst step is to purge each text (speech) whereby all abbreviations, generic stop words,
single letter words, numbers, and punctuation marks are removed from the analysis consistent
with pertinent literature in textual analysis (Anastasiou & Katsafados, 2023; Gandhi et al.,
2019; Katsafados et al., 2021; Loughran & McDonald, 2013). Second, we impose a minimum
occurrence threshold suggesting that words with low frequency are eliminated from the
analysis (Chen et al., 2020). More speciﬁcally, Bernabé-Moreno et al. (2020) underline that
such a ﬁltering process is crucial because it helps to limit sparsity and noise. To end up,
following Mai et al. (2019) and Katsafados et al. (2023a), we consider the 20,000 most
frequent words of ECB presidents’ speeches of the puriﬁed text.
3.2.2 Textual variables
After pre-processing, we need to proceed to the creation of quantitative textual features which
will be used as inputs in the models. In line with the vast majority of relevant literature, we
use the bag of words (BOW) approach. In fact, this approach considers each unique word
as a separate textual feature and generates a document-term matrix, where each column and
row represent a word and a speech, respectively (Loughran & McDonald, 2011). Notably,
we use the Python library, called Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK), to tokenize text into
individual words.
In our empirical setting, we compute our textual features based on two frequently used
alternative approaches: (1) the term frequency (TF), which is the raw count of words divided
by the speech’s length, and (2) the term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF). On
the one side, the TF of a speciﬁc word is practically the proportion of the word in the speech.
As mentioned in the pre-processing section, we consider only the 20,000 most frequent words
in the corpus. Thus, we calculate 20,000 TF scores, whereby these textual features are used
as inputs in the machine learning models.
On the other side, unlike the TF that treats each word with equal weight, the TF-IDF adjusts
the TF scores by assigning lower weight to textual features that appear more frequently in our
sample of ECB presidents’ speeches (Jegadeesh & Wu, 2013; Katsafados et al., 2021). Some
past studies cite that the TF-IDF weighting scheme could better estimate textual information
(Katsafados et al., 2023a; Loughran & McDonald, 2011; Mai et al., 2019). Given that t
represents a term in the document or text being analyzed, then the mathematical formula of
the TF-IDF weight of word i in the jth speech is presented in the equation as follows:

3.3 Macro-financial variables
In accordance with the past literature (among others, Hutton et al., 2009; Jin & Myers,
2006), we employ a set of control variables as stock price crash risk determinants in order to
mitigate unobserved heterogeneity. Hence, we use the following ﬁnancial variables that may
well inﬂuence euro area banks’ stock price crash risk:
• DTURN: The average monthly share turnover in quarter t minus the average monthly share
turnover in quarter t-1, where monthly share turnover is deﬁned as the monthly trading
volume divided by the total number of shares outstanding on the market.
• SIGMA: The standard deviation of the bank-speciﬁc daily return over the quarter.
• RET: The mean of the bank-speciﬁc daily returns over the quarter.
• KURT: The kurtosis of the bank-speciﬁc daily returns over the quarter.
• SIZE: The natural logarithm of the book value of assets over the quarter.
• MTBV: The ratio of the market value of equity to the book value over the quarter.
• LEV: The ratio of liabilities to the total assets over the quarter.
• ROE: The ratio of pre-tax earnings to equity over the quarter.
• NPLs: The ratio of impaired loans to total loans over the quarter.
As previous crash risk and investor sentiment literature suggest, we also consider the
effect of macroeconomic fundamentals (non-sentiment-related idiosyncratic variation). To
this end, we add to our analysis the following macroeconomic factors:
• YIELD: the 10-year government bond yield.
• LNGDP: the natural logarithm of GDP
• UNEMPL: the unemployment rate
• HICP: the Harmonized Indices of Consumer Prices.
Additionally, we also include the lagged dependent variable as an additional explanatory
variable in the forecasting models. Finally, following past crash risk studies (see, among
others, Jin & Myers, 2006; Hutton et al., 2009), we remove the effect of the outliers by
winsorizing our variables at the top and bottom 2.5%. Table 1 reports descriptive statistics
for all the variables described above.

4.1 Decision tree
Decision tree (DT), introduced by Quinlan (1986), is a supervised machine learning algo-
rithm. Kumar and Ravi (2016) mention that DT is frequently applied in many prediction
tasks and applications. In particular, it practically is a rule-based classiﬁcation system that is
organized into a tree structure (Nassirtoussi et al., 2014). Each non-terminal node represents
the test condition, whereas the leaf (or terminal) node denotes the output (Kumar & Ravi,
2016).
In fact, it generates “if–then” rules, thereby contributing to the decision-making proce-
dure. The process begins by evaluating the TF-IDF values of terms in the training dataset,
representing the frequency and importance of each term in the context of the documents

a set of ’if–then’ rules that guide the decision-making process.
For instance, a hypothetical if–then rule in the CART tree might be formulated as follows:
• If TF-IDF(term_i) > threshold_1, then proceed to Node_A.
• If TF-IDF(term_i) <  threshold_1, then proceed to Node_B.
These rules are recursively applied at each node in the CART tree until a terminal node
is reached, where a prediction regarding the likelihood of a stock price crash for a given
bank is made. The combination of various terms and their associated TF-IDF values in these
rules allows the model to capture complex patterns and relationships within the textual data,
enabling effective prediction of stock price crash risk.
Unfortunately, despite the plethora of variations of DT algorithms, most of them are merely
useful for handling classiﬁcation issues. Given that the crash risk prediction is a regression
prediction task, we adopt the classiﬁcation and regression trees (CART) model, a variation
of the decision tree model that can handle both classiﬁcation and regression problems. Fur-
thermore, CART is relevantly powerful compared to other types of DT algorithms, because it
enables real values and missing data. Finally, in such a model, the tree structure is the almost
same as in the rest of DT algorithms except for the terminal nodes that may contain either
discrete or continuous values.
Apart from its frequent use in ﬁnance-speciﬁc tasks, we chose to employ the DT model to
compare its predictive ability with the sophisticated random forest model, which is considered
the upgraded evolution of DT with promising results in relevant tasks (Katsafados et al.,
2024). To be more precise, this would allow the reader to assess whether random forest
indeed outperforms traditional machine learning models.
4.2 Support vector regression
Another standard machine learning model is the support vector machine (SVM) generated
by Vapnik (1998). In its simplest form, SVM is typically a supervised linear classiﬁer, which
creates a decision boundary that has the form of a hyperplane in the original feature space.
The training samples at the boundaries of the margin, or (when allowing ‘slack’ in the
separation) inside the margin, or on the wrong side of the hyperplane are known as support
vectors. Searching for the maximum margin hyperplane is done via quadratic programming
optimization. In our crash risk prediction task, we need to adopt the support vector regression
(SVR), which practically is a simple regression-based variation of SVM (Nassirtoussi et al.,
2014). In fact, although the optimization process behind the SVR is similar to the SVM, one
difference is that the SVR is trained on actual observed values. As is possible with SVMs
models, SVR can use non-linear kernel functions, when there are non-linearly separable data.
As a result, SVR has the ability to predict numerical property values. In this regard, the data
with the non-linear structure are projected into a higher dimensional space representation, in
which they can be more separable (Kumar & Ravi, 2016). The choice of non-linear kernel
function is received based on the hyperparameter tuning results. Notably, it is interesting
that some papers use the SVR model to handle textual data in their prediction task (Hagenau
et al., 2013; Schumaker et al., 2012).

4.3 Random forest
Random forest (RF) practically is an ensemble learning algorithm that is suitable for both
regression and classiﬁcation purposes. RF was initially created by Breiman (2001), which
practically is a variant of the Bagging ensemble learning method (Breiman, 1996). It is
pronounced that RF rather often outperforms the classical decision trees including CART,
because there is slightly less possibility to over-ﬁt to the training sample. In fact, RF cre-
ates plenty of uncorrelated DTs trained on bootstrap copies of original samples by randomly
choosing a subset of features (Mai et al., 2019). Notably, some papers use RF models to incor-
porate textual features in their prediction tasks (Katsafados & Anastasiou, 2024; Katsafados
et al., 2023a; Mai et al., 2019).
To address overﬁtting, we optimize the three key hyper-parameters of the RF model: (1)
the number of decision trees, (2) the number of features randomly chosen to grow each
decision tree when searching for the best split (max_features), and (3) the minimum required
number of observations in any given node in order to split it (min_samples_split). Notably,
all the hyper-parameters are tuned using a grid search process based on the ﬁvefold cross-
validation performance of the training set. First, the optimal number of decision trees is 200.
Second, beyond the randomization of training samples (bootstrap), the tuning optimization
of max_features beneﬁts the proper randomization of feature space, translating to a reduced
variance (low overﬁtting). We choose max_features to be equal to 2. Finally, we ﬁnd that the
optimal value of min_samples_split is 10.
4.4 Multilayer perceptron
So far, artiﬁcial neural networks are part of machine learning models, which are frequently
used in the domain of Natural Language Processing (Goldberg, 2017). In ﬁnance, one of the
most prevalent artiﬁcial neural networks is the multilayer perceptron (MLP) model (Kumar
& Ravi, 2016). We focus on the MLP models because they are more directly applicable to
BOW text representations and at the same time more directly comparable to the other models
we consider. Also, MLP is frequently used for prediction tasks (Ibrahim et al., 2022; Jiang
et al., 2022; Mai et al., 2019).
In an MLP architecture, there is initially an input layer of neurons, where our variables
are inserted as inputs into the network. Next, there are one or more hidden layers. Once
the hidden layer receives the content from the input layer, non-linear functions are activated
before transferring the estimated values to the next hidden or lastly to the output layer. In the
end, the output layer produces the predictive outcome based on the received input from the
hidden layers.3 Notably, all the hyper-parameters are tuned using a grid search process based
on the ﬁvefold cross-validation performance of the training set. In particular, the optimal MLP
model has 3 hidden layers, each of which has 200 neurons. Also, we use a rectiﬁed linear unit
(ReLU) as the activation function of each hidden layer. As a result, this kind of activation
function among the various hidden layers can highly handle non-linear relationships between
predictors and the dependent variable (Fig. 1).
3 For training purposes, we apply a backpropagation algorithm. In addition, we employ Adam as the optimizer
algorithm, and cross-entropy as the loss function. ReLU is deﬁned as f(x)  max (0, x). Finally, we use early
stopping to mitigate overﬁtting (Mai et al., 2019). To do so, we set aside 10% of training data as validation or
development set.

5.1 Prediction methodology
Our approach is to evaluate the models based on their out-of-sample performance. In par-
ticular, we initially split the data into training and out-of-sample datasets. Consistent with
previous studies, we split our data by selecting 80% of our sample as the training set and the
remaining 20% as the out-of-sample set (Doumpos et al., 2017; Mai et al., 2019). In addi-
tion, we select our out-of-sample set from a future period rather than at random (Pasiouras
& Tanna, 2010). In fact, the usefulness of a classiﬁcation model depends on its ability to
correctly classify objects in the future (Espahbodi & Espahbodi, 2003). In our case, our
evaluation methodology requires both an out-of-sample and an out-of-time approach. That
is, we ﬁrst sort the data by date, and only then do we consider the ﬁrst 80% as the training

application of RF models. Furthermore, the interpretability of RF models can be challenging.

precise contribution of individual features to the model’s decision-making process can be
intricate, limiting the model’s interpretability in certain contexts. To address this serious
issue, we follow some sophisticated methodologies in the next section whereby we shed

5.4 Identification of important textual features
5.4.1 LIME approach
To evaluate the importance of our textual features in our stock price crash risk prediction
task, we adopt the novel LIME method which practically explains the predictions of any
predictive model (Ribeiro et al., 2016). More speciﬁcally, we apply LIME to the RF model
as it is the best-performing model. With the aim to provide the best possible transparency of
our results, we use bigram textual features as inputs into the RF model.
Table 8 presents the results of the LIME analysis. In fact, positive impact translates to
a higher probability of stock price crash risk whereas negative impact implies the opposite
effect. By giving attention to the results, we conclude that they are in line with our expec-
tations. For instance, the bigrams with the most positive impact (increased crash risk) are
related to risks and uncertainty. Furthermore, in the case of negative impact (reduced crash
risk), we observe phrases such as established tools, keep commitment, forward guidance,
insights developing, and ﬁnancial stability.

6 Concluding remarks
We employ quarter-bank level European data and various measures for stock price crash
risk ensuring the robustness of our ﬁndings. First, we ﬁnd that the machine learning models
can generally perform better than the simple regressions. Next, our results also suggest that
textual information from the ECB president’s speeches has signiﬁcant predictive power.

Forecasting banks’ stock price crash risk with textual features is important because it can
help investors make informed investment decisions, by providing a measure of the potential
risk associated with investing in bank stocks. Additionally, it can help regulators assess the
stability of the ﬁnancial system and identify any potential systemic risks, allowing them to
take proactive measures to prevent or mitigate a ﬁnancial crisis. Moreover, forecasting stock
price crash risk for banks based on textual information can also provide valuable insights
for bank managers and executives, allowing them to identify potential risks and implement
risk management strategies to mitigate their impact. This helps to increase the stability and
resilience of the ﬁnancial system and reduce the likelihood of ﬁnancial crisis.
