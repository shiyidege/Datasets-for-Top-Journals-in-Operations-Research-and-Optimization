Abstract
Dendrograms are graphical representations of hierarchical clustering. Different definitions 
of the distance between two clusters in hierarchical clustering lead to different dendro­
grams. In this paper we focus on the dendrograms obtained when this distance is assumed 
to be the maximum of the distances between the elements of one cluster and the elements 
of the other cluster, known as complete-linkage dendrograms. For initial data where some 
elements are at the same distance as others, the number of different complete linkage 
dendrograms, which corresponds to the number of different ways to break ties for equal 
distances, can be large. We propose a system of linear inequalities whose set of solutions 
is the entire set of complete linkage dendrograms. Such a system of inequalities allows 
the inclusion of an objective function to select the best dendrogram among all these den­
drograms according to some criteria, which may not be possible with classical dendro­
gram computation algorithms. We also adapt the system of inequalities to single-linkage 
dendrograms, where the distance between two clusters is the minimum distance between 
an element of one cluster and an element of the other cluster. The benefits of describing 
complete-linkage dendrograms through a system of inequalities are illustrated in a com­
putational study in which five different objective functions are proposed.

1  Introduction
Hierarchical clustering is a classic method in data science, often used in unsupervised learn­
ing to construct a group hierarchy. The tree-structured graphs resulting from hierarchical 
clustering, called dendrograms, reveal relationships and patterns among the observations 
under study. Specifically, dendrograms represent the connections between observations at 
different levels: the lower the level at which they are connected, the more similar they are. 
Like any clustering technique, it leads to the formation of groups, although this technique is 
usually used when the number of groups is not fixed a priori. To create groups, a height is 
fixed, and the individuals that remain connected in the dendrogram below this height belong 
to the same cluster.
Dendrograms are constructed by running an iterative greedy algorithm in which observa­
tions in the data are hierarchically added or removed from the clustering. Adding observa­
tions leads to the agglomerative approach, where each of the observations is considered 
as an individual cluster in the first iteration of the algorithm, and in each of the following 
iterations the two most similar clusters are merged. In contrast, removing observations leads 
to the divisive approach, where the algorithms start with all observations in the same cluster, 
and in each iteration the further clusters are separated.
When constructing a dendrogram, the algorithm requires similarity or dissimilarity 
measures between individuals and groups to merge or split clusters. Proximities between 
individuals are commonly given in a dissimilarity matrix. To measure similarities between 
clusters different criteria, referred as linkages in clustering terminology, are considered. 
Two of the most popular linkages are the single-linkage and the complete-linkage. Single-
linkage looks for minimal intercluster dissimilarity; it computes all pairwise dissimilarities 
between the observations in a group and the observations in another group, and takes the 
smallest of these dissimilarities. Complete-linkage seeks maximal intercluster dissimilarity; 
it computes all pairwise dissimilarities between the observations in a group and the obser­
vations in another group, and takes the largest of these dissimilarities. Different linkages 
result in different dendrograms. We refer the reader to James et al. (2013) for further details 
on the topic. In this work, we focus on hierarchical clustering with complete linkage. This 
approach has been extensively studied in the literature and applied to many real data of dif­
ferent natures. For instance, just to mention a few specific cases of complete-linkage hier­
archical clustering recently applied to real data, Muthahharah and Juhari (2021) or Derosa 
et al. (2020) applied it to health data, (Cruz et al., 2021) to road safety, and Asnicar et al. 
(2021) or Go et al. (2021) to biological data.
A special mention is warranted for the issue of non-uniqueness of solutions in agglom­
erative hierarchical clustering. Except for the single-linkage method, the remaining linkage 
criteria can yield multiple optimal solutions when two or more (dis)similarity values are 
equal during the agglomerative process. In fact, among the different linkages used to gener­
ate dendrograms, complete linkage is particularly prone to producing alternative solutions, 
as it does not generate new similarity or dissimilarity measures. Ties in the initial similarity 
or dissimilarity matrices can arise from the use of integer-valued variables, although they 
may also occur when continuous variables are rounded or when precision is limited. In fact, 
in some cases, the absence of ties is a result of using more decimal places than necessary. 
Furthermore, ties also depend on the measure used to compute (dis)similarities.

Similarity matrices with ties are a subject of analysis in various fields, such as chemistry, 
biology, and psychology. As highlighted by Segura-Alabart et al.. (2022), the problem of 
non-uniqueness of solutions is a widespread issue in agglomerative hierarchical clustering. 
In that work, the authors calculated the fraction of publications in the Scopus database for 
which more than one hierarchical clustering solution is possible, finding that approximately 
46% of the articles are affected in the specific context of clustering microsatellite mark­
ers. Most software tools that generate dendrograms from such matrices resolve these ties 
arbitrarily. Most statistical software simply issues warnings or provides post hoc diagnos­
tics. In van der Kloot et al. (2005), it was proposed a more systematic approach applying 
the clustering procedure to numerous random permutations of the proximity matrix and 
selecting the solution with the best fit. This was illustrated using two examples. In the first, 
Power Strategies, the 16 × 16 proximity matrix contains 120 distances, of which only one is 
unique, some values appear up to 27 times, particularly the largest one. In the second case, 
the Smoking Survey, the 110 × 110 proximity matrix has 5,995 entries, but only 111 distinct 
values, just 13 of them unique, meaning that 99.78% of the distances are tied. Another 
approach to handling ties in hierarchical clustering is proposed by Fernández and Gómez 
(2008), who introduce a variable-group algorithm that merges more than two clusters simul­
taneously when ties occur. The resulting structure, called a multidendrogram, represents all 
possible agglomeration options in a single tree. They illustrate this method using the Glam­
organshire soils dataset, which includes similarity data between 23 soil types, originally 
reported with high precision (three decimal places).
This work aims to provide an optimization model for the complete-linkage clustering 
problem. Although a complete linkage dendrogram can indeed be obtained directly by 
applying a greedy algorithm without the need for a combinatorial optimization model, the 
possibility of using a model opens new doors for the design of efficient or optimal complete-
linkage methods concerning new criteria, as for instance, break ties. In the literature, many 
optimization models exist that represent problems that can be rapidly solved via algorithms. 
This is the case, for instance, of some classical Integer Optimization Problems such as the 
Minimum Spanning Tree Problem, the Shortest Path Problem, or the Max-flow Problem 
(see, for instance, Wolsey, 2020). This occurs also with the single-linkage clustering; as 
shown by Gower and Ross (1969), all the information needed to construct the single-linkage 
dendrogram is contained in a Minimum Spanning Tree (MST), making MST-based formu­
lations a natural and effective model for this clustering method.
In what follows, we briefly review some recent advances in cluster analysis that provide 
context for our work.
1.1  Advances in cluster analysis
Mathematical programming has long been used to support clustering techniques. Hansen 
and Jaumard’s work (Hansen & Jaumard, 1997) is a survey of the different types of cluster 
analysis from the perspective of mathematical programming. In the conclusions of their 
work, the authors state that although the interaction between mathematical programming 
and cluster analysis has been successful, there is still much to be done in this line of research.
Since then, effective mathematical programming models have been proposed for both 
hierarchical and non-hierarchical clustering.

Among the non-hierarchical approaches, we highlight the works of Piccialli et al. (2022) 
and Ágoston and E.-Nagy (2024). Both studies propose optimization models for the k-means 
problem: the former applies a semidefinite programming model, for which they compute a 
bound known as the Peng–Wei bound, while the latter uses a mixed-integer programming 
model. The model proposed in Ágoston and E.-Nagy (2024) requires significantly more 
computation time than other solution algorithms; however, the authors argue that this trade-
off is justified, as the optimization framework allows for the incorporation of additional 
aspects through linear constraints. On the other hand, Vichi et al. (2022) introduces a hier­
archical classification method based on the k-means algorithm. Broadly speaking, they pro­
pose solving a sequence of nested k-means problems. With this approach, previous results 
for k-means can, to some extent, be leveraged for hierarchical classification.
Among the models for hierarchical optimization, the work by Gilpin et al. (2013) is the 
first to propose a mixed-integer optimization model for hierarchical clustering. The authors 
demonstrate the scientific advancement of their model by relaxing certain constraints, which 
allows them to consider what they refer to as overlapping clusters. Recently, Willemsen et 
al. (2025) introduced two compact mixed-integer linear programming formulations to deal 
with the optimization of a global objective function in the hierarchical clustering process.
The interest in having optimization models for decision problems lies not only in their 
theoretical appeal but also in their flexibility to handle problem variants where efficient 
algorithms are unavailable. In clustering, certain conditions, such as fairness constraints, 
may require an optimization approach. This topic has gained increasing attention since the 
work by Chierichetti et al. (2017); see Chhabra et al. (2021) for a recent overview. Optimi­
zation models also enable integration into more complex frameworks; for instance, Labbé 
et al. (2023) proposes a feature selection model for single-linkage clustering using a mini­
mum spanning tree-based formulation. Along similar lines, García et al.(2011) leverages the 
presence of ties in distance matrices to develop a novel optimization model for the classical 
capacitated p-median problem, showing how ties, often seen as a nuisance, can be used 
constructively to enhance model design.
1.2  Main contributions
The main contribution of the paper is the proposal of a system of linear inequalities with 
binary variables whose solutions correspond to all complete-linkage dendrograms. First, we 
show that in the case that there are no ties in the dissimilarity matrix, the solution of such 
a system of linear inequalities is unique. Second, in the case that there exist ties in the dis­
similarity matrix, and hence several complete-linkage dendrograms, we prove a one-to-one 
correspondence between all these dendrograms and the solutions of the proposed system. 
Third, since the proposed system of linear inequalities contains two families with an expo­
nential number of constraints, we solve the associated separation problems. Finally, we also 
adapt the system of linear inequalities to the single-linkage case.
Modeling the complete-linkage dendrogram is useful both when there are no ties in the 
dissimilarity matrix, for example when dealing with feature selection, and when there are 
ties, for example when minimizing the maximum distance. Moreover, the paper (Landete & 
Marín, 2014) points out the interest of a family of objective functions for balanced weight. 
The relevance of our contribution lies in the importance of discriminating between dendro­
grams with the same linkage in an informed way. In the classification study, if you want the

clusters to be uniform in size, the system of linear inequalities that we introduce, together

important to note that the conventional greedy dendrogram algorithm does not allow us to 
take all this objective into account and tends to break ties arbitrarily.
Another noteworthy contribution of this work is the development of a computational 
study in which the proposed modeling constraints are implemented. The experiments illus­
trate that computation times remain practical and that the solutions obtained vary signifi­
cantly depending on the chosen objective function.
1.3  Structure of the paper
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we state the notation. In 
Section 3, we propose a system of linear inequalities to model the complete-linkage hierar­
chical clustering problem when there is no tie in the dissimilarity matrix, prove the validity 
of it, interpret the constraints in terms of a dendrogram, provide a characterization based 
on such interpretation and, analyze the effect of constraints removal in an example. In the 
following Section 4, we extend the system to the case of ties in the similarity matrix and 
also prove the correctness of it. We address the separation problems of the proposed fami­
lies of inequalities in Section 5. Later, in Section 6, we extrapolate it to the single-linkage 
hierarchical clustering case. Finally, Section 7 presents the computational results of several 
experiments in which computation times and solutions for different objective functions are 
compared. We close the work with some conclusions.
2  Notation
It is well known that all the information in a dendrogram can be encoded in a spanning tree 
(Gower & Ross, 1969; Labbé et al., 2023). Given a dendrogram, the corresponding span­
ning tree is the graph (tree) whose set of nodes is the set of observations of the dendrogram 
and whose edges are those representing the links between observations in the dendrogram, 
with the pairwise dissimilarities as weights. Thus, in the following, we will treat dendro­
grams as spanning trees.
Let G = (V, E) be a weighted undirected graph with weights we ≥0 for each e ∈E. 
For a subset S of nodes, ϵ(S) denotes the set of all edges with both ends in S, and δ(S) 
denotes the subset of edges with an endpoint in S and the other in V \ S. Also, for two dis­
joint vertex subsets S1 and S2, δ(S1, S2) represents the set of edges with one endpoint in 
S1 and one endpoint in S2. Given a subgraph H of G, V(H) denotes its vertex set and E(H) 
its edge set. Furthermore, for a subset F ⊆E of edges, we denote by ¯e(F) the edge with 
the highest weight, and by L(e) the set of edges in E with a weight less than or equal to we. 
Finally, G(e) represents the graph induced by the set of edges L(e), i.e. its vertex set is the 
set of vertices incident to at least one edge of L(e) and its edge set is L(e).

In general, the number of different complete-linkage dendrograms that follow from a 
dissimilarity matrix with ties in weights depends on the number of ties and also on the 
position of the ties in the dissimilarity matrix. When this number is huge, obtaning of the 
complete-linkage dendrogram diagram that best suits our interest cannot be done by inspec­

desirable to have a model helping us to decide which dendrogram among all those that are 
complete-linkage is the most appropriate. The objective may be the minimization of the 
total dissimilarity, as in single-linkage, or the minimization of an external weight, or even 
the optimization of certain quality/goodness measures such as the cophenetic or the purity 
indexes for unlabeled and labeled databases, respectively.

5  The separation problem of inequalities (12), (13) and (14)
In integer programming, the separation problem for a family of valid inequalities refers to 
the task of determining whether a given solution to a relaxation of the model violates any 
inequality from that family. Solving the separation problem is essential when the size of the 
inequality family is too large to be explicitly included in the model from the outset. In such 
cases, valid inequalities are not incorporated directly, but rather added progressively, as 
candidate constraints are identified through the separation procedure. Classical examples in 
operations research that require solving separation problems include the subtour elimination 
constraints and the rounded capacity inequalities in Vehicle Routing Problems. Already in 
the work by Hansen and Jaumard (1997), an overview published nearly 30 years ago, the 
importance of addressing the separation problem for the different families of valid inequali­
ties in clustering optimization models was strongly emphasized.
The three families of inequalities presented in Theorem 2 are too large to be directly 
included in models, even for medium-sized instances. Therefore, in this section, we study 
the separation problem for these three families. The separation of constraint family (14) has 
already been addressed in the literature. In this work, we introduce separation procedures 
for the two remaining families of valid inequalities, namely (12) and (13).
We consider a solution that satisfies constraints (15) and (16). The separation of con­
straint family (14) is relatively straightforward, as it corresponds to the classical subtour 
elimination constraints commonly used in routing and minimum spanning tree models. 
The standard separation procedure consists of computing a maximum flow and verifying 
whether the resulting flow is less than one; if so, the corresponding violated constraint is 
added to the model. From now on, we assume that the separation of (14) has already been 
performed and that the current solution corresponds to a spanning tree.
Given the current solution ˜x, let SOL denote the subgraph whose vertex set equals V and 
whose edge set contains all edges e such that ˜xe = 1. Since SOL satisfies (14), SOL contains 
no cycles and is connected.

7  Computational experience
We have conducted an extensive computational experiment to highlight the usefulness of 
the complete linkage formulation with constraints. In the first part of the computational 
study, we solved instances with a very small percentage of ties. We compare the results 
for the different objective functions and also the running times of our proposal with those 
required by the greedy algorithm when enumerating all possible permutations, showing 
that, even in this case, proper tie-breaking is a problem worth addressing. In the second part, 
we solved instances with a higher percentage of ties to illustrate that the model scales well 
and that, even when increasing the number of ties, the resolution does not become signifi­
cantly more complex in terms of computational time.
The computational experiments in this work have been performed on a HP ZBook Fire­
fly 16 inch G10 Mobile Workstation computer, containing 12 physical cores and 16 logical 
processors of a 13th Gen Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-1360P CPU @ 2.2GHz, and 32.0 GB of 
RAM. Python has been employed in Visual Studio Code. Mathematical optimization has 
been carried out using the Gurobi Optimiser, version 12.0.1.
In this section, we propose five objective functions that we consider particularly relevant 
in the context of classification.
The first objective is to minimize the total height of the dendrogram, thereby selecting 
the dendrogram that interprets the population in the most homogeneous way. Conversely,

The third objective aims to maximize the distance between the last two clusters merged, 
which can be interpreted as enforcing the presence of at least two well-separated groups.
The fourth and fifth objectives are concerned with the similarity between the selected 
complete linkage dendrogram and the single linkage dendrogram. Since the latter corre­
sponds to the minimum spanning tree (MST), we evaluate the number of edges shared 
between the complete linkage dendrogram and the MST. Depending on the intended simi­
larity, this quantity is either maximized (fourth objective) or minimized (fifth objective), 
depending on whether we seek to make the complete linkage dendrogram as similar as pos­
sible to the MST, or as different as possible.

solution of one of the models performs well for the rest of the models, the polygon drawn 
within the pentagon will be another inscribed pentagon with a similar area. In the opposite 
case, if the optimal solution of a model performs poorly for the others, the resulting polygon 
will resemble a segment from the center to the vertex corresponding to the optimized model. 
From the radar plot analysis, the most versatile models are (P3), (P4) and (P5). They cor­
respond to the polygons with the largest area, and indicate that objectives in (P2), (P3), and 
(P5) and also objectives in (P1), (P3), and (P4) are, to some extent, compatible. Optimiz­
ing objectives in (P3) or (P5) yields good results for (P2), and optimizing (P4) gives good 
results for (P1) and (P3), although optimizing (P2) does not provide information about the 
objective in (P3). It is also observed that optimizing objectives in (P3) and (P5) leads to 
the same results, which means that for this instance, the complete-linkage dendrogram with 
the maximum distance between the last two merged clusters is the one that differs the most 
from the single-linkage dendrogram. In fact, since the percentage of ties is very small in this 
case, it is expected that the optimal dendrogram will be the same for several of the objective 
functions.

7.3  On the scalability of the model
As we have observed in the previous sections, in less than 15 min, we can obtain results 
from the five models we proposed for different percentages of ties. The remaining question 
is how the time increases as the size of the weight matrix grows. To answer this question, 
we have generated instances with matrix sizes from 10 to 90 in increments of 5, maintaining 
a 60% of ties and that each repeated value appears between 2 and 30 times. We have solved 
model (P1) and recorded the execution time for these 17 instances. The results are shown 
in the Figure 6. We observe that while the weight matrix size is less than or equal to 60, the 
solving time does not exceed 30 min. However, starting from a size of 90, at least 6 h are 
required.
It would be possible to compute the time for all objective functions and all tie percent­
ages, but we believe it is not necessary to insist further on these timing values because our 
separation algorithm is basic. If we aimed to improve performance, we would likely need to 
add valid inequalities to the base models. The purpose of this work is to illustrate the useful­
ness of having a model for the complete linkage dendrogram as an alternative to the greedy 
algorithm, rather than focusing on solver-based solving times.

8  Conclusions
We proposed a mathematical model for the complete-linkage hierarchical clustering prob­
lem. Such a model, developed in Section 4, is a powerful tool to compare complete-link­
age dendrograms. Not all the complete-linkage dendrograms of a dissimilarity matrix are 
equivalent. Some of them suggest more clusters than others when fixing a certain height. 
The distance between pairs of elements is, for some complete-linkage dendrograms, closer 
to the corresponding distance in the dissimilarity matrix than for others. Similarly, different 
complete-linkage dendrograms for the same dissimilarity matrix may give different cluster 
sizes or densities, and thus some complete-linkage dendrograms would be desirable in a 
balanced-wise sense. Thus, to randomly unmatch equal values in the dissimilarity matrix, as 
is frequently done, is a questionable approach. Faced with this usual tendency, we propose 
to untie following a convenient criterion. Having chosen a criterion, the best complete-link­
age dendrogram results from the optimization problem that has the criterion as the objective 
function and the system in Theorem 2 as constraints.
The applicability and usefulness of the set of constraints introduced to describe the set of 
complete-linkage dendrograms has been validated through a computational study involving 
five different objective functions. This study illustrates how each objective can lead to the 
selection of different dendrograms and how the proposed model reduces computation time 
compared to the repeated application of the classical greedy-type algorithm for dendrogram 
construction. We have solved more than 550 instances with varying sizes and different per­
centages of tied values in the weight matrix.
Both the results in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, also apply to many other linkages. When­
ever the linkage is defined as the selection of an edge connecting the two connected com­
ponents, the results in both theorems hold by just modifying the meaning of ¯e. As we have 
mentioned in Section 6, if the linkage consists on choosing the minimum, ¯e must be defined 
as the minimum. It is not difficult to check that if the linkage chooses the closest to the aver­
age or the closest to the range, then ¯e must be the edge with weight closest to the average 
or to the range, respectively. Properly defining the second weight or the t-th weight, both

theorems can also be extended to the corresponding linkages. According to this discussion, 
in this paper, we have provided the tools to model not only the complete-linkage dendro­
gram algorithm but a family of dendrogram algorithms that includes the well-known single-
linkage and complete-linkage algorithms.
Since any minimal spanning tree represents the single-linkage dendrogram, the general 
system of inequalities we propose constitutes an alternative approach for obtaining the mini­
mal spanning trees. This approach has the great advantage that all the feasible solutions are 
optimal. Thus, if the goal is to solve a bilevel model in which the second level is the minimal 
spanning tree, the replacement of the second level by our system gives an alternative single-
level model that deserves researchers’ attention.
An interesting direction for future work is the design of a hybrid algorithm that leverages 
the strengths of the proposed model, for cases with ties, and the greedy approach, for cases 
without ties, aiming to obtain high-quality solutions for larger problem sizes.
