Abstract
Several recent works on non-atomic routing games suggest that the performance degrada-
tion of selпђБsh routing with respect to optimal routing is overall low and far from worst-case
scenarios. In this work, we study the performance degradation induced by the lack of coor-
dination in an atomic routing game over parallel links in which there are two types of links.
The latency function of "cheap" links is of the form c1ѕЖ(x), whereas the latency function of
"expensive" links is of the form c2ѕЖ(x), where c2 > c1. We obtain an explicit characteriza-
tion of the optimal and equilibrium пђВow conпђБgurations, and establish sufпђБcient conditions
on the latency function ѕЖ(x) under which the worst trafпђБc conditions occur when all users
have the same trafпђБc demand and the total trafпђБc demand is such that "expensive" link are
marginally used by selпђБsh routing. We also obtain some partial results on the worst network
conпђБguration for the inefпђБciency of selпђБsh routing. All in all, our results suggest that the
worst-case scenario for the inefпђБciency of selпђБsh routing corresponds to very speciпђБc traf-
пђБc conditions and to highly asymmetric network conпђБgurations, and thus that the Price of
Anarchy is probably an overly pessimistic performance measure for non-cooperative routing
games, as advocated in the above-mentioned works.

1.1 Motivation
In networks based on a centralized routing scheme, a central node computes the least-cost
path between source and destination nodes by using some global knowledge of the network
and then distributes the resulting routes to other nodes so that they can forward user trafпђБc.

is broadly admitted that a centralized routing scheme cannot be used in large networks due
to scalability, robustness and complexity reasons. An alternative approach is to let each
network user selпђБshly decide on which path to route its trafпђБc demand according to its own
interest. Although more robust and scalable, this decentralized scheme may lead to a loss in
performance as the individual optimizations performed by many interacting self-interested

Noncooperative routing games provide the natural framework to study the performance
degradationintheabovedecentralizedroutingscheme.Thesegamesaremathematicalmodels
of strategic interactions between selпђБsh, uncoordinated network users. One usually distin-

to games in which there is a continuum of users, each one controlling a negligible amount of
trafпђБc, and an equilibrium state (which is known as a Wardrop equilibrium (Wardrop, 1952))
is deпђБned as a set of routing strategies such that the trafпђБc demand of each user is forwarded

not always correspond to that of a globally optimal routing policy. In game theory terminol-
ogy, such an optimal policy is usually referred to as a social optimum because it minimizes
the social cost, that is the sum of all user costs.
A vast body of literature has been devoted to the study of the inefпђБciency of selпђБsh routing
under a variety of trafпђБc models. The most popular measure of the inefпђБciency of equilibria is
the Price of Anarchy (PoA) which was introduced by Koutsoupias and Papadimitriou (1999)
and is deпђБned as the performance ratio between the overall cost of an optimal routing policy
and that of the worst Nash/Wardrop equilibrium (that is, an equilibrium with the largest
social cost). As discussed in Section 1.3, it has been shown that the PoA of some selпђБsh
routing games can be arbitrarily large. Several recent works aiming at understanding when
is selпђБsh routing bad suggest however that the PoA is an overly pessimistic measure and
that non-cooperative routing achieves near-optimal performance in most realistic settings.
Nevertheless, most of these works have been carried out for non-atomic routing games, which
are usually much simpler to analyze thanks to the assumption of a continuum of players.
In contrast to the above mentioned works, the present paper studies the efпђБciency of
selпђБsh routing in atomic routing games. Owing to the complexity of the analysis, we restrict
ourselves to a topology of parallel links, as introduced in the seminal paper of Orda, Orda
et al. (1993). Given a strictly increasing and convex function ѕЖ(x) and two cost parameters
c1 and c2 such that c1 < c2, we further assume that there are two types of links: "cheap"
links whose cost is a function of the пђВow on the link of the form c1ѕЖ(x), and "expensive"
links whose cost function is of the form c2ѕЖ(x). We also assume a пђБnite number of users,
each one splitting its trafпђБc demand over the parallel links so as to minimize its own routing
cost, which is the sum of the costs incurred over all the links it uses. As the cost of a link is a
non-decreasing function of the trafпђБc пђВow on that link, the optimal strategy of a user depends
on how the other users split their trafпђБc demands. In this context, a Nash equilibrium is a
пђВow conпђБguration on the links in which no user can beneпђБt from a unilateral deviation of its
own routing strategy.

as their cost parameters are пђБxed, and study the efпђБciency of selпђБsh routing as a function of
the trafпђБc demands of users. Our goal is to understand under which trafпђБc conditions the worst
inefпђБciency of selпђБsh routing is obtained for a пђБxed network conпђБguration. As a measure of

1.3 Related work
We пђБrst review relevant works on nonatomic routing games. The analysis of the efпђБciency
of Wardrop equilibria has a long history which dates back to 1920 and the well-known
PigouвАЩs example which shows that the outcome of a selпђБsh routing game can lead to a

was shown in 1968 by Dietrich Braess that adding resources to a transportation network

BraessвАЩs paradox (Braess, 1968). More recently, Roughgarden and Tardos have shown that the

and that this bound is tight (Roughgarden and Tardos, 2002). This shows that selпђБsh routing
is always efпђБcient for such routing games. However, it was shown in Roughgarden (2002)

Other relevant works on the PoA of nonatomic routing games are Roughgarden and Tardos

On the empirical side, several recent works studied the efпђБciency of Wardrop equilibria
in real networks and observed that the performance degradation with respect to optimal
routing is overall low in spite of large theoretical values of the PoA. For instance, Monnot
et al. analyze data of commuting students in Singapore and conclude that routing choices
are near optimal (Monnot et al., 2017) (see also Youn et al. (2008) for a similar study). On
a more theoretical side, the authors in Colini-Baldeschi et al. (2016, 2019, 2020) prove that

setting and show that selпђБsh routing is efпђБcient when the total trafпђБc demand gets very large.
In Cominetti et al. (2021), Cominetti et al. study the efпђБciency of Wardrop equilibria as a
function of the total trafпђБc demand in the network. As an efпђБciency metric, they focus as we
do on the ratio of social costs obtained under the equilibrium and optimal routing strategies.
For afпђБne link costs, they show that this ratio has a пђБnite number of local maxima, which are
achieved where the set of active links changes. In summary, all the above works suggest that
the PoA is an overly pessimistic measure of the inefпђБciency of selпђБsh routing and that the
performance degradation is often low and far from the worst-case scenarios.

7 Conclusions
For the speciпђБc atomic routing game considered in this paper, it was shown that the worst
trafпђБc conditions occur when all users have the same trafпђБc demand and the total trafпђБc
demand is such that "expensive links" are marginally used by selпђБsh routing. Moreover, if
these worst trafпђБc conditions are met, the worst inefпђБciency of the selпђБsh routing scheme
is obtained when the number of "expensive" links is inпђБnitely larger than the number of
"cheap" links and under a very speciпђБc condition on the ratio c1/c2 (which we conjecture to
be c1/c2 вЖТ0 for ѕЖ(x) = eќљx). The worst-case scenarios for the inefпђБciency of selпђБsh routing
therefore corresponds to very speciпђБc trafпђБc conditions and to highly asymmetric network
conпђБgurations, which explain why the PoA is probably an overly pessimistic performance
measure, as advocated in many recent works on non-atomic routing games.
