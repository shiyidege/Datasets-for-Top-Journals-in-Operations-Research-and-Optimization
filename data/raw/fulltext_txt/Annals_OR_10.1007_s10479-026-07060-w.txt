Abstract
We consider a two-person zero-sum discounted stochastic game with random rewards and
known transition probabilities. The players have opposite objectives and are interested in
optimizing the expected discounted reward which they can obtain with a given conﬁdence
level when both the players play the worst possible move against each other. We model such
a game problem by deﬁning the chance-constrained optimization problem for each player.
In this framework, risk attitudes are determined by conﬁdence levels, where values of 0.5 or
below correspond to risk-seeking behavior and values above 0.5 correspond to risk-averse
behavior. When the reward vector follows a multivariate elliptically symmetric distribution,
the game is equivalent to a minimax formulation. We consider the game with risk-seeking
and risk-averse players separately. We show that the risk-seeking problem is equivalent to a
constrained optimization of a parameterized zero-sum stochastic game and the optimal payoff
of player 1 and optimal cost of player 2 can be computed using Riemann gradient sampling
algorithms. Later we use the solution of the constrained optimization problem of each player
to compute its optimal strategy by solving a linear programming problem. We reformulate
the risk-averse problem as a discrete minimax problem. We propose an algorithm based on a
linearization method and discuss its convergence properties. Alternatively, we reformulate the
risk-averse problem as a second-order cone programming problem with bilinear constraints.
The numerical experiments on randomly generated instances are performed to illustrate our
theoretical results.
Keywords Stochastic games · Chance constraints · Minimax · Zero-sum games

there always exists a Nash equilibrium in the class of stationary strategies (Sobel, 1971;
Takahashi, 1964; Fink, 1964) and it can be computed by ﬁnding a global optimal solution of
a nonlinear programming problem (Filar et al., 1991). The equivalent mathematical programs
and linear complementarity problems were introduced in the literature for stochastic games
with a special structure on rewards and transition probabilities (Krishnamurthy & Neogy,
2020; Raghavan & Syed, 2002; Parthasarathy & Raghavan, 1981; Singh & Hemachandra,
2014, 2015).
The aforementioned papers assume that the model parameters, namely the rewards and
transitionprobabilities,areexactlyknown.However,inreality,theseparametersareestimated
from historical data or learned from experiences. Thus, these parameters are susceptible to
uncertainty that can lead to erroneous equilibrium strategies. Stochastic games with uncer-
tain parameters have not received much attention in the literature. To our knowledge, few
results are available on robust stochastic games in which the model parameters are known
to belong to polytopic uncertainty sets (Karde¸s et al., 2011; Karde¸s, 2014). The authors
show that a stationary Nash equilibrium exists, and it can be computed by solving a non-
linear programming problem. The main drawback of robust optimization formulation is that
it generates a conservative solution. For Markov decision processes (MDPs), which may be
viewed as one player stochastic games, a chance constraint based payoff criterion has been
introduced which overcomes the conservatism (Delage & Mannor, 2010; Varagapriya et al.,
2023, 2025). The chance constraint based payoff criterion captures the situation where play-
ers are interested in payoffs that can be earned with certain conﬁdence level. Stochastic games
using chance constraint based payoff criterion has not been explored much. Recently, Verma
et al. (2025) considered a single controller discounted stochastic game with random costs
and known transition probabilities using the chance constraint based payoff criterion. They
showed that there exists a stationary Nash equilibrium of the resulting chance-constrained
game when the cost vector of each player follow an elliptically symmetric distribution. To
compute Nash equilibria, some equivalent mathematical programs are proposed. The static
games with random payoffs under chance-constrained game formulation are extensively stud-
ied in the literature (Singh et al., 2016; Singh & Lisser, 2018; Singh et al., 2017). Singh et
al. (2016), Singh and Lisser (2018) formulated a ﬁnite strategic game with random payoffs
as a chance-constrained game. When the payoff vectors follow elliptically symmetric dis-
tributions, there exists a mixed strategy Nash equilibrium (Singh et al., 2016). Singh and
Lisser (2018) showed that the mixed strategy Nash equilibria can be computed from the
global optimal solution of a mathematical programming problem. The payoffs with partially
known distributions which belong to an uncertainty set are formulated using distributionally
robust chance constraint (Singh et al., 2017). The authors showed that a mixed strategy Nash
equilibrium of the corresponding distributionally robust chance-constrained game exists and
it can be computed by solving an equivalent mathematical programming problem for certain
moments based uncertainty sets. There are some zero-sum chance-constrained games which
were introduced very early in the literature (Blau, 1974; Charnes et al., 1968; Song, 1992;
Cassidy et al., 1972; Charnes et al., 1968).

Stochastic games involving random rewards and transition probabilities under a chance-
constrained based payoff criterion are not considered in the literature, with the notable
exception of a recent study on single-controller stochastic games (Verma et al., 2025). In
this paper, we consider a zero-sum discounted stochastic game where the rewards are deﬁned
by random variables and the transition probabilities are exactly known. We assume that the
rewards follow elliptically symmetric distributions. Both the players have opposite objectives
and we consider the case where player 1 is interested in maximizing, whereas player 2 is
interested in minimizing the expected discounted payoff that can be attained with a given
conﬁdence level against worst move of its opponent. To ﬁnd optimal policy and optimal
objective value of each player, we propose a chance-constrained optimization model. The
players’ conﬁdence levels need not be the same and its value determines whether they are risk
seeking or risk averse. We show that the case of risk-seeking players is equivalent to a con-
strained optimization of a parameterized zero-sum stochastic game. We use Riemann gradient
sampling algorithms to compute the optimal payoff of player 1 and optimal cost of player 2
which later is used to compute players’ optimal strategies using linear programming method.
We show that the case of risk-averse players is equivalent to a discrete minimax problem. To
compute players’ optimal strategies in this case, we propose gradient ascent/descent algo-
rithms which converge to a Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) point which become local optimum
under certain conditions. Alternatively, we show that it is equivalent to second-order cone
programming problems with bilinear constraints. The numerical experiments are performed
to illustrate our algorithms and theoretical results on randomly generated instances.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we recall the deﬁnition of a zero-sum
discounted stochastic game, then we introduce chance-constrained optimization problems for
both players, and its deterministic reformulations when the random rewards follow elliptical
distributions. In Sections 3 and 4, we present the case of risk-seeking and risk-averse players
and their reformulations and algorithms, respectively. Section 5 contains the numerical results
and the paper is concluded in Section 6.
2 The model
We ﬁrst recall two-player zero-sum discounted stochastic games, introduced by Shapley
(1953). The game starts at an initial state and is played in stages. A reward is received at
each stage when both players choose actions and the game moves from one state to another
according to a controlled Markov chain. Then, we consider the case where rewards are random
which makes expected discounted reward random. The optimization problem of each player
against the worst possible move from its opponent is formulated as a chance-constrained
optimization problem.

6 Conclusions
In this paper, we consider a zero-sum discounted stochastic game with random rewards and
known transition probabilities. We propose a chance-constrained optimization formulation
for both the players. It captures the situation where player 1 is interested in maximizing, and
player 2 is interested in minimizing the expected discounted reward that can be obtained with
certain conﬁdence level against the worst possible move by its opponent. When the reward
vector follows elliptically symmetric distribution, the chance-constrained model is equivalent
to a minimax formulation. We study the case of both risk seeking and risk averse players and
propose equivalent deterministic reformulations. To compute optimal payoff/cost of both the
players, we propose gradient based algorithms. In the numerical experiments, we observe
that the proposed algorithms ﬁnd the players’ objective values and optimal strategies which
are close to optimal ones.
