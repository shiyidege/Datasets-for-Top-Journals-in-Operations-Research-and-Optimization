Abstract
Policy institutes such as central banks need accurate forecasts of key measures of economic
activity to design stabilization policies that reduce the severity of economic ﬂuctuations.
Therefore, this paper develops a kernel ridge regression estimator in a mixed data sampling
framework. Kernel ridge regression can handle many predictors with a nonlinear relationship
to the target variable. Consequently, it has potential to improve the currently used principal
component-based methods when the economic data follow a nonlinear factor structure. In a
Monte Carlo study, we show that the kernel ridge regression approach is superior in terms of
mean square error and is more robust than principal component-based methods to different
nonlinear data generating processes. By using a dataset consisting of 24 economic indicators,
we forecast Swedish gross domestic production. The results conﬁrm the superiority of the
kernel ridge regression approach. Therefore, we suggest that policy institutes consider the
use of kernel-based approaches when forecasting key measures of economic activity.
Keywords Big data · Kernel ridge regression · MIDAS · Forecasting
1 Introduction
The COVID-19 pandemic and the consequences of the war in Ukraine hastened a shift in
many areas of our lives. In economics, the shift spurred the need for better forecasts of
key economic variables to make an accurate stabilization policy. A common issue when
forecasting measures of economic activity such as gross domestic production (GDP) is that
the economic indicators (e.g., industrial production, unemployment and inﬂation rate) are
sampled at a higher frequency. Due to the frequency misalignment, a standard method that
policy institutes, such as, central banks apply when forecasting the GDP is based on mixed
data sampling (MIDAS) regression. Well-known examples of economic policy institutes

MIDAS regression was ﬁrst introduced in a seminal paper by Ghysels et al. (2004). To
ensure a parsimonious speciﬁcation in situations when the frequency mismatch is large,
Ghysels et al. (2007) suggested using polynomial distributed lag (PDL) models, where the
lag structure is based on, for example, beta lags or exponential Almon lags. The estima-
tion method commonly applied for MIDAS regressions is nonlinear least squares (NLS).
Andreou et al. (2010) provided asymptotic results for the estimators and showed the advan-
tage of this method compared to aggregating or equally weighting data. However, for models
when the frequency misalignment is not large, such as the situation faced by central banks
when forecasting GDP based on monthly economic indicators, the curse of dimensionality
is not necessarily relevant; therefore, Foroni et al. (2015) suggested unrestricted-MIDAS
(U-MIDAS) regression. For the U-MIDAS model the lag polynomial is unrestricted and can
therefore be estimated using simple ordinary least squares (OLS) techniques. The results
showed that the performance of the U-MIDAS model was superior when the frequency
mismatch was small, e.g., when mixing monthly and quarterly data. Due to the simple speciﬁ-
cation and superior performance of U-MIDAS, it has become very popular among economic
policy institutes when forecasting GDP (see, for example, Bok et al., 2018, den Reijer &
Johansson, 2019; Ba´nbura & Saiz, 2020, among others). To improve the standard U-MIDAS
model is still an active research area and improvements has been made by, for example,
including a moving average term in the forecasting equation since temporal aggregation
usually leads to such models (see Foroni et al., 2019).
Recently, machine learning approaches have gained signiﬁcant attention in the econo-
metric literature to address issues such as handling big data and modeling nonlinearities. In
this context, Uematsu and Tanaka (2019) explored the implementation of machine learning
methods within the MIDAS framework. The authors proposed using the least absolute shrink-
age and selection operator (LASSO), a modern feature selection approach that estimates a
sparse model in the presence of many potential economic indicators. Building on this, Babii
et al. (2022) introduced a sparse-group LASSO (sg-LASSO) estimator that accounts for the
grouping structure of economic indicators. Li et al. (2022) suggested a regime switching
MIDAS-LASSO estimator and applied it to stock market volatility predictions. Regression
trees, which is a nonlinear machine learning approach have also been introduced by Huber
et al. (2023).
In this article, we propose a kernel ridge regression (KRR) approach which can be mathe-
maticallyseenasajointventureofordinaryleastsquaresandridgeregression(seeChristmann
& Steinwart, 2007 and Hainmueller & Hazlett, 2014). This is the ﬁrst paper that general-
izes the successful KRR approach into a MIDAS setting, where the aim is to account for
non-linearity present among macroeconomic variables sampled at different frequencies. In
a simulation study we show that KRR using a Gaussian kernel is usually superior in larger
samples than principal component (PC) based methods and standard U-MIDAS. KRR using
a Gaussian kernel works especially well for nonlinear DGPs which follows the result in
Coulombe et al. (2022) and Exterkate et al. (2016) where it is shown to be a promising
machine learning procedure handling non-linearities. Our empirical application based on
data used by NIER in Sweden (see Ankargren & Lindholm, 2021), we demonstrate that both
linear and nonlinear KRR outperforms currently used principal component-based models.
This latter result is inline with the results in Coulombe et al., (2021), Huber et al., (2023)
and Shahzad et al. (2023) where it was found that modelling nonlinear relationships between

2 Methodology
The KRR approach is based on ordinary least squares (OLS) regression and ridge regres-
sion. We begin this section by brieﬂy providing a review of U-MIDAS regression, which
parsimoniously accommodates variables with mixed frequencies. In its original form, the
U-MIDAS model is estimated via OLS, which does not account for nonlinearity and a high
dimensional setting with many predictors in the data. Therefore, we proceed to introduce the
KRR estimator in the MIDAS framework that can overcome these limitations. At the end
of this section, we also present some kernel functions, which are popular because of their
computational efﬁciency, that are used in this study.

2.2 Kernel ridge regression in the U-MIDAS framework

Having obtained a computationally feasible equation for the forecast equation, the next major

2.4 Selection of tuning parameters
Selection of optimal tuning parameters is essential for reliable estimation. The optimal per-
formance of kernel ridge regression implemented in our case requires a suitably chosen
shrinkage parameter λ and scaling parameter σ and k-fold cross-validation provides a simple
way to address this problem. For this purpose, we choose a set of values for λ and σ and com-
pute the cross-validation error for each value of these parameters. We follow Exterkate (2013)
and choose the candidate values for σ based on consideration of the signal-to-noise ratio such
that the values of σ are closely connected to the values of λ. By using this approach we reduce
the potential number of the candidate values. The tuning parameters are selected to minimize
the cross-validation error. Finally, the model is reﬁt using all the available observations and
the selected value of the tuning parameter.
The simple k-folded cross-validation approach where the time structure of the data is
not accounted for may not be adequate due to serial correlation. Thus, we use block cross-
validation techniques suggested by Bergmeir et al. (2018) to remove the dependence among
the observations.

3.3 Factors varied in the design of the experiment
As displayed in Table 2, we have chosen the number of predictor variables in the DGP (p)
to be 10, 15, 20, 30, 60 and 120, and because we have a MIDAS structure, where the target
variableisobservedatalowerfrequencythanthepredictorvariables,theactualsetofpredictor
variables (p*  mp) becomes 30, 45, 60, 90, 180 and 360, respectively. This covers many
common applications, such as Bok et al. (2018) for the US, Anesti et al. (2017) for the UK and
Ankargren and Lindholm (2021), which used 32, 60 and 33 indicators, respectively. This will
enable us to investigate the behavior of the forecast methods in a wide variety of empirically
common model complexities. The choice of m equal to 3 reﬂects two typical settings, i.e.,
having data observed quarterly and monthly or approximately yearly and quarterly. These are
typical settings in many research areas where national or regional GDP is forecasted (see Bok
et al., 2018, Koop et al., 2020 and Ankargren & Lindholm, 2021, among others). Furthermore,
the number of observations is typically limited in macroeconomic applications; therefore,
the maximum number of low-frequency observations is set to 70, which corresponds to 70
yearly observations of the target variable when having a yearly/quarterly setting and almost
18 years when having a quarterly/monthly setting. This covers the situation in, for example,
Bok et al. (2018) and Ankargren and Lindholm (2021), where a rolling window quarterly
GDP for 15 years was used, and in Koop et al. (2020), where 50 years of yearly data were
used. To describe the complexity in the various combinations with the number of predictor
variables in the MIDAS setting (mp) and the number of low-frequency observations (N),
one typically calculates the mp/N ratio, which assumes a value less than one in a classical
setting where there are more observations than parameters to estimate. A value equal to or
greater than one reﬂects a typical high-dimensional setting. In Table 3, the right-upper corner
reﬂects settings with more observations than predictor variables, while the rest of the matrix
shows high-dimensional settings. One key issue is that we do not want to limit the amount
of information used in a setting; hence, a typical researcher should be able to incorporate all
available information in terms of predictor variables when facing a situation where the aim
is to make the best forecast of the target variable. Finally, the signal-to-noise levels in the
DGPs follow Exterkate et al. (2016), and weak and strong cases are considered. Therefore,
we set the R2 to be equal to 0.4 and 0.8.

5 Empirical application
In this section, we compare our suggested forecasting methods and some currently used
methods in a real-world application based on data used by NIER in Sweden. We start by
presenting the data and forecasting methods; then, we discuss the results.
5.1 Data and forecasting methods
This application aims to forecast quarterly sampled year-on-year Swedish GDP growth. The
explanatory variables are selected based on Ankargren and Lindholm (2021), where the
choice reﬂects important aspects of the economy that NIER considers. All data are collected
from the Macrobond database. The dataset contains monthly time series over the develop-
ment of important measures of economic activity, such as the labor market, consumption
and production. It consists of both national and international data, and the variables along
with the transformations applied are shown in Table 5. Descriptive statistics is available in
supplementary material. We have 24 monthly variables that becomes 72 predictors since
we use temporally disaggregated data. Transformations of these variables (e.g. squares and
cross-products) are also included in the nonlinear KRR models. We assume that we have all
monthly data available when we forecast as in Ankargren and Lindholm (2021). We refer to
Ankargren and Lindholm (2021) for a detailed discussion of the publication delays.
We use monthly and quarterly data from 2000M01/2000Q1 to 2021M03/2021Q1. We
evaluate the forecasting performance from 2011Q3 until 2021Q3. We compute out-of-sample
forecasts with time horizons t + 1 and t + 2. We use rolling window strategies including 44
quarters (or 11 years) for the model ﬁtting.
In our study, we include several different models as benchmarks to show that our new
method outperforms them. The sample of methods matches that of Exterkate et al. (2016) and
consists of PC-squared regression, squared PC regression and polynomial sieve regression,
which was ﬁrst proposed by Bai and Ng (2008). We choose to use dynamic versions of all
models to account for delayed effects through lags of the factor. Initially, we also considered
static models but excluded that approach due to poor performance. We follow Chernis et al.
(2020) to choose the number of principal components estimated based on the modiﬁed Bai
and Ng’s (2002) information criterion. The number of lags for the dynamic models is chosen

6 Conclusions
The standard method policy institutions use when forecasting GDP is based on the U-MIDAS
framework since the economic variables are measured at different frequencies. We take this
method one step further and recommend a KRR approach to account for nonlinearity and
123

Annals of Operations Research
high dimensionality in the data. Nonlinearity is especially relevant in the presence of the
pandemic and the Ukraine war, where many economic indicators and relationships between
variables have been far from normal, with many observations outside of the normal range.
Our simulation study shows that this new approach is relevant for data that follow a factor
structure, which is an empirically common situation in ﬁnance and economics.
In an empirical application, we forecast the quarterly sampled year-on-year Swedish GDP
growth based on 24 indicators used by the NIER. We compare the KRR method with dif-
ferent PC-based methods. Our results show that the KRR method outperforms the PC-based
methods. During the pandemic, the nonlinear kernels work especially well. Therefore, we
advise policy institutes such as the NIER to consider KRR methods in addition to PC-based
approaches when forecasting GDP and other key indicators of economic development.
