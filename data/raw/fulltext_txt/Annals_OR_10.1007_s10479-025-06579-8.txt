Abstract
This paper introduces a novel model integrating the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) with
time-dependent cooling energy consumption, considering external temperature variations
and including an optimal route start time decision variable to enhance management capac-
ity. Perishable items like fresh food and life science products require efﬁcient cold chain
logistics, and using zero-emission electric vehicles offers an eco-friendly alternative but with
operational challenges, particularly in energy efﬁciency and battery autonomy. The main aim
of this research is to show that introducing temperature as a time-dependent variable in route
design offers a more accurate approximation of energy consumption in a refrigerated ﬂeet
that is primarily inﬂuenced by the internal–external temperature differential. Results show
that considering time-dependent temperature variations provides more accurate refrigeration
consumption estimates, with differences up to 16% compared to average temperature models.
Refrigeration accounts for about 44% of total energy consumption, with product load and
inﬁltration contributing 80% and 17%, respectively. Optimizing these consumptions signif-
icantly alters route planning, highlighting the importance of time-dependent temperature in
effective logistics management.

commonly called as “cold chain” (Maiorino et al., 2021). According to Fan et al. (2021) a

cold chain includes a series of processes involving the storage, handling and transportation
of perishable products with temperature-controlled environments.
Perishable goods are typically transported in specialized vehicles equipped with refriger-
ation systems designed to cool the interior and maintain a set temperature. The inclusion of
these systems in vehicles leads to increases energy consumption, making refrigerated trans-
port a critical and energy-intensive phase of cold-chain logistics with a negative impact on
greenhouse gas emissions (Bottani et al., 2022). It is estimated that the refrigerated transport
sector consumes approximately 15% of global fossil fuel energy (Maiorino et al., 2021).
Thus, while refrigerated transport is necessary to preserve the quality and safety of per-
ishable products and reduce losses (Al Theeb et al., 2020; Awad et al., 2020; James & James,
2010), it also generates additional energy consumption that affect the environment. Conse-
quently, a balance must be struck between the objectives of preserving product quality and
reducing energy consumption (Fan et al., 2021).
Within the taxonomy of the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP), the consideration of refrig-
erated vehicles has evolved incipiently into the variant called Refrigerated Vehicle Routing
Problem (RVRP). This variant focuses on determining the route that minimizes energy or
fuel consumption for a refrigerated vehicle departing from a depot to pick up or deliver loads
of temperature-sensitive products to a set of geographically dispersed customers (Meneghetti
& Ceschia, 2020).
The primary challenge of the RVRP consists in accurately estimating energy consumption
along the route, which must consider not only the energy consumed for vehicle traction, but
also for the cooling load. Consequently, minimizing the total distance traveled is not a sufﬁ-
cient criterion and it is necessary to include changes in the objective function incorporating
approximations of energy consumption to achieve more effective solutions (Maiorino et al.,
2021).
This consideration of energy consumption in the objective functions of refrigerated routing
problems has been adopted in studies such as Moghaddasi et al. (2023), where two types
of refrigeration costs are addressed: the ﬁrst relates to energy consumption during transit,
modeled as a function of travel time multiplied by a constant cost parameter per hour; the
second accounts for energy costs associated with keeping the refrigeration unit open and
operating during product unloading at customer locations, calculated by multiplying the
service time at each node by a constant cost parameter. However, this approach does not
incorporate external temperature as a critical factor in energy consumption and does not
account for the impact of refrigeration energy consumption on fuel usage.
Similarly, Ma et al. (2023) include additional refrigeration costs in their routing problem.
These authors also consider two types of energy consumption: consumption during transit
(per trip) and consumption during product unloading at customer locations. However, they
enhance the modeling by making these consumptions dependent on the temperature differen-
tial between the internal and external environments. Additionally, they account for the impact
of refrigeration energy consumption on fuel usage. Despite these improvements, their model
assumes temperature differentials as constant parameter, neglecting the temporal variation
of external temperature.
Both models calculate energy consumption based on constant factors. In contrast, Escobar
et al. (2022) use thermodynamic models to improve the estimation of refrigeration energy
consumption for both transit and inﬁltration. Additionally, they incorporate an approximation
of temperature that considers possible variations throughout the day. However, their approach
does not propose a mathematical formulation to explicitly represent these temperature vari-
ations, relying instead on an algorithmic approach to address the problem.

While this latter approach is an improvement by considering daily temperature variations,
these variations are not explicitly formulated. In contrast, the methodology proposed in
this article explicitly formulates temperature variations using a piecewise function, which is
essential for accurately modeling the components of refrigeration energy consumption.
These cooling load can be attributed mainly to three components (Owen, 2010): transmis-
sion, inﬁltration, and product ingress load. Fundamentally, this cooling load is determined by
the temperature differentialbetween the externalenvironmentand the desired internaltemper-
ature. Since external temperature ﬂuctuates over time, capturing these variations throughout
the day and across different months of the year is essential.
To address this, some models have proposed dividing the planning horizon into time slots.
For example, the division of time into multi-period models has been utilized in the context of
District Heating systems (Meneghetti & Nardin, 2012) and refrigerated facilities (Meneghetti
et al., 2018a; Meneghetti & Monti, 2015).
As an alternative to these approaches, this paper introduces a novel methodology that
incorporates piecewise functions to dynamically represent the time-dependent nature of tem-
perature variations, offering an improved representation of these ﬂuctuations.
The consideration of time-dependent temperature is particularly relevant to routing, due
to the cooling energy required to balance thermal loads during transit, and this can affect
visit sequence decisions, deviating from traditional objectives of distance or time minimiza-
tion (Meneghetti & Ceschia, 2020). Furthermore, incorporating time-dependent temperature
offers a more realistic approximation of the vehicle’s overall energy consumption.
This research has two main objectives: on the one hand, to introduce temperature as a
time-dependent variable in route design decisions and to demonstrate that it offers a more
accurate approximation of energy consumption in a refrigerated ﬂeet. On the other hand,
to consider the deﬁnition of the optimal starting time of the route as a decision variable,
and to show that this allows to improve the management capacity in the route design. The
signiﬁcance of this study, as well as our primary motivation, lies in the fact that disregarding
the time-dependence of temperature may lead to an underestimation or overestimation of
the energy requirements for the routes. This could result in disruptions to the cold chain of
products or increased transportation costs.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Sect. 2, a literature overview is
provided regarding the refrigerated vehicle routing problem, energy consumption models, the
optimal departure time problem, and the consideration of sustainability elements in routing.
In Sect. 3 we present the problem formulation. A mathematical formulation for the strategic
optimization problem, incorporating an embedded vehicle routing model with a novel energy
consumption approach that accounts for time-dependent temperature variations, is presented
in Sect. 4. In Sect. 5, we present case studies and discuss the computational results.
2 Literature review
This article centers on the VRP, which, as described by Toth and Vigo (2002), involves
making decisions about which vehicle will handle each request and determining the sequence
in which these requests will be serviced. Due to its nature as a sequencing problem, it poses
signiﬁcant computational complexity as a combinatorial optimization problem. This inherent
difﬁculty, combined with its practical relevance, has attracted considerable attention from the
international research community across various problem variants. Consequently, researchers

from both academic and industrial domains are actively engaged in this area (Toth & Vigo,

To focus on this sequencing of visits, it is advantageous consider a single vehicle, a case
commonly referred to as traveling salesman problem (TSP). This problem entails ﬁnding the
tour (sequence of visits) with the shortest total distance for a business agent starting from
their home to visit a predeﬁned set of customers. The objective is to ensure that each customer
is visited exactly once, and after visiting all customers, the agent returns to their home (Gutin
& Punnen, 2003).
According to Laporte (2010), if in the TSP the home of the business agent and the cus-
tomers are considered as vertices or nodes, and the possible travels between nodes as edges,
then the problem can be represented as a complete undirected graph (for the symmetric case)
G  (V , E), where V  {1,2, . . . , n} is the set of nodes and E  {(i, j)/i, j ∈V , i ̸ j} is
the set of edges. The Edge weight or cost function C : (i, j) →R is deﬁned on E, assigning
each edge (i, j) its respective weight. The cost function corresponds to the distances and
commonly represented as a matrix di j indicating the traveled distance from node i to node j
(Gutin & Punnen, 2003).
It is, therefore, about ﬁnding the sequence of visits to the nodes belonging to V in such
a way that the total traveled distance is minimized. This sequencing problem forms the
foundation of the vehicle routing problem.
2.1 Refrigerated vehicle routing problem
Certain sectors within the transportation industry, particularly those involving refrigerated
goods, present speciﬁc constraints and challenges, resulting in a more resource-intensive
distribution process (Escobar et al., 2022). In this sector, it is essential to consider the inﬂuence
of speciﬁc process variables, as they could impact transportation costs (James et al., 2009)
and the environmental impact (Bottani et al., 2022).
The transport of the refrigerated products is dedicated to distributing goods that require
precise temperature conditions along distribution routes. This transport conditions typically
apply to industries such as food and life sciences, including meats, dairy, fruits, vaccines,
pharmaceuticals, among others. During the transport of these products, temperature is a
crucial factor for maintaining their quality and safety, as well as reducing product losses (Al
Theeb et al., 2020; Awad et al., 2020).
Multiple factors along the route inﬂuence the objective of preserving the desired temper-
ature within the vehicle. Among the most relevant are the external environmental climate,
the internal load conditions, the insulation properties of the truck container, the inﬁltration
of hot air, the duration of exposure to external air, and the quantity of refrigerated products
inside the vehicle, among others (Escobar et al., 2022).
The primary goal in the transport of refrigerated products is to ﬁnd routes for the ﬂeet of
vehicles that minimize the costs of maintaining stable thermal conditions within the vehicles
to avoid excess cooling system costs. This problem is referred to as the Refrigerated Vehicle
Routing Problem (RVRP). The problem poses signiﬁcant challenges in the transportation and
distribution of refrigerated products, such as the fact that refrigeration energy consumption
varies based on various day-inﬂuenced factors. Each variable changes over time, resulting in
higher or lower energy consumption depending on weather conditions.
Some studies in the literature address refrigerated transport within the broader frame-
work of the cold chain. For example, some research combines vehicle routing and inventory
assignment problems to establish a comprehensive model aimed at minimizing total costs

2.2 Energy consumption models
In the design of routes for vehicle ﬂeets, accurately predicting energy consumption during
operations is essential to effectively plan recharging events and manage autonomy limita-
tions (Basso et al., 2019). Recent literature has introduced some advancements in energy
consumption estimation models.
In Erdoˆgan and Miller-Hooks (2012), a linear energy consumption function was proposed
for internal combustion vehicles, based on the total distance traveled. A more advanced
energy consumption model for these Internal Combustion Engine Vehicles (ICEVs) was later
introduced by Demir et al. (2012). This model estimates mechanical energy consumption by
incorporating nonlinear functions with respect to speed that account for rolling resistance,
aerodynamic drag, and gravitational force.
Goeke and Schneider (2015) extended this model to include energy consumption for
Electric Vehicles (EVs). Their model considers key inﬂuencing factors such as vehicle mass
(comprising both empty mass and load), speed, and the terrain gradient. The terrain gradient
is particularly critical for EVs, as it enables energy recovery through regenerative braking
during downhill travel, a process known as recuperation.
The work of Goeke and Schneider (2015) signiﬁcantly enhanced the realism of energy
consumption models by calculating the energy required to travel between nodes, considering
the altitude differences and maintaining a constant speed. However, their model does not
consider acceleration or braking.
Basso et al. (2019) argued that in real road networks, slopes vary between segments and
there can be stops at intersections affecting speed. Therefore, using an average speed and a
simpliﬁed slope introduces approximation errors in energy consumption estimation. Thus,
Basso et al. (2019) proposed a more precise energy consumption model that includes detailed
route characteristics, such as topography, acceleration, and braking, into the routing problem.
Some studies, such as Bruglieri et al. (2023), treat the average vehicle speed on each arc as
a decision variable, although they exclude the impact of trafﬁc conditions. Ferro et al. (2020)
presented a more complex energy consumption model that considers key factors, including
speed, vehicle load, terrain slope and the effect of stops made during route.
Most of the research in energy consumption modeling has been developed in the context
of non-refrigerated transport, with studies speciﬁc to refrigerated transport being scarce.
According to Garnett (2007), energy consumption for refrigeration in logistics is a highly
uncertain variable, and it is challenging to ﬁnd a global estimate for it.
Meneghetti et al., (2018a, b) proposed a model to minimize total fuel consumption, which
calculates thermal loads from transmission and inﬁltration during stops. The transmission
load is calculated assuming a constant temperature difference between the internal and
external environments during the journey. Their results indicate that refrigeration-related
consumption accounts for about 10% of the total energy consumption (including both trac-
tion and refrigeration).

While the suggested route aligns with simpler VRP models, the study highlights that

In a more recent article, Liu et al. (2023) also consider the difference between the required
internal temperature and the external ambient temperature as a deterministic and constant
parameter. To simplify the model, the authors assume a ﬁxed consumption rate per hour
based on this temperature difference. However, this approach is unrealistic, since the external
temperature does not remain constant throughout the day.
Therefore, there will be moments during the route where refrigeration consumption
increases due to rising external temperatures, and other hours where consumption decreases
as temperatures drop. Consequently, for a more accurate estimation of refrigeration con-
sumption, it is essential to explore how to consider this ﬂuctuation in external temperature
over time.
Regarding the need to consider the time-dependent nature of temperature in energy con-
sumption models for refrigeration, no studies were found that provide an explicit formulation
for vehicle routing problems. This gap represents the primary challenge that this article seeks
to address, by proposing a novel formulation that incorporates thermodynamic models for
calculating these energy consumptions, using a piecewise function to represent external tem-
perature variations.
Therefore, the temporal control of the route becomes a determining factor, and for this
reason, the starting time of the tour is considered a decision variable from which all tem-
poral control of the visit sequence is derived. This enriches the decision-making process by
including a decision variable to control the operation in refrigerated logistics.
2.3 Optimal star time problem
In the context of routing that accounts for energy consumption due to refrigeration based on
the time-dependent nature of temperature, temporal control over the visit sequence is cru-
cial for calculating energy consumption. To achieve the lowest possible energy consumption,
determining the optimal starting time of the tour becomes particularly relevant. This subprob-
lem, known in the literature as the “Optimal Start Time Problem” (OSTP), was introduced
by Hashimoto et al. (2008) as part of a decomposition strategy for the overall routing prob-
lem with time-dependent travel times, known as the Time-Dependent Travelling Salesman
Problem (TDTSP).
Building on this, Kramer et al. (2015) worked on optimizing what they called the “depar-
ture time” as a relevant decision variable to minimize energy consumption in vehicle routing,
considering speed-dependent energy consumption in the arcs.
As a time-dependent problem, determining the visit sequence that minimizes the total
tour time requires consideration of the temporal dynamics based on the deﬁnition of the
optimal start time, denoted as T0. This temporal evolution ultimately determines the values
of time-dependent parameters that deﬁne the route time.
Since this paper addresses the time-dependent nature of temperature, it is also necessary
to tackle the temporal control problem, where the route’s starting time is deﬁned as a decision
variable to optimize energy efﬁciency.

2.4 Sustainability in routing
In recent years, the integration of sustainability into vehicle routing has garnered signiﬁcant
attention, leading to the development of the Green Vehicle Routing Problem (GVRP). This
variant of the traditional VRP emphasizes minimizing environmental impacts, particularly
through the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions. Sabet and Farooq (2022) provide a
comprehensive review of GVRP, highlighting its critical role in green logistics and identifying
future research directions, including the incorporation of machine learning and reinforcement
learning techniques.
The advent of smart transportation technologies has been instrumental in enhancing green
development efﬁciency. Zhao et al., (2023a, b) empirically examine the relationship between
smart transportation and green development efﬁciency, ﬁnding that smart transportation con-
tributes to improved efﬁciency by reducing carbon intensity.
Additionally, the application of intelligent transportation systems (ITS) and artiﬁcial intel-
ligence (AI) has shown promise in optimizing energy consumption and reducing emissions.
Rinchi et al. (2024) discuss the role of ITS and AI in achieving energy efﬁciency and emission
reductions, emphasizing the need for integrated approaches that leverage these technologies
to address challenges in the transportation sector.
These studies underscore the importance of incorporating sustainability considerations
into vehicle routing and the potential of smart transportation technologies to facilitate greener
and more efﬁcient logistics operations.
3 Problem statement
The vehicle routing problem (VRP) was introduced by Dantzig and Ramser (1959) in the sem-
inal article titled “the truck dispatching problem”. This problem can be generically described
as follows: given a set of transportation request and a ﬂeet of vehicles, the primary objective
is to determine a set of routes to fulﬁlls all (or some) of the transportation request using the
available ﬂeet, while minimizing the cost.
While refrigerated products require special logistics conditions to preserve their quality
and safety, where refrigeration is one of the most critical aspects (Awad et al., 2020), this
cooling in transportation leads to higher energy consumption (Maiorino et al., 2021) and,
consequently, ampliﬁes environmental pressures. For many authors, including Casella et al.
(2022); Hiermann et al. (2016); Wang et al. (2023); Zhang and Fujimori (2020), electriﬁcation
isviewedasanefﬁcientalternativeforreducingtheenvironmentalimpactinthetransportation
sector.
However, the application of electric vehicles to refrigerated transport of perishable goods
introduces the need of meticulous energy consumption control during vehicle routes to
properly manage battery autonomy, a major limitation in adopting this technology for trans-
portation (Wang & Zhao, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023a, b).
This article addresses the routing problem for a refrigerated electric vehicle in a collection
process, considering the challenge of precise energy control to achieve greater accuracy in
consumption and gaining greater management capacity from the optimal start time. This
involves not only the energy consumption due to the vehicle movements but also the con-
sumption from the operation of the cooling unit, which depends, among other factors, on the
quantity of products collected at visited nodes and the external temperature during operations.

As highlighted in the literature review (Sect. 2.2 Energy consumption models), most
existing energy consumption models for refrigeration assume a constant external temperature.
However, this assumption is not realistic, as external temperature strongly varies over time
and inﬂuences energy requirements.
3.1 Time-dependent temperature
The fact that temperature depends on time is quite well-known. In Fig. 1 we have the tem-
perature–time relationship in a region in the southwest of Colombia, observing changes in
temperature throughout the day. For example, the highest temperatures are recorded around
noon, and as night approaches, the temperature decreases.
If temperature varies as a function of time, then the energy consumption for refrigeration,
which depends on temperature, will also be a function of time. In the context of routing for a
refrigerated electric vehicle, where precise control of energy consumption is crucial, consid-
ering the time-dependent nature of external temperature is relevant to enhance representation
of the problem.
At this point, two research questions arise: Q1 How to model the energy consump-
tion of a refrigerated electric vehicle in the vehicle routing formulation considering the
time-dependent nature of temperature? And Q2: Does the consideration of time-dependent
temperature provide a better approximation of energy consumption?
To approach the ﬁrst question (Q1), it is appropriate to deﬁne the loads to consider in the
energy consumption for the vehicle´s refrigeration, as well as the equations that determine
each component of consumption and its dependence on external temperature.

authors perform from the Quantized State System (QSS) model of an electric motor. Based
on the regression coefﬁcient (φ) obtained, the relationship between the mechanical power
and the electrical energy discharged can be expressed as follows:
PE  φ ∗PM
(6)
It is important to note at this point that because this paper considers a constant and positive
gradient (α), only the battery discharge scenario is considered. This is because recharging in
this type of technology only occurs in negative gradients or vehicle descents.

4 Model description
A mixed integer linear programming model has been developed to design an optimal route
for an electric refrigerated vehicle tasked with pick up perishable goods, incorporating time-
dependenttemperature variations and the optimalstarttime problem. The collection operation
considered in this paper is on a set of supplying nodes. These bidders offer several units to
be picked up by the vehicle leaving the depot and returning to the depot. The characteristics,
sets, parameters, and decision variables of the model are mentioned below.
4.1 Visits sequence decisions

and Mi j are deﬁned.
Considering the signiﬁcance of time in determining the temperature, the deﬁnition of

4.3 Cooling consumption model
The cooling consumption is composed of the load generated by the three components pre-
sented in Sect. 3.2 Cooling consumption. The general expressions for cooling consumption
are presented in Eqs. (1)–(3). These equations show the clear dependence on the exter-
nal temperature, which, as previously discussed, is time-dependent nature (see Sect. 3.1
Time-dependent temperature). As an answer to question Q1 on how to model the energy
consumptions of a refrigerated electric vehicle in the vehicle routing formulation consider-
ing the time-dependent nature of temperature? The proposal presented in this paper is to use
a piece-wise function to approximate the temperature curve.
4.3.1 Pice-wise function for temperature
The piece-wise function allows approximating the temperature curve presented Fig. 1 through
line segments. This strategy requires the deﬁnition of time intervals over which the line
segments are deﬁned. Naturally, the more intervals deﬁned, the better the approximation to the
curve. In the present case, 8 times intervals are deﬁned, and their line segments approximate
the temperature curve as shown in Fig. 6. It is considered, as will be addressed later, a case
study in which the maximum arrival time (hfc) corresponds to 4 in the afternoon; hence, the
piece-wise function concludes at thar point in time.
The representation of this function within the mathematical model requires the deﬁnition
of a set of time intervals, as well as the lower and upper limit parameters of these time

To highlight the importance of modeling energy consumption in route planning for refrig-
erated vehicles, an illustrative case study was conducted involving a vehicle tasked with
collecting refrigerated cargo. The purpose is to validate the complex interaction between
energy consumption and routing decisions in refrigerated transport scenarios. The case study
involves a vehicle visiting eleven different suppliers (see Fig. 8), starting its tour from the cen-
tral depot (node P0). The suppliers provide bottles of raw milk that must remain refrigerated
during transport, and the red labels in Fig. 8 indicate the number of bottles offered by each

supplier. The analysis encompasses multiple dimensions to address the research questions
posed in this paper, which have not yet been resolved. Speciﬁcally: Q2: Does the consid-
eration of temperature time-dependent approximate energy consumption in a better way?
and Q3: Does the consideration of time-dependent temperature inﬂuence routing decisions
compared to traditional routing approaches that minimize energy consumption per trip?
Building on these considerations, the model is applied to the case presented in Fig. 8,
optimizing the Total Energy Consumption, which includes both energy consumption for
transportation and cooling (including transmission, inﬁltration, and product loading). In this
ﬁrst model run, temperature is modeled as a time dependent variable, and the resulting optimal
route is depicted in Fig. 9.
As an approximation to question Q2, a second iteration of the model is conducted, this
time assuming a constant external temperature equal to the average (to identify these cases of
constant temperature, the label “TC” is used). The resulting optimized route aligns with the
one obtained under the time dependent temperature case (Fig. 9). However, the differences
between the two cases are observed in the numerical estimation of energy consumptions.
In absolute terms, the difference in the calculation between the time dependent scenario
and the constant temperature scenario is shown in Fig. 10. It is observed that, in both cases, the
energy consumption per route remains unchanged, as the route does not change. However,
refrigeration energy consumption under the time dependent temperature case is reduced
by 16% compared to the traditional case with constant a temperature (TC) equal to the
average. This decrease is achieved because the model can manage the departure time (T0)
and node arrival intervals, targeting lower external temperatures where cooling consumption
is reduced. Thus, these results provide an afﬁrmative response to question Q2, conﬁrming that
incorporating time-dependent temperature modeling allows a more accurate approximation
of energy consumption and enhances time management capacity on routes.

cooling consumption within the context of the electric vehicle routing problem. The Fig. 11

These two additional scenarios represent the effects of seasonal climate variations on

Differences in refrigeration energy consumption between the time-dependent (TD) model

This improved estimation of refrigeration consumption facilitates best informed decision-
making. The Fig. 16 illustrates how the time-dependent (TD) model corrects the overesti-
mation in the constant temperature (TC) model regarding refrigeration energy consumption.
This correction can lead to more efﬁcient planning, such as scheduling longer routes that serve
more nodes more effectively, thereby increasing vehicle utilization. By contrast, the overes-
timation of energy consumption in the TC model could negatively impact these operational
efﬁciencies.
6 Discussion of results
The results obtained in this study highlight the signiﬁcance of refrigeration energy con-
sumption, which constitutes approximately 44% of the total energy consumption of electric
123

1848
Annals of Operations Research (2025) 349:1817–1854
vehicles used in refrigerated transport. This ﬁnding has important implications in two key
aspects. First, it exacerbates the range limitations of electric vehicles, as the high energy
demand associated with refrigeration becomes particularly critical in the context of refrig-
erated transport. Second, it demonstrates the signiﬁcant impact of refrigeration energy
consumption on route planning, as observed in the changes to visit sequences when minimiz-
ing energy consumption instead of distance traveled (see Fig. 14). These results underscore
the importance of accurately estimating energy consumption to ensure efﬁcient and sustain-
able route planning.
Traditional approaches, which often consider external temperature as a constant, exhibit
notable limitations. Such approximations can lead to overestimations or underestimations
(as shown in the Sect. 5.1 Experimental scenarios) of actual energy consumption, creating
challenges in planning. For example, overestimations can result in operational over costs
associated with inefﬁcient routes that could serve more nodes, while underestimations can
lead to cold chain failures or disruptions due to insufﬁcient vehicle energy.
To address these limitations, the model proposed in this paper represents a signiﬁcant
improvement by employing a more realistic approximation of refrigerated energy consump-
tion, particularly in scenarios with greater intraday temperature ﬂuctuations. This approach
also introduces an additional level of management capability by considering the optimal start
time, which can contribute to better operational efﬁciency.
6.1 Discussion of contributions
1. Development of charging infrastructure:
The range limitations for adopting electric vehicles in refrigerated transport emphasize
the need to develop speciﬁc charging infrastructure for this subsector—an aspect not
considered in the current model. A future improvement could include decisions related
to the establishment of charging stations and the scheduling of visits to these stations,
providing additional support for electric vehicle operations.
2. Thermodynamic Models vs. Constant Approximations:
The results demonstrate that moving away from constant-based energy consumption
approximations and adopting thermodynamic models, as proposed in this study, is cru-
cial for improving estimation accuracy. This shift would more effectively address the
complexities associated with energy consumption in refrigerated transport.
3. Consideration of Climatic Variables and Limitations:
Incorporating temperature variations into the model represents a signiﬁcant advancement,
but it also presents challenges. Potential random climate ﬂuctuations compared to planned
conditions could affect the model’s effectiveness. Thus, generating the route plan as close
as possible to the execution time would be ideal, posing a limitation on the optimal start
time’s feasibility. This approach also opens the door to considering real-time routing
strategies, enabling adjustments based on more accurate and updated climate information.
7 Conclusions
This study underscores the critical importance of energy consumption associated with
refrigeration in the transportation of perishable goods. The results reveal that refrigeration
signiﬁcantly contributes to the total energy consumption, representing 44%. This factor exerts
considerable pressure on the battery autonomy of electric vehicles, which, when implemented
123

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 349:1817–1854
1849
in logistics, pose speciﬁc challenges, primarily concerning precise energy management. This
management emerges as a determining factor for the success and sustainability of logistics
operations.
The proposal of a modeling strategy that incorporates the temporal variability of temper-
ature in refrigeration energy consumption and the optimal start time, contributes to the ﬁeld
of refrigerated logistics, providing not only a better approximation of energy consumption,
but also gaining manageability with the variable tour start time. Applying this proposal to
an illustrative case, where a vehicle is responsible for collecting refrigerated cargo, validated
the complex interaction between energy consumption and routing decisions in refrigerated
transport scenarios, addressing speciﬁc research questions.
The results demonstrated that time-dependent temperature signiﬁcantly improves refriger-
ation consumption calculations, which are overestimated by 24% when constant temperature
approximation is used. This ﬁnding conﬁrms the relevance of time-dependent modeling for
temperature, allowing a better management of energy consumption, which is an especially
important resource in route planning for electric vehicles with limited battery range.
The experimental cases revealed that as service times at nodes increase, refrigeration con-
sumption exceeds trip consumption, prompting the time-dependent temperature model to
optimize routes for reduced refrigeration usage. Additionally, the gaps in refrigeration con-
sumptions between the two models decrease (ranging from 24 to 6%), emphasizing a potential
issue with constant temperature planning. The potential underestimation of consumption with
this model could pose risks of operational disruptions due to battery depletion.
The detailed analysis of the components of refrigeration energy consumption revealed that
inﬁltration and product ingress are the components with the most signiﬁcant impact. Finally,
by exploring the routing impact when optimizing different objectives, it was evident that,
when considering time-dependent temperature, routes varied signiﬁcantly when refrigeration
consumptions were optimized, unlike constant temperature scenarios. Logistic efﬁciency,
measuredintermsoftotalenergyconsumption,wasnegativelyaffectedbyusingtheminimum
distance route, highlighting the importance of considering performance metrics suitable for
the application context, and in the case of refrigerated logistics with electric vehicles, the
modeling of time-dependent temperature in cooling energy consumption is desirable in route
planning.
