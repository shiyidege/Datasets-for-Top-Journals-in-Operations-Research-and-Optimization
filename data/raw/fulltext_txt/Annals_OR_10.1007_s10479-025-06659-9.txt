Abstract
We count the number and patterns of pairs and tuples of independent events in the following
simple random experiment: ﬁrst a fair coin is ﬂipped and then a fair die is tossed. The number
of independent pairs equals 888,888, whereas the number of independent pairs for a biased
coin and a biased die is the more “normal" looking number 124. We show also that the classic
example of S. Bernstein of tossing a tetrahedron providing three pairwise independent, but not
mutually independent events, is in a certain sense, unstable. This raises a question about the
stability of independent events. These observations lead us to discuss two different concepts
of independence. We also pose a few problems and hypotheses and give a few illustrative
examples. However, in this short note we do not give a review of the vast literature related
to these concepts.

1 Introduction
In [15] A. Kolmogorov wrote: “The concept of mutual independence of two or more exper-
iments holds, in a certain sense, a central position in the theory of probability".
However, although the standard deﬁnition of independence (the product property) is
straightforward, even for a very simple sample space it is not quite clear what the “sources”
of independence are and how to describe them. Let us consider the following simple ran-
dom experiment: ﬁrst we ﬂip a fair coin and then we toss a fair die. The sample space
S = {1, 2, ..., 12} ≡{(H, 1), (H, 2), ..., (T , 6)} consists of 12 outcomes each having a
probability of 1/12. This experiment is used in many textbooks as an illustration of the con-
cept of independent events. However, as far as we know, no one has asked the following two
questions:
Question 1 How many different pairs (A, B) of independent events are there ?

Let us suppose now that, in the previous random experiment, the coin and the die are
biased. It is easy to check that for almost all biased coins and dice the number K1 is reduced
to the more “normal" looking number 124 = (22 −2)·(26 −2). We subtract 2 from the total
number of subsets, because in this paper we consider only proper pairs (A, B) of independent
events (none of the sets is the empty set or the whole sample space). These 124 pairs are
“stable” in the sense that they are not affected by the changes in the probabilities for the coin
and the die. For a fair die and a fair coin the overwhelming majority (99.99%) of independent
pairs are “unstable”, i.e., they disappear no matter how small the bias is. Thus our ﬁrst remark
is that “Not all pairs of independent events are equal".
It is easy to show (see Proposition 4 below) that in any sample space all independent
pairs may disappear if we change, ever so slightly, the probabilities of the outcomes. Thus,
the distinction between stable and unstable pairs (tuples) only makes sense in regard to the
probabilities of the “Random Generators” (RGs), i.e., the factors in the decomposition of the
probability model into a direct product of other probability models.
The sample space S with 12 equally likely points can be represented not only as a product
of a die and a coin, but also as a product of 3-die (a die with three sides) and 4-die, or as
a product of two coins and a 3-die. Then the number of stable pairs will be even smaller,
(23 −2) · (24 −2) = 84 and 2 · 2 · (23 −2) = 24, respectively.
Note, immediately, that since a coin and a 3-die are indecomposable, we may think that
12 = 2 ∗2 ∗3 decomposition is a unique ultimate decomposition into RGs. However, this is
not true, and later we will give a simple example of a decomposition of a sample space with
6 = 2 ∗3 outcomes into two different products of a coin and a 3-die.
Thus, the other problem that we only partially address in this paper is:
Problem 2 Given a ﬁnite sample space, describe all of its potential decompositions.

2 Instability and partial independence

events are pairwise independent but not mutually independent. This is the case in the classic

An interesting and important attempt to describe the general structure of the indepen-
dence/dependence (i/d) relationship was presented in Stoyanov (2003), where a formal
language to describe partial structure of independence was developed. See also his papers
Wang et al. (1993), Stoyanov (1998), Ostrovska (2001) and book Stoyanov (2013).
The basic idea is simple and natural: Given n random elements (events or variables),
all their possible combinations are incorporated into n −1 levels, from level 2 to level n,
where level k contains all

the number of cases in which the basic product relation is, or is not, satisﬁed is counted.
Thus, a characteristic which is called the i/d-structure, is obtained. One of the main result of
Stoyanov’ paper in Stoyanov (2003), Theorem 1, says that for any structure of independence
there is a probability space where this structure exists. A very similar result was obtained
in the lesser known paper Balek and Mizera (1997). The proof is constructive and based on
considerations from linear algebra with the sample space S, |S| = 2n. In a sense, it is based
on tossing n fair coins and after that introducing perturbations that “destroy” all unwanted
equalities.
The potential problem with Theorem 1 in Stoyanov (2003) and many other similar results
is the same as with Bernstein example – the entire construction is unstable. If fair coins,

3 Two concepts of independence
When we think about independent events, we typically have two different concepts in mind,
which are covered by the same term. The ﬁrst is the intuitive “physical” idea that indepen-
dent events are those which are produced by independent sources of randomness, i.e., by
independent “Random Generators”. These are the events that are stable in our example with
12 equally likely outcomes with three possible decompositions.
The second is the formal deﬁnition of independence which states that given event B has
occurred, it does not change the probability of event A, i.e., P(A|B) = P(A), or equivalently,
the product property holds:
P(AB) = P(A)P(B)
(1)
We cite again the section on independence in [15], which gives a hint that A. Kolmogorov
may have foreseen the subtle difference between the formal deﬁnition of independence and
its more “physical” interpretation:
“In consequence, one of the most important problems in the philosophy of the natural
sciences is – in addition to the well-known one regarding the essence of the concept of
probability itself – to make precise the premises which would make it possible to regard any
given real events as independent. This question, however, is beyond the scope of this book.”
(italics mine).
A. Kolmogorov was not alone expressing such a view. Mark Kac wrote in [13]: “The
notion of independence, though of central importance in probability theory, is not a purely
mathematical notion. The rule of multiplication of probabilities of independent events is an
attempt to formalize this notion and to build a calculus around it.”

To conclude this section, we formulate on a heuristic level a hypothetical structure of any
ﬁnite probability model of size n, i.e., a potential answer to Problem 2 formulated in Section
1. To describe constructively such model we need three disjoint groups of coins A, B, C,
of sizes k, m and s, with k + m + s = N, plus a sequential program how to ﬂip or place
these coins. The k coins from group A are mutually independent, m coins from group B are
“conditionally independent", and s coins from group C are “rigid" (deterministic) coins.
Instead of a tedious formal description, we give an illustrative example. Let us say N = 7,
k = 2, m = 3, s = 2, i.e., we have seven possibly biased coins. The probability model for
this example is obtained by the following program. Coins from group A, i.e., 1 and 2, are
“ﬂipped”, and their outcomes deﬁne an underlying model S′, |S′| = 4, with four outcomes at
level 1: H1H2, H1T2, T1H2, T1T2. Coins 3, 4 and 5 from group B are “ﬂipped conditionally”
as follows. If H1H2 is observed, then coin 3 is ﬂipped, if T1T2 is observed, then coin 4 is
ﬂipped. Thus we obtain the outcomes at level 2, and so on. If coin 3 shows H3, then coin
5 is ﬂipped. We obtained an outcome at level 3. Coins from group C, 6 and 7 are “placed”,
their position Hi or Ti is deﬁned deterministically, e.g., as follows: If H1T2 is observed,
then coin 6 placed H6, and if T1T2H4 is observed, then coin 7 placed H7. The total list of
outcomes with positive probabilities is: H1H2H3H5, H1H2H3T5, H1H2T3, H1T2H6, T1H2,
T1T2H4H7, T1T2T4. If coins 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 are “fair" their probabilities are:

A reader may ask, “Why do we need deterministic coins?” We recall that in our inter-

independently and the third coin was placed. As a result we obtained three events A1, A2, A3,

ministic coins to obtain any arbitrary structure of dependence/independence for the whole
set of possible events.
6 Open problems
We have the following dilemma mentioned before:
Proposition 7 Every model is either indecomposable or has at least one pair of independent
events.
The challenge is to have a constructive way to check this dilemma, i.e., ﬁnd a good
polynomial algorithm for such detection. Of course in some situations one can use the speciﬁc
structure of the sample space as we did in the example with 12 outcomes.
In our note we discussed only ﬁnite models but at the same time, there are many papers,
including some in our reference list, which consider countable state spaces.
Finally, one of the challenging issues is to give a detailed survey of the hundreds (possibly
thousands) of papers dealing with independence and to make a systematic list of unsolved
problems. We mention here only a few papers treating problems similar to discussed in this
paper: Baryshnikov and Eisenberg (1993), Chen et al. (1997), Crow (1967), Edwards et al.
(2008), Eisenberg and Ghosh (1987), Gregorac and Meany (1992), Kohlas (2018), Shiﬂett
and Shultz (1997), Stoyanov (2013), Szekely and Mori (2002), Sun (1998), Wang et al.
(1993).
