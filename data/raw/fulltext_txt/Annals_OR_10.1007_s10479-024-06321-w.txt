Abstract
The water supply and sewage sector (WSS) is essential for promoting health and providing
the population with drinking water and the adequate disposal of efﬂuents. Assessing the evo-
lution of performance in WSS allows for highlighting the best and worst results achieved,
identifying benchmarks, and pinpointing sources of improvement for water services. Brazil
has a large population and immense freshwater reserves that are unevenly distributed through-
out the territory. This situation emanates a challenge that requires the efﬁcient management
of water resources. This study develops a composite indicator framework based on the robust
Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) approach to estimate the performance of municipalities of the
Brazilian State of Santa Catarina from 2009 to 2021, considering ﬁnancial, operational, and
quality dimensions associated with the provision of WSS services. Subsequently, the Global
Malmquist Index (GMI) is applied to assess the performance evolution of the municipalities
over time. The BoD results enable the quantiﬁcation of the relative contribution of each sub-
indicator to the performance score, allowing the assessment of the strengths and weaknesses
of each municipality. The GMI results show an average performance loss of 3.3% in Santa
Catarina state and considerable variability among municipalities, with scores ranging from
losses of 54.2% to gains of 109.3% in the period analysed.

1 Introduction
Universal access to drinking Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) is one of the sustainable
developmentgoals(SDG)establishedbytheUnitedNationstobeachievedby2030.However,
in 2022, 2.2billion people still lacked safely managed drinking water, 3.5billion people did
not have safely managed sanitation, and 2billion lacked a basic handwashing facility (UN,
2023).Giventheurgencytochangethisscenario,itiscrucialtoincreasethepriorityofpolicies
promoting adequate water supply and adequate sewage treatment worldwide. This challenge
requires expanding services while continually seeking to make the available structures more
efﬁcient.
The effectiveness of the policies depends on both the identiﬁcation and dissemination of
best practices, as well as the ability to implement new approaches that reinforce endoge-
nous drivers for reform. These may include incentives issued at the national level or rooted
in government programs and local policies managed by regional administrations. However,
the lack of data and information is often an initial barrier to policy compliance and imple-
mentation. When authorities have access to robust data and analytical frameworks, such as
benchmarking methods, they can establish an adequate baseline and adopt mechanisms that
imitate competition and encourage greater efﬁciency (Mumssen & Saltiel, 2018).
The WSS sector is affected by infrastructure economics, economic policies, and the
behaviour of institutions and society. The integrated analysis of these factors enables the
offering of various technical solutions that consider a broader context. This comprehensive
approach, suggested by Mumssen and Saltiel (2018), seeks to derive WSS objectives by learn-
ing lessons from what has worked or not worked, considering differing contextual realities
of countries.
Brazil holds the world’s largest surface renewable water reserve, although the availability
of this resource across its territory is highly variable. The southeastern and northeastern
Brazilian regions contribute only 7% and 6% of the total surface water, but include most
of the country’s population, 39% and 25%, respectively (Getirana, 2016). In other words,
water demand and availability across the country are inversely proportional, requiring active
management of WSS resources. Furthermore, Brazil’s recent experiences in WSS regulation
and policy, in addition to the organization of an extensive national system for water and
sanitation data (SNIS) database, establish favorable conditions for analysing municipalities’
performance and evaluating productivity changes over time.
The case study explored in the paper encompasses nearly three hundred municipalities
in the Brazilian state of Santa Catarina. It involves cities of varying sizes and a spectrum of
water and sewage systems, ranging from the most basic to the most advanced. This diversity
effectively characterizes the authentic challenges service providers face in the region.
Data envelopment analysis (DEA) is a benchmarking technique that can be applied to
monitor efﬁciency and the evolution of productivity in the water supply and sewage (WSS)
sector. This nonparametric method quantiﬁes the best and worst results of the sector, identify-
ing areas for intervention. The Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) approach, proposed by Cherchye
et al. (2007), is a framework derived from DEA that enables the construction of composite
indicators. A composite indicator aggregates individual indicators of different dimensions to
summarize the performance of the units under assessment.
The analysis of performance of WSS services requires the use of metrics representing vari-
ous dimensions - ﬁnancial, quality, and operational. These metrics, reﬂecting both desired and
undesired aspects, can be synthesised in a composite indicator that provides a comprehensive
assessment of performance. This paper aims to apply the BoD technique to municipalities

To overcome the limitations of score sensitivity to extreme values, a robust approach of
the BoD is employed in this study.
In the robust approach, a bootstrapping sampling procedure is conducted, ensuring that
the composite indicator score of each unit is an outcome of a high number of calculations.
This research also applies the global Malmquist index (GMI) to study performance trends
over time. In particular, this approach enables the assessment of the impact of WSS regulatory
and normative updates on utility performance. However, changes in the WSS sector are not
immediately reﬂected in the results of the indicators, as there is often a time lag until these
changes become effective. This situation is mitigated through an analysis of data spanning
more than a decade, considering data collected for four-year periods.
Finally, identifying speciﬁc municipalities’ strengths and weaknesses is explored to assist
the effective allocation of improvement efforts. This requires the analysis of the relative
importance of each dimension in the optimization process leading to the estimation of the
BoD Composite Indicator score.
Using a robust Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt technique, the global Malmquist index (GMI), and
identifying strengths and weaknesses in the water sector stands out as a novel and unique
approach not found in existing literature. This combination offers a fresh perspective for
examining the evolution of performance in the Brazilian WSS sector.
The remaining parts of this paper are structured as follows: Sect.2 presents a brief literature
review. An overview of the water supply and sewage sector in Brazil is presented in Sect.3.
The proposed methodology is explained in Sect.4. The case study is introduced in Sect.5.
Section6 discusses the results. Finally, the study’s conclusions are presented in Sect.7.
2 Literature review
The studies of performance in water and sewage systems have become more frequent in recent
years. The increased demand for efﬁciency in the sector, combined with stricter legal and
regulatory requirements, are some factors that can explain this growing interest. Research
on performance chance over time follow the same growth trend. It aims to identify key
factors that contribute to positive outcomes, with the intention of replicating and enhancing
successes.

In recent studies, the representation of the WSS activity required the speciﬁcation of desir-

Extensions of these approaches have also been proposed to answer speciﬁc challenges posed
by the empirical applications.
As noted by Pastor and Lovell (2005) and Oh (2010), the MPI and LPI fail the circular
property. In other words, the direct comparison between two periods, t and t + 2, does not
provide the same index that the indirect comparison through a third period, t + 1 (Rebelo,
2010). To overcome this problem a metafrontier formed by all DMUs from all the periods
under assessment is used, as in Tourinho et al. (2023), Mocholi-Arce et al. (2021), Sala-
Garrido et al. (2019), and See and Ma (2018).
Whilst DEA focuses on the production process transforming resources (inputs) into prod-
ucts (outputs), involving the estimation of an efﬁciency score, BoD composite indicators are
centered on the evaluation of the achievement of goals, representing an effectiveness score.
In the BoD formulation (Cherchye et al., 2007) the input is replaced by a dummy variable
equal to one for all decision making units (DMUs) under assessment. In the presence of
both desirable and undesirable outputs, the BoD approach has been extended by Zanella et
al. (2015) to enable the construction of Composite Indicatores using a directional distance
function (DDF). This type of formulation is known as the directional BoD approach.
Henriques et al. (2020) and Vilarinho et al. (2023b) applied a BoD model to support
regulation in the WSS sector in Portugal. These studies used performance indicators col-
lected by the Portuguese regulatory authority for the water and waste sectors (ERSAR) to
evaluate the quality of service provided to users and the asset management of the operators,
respectively. The results from Henriques et al. (2020) showed that six wholesale operators
and thirteen retail operators were found to be outstanding according to the quality of ser-
vice. In relation to the study of Vilarinho et al. (2023b), speciﬁcally for asset management,
it was concluded that the direct management model was unfavorable concerning developing
structured management systems, whilst urban environments favor managerial advancement.
In Brazil, there are no studies that applied the BoD approach to performance assessment
in WSS sector. The DEA model was used by Carvalho and Sampaio (2015), Macedo (2018)
and Tourinho et al. (2023). The input of these studies were the expenses associated with
the provision of services (representing the ﬁnancial dimension). The outputs were mostly
related to the assets of companies or municipalities (number of connections, network length,
among others, all representing desirable features). Carvalho and Sampaio (2015) and Macedo
(2018) applied the Malmquist productivity index to evaluate the productivity change over
time. Both studies revealed productivity gains mainly inﬂuenced by the movement of the
frontier (frontier shift effect) caused by the institutional changes implemented in Brazilian
WSS sector.
Tourinho et al. (2023) applied a MPI to assess productivity change in the period 2012–
2019, and a Pseudo-Malmquist productivity index (PMPI) to compare productivity levels
across Brazilian macro-regions. The PMPI required the speciﬁcation of a reference metafron-
tier that considered the three macro-regions in all years analyzed. The results showed a
productivity loss of approximately 4% from 2012 to 2019. This decline in productivity was
mostly due to an increase in the average distance to the frontier, as revealed by the efﬁciency
change score lower than one.
According to the literature review, to the best of our knowledge, no studies applied a
robust Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) approach to assess performance change over time in
conjunction with the global Malmquist index (GMI) in the WSS sector. This paper also
extends the existing literature by considering operational and quality features to complement
the ﬁnancial dimension of utility activity. These three dimensions are intrinsically related, as
the result of one directly affects the performance of the others and, consequently, the sector

3 The water and sewage sector in Brazil
As exposed by Mumssen and Saltiel (2018), WSS management in Brazil has gone through
three signiﬁcant reform periods: 1968–1986, characterized by a central national water sup-
ply and sanitation plan (PLANASA) and the establishment of state-level state-owned WSS
companies (CESBs); 1986-2007, the second period, that promoted public sector participation
and established the ofﬁcial source of data on basic sanitation, the national system for water
and sanitation data (SNIS); and the most recent phase, whereby the approval of a new basic
sanitation guideline (Law No¯ 11,445/2007) and its update (Law No¯ 14,026/2020).
The laws of this third period established that the set of public services, infrastructure, and
operational installations for drinking water supply, sewage collection and treatment, solid
waste management and, urban rainwater drainage are the four pillars of basic sanitation in
Brazil (Brazil, 2007, 2020a).
According to Law No¯ 11,445/2007 the municipalities are the entities responsible for the
provision of basic sanitation services. They can provide services themselves, hire companies
providers, and enter municipal partnerships, among other options. The provision of these
services must be planned by the municipalities through the preparation of the municipal
basic sanitation plan (PMSB). Law No¯ 11,445/2007 also established the creation of regulatory
entities, which must issue standards on the technical, economic, and social dimensions of
service provision.
The main updates in Law No¯ 14,026/2020 are the establishment of regionalized service
provision and expansion of water supply and sewerage systems. The regionalization estab-
lishes that states must group their municipalities into regions, with their speciﬁc governance
structures. The municipalities and the state jointly exercise the management functions of basic
sanitation policy. The goal of universalizing WSS services means that 99% of the population
should have access to potable water, and 90% should have proper sewage management by
2033 (Brazil, 2020a).
Quantitative targets for reducing water losses were established by Ordinance no¯ 490/2021
(Brazil,2021d).Thegoalsaremorestringentastheyearsgoby,reaching216l/connection/day
in 2034. In case of non-compliance, resources and ﬁnancing may be denied to service
providers. The most recent data on water losses in Brazil is from 2021: 325.4 l/connection/day
(Brazil, 2022). To achieve the water loss goal, the service providers have the challenge of
reducing water losses annually by around 10l/connection/day until 2034.
Regulatory agencies are responsible for overseeing adherence to these goals. WSS regu-
lation was established in 2007, and since that, some states founded regulatory agencies while
some municipalities have created their agencies or joined regional agencies. Brazil currently
has 89 regulatory agencies. The state of Santa Catarina, for example, has six agencies. Since
the enactment of Law No¯ 14,026/2020, the national water and basic sanitation agency (ANA)
has assumed the responsibility of editing reference standards for WSS sector. These general
rules must be observed by the basic sanitation regulatory entities in their activities (ANA,
2023).
According to SNIS (Brazil, 2022), in 2021, the total population served with water supply
in Brazil was approximately 177 million inhabitants, which represents 84.2% of coverage

It is important to note that SNIS has the limitation of not considering the individual
solutions in its indicators. Individual solutions consist of alternative means of access to water
supply and ﬁnal disposal of sewage in locations that are not directly served by the public
network. Therefore, water supply fountains or sewage treatment by septic tanks, for example,
are ignored in Brazil’s ofﬁcial data (Brazil, 2023). This situation has caused some controversy
in WSS management since other government documents, such as the national basic sanitation
plan (PLANSAB) and the national rural sanitation plan (PNSR), consider individual solutions
to be appropriate, especially in rural areas. According to PLANSAB (Brazil,, 2020), total
water coverage is 94.5% instead of the 84.2% shown by SNIS. The total sewage coverage in
PLANSAB is 74.3%, a difference of 18.5% from the SNIS data (55.8%). Until new guidance
is provided, the rule outlined in Decree No. 11,599/2023 remains in effect. This entails the
exclusion of individual systems from Brazil’s WSS ofﬁcial data.
Since 2018, by Ordinance no. 719 (Brazil, 2018), SNIS data has a methodology for
auditing and certiﬁcation known as Acertar. The objective of Acertar is to ensure that SNIS
information, which is self-declaratory, is reliable and allows for benchmarking between
different municipalities and providers. Audits are carried out by regulatory agencies to ensure
that, by 2024, all SNIS data will be analyzed to assess its reliability and accuracy.
Water supply and sewage services in Brazil are provided by various entities, including
local administrations, government-controlled companies, municipal companies, autarchies
(an autonomous Brazilian service with its own legal personality, assets and revenue, to carry
out activities typical of Public Administration) and, private companies. Discussions regard-
ing public versus private providers and their efﬁciency are very latent in Brazil, especially
after the promulgation of Law No. 14,026/2020. The scenario of WSS in Brazil should be
observed in the coming years as the legal ruling provisions are being reviewed. In this context,
benchmarking studies and the analysis of the evolution of performance over time are relevant
and timely.
4 Methodology
This section presents the BoD framework, which underlies the evaluation of water and sewage
systems performance conducted in this study. It also explains the global Malmquist index
(GMI), an alternative formulation to the original Malmquist index (MI) that is estimated
using a metafrontier. The GMI components, efﬁciency change and best practice change are
also discussed.
4.1 The BoD setting
The performance assessment conducted in this study involves the construction of a compos-
ite indicator (CI), according to the Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) framework popularized by
Cherchye et al. (2007). The composite indicator aggregates a set of individual indicators, also
referred to as sub-indicators, with the objective of summarizing the performance of DMUs
considering the multi-dimensional nature of their activities. The standard BoD formulation
is equivalent to an original DEA model (Charnes et al., 1978), with a dummy input equal to
one and multiple desirable outputs.

In this directional BoD model, yr j represents the desirable variables and bkj represents
the undesirable variables for DMU j (j=1,…,n), where n indicates the number of DMUs

The decision variables of the model are the weights, where ur is associated to the desirable
outcomes r, pk with the undesirable outcomes k and v is associated with the dummy input.
The directional distance vector is speciﬁed as (gy,−gb). It indicates the direction of expan-
sion of desired outputs and reduction of undesired ones towards the optimal condition. The
direction vector used in the model can inﬂuence the computed scores, so the choice must be
made according to the objective of the respective study. In this work the values of (gy,−gb)
were chosen as being equal to (yr j0,−bkj0), following Zanella et al. (2015) and Rogge et al.
(2017). In this case, each DMU can improve using the values of its output metrics, resulting
in a proportional expansion of desirable metrics and contraction of undesirable ones. There-
fore, the improvement is proportional to the DMU’s observed values. Because the directional
distance vector speciﬁes the direction of improvement for desirable and undesirable outputs
in opposite directions, there is no need to transform the scale of undesirable metrics to convert
them into desirable ones.
The model’s objective function, expressed by the variable β j0, represents the maximal
feasible expansion of desirable factors and contraction of undesirable factors that can be
achieved simultaneously.
The value of the composite indicator (CI) associated with DMU j0 can be obtained as
1/(1 + β j0). It ranges from 0 to 1, where 1 represents the best performance level and indicates
that DMU under assessment is in the best-practice frontier. Actually in the robust approach,
CI can present values higher that 1, what will be better explained in Sect.4.2.
Also, this model allows the incorporation of weight restrictions to reﬂect the relative
importance of the outputs and prevent assessments that could disregard certain indicators by
assigning them a weight equal to zero. There are several kinds of weight restrictions for DEA
models, as discussed in Wong et al. (1990), Allen et al. (1997), Sarrico and Dyson (2004),
among others.
In this study, the weight restrictions used are in the form of assurance regions type I (AR-
I), as proposed by Zanella et al. (2015). The AR-I weight restrictions displayed in (2) use

Another advantage of using BoD for the construction of CIs is that it allows extracting the
strengths and weaknesses of each DMU by analysing the magnitude of the virtual weights of
the optimal solution. Virtual weights are the result of the product of each DMU’s observed
output by the associated optimal weight. Higher virtual weights indicate the strengths and
the lower ones indicate the weaknesses of the DMU under assessment.
As exposed by Vilarinho et al. (2023) in DDF-based BoD models, the magnitude of virtual
weights cannot be interpreted directly as the relative importance of the indicators because,
in these cases, the virtual weights for the desirable and undesirable outputs present different
signs in the model objective function. To address this limitation, the relative strengths of each
DMU are estimated by the normalised values associated with the speciﬁcation of the weight
restrictions, as shown in mathematical expressions (2). In this case, a higher magnitude of
the relative virtual weights signals a strength both for desirable and undesirable indicators.
This method is applied in this study to identify the strengths and weaknesses of the DMUs
and to allocate improvement efforts more effectively.
4.2 Robust approach for the composite indicator calculation
Previousliterature(seeNardoetal.(2008),DaraioandSimar(2005),amongothers)discussed
some limitations related to the use of the BoD approach: the score sensitivity to extreme
values, the sample size, and the difﬁculty in performing statistical inference. In order to
overcome the sensitivity to outliers, Cazals et al. (2002), Daraio and Simar (2007) proposed
a robust composite indicator approach for BoD computations.

formed. This way each DMU is evaluated B times for a sample of size m randomly drawn with
replacement from the original set of DMUs. The variable B is often a high number then the
effects of extreme values are minimized on the average composite indicator C I calculation.

4.3 Global Malmquist Index
TheglobalMalmquistindex(GMI)isthemethodadoptedinthispapertoexploretheevolution
of performance over time. The GMI is an alternative formulation to the original Malmquist
index (MI) introduced by Färe et al. (1994). The main advantage of the GMI is that it satisﬁes
the circular property, which requires deﬁning a metafrontier that envelops all data.
According to Battese and Rao (2002) and Battese et al. (2004), the metafrontier is an
envelope function of the frontiers of different time period technologies, by which comparable
technical efﬁciencies can be estimated. It assumes each period p has its own technology T p
(p = 1, 2, . . . , t, t +1, . . . , P), but it is possible to specify a metatechnology T M equivalent
to the convex hull of T p, that envelops all individual period technologies.
Based on this concept, Pastor and Lovell (2005) proposed the GMI with a technology
formed from data of all units in all periods that allows to overcome the limitation of non-
circularity of the original Malmquist index. Afsharian and Ahn (2015) highlighted several
drawbacksintheworkofPastorandLovell(2005).Theseincludepotentiallossofinformation
regarding the role of each contemporaneous technology, possible infeasibility arising from
convex combinations of observations across time periods, and the possibility of changes in
the GMI when incorporating new time periods. Despite these limitations, we believe that the
water sector does not experience rapid technological or environmental changes. Furthermore,
the application of GMI did not cause infeasibilities in the cross-period analyses of this work.
Therefore, in this context, employing a global convex set provides sufﬁcient information to
effectively assess performance changes. The GMI formulation for a multi-input multi-output

5 Case Study
This section details the case study of this research. Subsection 5.1 describes the primary data
set containing the variables collected by SNIS (Brazil, 2022) that are used to construct the

5.1 Variable selection
The variable selection is based on the fundamental principles of Brazilian basic sanitation
guideline, Law no¯ 14,026/2020, and inspired by research conducted by the international
water association (IWA), the Brazilian association of regulatory agencies (ABAR), as well
as the studies reviewed in Sect.2.
Brazilian Law no¯ 14,026/2020, establishes among its fundamental principles the universal
access and effective service provision; safety, quality, regularity and continuity; reduction of
water losses and promotion of energy efﬁciency.
According to IWA (Alegre et al., 2016), water supply and sewerage services should adhere
to the principles of universal access, continuity, quality of service, efﬁciency, and equitable
tariffs.
Aligning with these principles, ABAR (Júnior & de, 2006) emphasizes that metrics reﬂect-
ing water supply and sewerage coverage, water loss control, drinking water quality, and
sewage treatment should be used to evaluate WSS performance.
The data utilized in this study were sourced from SNIS, and a detailed description is
presented in Table 2.
Financial performance is crucial to ensure the continuity of service provision and to
guarantee the necessary funds for maintenance and expansion of water and sewerage systems.
Thisisachievedbybalancingrevenuesandexpenses.Theoperationalrevenuesoftheﬁnancial
performance index (IN012) include the annual billed amount resulting from the application
of tariffs and/or fees arising from the provision of WSS services. Invoiced amounts resulting
from the sale of water, raw or treated, exported from wholesale to other distribution agents
are also considered. Total expenses include staff, chemicals, electricity, third party services,
expenses incurred with proﬁts and debt service charges (including expenses arising from
monetary and exchange rate variations), expenses with depreciation of ﬁxed operational
assets (machinery, equipment and installations in service) and expenses with amortization of
deferred assets (installation expenses and organization that contributes to the result of more
than one exercise), among others.
Treated sewage referred consumed water index (IN046) refers to treatment of total sewage
generated. It rarely reaches rates above 90%, as not all water generates sewage. It can exceed
100% in situations of rainwater inﬁltration into sewage networks (Brazil, 2022). This variable
summarizes both information about sewerage coverage and its effective treatment, since in
some situations sewage can be collected but discarded without previous treatment.

Total water coverage index (IN055) is aligned with the principle of universal access to
drinking water. It corresponds to the proportion of the total population that is effectively
served with public water networks, including those located in areas not considered urban.
The calculation does not consider individual or alternative solutions, such as wells, springs,
cisterns and fountains.
WSS is a massive consumer of energy, consequently electricity is one of the main operating
costs of water and sewerage systems. Energy moves electromechanical equipment, especially
pumps, which are installed in many operationalunits, such as capturing raw water, distributing
water to higher consumption points and aerating sewage in treatment tanks, for example.
Although the energy tariff value cannot be directly controlled by the WSS provider, there are
devices/tools available in the Brazilian energy sector that can reduce these costs and improve
the capacity of obtaining the same service with less energy consumption, the called energy
efﬁciency. According to Feldman (2009), energy efﬁciency can be achieved by installing
new technology, improving system design, installing variable speed of pump and reducing
leakages. Other alternatives are the installation of solar panels, energy use of methane from
efﬂuentsand,acquisitionofenergythroughtheMercadoLivredeEnergia(freeenergymarket,
in English), that is an environment that allows large consumers to negotiate the purchase of
energy directly with generators and suppliers. If energy efﬁciency is greater, the total costs
of WSS will be lower, consequently, the ﬁnancial performance will be better.
The regularity of water system operation is related to the principle of continuity and the
quality of service. The Water Supply Interruptions index (IN071-A) reﬂects interruptions
resulting from issues in water supply units (from production to distribution). The most com-
mon causes of these interruptions include power outage and repairs in the water system.
However, interruptions resulting from problems in drinking water production, network pres-
sure and undersizing of plumbing, which in SNIS constitutes the indicator IN073 (dwelling
units affected by shutdowns), were not considered due to the insufﬁcient amount of infor-
mation available in for this study. In order to allow the water supply interruptions index
(IN071-A) to increase according to a greater number of water system stops and affected
dwelling units, the indicator was adjusted to a percentage unit and identiﬁed as IN071-A
(Adapted). The total amount of active dwelling units used in this adaptation corresponds to
AG003 metric code from SNIS. It is important to note that IN071-A provides information
about interruptions lasting six hours or more.

control of distributed water. However, identifying the infectious agent is not simple because
the number of different possible pathogens is large and their concentration in potable water
is too small for direct enumeration to be possible. Therefore, the presence of pathogens is

Total equivalent staff productivity index (IN102) represents staff productivity depending

under performance, so the variable was considered desirable as the goals established by
Brazil’s Law No. 14,026/2020 tend to increase investments in the sector and, consequently,
demand greater staff productivity.
5.2 Data collection
The data of water supply and sewage services used in this study were obtained from the
national system for water and sanitation data (SNIS). SNIS is provided in a yearly basis
and is the ofﬁcial source of basic sanitation data in Brazil. This study employs the metrics
collected by SNIS during thirteen years, from 2009 to 2021, for the state of Santa Catarina,
a federative unit in southern Brazil. Santa Catarina has 295 municipalities and a population
of over 7,6million inhabitants (IBGE, 2022). The water and sewage services in Santa Cata-
rina are provided by the local administrations, government-controlled companies, municipal
companies, autarchies and private companies.
In some cases municipalities are served by more than one provider. This usually occurs
when one company manages water services and the other manages sewerage services. There
are also cases of different water companies serving speciﬁc regions of the same municipality.
Previous studies on the evaluation of performance of Brazilian water and sewage services,
as Tourinho et al. (2023), considered a sample only with municipalities with a single water
supply and sanitation company to avoid the bias of associating the volumes to a particular
municipality. To accomplish the objectives of this study, a decision was made to aggregate the
data from all service providers in municipalities with multiple providers, and subsequently
recalculate the indicators. This approach facilitates a comprehensive evaluation from the
municipality’s perspective in terms of goal attainment.

5.3 Descriptive statistics
The individual analysis of descriptive statistics of the variables allows a ﬁrst view of WSS
in Brazil in the period studied. The data available in Table 3 show that efforts to universalize
WSS services have been successful and sewage treatment (IN046) and water supply coverage
(IN055) have evolved gradually.
The pursuit for higher quality in services is also yielding positive results, as evidenced
by the indicators related to water supply interruptions (IN071-A) and out-of-standard water
quality (IN084), which have gradually decreased.
Financial performance (IN012), water losses (IN050) and staff productivity (IN102) show
stability, with no signiﬁcant variations over time.
On the other hand the energy spending has signiﬁcantly increased throughout the period.
This is likely a consequence of the Brazilian Tariff Flag System, which was implemented
in 2015, resulting in an almost 50% increase in energy tariffs in that year. The ﬂag system
is activated when there is a restriction to energy generation by hydroelectric plants, which
provide cheaper energy. The green ﬂag indicates that conditions for generating hydroelectric
energy are favorable and the tariff has no increases. For the other ﬂags, there are increases in
tariff values which are proportional to the energy generation costs (Brazil, 2022). A positive
side effect of the increase in energy tariffs is that, since then, the topic of energy efﬁciency
has become recurrent in WSS.

according to the methodology described in Sect.4.2;
3. Based on the results of the composite indicators for each year and for the metafrontier, the
global Malmquist index (GMI) is calculated as explained in Sect.4.3. The GMI is used
to determine performance change in the period considered, spanning from 2009 to 2021
considering intervals of four years;

6.1 Robust composite indicator’results
This subsection discusses the ﬁndings associated with the estimation of the robust BoD
composite indicator, which reveal the relative position of municipalities regarding their per-
formance in each of the four years analysed.
As the composite indicator considered both desirable and undesirable outputs, a Direc-
tional BoD model was computed adopting the formulation presented in (1). Regarding the
weight restrictions shown in (2), different values of φr and φk ranging from 1% to 5% were
tested, and the results remained stable.
After running this sensitivity analysis, the value of 1% was chosen since it is the option that
allows greater ﬂexibility for analysis, allowing more accurate detection of weaknesses, and,
simultaneously, preventing assessments that could disregard certain indicators by assigning
them weights equal to zero (Vilarinho et al., 2023a). For all indicators, the values of φr
and φk were set for the same value of 1%. This approach avoids pre-assigning any relative
importance and allows differences in importance to emerge naturally from the optimization
process.
For the robust composite indicator, the values of the parameters m and B must be deter-
mined. B is often a high number, and for this study, the value of 2,000 was employed for B.
Daraio and Simar (2007) explain that there are no ﬁxed rules to select the value of m. Follow-
ing those authors, a sensitivity analysis using several levels of m and evaluating the number of
super-performing units for 2021 was performed to decide the value of bootstrapping sample
size m.
Figure1 shows the graph with the resulting percentage of “super-performing” DMUs in
the robust composite indicator computations for several values of m. These ﬁndings led to the
choice of m=150. At this value, the proportion of “super-performing” units reduces, whereas
it remains relatively stable at higher values. The same value of m was used for the four years
analysed. The results were obtained using packages Rglpk (Hornik et al., 2012) and lpSolve
(Berkelaar, 2015) in R software.

6.2 Results of the global Malmquist index and its components
The results of the global Malmquist index (GMI) provide an overview of the evolution of
water supply and sewerage services in Santa Catarina’s municipalities for the period 2009–
2021. Table 6 shows the average results for GMI and its components, efﬁciency change (EC)
and best practice change (BPC).
The average score of the global Malmquist index (GMI) is equal to 0.967, and reveals
a performance loss of approximately 3% between 2009 and 2021. This result is mostly
attributable to a decline of the performance frontier, reﬂected by BPC lower than one. As
the performance frontier decreased, the DMUs’ average distance to the frontier reduced,
reﬂected by EC higher than one (1.083). As shown in Table 6, BPC and EC tend to have
opposite trends, except in the period 2017–2021, where EC, BPC and GMI values were very
close to one, indicating a scenario of stability.
This result aligns with the conclusions revealed by Tourinho et al. (2023) regarding pro-
ductivity change in Brazilian water services from 2012 to 2019, as exposed in Sect.2. The
authors hypothesized that the unusual pattern of the best-practices trend could be attributed
to the period of macroeconomic instability experienced by Brazil from 2011 onwards. The

6.3 Strengths and weaknesses analysis
The determination of the relative importance of each variable to the performance of each
DMU, based on the analysis of the AR-I weight restrictions, was carried out in this study to
identify the strengths and weaknesses of each municipality.
Figure3 shows the spatial distribution of strengths in Santa Catarina in 2021. Through
the map it is possible to note that total water coverage (IN055) and energy spending (IN060)
are the main strengths of the state. The water coverage was the main strength in some of
the largest municipalities, such as Blumenau, Chapecó, Criciúma, Florianópolis, Jaraguá do
Sul, Joinville and Lages. In the opposite direction, the variables treated sewage referred to
consumed water (IN046) and water supply interruptions (IN071-A) were not identiﬁed as
the main strength by any municipality.
As an example, the case of Anitápolis, the second municipality that improved performance
the most over time, will be presented. Figure4 shows the contribution of each variable to
Anitápolis performance in years 2009 and 2021.
It is evident from Fig.4 that in 2009 the quality dimension was the most relevant for
the municipality, followed closely by the operational dimension. In terms of quality, IN050
(related to water losses) was Anitápolis greatest strength, representing around 50% of the
relative contributions of all indicators. In the operational aspect, IN060 (related to electricity
expenses) was its greatest strength, despite the metric value was replaced from missing
instances. The ﬁnancial performance index (IN012) also was replaced from missing instances
and contributed just 1%, resulting from the calculation of the left-hand side of the weight
restriction, as shown in Subsection 6.1.
In 2021, IN060 continued to be the second largest strength, but with a smaller contribution
than that observed in 2009. Water losses represented the main strength of Anitápolis both in
2009 and 2021. The fact that the two main strengths are undesired variables indicates that the
municipality may be adopting effective actions to reduce energy consumption and to mitigate
water losses, becoming a benchmark for other municipalities with similar characteristics that
need to evolve in these indicators.

7 Conclusions
This study assesses the evolution of performance in municipalities of Santa Catarina State in
Southern Brazil for the provision of water supply and sewage services. The BoD technique is
used to synthesize eight metrics that reﬂect the ﬁnancial, operational, and quality dimensions
of WSS into a composite indicator.
Theframeworkadoptedisorganisedinthreestages.Theﬁrststageusedarobustdirectional
BoD model to overcome the limitation related to sensitivity to extreme values that can bias
the results from basic DEA applications. The eight indicators to be aggregated reﬂect desired
and undesired aspects of the WSS requiring the use of a directional distance functions in the
BoD computation.
The second stage involved the estimation of the global Malmquist index (GMI) and its
components, efﬁciency change (EC) and best practice change (BPC). This allowed the assess-
ment of performance change over time and determining whether the performance changes
were related to the general development of the sector in terms of the maximum performance
levels observed at the frontier or to the convergence of the municipalities under analysis
towards the frontier.

The third stage was the identiﬁcation of the strengths and weaknesses of each municipal-

The unprecedented integrated application of these three methods in WSS analysis provides
a holistic picture of the sector, starting with a state-level overview and reaching speciﬁc results
bymunicipalities.Consequently,factualguidelinestoincreaseperformanceandachievegoals
may be pursued.
The study’s ﬁndings shows the existence of signiﬁcant room for improvement in Santa
Catarina’s WSS. Several municipalities did not reach the intended performance levels, cor-
responding to the best practices observed in peers. Urgent changes are necessary so that the
universalization and quality goals imposed by Brazilian Law no. 14,026/2020 are met. The
same applies to the sustainable development goals established by the United Nations.
Subsequent studies could use the most recent data to continue evaluating performance and
to check whether the stability of the 2017–2021 period characterizes a transition phase for
effective advances in the sector. Contextual variables can also be used to evaluate the effects
of exogenous factors on the results.
The main limitations of the study are based on the available data set. Even though the
data is provided by an ofﬁcial source, the information collected is self-declared by providers,
which in some speciﬁc cases may compromise the reliability.
In conclusion, the approach proposed in this study to assess the evolution of perfor-
mance constitutes a relevant diagnostic and prognosis tool for WSS, contributing to a better
understanding and proposition of assertive measures for the sector to comply with legal and
regulatory requirements and provide the population with high-quality services.
