Abstract
This paper investigates the dynamics of crude oil markets proxied by the USO ETF. Based on
a preliminary analysis of the time series of implied volatility surfaces, we propose a model
accounting for multiple stochastic volatilities, co-jumps, and self-exciting jump intensity. We
estimate the model on a time series of option prices using a quasi-Bayesian methodology
that exploits sequential Monte Carlo methods. We compare the performances of the proposed
model with several nested and non-nested speciﬁcations. Our results show that both multiple
variance factors and self-excitation in the jumps process are needed for accurate model ﬁtting
and to improve out-of-sample forecasting.

1 Introduction
The United States Oil (USO) fund is an Exchange Traded Fund (ETF) that tracks the spot
price of West Texas Intermediate (WTI) through trading strategies based on the ﬁrst near-
month WTI Light Sweet Crude Oil futures. As a result, it is typically considered a good
proxy for the WTI spot price (see Ignatieva & Wong, 2022). It offers an effective means of
investing in the spot price of WTI without the need to hold the physical commodity, enabling
investors to ride short-term moves in crude oil prices (see Ruan & Zhang, 2018). For these
reasons, it has become one of the largest and most liquid oil ETFs available on the market,
with a liquidity approximately ten times greater than that of lead-month WTI futures (see
Ignatieva & Wong 2024, Figure 3). The Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE) launched
options on USO in 2007 due to its great popularity among traders. In Figure 1 we show the

To this aim, Ignatieva and Wong (2022), Ignatieva and Wong (2024) estimate a battery of
one-factor models for the spot price of USO featuring stochastic volatility and price-volatility
co-jumps. They examine the empirical performance of these models using daily and high-
frequency spot returns, and conclude that co-jumps and stochastic volatility are essential
for appropriate modeling. The scope of the present paper is to offer novel perspectives on
the empirical behavior of USO’s returns by complementing the results of Ignatieva and
Wong (2022), Ignatieva and Wong (2024). This is achieved by comparing several multi-
factor models estimated using time series of option prices. The main difference between the
two approaches is that using spot returns allows for estimating model parameters under the
historical measure, while option data allow for identifying parameters under the risk-neutral
measure (only the latter can be used directly for pricing and hedging derivatives). In addition,
option data are notoriously more informative than spot returns (see, e.g., Fulop & Li 2019),
with important implications on the econometric estimation. Therefore, time series of option
prices can be used to provide new insights on the empirical behavior of USO’s returns.
Motivated by a preliminary data exploration, we propose a multi-factor model for the spot
price of USO, featuring stochastic volatility and co-jumps (as in Ignatieva & Wong, 2022),
but with the addition of a second volatility factor and stochastic (self-exciting) jump intensity.
The increased complexity of the proposed model requires the usage of more informative data
rather than just spot returns.1 However, the highly non-linear relationship between option
prices, static parameters, and latent states renders econometric estimation difﬁcult and com-
putationally heavy. When considering options on USO, Ruan and Zhang (2018) show that
risk-neutral cumulants can be efﬁciently computed from quoted option prices using the pro-
cedure outlined in Bakshi et al. (2003). Therefore, we can use the econometric methodology
proposed in Brignone et al. (2023) for fast and accurate estimation. This is a quasi-Bayesian
extension of the method proposed in Feunou and Okou (2018), which exploits risk-neutral
cumulants of log-returns as observable variables and their linear relationship (under afﬁne

model with a single volatility factor, stochastic central tendency, and self-exciting co-jumps.

demanding and do not provide clear improvements over our main speciﬁcation.
The contents of this paper relate to existing literature in various ways. First, they are
connected to a recent stream of literature focusing on the accurate modeling of USO spot
returns (Ignatieva & Wong, 2022, 2024; Le Grand & Schneider, 2022). With respect to
Ignatieva and Wong (2022), Ignatieva and Wong (2024), we highlight three main differences.
i) We consider a more general model setup.3 ii) We consider option data rather than spot
returns for model estimation. iii) It is well known that informative observations such as
derivative prices render the standard Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods adopted
in Ignatieva and Wong (2022) difﬁcult to tune (e.g., Fulop & Li, 2019). Therefore, to deal
with the highly informative nature of option data, we employ a ﬂexible SMC method based
on likelihood tempering. Le Grand and Schneider (2022) propose and estimate a two-state
regime-switching volatility model using the CBOE Oil Volatility Index and WTI crude oil
futures as observed variables. In the present work, we consider a richer option-based dataset
and estimate a more general model speciﬁcation.
Second, it relates to several papers on modeling the price of energy-related products using
afﬁne option pricing models. Classical models have been proposed in Gibson and Schwartz
(1990), Schwartz (1997), Casassus and Collin-Dufresne (2005). More recently, Shiraya and
Takahashi (2011) consider the Heston model, Larsson and Nossman (2011) and Brooks
and Prokopczuk (2013) consider the Dufﬁe et al. (2000) stochastic volatility model with
jumps in both the variance and spot price processes, Trolle and Schwartz (2009), Hughen
(2010) and Cortazar et al. (2017) consider multi-factor models without jumps. Some authors
have focused on more elaborate jump structures. Brix et al. (2018) propose a multi-factor
model with jumps and seasonality to model the spot price of natural gas, Crosby and Frau
(2022) extend the model of Trolle and Schwartz (2009) to accommodate for multiple types
of jumps with constant jump intensity. Brignone et al. (2024) consider a four-factor model
with self-exciting jumps for commodity markets with the scope of pricing exotic options.
Gonzato and Sgarra (2021) propose a model featuring one volatility factor and self-exciting
jumps estimated using crude oil spot and futures data. By contrast, we consider an alternative
underlying and propose a different model with a more general jump intensity speciﬁcation
and a second distinct volatility factor.

Third, it relates to the extensive literature on estimating afﬁne models using option data.
For a more comprehensive list of important contributions, we refer to Andersen et al. (2015),
Cortazar et al. (2017), Gruber et al. (2021), Brignone et al. (2023), Dufays et al. (2024), as
well as the references therein. Using option prices can generally be quite time-consuming,
as highlighted, for example, by Du and Luo (2019); Dufays et al. (2024). For this reason,
authors have started to condense the information embedded in option prices into simpler, but
economically meaningful, observed variables. With regard to commodity markets, Chiang et

In this paper, we additionally include measures of the skewness of log-returns. We stress that
incorporating this additional information is crucial for accurately identifying the parameters

Fourth, we relate to the literature on the empirical study of commodity price behavior
using risk-neutral cumulants. In particular, their predictive power is studied in the context of
WTI and USO spot price forecasting by Chatrath et al. (2016) and Ruan and Zhang (2018),
respectively. The difference between our paper and that of Ruan and Zhang (2018) is that
they use risk-neutral cumulants as covariates in regression models to study the USO’s returns
predictability, whereas we use such cumulants to efﬁciently estimate complex continuous-
time stochastic volatility models for USO’s returns.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2 we explore our data and doc-
ument important stylized features of USO’s returns, in Section 3 we introduce the proposed
option pricing model, in Section 4 we outline our estimation methodology, in Section 5 we
discuss empirical performances and present model comparisons, and in Section 6 we illustrate
the performances of some non-nested model speciﬁcations. Section 7 concludes.
2 Data exploration
In this section, we conduct a preliminary data analysis useful to depict the stylized features of
USO’s spot returns and to formulate a suitable model speciﬁcation, as outlined in Section 3.
We start by analyzing some basic facts about the behavior of the log returns. Then, we perform
a principal component analysis in order to investigate the necessity of multiple stochastic
factors without relying on any speciﬁc option valuation model. Finally, we analyze the jump
structure of variance and spot returns. Our analysis is based on the daily time series of USO’s
spot returns and on the daily time series of implied volatility surfaces, both of which are
available from Reﬁnitiv Eikon (formerly Thomson-Reuters’ Datastream). More speciﬁcally,
as in Christoffersen et al. (2009), we consider standardized time series of implied volatility
surfaces with six different maturities (τ = {1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 12} months) and nine levels of
moneyness (K = {80, 85, 90, 95, 100, 105, 110, 115, 120}).
2.1 Is there mean-reversion in the price dynamics?
We start by considering the historical time series of USO spot prices from 6 Oct 2006 to 5
Oct 2022. We perform the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test on the time series of log
prices to determine whether unit roots are present. If the unit-root hypothesis is rejected,
then the log-prices are stationary and therefore mean-reverting. Thus, we run the ADF test
and obtain a p-value of 0.17, indicating a strong failure to reject the null hypothesis of a
unit root against the autoregressive alternative. This ﬁnding is in line with recent literature
concerning the modeling of oil prices. Indeed, Larsson and Nossman (2011) ﬁnd no evidence

of mean reversion in the autocorrelation of log-returns from 25-May-1989 to 25-May-2009,
Meade (2010) ﬁnds better out-of-sample performance for models without mean reversion,
and the empirical results in Ignatieva and Wong (2022), Ignatieva and Wong (2024) indicate
that mean reversion is not necessary. Additionally, Shiraya and Takahashi (2011), Brooks
and Prokopczuk (2013), and Gonzato and Sgarra (2021) exclude mean reversion from their
models for oil price. Therefore, we will also exclude mean reversion from our model for
USO spot prices.
2.2 Principal Components Analysis
Next, we aim to study how many latent factors are needed to properly model the dynamics
of the implied volatility surfaces. Following Christoffersen et al. (2009), we circumvent the
unobservability of the variance process by using an observable proxy, i.e., the implied Black-
Scholes variance. Although this approach has some limitations, it provides preliminary data
exploration and is not a substitute for a more detailed statistical analysis (see Christoffersen
2009,Section2),whichwillbeperformedlaterinthispaper.Inordertoinvestigatewhetherthe
data support multiple factors, we consider implied variance rather than implied volatility since
we will then model variance unobservable components and not volatility (see Christoffersen
2009, Footnote 10).
The results are reported in Table 1 and show that the ﬁrst principal component alone
explains 91.92% of the variation in the data and the ﬁrst two components together account
for nearly 98% of the variation in the data. From this preliminary result, we can conclude that
the data support multiple factors. Indeed, we can compare our results with those obtained by
Andersenetal.(2015)implementingthesameanalysisonS&P500optiondata.Acomparison
is reported in Table 1. Although Andersen et al. (2015) consider a different underlying and a
different time span, the comparison in Table 1 is still useful because it provides a benchmark
for interpreting our results. The robustness of the PCA results across different time periods
is also examined in Table 1, where we report the proportion of variance explained by each
of the ﬁrst four principal components for some subsets of the whole time period. The results
remain qualitatively unchanged when considering sub-periods.
In conclusion, a PCA indicates that an option pricing model with multiple factors is likely
to capture a large amount of the variation in the data. However, with the disclaimer that
this approach can only be interpreted as an initial, model-free gauge of the option price
dynamics. The empirical question is whether a richer model yields a better ﬁt. It will be the
scope of Section 5 to conﬁrm this empirical evidence by comparing the performances (in-
and out-of-sample) of different models displaying two and three latent factors.
2.3 Analysis of jumps process
Next, we investigate the presence of jumps in both the asset price and the variance processes.
For the latter, we use the one-month at-the-money (ATM) implied variance as a proxy for
the (unobservable) instantaneous variance, since it is the shortest maturity available. This
assumption is widely adopted in the industry for model calibration purposes, where the
instantaneous variance is typically set equal to the short-term (usually one or two months)
ATM Black-Scholes implied variance, thereby reducing the number of parameters that need
to be calibrated (see Cui et al., 2017). To detect jumps, we employ the iterated re-weighted
least squares technique developed in Callegaro et al. (2017) and also implemented in Bernis
et al. (2021), Brignone et al. (2024), Brignone et al. (2024).

We would like to emphasize that this approach to jump detection has some limitations
since is based on the assumption of a Gaussian mixture. In principle, an algorithm that is
robust to stochastic volatility would be preferable. However, we ﬁnd that this algorithm
works well enough for our preliminary data exploration purpose. We detected 97 jumps in
the USO price process and 208 (positive) jumps in the implied variance process. These are
plotted as dashed gray lines in Figure 2. Many commodity price models assume a constant
jump intensity for the price process (e.g., Larsson & Nossman 2011, Brooks & Prokopczuk
2013, CrosbyandFrau 2022, Ignatieva & Wong 2022; 2024). However, Figure 2 shows that
jumps tend to appear in clusters. For instance, we observed 6 jumps in the period Feb 2011-
Dec 2011, while only 1 jump occurred in the period from Jan 2012 to Oct 2014. Then,
we observed 17 jumps in the period from Nov 2014 to Dec 2015. This is even more evident
when considering the period between Mar 2020 and Jun 2020, which was characterized by the
COVID ﬁnancial outbreak. During this period, we observed a total of 28 jumps, accounting
for around 29% of the total number of jumps in the spot price. This suggests that jumps in
USO spot prices (as well as variance) are unlikely to exhibit a constant arrival rate. We can
test this hypothesis with a simple statistical test. If the arrival rate were constant, the jump
process would be a homogeneous Poisson process and the distribution of interarrival times
would be exponential with mean 1/λ, where λ is the average arrival rate computed as the
ratio of the total number of jumps to the number of observations in the sample. For the USO
spot price λ = 0.0320, and for the implied variance, λ = 0.0685. We then perform a two-
sample Kolmogoroff-Smirnov (KS) test, in which the null hypothesis is that the interarrival
times are exponentially distributed with mean 1/λ. We strongly reject the null hypothesis
with p-values around 0.0032 for USO spot price and 0.0237 for variance process. Figure 3
shows a comparison between the theoretical and empirical cumulative distribution functions.
Having conﬁrmed that jumps do not exhibit a constant arrival rate, we will assume that the
arrival rate is stochastic. In Section 3, we will propose two model speciﬁcations in which the
jump intensity evolves according to a square-root (CIR) diffusion or a a self-exciting CIR
process (Dassios & Zhao, 2017).
Finally, we examine the dependence structure of the jumps in the spot returns and the
variance process. In other words, we study if price and variance co-jump. Overall, we ﬁnd
that there are simultaneous jumps in the price and variance processes 56 times out of 97
(i.e., the total number of jumps in the price process). This result only partially supports the
presence of co-jumps. However, when we consider only the twenty largest daily increments
of the variance, the results change slightly, and co-jumps become more evident. To provide
descriptive evidence of co-jumps, we follow Bandi and Renò (2016): we consider the 20

5 Empirical results
In this section, we discuss the performance of the various models introduced in Section 3. We
start by illustrating the statistical performance of the estimation method for Model I. Then,
we present the parameter estimates and compare the models in terms of market cumulants
matching (i.e., quasi log-likelihood). Finally, we present out-of-sample model comparison
in terms of forecasting the risk-neutral cumulants and estimating the time series of variance
and skewness risk premia.
5.1 Econometric performance
We discuss the econometric performance of our estimation approach for our most gen-
eral model speciﬁcation, Model I. The QB-SMC runs with N = 1500 particles and
NESS = N × 0.7. After resampling, we rejuvenate the particles by running a Gaussian
random walk Metropolis-Hastings NMCMC times, which can be fully adapted to the current
particle population. We keep moving the particles until the cumulative acceptance rate reaches
5. For Model I, we obtained an average acceptance rate of 32%, meaning that on average
NMCMC ≈15. We initialize the algorithm with the prior in Table 4. Due to the complex form
of the likelihood, we assign Normal priors to all parameters, but use truncated Normal priors
to respect the domain of certain parameters.6 In this regard, Figure 7 illustrates the ﬂatness
of the priors within regions of high quasi-posterior density. This indicates that the prior spec-
iﬁcation does not affect the ﬁnal result (see Chopin et al. 2013, Figure 1, Fulop 2015, Figure
A1, Brix et al. 2018, Figure 10). The acceptance rate and the ESS measure the efﬁciency of
the resample-move step. Figure 5 shows the acceptance rate (left panel) and the ESS (right
panel). A few comments are in order here. First, the acceptance rate remains high and stable
throughout the tempering iterations. This indicates that our algorithm efﬁciently incorporates
the increasing degree of informativeness revealed by the data. The ESS is required to remain
close to a constant, which is set equal to NESS, and as expected, the ESS ﬂuctuates around
NESS.
In Figure 6 we analyze the convergence of the parameters with respect to the tempering
iterations. As the algorithm advances, we observe that the conﬁdence bands become tighter.

5.2 Parameter estimation
Next, we discuss the empirical performance of Models I-IV. We start by reporting in Table
5 the parameter estimates along with standard errors, quasi log-likelihoods, and Bayesian
Information Criteria (BIC) in correspondence with those parameters. Model I largely outper-
forms the other models in terms of market cumulants ﬁt, as evidenced by its highest quasi
log-likelihood. These results suggest that both self-excitation and multiple factors are neces-
sary for modeling volatility and spot returns. The parameter β controls the self-excitation of
the jump intensity. Its quasi-posterior mean in Model I (respectively, Model III) is about 12.14
(19.74), and its quasi-posterior standard deviation is about 3.59 (7.22). These values indicate
that self-excitation is a key feature of the jump dynamics, and β is well identiﬁed, despite
its high posterior standard deviation. The parameters of the jump intensity are comparable in
magnitude in Models I and III. Mean reversion is controlled by kλ −β in Models I and III and
by kλ in Models II and IV. As a result, all four models exhibit similar mean reversion speeds.
Indeed, we obtain kλ −β = 14.95 (respectively, 16.85) for Model I (Model III), whose

the third is Model III (-2.85E+05), and ﬁnally Model IV is the worst-performing (-2.74E+05).

5.3 Out-of-sample performances
Then, the performance of Models I–IV in predicting risk-neutral cumulants is compared.
The dataset has been divided into two sections. The parameters are estimated using data
from 16 February 2011 to 5 July 2022 (2970 days) (results are in Table 5). To assess out-
of-sample performance, we consider data from 07 Jul 2022 to 08 May 2025 (740 days). In
order to determine the model-implied cumulants, we ﬁrst take the parameters from Table 5,
and then we apply our modiﬁed Kalman ﬁlter to the out-of-sample data in order to extract
the latent states. Next, we contrast the market and model cumulants by computing the Root
Mean Squared Error (RMSE). The results reported in Table 6 show that Model I performs
slightly better than Model II. We infer from this result that self-exciting jumps provide a very
marginal improvement out-of-sample (in addition to two volatility factors). The relatively
small impact of jumps out-of-sample has already been documented in the literature; see,
for instance, Shackleton et al. (2010), Sévy (2014). However, Model I largely outperforms
Models III and IV in ﬁtting both the second and third risk-neutral cumulants. Therefore, two
volatility factors are important not only in terms of in-sample ﬁt but also for out-of-sample
forecasting. Regarding the models displaying only two factors, we notice that Model III
outperforms Model IV in ﬁtting the third risk-neutral cumulant, but not the second. Thus,
when considering only one volatility factor, including self-excitation implies a trade-off
between ﬁtting the risk-neutral variance and the skewness out-of-sample.

6.2 Performances and comparison
We estimate Models V and VI using the same methodology and algorithmic setup as for
Model, on the same machine. The priors are reported in Table 7. The results are presented
in Table 8, where we compare the estimates of Model I with those of Models V and VI.
We observe that Model I outperforms Models V and VI in terms of quasi log-likelihood and
BIC. Additionally, Model V outperforms Model VI. This means that, when considering only
one volatility factor, introducing disjoint jumps with two separate jump intensities is more
important than including stochastic central tendency. Furthermore, Model V outperforms
Models II-III-IV. Model II shows minimal differences compared to Model V and provide
substantial better ﬁt than Model VI. These results suggest that two volatility factors without
self-excitation provide a better ﬁt than one volatility factor with a stochastic long-run mean
and self-exciting jumps. By comparing the pricing errors σe1 and σe2, we ﬁnd that σe1 is
smaller for Models V and VI than for Model I, meaning that Models V and VI better ﬁt the
risk-neutral variance. However, σe2 is much smaller for Model I than for the other models.
This means that Model I ﬁts decisively better the skewness. This result conﬁrms that a
model with two variance factors better captures ﬂuctuations in option skewness (see also
Christoffersen et al. 2009, p. 1921).
Finally, we observe that the computational time required to estimate Model V (169 min-
utes) and Model VI (577 minutes) is almost 4 times and 13 times larger compared to that
required for Model I (45 minutes). Therefore, we conclude that (i) including disjoint jumps
or a stochastic central tendency instead of a second volatility does not provide a statistical
improvement over Model I, and (ii) the increased computational cost is remarkably larger
for Models V and VI.
To conclude the analysis, we consider out-of-sample forecasting of risk-neutral cumulants
as in Section 5.3. In particular, we compare Model I with Models V and VI. The results of this
exercise are presented in Table 9 and demonstrate that Models V and VI outperform Model I
in forecasting the second risk-neutral cumulants. However, Model I outperforms both Models
V and VI i forecasting the third risk-neutral cumulants. In summary, we ﬁnd that (i) Model I
strongly outperforms the alternatives in the sample and (ii) Models V and VI produce more
accurate results out-of-sample for the second cumulants, while Model I performs better for
the third cumulants. From a practitioner’s point of view, there are two main takeaways. First,
different applications may require distinct alternative model speciﬁcations. For instance,
there are scenarios in which the risk-neutral variance is relatively small (and therefore less
concerning), yet the distribution of interest is strongly left-skewed. For instance, Gormsen
and Jensen (2022) show that, before ﬁnancial crises the risk-neutral variance is small, but
the skewness becomes signiﬁcantly more negative. In these cases, Model I is better suited
for studying speciﬁc issues such as tail-risk and skewness risk-premia (see Bollerslev et
al., 2015), hedging exotic options, and portfolio optimization problems where the objective
function depends on the skewness. Second, a natural consequence of these ﬁndings is that
Model I could be improved by allowing for disjoint jumps. This would enable the model to
accurately forecast both the second and third risk-neutral cumulants, which has been shown
to be very important for modeling the equity market (see Brignone et al., 2023). However, the
enriched model speciﬁcation will feature much stronger non-Gaussian properties and lead to
hard computational constraints. Therefore, efﬁcient estimation of such a model calls for more
advanced ﬁltering methods and fast approximations of the ODE system. These challenging
tasks are left for future research.

7 Conclusions
In this paper, we address the problem of accurately modeling the spot returns of USO, a
popular ETF that tracks the spot price of WTI. Through preliminary data exploration, we
investigate the necessity of including stochastic (possibly multi-factor) volatility and jumps
(with stochastic intensity) in variance and return processes for accurately modeling spot
returns. Based on this analysis, we propose a model for USO spot returns that accounts for
multi-factor volatility and self-exciting jump intensity. We use the methodology of Brignone
et al. (2023) to estimate the model on real data. For an accurate estimation, we consider
historical time series of option prices and condense information from option panels into
risk-neutral cumulants observed at various maturities. In addition to the proposed model,
we also consider three nested speciﬁcations, which account for multi-factor volatility but
not self-excitation, for self-excitation but not multi-factor volatility, and for neither multi-
factor volatility nor self-excitation. Moreover, we consider two additional non-nested model
speciﬁcations featuring disjoint jumps (as advocated by Da Fonseca & Ignatieva 2019) and
stochastic central tendency. We compare the performance of the various models in terms
of log-likelihood and cumulants ﬁtting both in- and out-of-sample. The results show that
both self-excitation and multi-factor volatility are both important for properly model USO’s
spot returns. Furthermore, we show that disjoint jumps and stochastic central tendency are
computationally much more demanding without providing a clear improvement. We focused
on the USO ETF. However, since time series of option prices are available (see, for example,
Jia et al. 2021, Section 2.3), the econometric approach used in this article could be used to
analyze the empirical features of other commodities’ prices, such as natural gas, gold, silver,
and copper.

Appendix A
Choice of the dataset
The scope of this section is to motivate our selection of the estimation dataset. Speciﬁcally,
we chose to estimate our model based on time series measures of risk-neutral variance and
skewness. First, there are computational reasons to consider estimation methods based on
cumulants. Indeed, using option prices directly increases the computational burden dramat-
ically. For example, Hurn et al. (2015) exploit a particle ﬁlter to estimate the Heston option
pricing model using enormous cross-sections of option prices. However, the implementa-
tion was only possible because because two supercomputers were used (see the introductory
section in Brignone et al. (2023) for more examples). In a nutshell, risk-neutral cumulants
