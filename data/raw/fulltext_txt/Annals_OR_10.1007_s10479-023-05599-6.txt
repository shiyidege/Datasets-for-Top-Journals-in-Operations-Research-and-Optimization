Abstract
We present a cohesive generalized framework for an aggregation of the Nerlovian pro’¼üt
indicators and of the directional distance functions, frequently used in productivity and ef’¼ü-
ciency analysis in operations research and econometrics (e.g., via data envelopment analysis
or stochastic frontier analysis). Our theoretical framework allows for greater ’¼éexibility than
previous approaches, and embraces many other approaches as special cases. In the proposed
aggregation scheme, the aggregation weights are mathematically derived from assumptions
made about the optimization behavior and about the chosen directions of measurement. We
also discuss various interesting special cases of popular directions, including the case of
Debreu-Farrell-type ef’¼üciency.

1 Introduction
Measuring the ef’¼üciency or productivity of entities, e.g., companies, hospitals, banks, depart-
ments, is an important task for academics, practitioners, and policy-makers. Indeed, a
researcherŌĆÖs interest often lies in the performance of a group, e.g., industry or a country, which
in turn raises the question of a coherent aggregation of individual performance measures
(scores) into a group performance measure (score). While it may appear simple, resolving
such an aggregation question can be challenging because the individual units can be quite
heterogeneous in many respects: in terms of the volumes or sizes of their outputs or inputs, or
the mixes of outputs or inputs, or possibly in terms of the technologies deployed to produce

One of the ’¼ürst proposals goes back to Farrell (1957), whose approach has gained a wide-

In a nutshell, the goal of this paper is to further re’¼üne, develop and generalize this aggrega-
tionliterature.Speci’¼ücally,wedevelopnewresultsthathelptounifythetwosub-streamsŌĆöthe
aggregation of Debreu-Farrell-type ef’¼üciencies and the aggregation of directional distance
functionsŌĆöthat so far seems to be more different than they actually are.
To be more speci’¼üc, recall that the aggregation theorem from Koopmans (1957) can be
used to obtain a closed-form solution for aggregation of the Debreu-Farrell-type ef’¼üciency
measures, with derived weights for aggregating the individual measures from the economic
micro-foundations (F├żre & Zelenyuk, (2003)). The derived weights are based on the revenue
shares for the output-oriented measure and on the cost shares for the input-oriented measure,
which are generalizations of the ideas from Farrell (1957). Most of the works on aggregation
since then focused on the Debreu-Farrell-type ef’¼üciency measures, which consider either
input or output orientation.
On the other hand, the Debreu-Farrell-type measures were also shown to be special cases
of the directional distance functions, introduced by Chambers et al. (1996, 1998), who elabo-
rated on ideas from Luenberger (1992). Being more general, the directional distance function
is more ’¼éexible, as it may simultaneously handle input and output directions and embeds
Debreu-Farrell-type measures as special cases. Chambers et al. (1998) also introduced a new
measure of pro’¼üt ef’¼üciency and named it after Mark Nerlove. The resulting measure, Nerlo-
vian pro’¼üt indicator (NPI), compares the maximum attainable pro’¼üt given the technology
and input/output prices and the observed actual pro’¼üt of a unit, and the direction of mea-
surement chosen by the researcher.3 Also utilizing Koopmans aggregation theorem, NPIs
and directional distance functions can be coherently aggregated under certain conditions,
most important of which is the requirement of the same direction of the aggregate measure
and all the individual measures (Briec et al. (2003), F├żre et al. (2008)). Furthermore, their

Hence, the aggregation structure for the directional distance functions looks very different

More speci’¼ücally, the contribution of this paper is to develop new results that generalize

results from F├żre et al. (2008) as a special case in this generalized theory.

6 Conclusions
The paper presents a cohesive generalized framework for aggregation of the Nerlovian pro’¼üt
indicators and directional distance functions. The proposed aggregation framework allows
for a more ’¼éexible choice of the direction in the Nerlovian pro’¼üt indicators. We consider
the general case and a few popular special cases of speci’¼üc directions and discuss their
practical implications (see Table 1). Furthermore, the proposed aggregation scheme uses
aggregation weights, which brings it closer to the aggregation theory for the Debreu-Farrell-
type ef’¼üciency measures. In line with the expectations, the weights depend on the selected
directions. We expect that the results presented in this paper will further popularize the use of
the Nerlovian pro’¼üt indicators and directional distance measures, in general, and especially
among empirical researchers and practitioners.
Among the natural avenues for further developments of this work are the extensions to
productivity indexes,18 the developments of the bootstrap approaches and the central limit
theorems for such aggregate measures,19 the context of networks in production analysis, and
game theory context of ef’¼üciency analysis (e.g., see Briec et al. (2021)) to mention a few.
