Abstract
This paper studies a continuous-review inventory replenishment model with a limited storage
capacity S in an uncertain environment. We assume that the demands and returns follow
independentPoisson processes. We further assume a ra1079 shelf life, a random lead time, and
early loss. The storage is managed according to the base-stock (S, s) policy for s < S, S > 0.
In case of overstock, each returned item exceeding S is transferred to a foreign facility. If
during the lead time a demand reaches zero stock, we consider two alternatives: either allow
partial backordering up to L B items, beyond which the unsatisﬁed demand is lost, or call
for an immediate and costly emergency supply up to level 0 < Qe
B ≤S. Our objective is to
study how the thresholds s, S, L B, and Qe
B are impacted by the system’s parameters, such
as returns, demands, and costs. Using a Markovian framework, we derive the steady-state
probabilities for the inventory level, and construct closed-form expressions for the average
cost functions. Then, we numerically investigate the impact of the different parameters on
the best policy and on the threshold levels. We compare the two alternatives and identify
situations in which calling for an emergency supply is economically proﬁtable.
Keywords Inventory · Base-stock policy · Returns · Emergencies · Backordering
1 Introduction
In the twentieth century, inventory replenishment models and supply chain management
underwent signiﬁcant changes due to increased production volumes, rapid changes in the
economic environment, extreme intolerance of customers, and increased competitiveness.
These changes were further accelerated by global events, such as the 2019 COVID epidemic,
the 2023 earthquakes in Turkey, and more (Li et al., 2021). These events forced companies to
offer a take-back guarantee and to allow non-prerequisite returns that become an inﬂuential
factor for inventory level. For example, U.S. consumers returned goods worth $261 billion and
the return rates for online sales sometimes exceeded 30%, a California-based online clothing
retailer disclosed that it made just under 400$ million in net sales a year ago, but paid out 385$
million for return items. However, despite the use of advanced inventory management tools

Inspired by this inventory-management challenge, this paper considers a continuous-
review single-item inventory replenishment process under uncertainty with the following
characteristics:
(i) The inventory level. We assume independent demand and return Poisson processes of
inventory, where each item has a random shelf life. The Poisson distribution is a com-
mon assumption in inventory models; statistical analysis has shown that it models well
customers’ arrivals in inventory systems, queueing systems, and service systems (Kouki
et al., 2020). We further consider the presence of an unexpected shock (obsolescence)
that causes all items held in stock (if any) to lose their value at once and become unusable.
(ii) Replenishment policy. The inventory is managed according to a base-stock (S, s) policy;
i.e., whenever the on-hand inventory reaches a reorder point s, an order is placed to raise
the inventory up to level S. In what follows, this replenishment is called the "regular
order." As in practice, this order take some random lead time.
(ii) Overstock policy. The stock has a limited capacity, which is an important realistic restric-
tion, and considers the effect of storage capacity on the optimal control parameters
(Ghadimi & Aouam, 2021). Each time a return arrives while the stock is at its maximum
level S, the item is transferred to a secondary unlimited warehouse or foreign facility.
(ii) Shortage policy. In case of stockout, we consider two different policies to retain the
customers and ease their impatience: the (S, L B, s) policy (denoted as Policy A) and
the (S, s, Qe
B) policy (denoted as Policy B). Under the (S, L B, s) policy, the company
allows a partial backorder up to maximum level L B. Thus, if a demand arrives and meets
zero items in stock, it is backlogged, provided that the number of backlogged items is
less than or equal to L B. The backlogged demand is satisﬁed either by returns or at
the next replenishment, whichever occurs ﬁrst. Any demand exceeding L B backlogged
items is lost. Under the (S, s, Qe
B) policy, when a demand arrives and meets zero stock,
an immediate emergency supply is triggered. We assume that this supply amount is set to
Qe
B + 1 items (0 < Qe
B ≤S) and takes no time; thus, the inventory level is immediately
raised up to level Qe
B (note that the additional item is intended to satisfy the demand that
triggers this emergency supply). As in practice, the emergency supply is likely to cost
more than the regular supply due to the need for quick order delivery and transportation.
Thus, the emergency supply serves as a temporary source of supply in addition to the
regular one, and helps to prevent a costly shortage. Note that it may happen that multiple
emergencies will be triggered during the lead time until the regular order arrives.

From the manager’s perspective, a combination of backordering and lost sales, whereby
some of the items (up to B) are backordered and additional demands for items are lost,
improves the service level and the customer satisfaction; however, it requires payment of
overhead costs and building of appropriate infrastructure. An excellent example of such a
combination found in the ﬁeld of healthcare. Here, customers are waitlisted for treatment
by a specialist and, thus, can be considered backlogged items. However, the waiting list is
limited in the number of waits. Thus, when the specialist’s calendar ﬁlls up, the customers
are referred to other specialists and are considered lost sales.
However, nowadays, customers become increasingly impatient and their attitude to stock-
outs appears to be non-lenient (Barron, 2022). Hence, many companies have come to realize
that an emergency supply may be an effective option for facing the increased demand, staying
competitive and remaining proﬁtable.
The present study is motivated by two actual events in production and operations manage-
ment and healthcare management. In the former case, we note two leading companies that
had different responses to a stockout event. The ﬁrst company, the Edentek Group (estab-
lished in 2004 in Israel; see https://edentek.co.il), develops smart units for storage industrial
equipment used in production lines (e.g., adhesive, slotting, injection and gloves). Each type
of that unit has its predetermined maximum quantity; as soon as the number of items reaches a
minimum threshold, a replenishment request is placed. Recently, the company implemented
a number of improvements; e.g., it is now possible to return items that are no longer needed.
In addition, the company made an agreement with an external supplier for a quick S.O.S.
supply when a particular item is out of stock and is urgently required. Similarly, the second
company, ONO, a vending machine company that provides innovative storage and distri-
bution solutions (https://onno.co.il). Its ﬂagship product is LogisticCare, which is a smart
unit for storage and distribution of a wide range of equipment and products (mostly ofﬁce
supplies and electronic equipment). Here, the customer can order an item that is out of stock;
when the delivery arrives, backlogged items are satisﬁed ﬁrst, and then the vending unit is
ﬁlled up to its maximum capacity.

and random systems. Any deﬁciency in the stock results in customer dissatisfaction and
signiﬁcantcosts.Here,bothbackorderingandemergencysupplypolicieshaveastronganchor
in practice. On the one hand, in a monopolistic market, e.g., in the case of famous brands
of products or fashionable commodities, customers prefer to wait for the upcoming order to
satisfy their demand. In this case, the backordering policy serves as an effective alternative.
Military systems and medical products are examples of such markets where backordering
becomes necessary to ensure the reliability and availability of critical systems and medical
supplies (Yu et al., 2005; Rezaei et al., 2018). The ability to backorder and fulﬁll demand in
these markets is paramount to meeting urgent needs and saving lives.
On the other hand, an extensive study revealed that only 15% of customers who observe
a stockout will wait for the items, whereas the remaining 85% will visit another store (Bar-
ron & Hermel, 2017; Topan et al., 2018). Especially nowadays, as a consequence of home
shopping, and increasing competition, the percentage of impatient customers has signiﬁcantly
increased even more as the volatility and unpredictably of the demand increases (Poormoaied
& Hosseini, 2021). Examples include the American apparel industry (Fisher et al., 2000), the
groceryindustry(Chiuetal.,2019;Sonetal.,2019),thesteelindustry(Elsa&Bett,2019),and
the fast-moving consumer goods sector (Omoregbe & Ogbeide, 2013). Furthermore, in the
manufacturing sector, stockouts can disrupt production schedules, lead to delays, and result
in lost business opportunities. Here, emergency supplies are considered as an efﬁcacious tool
for mitigating the risk of such a loss and increasing resilience. For some speciﬁc examples,
consider that Amazon used FedEx to deliver an emergency order of 400,000 copies of Harry
Potter books in 2003, while continuing to use UPS to deliver regular orders. In the same
year, Nintendo placed an emergency order of 900,000 games in the United States for pre-
Christmas shopping (Veeraraghavan & Scheller-Wolf, 2008). L’Oréal, the world’s largest
cosmetics company, implemented an effective dual-sourcing strategy (Song et al., 2017).
Recently, some manufacturers employed emergency supply sources such as subcontractors
and the spot market due to lack of organic milk supply in the United States (Poormoaied &
Hosseini, 2021). In early 2021, the COVID-19 emergency relief shipments from the United
States arrived in New Delhi and included 440 oxygen cylinders and regulators donated by
the state of California, 960,000 rapid diagnostic tests, and 100,000 N95 masks provided by
USAID (USAID, 2021). An emergency supply policy also plays an important role in the ﬁeld
of reliability and maintenance. Due to the high downtime costs of systems, it is important
to have a quick supply of spare parts via emergency orders. Examples include Volvo Parts
Corporation in Sweden (Howard et al., 2015), aircraft and oil rig systems, the supply of
spare parts to Océ, a manufacturer of professional printing systems (Rijk, 2008). In weapons
systems, the global shortage of microchips has intensiﬁed the need for a secure a sufﬁcient
supply of microchips to prevent production disruptions (Underwood, 2011). As the above
example show, emergency supplies are used for a wide range of supply chain problems, espe-

Our model belongs to the class of base-stock policies. The class of base-stock policies
includes the constant-order (Q,r) policy and the variable-order up-to-level policy (S, s).
Experience shows that assuming a variable order size following a random lead time is par-
ticularly practical in numerous scenarios. In the automotive industry, the (S, s) policy is
commonly used in practice; more examples include the management of spare parts, hospital
platelet, perishable products in the food industry, the pharmaceutical industry, the electronics
and automotive industry, and fast-moving consumer goods (Rathore et al., 2011; Topan et
al., 2018; Khan et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022).
We seek to determine the optimal threshold levels s, S, and Qe
B (Policy B) that minimize
the overall average cost of managing the stock. To do so, we assume the following cost
structure: (1) for both regular and emergency supplies, there are a ﬁxed cost per order and
a purchasing cost for each item. As a result of these ﬁxed and purchasing costs, emergency
supplies cost more than regular supplies. (2) There is the cost of handling each returned item.
(3) There is a holding cost for each item in stock. (Here, we include the cost for a positive and
negative inventory level in case of Policy A). (4) A lost cost is charged for each unsatisﬁed
item when stockout (Policy A). And, ﬁnally, (5) there are loss costs for each perished item
and for each obsolete item in case of an unexpected collapse of inventory.
Using a Markovian framework, we derive the steady-state probabilities of the inventory
level and construct closed-form expressions for the average cost functions. In stochastic
analysis. The Markov chain model has been widely applied in inventory-production models;
for examples and applications see, e.g., Wang and Li (2019), and the references therein. We
conduct a numerical analysis to study the impact that each of the different parameters, such
as demand rate, lead times, and purchasing cost, has on the system’s performance under
each policy and study the interaction between these parameters. In this way we identify
situations in which allowing backordering or emergencies is economically proﬁtable. We
are aware that from the theoretical point of view, using the Markovian framework and its
memoryless property simpliﬁes the analysis. If, however, a general distribution is considered
(e.g., for the lead time of the life time), the system becomes fairly complicated. In that case,
our framework may still be used as an approximation. Particularly, this work addresses the
research questions:
1. How does incorporating uncertainty in demand, return, lead times, and shelf life impact
the optimal parameters?
2. What are the implications of introducing alternative policies, such as partial backordering
and emergency supply orders, for managing shortages in inventory systems with limited
storage capacity?
3. How do different system parameters, such as demand rate, lead time, limited capacity,
and costs, impact the economic beneﬁts of one policy compared to the other?
4. What does this study contribute to the practical decision-making process for managers
regarding inventory control policies?
Summarizing, our research thus contributes to the literature in the following directions:
First, we coordinate a wide range of uncertainty in both lead times, shelf life, unexpected
events,andthearrivalprocess.Thearrivalprocessincludesrandomdemandandreturn;bythat
we capture the reality of the changing nature of customers in a real customer-vendor-supplier
relationship. We further study the impact of uncertainty on the system’s control parameters
and costs, contribute to the practical decision-making process for managers, and provide
insights into the best parameters and how they depend on different factors, particularly in

environments with uncertain demand patterns and limited storage capacities. For example,
studying the impact of returns implies that as returns become frequent, the optimal reorder
point decreases and may achieve negative values, meaning that sometimes it is ﬁnancially
worthwhile for the company to implement a deliberate shortage strategy.
Second, we introduce a replenishment policy that incorporates a backordering and lost
sales strategies. From the practical point of view, this combination highlights the interplay
between stockout events (due to random demands and lead times), which result in loss costs,
and overstock events, which result in holding costs. Furthermore, comparing the partial
backordering policy (i.e., L B > 0) to the lost sales policy (i.e., L B = 0) shows that allowing
backorders may yield economic savings, especially when fewer demands arrive and with
shorter lead times. Studying the impact of the costs reveals that the optimal policies for
both backorders and lost sales are highly affected by the purchasing cost, in particular the
backordering policy. Although several papers have considered a backordering policy, to
the best of our knowledge none of them have addressed the combination of base-stock
replenishment, backordering, and lost sales policy compared to only lost sales; thus, our
comparison signiﬁcantly expands the perspective on inventory management and has a wide
range of applications.
Third, as an effective option for companies facing an increase in pressure from impatient
customers and in order to stay competitive, we introduce the emergency supply policy as an
alternative to the backordering policy in case of stockout. Especially nowadays, as a result
of home shopping and growing competition, customers are becoming increasingly impatient
and their attitude toward stockouts increasingly intolerant. Our work provides a framework
to derive the best control parameters under such emergencies, and studies how the parameters
and costs are affected by adding an emergency supply policy. Our numerical results show
that the total average cost is convex in s and Qe
B, and that s∗and Qe∗
B are hardly affected
by obsolescence events and (quite unexpectedly) return costs. We further show that Qe∗
B is
changed inversely as a function of the ﬁxed cost and the item cost of the emergency supply;
accordingly, increasing the item cost usually increases Qe∗
B beyond s∗.
Fourth, using numerical analysis we compare the backordering and emergency policies in a
variety of scenarios, and identify those scenarios where one policy is economically preferable
to the other. We also discuss the consequences of changing from a backordering policy to an
emergency supply one, and the impact of such a change on the system’s performance and
proﬁtability. Our numerical analysis reveals that as demand and the lead time increase, so
does the beneﬁt of the emergency supply policy. Furthermore, when the emergency supply
policy is economically viable, then s∗decreases and, somewhat surprisingly, Qe∗
B increases.
Our numerical analysis also reveals that, unexpectedly and contrary to the effect of changing
the return cost, changing the inventory cost has a more signiﬁcant impact on the total cost
under the emergency supply policy than under the backordering policy.
In summary, we provide managers with a simple easy-to-implement Markov equations for
both emergency supply and backordering policies that make it easy to compute their steady-
state distributions and cost formulas. These formulas can then be used for an analysis of the
dependence of the cost functions on the parameters when some of the parameters are taken
as decision variables, as well as for optimization purposes. The remainder of the paper is
organized as follows. Following the literature review in Sect. 2, basic features, assumptions,
and notation are presented in Sect. 3. Section 4 studies the (S, L B, s) policy with partial
backordering and lost sales. Section 5 compares the traditional backordering policy to our

provided. Finally, Sect. 6 presents concluding remarks and suggests future research.

2 Literature review
The model presented in this paper is closely related to the literaure on base-stock inventory
systemswithreturnes,shortagepoliciesandinventorysystemswithasecondsourceofsupply.
There is a rich literature on inventory systems under base-stock policies; the most widely
used of these policies are the constant-order policy (Q,r) and the variable order-up-to-level
policy (S, s). Prior research shows that assuming a variable order size following a random
lead time is particularly practical in numerous scenarios, e.g., in vendor-managed contracts,
limited shelf space, and powerful retailers. Real-world examples include a gas supplier send-
ing trucks to ﬁll up the tanks of the customers, or a retailer ﬁlling food vending machines
in public buildings (e.g., hospitals and academic institutions). An excellent comprehensive
survey on continuous-review inventory (S, s) policies is found in Perera and Sethi (2023).
Beyond the Poisson demand process, we assume that items may be returnable. Indeed, the
uncertainty and changing nature of customers have intensiﬁed the frequency of returns; thus,
competition for customers requires companies to allow returns that directly impact both the
inventory level and the policy used to handle that inventory. In the literature, some papers
consider inventory replenishment models with random returns, e.g., Heyman (1978), Muck-
stadt and Isaac (1981), and Yuan and Cheung (1998), and Fleischmann and Kuik (2003).
In more recent research, Ahiska et al. (2017) treat an inventory replenishment model for a
hybrid manufacturing and remanufacturing system under stochastic demands and returns.
Yu et al. (2019) focus on sustainable inventory management with random returns by incor-
porating environmental considerations into inventory decisions. Barron and Dreyfuss (2021)
propose heuristic algorithms for determining the optimal policy under uncertain returns and
demands. Yang and Ji (2022) and Goedhart et al. (2023) consider three innovative mecha-
nisms associated with effectively managing consumer returns for an omni-channel retailer
that has both online channels and physical stores. Gökbayrak & Kayı¸s (2023) use a dynamic
programming formulation to solve for a retailer’s optimal inventory replenishment model
under periodic review with returns. Barron (2023) considers an inventory process with ﬂuid
ﬂow movements of outputs (demands) and inputs (returns). Finally, Mutiso and Gatari (2023)
study the inﬂuence of inventory return management on the performance of food and beverage
manufacturing ﬁrms in Kenya.
As in the real world, we assume a random lead time and, speciﬁcally, an exponentially
distributed lead time. Although this assumption clearly simpliﬁes our derivation, most realis-
tic inventory applications warrant stochastic lead times to capture uncertainty at the supplier
level. Mutharaman et al.Muthuraman et al. (2015) have shown that signiﬁcant losses occur
when approximating a stochastic lead time by a ﬁxed one. In particular, the exponentially
distributed lead time is a common assumption in the literature, and it is also applied in practice
when the lead time is inﬂuenced by many logistical factors. For example, when a supplier
serves multiple independent retailers, then the time needed to prepare and deliver the order
can be considered as a sojourn time; it is well known that the sojourn time in an M/M/1 queue
is an exponentially distributed random variable (Berman et al., 2008; Barron, 2022; Boxma
et al., 2023).
Our study focuses on various alternatives in case of stockout. Many recent works have
focused on inventory replenishment models enriched by shortage and partial backorder under
the base-stock policy. Singh et al. (2009), and Tripathy and Pradhan Tripathy and Pradhan

et al. (2022) extend EOQ to EPQ (economic production quantity) with backorders. Inventory

3 Description, notation and assumptions
We consider a continuous-review inventory replenishment model managed according to the
well-known base-stock (S, s) policy, with independent demand and return processes, lost
sales, random lead times, random life times, and unexpected events. Speciﬁcally, we assume:
(i) Demand and return processes. Demands arrive according to a Poisson process with an
exponentially distributed interarrival time at rate λ, where each arrival is a demand for
one item. Accordingly, returns are governed by another independent Poisson process
with an exponentially distributed interarrival time at rate ν, where each arrival is a return
of one item. We assume that returned items are as good as new and satisfy the same
demands as new items. Note that although we assume ﬁxed rates λ and ν, it is easy to
generalize the model to support state-dependent demand and return rates λ(i) and v(i),
respectively, as functions of i on-hand items; see Remark 2.
(ii) Storage capacity. We assume a limited storage capacity of size S. All returned items
exceeding level S are transferred immediately to a second storage unit used for related
products. The assumption of limited storage allows real-life inventory replenishment
models to be analyzed and hence provides a more useful tool to decision-making in
inventory management.
(iii)
Storage Management. Storage is managed according the (S, s) policy. Speciﬁcally,
under the (S, s) policy, whenever the inventory level decreases to or below s (either due
to demand, outdate, or early loss), an order is placed raise the on-hand inventory level to
its maximum capacity S. The order is delivered after an exponentially distributed lead
time with parameter μ (note that the actual replenishment amount is random, depending
on the on-hand inventory level just before the order arrives). During the lead time, new
orders are not allowed; thus, at most one upcoming order occurs at any given time
(we use the terminology upcoming to indicate that the order hasn’t arrived yet and the
system is dormant during the lead time). For simplicity, we assume the same S for both
the target inventory replenishment level and the storage capacity; however, in general,
there is no need for this assumption.
(vi) Limited life time. We assume that each item has an exponentially distributed lifetime
with parameter θ. The exponential distribution adequately models real-life time and
is well common in the literature (Muthuraman et al., 2015; Pacheco-Velázquez et al.,
2023). We further assume a total loss of all items in storage at exponentially distributed
times at rate . That means that all items held in stock (if any) lose their value at once,
and the inventory collapses instantaneously to zero after which the items are unusable
(this collapse of all products can also be considered as obsolescence event).

(v) Shortage policy. In case of shortage (which occur when the inventory level is zero and
a demand arrives), we study two policies: an (S, L B, s) policy that integrates partial
backordering and lost sales (Policy A), or using an (S, s, Qe

(A) The (S, L B, s) policy. Here, any demand that cannot be satisﬁed immediately is

(B) The (S, s, Qe

the lead time ends and the pending order arrives, the inventory is eventually replenished up

Changing policies
Obviously, the decision as to which policy is preferred is inﬂuenced by all parameters
and costs; sometimes, managers are even forced to change the policy in order to raise the
service level, satisfy customers’ needs, and stay competitive. Accordingly, we consider two
scenarios: adding the option of backorders instead of only lost sales, and vice versa.

Conclusion 2
(i) We may conclude that the more the demands (and, thus, the fewer the returns) and the
longer the lead times, the higher the values of L∗
B; i.e., the less proﬁtable the backorders.
(ii) As expected, the interplay between the different shortage costs implies that increasing
kb decreases L∗

kl has the opposite effect.
(iii) Comparing to early studies implies that the more returns there are, and the shorter the
lead time, the more proﬁtable it is to utilize the returns for backorders than for order
cancellations.
(iv) Finally, the equilibrium costkb is decreasing in λ and increasing in μ. These results are
in line with (i).

Conclusion 3
(i) In accordance with Table 6, Table 7 shows that while increasing the item cost cr
decreases both s∗and S∗, the ﬁxed cost Kr has an opposite effect on s∗and S∗. When both
costs increase, the impact of cr is dominant and, thus, S∗decreases. Increasing Kr while
ﬁxing cr increases (S∗−s∗); that is, when the order is costly, it is less costly to place fewer but
larger orders (see Fig.11b). On the other hand, when there are zero costs, i.e., Kr = cr = 0,
s∗increases towards S∗; that is, it is less costly to place more but smaller orders.
(ii) Clearly, TC∗is increasing in Kr and cr. However, Fig.11a highlights that the growth
rate is more affected by cr, and this rate is similar when L B = 0 and L B = 10.
(iii) Studying the interplay between cr and Kr shows that cr(Kr) decreases more mod-
erately under the backordering policy than under the “pure” lost sales policy (the slopes are
|-0.021703| < |-0.026022|, respectively; see Fig.12). In other words, under the backorder-
ing policy, increasing Kr while ﬁxing TC yields a smaller decrease in cr. This is probably
because, under backorders, allowing negative inventory increases the order size, thereby
intensifying the effect of cr. Accordingly, the absolute slope of Kr(cr) is bigger.
Summarizing the above, we see that the optimal control parameters and cost are more
sensitive to changes in cr, particularly under the backordering policy.

6 Summary and further research
This paper studies a continuous-review (S, s) inventory replenishment model in an uncertain
environment where the demand, return, lead time and shelf life are subject to change. We
introduce two policies in case of shortage: the (S, L B, s) policy with partial backordering
and lost sales, and the (S, s, Qe
B) policy with emergency supply orders. Using a Markovian
framework, we derive the steady-state probabilities of the inventory level, and construct
closed-form expressions for the average cost functions. We conduct a numerical analysis to
study the impact that each of the different parameters, such as, demand rate, lead time, and
purchasing cost, has on the system’s performance under each policy. In this way we identify
situations in which an emergency supply order is economically proﬁtable; it is shown that
managers need to make good decisions regarding inventories in order to gain the full beneﬁt
from using emergency supplies with short lead times but high costs.
There are several avenues for future research. The assumption of demand and return
Poisson processes may not be exhaustive in some scenarios, as customers often buy (and
123

Annals of Operations Research (2024) 343:175–221
213
return) in batches. Thus, expanding the arrival processes to compound Poisson processes
would extend their applicability domains not studied here, such as wholesale goods and
spare parts distribution systems. In this case, it would be interesting to investigate how the
control parameters are affected by the batch size distribution (average and variance) and how
the batch pattern impacts the economic beneﬁt of each policy. It should be noted that such an
investigation could result in complicated situations. For example, it could happen that part
of the demand is satisﬁed, part is backlogged, and part is lost. Moreover, due to the batch
pattern of returns, the inventory level could far exceed its recommended capacity. Therefore,
an unequivocal and clear policy should be formulated in these cases. As for batch order size,
the possibility of discounts on bulk purchases could open an interesting and practical avenue
of research.
This paper derives the optimal control parameters under the long-run average cost criteria.
Deriving the optimal controllers under the discounted cost criteria may be another important
avenue of research. Here, as in many real-world supplier-customer contracts, the ﬁxed cost
is paid when the order is placed, whereas the purchasing cost is paid when the order arrives.
This difference in the timing of payment is particularly important in decision models with
varying discount factors, where a full payment must made immediately.
Finally, a natural extension is to apply other base-stock policies, such as the (Q,r) policy
with partial backlogging and lost sales. Under the (Q,r) policy, when the inventory drops
to level r, a ﬁxed order of size Q is placed. Thus, the added complexity of analyzing such a
policy lies in the fact that the inventory level is no longer known at the replenishment times.
Obviously, integrating the (Q,r) policy with compound Poisson processes for demands and
returns is signiﬁcantly challenging and worthy of study.
