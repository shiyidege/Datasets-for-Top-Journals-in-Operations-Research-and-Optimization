Abstract
This paper explores the role of the seven constructs—Digitalization, Entrepreneurial
Orientation, Funding, Governance, Managerial Capabilities, Market Conﬁguration, and Per-
formance—towards SME sophistication and managerial performance in Brazil. Based on a
survey conducted among Brazilian SME managers, focusing on seven key constructs—Dig-
italization, Entrepreneurial Orientation, Funding, Governance, Managerial Capabilities,
Market Conﬁguration, and Performance, a novel entropy maximization approach based on
neutrosophy, fuzzy logic, and gray relational analysis was designed to retrieve information on
perceptual Likert scales so as distinct construct indexes could be built. Subsequently, a robust
MCDM approach was implemented on the sophistication scales of each construct to iden-
tify the key drivers within and between superior SME construct indexes towards improved
managerial performance. The study found that funding, governance, entrepreneurial orien-
tation, and digitalization are major factors contributing to SME sophistication in Brazil.
Additionally, managerial capabilities and market conﬁguration were identiﬁed as critical for
supporting and enhancing managerial performance and competitiveness. This suggests that
a combination of strong internal management practices, strategic governance, and access to
funding and digital resources plays a central role in SME growth and performance. These

1 Introduction
Micro and Small Enterprises (SMEs) play a crucial role in the socio-economic develop-
ment of Brazil, contributing signiﬁcantly to employment generation, innovation, and overall
economic resilience (Cravo, 2010). Despite their importance, the SME sector in Brazil is
marked by signiﬁcant heterogeneity in terms of operational capacity, resource utilization,
and managerial practices (Moreno et al., 2012). This diversity in sophistication levels among
SMEs results in varying levels of competitiveness and overall performance, highlighting the
need for systematic assessment tools that can effectively capture and enhance managerial
sophistication across the sector (Arbelo et al., 2022).
The Brazilian SME ecosystem faces challenges such as limited access to ﬁnance (Veiga
& McCahery, 2019), an evolving regulatory environment (Cardoza et al., 2016), and pres-
sures to adapt to rapid technological advancements (Klein & Todesco, 2021). Therefore,
understanding the underlying drivers of managerial performance and sophistication becomes
essential for designing effective policies and business interventions (Ipinnaiye et al., 2017).
While traditional studies have examined factors like digital adoption (Affandi et al., 2024),
ﬁnancial management (Mcmahon, 2001), and governance practices in isolation (Gama et al.,
2024), there is a growing need for a more integrated, data-driven approach to assess SME
performance in a holistic manner. Such an approach should not only capture the individual
inﬂuences of various constructs like Digitalization, Entrepreneurial Orientation, Funding,
Governance,ManagerialCapabilities,MarketConﬁguration,andPerformance,butalsoquan-
tify the interactions and contributions of these constructs towards a uniﬁed measure of SME
sophistication.
Although prior research has extensively examined SME performance determinants, exist-
ing models exhibit three key limitations. First, most studies rely on single-dimensional or
narrowly deﬁned composite indicators—typically based on ﬁnancial ratios, growth metrics,
or innovation proxies—without integrating multiple managerial and strategic constructs into
a uniﬁed sophistication framework (Hassan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Song et al., 2022).
To the best of our knowledge, no study has proposed a holistic SME sophistication index
that simultaneously captures digitalization, governance, funding, entrepreneurial orientation,
managerial capabilities, and market conﬁguration within a single entropy-consistent struc-
ture.
Second, existing composite indices built from survey data often treat Likert-scale
responses as cardinal and apply linear aggregation methods such as principal component
analysis or simple averaging (Dey et al., 2021; Talib et al., 2014). These approaches
implicitly assume equal distance between ordinal categories and neglect the epistemic uncer-
tainty embedded in perceptual managerial assessments. Consequently, important information
regarding indeterminacy and distributional dispersion may be lost, potentially leading to
biased rankings and unstable performance classiﬁcations.

Third, the absence of entropy-based information balancing across constructs may result
in inconsistent weighting structures, where highly correlated or redundant indicators distort
the contribution of individual drivers. As a result, SMEs may be misclassiﬁed in terms of
their managerial sophistication, and policy recommendations derived from such models may
overlook critical trade-offs between internal capabilities and external strategic positioning.
Therefore, the research gap is not merely the lack of integration, but the absence of
a methodologically rigorous framework capable of (i) transforming ordinal perceptual data
into information-consistent metric scales, (ii) preserving and quantifying uncertainty in man-
agerial responses, and (iii) constructing a uniﬁed SME sophistication index grounded in
entropy maximization principles. The present study addresses this gap by proposing a hybrid
neutrosophic–fuzzy–grey relational entropy approach that enables a holistic and information-
balanced assessment of SME managerial sophistication in Brazil.
This paper introduces the SME Sophistication Index for managerial performance in
Brazil—a hybrid entropy maximization model speciﬁcally designed to navigate the com-
plexities inherent in the measurement of SME performance using perceptual Likert scale
data. The proposed index leverages neutrosophy, fuzzy logic, and gray relational analy-
sis to address the challenges associated with information uncertainty, thereby providing a
robust mechanism to extract meaningful insights from Likert-scale survey data. By employ-
ing entropy maximization, the model identiﬁes and measures the inherent uncertainty across
multiple constructs, thus ensuring that the resulting construct indexes are not only valid but
also reﬂective of the underlying realities faced by SMEs in Brazil.
Importantly, the SME Sophistication Index is conceptualized in this study primarily as
a measurement instrument designed to operationalize and quantify the latent construct of
managerial sophistication. By aggregating multiple dimensions—such as entrepreneurial
orientation, governance, funding, digitalization, and managerial capabilities—the index pro-
vides a structured representation of ﬁrms’ internal capability conﬁgurations. Subsequently,
the index is employed as an analytical tool to examine how variations in managerial sophisti-
cation are associated with differences in SME performance. This dual role allows the study to
ﬁrst establish a robust measurement framework and then leverage it to generate explanatory
insights, thereby bridging measurement and performance analysis.
The novelty of the current study lies in the development and application of a multi-criteria
decision-making (MCDM) framework that operates on the sophistication scales derived for
each construct. This approach allows for an in-depth analysis of how speciﬁc factors con-
tribute to superior performance outcomes and provides a nuanced understanding of the key
drivers within and across these constructs. By identifying both the internal (e.g., Managerial
Capabilities, Digitalization, and Governance) and external (e.g., Entrepreneurial Orientation,
Market Conﬁguration, and Funding) factors that foster managerial sophistication, this study
aims to bridge the gap between conceptual understanding and practical implementation for
SMEs striving for enhanced managerial performance.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: the following section provides an
overview of the relevant literature on the seven constructs considered fundamental to SME
performance. Next, the methodological framework, including survey design and the entropy
maximization approach, is presented. The results section discusses the ﬁndings of MCDM
analysis, highlighting the interrelationships between the constructs and their inﬂuence on
managerial performance. Finally, the paper concludes with implications for practitioners and
policymakers, along with suggestions for future research directions.

2 Literature review
The performance of SMEs has been extensively examined in the literature using a wide range
of ﬁnancial, operational, and productivity-based indicators. Empirical studies have tradition-
ally relied on outcome-oriented measures such as sales, sales growth, proﬁtability ratios, and
return indicators to assess ﬁrm success. For instance, Agostini et al. (2015) document a posi-
tive relationship between brand-building efforts and sales performance among Italian SMEs,
while Hansen-Addy et al. (2024) employ sales as the main indicator to evaluate the effects of
tradefacilitationonSMEsinAfrica.Similarly,ﬁnancialperformanceindicatorssuchasreturn
on assets, return on equity, and return on sales are widely adopted to explain variations in ﬁrm
outcomes across different institutional contexts (De Massis et al., 2015; Hilmersson, 2014;
Johnsen & McMahon, 2005; Rocca et al., 2019). Beyond ﬁnancial measures, literature has
expanded performance assessment to include productivity-related and organizational indica-
tors. Labor productivity, value added ratios, and workforce-related measures have been used
to capture efﬁciency and internal operational performance (Konte & Tetteh, 2023; Landuyt
etal., 2016;Motta, 2020). Human resource practices, compensation systems, and absenteeism
rates have also been linked to ﬁrm growth and organizational effectiveness, highlighting the
relevance of internal management processes for SMEs performance (Carlson et al., 2006;
Whyman & Petrescu, 2015). More recent contributions acknowledge the multidimensional
nature of SME performance by employing composite indicators that integrate multiple out-
come dimensions. Studies such as (Booltink & Saka-Helmhout, 2018) and Gronum et al.
(2012) combine ﬁnancial, productivity, and growth-related measures to construct broader
performance indices, while innovation-oriented research incorporates survey-based indica-
tors to capture both ﬁnancial and operational dimensions of ﬁrm outcomes (Exposito &
Sanchis-Llopis, 2018; Hassan et al., 2024). These approaches represent an important method-
ological advance by recognizing that SME performance cannot be fully captured by single
indicators. Despite these methodological advances, existing composite indicators remain
predominantly outcome-oriented, aggregating ﬁnancial and productivity results rather than
underlying managerial capabilities. Consequently, prior studies do not explicitly measure
the structural sophistication of internal decision-making systems, governance arrangements,
ﬁnancial discipline, or adaptive capacity. As a result, heterogeneity in managerial quality is
inferred indirectly through performance outcomes rather than measured directly. This creates
an important gap in the SME literature, particularly in emerging economies where internal
capability differences may precede and shape observable performance results.
Traditional performance measures tend to capture realized results but provide limited
insight into ﬁrms’ internal ability to plan, coordinate, adapt, and execute strategies under con-
ditions of uncertainty. This limitation is particularly salient in emerging economies, where
resource constraints, and heterogeneous managerial practices amplify differences in ﬁrms’
internal capabilities (Barbosa et al., 2024). In such contexts, performance indicators alone
may obscure meaningful variations in managerial quality, organizational maturity, and strate-
gic preparedness. Building on this perspective, the concept of managerial sophistication shifts
the analytical focus from isolated performance outcomes to the internal dimensions that con-
dition how ﬁrms mobilize resources and respond to market dynamics. This approach provides
a more nuanced framework for understanding heterogeneity among SMEs and supports the
use of multiple constructs—such as entrepreneurial orientation, managerial practices, fund-
ing capacity, governance mechanisms, and digitalization—to assess ﬁrms’ relative levels of
sophistication.

From a theoretical perspective, the notion of managerial sophistication is consistent with
established views in the Resource-Based View and Dynamic Capabilities literature, which
emphasize ﬁrm-speciﬁc resources, managerial capabilities, and adaptive capacity as key
drivers of sustained performance (Barney, 1991; Helfat & Martin, 2015; Teece et al., 1997).
In addition, Institutional Theory suggests that ﬁrms operate within regulatory, normative, and
cultural environments that shape strategic behavior and governance structures. In emerging
economies, institutional voids amplify the importance of internally developed managerial
sophistication as a compensatory mechanism (Saikia et al., 2025). Rather than testing these
theories directly, the present study adopts an index-based approach to operationalize these
underlying capabilities in an integrated and data-driven manner.
2.1 Contextualizing managerial sophistication in the Brazilian SME environment
Brazilian SMEs operate in an environment marked by high bureaucratic complexity, tax
system fragmentation, regulatory volatility, credit constraints, and pronounced regional dis-
parities (Cravo et al., 2012; Silva et al., 2025). Institutional inefﬁciencies and administrative
burdens increase transaction costs and create additional managerial demands relative to
ﬁrms operating in more institutionally consolidated economies (Klein & Braido, 2024).
Furthermore, access to long-term ﬁnancing remains limited for many SMEs, reinforcing the
importance of internal ﬁnancial discipline and governance mechanisms (Veiga & McCahery,
2019). Informality and managerial skill heterogeneity are also more pronounced, potentially
amplifying performance differences driven by internal capability gaps (Galetti et al., 2021;
Ulyssea, 2018).
These contextual characteristics suggest that managerial sophistication in Brazil may not
merely enhance performance but may function as a compensatory mechanism in response to
institutional frictions. By operationalizing managerial sophistication through an integrated
index tailored to the Brazilian SME environment, the present study addresses this contextual
gap and strengthens the empirical rationale for examining internal capability structures in
emerging markets. Compared to SMEs operating in more institutionally stable environments
(Livieratos et al., 2022), Brazilian SMEs face a higher reliance on internally developed man-
agerial capabilities to compensate for external constraints, making managerial sophistication
a particularly salient determinant of managerial performance in this context.
While the Resource-Based View conceptualizes competitive advantage as stemming
from valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable resources (Ozdemir et al., 2023), and
Dynamic Capabilities theory emphasizes ﬁrms’ ability to integrate, build, and reconﬁgure
resources (Ostadi et al., 2024), these frameworks remain largely conceptual and are rarely
operationalized through uniﬁed measurement structures in the SME context. The present
study extends these theoretical perspectives by translating heterogeneous managerial and
strategic capabilities into an integrated sophistication index. In doing so, it bridges the gap
between abstract capability-based theory and empirical measurement, offering a structured
and entropy-consistent operationalization of managerial sophistication tailored to SMEs in
emerging economies.
In the following paragraphs, we brieﬂy develop the theoretical foundations of each dimen-
sion, justify their inclusion as core components of managerial sophistication, and establish
the basis for their subsequent operationalization within a composite index.
Entrepreneurial Orientation (EO) refers to a ﬁrm’s strategic posture that reﬂects
entrepreneurial decision-making styles and practices. It is a well-established construct in the

SME literature and is commonly operationalized through ﬁve dimensions: autonomy, inno-
vativeness, risk-taking, proactiveness, and competitive aggressiveness (Lumpkin & Dess,
1996). These dimensions capture how ﬁrms identify and exploit opportunities under condi-
tions of uncertainty and competitive pressure.
A substantial body of empirical research demonstrates a positive association between EO
and ﬁrm performance, particularly in dynamic and competitive environments (Wales, 2016).
Studies focusing on emerging economies further highlight EO as a critical factor explaining
heterogeneity in ﬁrm growth, innovation capacity, and strategic renewal (Hughes & Morgan,
2007; Shirokova et al., 2016). These ﬁndings support the inclusion of EO as a core construct
in models seeking to differentiate SMEs based on strategic orientation and entrepreneurial
behavior.
Managerial practices and capabilities refer to the routines, procedures, and monitoring
mechanisms through which ﬁrms organize operations, allocate resources, and evaluate perfor-
mance. Prior research has shown that the quality of managerial practices is a key determinant
of productivity and ﬁrm-level efﬁciency, particularly among small and medium-sized enter-
prises (Bloom & Van Reenen, 2007; Bloom et al., 2019). Moreover, performance-based
human resource policies and systematic evaluation mechanisms have been associated with
superior organizational outcomes (Bryson & Forth, 2018). Given their role in shaping inter-
nal coordination and execution capacity, managerial practices and capabilities constitute a
fundamental dimension of managerial sophistication and are therefore included as a distinct
construct in the proposed SME Sophistication Index.
Access to ﬁnance and the ability to manage ﬁnancial resources effectively are central
challenges faced by SME. The funding construct encompasses not only the sources of capi-
tal—such as internal funds, bank credit, and alternative ﬁnancing—but also ﬁrms’ ﬁnancial
planning capabilities and record-keeping practices. Empirical studies indicate that SME with
organized ﬁnancial management, separation between personal and business ﬁnances, and
formal accounting records exhibit greater ﬁnancial resilience and higher creditworthiness
(McKenzie & Woodruff, 2017; Robb & Robinson, 2014). In this context, funding is a dimen-
sion of managerial sophistication that reﬂects ﬁnancial discipline, planning capacity, and
strategic investment behaviour. Accordingly, it is treated as a separate construct within the
index.
Corporate governance in SME refers to the mechanisms that support strategic decision-
making, accountability, and control. Although governance structures in SME are often
informal, the adoption of basic governance principles—such as transparency, delegation of
responsibilities, and internal controls—has been shown to positively affect ﬁrm performance.
Empirical evidence suggests that governance quality is associated with higher productivity
and improved strategic alignment, particularly when ﬁrms introduce advisory boards, exter-
nal auditing practices, or formalized decision-making processes (Brunninge et al., 2007; IFC,
2019. Given its inﬂuence on managerial accountability and strategic coherence, corporate
governance is incorporated as an independent construct contributing to overall managerial
sophistication.
Technological dynamism refers to a ﬁrm’s capacity to respond to technological change
and to integrate new technologies into its production, management, and decision-making
processes. In the context of SME this capability has become increasingly intertwined with
business digitalization. The progressive digitalization of the economy has signiﬁcantly
reshaped social behaviours, consumption patterns, and competitive dynamics, making digital
transformation a critical issue across sectors in which SMEs operate (Klein & Todesco, 2021;
Mandviwalla & Flanagan, 2021; Priyono et al., 2020). Pﬁster and Lehmann (2023, 2024)
identify efﬁciency and effectiveness gains, cost reduction, productivity growth, customer

more likely to formalize managerial practices, invest in digitalization, and adopt governance
mechanisms to sustain competitiveness (Bettiol et al., 2023; Davis et al., 2009). Accord-
ingly, market conﬁguration is incorporated as an environmental dimension that conditions
and interacts with internal managerial sophistication.
2.1.1 Integrating strategic and managerial dimensions of SME sophistication
The literature reviewed above highlights entrepreneurial behavior and managerial capabili-
ties jointly shape ﬁrm outcomes. Entrepreneurial Orientation deﬁnes a ﬁrm’s strategic intent
and opportunity-seeking posture, while managerial practices, funding capacity, governance
mechanisms, and digitalization provide the operational foundations necessary for execution.
Firms exhibiting high entrepreneurial orientation, but weak managerial structures may strug-
gle to implement innovations effectively, whereas ﬁrms with strong managerial capabilities
but limited entrepreneurial orientation may underperform in dynamic markets (Moreno et al.,
2008; Casillas et al., 2009; Wales et al., 2011). Empirical evidence suggests that alignment
between these strategic and managerial dimensions is associated with superior performance
across multiple indicators, including proﬁtability, growth, and market share (Hughes et al.,
2018). This integrated perspective is particularly relevant in emerging economies, where insti-
tutional instability and resource constraints amplify the importance of internal managerial
sophistication (Manimala & Wasdani, 2015).
These constructs provide a rationale for the selection of Entrepreneurial Orientation, Man-
agerial Practices, Funding, Governance, Digitalization, and Market Conﬁguration as core
formative dimensions of managerial sophistication, while performance is modeled as an out-
come of this integrated capability structure. These constructs guide the design of the survey
instrument and inform the operationalization of perceptual indicators measured through Lik-
ert scales. In the next section, these dimensions are translated into composite indices using
an entropy-based framework combined with multi-criteria decision-making techniques.

3 Methodology
The methodological framework is structured in two sequential stages. In the ﬁrst stage, the
study develops a measurement model by transforming ordinal survey responses into con-
tinuous metric scales and aggregating them into construct-level sophistication indices. In
the second stage, these indices are used as inputs in econometric and endogeneity analy-
ses to investigate their relationships with SME performance. This separation ensures that
measurement and inference are conceptually and methodologically distinct.
Transforming ordinal data into continuous, metric scales is a common requirement in
statistical analysis, with several methods available (Liddell & Kruschke, 2018). A simple
approach assigns numerical values (e.g., 1 to k for a k-point Likert scale) and treats them
as continuous data (Li, 2013). More sophisticated alternatives include the Normal score
transformation (Casacci & Pareto, 2015), neutrosophic set theory (Das et al., 2020), fuzzy
set theory (Dubois et al., 2005; Ejegwa, 2020), and grey relational analysis (Lee & Lin,
2011). Each of these methods addresses a speciﬁc aspect of the uncertainty embedded in
perceptual scales: neutrosophic sets capture indeterminacy and inconsistency in category
assignment (Kandasamy et al., 2020; Unver et al., 2022), fuzzy sets model the degree of
membership when category boundaries are imprecise (Ocampo-Duque et al., 2013), and
grey relational analysis identiﬁes the relative contribution of each category to data variation
(Wang et al., 2013). However, applying any single transformation in isolation risks discarding
complementary information. The present study addresses this limitation by developing an
integrated pipeline in which all three transformations are combined through entropy-based
optimization, and the resulting metric scales are subsequently used to construct composite
sophistication indices and to explore causal relationships among managerial constructs.

The methodological pipeline comprises six sequential stages, each designed to address a
speciﬁc analytical requirement while using the output of the preceding stage as its input. The
pipeline is structured as follows:
First, three independent transformations, Neutrosophic (N), Fuzzy (F), and Grey (G), are
applied to the original Likert-scale survey data to convert ordinal responses into contin-
uous metric scores (Sects. 3.1–3.3). Each transformation captures a distinct dimension of
uncertainty: N models indeterminacy and inconsistency in category boundaries; F represents
graded membership when boundaries are imprecise; and G quantiﬁes the relational contribu-
tion of each category to overall data variation. Because each transformation encodes different
information about the same underlying responses, their outputs are complementary rather
than redundant.
Second, the three resulting continuous matrices are combined into a single optimal NFG
matrix through a mutual information (MI) maximization procedure (Sect. 3.4). This step
determines the weights w1, w2, and w3 that maximize the shared information between the
original ordinal data and the weighted composite transformation, subject to the constraint that
information entropy increases — thereby ensuring that the combined matrix preserves the
originaldatastructurewhileintroducingnewinformationcontent.TheMIoptimizationserves
as an information-theoretic ﬁlter that balances the contributions of N, F, and G, preventing
any single transformation from dominating the composite and reducing noise ampliﬁcation
from correlated criteria.
Third, a Robust Compromise (RoCo) multi-criteria decision-making model is applied
to the NFG metric scales to construct a uniﬁed sophistication index for each of the seven
constructs (Sect. 3.5). The RoCo framework aggregates multiple MCDM ranking models
(e.g., TOPSIS, VIKOR, COPRAS) and weighting schemes (e.g., AHP, SWARA) through a
non-linear optimization that minimizes the weighted covariance among model outputs. This
produces scores bounded between 0 (minimal sophistication) and 1 (maximal sophistication)
that are robust to model selection and criteria weighting.
Fourth, Tobit regression models are estimated to assess the relationship between contextual
variables and the RoCo sophistication scores (Sect. 3.6). The Tobit speciﬁcation is adopted
because the dependent variable — the RoCo score — is naturally censored at 0 and 1. A
wild bootstrap procedure with 1,000 replications is used to obtain robust coefﬁcient estimates
under potential heteroskedasticity and non-normality in the residuals.
Fifth, an endogeneity analysis based on the Tobit residuals is conducted to identify sig-
niﬁcant mutual dependencies among the construct-level RoCo scores (Sect. 3.7). Since
SME managers’ responses across constructs are likely interdependent, the residuals from
construct-speciﬁc Tobit regressions may exhibit correlated structures indicative of simultane-
ous causality. A differential evolution algorithm maximizes the weighted mutual information
across all residual pairs, and a sequence of Wilcoxon-based tests determines the existence and
directionality of endogenous relationships. This ﬁnal stage transforms the initial set of inde-
pendent construct indices into a network of directed associations, enabling the identiﬁcation
of causal pathways among sophistication dimensions.

3.1 Positioning relative to hybrid MCDM approaches
Hybrid multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) models combining entropy weighting, fuzzy
logic, and gray relational analysis (GRA) have been widely adopted to address uncertainty
and subjective evaluation in complex decision environments (Mizra et al., 2024; Sun, 2014;
Yang, 2023). Prior entropy–fuzzy–GRA frameworks typically follow a linear structure in
which entropy is used for weight determination, fuzzy sets model linguistic ambiguity, and
GRA provides relational ranking of alternatives.
The present study differs from these approaches in three key respects. First, rather than
treating fuzziness and gray relational structures as separate modeling layers, the proposed
NFG pipeline embeds indeterminacy explicitly at the transformation stage. This allows
survey-based Likert data to be mapped simultaneously into truth-membership, indeterminacy,
and falsity components before relational aggregation. In contrast, conventional fuzzy–GRA
123

Annals of Operations Research
hybrids typically assume ﬁxed membership functions without explicitly modeling indeter-
minate information. Second, while entropy weighting is common in hybrid MCDM models,
it is often used solely for weight assignment. In this study, information-theoretic measures
serve both as discriminatory ﬁlters and robustness-enhancing mechanisms, reducing noise
ampliﬁcation before compromise aggregation. This step improves stability in the presence
of correlated managerial dimensions and mitigates weight distortions common in purely
entropy-based schemes. Third, instead of relying exclusively on classical GRA ranking or
TOPSIS-type distance metrics, the RoCo compromise solution provides a balanced aggrega-
tionmechanismthatpreservesmultidimensionaltrade-offsacrosssophisticationcomponents.
This improves interpretability for decision makers by enabling the identiﬁcation of foun-
dational versus enabling dimensions, rather than producing a purely ordinal ranking. The
approach therefore supports system-level assessment of managerial capability conﬁgurations
rather than single-channel optimization.
From an operations research perspective, the contribution lies not merely in combining
established tools, but in structuring them into a coherent pipeline that enhances (i) robustness
to indeterminacy, (ii) discrimination power across correlated criteria, and (iii) conﬁgurational
interpretability of composite performance indices. This integrated architecture extends prior
entropy–fuzzy–gray hybrids by embedding indeterminacy modeling and compromise opti-
mization within a uniﬁed framework tailored to SME capability assessment.
3.1.1 The proposed neutrosophic approach (N)
Neutrosophic set theory is a framework for handling incomplete, indeterminate, and incon-
sistent information (Abdel-Basset & Mohamed, 2018). When transforming preferences
measured on a k-point Likert scale into continuous values or probability distributions,
neutrosophic theory models the uncertainty involved in assigning numerical values or proba-
bilities to categories (Ansari et al., 2011). Neutrosophic values and neutrosophic probabilities
obtained from the MCDM matrix quantify this uncertainty in decision-making process (Li
et al., 2019).
These neutrosophic values reﬂect how much an alternative is preferred or not, considering
the uncertainty and indeterminacy of preferences (Xu et al., 2018). Neutrosophic probabil-
ities indicate the likelihood that each alternative ﬁts a preference category, accounting for
uncertainty (Khan Sherwani et al., 2021). Together, these values offer a more nuanced under-
standing of the decision-making process by integrating both preferences expressed by the
decision-maker and the uncertainty associated with those preferences (Abdel-Basset et al.,
2019).
These two levels of uncertainty are made operational in the next two subsections:

3.4 Information balance and optimal NFG matrix
Ininformationtheory,entropymeasurestheuncertaintyorrandomnessinadataset(Chapman,
1986) and reﬂects the amount of information it contains in a matrix (Contreras-Reyes, 2021).
Transformations can increase or decrease entropy depending on how they impact randomness
in the data, while increasing entropy means more randomness and decrease entropy means
decreases in randomness (Liu et al., 2016). When transforming metric data to an ordinal scale,
exact values are lost, replaced by rankings (Bhat & Nayar, 1998), and the loss of information
make the backward transformation impossible. However, when applying neutrosophic, fuzzy,
123

Annals of Operations Research
or grey models to convert Likert scale data to continuous data, new information is introduced,
typically in the form of probability distributions or uncertainty measures (Biswas et al., 2014).
To measure information preservation and enhancement, we use mutual information (MI)
between the original ordinal dataset X and the transformed metric dataset Y, which quan-
tiﬁes how much information they share (Zheng, 2019). Higher MI values indicate better
preservation of the original data structure while the transformation adds new information.
Additionally, comparing H(Y) with H(X) reveals whether the transformation increases infor-
mation content: if H(Y) > H(X), the transformed data contains more information than the
original (Hoberman & Ivanova, 2022). The transformation quality can be assessed by exam-
ining whether it maintains high MI(X;Y) while achieving H(Y) > H(X), indicating both good
information preservation and enhancement (Yanto et al., 2021).
To ﬁnd the optimal weights w1, w2, and w3 for the weighted sum of three alternative data
transformations say Y1 (Neutrosophic), Y2 (Fuzzy), and Y3 (Grey), we can use an optimization
approach that maximizes the mutual information (M I) between the original ordinal-scale
dataset X and the transformed metric scale dataset Y, subject to the constraints w1+w2+w3 
1 and H(Y) > H(X, Y) > H(X).

4 Results and analysis
The results should be interpreted in light of the dual role of the SME Sophistication Index.
First, the index provides a measurement-based ranking and distribution of SMEs across

different dimensions of managerial sophistication. Second, it serves as an analytical input to
identify which dimensions are most strongly associated with performance outcomes.
Table 4 reports on the descriptive statistics. In this study on the SME Sophistication Index
for managerial performance in Brazil, the sample data was collected through a survey target-
ing Brazilian SME managers, focusing on seven constructs: Digitalization, Entrepreneurial
Orientation, Funding, Governance, Managerial Capabilities, Market Conﬁguration, and Per-
formance.ThesamplingprocedurereliedonthedatabaseofﬁrmsregisteredwiththeBrazilian
Micro and Small Business Support Service (SEBRAE), a nationwide institution that provides
technical assistance and capacity-building programs to SMEs across all Brazilian states. The
survey instrument was distributed electronically to ﬁrms included in SEBRAE’s registry,
yielding 1,654 valid responses. Because SEBRAE did not disclose the total number of ﬁrms
to which the questionnaire was sent, an exact response rate cannot be reported. Neverthe-
less, the resulting sample size is substantial and exceeds common thresholds for multivariate
analyses involving the number of constructs and indicators considered in this study. The
survey used Likert scales for responses, with a hybrid entropy maximization approach that
incorporated neutrosophy, fuzzy logic, and gray relational analysis to convert these ordinal
data points into metric scales. This methodology allows for capturing perceptual data effec-
tively, accommodating uncertainties and ambiguities in responses. The survey questions were
designed to gauge each of these constructs comprehensively, reﬂecting the diverse dimen-
sions that contribute to SME sophistication. Demographically, the responses primarily came
from white male SME owners or managers, typically over the age of 36 and holding at
least a bachelor’s degree, predominantly based in the Southern regions of Brazil. The sur-
vey predominantly included service and commerce-based microenterprises that have been in
operation for over ten years. While early-stage startups are underrepresented, substantial het-
erogeneity exists within the sample in terms of managerial practices, digitalization intensity,
governance arrangements, funding access, and market exposure. This within-group varia-
tion enables meaningful differentiation of SMEs along multiple dimensions of managerial
sophistication.

5 Discussion and managerial implications
5.1 Validation of the SME sophistication index
The empirical ﬁndings provide strong support for the validity of the proposed SME Sophis-
tication Index. First, the entropy-based NFG transformation ensures that the index preserves
and enhances information embedded in ordinal Likert-scale responses, addressing a key
123

Annals of Operations Research
limitation in prior composite indicators. Second, the RoCo aggregation produces stable and
bounded construct scores that exhibit meaningful variation across ﬁrms, enabling clear differ-
entiation of sophistication levels. Third, the Tobit regression results demonstrate that these
construct scores are signiﬁcantly associated with performance outcomes, conﬁrming the
index’s explanatory relevance. Finally, the endogeneity analysis reveals structured inter-
dependencies among constructs, indicating that the index captures a coherent system of
managerial capabilities rather than isolated dimensions.
5.2 Integration of empirical findings
Taken together, the results from descriptive statistics, index construction, regression analysis,
and endogeneity testing form a coherentnarrative. The descriptive statistics highlightsubstan-
tial heterogeneity among SMEs, justifying the need for a composite sophistication measure.
The entropy-based transformation and RoCo aggregation translate this heterogeneity into
robust construct-level indices. The regression results establish the performance relevance of
these indices, while the endogeneity analysis reveals the underlying structure linking them.
This integrated evidence conﬁrms that SME sophistication is a multidimensional and inter-
connected phenomenon.
5.3 Managerial implications for SMEs
The ﬁndings of this study provide several important implications for SME managers seeking
to enhance ﬁrm performance through increased managerial sophistication. 1) Prioritiza-
tion of key capabilities: the results indicate that funding structures, governance practices,
entrepreneurial orientation, and digitalization are the most inﬂuential drivers of SME sophis-
tication. Managers should therefore prioritize investments in these areas, as improvements in
thesedimensionsaremoststronglyassociatedwithhigherperformanceoutcomes;2)Comple-
mentarity of capabilities: the endogeneity analysis demonstrates that managerial capabilities
operate as an interconnected system rather than as isolated components. Enhancements in
one area, such as digitalization, may reinforce the effectiveness of other dimensions, such as
entrepreneurial orientation or governance. This suggests that managers should adopt a holis-
tic and coordinated approach to capability development; 3) Strategic resource allocation:
the SME Sophistication Index can serve as a diagnostic and benchmarking tool, enabling
ﬁrms to identify relative weaknesses across multiple dimensions. By systematically assess-
ing construct-level scores, managers can allocate resources more efﬁciently toward areas that
constrain overall performance; 4) Adaptation to market conditions: the signiﬁcance of market
conﬁguration highlights the importance of aligning internal capabilities with external envi-
ronmental conditions. SMEs operating in dynamic or uncertain markets should emphasize
adaptability, leveraging digital tools and strategic planning to respond effectively to changing
competitive pressures.
6 Conclusions
This study introduced a new SME Sophistication Index to assess managerial performance
in Brazilian SMEs, using factors like Digitalization, Entrepreneurial Orientation, Fund-
ing, Governance, Managerial Capabilities, Market Conﬁguration, and Performance. From
a theoretical standpoint, these results provide empirical support for the conceptualization of
123

Annals of Operations Research
managerial sophistication as a conﬁguration of ﬁrm-speciﬁc resources and capabilities, con-
sistent with perspectives from the Resource-Based View and Dynamic Capabilities literature.
Funding and governance reﬂect foundational managerial resources, while entrepreneurial
orientation and digitalization capture ﬁrms’ strategic posture and adaptive capacity. The
index-based approach adopted in this study allows these latent constructs to be operational-
ized and jointly assessed within a uniﬁed analytical framework. By combining advanced
methods like neutrosophy, fuzzy logic, and gray relational analysis, we transformed survey
data from a traditional Likert scale into meaningful insights that reveal the core drivers of
SMEsophistication.OurresultsshowthatFunding,Governance,EntrepreneurialOrientation,
and Digitalization are especially important in shaping higher sophistication levels in SMEs.
Access to funding and strong governance structures are particularly impactful, providing the
foundation for SMEs to adopt innovative practices, boost efﬁciency, and remain competitive.
Digitalization also plays a valuable supporting role, helping to amplify the effects of other
areas, such as management capabilities and market responsiveness.
This study contributes by developing a robust measurement framework for managerial
sophistication, which can be applied across different SME contexts. Additionally, by linking
the index to performance outcomes, it demonstrates how such measurement tools can be used
for explanatory and policy-oriented analysis.
The study’s ﬁndings underscore the importance of tailored policy support in enhancing
SME sophistication and performance. These implications follow directly from the empirical
relevance of the theoretical dimensions examined in the study, reinforcing the role of man-
agerial capabilities, strategic orientation, and adaptive resources as key levers for improving
SME performance. Speciﬁcally, policymakers could focus on the following areas: 1) Improv-
ing Access to Funding: Since funding emerged as a signiﬁcant driver, policies that improve
SMEs’ access to affordable and ﬂexible ﬁnancing are essential. Governments and ﬁnancial
institutions could establish SME-speciﬁc loan programs with reduced interest rates, simpli-
ﬁed application processes, and support for non-traditional forms of collateral. Additionally,
offering ﬁnancial literacy programs could help SMEs optimize funding use, enhancing both
immediate access to capital and long-term ﬁnancial health; 2) Strengthening Governance
Practices: Governance was also highlighted as a critical factor for sophistication. Policy-
makers could encourage robust governance by incentivizing the adoption of transparent
management practices and ethical standards. This could involve the creation of governance
guidelines for SMEs or providing subsidies and tax breaks for businesses that adopt veriﬁed
governance frameworks. Establishing national or regional governance certiﬁcations could
also improve market conﬁdence and encourage SMEs to prioritize governance; 3) Fostering
Entrepreneurial Orientation and Innovation: Policies that support entrepreneurial orienta-
tion and foster a culture of innovation could have substantial positive impacts on SMEs.
Government-sponsored incubators, accelerators, and R&D grant can encourage SMEs to
pursue innovative projects and compete effectively in their markets. Offering tax incentives
for R&D investments and establishing public–private partnerships for technology develop-
ment could provide further support; 4) Promoting Digitalization: Although digitalization was
found to be a supporting rather than primary driver, it enables other capabilities to thrive.
Policymakers could focus on digital literacy and technology adoption programs speciﬁcally
designed for SMEs, such as subsidized access to digital tools, cloud services, and e-commerce
platforms. Training programs and mentorship on digital skills could also empower SMEs to
better use digital tools to improve operational efﬁciency and customer engagement; 5) Cre-
ating a Supportive Business Environment: A comprehensive approach to SME development
should also consider the broader ecosystem in which these businesses operate. Streamlining
regulatory processes, simplifying tax requirements, and reducing administrative burdens can
123

Annals of Operations Research
help SMEs focus resources on growth rather than compliance. Policies that enhance market
access, both locally and internationally, could also help SMEs expand their customer base and
increase competitiveness; 6) Building Knowledge Networks and Collaborative Platforms:
Establishing networking platforms that connect SMEs with larger corporations, research
institutions, and government bodies can foster knowledge-sharing and partnership opportu-
nities. Such platforms can promote best practices in areas like governance, digitalization,
and funding utilization, helping SMEs learn from industry leaders and access new growth
opportunities.
However, the study has limitations. Firm age was described and discussed in the Analysis
section as part of the company demographics, where the predominance of long-established
SMEs was acknowledged and interpreted. Nevertheless, ﬁrm age was not explicitly modeled
as a moderating or dynamic factor, which is recognized as a limitation and a relevant direction
for future research. While the proposed index captures theoretically grounded dimensions of
managerial sophistication, its empirical application remains context-dependent and reﬂects
the institutional characteristics of Brazilian SMEs. In addition, the analysis relies on self-
reported survey data, the results may be subject to response bias. Given that the sample
predominantly consists of established SMEs, future research could extend this framework
to SMEs in different countries and adopt longitudinal designs to examine how managerial
sophistication evolves over time and across ﬁrm life-cycle stages.

Appendix D
This appendix presents a comprehensive sensitivity analysis assessing the stability of the
RoCo rankings under perturbations in the NFG weighting scheme. The analysis addresses two
complementary objectives: (i) evaluating the variability of optimal weights through bootstrap
resampling, and (ii) examining the robustness of construct rankings across alternative weight
conﬁgurations. A total of 36 weight combinations were evaluated, with neutrosophic (N),
fuzzy (F), and grey (G) weights varying from 0.1 to 0.9 in increments of 0.1, subject to the
constraint that weights sum to unity. For each combination, the RoCo MCDM method was
executed to generate alternative scores. Ranking stability was assessed through Spearman
correlation between the scores obtained under each weight conﬁguration and those derived
from the optimal weights identiﬁed by the methodology. Results are consolidated in scatter
plots displaying cosine similarity between perturbed and optimal weight vectors against
Spearman correlation coefﬁcients.
To assess the stability of the optimal NFG weights, a bootstrap procedure with 100 repli-
cations was conducted. Figure 13 presents boxplots summarizing the distribution of optimal
weights across constructs. The results reveal heterogeneous patterns of variability. Funding
