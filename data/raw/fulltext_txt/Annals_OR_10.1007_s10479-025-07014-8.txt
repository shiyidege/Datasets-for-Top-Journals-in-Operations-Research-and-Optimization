Abstract
The rise of low-altitude economies and the rapid development of unmanned technolo­
gies underscore the strategic role of Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV)-based delivery. 
This study examines a two-tier food delivery supply chain with a dominant platform 
(e.g., Meituan) and two types of providers: riders and UAV service suppliers. We analyze 
stakeholder strategies under varying UAV technological value, government subsidies, and 
platform cooperation models. Findings show that UAV technological value strongly in­
fluences decisions, enhancing rider orders and delivery fees while temporarily suppress­
ing UAV orders until clear time advantages emerge. Government subsidies enhance UAV 
competitiveness, adjust fees, and increase profits for both platforms and UAV suppliers. 
Platform cooperation models and subsidies interact synergistically: revenue-sharing with 
subsidies is optimal under high technological value, while per-order payments with sub­
sidies are preferred under medium value or extreme time gaps. Furthermore, riders act 
as flexible incumbents, differentiating when UAV technological value is high, but time 
advantages are limited, collaborating through revenue-sharing when UAV value is moder­
ate, and adjusting services (e.g., night or niche delivery) when UAV subsidies are high 
but technological value is low, with some conditions creating a “strategy vacuum.” UAV 
service suppliers behave as agile entrants, favoring per-order payment with subsidy under 
moderate technological value and extreme time gaps, and revenue-sharing with subsidy 
when technological value or combined subsidy conditions support shared-risk growth. 
Platforms act as strategic coordinators, dynamically selecting between revenue-sharing 
and per-order payment with subsidy based on UAV value, time gaps, and subsidies to 
balance risk, secure profits, and align incentives.

1  Introduction
The rapid advancement of low-altitude airspace technology and unmanned aerial vehicles 
(UAVs) has transformed the landscape of last-mile logistics, particularly within platform-
based food delivery systems (Jahani et al., 2025; Zhen et al., 2025). Major platforms such 
as Meituan, JD Logistics, and Wing have launched UAV delivery pilots to improve deliv­
ery efficiency and alleviate labor dependence, exemplifying the global momentum toward 
automation in urban logistics (Hossain et al., 2022). For example, Meituan’s Keeta/Meituan 
Drone has piloted operations across China since 2021 and, by 2025, launched regular and 
cross-sea BVLOS routes in Hong Kong and Dubai, illustrating a viable policy–market path­
way for platform-driven UAV commercialization.1 Internationally, Amazon’s Wing opera­
tions in the U.S. and Australia—with supporting user surveys such as Virginia Tech’s study 
of Wing service areas—show rising public acceptance and reuse intention, providing behav­
ioral evidence for UAV delivery viability in urban and suburban settings.2 These techno­
logical trends are fostering the emergence of hybrid delivery ecosystems, where platforms 
must simultaneously coordinate human riders and UAV operators within a unified service 
architecture (Chen et al., 2022a, 2022b).
Despite the operational promise, integrating UAV delivery into existing platform eco­
systems introduces significant economic, strategic, and regulatory challenges (Hu & Chen, 
2025; Murray & Karwan, 2010). Human riders provide flexible and adaptive capacity 
with low fixed costs, whereas UAV delivery requires high upfront investment, regulatory 
approval, and route restrictions, limiting its scalability in dense urban environments (Hos­
sain et al., 2022; Jahani et al., 2025). Consequently, platforms face a fundamental coordina­
tion problem: how to efficiently integrate two heterogeneous delivery modes with distinct 
cost structures, service attributes, and capacity constraints while maintaining profitability 
and service quality.
A key managerial challenge lies in the design of cooperation mechanisms between the 
platform and UAV service providers. Empirical evidence suggests that platform logistics 
contracts predominantly take two forms—per-order payment and revenue-sharing agree­
ments (Chang et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2024). The former offers cost predictability but weak 
incentive alignment, while the latter fosters coordination but compromises pricing auton­
omy. Prior studies in supply chain contracting and platform pricing (Besbes et al., 2021; Ha 
et al., 2003; Hu et al., 2022) show that incentive misalignment can lead to inefficiencies in 
price-setting and capacity allocation. However, how these traditional mechanisms perform 
in multi-modal, platform-led UAV delivery systems remains underexplored.
The coordination problem is further complicated by government subsidy policies 
intended to promote UAV adoption. In cities such as Shenzhen and Hangzhou, local gov­
ernments provide direct subsidies or tax incentives to certified UAV operators, aiming to 
accelerate technological diffusion. Prior research on government intervention (Bai et al., 
2021; Tang et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2023) demonstrates that while subsidies can enhance 
adoption and welfare, they also risk distorting competition, creating dependency, and shift­
ing bargaining power within the supply chain. Yet, limited attention has been paid to how

Moreover, the coexistence of UAV and rider delivery modes gives rise to intra-platform 
order competition. Consumers may value UAV delivery for its speed and novelty, but their 
willingness to pay a premium is often limited (Ha et al., 2003; Hu et al., 2022). Platforms 
must thus determine optimal order allocation and delivery pricing strategies that balance 
total cost efficiency, consumer surplus, and provider incentives. For instance, Meituan’s 
UAV service can complete a 3-km delivery in under 10 min—faster than riders—but at 
significantly higher operational costs, posing a question of whether consumer preference for 
speed justifies price differentials and how this trade-off affects equilibrium outcomes. These 
practical and theoretical gaps motivate three core research questions:
(i) Cooperation Strategy Design: What is the platform’s optimal cooperation strategy with 
UAV providers under different contractual mechanisms, and how do these mechanisms 
affect the strategic choices of UAV and rider providers?
(ii) Policy Interface: How do government subsidies targeted at UAV providers influence 
their market participation incentives, and how should platforms adjust their coordina­
tion strategy accordingly?
(iii) Order Competition and Pricing: When UAVs and riders coexist, how should the plat­
form allocate orders, set delivery prices for consumers, and negotiate compensation 
terms to balance profitability?
To address these questions, we develop a Stackelberg game model for a two-tier delivery 
supply chain integrating contract design, pricing decisions, and government policy. This 
analytical framework extends the classic literature on platform pricing and service opera­
tions (Besbes et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2022; Lai et al., 2022), complements recent studies on 
cooperation and contract coordination under digital platforms (Chang et al., 2022; Du et al., 
2025; Ji et al., 2024), and enriches the policy-subsidy stream in supply chain management 
(Bai et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2023). Importantly, this study systemati­
cally examines the UAV-based food delivery ecosystem by modeling the strategic interac­
tions among rider service suppliers, UAV service suppliers, and the platform under varying 
levels of perceived technological value, government subsidies, and cooperation models.
The results reveal several key insights. First, the perceived technological value of UAV 
delivery has a dual effect: it enhances service pricing flexibility and consumer payments 
but reduces UAV order volumes unless delivery time advantages are substantial. Sec­
ond, government subsidies effectively promote UAV adoption, reduce delivery costs, and 
improve both supplier and platform profitability, demonstrating their regulatory and effi­
ciency-enhancing roles. Third, platform cooperation models and subsidies exhibit strong 
synergy—high perceived technological value and generous subsidies jointly amplify UAV 
competitiveness and market demand. Fourth, riders act as flexible incumbents: they engage 
in differentiated competition when UAV value is high, but time advantages are limited, col­
laborate through revenue-sharing when UAV value is moderate, and proactively adjust ser­
vices when subsidies are high, but UAV value is low. Finally, UAV suppliers and platforms 
should dynamically align their cooperation strategies with technological progress and sub­
sidy intensity to optimize profitability and market penetration. Collectively, these findings 
offer actionable insights into how coordinated technological investment, policy incentives,

sustainability. The findings provide theoretical insights and managerial implications for the

2  Literature review
2.1  Delivery service and platform pricing
The integration of delivery service design and platform pricing has long been a central topic 
in operations and supply chain management. Early studies established foundational models 
of price–delivery competition. For instance, Ha et al. (2003) developed a noncooperative 
game model involving two suppliers and one customer, showing that customer-controlled 
delivery frequency can intensify price competition and deteriorate overall supply chain effi­
ciency despite benefiting consumers. Extending such classical insights to multi-sided mar­
kets, platform pricing theory has evolved to account for cross-network effects, information 
asymmetry, and behavioral factors.
Recent research has increasingly focused on digital and platform-mediated delivery eco­
systems, particularly those involving emerging technologies such as UAVs and blockchain. 
Jahani et al. (2025) conducted a large-scale text-based literature review of over 5,000 pub­
lications, revealing that UAV research in logistics remains fragmented, with limited work 
integrating pricing, subsidy, and coordination issues. Similarly, Hossainet et al. (2022) 
adopted a Bayesian network to assess UAV performance under uncertainty, highlighting the 
complex interplay between technical efficiency and economic feasibility in UAV-enabled 
logistics.
From a platform design perspective, multi-sided pricing and capacity coordination remain 
critical. Li and Lev (2025) develop a dynamic pricing model for experiential products that 
integrates perceived quality and AI-driven consumer learning, showing that firms adjust 
prices strategically as AI-shaped information cascades and demand signals shift. Mardan 
and Tremblay (2025) showed that users’ multi-activity participation can endogenously alter 
the structure of network externalities, reshaping optimal platform pricing. He et al. (2025) 
incorporated blockchain-based capacity sharing into pricing models, demonstrating how 
cross-network effects and risk-sensitive behaviors affect platform profitability and technol­
ogy adoption. Similarly, Tao et al. (2023) examined blockchain’s dual effect on pricing 
and quality, showing that while it can lower prices and enhance quality, it may also create 
adverse incentives leading to quality degradation.
The role of information and behavioral responses in pricing has also been widely 
explored. Liu et al., (2021a, 2021b) analyzed information-sharing strategies on retail plat­
forms, finding that selective or symmetric sharing influences competitive pricing precision. 
Tsunoda and Zennyo (2021) further demonstrated that transparency and commission design 
affect suppliers’ channel choices, often pushing them toward agency models that can benefit

both suppliers and platforms. In the context of consumer behavior, Liu et al. (2022) mod­
eled anticipated regret in online celebrity retailer settings, revealing that such psychological 
effects can enhance mutual welfare when search and return costs remain low.
Within dynamic and spatially distributed delivery markets, surge and congestion pricing 
mechanisms have received extensive attention. Hu et al. (2022) modeled two-sided temporal 
responses in ride-hailing, showing that penetration surge pricing—supported by informa­
tion sharing—can outperform short-lived sharp surges in welfare and efficiency. Likewise, 
Besbes et al. (2021) analyzed spatial surge pricing and showed that platforms optimally 
balance local over congestion and supply reallocation to maximize revenue. Bernstein et al. 
(2021) extended this logic to competition between two-sided platforms, highlighting that 
multihoming, while appealing to drivers, may harm all participants under congestion unless 
incentive alignment mechanisms are introduced.
In e-commerce and hybrid service platforms, price–service competition and coopera­
tion have also been key themes. Lai et al. (2022) analyzed Amazon’s “Fulfilled by Amazon 
(FBA)” system, revealing that while FBA intensifies service competition, it can soften price 
competition, ultimately benefiting both Amazon and sellers under appropriate conditions. 
Guo et al. (2021) explored optimal bundling under agency selling, finding that bundling is 
more likely under high commission rates but may paradoxically reduce platform profits. 
Cohen and Zhang (2022) examined competition between two-sided platforms and showed 
that profit-sharing agreements can enhance market efficiency under strong demand competi­
tion but may harm at least one platform when supply-side rivalry dominates.
Finally, regulatory, and ethical concerns in platform pricing governance have gained 
growing attention. Liu et al., (2021a, 2021b) developed an evolutionary game among the 
government, platforms, and consumers to address big data discriminatory pricing, revealing 
that optimal penalties depend on platform risk preferences and enforcement costs. Zhang 
and Nie (2021) further examined inter-platform competition in regulated ride-hailing mar­
kets, showing that policy interventions and specialization can mitigate destructive price 
wars triggered by multi-homing.
Collectively, these studies demonstrate that platform pricing mechanisms are increas­
ingly shaped by digital technology, regulatory design, and behavioral dynamics. However, 
while prior research provides valuable insights into ride-hailing, e-commerce, and block­
chain-based platforms, relatively limited work explores multi-agent interactions among 
UAV service suppliers, human riders, and platforms under factors such as government sub­
sidies, perceived technological value, and cooperation modes. Addressing these gaps, the 
present study develops a unified analytical framework to examine how these factors jointly 
influence pricing, service adoption, and stakeholder profitability in UAV-based food deliv­
ery ecosystems.
2.2  Cooperative mode
Cooperation mode selection has emerged as a critical determinant of operational efficiency 
and innovation outcomes in digitally enabled supply chains. Recent studies have examined 
diverse cooperation structures—including joint ventures, authorization, and platform-based 
contracts—highlighting how information sharing, technological capabilities, and regulatory 
contexts shape firms’ optimal cooperation choices.
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
Li et al., (2025a, 2025b) use a duopoly model to show how non-brand product reliability 
and compensation mechanisms reshape competition by boosting consumer trust, narrowing 
brand premiums, and in some cases creating win–win outcomes that enhance profitability 
for both branded and non-branded suppliers. Ji et al. (2024) investigated cooperation inno­
vation under varying levels of big data analytics capability and showed that stronger analyt­
ics capabilities incentivize firms to adopt tight (joint) cooperation over loose collaboration. 
Their game-theoretical analysis further revealed that the performance gap between tight 
and loose cooperation widens as analytic capability increases, while cost subsidy contracts 
can serve as an effective mechanism to bridge these gaps and sustain cooperative innova­
tion. Similarly, Xu et al. (2023) examined a manufacturer’s choice between marketplace 
and reselling modes under demand disruptions and cap-and-trade regulation. They found 
that demand shocks can paradoxically improve profits and complicate platform coopera­
tion choices, with coordination intensity differing by disruption severity and regulatory cost 
exposure. Li et al., (2025a, 2025b) show how influencers’ AR services, customer satisfac­
tion, and return costs jointly shape pricing, return rates, and cooperation choices, generally 
making the agency mechanism more advantageous.
In the context of transportation and mobility platforms, Zhou et al. (2025) explored 
whether metro operators should build or cooperate with bike-sharing systems under infor­
mation-sharing strategies and demand uncertainty. Their results indicated that information 
sharing generally enhances the value of metro–bike integration, though investment effi­
ciency and risk transfer determine the relative advantages of self-ownership versus partner­
ship. These findings underscore that information-sharing efficiency and cost asymmetry are 
pivotal in determining cooperative feasibility.
Other scholars have focused on authorization and alliance-based cooperation. Jin et al. 
(2022) studied cooperation between original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) and inde­
pendent remanufacturers through authorization mechanisms. They showed that remanufac­
turing authorization generally enhances consumer willingness-to-pay and yields higher joint 
profits, although in some cases dealer authorization becomes the only feasible option due to 
capability or cost constraints. The study also highlights the trade-off between environmental 
benefits and consumer welfare, emphasizing the strategic importance of authorization-based 
cooperation in sustainable operations.
In the e-commerce context, cooperation modes are also deeply intertwined with financ­
ing and contractual design. Chang et al. (2022) developed a joint optimization framework 
linking retailers’ financing decisions with e-commerce platforms’ channel contracts. Their 
findings suggest that retailers’ working capital conditions critically shape cooperation pref­
erences, and that platforms can mitigate financing risks through direct financing mechanisms 
or risk control measures. Moreover, moderate commission and transfer fees are essential to 
balance profit distribution and ensure long-term cooperation stability.
Beyond traditional supply chains, new forms of digital cooperation have emerged in 
the platform economy. Du et al. (2025) analyzed bundling cooperation strategies between 
online membership programs, revealing that the optimal cooperation structure depends on 
firms’ member base sizes and value extraction abilities. Interestingly, startups without mem­
bers may become preferred partners due to their growth potential, while established firms 
may need to provide compensation to sustain cooperation. This study highlights that cross-
platform bundling and profit allocation mechanisms are key to fostering mutually beneficial 
partnerships in digital ecosystems.

Taken together, these studies illustrate that cooperation mode selection is contingent 
upon multiple interrelated factors, including technological capability, demand uncer­
tainty, financing structure, and regulatory design. Literature collectively underscores that 
tight cooperation modes enhance joint value creation when technological or informational 
complementarities are high, whereas loose or contract-based modes may be optimal under 
uncertainty or cost asymmetry. However, despite extensive exploration across manufac­
turing, e-commerce, and transport sectors, little attention has been given to cooperative 
mechanisms in UAV-based delivery ecosystems, where human riders, UAV suppliers, and 
platforms must jointly determine revenue-sharing schemes and contract modes under tech­
nological uncertainty and government subsidies. Addressing this gap, the present study 
extends cooperative mode analysis to the UAV delivery context, providing a multi-agent 
perspective on how cooperation, technology value, and policy interact to shape strategic 
equilibrium.
2.3  Government subsidies
Government subsidies play a pivotal role in shaping firms’ strategic behaviors, technologi­
cal adoption, and sustainability outcomes within supply chains. Existing studies explore 
subsidy mechanisms across various contexts—including blockchain implementation, cir­
cular economy transitions, green production, and electric vehicle operations—highlighting 
their diverse impacts on efficiency, innovation, and welfare distribution.
Zhong et al. (2023) investigate blockchain adoption in a dual-channel supply chain 
and reveal that innovation-oriented subsidies yield a “win–win–win” outcome for manu­
facturers, retailers, and consumers by enhancing transparency and social welfare, outper­
forming traditional quantity-based subsidies. Similarly, Li et al. (2023) provide empirical 
evidence that government subsidies stimulate circular supply chain management (CSCM) 
through eco-innovation, with their effectiveness contingent upon top management’s tech­
nological and environmental experience, underscoring the mediating role of organizational 
capabilities.
From a dynamic consumer perspective, Chen et al., (2022a, 2022b) demonstrate that 
optimal production and subsidy rates depend on evolving green perceptions and that gov­
ernments prioritizing their own utility rather than pure welfare can still achieve balanced 
outcomes between profitability and environmental quality. Extending to the platform econ­
omy, Zhao et al. (2023) model ride-sourcing competition under carbon-peak objectives, 
showing that subsidies aligned with consumers’ green preferences not only promote NEV 
adoption but also reshape fleet size and pricing strategies, leading to improved sustainability 
performance. Lyu et al. (2023) examine how different regulatory tools shape NEV automak­
ers’ green manufacturing and innovation investments, showing that subsidy structures and 
cap-and-trade policies have distinct advantages for promoting innovation, emissions reduc­
tion, and overall welfare.
At the product policy level, Bai et al. (2021) develop a three-stage Stackelberg game to 
optimize trade-in subsidy programs, revealing that proportional subsidies linked to manu­
facturer rebates outperform fixed subsidies, and that targeted allocation toward high-impact 
products maximizes welfare gains. Tang et al. (2024) further compare trade-in, consump­
tion, and mixed subsidy schemes, suggesting that flexible mixed designs can accommodate

multiple stakeholder interests and yield superior outcomes across varying product durability

In the electric vehicle sector, Gu et al. (2021) examine battery secondary use under sub­
sidy incentives, highlighting that subsidy effectiveness hinges on the remaining capacity 
of recycled batteries and remanufacturing rates, thereby offering actionable guidance for 
closed-loop supply chain design. Zhang and Huang (2021) extend this analysis to EV prod­

costs, while R&D subsidies are preferable under higher innovation costs and buyer risk 
aversion—although evolutionary instability may limit their practical sustainability.

well-designed, performance-based, or mixed subsidy schemes can better align environmen­

3  Model description
We consider a two-tier takeaway supply chain, which includes a dominant platform (e.g., 
Meituan) and two types of delivery service providers (riders and UAV service suppliers). In 
the context of delivering takeaway products, this study explores how the dominant platform 
(Meituan) can formulate reasonable delivery service pricing strategies to guide demand and 
maximize profits. The study mainly focuses on two delivery methods existing in the take­
away product delivery market: rider delivery and UAV delivery of takeaway products. The 
supply chain structure is shown in Fig. 1.
Consumer Utility: The price consumers need to pay to obtain products through either 
of the two methods is p (Ha et al., 2003; Mardan & Tremblay, 2025). Delivery times dif­
fer by mode: rider delivery requires L1, whereas UAV delivery requires L2. The advan­
tage of UAV delivery lies in reducing consumers’ waiting time compared to rider delivery, 
so L2 < L1. To further reflect the difference in consumers’ waiting time between UAV 
delivery and rider delivery of takeaway products, this study defines ∆L = L1 −L2, where 
∆L > 0 represents the difference in consumers’ waiting time, which directly contributes to 
higher consumer utility from UAV delivery.
Additionally, the perceived value of the product also varies by delivery mode. When 
riders deliver takeaway products, the basic perceived value of the product for consumers 
is v. When UAVs deliver takeaway products, consumers will have an increased sense of 
technological experience, so the basic perceived value for consumers is (1 + x) v, where x 
represents the degree of perceived value increment brought by the technological experience 
of UAV takeaway delivery. Importantly, when consumers receive takeaway products, they 
need to pay a delivery fee: for rider delivery, the delivery fee is f; for UAV delivery, the 
delivery fee is ξ. Therefore, the utility of consumers under the two methods is as follows.

Cooperation Modes between the Platform and Delivery Service Providers: First, the 
cooperation modes between the platform and UAV service suppliers include two types: 
per-order payment and revenue-sharing cooperation. In the per-order payment mode, the 
platform pays the UAV service supplier a fee of ∆1 per order. In the revenue-sharing coop­
eration mode, the platform allocates a proportion of 1 −θ of the revenue to the UAV service 
supplier. Second, the main cooperation mode between the platform and rider service sup­
pliers is per-order payment, where the platform pays the rider service supplier a fee of ∆2 
per order. Moreover, the platform needs to consider providing riders with a basic salary K.
Government Subsidies: The UAV service supplier receives a government subsidy of Sg 
per order (Tang et al., 2024), generating additional revenue and thereby incentivizing UAV 
adoption. The total subsidy-based revenue is SgQ2 where Q2 denotes the number of UAV 
deliveries. This additional revenue enhances the UAV supplier’s profitability and can sig­
nificantly affect platform-level pricing, contracting, and cooperation decisions.
Game Sequence: The game sequence is as follows.
(i) The platform decides which cooperation mode to adopt with the UAV service supplier: 
per-order payment or revenue-sharing cooperation.
(ii) After the platform determines the cooperation mode with the UAV service supplier, the 
government decides whether to provide subsidies to the UAV service supplier based on 
the cooperation mode.
(iii) After the government decides whether to provide subsidies: Under the per-order pay­
ment mode, delivery service suppliers determine the unit delivery fees ∆1 and ∆2 
charged to the platform, and the dominant platform determines the delivery fees f and 
ξ that consumers need to pay. Under the revenue-sharing cooperation mode, delivery 
service suppliers determine the unit delivery fee ∆1 charged to the platform, and the 
dominant platform determines the delivery fees f and ξ that consumers need to pay.

4  Per-order payment cooperation mode
In the Per-Order Payment Cooperation Mode, we focus on two scenarios: the Government 
Non-Subsidy Policy (UN) and the Government Subsidy Policy (UY). Detailed model speci­
fications are described as follows.
4.1  Government non-subsidy policy (UN)
Under the Government Non-Subsidy Policy (UN), the profit functions of the dominant 
platform, rider service suppliers, and UAV service suppliers each capture the relationship

should align with the UAV’s perceived technological value and delivery time gap, consider­

7  Conclusion
This study develops a triadic analytical framework to examine the strategic interactions 
among rider service suppliers, UAV service suppliers, and platforms in the emerging UAV-
based food delivery ecosystem. By jointly considering perceived technological value, 
government subsidies, and platform cooperation models, we identify how technology per­
ception and policy incentives jointly shape equilibrium outcomes and stakeholder behavior. 
Some interesting conclusions are as follows.
Perceived UAV technological value, Government subsidies, and cooperation models: 
While it increases riders’ and suppliers’ delivery fees and enhances overall pricing flexibil­
ity, it initially suppresses UAV order volume as consumers prioritize delivery speed. Per-
order UAV subsidies increase UAV order share and reduce delivery fees for consumers by 
partially passing cost savings through platforms. Under the per-order with subsidy mecha­
nism, both UAV suppliers and platforms achieve higher profits relative to the no-subsidy 
benchmark, as subsidies offset suppliers’ costs and improve consumer acceptance. Under 
the revenue-sharing with subsidy regime, profitability depends on technological percep­
tion: at low perceived values, implicit costs dominate; at high perceived values, joint gains 
emerge.
Stakeholder Strategy Implications: Riders act as flexible incumbents whose strategies 
must dynamically adjust to UAV technological progress, delivery time differentials, and 
subsidy intensity. When UAVs exhibit high technological value but limited time advantage, 
riders should pursue differentiated competition, emphasizing reliability, personalized deliv­
ery, and urban adaptability (e.g., high-rise or traffic-congested areas) to retain consumers 
less sensitive to novelty. Under moderate UAV value and medium subsidies, riders ben­
efit from revenue-sharing collaboration with platforms and UAV suppliers, forming hybrid 
delivery alliances to reduce idle time and share incremental revenue from UAV visibility. 
Conversely, when technological value is low, but subsidies are high, riders should strategi­
cally adjust by expanding off-peak or niche services (e.g., night delivery, localized logistics) 
to sustain demand. In regions where both policy and technological advantages favor UAVs, 
riders may encounter a temporary strategy vacuum, requiring innovation in service modes 
or waiting for market or policy shifts before reinvestment.
UAV suppliers operate as agile entrants whose cooperation mode and investment inten­
sity hinge on technological maturity and policy incentives. When UAV technological value 
is medium and delivery time gaps are either narrow or large, suppliers should adopt the 
per-order payment model with subsidy (UY) to stabilize revenues and offset operational 
costs. When technological value is high or subsidies are substantial, the revenue-sharing 
with subsidy (CY) model becomes superior, as it allows shared investment with platforms, 
improved demand responsiveness, and enhanced bargaining power through joint scaling. 
Under low technological value and weak subsidy support, UAV suppliers should prioritize 
technological refinement, cost efficiency, and pilot cooperation to accumulate operational 
experience before large-scale deployment. Overall, UAV suppliers must evaluate the joint 
effects of technological experience, delivery efficiency, and policy support to determine the 
optimal balance between cost recovery, market expansion, and long-term competitiveness.
Platforms, as ecosystem orchestrators, should design adaptive cooperation mecha­
nisms that balance risk, efficiency, and policy responsiveness. When UAVs possess high 
perceived value and subsidies remain modest, revenue-sharing models (CY/CN) enable

platforms to sustain mutual growth with suppliers through joint risk-sharing and ser­
vice innovation. Under medium technological value and high subsidies, the per-order 
payment model with subsidy (UY) offers cost transparency and simplified coordina­
tion, enhancing profit stability while promoting UAV adoption. Moreover, platforms 
should implement dynamic matching mechanisms—such as performance-based sub­
sidies, adjustable sharing coefficients, or dual-channel pricing—to continuously align 
the incentives of riders and UAV suppliers with evolving market conditions. By coor­
dinating subsidy intensity, technology adoption, and service diversity, platforms can 
transition UAV food delivery from a technically feasible to a commercially sustainable 
system, achieving long-term equilibrium across all stakeholders.
Government subsidies play a vital role in accelerating UAV adoption and improving 
system efficiency. Per-order subsidies directly stimulate UAV order shares by lower­
ing consumer-facing delivery fees and partially offsetting suppliers’ operational costs. 
Policymakers aiming to promote UAV diffusion should therefore prioritize targeted, 
order-linked incentives rather than broad revenue-sharing schemes, unless technologi­
cal perception is already sufficiently high. When technological value remains low or 
uncertain, subsidies should be complemented by pilot programs and regulatory support 
to ease operational constraints and build consumer trust.
Future research could extend this study by incorporating dynamic and stochastic 
factors such as traffic, weather, and UAV reliability, as well as consumer and rider 
heterogeneity in technology adoption and trust. Examining multi-platform competition 
and regional subsidy differences could reveal broader market impacts. Finally, empiri­
cal validation or simulation studies using real UAV delivery data would strengthen the 
practical relevance of theoretical findings.
