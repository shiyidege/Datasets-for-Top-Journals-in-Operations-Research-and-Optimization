Abstract
In this paper we consider decision functions in which voters have three options and one of
them is abstention or indifference and the output is a binary decision between two alternatives
so that a tie is not possible. These resolute decision functions appear frequently in grading,
in some sport competitions, in voting situations in which the status quo is put to the vote,
etc. It is a more restricted case of the voting context considered in the seminal article by
Kenneth May on decision functions, published in Econometrica in 1952, because the output
set does not admit a tie. Among these resolute decision functions we focus on the study of
majority functions, in which the number of favorable votes to an alternative must be strictly
greater than the number of votes against it. This work provides an axiomatic characterization
for the set of majority functions and for the relative majority function with status quo bias.
Both characterizations are based on weaker versions of neutrality. Other complementary
characterizations are also provided.
Keywords May’s theorem · Majority rules with abstention · Resolute majority rules ·
Relative majority rule with status quo bias · Weaker forms of neutrality · Axiomatic
characterizations
1 Introduction
In 1952, May (1952) characterized simple majority by a set of four independent axioms:
universal deﬁnition, anonymity, neutrality and positive responsiveness. In this paper we
specialize, as done in Jeong and Ju (2017), by restricting the output set to the two effective
options without allowing a ﬁnal tie. May’s result considers only (anonymous) voting systems
for two alternatives that are neutral: there is no built-in bias towards ‘yes’ or ‘no’ as outcomes,
so that they are treated symmetrically. Neutrality is a natural assumption when the alternatives
are two candidates for an elected position. However, when the alternatives are yes (yes, change
the status quo by passing a certain new law or proposal) and no (no, reject the proposal and

In the following we describe some examples of situations of different nature that corre-
spond to the resolute voting rules we deal with in this paper.
Example 1.1 Assume that a single alternative, such as a bill or an amendment, is pitted against
the status quo and only the votes of ‘active’ voters (those who participate in the vote) are
taken into account. In these systems each voter responds with a vote of ‘yes’, a ‘no’ or an
‘abstention’. A set of rules specify which collections of votes yield passage of the issue at
hand.
The simplest rule to yield passage consists in obtaining a greater number of favorable than
unfavorable votes. This rule makes possible to break the status quo with a small number of
‘yes’, a way to avoid this situation is to require a minimum threshold for the total number
of afﬁrmative votes with respect to the total number of votes cast. Let’s say for example that
two thirds of the votes cast must be afﬁrmative to pass the issue at hand. This rule strongly
legitimizes the option of breaking the status quo in front of the option of maintaining it,
although it treats them asymmetrically.
Example 1.2 Sara takes a multiple choice test with 100 questions. Each question is accompa-
nied by two possible answers and one and only one is true. For each question she chooses the
option she considers correct or does not answer it. She receives +1 for each correct answer,
−1 for each wrong answer and 0 for each unanswered question. She must obtain a minimum
score of 70 to pass the exam. The high qualiﬁcation required to pass the exam, treats the two
outputs asymmetrically.
Example 1.3 The 1951 world chess championship was a match between challenger David
Bronstein of the SovietUnion and his compatriotand defending champion MikhailBotvínnik.
The tournament format was the best of 24 games, but in case of a tie, the champion retains
the title. The result was 5 games won by each opponent and 14 draws. Due to the ﬁnal draw,
Mikhail Botvínnik retained the world title.

The formal language we use in this article is that of voting systems (decision functions),
and we refer exclusively to binary legislative voting, either to change the status quo or to
maintain it. We do not consider our context as suitable for the election of two candidates, as
it is not possible to treat them symmetrically. For instance, the abstention of all voters must
lead to a winner, which is unfair for the loser.
It should also be understood, as reﬂected in Examples 1.2 and 1.3, that the context we use
may be broader, i.e., it goes beyond the voting itself. Thus, the inputs can be questions, as in
Example 1.2, sport competition matches, as in Example 1.3, and so on.
In this article we are interested in characterizing axiomatically a set of resolute functions
called majority functions and the ‘relative majority function with status quo bias’ as a par-
ticular case of them. As we show, we just need to properly weaken neutrality to get these
characterizations.
The paper is organized as follows. Section2 introduces several classes of resolute rules,
i.e., discrete functions with three inputs and two outputs, among them we focus on major-
ity functions and the relative majority function with status quo bias. Section3 provides an
axiomatic characterization for the majority functions and for the relative majority function
with status quo bias. It also provides axiomatic characterizations for subfamilies of majority
functions: the incontestable functions, the yes-quota functions, the absolute majority func-
tion and the unanimity function. The paper concludes in Sect.4 by summarizing the main
achievements, relating the work in this paper with that in Jeong and Ju (2017) and proposing
some open questions.
2 Resolute majority functions
Assume that N is a set of n individuals (n ≥2), N = {1, . . . , n}, that have to make a
decision on a proposal. Each individual/voter has three balloting options: 1 (in favor of it),
−1 (against it) or 0 (indifferent to it). We assume that each voter chooses one of these three
options. The aggregated rule for the collective decision is formalized by a general decision
function f : D →E, where E is a subset of J = {−1, 0, 1} and D is a subset of J n. Then
May’s deﬁnition May (1952) of decision function amounts to the most general case: D = J n
and E = J , i.e., in May’s model, voters express preferences by three integers in J , where
1 means ‘A is preferred to B’, −1 means ‘B is preferred to A’ and 0 means indifference.
Throughout this article we assume that the decision functions we deal with satisfy:
• Universal Deﬁnition: each set of individual votes must lead to a valid result. In mathe-
matical terms, the domain of f is D = J n.
• Resoluteness: social indecision is avoided. In mathematical terms, the range of f is a
subset of K = {−1, 1}.
Observe that the number of universally deﬁned and resolute functions f : J n →K is
23n. Resoluteness means that the aggregated rule for the collective decision is binary: 1 (the

Deﬁnition 2.1 Majority function A majority function for n voters is a non-degenerate func-
tion deﬁned for all x = (x1, . . . , xn) ∈J n, such that f (x) = 1 implies:
• Nx(1) > Nx(−1), and
• f (y) = 1 for all y ∈J n such that Ny(1) ≥Nx(1) and Ny(−1) ≤Nx(−1).
Note that the non-degeneracy is necessary to discard the constant function f ≡−1 as a
majority function.
A necessary but not sufﬁcient condition for a majority function is that the required number
of ‘yes’ should be greater than the number of ‘no’ to break the status quo. If the condition
is also sufﬁcient then the obtained function is the relative majority function with status quo
bias.

3 Axiomatic characterizations
The purpose of this section is to axiomatically characterize the class of majority functions
and among these functions: the relative majority function with status quo bias, the subclasses
of incontestable functions and yes-quota functions and the absolute majority function and
the unanimity function as particular cases of them. We provide respective results and also
show that the axioms used in the respective proofs are independent. We start by introducing
the axioms we need.
3.1 Axioms
Now we consider some additional axioms, apart from universal deﬁnition and resoluteness
that are assumed the facto, which are used to provide the axiomatizations. Universal deﬁnition
and anonymity were already considered by May (1952) in his characterization of the relative
majority rule in the context of decision functions f : J n →J . The other two axioms he
used are: neutrality and positive responsiveness. Although the properties stated below make

4 Conclusion
This article investigates resolute decision functions, for which social indifference is not
allowed. As illustrated in the introduction these functions are commonly used in legislative
voting:referendums,amendingconstitutionsorlaws,etc.;butalsoinothercontextsasgrading
or sports competitions. In short, in any context where a collective decision must be made
between two alternatives without allowing social indifference.
The results that we have obtained are axiomatic characterizations of the set of majority
functions and the set of incontestable functions, as well as for the speciﬁc functions of
123

2122
Annals of Operations Research (2024) 336:2111–2124
relative majority functions with status quo bias and the absolute majority function. For all
these studied rules there is a built–in–bias towards ‘no’ (reject the proposal and leave the
status quo unchanged), which is seen as appropriate in the legislative setting.
Jeong and Ju (2017) also study resoluteness and characterize axiomatically two subfam-
ilies of resolute functions: relative majority rules with a tie-breaking function and majority
rules with a quorum and a tie-breaking function. Similarly, they also characterize the speciﬁc
functions of relative majority rules with status quo bias (the only coincidence with our work)
and the majority rule with a quorum and status-quo bias. In their model of relative majority
rules with a tie-breaking function, one afﬁrmative vote more than a negative one is sufﬁcient
to break the status quo. In addition, some ties can still break the status quo, the only exception
is the relative majority function with status quo bias.
There are two fundamental differences between the majority functions that we study and
the relative majority rules with a tie-breaking function that these authors study. The ﬁrst is
that in our model it is essential to get more favorable votes than unfavorable ones to approve
an amendment, i.e., a built–in–bias towards ‘no’. The second is that the previous condition
is not necessarily sufﬁcient. In other words, the resolute majority functions we use in this
article, compared to those in Jeong and Ju’s article, are more demanding to get the amendment
passed. In our model it is not possible to break the status quo with a tie, while in their model
it is.
The only function that is a majority function and a relative majority rule with a tie-
breaking function is the relative majority rule with status quo bias. This function is the least
demanding of the majority functions we consider in order to break the status quo, but the
most demanding of the relative majority rules with a tie-breaking function in order to break
the status quo. The axiomatic characterizations of the relative majority function with status
quo bias obtained in Jeong and Ju (2017) and the one in this paper share the assumptions
of: resoluteness, anonymity and amendment reversibility. Moreover Jeong and Ju assumes
weak monotonicity, conditional neutrality and strong Pareto, whereas our characterization
assumes monotonicity and weak neutrality. Thus, their three conditions are equivalent to our
two conditions as long as the three common axioms are assumed.
A third difference between the two works is that we consider yes-quota functions, which
require a minimum quota of yes votes to legitimize the amendment. Instead, they require
quorums, i.e., a certain minimum number of votes cast either favorably or unfavorably to
amendment. We think that demanding a minimum number of favorable votes is a procedure
that strongly legitimizes the breakdown of the status quo. Furthermore, these functions avoid
the strategic vote by the status quo supporters, who can speculate between voting ‘no’ or ‘not
voting’ when a quorum is required.
We conclude by pointing out a combinatorial remark, which could be of interest for future
research. Since its origins, mathematics has been devoted, among other topics, in classifying
and listing the set of objects or solutions to a given problem. In this sense, enumerating
subclasses of resolute functions is useful for knowing the order of magnitude of the voting
rules we deal with and for the design of appropriate rules from among those available.
Moreover, enumerations with respect to the number of voters allow to know the rate of
growth (polynomial, exponential, etc.) of the resulting integer sequences. Since 1964, the
online encyclopedia of integer sequences Sloane (1964), collects a huge number of integer
sequences that enumerate many kinds of objects. It would be interesting to compute the
number of resolute majority functions and the number of incontestable functions, up to
isomorphisms, as a function of the number of voters, since its growth is fast.
