Abstract
The growing number of people living alone in single-person households is a recent trend
which reveals the growing incidence of loneliness and social isolation. Loneliness has tra-
ditionally been associated with ageing, and problems of health and well-being. However,
voluntarily loneliness among young people and professional groups is now on the rise,
possibly linked to growing individualism, narcissism and, spatially, to a new dimension of
socio-spatial segregation. This makes single-person households highly heterogeneous nowa-
days, and lends greater importance to their study. To address this issue, a census tract analysis
was conducted in the four largest Spanish cities to examine the socio-spatial characteristics
of single-person households. The study explored both their global traits and spatial and local
heterogeneity using Geographically Weighted Regression models. Our results show that, in
urban Spain, these types of households are closely linked to the presence of the immigrant
population from the EU, the ageing population and people of working age, and show an
inverse relationship with income level at local scale. This inverse relationship, together with
the signiﬁcant geographical concentration of single-person households, is of particular inter-
est to ehp us draw conclusions which could facilitate planning and social dynamics in the
cities analyzed. Finally, we reﬂect on the challenges that living alone and social isolation
poses in the social context, and the importance of analyzing its effects and of promoting
public urban policies that favor social cohesion and social encounters.

1 Introduction
In more developed countries in general, and particularly in Europe, there has been a steady
growth in the trend towards living alone (e.g. Cacioppo & Cacioppo, 2018; Snell, 2017),
which is especially prominent in cities and metropolitan areas (Burlina & Rodriguez-Pose,
2023; Sandström & Karlsson, 2019). Indeed, some authors refer to Western societies as
suffering from a purported epidemic of loneliness (Kar-Purkayastha, 2010). Individualism
and post-materialism are a key feature in the process of the Second Demographic Transition,
which has taken place in most developed economies, but has barely begun in much of the rest
of the world (Bauman, 2000; Beck & Beck-Gernsheim, 2002; Esteve et al., 2020; Lesthaeghe,
2010): living alone is therefore a growing trend only in highly industrialized and urbanized
societies (Jamieson & Simpson, 2013; Klinenberg, 2012). In addition, in countries as diverse
as South Korea, India, China and in Europe, we ﬁnd studies that relate living alone and social
isolation with higher mortality or incidence of mental health problems (e.g. Byeon, 2021;
Qin et al., 2022; Simons et al., 2020; Srivastava et al., 2021), and there is little doubt about
the relationship between loneliness, physical problems, sociability deﬁcits and even higher
rates of suicide (Shaw et al., 2021). In line with this, a signiﬁcant part of the literature has
addressed the major age-related effects of loneliness (e.g. Banks et al., 2009; Mund et al.,
2020).
Studies looking at loneliness in single-person households have approached it as con-
sequence of the process of ageing. However, current changes in family and socialization
structures in Western societies show an increasing trend towards younger adults living alone
(Hall & Ogden, 2003), which may be the result of voluntary decisions, such as divorce or
separation. This has been further complicated by the notable reduction in the expectations
and possibilities of access to housing for single individuals (Crawford & McKee, 2018;
Hoolachan et al., 2017). In any case, a growing number of adults, rather than older people,
are living alone by choice (Vespa, 2017; Wilkinson, 2014), fuelled by factors such as the
increased participation of women in the labor force, longer life expectancy and growing
urbanization, which have led to new concepts in the family and cohabitation (Sandström
& Karlsson, 2019). Nevertheless, this does not mean that all these people are lonely, as
they often avoid loneliness by forging an extensive network of social relationships (Klinen-
berg, 2012). Other studies have analyzed, albeit with mixed results, the relationship between
single-person households and the socioeconomic status of people living alone and their resi-
dential contexts (Burlina & Rodríguez-Pose, 2023; Quintano & D’Agostino, 2006). In turn,
the growing socio-economic uncertainties, derived from precarious employment, the rising
cost of living and new modes of maintaining relationships, increase the tendency to live alone
for longer (Aeby & Heath, 2020), and since this has been shown to make access to housing
more difﬁcult in several European countries (Fritsch et al., 2023), it has been reﬂected in
neoliberal societies in an increase in shared accommodation (Druta et al., 2021).
Nevertheless, studies that have focused on the context of people living alone have typically
addressed the territorial distribution of single-person households as a proxy for loneliness
(e.g. Burlina & Rodríguez-Pose, 2023; Fritsch et al., 2023), linking the risk of loneliness
and social isolation to higher rates of single-person households (Havens et al., 2004; Kim
& Kim, 2024; Wenger & Burholt, 2004), and social isolation is also found to be common
among people living in ﬂats in urban areas (Chile et al., 2014). Other studies have linked
living alone with a higher degree of individualism, narcissism and post-materialistic values,
which seems to be closely connected to social behavior (Bosma et al., 2015; Hettlage, 2000;
Lesthaeghe, 2010; Tani et al., 2022).

These studies which analyze loneliness in single-person households have also shown that
they tend to be characterized by greater general ageing, which means it affects a higher
proportion of women than men, and a better socio-economic situation, especially in male
single-person households. In this context, although some studies have highlighted how this
type of household is better-off in Europe as a whole (Vespa, 2017), in southern European
countries such as Italy, Spain and Portugal, conversely, it is related to more vulnerable eco-
nomic situations (Burlina & Rodríguez-Pose, 2023; Iglesias-Pascual & Benassi, 2024).
Therefore, although single-person households are more numerous in northern Europe than
in southern Europe, the links between this type of household in southern Europe and higher
degrees of economic vulnerability makes it particularly important to analyze the situation
in southern Europe. Our focus should be not only on the role of living alone, but also on
the major problems of access to housing (Allen, 2006), the residential and social integration
of the immigrant population (Benassi & Iglesias-Pascual, 2023) and the social dimension
of the increasing tendency to live alone as a major driver of economic growth (Burlina &
Rodríguez-Pose, 2023).
Many studies have been carried out using sample data or low-detail scales and types
of data, which means that ecological fallacy or the low territorial representativeness of the
sample may be biasing the results. This is why it makes more sense to carry out contextual
studies to analyze the intra-urban local characteristics of the areas where such households
are common (Benassi et al., 2024a, 2024b). As regards the intra-urban spatial distribution
of single-person households, the few existing studies have found a range of patterns, from
cities where these types of households are distributed widely across the territory, such as the
complex pattern of Vienna, Milan or Rome, to others, as in Spanish and Portuguese cities,
where a concentration of these types of households is observed in city centers (Fritsch et al.,
2023; Iglesias-Pascual & Benassi, 2024; López Villanueva et al., 2019; Piekut, 2020). In the
case of Southern European cities, the combination of spatial concentration and the increased
vulnerable characterization of this type of household could be related to new patterns of
spatial polarization, which could represent a new dimension to take into account in studies
of residential segregation.
So far, the studies have focused on the analysis of the internal composition of this type of
household and its relationship with health and well-being. However, very few studies have
addressed their relationship with the surrounding context and their local heterogeneity in
spatial distribution. It is evident, then, that single-person households and the areas in which
they are commonly found are highly heterogeneous, and their contexts need to be analyzed
in order to develop policies aimed at well-being, socialization and an equitable distribution
of social services in the territory (Benassi et al., 2024a, 2024b; Esteve et al., 2020; Fritsch
et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023).
Therefore, there is a clear need to conduct new research which, in addition to focusing
on the relationship between living alone and health problems, analyzes the socio-economic
characteristics of this model of household, which is already common in Europe. It should
be based on rigorous proposals for studying spatial patterns and intra-urban heterogeneity in
order to detect the patterns of territorial distribution of these households, which is a key step
for putting forward proposals for adapting public services to promote greater socialization in
environments where the population living alone is in the majority. To ﬁll these gaps, this paper
presents a contextual analysis of the socio-spatial characteristics of single-person households
in Barcelona, Madrid, Seville and Valencia, the four most highly-populated cities in Spain.
We use, ﬁrst, a standard linear regression model (OLS) to analyze global dynamics, followed
by a Geographical Weight Regression (GWR) to study local patterns the spatial distribution
pattern of single-person households in the four selected cities evaluated at the census tract

level. Using this methodological approach allows us to observe the global and local patterns
of the main social variables related in the literature to loneliness in single-person households.
With this aim in mind, we posed the following research questions:
RQ1: What spatial pattern(s) do single-person households show in the main Spanish cities?
RQ2: What is the relationship between the main social variables highlighted in the liter-
ature and single-person households in the Spanish urban space?
RQ3: Can the relationship between spatial concentration and lower income levels be
considered a new pattern of residential segregation in Southern European cities?
To our knowledge, this paper represents the ﬁrst attempt to describe the contexts in which
single-person households are concentrated at a highly detailed geographical scale (i.e., the
intra-urban level), using spatial statistical analysis tools. In fact, its particular novelty lies
in the fact that it facilitates a global and local understanding of a social process which has
acquired growing importance, especially in European urban societies. This analysis of local
contexts with a majority of single-person households and people living alone is particularly
relevant both for planning public services adapted to the social demands of this new house-
hold model and for understanding social behavior related to increased individualism. After
reviewing the main studies on living alone, social isolation, and single-person households,
we discuss the characteristics of single-person households in Spain, and describe in detail
the variables and methodology used. The main results are then presented, and the subsequent
discussion reﬂects on the characteristics of single-person households in the four cities, con-
cluding with a reﬂection on the social and public policy implications of isolation, new forms
of vulnerability and the need to plan urban spaces that favor social interaction.

The empirical analysis was based upon explicit local spatial analysis techniques, which
allowed us to assess the spatial heterogeneity of the phenomenon. As shown in Table 2,
the dependent variable (y) was represented by the percentage of single-person households
computed at the sub-municipality level (i.e., at the census-tract level) for each of the four
cities analyzed. The idea was to estimate (and to map) its local determinants using local
regression models (GWR) (Brunsdon et al., 1996; Fotheringham et al., 2003).
As is well known, one of the key advantages of the local regression approach is that it
allows us to map local estimates (Matthews & Yang, 2012), thereby accounting for spatial
heterogeneity, which is often overlooked in global analyses (Lu et al., 2024). Indeed, the
fundamental idea underpinning GWR models is that the spatial stationarity of the relationship
between the dependent and independent variables is not assumed a priori, as occurs implicitly
with standard linear regression models like OLS (Benassi & Naccarato, 2017). In fact, in
our case, this assumption was tested using speciﬁc statistical methods, such as the Monte
Carlo test for spatial variability (Li & Fotheringham, 2020). The results (see the bottom
of Table 4) conﬁrmed that all the independent variables are affected by spatial variability,
reinforcing the need to adopt a local regression approach to better capture the local dimensions
of the observed process (Matthews & Yang, 2012). In local estimation approaches, we are
interested precisely in detecting the spatial non-stationarity of the regression coefﬁcients.
According to Oshan et al. (2019) for a generic GWR model, the linear regression model can
be formalized, assuming that there are n observations (i.e., n statistical units), for observation
i ∈{1, 2, . . . , n} at location (ui, vi), as follows:

two models was conducted according to the classic parameters relating to the explanatory

the results is required, was also made to facilitate efﬁcient computation, favoring interactions
and therefore enabling faster ﬁtting of the model itself (Oshan et al., 2019). The statical
signiﬁcance of the local estimation was evaluated using the adjusted t-value (95%) (Yu et al.,
2020). Finally, particular attention was given to possible problems of local multicollinearity
that may eventually arise in a local estimation (Wheeler & Tiefelsforf, 2005). To achieve this,
we conducted a rigorous analysis of the Local Condition Number (Local CN) parameter (see

3.2 Mapping local regression results
As mentioned above, one of the major advantages of using GWR models is its ability to
map local coefﬁcients, thereby providing insights into their geographical distribution and
identifying potential local spatial patterns (Benassi & Naccarato, 2017; Matthews & Yang,
2012). The geographical representation of the local coefﬁcients (Figs. 1, 2, 3, 4) provides
us with key data about the spatial relationships that occur at intra-urban level between each
independent variable and the dependent variable for each city analyzed here. In the case of
Barcelona (Fig. 1), for instance, the spatial distribution of the signiﬁcant local coefﬁcients is
relatively random and widespread.
No clear spatial patterns emerge, and only a small number of census tracts are statistically
signiﬁcant considering the adjusted t value (95%). This essentially means that in Barcelona,
the drivers of the dependent variables are essentially global, and, in any case, seem not to
be well accounted for by the independent variables used in the model. Indeed, the improved
performance of the GWR is mainly due to the intercept of the local model. Despite this
general premise, we should also underline that in some cases, there may be weak spatial
conﬁguration, as is the case of the variable related to the elderly population, which has a
positive impact on the dependent variable both in global and local estimate, with the latter
presenting a geographical distribution centered partly on the outskirts of the city. However,
these results may be affected by speciﬁc characteristics of the city of Barcelona, especially
when compared to those related to the other cities presented below. It is worth mentioning
that, as the results of other similar contextual studies carried out in Barcelona have shown,
the urban morphology and the socio-territorial conﬁguration of the city have inﬂuenced the
results, showing territorial patterns that do not correspond to what is usually described in the
literature (e.g. Lagonigro et al., 2018; Martori et al., 2022; Iglesias-Pascual et al., 2023).
In Madrid (Fig. 2), however, the situation is very different. Here, indeed, the spatial
distribution of the local coefﬁcients reveals interesting spatial patterns. The majority of the
local coefﬁcients of x1 have a negative impact on the dependent variable, which is precisely
the opposite effect that we estimated with the global model. This is of particular interest,
because it means that in several speciﬁc places of the city, especially outside the city center,
a lower incidence of single-person households corresponds to a higher average household
income. In contrast, the variable related to EU immigrants has a positive impact on the
dependent variable, as in the global model, although with a speciﬁc spatial distribution.

spatial patterns of single-person households have highlighted the presence of differentiated

Our approach was based on an analysis and description of the global and local spatial
patterns of these households based on the application of the GWR in the main Spanish cities.
First, the analysis and observation of the spatial patterns in the four cities of the dependent
variable ‘single-person households’ (see map in Appendix 1), allows us to conﬁrm results
found in previous studies on Spain: in other words, that single-person households show an
important degree of spatial concentration in the central areas of the cities (López Villanueva
et al., 2019; Iglesias-Pascual & Benassi, 2024). Based on this tendency towards concentration
and the results of the global and local dynamics shown by the OLS and GWR regression
analyses, we are able to answer the ﬁrst and third research questions. The clear pattern

of spatial concentration and the inverse local relationship with income levels leads us to
conclude, at least in southern Europe, that this type of household appears to reveal a new
pattern of spatial polarization related to vulnerability at a local level, which represents a new
pattern of residential segregation in Southern European cities and could be a new dimension
to consider for further studies in other countries. In this context, we would the growing
importance of single-person households in Southern Europe, and their relationship with
lower income levels and social isolation allow us to recommend that the analysis of this
type of household should be taken into account as a variable in studies focusing on social
vulnerability in urban environments (Kim & Kim, 2024). In fact, this type of household
should be a key variable in the planning of public services that attempt to promote social
interaction, such as social infrastructure (Iglesias-Pascual et al., 2023).
Similarly, the global trends in the relationship between single-person households and vari-
ables related to ageing, people of working age and foreigners allow us to answer the second
research question and conﬁrm the heterogeneous nature of the composition of single-person
households (Fritsch et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023). The clearly positive relationship with these
three variables indirectly reﬂects how common the increase in single-person households is
among different proﬁles of citizens. In particular, the relationship between the ageing and
foreign populations on the one hand, and single-person households on the other, reafﬁrms the
importance of addressing this issue as a key element for planning public services and reori-
enting vulnerability analyses in Southern European cities. Similarly, the clear relationship
with the working-age population and lower income levels makes it particularly interesting
to explore the possibility that this type of household can be considered as a key indicator of
the thinning-out and impoverishment of the middle classes and working people in Southern
European cities.
The analysis carried out at the local level, thanks to the GWR maps and their comparison
with the territorial distribution of the independent variables (Appendix 1), has allowed us
to study in depth the heterogeneity and diversity of this type of household. Although the
results for Barcelona are less clear, the local analysis shows how the relationship between
single-person households and the different variables is signiﬁcant in neighborhoods with
different income levels and situations. Thus, the relationship between income and single-
person households is negative, especially in certain areas of the selected cities, showing
spatial patterns in the peripheral areas of cities in Madrid, Seville and Valencia, which seems
to reafﬁrm the idea that single-person households, at least in Spanish cities, are exhibiting a
new pattern of spatial polarization. This pattern could be related to the previously high levels
of residential micro-segregation that some studies have shown for these cities, and the highly
speculative, divisive and fragmented nature of the Spanish real estate market (Benassi et al.,
2023; Castellano et al., 2022; Marci´nczak et al., 2021). In turn, in Madrid and Seville, the
relationship between single-person households and the working-age population in central
and high-income areas of the city appears to show the importance of gentriﬁcation dynamics
in both cities (Jover & Diaz-Parra, 2020; López-Gay et al., 2020). This is conﬁrmed by the
pattern of the ageing population in Madrid, and partially in Seville, where, as in Valencia, the
relationship between single-person households and the ageing population shows a predomi-
nantlyspatialpatterninlow-incomeareas.Finally,theimmigrantpopulationshowsperipheral
patterns in the large, bustling cities of Madrid and Valencia. In Seville, this population shows
high coefﬁcients in central areas with a high income level, which, as the population of EU
origin is usually made up of migrants with a higher income level and less social rejection
(Benassi & Iglesias-Pascual, 2023), can be related to the aforementioned major processes of
gentriﬁcation and touristiﬁcation occurring in this city (Diaz-Parra & Jover, 2021).

5 Conclusions
To conclude, we can highlight that the results at the local level demonstrate that, although it
may be an exaggeration to refer to an epidemic of loneliness (Kar-Purkayastha, 2010), it is
clear that single-person households have seen a major geographical and social growth in the
urban environment. The relationship shown between this type of household and loneliness,
with its accompanying physical and mental health problems (Shaw et al., 2021; Simons
et al., 2020), together with the high degree of socio-spatial polarization in the cities analyzed,
shows the importance of studying the social impact of this type of household using different
approaches, at least in Southern European societies. In fact, the increasing processes of social
polarization and the signiﬁcant trend towards house-sharing (Druta et al., 2021) could be
considered elements that mask the relationship between loneliness and social vulnerability
in other EU countries. Thus, for future studies, in order to conduct a holistic analysis of
loneliness and social isolation at the European level, it would be necessary to analyze the
role of single-person households and of shared housing jointly at the highest possible spatial
disaggregation level.
Methodologically, it is also worth highlighting how the use of a GWR model has allowed
us to explore in greater depth the heterogeneity and spatial variability of the latent particu-
larities within the global trend. The diversity of the results obtained shows the importance
of conducting studies based on spatial analysis that address the particular details of each
city in order to obtain results that are genuinely useful for designing future public policies
(Mashhoodi, 2021; Marci´nczak et al., 2025; Huete et al., 2024).
Fromthisviewpointofpublicpolices,giventheincreaseinprocessesofsocialisolation,we
would point to the accessibility to public services as a key factor to ensure the maintenance of
adequate levels of social cohesion in the urban space (Kim & Kim, 2024). The availability of
public services has been underlined previously as a fundamental element for the development
of social interaction in the city (Harvey, 2010; Allen & Farber, 2020; Bittencourt & Giannotti,
2023), especially in the case of neighborhoods with the most vulnerable population, and areas
of residential precariousness (Davern et al., 2017). Currently, the main approaches analyzing
the importance of loneliness and social isolation in the EU (Casabianca & Nurminen, 2024)
consider that action to prevent the consequences of living alone, social isolation and loneliness
should be aimed at increasing social contact and sociability (e.g., Gardiner et al., 2018; Mann
et al., 2017; Masi et al., 2011). In view of this, as other studies (Iglesias-Pascual et al., 2023)
have already shown, it is clear that local and contextual studies at an intra-urban scale, such
as the one we have conducted into single-person households, can be of great help when
planning social infrastructures aimed at increasing social cohesion in cities. In the same way,
the study of loneliness and single-person households could become an indicator to be taken
into account both in socio-territorial analyses of the city and in the design of local public
policies or intervention instruments such as the Urban Agenda in order to build socially and
environmentally sustainable cities.
Finally, with a view to developing this research line in the future, it is of particular impor-
tance to analyze the degree of accessibility of this type of household to social infrastructures
or basic services and study their model of territorial equality. Similarly, we should look into
the possible local relationship between urban areas where single-person households pre-
dominate and variables such as electoral behavior, ideology, mobility, consumption models,
levels of inequality and segregation. Only then can we fully understand the socio-territorial
implications that this household model and the growing trend towards living alone have on
societies.
