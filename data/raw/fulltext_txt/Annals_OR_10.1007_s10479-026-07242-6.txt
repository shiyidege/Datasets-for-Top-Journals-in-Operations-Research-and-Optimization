Abstract
In this study, due date assignment problem is addressed in a dynamic and stochastic single
machine environment with family setups. A due date is to be assigned for each new job
immediately at the time of its arrival. While assigning a due date for the new arrival, non-
complete jobs that have arrived before that job and whose due dates are already assigned, are
also considered and rescheduled. While assigning a short due date for the new arrival as close
as possible, compliance to assigned due dates of non-complete jobs is necessary. These two
objectives conÔ¨Çict with each other. The trade-off between assigning tight and loose due dates
reÔ¨Çects the trade-off between competitive power and delivery performance. To solve this
problem, a two-phase solution methodology is proposed where a sequential lexicographic
approach is followed. In the Ô¨Årst phase, a capacity allocation takes place for families before
observing any actual jobs arrivals, based on expected workload and arrival estimation, in a
periodic and static manner. In this phase, families can be assigned to batches, and a batching
structure is formed. In the second phase, a due date is assigned for the new arrival immediately
in an online fashion, based on the outputs of the Ô¨Årst phase. A mixed integer programming
model is developed for each phase. Also, a heuristic algorithm is proposed for the second
phase. Simultaneously, a discrete event simulation is carried out to imitate a real production
system.Theperformanceofthedesignedpolicyismeasuredthroughthedevelopedsimulation
model and results are reported under different control and system parameters.

1 Introduction
Due date is the date a job (order) is promised to be delivered to customers. Quoted lead time
is the difference between the assigned due date and arrival time of a job. Due date assignment
and lead time quotation has been receiving considerable attention in recent years among
academicians and practitioners. If a job is delivered before / after the due date, it is said to
be early / tardy. In both cases, we call the job as late. Earliness represents negative lateness
whereas tardiness represents positive lateness. When a job is completed before the due date,

When a job is completed after the due date, a company may confront tardiness penalties
such as monetary compensations for late delivery, customer dissatisfaction, loss of customer

During due date assignment process, depending on the closeness to job arrival times, due
dates can be set as tight or loose. Assigning loose due dates provides Ô¨Çexibility to assure
production and delivery reliability for a company but also leads to loss of competitive power
with respect to responsiveness to customer demand in highly competitive markets. This
may cause customers to prefer a competitor company that promises earlier delivery. On the
contrary, assigning tight due dates is attractive in terms of retaining customers but increases
the potential tardiness risk of jobs. Tardiness is an indicator of the compliance to the assigned
due dates. The trade-off between assigning tight and loose due dates reÔ¨Çects the trade-off
between competitive power and delivery performance. Hence, assigned due dates must be
both attainable and reliable.
Within the scope of this study, due date assignment problem is considered for batch
processing companies. The important attribute of batch processing companies is that they
have product family structures and family setups. A family is a set of jobs that can share a
common setup (changeover). A setup activity includes any preparation time of the resources
for the production run, such as cleaning, changing tools, adjusting, setting jigs and Ô¨Åxtures
etc. A signiÔ¨Åcant setup time may be required while switching from one to another product
family. Metal, automotive supplier, glass, paper and textile manufacturers are examples of
such companies. However, setups are non-value-added activities.
In practice, two different types of due date assignment methods are used in industry in
the presence of family setups. The Ô¨Årst one is the periodic due date assignment where due
dates of jobs are quoted on a periodic basis at Ô¨Åxed scheduling periods. In this method, due
dates can be assigned by a straightforward batching policy based on the jobs gathered within
a scheduling period whose data are already revealed at arrival times. A batch is a group
of jobs from the same family which can be processed consecutively by sharing a common
setup. In group technology, a single batch is formed for each family. Batching increases
operational efÔ¨Åciency through sharing a single setup for the jobs that belong to the same
family. The downside of periodic due date assignment is that customers must wait to get a
due date in addition to the Ô¨Çow time of jobs. This method may be acceptable for monopolistic
structures in low competitive markets where the company has full control on its customers
and usually produces in large volumes of batches. In monopolistic structure, companies may
prepare schedules only based on their internal operational efÔ¨Åciency concerns, without any
competitive pressure. But it is not a valid procedure for every market, especially in highly
competitive markets where companies usually produce in small volumes of batches.
Alternatively, due dates can be assigned immediately at the time of a new job arrival.
By this method, the waiting time to get a due date is removed. However, since job related
data is not known in advance at the time of due date quotation, gaining operational efÔ¨Åciency
through batching cannot be possible in a straightforward way from the point of manufacturer.
It may also potentially lead to a high number of setups if each new arrival is assigned based
on Ô¨Årst-come-Ô¨Årst-served (FCFS) rule.
Based on the nature of job arrivals, problems can be categorized as static and dynamic.
In static problems, all jobs are readily available at the time of scheduling. In dynamic prob-
lems, jobs may arrive over time and become schedulable at different time points. Another
classiÔ¨Åcation can be made regarding the characteristics of problem data. If all problem data

follows a probability distribution, the problem is called as stochastic.
In this study, immediate due date assignment is considered on a dynamic single machine
environment where jobs may arrive over time and job-related data reveal at its arrival time.
Moreover, family types of jobs, family setup times, interarrival times of jobs, job processing
times follow probability distributions. In immediate due date assignment process, at any time
instance, there may be at most one job whose due date is to be assigned instantly, as opposed
to the periodic due date assignment policy. While assigning a due date for a new arrival,
non-complete jobs must be taken into consideration. Once the due dates are set through
commitments, they cannot be altered. A shorter due date (and a shorter lead time) can be
assigned for the new arrival by shifting the scheduled non-complete jobs to the right-hand
side of the schedule at the expense of causing tardiness of non-complete jobs or a further due
date (and a longer lead time) can be assigned for the new arrival by keeping the promised
due dates for the non-complete jobs and preventing their tardiness. In this study, we want
to assign a close due date for a new arrival but we also want to impose an upper bound on
the tardiness of non-complete jobs with already assigned due dates. From companies‚Äô point
of view, tardiness minimization is vital since tardiness is an indicator of the compliance to
the assigned due dates. From customers‚Äô point of view, a shorter but reliable due date is
critical. Hence, an efÔ¨Åcient due date assignment policy should balance the trade-off between
competitiveness (tight due dates) and delivery reliability (limited tardiness of existing jobs).
If companies assign a Ô¨Åxed lead time to all customers without considering the order
characteristics and current system status, lead times may be understated in case of high
demand whereas lead times may be overstated in case of low demand. In the former case,
too tight and potentially missed due dates may lead to disappointed customers whereas in the
latter case, customers may prefer to go to competitor companies. A company‚Äôs competitive
position is determined by the capability to reduce lead times and meet tight due dates. Due
date performance is generally measured from two features: delivery reliability and delivery
speed. Delivery reliability is achieved by regularly meeting promised delivery dates. Delivery
speed is related to delivering customer orders through short lead times. Yet, short lead times
make sense if delivery reliability is guaranteed.
To the best of our knowledge, there is no study that combines dynamic and stochastic
environment with family setups to minimize the quoted lead time / assigned due date for new
arrivals while considering the tardiness of non-complete jobs. Therefore, this study can be
a contribution to Ô¨Åll part of this gap. Moreover, it can bring a solution to a real signiÔ¨Åcant
problem that is encountered frequently at batch type of processing companies in highly
competitive markets. A single bottleneck stage in a complicated production system may be
treated as a single machine environment. The single machine model may provide a basis for
multi-machine production systems.

2 Literature review
There is a noteworthy amount of research regarding due dates, family setups, and batching in
the Ô¨Åeld of scheduling. In most of the studies in scheduling, setup times are either ignored or
included in job processing times. But setups exist in real life problems. In family scheduling
problems, a family can be processed in a single batch or splitted into more than one batch.
A batch is a maximal set of jobs which are processed consecutively on a machine by sharing
a single setup.
In the presence of family setups, a setup is required before processing a job from a different
family. No setup is required between jobs from the same family. Setup time can be either
sequence-dependent where the duration depends both on the family to be processed next
and the immediately preceding family or sequence-independent where the length of setup
depends only on the family to be processed next.
In due date assignment (DDA) problems, due dates are decision variables in contrast to
classical scheduling problems where they are given parameters. In the studies summarized
in Table 1, due dates are to be determined in different settings with setups.

from different groups, but not of the same group. The loss of capacity due to setups can
be minimized by combining jobs with similar setup characteristics. In such environments,
jobs within the same group are processed in succession. GT does not allow family splitting.
Families cannot be partitioned into batches and a single batch must be formed for each family.

3 Problem deÔ¨Ånition
In immediate due date assignment, as it can be seen in Figure 1, at the arrival time of a new
job, there may be a set of completed jobs with already assigned due dates, a set of non-
complete jobs with already assigned due dates, and a setup operation may be carried out or
there may be a single job being processed with already assigned due date. The problem under
consideration can be deÔ¨Åned as follows. There is a single machine that can process one job at
a time. It is assumed that each job is composed of a single operation and once that operation
is processed, the job is said to be completed. Preemption is not allowed. It is assumed that
the machine is not subject to any breakdown or maintenance. The environment is dynamic,
jobs are not readily available at the beginning and the number of jobs to be scheduled and
job related information are not known in advance until jobs arrive. Moreover, job-related
data, namely interarrival times, processing times and family types are stochastic variables
that follow probability distributions.

time a j and processing time p j. A due date d j is immediately assigned for each job at their
arrival time. Once a due date is assigned for a job, it is irreversible and cannot be changed.
Also, there is an actual completion time c j associated with each job.
Jobs belong to different families based on their process similarities in production. For
each job j, the family it belongs is characterized by f ‚ààF where F is the set of all families.
A family is a set of jobs that require similar setup characteristics. Because of production
similarities, the jobs from the same family can be processed consecutively on the machine by

4 Solution methodology
As a solution methodology, a two-phase decision support mechanism is proposed. A forward-
looking family allocation problem is solved in the Ô¨Årst phase and capacity is allocated to
families before observing actual job arrivals, based on expected workload and arrival esti-
mation of families. As the schedule is allocated for families, in addition to expected work
congestion of new arrivals, non-complete jobs from previous periods are taken into consid-
eration and rescheduled. This problem is solved in an ofÔ¨Çine and periodic manner. Then due
date quotation takes place in the second phase in an online fashion.
The motivation behind implementing a two-phase structure is the different decision hori-
zons and information availability inherent in capacity planning and due date quotation.
Family-based capacity allocation and batching decisions have long-term implications on
machine utilization and setup efÔ¨Åciency and therefore are made in a proactive, ofÔ¨Çine, and
periodic manner using expected workload information and non-complete jobs from previous
periods. Once this family-level batching conÔ¨Åguration is determined, due date assignment
must be performed instantaneously upon each job arrival, which necessitates an online deci-
sion process. This separation allows us to balance operational efÔ¨Åciency (through proactive
capacity planning) and customer responsiveness (through immediate due date assignment).
The Ô¨Årst phase is designed in such a way that jobs from each family can be processed
in several batches formed for their family by allowing family splitting, as opposed to group
technology. The motivation for allowing family splitting in the Ô¨Årst phase arises due to the
trade-off between setup efÔ¨Åciency and delivery performance. While larger batches reduce
setup losses, they may deteriorate due date performance for other families, whereas smaller
batches improve responsiveness at the cost of additional setups. Unlike group technology
environment, family splitting enables multiple smaller batches for the same family, providing
Ô¨Çexibility to manage congestion and reduce excessive waiting times for jobs belonging to
families scheduled later in the horizon. Allowing family splitting is a deliberate modeling
choice to relax the rigid assumptions of classical group technology. By permitting a family
to be assigned to multiple batches, the model gains Ô¨Çexibility to mitigate excessive waiting
times caused by long production runs while still exploiting setup sharing among similar jobs,
rather than enforcing rigid batch structures. This structure explicitly captures the fundamental
trade-off between operational efÔ¨Åciency (fewer setups and higher machine utilization) and
delivery performance (shorter quoted due dates and improved customer satisfaction), which
is central to the problem studied.
Generally, large batches have the advantage of low number of setups and high level of
efÔ¨Åciency in terms of machine utilization. However, processing a large batch may delay
the processing of a job whose family is allocated further in the schedule. Setups are time-
consuming non-value-adding activities but dedicating a machine to long production runs for
one family may unavoidably cause jobs from other families to be tardy. On the contrary,
producing in smaller batches causes a greater number of setups and loss of productive efÔ¨Å-
ciency, yet customer satisfaction can be improved since shorter due dates can be quoted.
This trade-off between large batches with high operational efÔ¨Åciency and less setups ver-
sus small batches with competitive due dates and more setups must be solved. This also
reÔ¨Çects the trade-off between production efÔ¨Åciency (machine utilization) and delivery per-
formance (customer satisfaction). The solution should consider the batching structure and
family sequencing decisions.
Tight due dates reÔ¨Çect fast responsiveness to customer orders and are more attractive to
customers and provide an advantage in competitive markets. But as the tightness of due dates

increases, the risk of not completing and delivering the products on time also increases due to

The trade-off between short lead times and missed due dates must be considered by an
efÔ¨Åcient due date assignment policy. If the assigned due date is too early, the company may
not be able to complete the order on time and confronts delayed jobs. If the assigned due date
is further, the company may lose their customers to competitor companies offering shorter
delivery times. In addition, the company may be forced to offer a discount to their customers
to retain the business. Due date assignment cost may arise from lost sales in case of setting
further due dates and deferring customers from placing orders.
From a managerial perspective, quoted lead times, which are directly determined by
assigned due dates, represent the commitment made to the customer at the job arrival time. For
batch processing companies in competitive markets, customers‚Äô order acceptance decisions
are strongly inÔ¨Çuenced by quoted lead times. Quoted lead times are key determinants for
a customer whether to place an order or not. Generally, a maximum time limit is imposed
by the customers (called acceptable lead time). Therefore, minimizing quoted lead times
corresponds to enhancing the company‚Äôs competitive position, while controlling tardiness
ensures delivery reliability for already accepted orders. Shorter quoted lead times increase
competitiveness and increase the likelihood of order acceptance, while overly aggressive
quotations may lead to excessive tardiness and deterioration of delivery reliability for existing
orders, making lead time quotation a critical operational decision.
In the Ô¨Årst phase, a mathematical model is proposed to solve the batch allocation problem.
After loading batches into schedule, in the second phase, due date quotation for new arriving
jobs takes place. Whenever a new job arrives, a due date is assigned instantly based on the
outputs of the Ô¨Årst phase. In that sense, two phases are strongly interrelated. To solve due
date assignment problem, a mathematical model and a heuristic algorithm are developed.
4.1 1st phase: Batch allocation
The proposed MIP model constitutes the Ô¨Årst phase of the two-phase solution approach.
The planning horizon is split into time frames that correspond to scheduling periods. Batch
allocation model is solved at the beginning of every scheduling period to cover two scheduling
periods ahead and solved at the midpoint of that interval again to cover two periods. The
reason is always to be able to have an available position inside a batch and assign a due date
for a new arrival immediately.
In the model, in addition to the new arrivals that are expected during current scheduling
period, non-complete jobs whose due dates are assigned in previous period(s) but not com-
pleted yet by the beginning of the present scheduling period, are also taken into consideration
and rescheduled according to newly constructed family-batch structure (see Figure 3). The
family type and processing time of non-complete jobs reveal at their arrival times and their
due dates are assigned; hence these values are already known. Rescheduled completion times
of non-complete jobs in the new schedule need to be computed for tardiness calculations and
to Ô¨Ånd deviations from the assigned due dates.
Every time batch allocation problem is solved, the current status of the machine is taken
into consideration. Hence, the concept of Earliest Start Time (EST ), current batch,
current job and current time concepts are introduced into the model regarding the real-
izations on the machine.

7 Conclusions and further research
Due date assignment and lead time quotation are two popular concepts of the last decades.
Missingpromisedduedatesanddeliveringordersafterwardscausedifferentkindsoftardiness
penalties for a company. Assigned due dates must be as tight as possible to gain and sustain
competitive power in competitive markets, yet compliance to these dates is also critical in
terms of delivery reliability.
In this study, a modeling and decision framework is presented and analyzed for a novel due
date assignment policy under family setups which is based on a two-phase solution method-
ology. The proposed methodology enables to assign due dates for new arrivals immediately.
Within the framework of the solution approach, a mathematical model is developed for both
phases and a heuristic algorithm is also developed for the second phase. Two conÔ¨Çicting
performance measures are examined: average quoted lead time of new arrivals and average
tardiness of non-complete jobs.
Asimulationenvironmentisconstructedtomimicavirtualsinglemachineshopproduction
system to measure the effectiveness of the proposed solution approach. The effect of three
system control parameters (effective machine utilization parameter, setup time multiplier,
and the number of families) and two due date assignment control parameters (the length of
scheduling period and tardiness coefÔ¨Åcient) on two key performance indicators are examined.
The inference to be drawn from the computational tests can be summarized as follows.
When the length of scheduling period inclines, average quoted lead time grows due to larger
batch sizes and further due dates. Moreover, the increment in the length of scheduling period
leads to a decrement in average tardiness. When allowed total tardiness on non-complete jobs
rises, it enables rescheduling of non-complete jobs at the time of a new arrival in the proposed
due date assignment optimization model. By this way, lower average quoted lead times are
obtained through assigning closer due dates for new arrivals, at the expense of inÔ¨Çating the
tardiness of committed non-complete jobs through shifting their expected completion times
further in the schedule. On the contrary, when allowed total tardiness on non-complete jobs
123

Annals of Operations Research
drops, it ensures minimizing tardiness of non-complete jobs by the help of the proposed
optimization models, at the expense of causing higher average quoted lead times. Thus, the
trade-off between competitive power and delivery reliability is captured by the proposed
optimization models.
It is worth noting that the computational setting embeds multiple sources of stochasticity,
including random job arrivals, family assignments, processing times, and setup times. More-
over, the computational study explores sensitivity across several parameters and robustness
is partially assessed by varying the number of families, the length of scheduling period,
machine utilization level, setup time multiplier, and tardiness coefÔ¨Åcient. The combination
of these factors provides representative and sufÔ¨Åcient heterogeneous operating conditions for
batch manufacturing systems. In addition, the proposed methodology in this study is generic
and Ô¨Çexible and can be adapted to different stochastic environments by adjusting the param-
eter values according to the characteristics of the production system. A more comprehensive
stochastic robustness or sensitivity analysis over a wider range of parameters is an avenue
for future research.
As another future research direction, the production environment can be extended to multi-
machine setting such as parallel machines, Ô¨Çow shop, and job shop. Machine breakdown
can be considered. As a different solution methodology, meta-heuristic algorithms, such as
simulated annealing, tabu search, and genetic algorithm, can be implemented.
