Abstract
Mean–Variance (M–V) is the most popular investment rule employed by both practitioners
and researches. For a short-planned investment horizon, this rule generally conforms with
expected utility paradigm. However, for a relatively long horizon, generally more than one
year, the distributions of returns become positively skewed, hence the M–V rule loses ground.
As the horizon increases, by the M–V rule one needs (mistakenly) to increase the weight of
bonds in the stock–bond portfolio, e.g., almost 100% in bonds for a 30-year horizon. Expected
utility maximization recommending almost 100% in stocks for long horizons. This, gap is
of crucial importance, because life expectancy is increasing, implying longer investment
horizons. For long horizons the mean–coefﬁcient-of-variance rule conforms with expected
utility, as the distributions are close to log-normal. For intermediate horizons one should
employ stochastic dominance or direct expected utility maximization.
1 Introduction
This paper deals with portfolio selection, a topic that Professor Ziemba has a huge contribu-
tion, mainly in the area of static and dynamic portfolio and capital growth, various type of
trading and prediction and risk control models for variety of asset classes. He also employed
portfolio selection models to study the Japanese security market irregularities and the U.S.
Index Futures stock market (Ziemba, 1991a, 1991b, 2016, 2020). Inspired by his work this
study adds one more aspect to portfolio analysis: the effect of the assumed investment horizon
on optimal portfolio diversiﬁcation.
Most theoretical and empirical studies in ﬁnance assume, explicitly or implicitly, a one-
period investment model—typically a period of one month or at most one year. For example,
Markowitz’s portfolio selection model (Markowitz, 1952) and Sharpe (1964) and Lintner’s
(1965) equilibrium Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) assume one-period model. The
commonly employed Sharpe Ratio, reporting the performance of various funds, generally
employs monthly data. Thus, if investors employ this ratio in selecting their investment, they
explicitly or implicitly assume a one-month planned investment horizon. Similarly, testing
empirically the CAPM and other theoretical models is also generally done by employing
monthly or annual rates of return, e.g., see Fama and French (1992), and namely Sharpe

(1966). In contrast to this assumed short horizon, in practice, many investors plan to invest
for long periods, certainly for longer than one month or even one year.
Suppose that one employs monthly rates of return to construct her optimal portfolio, while
her planned investment horizon is longer, say, 10 years. Does this horizon mismatch lead to a
distortion in the investment selection? To economic loss? Moreover, most portfolio selection
models rely on the Mean–Variance (M–V) rule. Is it possible that employing the M–V rule
is consistent with expected utility maximization (hence, does not incur an economic loss),
for relatively short investment horizons, but for relatively long horizons employing the M–V
rule does imply a loss in terms of expected utility? Employing historical data, we ﬁnd that for
a one-year investment horizon the weight of stocks in the optimal M–V stock–bond portfolio
is about 41%, but for 30-year investment horizon it is merely 4%! This is in contrast to the
optimal weight in stocks implied by expected utility maximization which is 80–100% for
short horizons (depending on the utility function) and approaches100% for longer horizons.
We show that employing the M–V rule for long investment horizons (a few years, or more)
induces an economic loss; the longer the horizon, the larger the loss.
The reason for the sensitivity of the portfolio composition to the assumed horizon is
that the various parameters (e.g., means, variances and correlations) needed for investment
decision making do not change linearly with the horizon. Moreover, we theoretically show
that positive correlations decrease with the horizon and negative correlations increase with the
horizon. Therefore, employing monthly parameters for investment decision corresponding
to a longer investment horizon may induce economic distortion. This loss may occur in both
M–V and expected utility frameworks, but we ﬁnd that the dramatic loss corresponds to
the M–V framework. The explanation for this result is that the M–V rule is inappropriate to
employforlonghorizonsasthedistributionsofreturnbecomepositivelyskewed,acasewhere
the M–V rule loses ground. Yet, the expected utility maximization and the corresponding
stochastic dominance (SD) rules are valid regardless of the shape of the distributions under
consideration, namely, skewness is taken into consideration by these two paradigms.
It is important to emphasize at the outset that assuming a relatively long investment horizon
does not mean that investors cannot revise their portfolios periodically. For example, suppose
that an investor plans to invest for retirement, say in 30 years. Given the current estimated
distributions of return (which may be objective or subjective) of various assets, say, stocks
and bonds, corresponding to a date 30 years ahead, a decision may be made to allocate,
say, 70% to stocks and 30% in bonds. After one year has elapsed, due to changes in the
economy, this investor may revise the estimated distribution of the return (corresponding
to a date 29 years ahead and may change their portfolio, say, to allocate 50% to stocks
and 50% to bonds. Note that this change may occur even under the i.i.d assumption as the
30-year’s distribution looks different than the 29-year’s distribution. Thus, having a long-
planned investment horizon does not imply that revisions are not allowed. Notwithstanding,
faced with the same assets, and even with the same mean return and risk estimates, a 70-
year-old investor with a relatively short investment horizon may have a completely different
optimal diversiﬁcation than a 30-year-old investor who plans to invest for, say, 40 years.
Thus, the planned (rather than the actual) investment horizon plays a crucial role in selecting

the optimal investment.1 Therefore, the ﬁrst step in selecting the optimal investment is to
determine the planned investment horizon.
The importance of the assumed investment horizon for choosing the optimal investment
(particularly of stocks and bonds) has increased rapidly in the last few decades due to two main
factors: a continuous increase in life expectancy (hence long investment horizons become
more relevant), and very low prevailing real interest rates for many years, at least until year
2021.
Finally, note that we do not rule out that there are two groups of investors in the market,
despite the fact that the investors in both groups plan to invest for the same number of years,
say, 4 years. The investors in one group make investment decisions based on the distribution
of 4-year return and the investors in the other group, despite planning the investment also for
4 years, make the investment decisions based, say, on the distributions of the annual return,
namely revise their portfolio every year. In this study we relate only to the ﬁrst group of
investors who do not plan short run revisions, hence beneﬁt from across time diversiﬁcation,
a beneﬁt that does not exist for the other group of investors. Thus, if the planned investment
horizon is N-years, only the N-year distributions of return on the various assets are considered.
The structure of the rest of the paper is as follows: Sect. 2 reports on the increase in life
expectancy, hence the relevancy of long planned investment horizons. Section 3 analyzes
the changes of the distributions of return and focuses particularly on the skewness built
up with the horizon. Section 4 analyze the best ﬁt theoretical distribution to the empirical
distribution for various assets and for various horizons. Section 5 presents the relationship
between one-period and multi-period M–V dominance and the changes in the correlations
with the horizon. Section 6 provides the one–period and multi-period stochastic dominance
(SD) rules. The opposite changes in the M–V and SSD efﬁcient set size with the horizon is
also presented. Section 7 is devoted to the theoretical relationships between the one-period
and multi-period dominance by the M–V and SSD rules when diversiﬁcation is considered.
Section 8 is devoted to the empirical stocks-bonds optimal M–V diversiﬁcation for various
horizons. Section 9 is similar to Sect. 8 when diversiﬁcation between ten deciles of stocks
is considered. In Sect. 10 we report on the optimal expected utility diversiﬁcation between
stocks and bonds for various utility functions and for various horizons. We also contrast the
expected utility results with the optimal M–V recommended diversiﬁcation between these
two assets that are reported in Sect. 8. Section 11 concludes.
2 The increase in life expectancy accompanied with low real interest
rates
A little more than a century ago, with life expectancy of about 40 years (see Fig. 1), saving
for pension was unnecessary, but nowadays, with a life expectancy of 80 years or even more
in many western countries, one should plan an investment-consumption behavior such that
there are enough savings for retirement, which should last for 2–3 decades.

The second issue is related to the available savings vehicle. A few decades ago the real
risk-free interest rate and the interest rate on Government bonds with moderate risk was high
enough to allow investors in bonds to accumulate some wealth for retirement. However, in
the last two decades, the risk-free interest rate, as well as the interest rate on bonds with
moderate risk, in most western countries has been very low, and in certain countries, it was
even negative in real terms, implying that the relative attractiveness of bonds has decreased
relative to the 1980s when the interest rate was relatively high. Let us discuss each of these
two factors affecting the investment strategy for the long run.
Figure 1 reveals that during the period 1950–2019 life expectancy increased rapidly due to
substantial health improvements. In this ﬁgure, it is shown that the life expectancy in Ethiopia,
South Africa, and India increased from less than 40 years to 60–70 years in less than seven
decades. Worldwide, life expectancy, on average, has risen from less than 50 years to more
than 70 years. Thus, roughly every year, 0.39 years have been added to the life expectancy.
In the richest countries, life expectancy in 2019 was over 80 years. For example, in Spain,
Switzerland, Italy, and Australia, life expectancy was over 83 years, and in Japan, it was close
to 85 years. Thus, even under the realistic assumption that this trend in the increase in life
expectancy will not continue at the same pace in the future, with the existing life expectancy
in many countries, people are living for a relatively long time after retirement; hence, funds
should be saved for this long period, savings which were not needed about 100 years ago.
For example, in 2020, the retirement age in Japan was raised to 65. This means that in Japan
one needs, to save, on average, for about 20 years of consumption after the retirement age.
Therefore, long planned investment horizons become very relevant which, in turn, may affect
the portfolio choice, as long as the portfolio composition depends on the investment horizon.
The second issue, which is related to the previous one, is the low prevailing interest rate,
particularly over the last two decades, ending in 2021. Figure 2 presents the nominal one-year
interest rate on US Treasury bonds, the inﬂation rate, and the implied real interest rate. As

are the forward-looking real interest rates which are relevant for savers who are beginning to

the ﬁnancial terms improved as the real yield on TIPS was mainly in the positive territory,
close to zero for short maturities and about 1.5% for relative long maturities. Of course, one
can obtain a larger expected real yield by investing in risky long-term corporate bonds, but
at the cost of being exposed to a larger risk.

asters for the retirees. This phenomenon is not new, and is well known to economists and

In this study, we try to explore alternative investment strategies that may mitigate the
economic challenge posed by the two factors discussed above. We focus on the investment
in stocks and risky bonds for various investment horizons noticing that the distributions of
rates of return on risky assets change with the horizon (see next section). It is well known that
the expected return of an investment in stocks is larger than that of bonds, and this is intact
for all horizons. Moreover, based on the past returns of about a century, the probability of
ending up with larger wealth by investing in stocks, rather than in bonds increases with the
investment horizon. Yet, this does not imply that all long investment horizon investors should
necessarily invest mainly in stocks. The reason is that even for a relatively long horizon an
extreme loss (with a relatively low probability) may occur with holding stocks rather than
bonds. Thus, the choice of stocks or bonds, or a mix of these two types of assets, may change
with the horizon, but the desired direction of these changes is theoretically ambiguous as it
depends on the individual’s tolerance to this risk located in the left tails of the distributions
of the returns on stocks and bonds. However, we show in this study that for most, albeit not
all, common utility functions the optimal investment in stocks increases with the horizon.
3 Changes of the distributions of return with the horizon: skewness
built up
The investment criteria for optimal investment decision making that conform with expected
utility maximization (or expected value function as advocated by prospect theory) depend on
the distribution of the returns characterizing the various risky prospects under consideration.
For example, if the distributions of returns are normal (or more generally, distributions that
belong to the elliptic family distributions), one can safely employ the well-known Markowitz
mean–variance (M–V) rule for choosing between prospects, as well for ﬁnding the efﬁcient
investment diversiﬁcation strategies.3 Moreover, if the riskless asset is also available, one can
also ﬁnd for all risk-averters the optimal portfolio of risky assets, and under some additional
assumptions, the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) suggested by Sharpe4 can be derived.
Unfortunately, the distributions of returns, particularly of long horizon returns, are not normal
and employing the M–V rule may be economically misleading.

Whereas there is a lot of information on the distribution of returns calculated for relatively
short horizons (mainly weekly and monthly distributions), less information is available on
long-horizon investment, say, 5 or 10-year return distributions. Knowing the shape of the
distributions of return corresponding to various investment horizons is important, as there
are in the market investors who invest for various investment horizons, and for each horizon,
a different distribution of returns may emerge. Speciﬁcally, relatively short-horizon investors
may apply the M–V rule for investment decisions (as the distributions are symmetrical, e.g.,
logistic or normal, or the range of returns is relatively small, see Levy & Markowitz, 1979),
but long-horizon investors need to employ different investment criteria, as the distribution of
returns are no longer elliptic. Thus, investors with different horizons need to employ different
investment rules, ending up with different optimal investments.
Moreover, as the distributions of return change with the horizon, investment consultants
must ﬁrst know the length of the planned investment horizon of each client, as the opti-
mal investment decision that is relevant to the investor under consideration depends on the
shape of the distribution of returns corresponding to the investor’s planned investment hori-
zon. Therefore, different investors with different planned investment horizons may receive
different advice concerning their optimal investment.
The following citation taken from the study of Fama and French (2018) regarding the
investment horizon explains the importance of the main subject discussed in this study:
“Wealsoknowalotaboutthecharacteristicsofshort-horizonstockreturns.Distribution
of daily and monthly returns, for example, are leptokurtic relative to normal distribution
(Fama, 1965). Because we have fewer observations, we know less about distributions
of long run returns. It seems likely, however, that the characteristics of long-horizon
returns are of central interest to investors saving for distant payoffs. If so, it is likely
that the characteristics of long-horizon returns affect asset pricing in ways missed by
our models. Evidence on distribution of long-horizon returns is a logical ﬁrst step in
remedying this deﬁciency” (Fama and French p.232).
To overcome the shortage of observations when long-run distributions are analyzed, Fama
and French suggest the bootstrap methodology. Employing this methodology, they ﬁnd that
the long-term (30 years) rates of return of the empirical distributions ﬁt well the log-normal
distribution. However, it is well known that the log-normal distribution is positively skewed,
hence one must take this property into account in the investment decision-making process.
Speciﬁcally, one cannot safely employ the M–V rule for long investment horizons, a rule that
ignores the skewness. Recall that skewness is important for all risk averters; the larger the
skewness, other things being held constant, the larger the risk averters expected utility (see
Arrow, 1971).
WeemploybelowtheCrystalBall programtoﬁndoutwhichofthetheoreticaldistributions
out of a set of a relatively large number of theoretical distributions best ﬁts the empirical
distribution of return corresponding to various horizons. As we shall see, the distribution that
best ﬁts the data changes with the horizon, which causes a major difﬁculty for the existing
methodology of investment decision-making under risk, even if there is full information on
the shape of the distribution corresponding to each given horizon.
Speciﬁcally, we ﬁnd that for a relatively short horizon, less than about 9 months (depending
on the data set) the distributions are approximately symmetric, and the skewness can be
safely ignored. For longer horizons, we get some instability in the shape of the empirical
distributions, as the best ﬁt theoretical distribution changes from one horizon to another.
However, for a horizon longer than about one year (once again, this horizon also changes

skewness is generally built up, and the distribution tends to be log-normal. Thus, unless the
horizon is relatively small, the popular M–V rule generally cannotbe employed for investment
choices without incurring economic loss.
4 The best ﬁt theoretical distribution for various investment horizons
In this section we report on the changes in the shape of the empirical distributions of vari-
ous assets with the assumed changes in the investment horizon under the i.i.d. assumption.
Introducing the relevant empirical autocorrelation does not change much the results reported
below (for more details, see Levy, 2022). The goal is to ﬁnd out, for each horizon and for
each asset, the theoretical distribution that best ﬁts the empirical distribution, with particular
emphasis on the speed of the skewness built up with the increase in the horizon. We partic-
ularly analyze for which horizons the empirical distributions are symmetrical and also close
to elliptic (e.g., normal, logistic, etc.), a case where the M–V rule may safely be employed.
We next analyze for which horizons a substantial skewness exists, a case where, the elliptic
distribution is no longer the best ﬁt and, therefore, the M–V rule is not optimal in these cases.
We also analyze assets with different degrees of volatility; hence, we also indirectly examine
empirically the effect of the one-period mean and standard deviation on the skewness built
up corresponding to various horizons.
The bootstrapping procedure, that assumes i.i.d., is employed based on two sets of data:
the ﬁrst one is French’s Website5 that provides the monthly rates of return corresponding
to the period July 1927–October 2020, and the other is the Aswath Damodaran data-set6
providing annual rates of return for the period 1928–2019. In the ﬁrst data set, we have
monthly rates of return for various classes of assets, particularly for ten deciles of stocks
classiﬁed by their market value. In the second data set, we have annual rates of return for the
S&P 500 stock index, for 10-year Treasury bonds, and for short term Treasury bills.
With the ﬁrst data set we rely on the monthly historical empirical distribution. For invest-
ment horizons longer than one month, we employ the bootstrapping technique. For example,
for an investment horizon of N  2 months, we randomly draw two observations from the
monthly return distribution (with replacement), and multiply the two returns to obtain the
2-month horizon return. We repeat this procedure 100,000 times to obtain the two-month
horizon distribution. We employ the same procedure up to a 360-month investment horizon.
Thus, we cover also the investment horizon for investment for retirement, namely 30 years.
With the other set of data as we have annual rates of return, we employ the same procedure,
but in this case, the shortest horizon is one year. With these two data sets, we analyze the
changes in the empirical distribution with the horizon for relatively risky assets (e.g., the
ﬁrst decile of the smallest ﬁrms traded in the U.S. market) as well as for relatively less risky
assets like the decile of the largest 10% stocks as well as Treasury bills and 10-year Treasury
bonds.

5.1 The mean–variance rule and the horizon
The most popular investment rule under uncertainty is the mean variance (M–V) rule, sug-
gested and developed by Markowitz12 and Tobin (1958), two Nobel laureates in Economics.
Facing two uncertain prospects (stocks, bonds, mutual funds, or any other pair of investments)
denoted by F and G, prospect F dominates prospect G by the M–V rule if the following two
conditions hold,
a) EF(x) ≥EG(x)
b) σF(x) ≤σG(x)
(1)
and to avoid trivial cases, it is required to have at least one strict inequality, either in condition
a) or in condition b) given above.
The interesting question to which we devote this study, among others, is whether the
assumed investment horizon affects the M–V and the SD dominance relationship. This is
theoretically, as well as practically important, as in the market, there are investors with
various investment horizons.
Suppose that one investor considers investing either in prospect F or in prospect G for
one-year, and another investor considers investing in one of these two prospects for two-years.

prospect over the other, but for a two-year horizon such dominance emerges? The answer to
such questions, which are of crucial importance to investors with various investment horizons,
are given below.

5.2 Correlations and the horizon
So far, we have compared two mutually exclusive prospects, F and G, and the effect of the
assumed horizon on the mean and on the variance, hence on the M–V dominance relationship.
In practice, one can invest in a diversiﬁed portfolio of these two assets, or even can invest in
a diversiﬁed portfolio composed of many available assets in the market. In this more relevant
case one needs to consider the correlations’ effect on the optimal M–V diversiﬁed portfolio.
These pair-wise correlations may be also affected by the horizon, hence affect the optimal
diversiﬁcation. To this issue we turn next.
Onceagainbecausethereturnsaremultiplicative,thecorrelationschangewiththeassumed
interval horizon over which the various variables are measured. Using the same data set (say
the last 20 years of data), with i.i.d assumption, we may obtain different correlations for,
say, monthly and annual rates of return. In other words, the way we cut the given data set
into intervals affects the calculated correlations. Therefore, selecting the appropriate horizon
for measuring the correlation is very important.
It is worth mentioning that although this study focuses on portfolio investment decision
making, the implications of the correlation results presented in this section may be relevant to
studies in various research areas such as chemistry, biology, medicine, physics, and mainly,
economics. Whenever one of the two variables under consideration is multiplicative, the
horizon, or the selected interval unit for measurement affects the obtained correlation. When
the two variables under consideration are additive, such problems generally do not arise. Just
to mention a few economic phenomena where the variables are, by their nature, multiplicative,
consider studies that analyze the relationship between the growth rate in the economy, say,
in terms of the growth of the GDP, and the growth rate in the population. Exactly like with
ﬁnancial assets, a growth rate of 5% in the GDP in each of two consecutive years implies
a growth rate for the two years of 10.25%, rather than 10%, as would be obtained with an
additive measurement. This seemingly small difference has an accumulated effect and may
drastically affect the obtained long-run correlation. Despite the existing horizon’s effect on
many multiplicative phenomena, in this study, we focus on the effect related to the capital
market, particularly to ﬁnancial assets.
Suppose that, based on the last 50 years of data, for two random variables, say, the return
on two risky assets corresponding to given one-period, say, one-year, the obtained correlation
is ρ1  0.6. What would be the correlation for a longer interval horizon measurement, say,
a bi-annual rate of return? Thus, based on 50 years of available data, we can calculate the

of returns. Adhering to the above example, it is obvious that if the returns are dependent over
time, the bi-annual correlation may increase or decrease depending on the type of dependency
over time of the return of each of the two assets under consideration. However, as with the

misleading, and the correlation changes with the horizon in some peculiar way even in the
i.i.d. case. Let us elaborate on the effect of the horizon on the correlation with ﬁnancial

multi-period M–V rule, as both rules assume risk aversion.
Proposition 4 Denote by F N(x) and G N(x) the N-period cumulative distribution of termi-
nal wealth of prospects F and G, respectively. Then, assuming independence over time, a
sufﬁcient condition for SSD of F N(x) over G N(x) is that there is SSD in each one-period for
all periods, i  1,2, . . . , N (and as usual, to avoid trivial cases, we need to have at least
one strict inequality). However, if there is no one period SSD we may have multi-period SSD.
(For a proof see Levy, 1973b).
Corollary 1 If returns of each prospect under consideration are i.i.d., the multi-period FSD
and SSD efﬁcient sets, respectively, decrease, in the weak sense, with the horizon. This is
straightforward from the above propositions.
The most interesting case is the comparison of SSD and M–V efﬁcient sets as both rules
assume risk aversion. Suppose that the one-period distributions are normal, hence the one
period M–V and SSD efﬁcient sets are identical. Figure 5 illustrates the changes in the size
of the M–V and SSD efﬁcient set as a function of the assumed investment horizon.
Thus, the multi-period M–V efﬁcient set increases with the horizon while the SSD efﬁcient
set decreases with the horizon. As the multi-period distributions are not normal for N > 1,
and as the SSD is distribution-free, it is obvious that the reduction in the SSD efﬁcient set
is correct and the increase in the M–V efﬁcient set with the horizon is technically correct
but is economically wrong. Investors who follow the M–V rule may select a prospect that is
multi-period M–V efﬁcient, but is inferior by SSD, hence an economic loss occurs by such
choices.
Finally, it is worth mentioning that the multi-period stochastic dominance rules may be
derived under several assumptions regarding the multi-variate utility function and not only
as a utility function deﬁned on terminal wealth as done in this study. For more details on the
various possibilities, see Haung, Vertinsky and Ziemba (1978).

7 Diversiﬁcation: M–V and SSD eﬃcient sets: the one-period
and multi-period cases
So far, we assumed that the investor faces two prospects, F and G, and wishes to invest
in one of them. In this section, we assume many available assets and that diversiﬁcation is
allowed, hence also correlations discussed above play a central role in selecting the optimal
portfolio. We analyze the one-period M–V efﬁcient frontier, that is identical to the one-period
SSD efﬁcient frontier, when the one-period distributions are assumed to be normal. Then we
analyze the optimal choices when one shifts from the one-period case to the multi-period
case, knowing that the multi-period distributions are no longer normal. In this case, the M–V
and SSD models depart. Figure 6 illustrates the well-known M–V frontier and the M–V
efﬁcient and inefﬁcient sets.
Curve abc is the M–V frontier where segment ab is M–V inefﬁcient and only segment
bc is the efﬁcient curve. Thus, with one-period normal distributions, all risk-averse investors
(by M–V and SSD alike) would choose the optimal portfolios from segment bc, where the
optimal portfolio of each individual investor is selected from this segment depending on their
risk tolerance, namely, depending on their personal preference. As for normal distributions
the M–V rule and the SSD rule coincide, we can safely conclude that all the SSD one-period
efﬁcient portfolios are also located on segment bc.
Shifting to the multi-period case, the M–V rule and the SSD rule yield different results
induced by the fact that normality no longer holds. Let us ﬁrst start with the multi-period
M–V case. First note that any interior portfolio in the one-period M–V inefﬁcient set is also
included in the multi-period M–V inefﬁcient set. For example, for any one-period interior
portfolio like portfoliod, there is a portfolio d′ that dominates it by the multi-period M–V rule.
To see this claim, note that the multi-period means of d and d′ are identical (just as the one-
period means are identical). As the means are identical and for the one-period variances, we
have σ 2(d)σ 2

d′ is smaller than the multi-period variance of portfolio d; therefore, portfolio d′ dominates
portfolio d by the multi-period M–V rule. Using the same logic, we can safely conclude that
all interior portfolios are M–V inefﬁcient both in the one-period and multi-period cases.

We turn now to segment ab, which is M–V inefﬁcient with a one-period horizon. Actually,
in the one-period case, portfolio b dominates all portfolios located on segment ab as it has a
larger mean and a smaller variance of all these portfolios. This M–V dominance is not intact
in the multi-period case. To see this, let us compare portfolios e and b given in Fig. 6. It is
obvious that the mean of portfolio b is also larger than the mean of portfolio e in the multi-
period case. However, as the multi-period variance is an increasing function of the mean, we
may ﬁnd that the multi-period variance of portfolio b is larger than the multi-period variance
of portfolio e. Thus, both portfolios may be M–V multi-period efﬁcient. One is tempted to
believe that although portfolio b does not dominate portfolio e by the M–V multi-period rule,
portfolio e′ may dominate portfolio e. However, this assertion also does not hold, as these
two portfolios have the same one-period variance, and portfolio e′ has a larger mean, and by
Eq. (6), portfolio e′ has a larger multi-period variance than portfolio e. Hence, portfolio e′
does not dominate portfolio e according to the multi-period M–V rule.
Thus, in principle, all portfolios located on the one-period segment bc are multi-period
M–V efﬁcient, and some or all portfolios (depending of the speciﬁc parameters) located on
segment ab may also be M–V efﬁcient in the multi-period case. In the extreme case, all of the
frontier abc, unlike in the one-period case, may be M–V efﬁcient in the multi-period case.
We turn now to the multi-period SSD. As for normal distribution the SSD and M–V
rules coincide, the one-period SSD efﬁcient set is, like the one-period M–V efﬁcient set, the
segment bc. However, as by Proposition 4, SSD in one-period implies SSD in the multi-period
case, we can safely conclude that all portfolios not located on segment bc are relegated to
the SSD inefﬁcient set both in the one-period, as well as in the multi-period case. However,
we may have no SSD in the one-period case, but may have SSD in the multi-period case.
Therefore, we may have “holes” in segment bc where the holes represent multi-period SSD
inefﬁcient portfolios. For example, portfolio f may be one-period SSD efﬁcient, but multi-
period SSD inefﬁcient. The determination of these potential holes depends on the data set
under consideration.
In conclusion, there is very little in common between the multi-period M–V and SSD
efﬁcient diversiﬁcations, despite the fact that both rules are identical in the one-period case,
as long as normality is assumed. The M–V multi-period efﬁcient set may include all portfolios
located on segment abc, while the SSD multi-period efﬁcient set is a subset (in the weak
sense) of segment bc.
The economic consequence of the above analysis is clear: if one employs the M–V rule for
any horizon longer than one period, an economic loss may emerge. For example, it is well-
known that for relatively short horizons, say, up to one year, the distributions are symmetric;
hence, one can employ the M–V rule, at least as an approximation to make the optimal choice.
However, for longer horizons, the distributions are not symmetric and a positive skewness
exists. Thus, in the multi-period case, an investor who employs the M–V rule may choose
portfolio e that may be M–V efﬁcient. However, by the SSD rule portfolio e is multi-period
inefﬁcient, as we can safely conclude that for all risk averters, there is at least one portfolio,
say, portfolio e′, located on segment bc, with a higher expected utility than EU(e). Thus,
every risk averter loses by investing in a portfolio like portfolio e rather than choosing a
portfolio from segment bc. Note, however, that this potential loss is not necessary for all
investors, as it is possible for some the risk averters to choose a portfolio, say d′, that is
included in both the M–V and SSD multi-period efﬁcient set.
So far, we assumed in most of the analyses that the one-period return distribution is
normal, and we analyzed the effect of the horizon on the M–V and SSD efﬁcient sets and the
possible economic loss by employing the M–V rule corresponding to horizons longer than
one year. The same analysis intact if the one-period distribution is assumed to be logistic. The

assumption that the short horizon distributions are logistic, or at least approximately logistic,
has some empirical support (see Sect. 4). This implies that using the M–V rule, rather than the
SSD rule, does not imply a substantial economic loss for relatively short horizon investors.
However, for some assets, even the one-period return is not symmetric (e.g., the return on
options)—a case where SSD remains the optimal investment rule for all risk averters, but the
M–V rule does not. The reason for failure of the M–V rule in such cases is that, generally,
higher distribution moments, particularly skewness, also affect expected utility, which the
SSD rule considers and the M–V rule ignores.
Finally, recall that although the SD rules are theoretically superior to the M–V rule, there
is no SD algorithm for ﬁnding SD efﬁcient diversiﬁcations. In this case, to make an optimal
choice one needs to calculate the direct expected utility. This issue is discussed in Sect. 10.
8 The optimal mean–variance (M–V) portfolio and the horizon: stocks
and bonds
In the very long run even Markowitz does not recommend to employ the M–V rule (see Levy
& Markowitz, 1979) and suggests that maximizing the portfolio’s geometric mean for the
very long run, namely for N →∞, is optimal. However, for long but ﬁnite horizon,N,
e.g., N  5, 10 years, there is no obvious optimal rule. As the M–V rule is easy to employ,
investors may employ it for various horizons longer than one year. There is virtually no
research on the horizon’s effect on optimal M–V diversiﬁcation, and particularly on the
consistency of the M–V rule with expected utility maximization for relatively long but ﬁnite
investment horizons. This section ﬁlls this void by studding the optimal M–V stocks-bonds
diversiﬁcation for various long but ﬁnite horizons.
We show in this section that the optimal M–V portfolio of risky assets is not invariant to
the assumed investment horizon. Even when one assumes that returns are identical and inde-
pendent over time (the i.i.d. assumption), the optimal M–V portfolio composition changes
with the horizon, let alone when the i.i.d. assumption does not hold, and autocorrelations in
the series of returns prevail, namely, in the case where returns are dependent over time.
The reason for the strong horizon’s effect on the optimal M–V portfolio of stocks and
bonds is that even in the i.i.d. case, the asset’s mean and variance increase with the assumed
horizon in some complex manner, where the multi-period variance of each asset increases not
only as a function of the one-period variance, but also as a function of the mean return; the
larger the mean return, the faster the increase in the multi-period variance with the horizon.
Thus, stocks with a relatively large mean may be attractive by the M–V rule in the short
run, but not in the long run, as their variance also increases as a function of the relatively
large mean. When we have more than two assets (see next section) all pair-wise correlations
theoretically vary with the horizon in a peculiar way: positive correlations decrease with the
horizon, and negative correlations increase with it. Thus, focusing solely on the correlations’
effect on the M–V portfolio selection, we ﬁnd that assets with a positive correlation, other
things being held constant, become more attractive with the horizon, whereas assets with a
negative correlation become less attractive with the horizon—quite a surprising result!
As we ﬁnd that in the stocks-bonds portfolio the optimal M–V investment weight in bonds
increases with the horizon, we also calculate the composition of the optimal expected utility
portfolio of stocks and bonds for various horizons for commonly employed utility functions
(see Sect. 10). We obtain with the expected utility maximization just the opposite results; for
most utility functions and for most horizons the optimal investment weight in stocks is very

stocks becomes very positively skewed, a desired property by risk-averse expected utility

difference between the M–V results reported in Table 7 and the “stocks for the long run”
assertion.

stock portfolios
So far, we compared portfolios composed of stocks and bonds revealing that as the horizon
increases, the optimal M–V portfolio contains a larger proportion of bonds, which is counter-
intuitive and contradicts the "stocks for the long run” common view. To see whether this is a
typical characteristic of the M–V horizon’s effect is limited only to stocks-bonds portfolios,
we now examine the portfolio composition of ten size deciles of risky stocks, as provided
by French’s website.15 Decile 1 includes 10% of stocks with the smallest market value (the
riskiest decile), and Decile 10 includes the largest 10% of stocks (the safest decile). Generally,
as we shift from Decile 1 toward Decile 10, both the variance and the mean rate of return
decrease. Thus, if indeed there is a trend by the M–V rule to take less risk with the horizon,
we expect to ﬁnd an increase in the weight allocated to Decile 10, the safer stocks, with
the horizon. Table 9 reports the results corresponding to the tangency portfolio. As we can
see similar to the stocks-bonds results also with the ten stocks portfolio we obtain the same
pattern. There is a tendency to increase the allocation to large stocks with an increase in the
horizon. While this investment weight in large stocks is only about 6.5% for a one-month
horizon, it grows monotonically with the horizon, and for a horizon of 20 years or more, it
is 100%.
In sum, the M–V rule reveals (mistakenly, see next section) that the longer the horizon, the
more should be invested in the less risky asset, namely, in bonds in the stock–bond portfolio,
and in Decile 10 in the ten decile portfolios. This investment strategy is in contradiction to
the common view that one should invest more in the high risk- return assets as the horizon
increases. We will explain these contradictory results in the next section.

11 Concluding remarks
With increasing life expectancy investors need to save for many years after retirement, hence
the investment should be planned for relatively long investment horizons. In contrast, most
research in ﬁnance and most practitioners employ monthly or at most annual returns, hence
implicitly assume relatively short investment horizons. Does this horizon mismatch induce
economic loss?
The planned investment horizon affects the M–V rule, the M–V efﬁcient set, as well
as the composition of the optimal M–V portfolio of risky assets, known as the tangency
portfolio. The three factors that affect the M–V choices are the individual’s asset mean,
variance, and all pairwise correlations with other assets under consideration. As even with
the i.i.d. assumption all these parameters change dramatically with the assumed investment
horizon, they affect the M–V optimal choices. Skewness, that also changes with the assumed
investment horizon affects the optimum expected utility portfolio but not the optimum M–V
portfolio.
While the mean and variance of each asset increases with the horizon, surprisingly, the
correlations tend to zero with the horizon regardless of the one-period assumed correlation.
Even if the one-period correlation is perfect (+ 1), it decreases with the horizon and becomes
equal to zero where the horizon increases indeﬁnitely. Similarly, if the one-period correlation
is negative, it increases and tends to zero as the horizon increases indeﬁnitely. Thus, other
things being equal, assets with positive one-period correlations become more attractive with
the horizon as the correlation decreases. An opposite phenomenon occurs with one-period
negative correlations, as the advantage of the negative correlation slowly vanishes with the
horizon, as the negative correlation increases and tends to zero for a very long horizon.
The compositions of the optimal M–V portfolio (the tangency portfolio TP) and the
minimum variance portfolio (MV P) change with the horizon. In a portfolio composed from
stocks and bonds, as the horizon increases, the optimal M–V weight invested in stocks
decreases, from 41% for one-year horizon to merely 4% for 30 years planned investment
horizon; hence, stocks seemingly become less attractive than bonds with the horizon. More
generally, according to the M–V rule, assets with relatively high mean and high variance
become less attractive than relatively safer assets with the horizon. For example, "small
stocks" become less attractive than "large stocks" by the M–V rule as the horizon increases.
Similarly, by the M–V rule bonds become more attractive than stocks with the horizon. These
M–V results contradict the “stocks for the long run” assertion, as well as the investment
strategy of life cycle mutual funds, that invest more in stocks and less in bonds for long
horizon investors.
Are relatively risky assets (the S&P 500 stock index relative to bonds, or "small stocks"
relative to “large stocks”) really less attractive in the long run? Calculating the expected
utility of various commonly employed utility functions, as well as by the Prospect Theory
value functions, reveals that the attractiveness of relatively risky assets relative to that of less
risky assets generally (depending of the risk aversion parameter) increases with the horizon,
consistent with the “stocks for the long run” investment strategy, and in contradiction to the
M–V results.
Despite this horizon effect, it is worth mentioning that the optimal diversiﬁcation in stocks
and bonds, for most utility functions, does not change much with the horizon, in contrast to
the M–V portfolio compositions that change dramatically with the horizon. These ﬁndings
indicate that employing the M–V rule for the long run is economically unjustiﬁed, particularly
because the built-up positive skewness with the horizon that is ignored by the M–V rule. Thus,

employing the M–V rule for long horizons may induce an economic distortion, namely
expected utility loss.
In sum, for relatively short horizons (one year or less), the distributions of returns are sym-
metrical and close to elliptical (the logistic distribution); hence, the M–V rule can be safely
employed as it yields results that are consistent, or approximately consistent, with expected
utility results. Also, the M–V rule can be employed for short horizons as an approximation
to the expected utility even in the case where normality of the distribution does not exist.
However, for long horizons, the distributions of returns become positively skewed, and the
approximation to expected utility is not intact. Therefore, employing the M–V rule leads to
the wrong conclusions, asserting that bonds become more attractive than stocks in the long
run.
For relatively long horizon the Mean–Coefﬁcient of Variance (M–C) is optimal, and for
intermediate horizons one cannot employ either the M–V rule or the M–C rule. In this
case one should employ, either stochastic dominance (SD) rules or direct expected utility
maximization.
