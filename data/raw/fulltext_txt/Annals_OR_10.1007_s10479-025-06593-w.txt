Abstract
We prove that, for any given set of networks satisfying suitable conditions, the net-oudegree
network solution, the net-indegree network solution, and the total network solution are the
unique network solutions on that set satisfying neutrality, consistency and cancellation. The
generality of the result obtained allows to get an analogous result for social choice cor-
respondences: for any given set of preference proÔ¨Āles satisfying suitable conditions, the
net-oudegree social choice correspondence, the net-indegree social choice correspondence
and the total social choice correspondence are the unique social choice correspondences on
that set satisfying neutrality, consistency and cancellation. Using the notable fact that several
well-known voting rules coincide with the restriction of the net-outdegree social choice cor-
respondence to appropriate sets of preference proÔ¨Āles, we are able to deduce a variety of new
and known characterization theorems for the Borda rule, the Partial Borda rule, the Averaged
Borda rule, the Approval Voting, the Plurality rule and the anti-Plurality rule, among which
Young‚Äôs characterization of the Borda rule and Fishburn‚Äôs characterization of the Approval
Voting.

In order to evaluate the quality of a network solution, it is certainly important to understand
whether it satisÔ¨Āes desirable properties. Among the possible properties one can consider there
are neutrality, consistency and cancellation. A network solution F satisÔ¨Āes neutrality if the
names of the elements in A are irrelevant to the selection process; consistency if, every time
the outcomes of F at two networks have nonempty intersection, then that intersection must
be the outcome of F at the sum of the two networks; cancellation if F selects all the vertices
whenappliedtoreversalsymmetricnetworks,namelythosenetworkswhosecapacityofevery
arc (x, y) equals the capacity of the arc (y, x). Interpreting A as a set of teams, networks4 as
a complete description of the wins and losses in a competition (where ties are not allowed),
and a network solution as a procedure for selecting the winners of each possible competition,
the above described properties sound very natural. Indeed, neutrality implies that no team or
group of teams has an exogenous advantage on the others; consistency means that if there are
teams that are the winners of a competition N and also of a competition N ‚Ä≤, then those teams
are exactly the winners of the competition that considers the outcomes of all the matches
played in N and N ‚Ä≤; cancellation means that if every team won and lost the same number of
times against any other team, then all the teams are the winners.

Some characterization results for suitable restrictions of the net-outdegree network solu-
tion [method] are available in the literature. Indeed, the net-outdegree network method

Thus, Theorem A2 is qualitatively not comparable with those results.
The proof of Theorem A1 is made possible thanks to Theorem 17, which is inspired by the
proof of Young (1974) characterization of the Borda rule. Young‚Äôs arguments are strongly
grounded in linear algebra and graph theory and are widely regarded as non-elementary (see,
for instance, Hansson & Sahlquist 1976; Al√≥s-Ferrer 2006). Thus, the need to Ô¨Ānd a simpler
approach to prove Young‚Äôs theorem soon emerged. Hansson and Sahlquist (1976) simplify
Young‚Äôs arguments by adopting a combinatorial approach and avoiding linear algebra and
graph theory. Their approach, which works well under the assumption that individual pref-
erences are expressed as linear orders, seems difÔ¨Ācult to extend in order to prove analogous
results for extensions of the Borda rule to broader domains.7 Other authors provide char-
acterization theorems for Borda-type rules deÔ¨Āned on various domains that involve axioms
inspired by the ones by Young, such as consistency, and that are still proved by means of
combinatorial arguments (see, Cullinan et al., 2014; Terzopoulou & Endriss, 2021). In con-
trast to those authors, in this paper we build upon Young‚Äôs approach and extensively exploit
linear algebra, graph theory and network theory to generalize his Ô¨Āndings to the framework
of network solutions.8 This research approach proved fruitful, since it not only led to Theo-
rems A1 and A2 but also allowed us to determine, as described below, a common root to a
variety of characterization theorems in social choice theory among which, of course, Young‚Äôs
theorem. As a consequence, Theorem A1 can be naturally interpreted as a generalization to
networks of Young‚Äôs characterization of the Borda rule.

stated by Young (1974) and whose proof, at the best of our knowledge, never appeared in the
literature. Our results reveal then the deep structural connection among all the aforementioned
rules and clarify why, despite being deÔ¨Āned over distinct domains, they can be basically
characterized by the same axioms, with possible minor adjustments depending on the domain
under consideration.
We emphasize that Theorem B2 provides, in particular, characterization results for exten-
sions of the Borda rule [Approval voting] over appropriate subsets D of R(A)‚ąówhich include
all the linear order proÔ¨Āles [dichotomous order proÔ¨Āles], such as the set of order proÔ¨Āles and
the whole set R(A)‚ąó. Thus, this result contributes to the remarkable line of research aimed
at characterizing extensions of scoring rules over very general domains using consistency
(and additional axioms). This direction has been extensively explored in several works. The
consideration of kinds of domain restrictions that permit a voting rule to fulÔ¨Āll a weak version
of consistency are at the core of Saari (1990). Myerson (1995) utilizes consistency, along
with other axioms, to characterize scoring rules deÔ¨Āned in a broader framework where voters,
instead of expressing a preference relation, specify a signal. These signals are elements of a
given set that represents the set of all the possible opinions that voters are allowed to express.
Pivato (2013) extends Myerson results providing characterization theorems for scoring rules
deÔ¨Āned on special domains of signal proÔ¨Āles called cones. Ceron and Gonzalez (2021) char-
acterize instead the approval voting in a framework comparable to the one proposed by
Myerson (1995). More generally, our research Ô¨Āts into the ambitious project of providing a
normative viewpoint on which domains are most appropriate for each voting rule. This would
guide decision-makers in choosing whether to ask voters for linear, partial, or dichotomous
rankings of the alternatives or other kinds of signals and, crucially, in understanding the
normative reasons behind these choices.
