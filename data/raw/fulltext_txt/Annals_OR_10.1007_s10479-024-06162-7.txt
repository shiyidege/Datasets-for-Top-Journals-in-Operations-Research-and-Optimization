Abstract
Multicriteria aggregation methods typically require inputs from decision makers concerning
the relative importance of the criteria. This work presents an approach to use qualitative infor-
mation elicited from a panel, which can be applied to compensatory and non-compensatory
multicriteria aggregation methods. In particular, it considers the additive multiattribute value
function and ELECTRE, two classical methods with well-known differences in the mean-
ing of the criteria weights. Moreover, the proposed protocol makes a distinction between the
importance of improving the current situation and the importance of not worsening the current
situation. The inputs from the panel are aggregated to deﬁne constraints on the importance-
related parameters, which can then be used for robustness and stochastic analyses. As a
real-world application, a comparison of Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is performed,
considering the case of carrots cultivation in a French region. The comparisons are based
on a sustainability assessment of the current practices and alternative IPM systems, using
data from ﬁeld trials, laboratory experiments and preferences from stakeholders. Results are
robust to weighting choices, thus identifying which changes are recommended.

1 Introduction
One of the key choices when applying decision aiding methods, namely Multi-Criteria Deci-
sion Aiding (MCDA) (for expositions on this topic see, e.g., Belton & Stewart, 2002; Greco
et al., 2016; Ishizaka & Nemery, 2013)) is involving stakeholders to deﬁne how important
each criterion will be.
Criteria importance parameters can be elicited in different ways from a Decision Maker
(DM), expert, or stakeholder (Cinelli et al., 2020). Here we mention weights as the most
common parameters, but the following possibilities can apply to other parameters such as veto
thresholds or the shape of a value function. The simplest way is to directly ask for the weight
of each criterion, either asking for instance to provide a number from 0 to 100, or asking to
allocate a total of 100 points among the different criteria (Bottomley & Doyle, 2013). Another
type of quantitative elicitation occurs for methods such as the swings weighting or tradeoff
weighting (Morton, 2018), in which the quantities elicited are processed to compute weights.
Other types of quantitative elicitation are based on one of the previous types, but instead of
numbers the respondents can provide interval, fuzzy, imprecise or ordinal information about
the weights (e.g., Barron & Barrett, 1996; de Almeida et al., 2016; Dias & Clímaco, 2000;
Sarabando & Dias, 2009; Tzeng & Huang, 2011).
Since respondents may feel it is cognitively easier to provide qualitative information,
the seminal Analytic Hierarchy Process method (Saaty, 2008) and other methods based on
qualitative scales have been proposed (e.g., Bana e Costa et al., 2012; Rezaei, 2015), namely
to ask respondents to compare criteria pair by pair, and then deriving a vector of weights
maximizing a ﬁtness criterion for this information. Another type of qualitative elicitation,
usually named indirect elicitation (Cinelli et al., 2020) or preference disaggregation approach
(Matsatsinis et al., 2018), consists in asking for examples of the results the aggregation should
provide for some exemplary, possibly ﬁctitious, alternatives (Jacquet-Lagrèze & Siskos,
2001). Even simpler for the respondent, in cognitive terms, is to rate each criterion on a
qualitative importance scale, in a precise way choosing one level (e.g., Dias et al., 2018), or
in an imprecise way (e.g., Liao et al., 2014).
The types of elicitation indicated above are available to most MCDA aggregation methods,
including the additive multiattribute value function and ELECTRE used in this work (see, e.g.,
Dias & Mousseau, 2018; Matsatsinis et al., 2018; Morton, 2018). All elicitation approaches
have advantages and disadvantages, since requiring more precision comes at the cost of
time and cognitive load for the respondents (Schuwirth et al., 2012). Approaches that do
not elicit exact quantitative information either ﬁnd a weights vector, which is admittedly an
approximation, using some formula or optimization (Barron & Barrett, 1996; Figueira &
Roy, 2002; Sarabando & Dias, 2009; Siskos et al., 2016) or accept a subset of values for
the weights. In the latter case, results can be computed in the form of robust conclusions or
stochastic results (Dias, Passeira et al., 2022a, 2022b, 2022c; Kadzi´nski & Tervonen, 2013;
Kadzi´nski et al., 2015).
When seeking to obtain the perspective of multiple stakeholders, the elicitation process
can be conducted as a group or individually for different parameters (Belton & Pictet, 1997).
A decision conference (Phillips & Bana e Costa, 2007) is a well-known setting for a group of
stakeholders to meet, during over one or two days, to conjointly decide the criteria weights
(among other tasks). This is time-consuming, but ensures tight interaction among the par-
ticipants. Another option is to have stakeholders answer a survey providing their individual
perspectives, and then use group aggregation methods to synthetize their views, either com-
puting a consensus vector (e.g., Contreras & Mármol, 2007), or a subset of widely accepted

values for the weights (e.g., Dias, Cunha, et al., 2022a, 2022b, 2022c). This is the least time-
consuming option for the respondents, and the only one available when no interaction is
sought. As an intermediate situation, some interactive methods have been proposed to elicit
weights from a group that meets (possibly virtually) in successive rounds until the sought
degree of agreement is reached (e.g., Damart et al., 2007; Dias et al., 2018; Freitas et al.,

match the different meaning of the weights in different MCDA aggregation methods (Cinelli
et al., 2020).
The present work, grounded on a real-world application, contributes to the literature by
proposing an elicitation protocol to elicit the importance of criteria from a panel. Elicitation
processes should be tailored to the distinguishing features of each application (Marttunen
et al., 2015). The proposed protocol is aimed at requiring as little effort as possible from the
stakeholders, so that it can be used even in online questionnaires, where fatigue of respondents
is often a barrier (Aubert et al., 2020). Moreover, the protocol aims at eliciting information that
can be used in methods based on tradeoffs, namely the additive multiattribute value function
(Keeney, 1992; Keeney & Raiffa, 1993), as well as outranking methods such as ELECTRE
(Figueira et al., 2013; Roy, 1991). These methods rest on different axiomatic foundations
(Bouyssou et al., 2015). Nevertheless, value functions and ELECTRE were applied jointly
in several studies to observe how their results differ (e.g., Kangas et al., 2001; Lahdelma &
Salminen, 2002; Salminen et al., 1998), as well as to understand how DMs perceive them in
terms of comfort and other characteristics (e.g., Bell et al., 2001), or to compare their ability
to reproduce holistic classiﬁcations of alternatives provided by the DMs (Sobrie et al., 2019).
We tackle the issue that the criteria weights have different meanings in these methods
(Roy & Mousseau, 1996), and therefore different elicitation questions would be needed for
the same purpose, but we wish to avoid asking an excessive number of questions to the
panel members. We also propose a way to derive from the panel members constraints on
the ranking of the importance parameters from a simple rating on a qualitative scale. As a
further contribution, we present an application of this protocol to a real-world problem of
deciding how to improve pest management systems in agriculture, combining the different
perspectives of value functions and ELECTRE and seeking to obtain robust conclusions using
a stochastic multi-criteria acceptability analysis (SMAA) (Lahdelma et al., 1998; Tervonen
& Lahdelma, 2007) type of approach. As a case study, this work considers ﬁeld trials in a
farm with carrots cultivation located in Aquitaine, France, where different integrated pest
management (IPM) systems were compared in the context of the OPTIMA (Optimised Pest
Integrated Management to precisely detect and control plant diseases in perennial crops and
open-ﬁeld vegetables) European project. Data on the pesticides application in these ﬁeld
trials, as well as laboratorial experiments and databases were used to assess environmental
life-cycle impacts, ecological risks, social impacts (human health risks for the farmers) and
economic impacts (operational costs for farmers).
The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Section 2 brieﬂy reviews the MCDA
aggregation methods used in this work, as well as previous use of MCDA in related contexts.
Section 3 presents the methodology, addressing the elicitation questions and how to aggregate
the answers from the panel. Section 4 presents the application. Finally, Sect. 5 presents the
conclusions.

2 Background
2.1 On MCDA aggregation
Many MCDA aggregation methods have been proposed in the literature (e.g., see Cinelli et al.,
2020, 2022; Greco et al., 2016). One of the main distinctions made between these methods
is to separate those that rely on computing a single score for each alternative and those that
build a binary relation between the set of alternatives, without computing a synthetic score.
The former methods include most of the so-called full compensation methods, where a poor
performance on some criterion can always be compensated by very good performance on
the remaining criteria. The additive multiattribute value function (Keeney, 1992; Keeney &
Raiffa, 1993) is one of the seminal and most widely known examples, which has been applied
in many ﬁelds, including sustainability assessment (Dias et al., 2015). The latter methods,
often called outranking methods, typically do not allow compensation of poor performances.
Among other, ELECTRE methods (Figueira et al., 2013; Roy, 1991) are some of the seminal
and most widely known ones, also including sustainability assessment (Dias, 2021). These
two contrasting approaches are brieﬂy outlined here.
As common notation, let a1, . . . , am denote a set of m alternatives and let g1(.),…, gn(.)
denote a set of n criteria. The performance of alternative ai on criterion g j is denoted g j(ai).
Without loss of generality, all criteria are considered to be minimized (the less the better, as
the application deals with negative impacts).
To apply the multiattribute value function, one needs to initially deﬁne a decreasing value
function v j(.) for each criterion. This function deﬁnes how performance levels are valued, not
necessarily in a linear way. The greater the value v j(g j(ai)), the better is the performance of
alternative ai on criterion g j. The value functions on the multiple criteria are then aggregated
into a score indicating the global value of the alternative. The most popular aggregation
model is the additive value function, which assumes certain independence conditions among
the criteria (Keeney & Raiffa, 1993; Morton, 2018):

2.2 On applying MCDA to IPM
The natural environment has been one of the main topics for decision-making supported by
MCDA (Huang et al., 2011; Linkov et al., 2020), including agriculture as one of the most
relevant sectors. In reviewing over 200 articles on MCDA applications for sustainability
assessment, Lindfors (2021) identiﬁed the ﬁeld of ‘Food and feed systems’ as the third
largest group. A recent review on the use of MCDA assessing agricultural sustainability
(Cicciù et al., 2022) covered 41 articles. Another recent review on agricultural sustainability
(Soulé et al., 2021) found almost 90 studies using some kind of MCDA, although most of
them were simple sums of scores.
Only a few studies have applied MCDA in agricultural studies addressing pesticide man-
agement, either alone, or in the broader scope of comparing cropping systems (the latter
encompassing other variables such as tillage and rotation of crops). In the review by Soulé
et al. (2021), MCDA applications (excluding sum of scores) included pesticide management
aspects only in eight studies, using multiattribute value/utility theory (MAVT/ MAUT) (ﬁve
cases), the analytic hierarchy process (AHP), data envelopment analysis, and a version of the
DEX expert system (one case each). We are not aware of previous applications of MCDA

an additive aggregation, the authors ranked the criteria and used SMARTER (Edwards &
Barron, 1994), i.e., the Rank Order Centroid, as an approximation for the weighting vector.
Rodrigues et al. (2010) developed a decision support system, APOIA-NovoRural, which
was applied in several studies in Brazil and Uruguay assessing different types of farms. The
system allows building nonlinear utility (value) functions, setting the level corresponding to
value 0.7 based on benchmarks deﬁned by experts, and assuming equal weights to perform
an additive aggregation. More recently, Trivino-Tarradas et al. (2019) proposed the INSPIA
composite index, which was applied in several European countries for different crops. The
system is based on a min–max normalization of the impacts on each criterion, with minimum
and maximum thresholds set based on existing knowledge. The weights were obtained from a
panel, averaging the points allocated by each panel member. None of these applications fully
applied the MAVT methodology, in some cases because weights were not elicited based
on tradeoffs or swings, in other cases because normalization-based linear functions were
assumed.
Considering again all types of crops, but now focusing on outranking methods, the main
approach has been the use of ELECTRE TRI and its variants. Girardin et al. (2000) used this
method in a French region to sort combinations of agricultural techniques into four categories,
with parameters set by consensus of four experts. Macary et al. (2013) used ELECTRE TRI-
C to sort farming parcels into ﬁve risk categories concerning pesticide contamination, with
parameter values set by consensus of experts. More recently Aouadi et al. (2020) assessed
viticulture systems in France, including foreseen changes, again using ELECTRE TRI-C
(deﬁning four performance categories) and then ranking systems assigned to the same cat-
egory using ELECTRE III. Weights were set through a compromise reached by a group of
stakeholders.
From a practice perspective, besides the speciﬁc crop IPM studied, the main novelties of
the present work with regards to this background are the elicitation of preferences from a
relatively large group of stakeholders, the combined use of MAVT and ELECTRE, and the
use of a stochastic approach to assess the robustness of the conclusions. In addition, this
article includes a sensitivity analysis addressing a possibly critical assumption concerning
costs.

3 Methodology
3.1 Elicitation
In terms of MCDA methodology, this work tackles two challenges in eliciting qualitative
information from a panel regarding the importance of criteria, intending to use it for multiat-
tribute value functions and ELECTRE: addressing the different meaning of the weights and
aggregating qualitative levels provided by multiple respondents.
3.1.1 Addressing the meaning of the weights
The ﬁrst challenge is to elicit information that is suitable for both types of methods. As
indicated in Sect. 2.1, weights have very different meanings in both methods. For ELECTRE,
the weights are importance coefﬁcients reﬂecting the intuitive importance attached to the
criteria when assessing whether a concordance coalition reaches the required majority level.
The veto threshold can also reﬂect a facet of criteria importance: a smaller veto threshold will
lead a criterion to pose a stronger opposition to outranking relations it does not agree with.
On the other hand, in the additive model only weights are involved, but they do not reﬂect an
intuitive notion of importance, and thinking the contrary has been deemed by Keeney (1992)
the most common critical mistake in using this method. Indeed, these weights strongly depend
on the deﬁnition of the value functions (Bana e Costa et al., 2008).
A solution to this challenge would be to ask separate questions for the two methods. This
has two potential drawbacks. One is that DMs or other stakeholders will need to respond
to many questions, demanding more of their time. This is particularly worrisome when the
elicitation process requires interviews or surveys to actors (experts or other stakeholders)
who may not be extremely motivated to participate. The other drawback is that the process of
answering a set of questions pertaining to one method might bias the responses to a second set
of questions (e.g., after concluding that a criterion has more weight for one MCDA method,
the tendency to do the same for another method might be hard to counter).
Another solution is to ask questions in such a way that the answers provide useful infor-
mation for both methods. The proposed solution builds on the mild assumptions that a current
situation is already in place and that respondents are familiar with this reality. Then, one can
ask the respondents to rank or to rate the importance of the following changes:
• To improve by x% the impacts of the current situation on criterion 1;
• To avoid worsening by x% the impacts of the current situation on criterion 1;
• To improve by x% the impacts of the current situation on criterion 2;
• …
• To avoid worsening by x% the impacts of the current situation on criterion n.
The number of statements is twice the number of criteria, hence rating these statements
along a Likert-like scale is less demanding than ranking these. An example (taken from the
application described in Sect. 4) to rate 20% changes in four criteria is given in Fig. 1.

3.1.2 Aggregating qualitative levels
The process described above can be used when preferences are elicited from a panel (multiple
individuals), by specifying how the qualitative ratings translate into rankings. One possibility
is to consider an average rating, but this would imply assigning cardinals to these four levels
(e.g., 1, 2, 3, and 4) somewhat arbitrarily. Indeed, treating Likert scales as if these were interval
scales is considered to be debatable at best (Jamieson, 2004; Wu & Leung, 2017). To avoid
this issue, Dias et al. (2018) proposed a method to rank weights based on qualitative ratings
based on pairwise comparisons that are robust to any transformation of the qualitative levels
into an interval scale. However, the comparisons will typically result in a partial ranking,
rather than a complete one.
In this study a simpler approach is proposed, consisting in using the Majority Judgment
method proposed for elections (Balinski & Laraki, 2007). This method considers item j is
more important than item k if a majority (i.e., more than half) of the panel rates the importance
of j at level L or better, and the same does not hold for item k. More speciﬁcally, suppose the
items are rated by s stakeholders, and for each item j sort the received ratings from the worst
to the best one and take the median rating, i.e., the ⌊s/2⌋th lowest rating (rounded to the left
if s is even). Then, the method considers j is more important than k if the median rating of
the former is higher than the median rating of the latter. In case the medians are the same,
one successively removes the median element (decreasing the number of ratings by 1) and
repeats the process until the recomputed medians differ. This method produces a complete
ranking, and an ex-aequo situation occurs only if the sorted ratings obtained by two items
are exactly the same.
3.2 Aggregation with stochastic analysis
To obtain results, the additive value function model and ELECTRE are applied considering
a stochastic approach following a Stochastic Multicriteria Acceptability Analysis (SMAA)
strategy (Dias, Passeira et al., 2022a, 2022b, 2022c; Lahdelma, 1998; Tervonen & Lahdelma,
2007). Per this strategy, the parameter space is sampled using Monte-Carlo simulation (10 000
iterations), drawing from uniform distributions respecting the ranking of the parameters
(Butler et al., 1997), and statistics are computed for the results of interest. The analysis was
repeated considering uniform distributions disregarding the ranking of the parameters, in
order to assess to which extent do conclusions depend on the information provided by the
panel.

different ELECTRE methods exploit this relation for choice, ranking, or sorting purposes.
Given the presence of intransitivity, providing a recommendation can be hard, if the decision
problem is framed as “Which is the best alternative in a set?”. However, in this work we
consider a different question: “Which is, if any, the best alternative to the current situation?”.
The reason is that, as described in the next section, the current situation is the real state of the
world, and we wish to ﬁnd out how to improve it. We are therefore looking for an alternative
for which there are good reasons for, and no stringent reasons against, being considered an
improvement of the current situation. And if more than one alternative is an improvement,
we wish to know which one we are more convinced that it is better than the current situation.
Thus, if one is interested only in assessing how each alternative a1, …, am compares to the
current situation a0, then the lack of transitivity in ELECTRE is no longer a barrier. As a
note, one can even reiterate this reasoning of successively replacing the incumbent alternative
in a Markovian spirit (Dias & Rocha, 2023). For the value function model, the distinction
between those two questions (being the best vs. being the best replacement) usually does not
matter, as the value of each alternative is obtained independently of any other alternatives.
For each potential conclusion, e.g., concluding that an alternative is preferred per the value
function model or per the ELECTRE model, the stochastic analysis indicates the proportion
of cases supporting it. If all the cases or a very large proportion support it, DMs can accept
it as being robust to an exact speciﬁcation of the weights. On the contrary, if the proportion
of instances supporting a conclusion is insufﬁcient for the DMs, one will need to elicit more
information besides a ranking of the criteria, such as holistic pair-wise preference statements
(e.g., Kadzi´nski & Tervonen, 2013).
A sensitivity analysis can also be performed on some criterion of interest to assess critical
performance values to achieve a given level of support concerning a conclusion, e.g., ﬁnding
the worst performance level on a criterion for an alternative to be preferred to another one in,
say, 95% of the simulated instances. This can be achieved by trial and error through a simple
bisection search algorithm: given a low performance level L for which the 95% target is not
met and a high performance level H for which the target is met, one tests whether the target
is met for the midpoint (L + U)/2, then updates L or U depending on the result of the test,
and reiterates the process.

5 Conclusions
This work presented an approach to elicit qualitative ratings from a panel (potentially a large
one) asking questions in such a way thatthe answers provide information thatis usefulfor both
tradeoff-based methods (MAVT) and importance coefﬁcient -based methods (outranking).
This is a methodological contribution to the practice of MCDA, particularly when surveys are
used and one plans to use both types of methods, or when elicitation occurs before knowing
which type of method will be applied.
Another methodological contribution is the proposal to use the Majority Judgment method
to obtain a ranking of the parameters based on qualitative ratings from a panel. A qualitative
rating is not hard to provide, especially when the number of rating levels is small, as was the
case in this work. In the case of a single DM, such a rating might not be so useful, as several
criteria can be given the same rating level. However, in the case of the panel’s “wisdom of
the crowd”, a ranking could easily be obtained following this approach.
From the application stakeholders (farmers and technicians) viewpoint, the alternative that
applied both synthetic and bioPPPs was found to be worse than the current situation without
bioPPPs. This surprising result is due to disadvantages on several indicators, and particularly
in terms of risk for honeybees caused by copper sulphate in one of the bioPPPs. The alterna-
tive that applied the synthetic PPPs with a smart sprayer was always better than the current
situation according to MAVT, and also preferred according to ELECTRE, even if for some
simulated parameters they were considered to be indifferent. The alternative that combined
both innovations, i.e., bioPPPs and smart sprayer, presented mixed results often being con-
sidered worse in both methods, but being mainly considered incomparable by ELECTRE,
for it has important advantages (risk for farmers and soil organisms) mixed with important
disadvantages (risk for honeybees). The main conclusion is therefore to support the introduc-
tion of smart sprayers, and this conclusion is quite robust with regards to criteria weighting
and choice of MCDA aggregation method. The assumption that using a smart sprayer would
not increase costs was also put into question through a sensitivity analysis, which shows that
the only conclusion that might be revised would be the one corresponding to the value func-
tion model when considering preferences from respondents in France. Conclusions when all
responses are considered or when the ELECTRE model is applied are quite robust, even if
the smart sprayer costs were much higher than those of a traditional sprayer.
A limitation of this work is that it involved a speciﬁc set of participants and it concerns a
speciﬁc application. Further applications should be conducted to better appraise and contin-
ually improve these proposals. A potentially interesting evolution would be to use the rating
levels to obtain information that is richer than a ranking, providing some indication of the
differences between the ranked objects.
