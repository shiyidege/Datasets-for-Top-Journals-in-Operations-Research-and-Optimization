Abstract
In this paper, we employ stochastic data envelopment analysis (SDEA) to render manage-
rial ability that is comprised of seven inputs and one output proposed by Demerjian et al.
(Manag Sci, 58(7):1229-1248, 2012). A random effects panel data analysis (PDA) model is
used to explore the relationship between managerial ability and environmental, social, and
governance (ESG) performance based on longitudinal data. Our results show that there is
a signiÔ¨Åcant positive link between managerial ability and ESG performance in the energy
industry, and there are signiÔ¨Åcant positive associations between managerial ability and all
three aspects of ESG. Our Ô¨Åndings further suggest that the higher the managerial ability
derived from the managerial efÔ¨Åciency theory proposed by Demerjian et al. (Manag Sci,
58(7):1229-1248, 2012) of a Ô¨Årm‚Äôs executives, the better the ESG performance of the Ô¨Årm,
which underlines managerial ability as a major enabler of the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs ESG capabilities and
performance.

industry ¬∑ Stochastic data envelopment analysis (SDEA)

2 Literature review and hypotheses
2.1 Managerial ability measurement
Managerial ability refers to a diverse range of skills, traits, and competencies that enable man-
agers to effectively achieve a Ô¨Årm‚Äôs objectives through strong leadership and vision, effective
communication and decision-making, and adaptability and change management. Manager-
speciÔ¨Åc features such as ability, talent, reputation, or style, affect economic outcomes of a Ô¨Årm
(Demerjian et al., 2012). Developing and nurturing managerial ability is essential for Ô¨Årms to
thrive in today‚Äôs competitive and rapidly changing environment. Effective managerial ability
plays a vital role in ensuring that Ô¨Årms address and manage challenges in a responsible and
ethical manner. In this study, we adopted variables measuring the managerial ability proposed
by Demerjian et al. (2012), including the output variable (i.e., annual revenue) and the input
variables (i.e., net property, plant, and equipment (PP&E); net operating leases; net research
and development (R&D); purchased goodwill; other intangible assets; cost of inventory; and
selling, general, and administrative expenses (SG&A).
Past research used a variety of proxies to measure managerial ability. Traditional mea-
surements such as Ô¨Årm size, compensation, tenure, and education, were included to infer
managerial ability. Fee and Hadlock (2003) examined stock return to infer managerial ability
and found that executives who are hired as chief executive ofÔ¨Åcers (CEOs) by Ô¨Årms that
exhibit above-average stock price performance. Milbourn (2003) used CEO tenure, prior
media mentions, appointment from outside of the Ô¨Årm, and prior industry-adjusted stock
returns as proxies for CEO reputation and showed that there exists a positive and econom-
ically meaningful relationship between stock-based pay sensitivities and CEO reputation.
Tervio (2008) used CEO pay levels to infer managerial talent and analyzed Ô¨Årms‚Äô market
values. Similarly, Carter et al. (2010) used three proxies to capture the executive‚Äôs talent,
including tenure, and compensation, among others, to examine the effect of executive pay

2.2 Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) performance
The concept of environmental, social, and governance (ESG), a framework of the Associ-
ation for Supporting the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), was introduced by the
United Nations. The objectives of ESG are concerned with helping and improving compa-
nies and business communities achieve sustainable development with both environmental
and social responsibilities. The environmental aspects address a Ô¨Årm‚Äôs required resources,
consumed energy, waste management, and the impacts of the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs activities and invest-
ments on the environment. The social factor addresses the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs impact on society via its
internal and external relationships with its stakeholders and its ability to achieve coordination
and balance with stakeholders. The corporate governance factor addresses the structures and
mechanisms that guide a Ô¨Årm‚Äôs decision-making, accountability, ethics, and sustainability.
Environmental performance refers to a company‚Äôs efforts to minimize its ecological foot-
print and manage environmental risks through sustainable resource management and efÔ¨Åcient
operations. Social performance evaluates a company‚Äôs impact on its employees, customers,
local communities, and other stakeholders. Governance performance pertains to the qual-
ity of a company‚Äôs internal controls, leadership, transparency, and ethical decision-making
practices (Tsang et al., 2023).
There is abundant literature studying ESG in Ô¨Årms, with most of them arguing that bet-
ter ESG decisions and practices have positive impacts on Ô¨Årm value and/or performance.
Generally, Ô¨Årms that have incorporated environmental, social, and governance elements into
management practices can create and maintain more value for their stakeholders, through
delivering better products and services, winning, and retaining higher quality employees,
boosting the company‚Äôs reputation and image, enhancing customer loyalty, gaining social
legitimacy, and improving risk management (Cheng et al., 2023).
Charumathi and Ramesh (2017) investigated Indian companies and reported that envi-
ronmental disclosure positively and signiÔ¨Åcantly inÔ¨Çuences Ô¨Årm value or market valuation.

Alipour et al. (2019) analyzed the link between environmental disclosure quality and Ô¨Årm
performance via static and dynamic panel data analysis on Iranian Ô¨Årms. The result showed

Ô¨Årm performance. Hao and Kang (2019) documented that corporate environmental responsi-

Social responsibility expects a Ô¨Årm to consider the interests of society, including those
of the government, employees, shareholders, and environmental protection. Michelon et al.
(2013) examined the link between strategic corporate social responsibility and Ô¨Årm per-

need to connect their corporate social responsibility initiatives to the likely preferences of

and proÔ¨Åtability, including both one- and two-year lag analyses. Plumlee et al. (2015) and

Ô¨Ånancial performance and value.
A corporate governance structure is an important mechanism for sustainable corporate
development, as it inÔ¨Çuences the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs strategic management, promotes economic growth,
and determines the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs performance (Cheng et al., 2023). Recent studies documented the
importanceofcorporategovernanceanditspositiveimpactonÔ¨Årmperformanceintheareasof
shareholding structure, board size, chairmen and managing directors, director shareholding
ratio, independent director ratio, institutional investor ratio, executive shareholding ratio,
and executive compensation factors (Al-Maskati et al., 2015; Ararat et al., 2017; Black et al.,
2015; Schmidt & Fahlenbrach, 2017).
Though most literature found that the role of ESG is a positive one (Cheng et al., 2014;
Fatemi et al., 2018; Giannarakis et al., 2016; Li et al., 2018), some studies provided evi-
dence of a negative or uncorrelated relationship between corporate ESG performance and
Ô¨Årm value. Sekhon and Kathuria (2019) reported that the impact of corporate social respon-
sibility on Ô¨Ånancial performance may be neutral or negative. Atan et al. (2018) evaluated the
performance of ESG factors with respect to Ô¨Årm value for Malaysian companies and discov-
ered no signiÔ¨Åcant relationship between them. Ruan and Liu (2021) reported an unfavorable
association between ESG activities and Ô¨Årm Ô¨Ånancial performance. These studies argue that
spending on environmental and social responsibility factors with strong externalities does not
provide monetary beneÔ¨Åts to a Ô¨Årm but rather causes creditors to believe that managers are
overinvesting in social responsibility to bolster their personal reputation and shift resources
away from core technology development and product innovation (Barnea & Rubin, 2010; Di
& Thornton, 2020; Lee et al., 2009).
Despite these mixed Ô¨Åndings, it is crucial to understand the factors that inÔ¨Çuence ESG
performance because they offer insights into strategic opportunities and risk mitigation.
Factors such as industry characteristics, regulatory pressures, consumer expectations, and
resource allocation shape ESG strategies and their subsequent impacts (Kotsantonis et al.,
2016). Furthermore, even when Ô¨Ånancial beneÔ¨Åts are uncertain, ESG engagement can build
intangible assets such as brand equity, employee loyalty, and consumer trust as key drivers of
long-term success. Therefore, identifying these inÔ¨Çuencing factors allows Ô¨Årms to navigate
challenges and align strategies with stakeholder priorities, positioning them for resilience
and sustainability in a rapidly evolving market landscape (Chatterji et al., 2016).

2.3 Data envelopment analysis (DEA)
Pioneered by Charnes et al. (1978), DEA was developed as a non-parametric approach for
evaluating the efÔ¨Åciency of a set of entities, namely, Decision Making Units (DMUs), whose

DEA has attracted much attention in business research. DEA techniques have been applied
in a variety of business domains, including banking (Charnes et al., 1978; Paradi & Zhu,
2013; Puri et al., 2017), supply chain management (Izadikhah et al., 2016; Soheilirad et al.,
2018), energy industry (Hawdon, 2003; Kashani, 2005; Zhou et al., 2008), healthcare (Beata
et al., 2021; Harrison & Sexton, 2006; Kim & Hyun, 2019; Langabeer & Ozcan, 2009),
and managerial efÔ¨Åciency (Avkiran & Rowlands, 2008; Demerjian et al., 2012; Sav, 2012;
Untong et al., 2011). The original deterministic DEA (Charnes-Cooper-Rhodes model) can
be formulated in a dual form (Charnes et al., 1978; Soheilirad et al., 2018) as below:

2.4 Stochastic data envelopment analysis (SDEA)
According to Olesen and Petersen (2016, p.2), ‚ÄúDEA was originally developed within the
Management Science (MS) framework, but without any axiomatic consideration concerning
distributional characteristics of the deviation of inefÔ¨Åcient DMUs from the best practice
frontier and without any speciÔ¨Åcation of noise, i.e., without consideration for measurement
errors, sample noise and speciÔ¨Åcation errors.‚Äù As such, they argued that ‚Äúthe classical DEA
models can be interpreted as providing a deterministic frontier and are for that reason often
denoted deterministic‚Äù Olesen and Petersen (2016, p.3).
Several recent DEA studies suggested that Stochastic DEA (SDEA) needs to be used in
the presence of stochastic data (e.g., Izadikhah & Farzipoor, 2023; Izadikhah et al., 2022; Kao
& Liu, 2022). There are two basic SDEA approaches developed to date, the semi-parametric
stochastic frontier analysis (SFA) pioneered by Banker (1989) and chance constrained DEA
(CCDEA) pioneered by Cooper et al. (1996). While the semi-parametric SFA method is
generally perceived as limited (Olesen & Petersen, 2016), CCDEA is widely used in dealing
with longitudinal data with multiple inputs and outputs. As a result, we employ CCDEA
in the current study to derive the construct data‚Äîmanagerial efÔ¨Åciency. The mathematical
formula of CCDEA will be presented in the methodology section.

2.5 Hypothesis
The relationship between managerial ability and ESG factors is becoming increasingly inter-
twined. Robust managerial ability is central to integrating ESG into Ô¨Årms‚Äô decision-making
processes (Doukas & Zhang, 2021). Managers should understand the importance of sus-
tainability and social responsibility in generating and increasing long-term value for Ô¨Årms,
aligning Ô¨Årm‚Äôs goals with the interests of stakeholders (Welch & Yoon., 2022). Vigorous
ESG governance structures should be developed and implemented to consistently monitor
ESG performance, fostering a culture of transparent, accountable, sustainable, and ethical
practices.
As most studies tend to focus on ESG‚Äôs impact on Ô¨Årms, the link between managerial
ability and ESG performance is largely overlooked. Hence, we examine managerial ability
derived from the managerial efÔ¨Åciency theory developed by Demerjian et al. (2012) and its
impact on ESG performance from a multi-dimensional perspective. We employ a stochastic
data envelopment analysis (SDEA) to render managerial efÔ¨Åciency based on longitudinal
data that is inherently stochastic (Izadikhah et al., 2022). We Ô¨Årst use chance constraints
based SDEA (c.f. Kao & Liu, 2022; Land et al., 1993) to measure managerial efÔ¨Åciency.
Then, we construct a random effects panel data analysis (PDA) model to further investigate
the relationship between managerial ability and ESG performance based on the longitudinal
data. Drawing on the analysis above, we hypothesize:
Hypothesis 1 There is a positive signiÔ¨Åcant association between managerial ability and ESG
performance.
Hypothesis 1aThere is a positive signiÔ¨Åcant association between managerial ability and
environmental performance.
Hypothesis 1bThere is a positive signiÔ¨Åcant association between managerial ability and
social performance.
Hypothesis 1cThere is a positive signiÔ¨Åcant association between managerial ability and
governance performance.

4.3 Theoretical and managerial implications
There are several theoretical implications worth to note. As far as we know, it is the Ô¨Årst
research linking and revealing the connection between managerial ability and ESG per-
formance in the energy industry in the U.S. Secondly, we used Stochastic DEA to render
managerial ability rather than the deterministic DEA used by Demerjian et al. (2012), as the
dataset is stochastic by nature (i.e., longitudinal data). Thirdly, the Ô¨Åndings of the current
study substantiate the positive relationship between managerial ability and ESG activities
and performance found in the recent empirical ESG studies.

The current study also delivers important managerial implications. First, it provides energy
Ô¨Årms with a more viable tool to assess the managerial abilities of their top managers due
to the more objective nature of the stochastic DEA-based assessment. Second, the study
demonstrates that high managerial ability is a major driver of better CSR performance, which
is mandated by government bodies and society, compelling these public energy Ô¨Årms to care
about their CSR performance instead of only focusing on short-term Ô¨Ånancial outcomes.
Lastly, our Ô¨Åndings suggest that energy Ô¨Årms in the U.S. must hire top executives with
excellent managerial abilities or enhance their managerial abilities to improve Ô¨Årm ESG
performance. Indeed, a recent study by Lee et al. (2023) echoed our Ô¨Åndings that there is
a signiÔ¨Åcant positive link between managerial ability and voluntary disclosure of carbon
emissions, a major KPI for environmental performance.
5 Conclusion, limitations, and future research
In this research, we utilized a two-step approach for examining the relationship between
managerial ability and Ô¨Årm ESG performance in the energy industry in the U.S. In the Ô¨Årst
step, we employed a stochastic DEA approach to derive managerial efÔ¨Åciency for managerial
ability. In the second step, we tested the hypotheses via random-effects panel data analysis
to examine the associations between managerial ability and different aspects of ESG perfor-
mance.
There are several limitations of this research. We used one type of stochastic DEA meth-
ods, namely change-constraint based SDEA. In future research, we suggest compare a variety
of SDEA methods (e.g., semi-parametric stochastic frontier analysis (SFA) or interval based
SDEA (Kao & Liu, 2022; Olesen & Petersen, 2016). In addition, the sample data were col-
lected from publicly traded energy companies in the U.S. and as such our Ô¨Åndings may not
be applicable to Ô¨Årms in other countries. As different countries have different political reg-
ulatory, and managerial systems (Gu, 2023), international datasets from different industries
could be used in future research to address the generalizability issue.
