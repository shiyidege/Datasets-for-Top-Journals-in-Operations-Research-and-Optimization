Abstract
In data envelopment analysis literature, the estimation of economies of scope involves
comparing two frontiers: one for diversiﬁed ﬁrms producing two outputs together (the diver-
siﬁed frontier) and the other involving additive ﬁrms (the additive frontier). A ﬁrm’s scope
economies can be assessed by comparing the costs of its outputs against these two frontiers.
If the cost for the additive frontier exceeds or falls short of that for the diversiﬁed frontier, one
can determine the presence or absence of scope economies for the diversiﬁed ﬁrm. However,
constructing the additive frontier requires the assumption that the cost of joint production by
a diversiﬁed ﬁrm equals the cost of separate production by specialized ﬁrms. In the absence
of such an additive cost structure in empirical studies, scope economies evaluation becomes
problematic. To address this, we propose an alternative measurement scheme. Using the free
disposability postulate of production theory, our scheme creates two sets of virtual special-
ized ﬁrms from the observed diversiﬁed ﬁrms, then updates them within their respective
specialized ﬁrm sets. This approach enables an evaluation of a ﬁrm’s scope economies by
comparing the cost of separate production against updated specialized technologies with
the joint production cost against diversiﬁed technology. Finally, the scheme also allows an
examination of whether a merger between any two specialized ﬁrms would be proﬁtable.

1 Introduction
The concepts of economies of scale (returns to scale)1 and economies of scope are increas-
ingly used in the formulation and evaluation of competitive strategies of multi-product ﬁrms.
The cost advantages of a multi-product (diversiﬁed) ﬁrm can stem not only from economies
of scale, which arise from size-related indivisibilities, but also from economies of scope,
which result from producing multiple outputs together rather than separately in specialized
ﬁrms (Sahoo & Tone, 2013).
Most real-life production units operate with idle capacity due to indivisibilities in ﬁxed
inputs, such as capital. This idle capacity gives rise to economies of scale when production
levels are expanded. However, if the demand for output is downward-slopping, instead of
scaling up the existing output, the production unit could leverage its idle capacity to diversify
into other products, thereby beneﬁting from economies of scope. This highlights the close
relationship between scale, scope, and idle capacity, all of which contribute to various mea-
sures of productive efﬁciency. As a result, suitable estimation models are required to capture
both scale and scope economies when assessing competitive strategy. However, this paper
focuses speciﬁcally on an estimation model for economies of scope.
Economies of scope exist between two products if the cost of producing them together in
a diversiﬁed ﬁrm is lower than producing them separately in two specialized ﬁrms (Baumol
et al., 1982). Two analytical approaches are commonly used to estimate scope economies:
the neoclassical approach and the axiomatic approach (Färe et al., 1988). The neoclassical
approach typically relies on parametric econometric methods, such as stochastic frontier
analysis, while the axiomatic approach is often estimated using nonparametric data envelop-
ment analysis (DEA). DEA2 offers several advantages over the stochastic frontier method.
First, DEA avoids the need to specify functional forms or stochastic structures, a limitation
in the stochastic frontier analysis that may confound the effects of misspeciﬁcation with
scale and scope economies (Fusco et al., 2018). Second, contrary to common belief, DEA is
a comprehensive statistical methodology that treats ﬁrm efﬁciency as a stochastic variable.
DEA estimators have desirable properties and support the development of formal statistical
tests (Banker, 1993; Banker & Natarajan, 2004; Banker et al., 2022). Third, DEA highlights
individual ﬁrm efﬁciencies or inefﬁciencies, making it particularly useful for managerial
decision-making. Fourth, DEA enables the attribution of total factor productivity change in
broader applications, considering both joint and output-speciﬁc inputs in the production pro-
cess (Walheer, 2018). This disaggregation aligns with our goal of developing a more reﬁned
measurement framework for economies of scope.
In the DEA literature, two main schemes are used to estimate economies of scope. The
ﬁrst is based on a usual technology set that incorporates physical inputs and outputs (Färe,
1986, 1988; Färe et al., 1994; Morita, 2003; Prior, 1996), while the second uses a value-based
technology set3 that includes input costs and physical outputs (Fried et al., 1998; Grosskopf

The DEA approach to measuring economies of scope compares two frontiers: one rep-
resenting real diversiﬁed frontier and the other an artiﬁcially constructed one. Building this
artiﬁcially frontier relies on an oversimplifying assumption that the cost of joint production
by a diversiﬁed ﬁrm equals the sum of the costs of separate production by specialized ﬁrms,
thus implying an additive technology structure. This assumption ignores interactions between
product lines and fails to capture the cost-saving synergies inherent in joint production, often
leading to an overestimation of costs and an underestimation of economies of scope. To
address this limitation, we propose a straightforward yet innovative measurement scheme
that avoids this assumption by leveraging the postulate of free disposability from production
theory.
In our proposed scheme, similar to existing approaches, we ﬁrst estimate the minimum
cost of joint production of two outputs by an observed diversiﬁed ﬁrm using the standard cost-
efﬁciency model of Färe et al. (1985a, 1985b). Next, applying the output-free disposability
postulate, we generate two sets of hypothetical specialized ﬁrms from the observed diversiﬁed
ﬁrms producing two outputs: one set producing only the ﬁrst output (with zero second output)
and the other producing only the second output (with zero ﬁrst output). We then update the
specialized production technologies by incorporating these two sets of hypothetical ﬁrms.
Third, for each diversiﬁed ﬁrm, we estimate the minimum cost of producing the ﬁrst output
using the updated technology for the ﬁrst output, and similarly, estimate the minimum cost
of producing the second output using the updated technology for the second output. Finally,
we compare the minimum cost of joint production in the diversiﬁed technology (from Step
1) with the sum of the minimum costs of separate production in their respective updated
specialized technologies (from Step 3) to determine whether it exhibits economies of scope.
A ﬁrm exhibits economies of scope if the minimum cost of joint production is less than the
sum of separate production costs, diseconomies of scope if it is greater, and neither economies
nor diseconomies if the two costs are equal.
Several distinguishing features characterize our proposed scheme. First, unlike existing
evaluation models, our approach is always feasible for estimating economies of scope, as
demonstrated in Theorem 1 in the methods section. Second, the minimum cost of a diversiﬁed
ﬁrm producing two outputs in a diversiﬁed technology is no less than the cost of the same
ﬁrm producing one of those outputs in our proposed updated specialized technologies, as
shown in Theorem 2. Third, in an additive technology, the minimum cost of a diversiﬁed
ﬁrm producing two outputs is no less than the sum of the minimum costs of their separate
production in the respective updated specialized technologies, which is detailed in Theorem 3.
Finally, we propose a method to assess whether a potential merger between two specialized
ﬁrms would be proﬁtable.

7 Concluding remarks
To assess the economies of scope of a diversiﬁed ﬁrm, the existing scheme compares the
minimum cost required to produce its two outputs jointly by an observed diversiﬁed ﬁrm
with the minimum cost that it would incur if those outputs were produced separately by vir-
tual additive (artiﬁcially diversiﬁed) ﬁrms. However, constructing these artiﬁcial diversiﬁed
ﬁrms relies on an additive structure for the underlying technology, which overlooks the cost
advantages that come from sharing ﬁxed resources across multiple products. As a result,
this additive structure tends to overestimate total costs and underestimates the economies of
scope.
To address this issue, this paper proposes an alternative measurement approach that avoids
the need for an additive cost structure. By leveraging the property of free disposability in
production theory, our proposed scheme creates two sets of virtual specialized ﬁrms from the
observed diversiﬁed ﬁrms and embeds them into their respective specialized technologies.
We then compute the minimum costs of producing the two outputs separately, using these
extended specialized technologies.
A diversiﬁed ﬁrm exhibits economies of scope if the cost of jointly producing two outputs
in a diversiﬁed technology is lower than the cost of producing them separately in updated
specialized technologies. The two key ﬁndings are as follows: (1) for each diversiﬁed ﬁrm,
the cost of its joint production of two outputs in the diversiﬁed technology is no less than
the cost of their separate production in updated specialized technologies; and (2) for each
diversiﬁed ﬁrm, the minimum cost of separate production of its two outputs in the updated
specialized technologies is no more than the minimum cost of their joint production in the
additive technology. Finally, using our proposed scheme, one can empirically assess whether
a potential merger between any two specialized ﬁrms would be proﬁtable.
This study concentrates on two outputs with overlapping inputs, but economies of scope
can also arise with non-overlapping inputs when specialized ﬁrms produce multiple outputs.
In such cases, efﬁciencies may arise from shared managerial expertise, streamlined oper-
ations, or technological complementarities. Future research could address these scenarios
by integrating methodologies like the multi-output proﬁt efﬁciency and directional distance
functions proposed by Cherchye et al. (2009), which offer a more ﬂexible framework for
evaluating ﬁrm performance across multiple outputs while considering the directional nature
of inputs and outputs. Additionally, integrating the frameworks developed by Mehdiloo and
Podinovski (2024), which accounts for embedded inputs and outputs within DEA, could

reﬁne the measurement of economies of scope in more complex production contexts. Such
advancements would enhance the robustness of our measurement framework and provide
deeper insights into the efﬁciency dynamics of multi-output ﬁrms operating in intricate pro-
duction environments.
