Abstract
Intervalgamesareanextensionofcooperativecoalitionalgamesinwhichplayersareassumed
to face payoff uncertainty. Characteristic functions thus assign a closed interval instead of a
real number. In this paper, we focus on interval game versions of Shapley values. First, we
modify Young’s strong monotonicity axiom for coalitional games into two versions so that
they can be applied to the Shapley mapping and show that this can be axiomatized within the
entire class of interval games using either version. Second, we derive the Shapley mapping
for speciﬁc examples by employing two approaches used in the proof of the axiomatization
and argue that our approach effectively works for a wide range of interval games.
Keywords Cooperative interval games · Shapley value · Shapley mapping · Interval
Shapley value · Axiomatization · Strong monotonicity
1 Introduction
This paper examines cooperative interval games in which players face payoff uncertainty.
One of the most familiar representations of cooperative game theory without uncertainty is
coalitional form games with transferable utility (also called coalitional games or TU games).
A coalition form game consists of the set N of players and a characteristic function v that gives
a real number v(S) (worth of S) to every subset S of N (coalitions). v(S) is the total payoff
that S can obtain by itself without uncertainty. However, the payoffs a coalition can obtain
may entail uncertainty. Therefore, introducing uncertainty into classical coalition form games
is a natural and important extension. The interval games, initially proposed and examined
by Branzei et al. (2003) and Alparslan Gök et al. (2009), consider interval uncertainty in
that the uncertainty regarding coalition payoff is represented by an interval rather than a real
number.Morespeciﬁcally,anintervalgamecomprisestheset N ofplayersandacharacteristic
function w that gives a closed interval w(S) to every coalition S. Therefore, interval games
can be regarded as a generalization of coalitional games.

In the existing literature on interval games, various solution concepts have been proposed
and their properties duly investigated. Alparslan Gök et al. (2010) (Alparslan Gök et al., 2009)
proposed the notion of interval solution concepts, a category that includes the interval core,
interval stable set, and interval Shapley value (ISV). Fei et al. (2018) and Liang and Li (2019)
investigated the notions of the discount Shapley value and Banzhaf value, respectively. Mal-
lozzi and Vidal-Puga (2021) examined interval games where players hold different attitudes
toward uncertainty, as represented by Hurwicz coefﬁcients. As for applications of interval
games to actual economic and social situations, Palanci et al. (2016) introduced uncertainty
into transportation games and formalized them as interval games. Alparslan Gök et al. (2009)
examined the Shapley value and Baker–Thompson rule in the interval game version of the
airport game. Recently, interval games have even been applied to the context of machine
learning interpretability, as in Napolitano et al. (2024). For additional details, see Alparslan
Gök (2010), Branzei et al. (2010) and Ishihara and Shino (2021).
In this study, we focus on interval game versions of the Shapley value, originally proposed
by Shapley (1953) as a solution concept for coalitional games. Several different solutions
categorized into this type have been proposed, among which, the most familiar and intensively
investigated property is the ISV deﬁned in Alparslan Gök et al. (2009). As will be discussed
in Sect.2, the ISV cannot be deﬁned in all interval games, but only in a subclass of interval
games known as size-monotonic interval games (SMIGs). The axiomatization of the ISV
had initially been carried out within a signiﬁcantly limited subclass of the SMIGs called
KIG games. Alparslan Gök et al. (2010) showed that within KIG games, the ISV is a unique
solution concept that satisﬁes the axioms of efﬁciency, symmetry, the dummy player property,
and additivity. Alparslan Gök (2014) also demonstrated that within KIG games, the ISV
is a unique solution concept that satisﬁes efﬁciency, symmetry, and strong monotonicity.
Recently, Ishihara and Shino (2023) revealed that the above axiomatizations can be extended
to all SMIGs.
In contrast to that analysis and axiomatization of the ISV, using the solution mapping in
Alparslan Gök et al. (2009), Shino et al. (2022) proposed an alternative interval game version
of the Shapley value, called the Shapley mapping, which is deﬁnable for all interval games
and assigns an n-dimensional real-valued vector to a pair consisting of an interval game and
a realization of the value of the grand coalition in the game. Furthermore, Shino et al. (2022)
showed that the Shapley mapping is a unique solution that satisﬁes the axioms of efﬁciency,
symmetry, the null player property, and additivity within the class of solution mappings.
Our study is composed of two parts. In the ﬁrst part, we implement axiomatizations of the
Shapley mapping by using the notion of strong monotonicity. To do so, we ﬁrst transform the
strong monotonicity axiom, originally proposed by Young (1985) in the context of coalitional
games, into two different versions that are applicable to interval games, and then show that
the Shapley mapping can be axiomatized within all interval games by using either version
of the strong monotonicity axiom. It should be noted here that in those axiomatizations, our
proof method is original and unlike that used in Young (1985) and Peleg and Sudholter (2007)
(or Neyman (1989)). More speciﬁcally, Young (1985) showed that the Shapley value is the
unique solution satisfying the axioms of efﬁciency, symmetry, and strong monotonicity by
expressing any coalitional game as a sum of bases and its associated coefﬁcients. Peleg and
Sudholter (2007) proved the same remark, but in a different way.
They ﬁrst constructed a sequence of games from the original game in which (i) the solution
of the last game in the sequence can be obtained straightforwardly, and (ii) the marginal
contributions of a speciﬁc player are unchanged between any two adjacent games, and then
showed that the solution in the original game coincides with the Shapley value. However,
if we were to employ those approaches directly to an axiomatization in the interval games,

2 Preliminaries
2.1 Coalitional games and interval games
An n-person coalitional game (or TU game) is a pair (N, v), where N = {1, 2, . . . , n} is
a set of players and v : 2N →R is a characteristic function that associates a real number
v(S) ∈R for each set S ∈2N, with the condition v(∅) = 0. Number v(S) is called the worth
of S. We refer to S and N as a coalition and grand coalition, respectively. For a coalition
S ∈2N, let |S| denote the number of players in S. Let CG be the set of all coalitional games.
We also denote n-person coalitional games (N, v) simply by v, and use the simpler notation
v(·) instead of v({·}).

and Morgenstern stable set (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1953), core (Gillies, 1959), and
Shapley value (Shapley, 1953). In this study, we use the notion of the Shapley value. For an
n-person coalitional game v ∈CG, the Shapley value φ(v) is deﬁned as the n-dimensional
real-valued vector φ(v) = (φ1(v), ..., φi(v), ..., φn(v)) with:

2.3 Solution mapping and Shapley mapping
As an alternative solution concept to the interval solution concept, our study focuses on
solution mapping. The notion of solution mapping, initially proposed by Alparslan Gök et
al. (2009) and recently investigated by Shino et al. (2022), is deﬁned as a mapping that
assigns n-dimensional real-valued vectors to each realization in the worth set of the grand
coalition. Formally, for an n-person interval game w ∈IG, a function F(w) : w(N) →Rn
is called solution mapping. For any interval game w, F(w)(t) assigns n-dimensional payoff
vectors to each realization t ∈w(N). It should be noted that the solution mapping and the
interval solution concept are alternative approaches, and neither is inherently superior to the
other. The solution mapping can be deﬁned by introducing t, an element of an interval, as
an ex-post realization of the grand coalition. In contrast, the interval solution concept can be
deﬁned without using such a selection from an interval. These solution concepts represent
different theoretical aspects and should be used alternatively depending on the situation being
analyzed.

3 Main result
In contrast to Shino et al. (2022) axiomatization using the additivity axiom, in the present
study, we modify Young’s (Young, 1985) strong monotonicity axiom for coalitional games
into forms that can be applied to interval games, and show that the Shapley mapping can be
axiomatized with the sets of axioms including the modiﬁed strong monotonicities. To start,
Young’s strong monotonicity axiom is as follows:
• Young’s strong monotonicity axiom (Young, 1985) Let ψ be a solution for coalitional
games. For two coalitional games v1, v2 ∈CG and for a player i ∈N, suppose that the

We now consider applying this strong monotonicity axiom to interval games. The issue
here is that while the above strong monotonicity axiom for coalitional games speciﬁes the
conditions that a solution ψ should satisfy based on the relative size between two real numbers
of ψi(v1) and ψi(v2), in the case of interval games, σi(v1) and σi(v2) are not real numbers,
but mappings. Therefore, if we specify the condition based on the relative size of real numbers
as that in coalitional games, we need to consider σi(w1)(t1) and σi(w2)(t2) instead of σi(v1)
and σi(v2), where t1 and t2 are realizations of the worth of the grand coalition in games w1
and w2, respectively. However, σi(w1)(t1) and σi(w2)(t2) are crucially inﬂuenced by t1 and
t2, which represent uncertainties of the worth of the grand coalitions. Therefore, to specify
the conditions, the effects of uncertainty should be appropriately controlled.
To address this issue intrinsic to interval game analyses, we employ two related yet distinct
approaches. First, in Sect. 3.1, while keeping the mappings σi(w1) and σi(w2) unchanged,
we transform a realization of the worth set of the grand coalition in either w1 or w2 so that
the degree of uncertainty in the game is matched with that in the other game. Second, as
an alternative approach, in Sect. 3.2, we transform either w1 or w2 with different worth sets
of the grand coalition so that the worth set in the transformed game is identical to that in
the other game, making them comparable. With both approaches, we demonstrate that the
Shapley value can be axiomatized across all interval games.

so that it can be applied to interval games. We then show that the Shapley mapping can
be axiomatized with a set of axioms, including the interval game version of the strong
monotonicityaxiom,withinthecoverageoftheallintervalgames.Here,itshouldbenotedthat
our proof method is distinct from those presented in Young (1985) and Peleg and Sudholter
(2007). To elaborate further on this point, ﬁrst, if Young’s (Young, 1985) proof method were
adopted, it would only be possible to axiomatize within the class of KIG.3 Second, even if
Peleg and Sudholter (2007) approach were adopted, the range within which axiomatization
is possible would be limited to SMIGs. Our proof method, while fundamentally relying on
Peleg and Sudholter (2007) approach, possesses originality in the following aspect: when
deﬁning an interval game w′(T ) in (10), we construct it as a sum of the original game w(T )
and its associated interval, rather than as a difference of the original game and its associated
game, as described “w(T ) = v(T )−vs(T )” on p.158 of Peleg and Sudholter (2007), to avoid
using the subtraction operator. As a result, we are able to provide a proof of axiomatization
for all interval games without encountering the speciﬁc problem inherent in interval games,
where the range of axiomatizable games is signiﬁcantly reduced when using the subtraction
operator (hereafter, we refer to this as the “subtraction operator problem”).4 These issues
will be revisited and discussed in greater detail in Sect.4 using numerical examples.

4 Discussion and example
In this section, we ﬁrst employ the two methods used in the proof of uniqueness in the pre-
vious section to derive the Shapley mapping for speciﬁc interval games. Simultaneously, we

also apply (i) Young’s (Young, 1985) method, which axiomatizes the Shapley value using
the axiom of strong monotonicity, and (ii) the method used by Peleg and Sudholter (2007)
(or Neyman (1989)) in their proof, to the same numerical examples and derive the Shap-
ley mapping. Young (1985) expressed a characteristic function game as a sum of bases and
demonstratedthatsolutionssatisfyingthreeaxioms,includingtheaxiomofstrongmonotonic-
ity, are limited to the Shapley value. Furthermore, Peleg and Sudholter (2007) constructed a
sequence of games from the original game step-by-step, in which the solution of the last game
in the sequence can be obtained straightforwardly while keeping the marginal contribution of
a speciﬁc player unchanged. By tracing back to the original game using strong monotonicity,
they showed that the solution coincides with the Shapley value.
By contrast, this section conﬁrms that when attempting to apply these existing approaches
directly to interval games, the Shapley mapping can only be determined for a limited range
of games, such as KIGs and SMIGs. This indicates that merely applying existing approaches
conﬁnes the range of interval games axiomatizable using the strong monotonicity axiom to
KIGs and SMIGs, which in turn, implies the necessity of our proof method for axiomatizing
the Shapley mapping in all interval games. Finally, we summarize the axiomatization of the
ISV and Shapley mapping, clarifying the contributions of both the Shapley mapping and our
proof method.

Shapleymappingcanbedeterminedusinganyofthefourapproaches.InSect.4.2,wefocuson
an interval game that is neither KIG nor SMIG, where only our approach works effectively.

here are abstract ones, but it should be noted that, since the Shapley mapping can be deﬁned
for all interval games, it can also be used to more practical applications of interval games. As

4.3 Discussion
Summarizing the analysis and discussion thus far, our study can be considered to have
two related but distinct contributions. The ﬁrst contribution is to modify Young’s strong
monotonicity axiom for coalitional games into two versions applicable to interval games,
and to prove that the Shapley mapping can be axiomatized within the entire class of interval
games by using either version. The second contribution is to adopt a proof method distinct
from both Young’s (Young, 1985) original proof, which axiomatizes the Shapley value in
coalitional games, and the proof by Peleg and Sudholter (2007) (or Neyman (1989)), to
address the subtraction operator problem inherent to interval game analyses. In this regard,

only when a solution applicable to SMIGs, such as the Shapley mapping, is employed.
5 Conclusion
In this study, we focused on the Shapley mapping, a Shapley value-type solution concept
applicable to all interval games, and showed axiomatizations using interval game versions
of the strong monotonicity proposed by Young (1985).
Speciﬁcally, we employed two relevant but different approaches to deﬁne strong mono-
tonicity axioms applicable to the Shapley mapping. First, while keeping the mappings σi(w1)
and σi(w2) unchanged, we transformed a realization of the worth set of the grand coalition
in either w1 or w2 so that the degree of uncertainty in the game is matched with that in the
other game, and evaluated the relative size of σi(w1)(·) and σi(w2)(·). Next, as an alter-
native approach, we transformed either w1 or w2 so that the worth set in the transformed
game is identical to that in the other game, making them comparable. With both approaches,
we showed that the Shapley mapping can be axiomatized across the entire class of interval
games. Furthermore, we adopted a proof method distinct from both Young’s (Young, 1985)
original proof and that by Peleg and Sudholter (2007) to address the subtraction operator
problem inherent to interval game analyses.
As discussed earlier (see 4.3), there appear to be quite a few cases in actual economic and
social situations where it is appropriate to apply interval games that are not in SMIGs. Con-
sequently, the Shapley mapping, which can be applied to all interval games, including those
that are not in SMIGs, and allows for axiomatization using additivity and strong monotonicity
across the entirety of interval games, is considered to have substantial practical signiﬁcance
in interval games analyses.
Finally, we identify three topics for future research. The ﬁrst notes that while axiom-
atizations of the Shapley mapping using additivity and strong monotonicity have been
completed, characterizing this solution concept using the notion of consistency has not yet
been undertaken. Second, since its introduction by Shino et al. (2022), the Shapley mapping
has undergone only theoretical analysis. However, since the Shapley mapping can be applied
to all interval games, including practical ones, it is worth exploring its application to interval
games. The third concerns the relationship between players’ risk preferences and resource
allocation within the framework of cooperative game theory. This topic becomes feasible for
