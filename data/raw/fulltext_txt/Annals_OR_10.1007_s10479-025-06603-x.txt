Abstract
This paper uses Poisson autoregressions to forecast earthquakes and heatwaves in Italy. Statis-
tical modelling of counts of earthquakes and high temperatures is of fundamental importance
for estimating risks involving natural disasters and climate changes. Four different Poisson
models are put under scrutiny in terms of forecasting performance: a standard Poisson model
with constant rate; a Poisson autoregressive model; a Poisson autoregressive model with
covariates; and a self-excited threshold Poisson autoregression model. The standard mean
square forecast error and the average forecasting score are used to evaluate the forecasts. A
Monte Carlo study is ﬁrst carried out. Then, two empirical applications on earthquake data
for Italy and on temperature data for six Italian cities are offered. For the earthquakes, the
best forecasting performance is achieved by the Poisson model with covariates. In case of
temperatures, the results are more mixed for the forecasting score measure, while the Poisson
autoregressive model with covariates prevails in all the cases when using the mean square
forecast error.

Statistical modelling of counts of earthquakes is of fundamental importance for estimating

to model earthquake (see Lu, 2012; Wang et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018;

models are also used to investigate the impact of high temperatures on death (Do-Woo et al.,
2019; Lopez-Bueno et al., 2021), crime counts (XU et al., 2020) and storms (Asaad, 2023). In

This paper ﬁrst carries out Monte Carlo simulations to assess the forecasts of four Pois-
son models, namely a standard Poisson model with constant rate, a Poisson autoregressive
model (PAR) (Fokianos et al., 2009), a Poisson autoregressive model with covariates (PARX)
(Agosto et al., 2016), and a self-excited threshold Poisson autoregression model (SETPAR)
(Wang et al., 2014). For the evaluation, the mean square forecast error (MSFE) and the aver-
age (logarithmic) forecasting score (FS) (see Amisano and Giacomini, 2007) are used. To
the best of our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst paper to investigate the forecasting performance
of these four models through simulations. The Data Generating Processes (DGPs) used to
evaluate the forecasting performances are taken from Blasques et al. (2019). These processes
are designed to replicate the main characteristics of the time series employed in the empirical
applications.
Second, the same four models are used to model and forecast earthquakes and temperatures
in Italy. In particular, this paper uses monthly data counts of earthquakes at national level
from January 1900 to December 2020 and monthly counts of high temperatures for six Italian
cities from January 1973 to June 2024. The cities under scrutiny are Turin, Milan, Bologna,
Rome, Naples and Palermo.
The simulation results show the two best models are PARX and SETPAR, depending on
the values of the parameters of the data generating process. The empirical results are more
mixed in case of temperatures than earthquakes. For the temperatures, the PARX model
outperforms all the other models across all the cities in case of the MSFE measure. When
using the FS accuracy measure, the PAR model prevails in 4 out of 6 cases (Milan, Bologna,
Rome and Naples), while the PARX model ranks ﬁrst for Palermo, and the SETPAR model
outperforms the other models for Turin. For earthquakes, the PARX model is the best model
overall.

2 Modelling earthquakes and heatwaves with Poisson autoregressive
models
This section describes the Poisson Autoregressive models for time series count data. Let Yt
be a time series of counts, and Ft−1 the σ-ﬁeld generated by {Y0, ..., Yt−1, λ0, ..., λt−1},
where {λt} indicates the intensity of the Poisson distribution at time t. The simplest approach
to modeling a time series of count is to assume that Yt follows a Poisson distribution with a
constant intensity parameter λ. The Poisson model assumes that:
Yt|Ft−1 ∼Poisson(λ),
(1)
where λ > 0 is the intensity of occurrences. This model is quite restrictive because it assumes
that the number of events is independent across time periods and that λ does not vary over
time. Despite its simplicity, this model can provide a useful benchmark for comparison with
more complex models.2
To account for potential time dependence in Yt, Fokianos et al. (2009) proposed the PAR
model. In this model, the conditional distribution of Yt given the past observations follows
a Poisson distribution with a time-varying rate λt, Yt|Ft−1 ∼Poisson(λt). Speciﬁcally, the
intensity λt is given by:
λt = α + βYt−1 + γ λt−1,
(2)
where the parameters α, β, and γ are assumed to be positive, and λ0 and Y0 are assumed to
be ﬁxed. The parameter α is the baseline rate, and β controls for the inﬂuence of the previous
counts on the current rate and γ is the autoregressive parameter. This model introduces time
dependence into the process, allowing for the possibility that high values of Yt in the past
leads to higher future values.
An extension of the PAR model considers the inclusion of exogenous covariates, as
described by Agosto et al. (2016). In this case, a deterministic trend can be incorporated
into the model as an exogenous variable to capture potential temporal patterns in Yt. The
PARX model is given by:
Yt|Ft−1 ∼Poisson(λt)
(3)
λt = α + βYt−1 + γ λt−1 + δxt
(4)
where xt represents the deterministic components (i.e. seasonality and trend). The inclusion
of seasonality and trend terms allow for greater ﬂexibility in modeling the evolution of Yt
over time.3 Finally, we consider the SETPAR model introduced by Wang et al. (2014). This
model is able to capture negative serial dependence. Further, it allows for a more general
framework for the intensity process. It incorporates a threshold mechanism, where the rate
λt is allowed to change depending on whether past values of Yt exceed a certain threshold r.
The data used in this paper show large volatility, asymmetry and nonlinearity (see Sect.4).
The intensity rate λt is given by:
λt =

4.1 Earthquakes
The dataset consists of the monthly counts of earthquakes in Italy from January 1900 to
December 2020. The data were obtained from the Italian Macroseismic Database DBMI15,
that is a scientiﬁc product released by the National Institute of Geophysics and Volcanology.6
The DBM15 is openly available at http://emidius.mi.ingv.it/CPTI15-DBMI15/ (see Locati
et al., 2022) and a “web service” through the Italian Archive of Historical Earthquake Data
(ASMI), see http://emidius.mi.ingv.it/ASMI/services/ (for CPTI15, see Rovida et al., 2020;
2022). The CPI catalogue includes the earthquakes with a maximum intensity greater than
or equal to 5 along with those of instrumental magnitude equivalent to moment magnitude
(Mw) 4.0 or larger. Some earthquakes with Mw smaller than 4.0 are also included in the
catalogue.7
The time series covers a total of 1452 observations, providing a comprehensive view
of seismic activity in Italy over the course of 120years. Figure2 plots the time series of
earthquake counts over the entire period of study. The time series shows signiﬁcant variation
over time, with periods of heightened seismic activity followed by quieter intervals.

models considered in this study: the Poisson model with constant rate (Poisson), the PAR
model, the PARX model, and the SETPAR model. These forecasts were generated using a
rolling window procedure, where the model was re-estimated at each step using data from
the preceding years, and a one-step-ahead prediction was made for the next month. This
approach ensures that each forecast is based on information available at the time of prediction,
mimicking real-time forecasting conditions. The ﬁgure compares the observed earthquake
counts with the predictions from each of the models, providing insights into their forecasting
performance. The rolling window approach captures the dynamic nature of seismic activity
and allows for an evaluation of how well each model predicts future earthquake counts. The
rolling window forecasting procedure was implemented as follows: starting from the initial
portion of the dataset, the models were ﬁtted using a ﬁxed window of data, and a one-step-
ahead forecast was made for the next time point. The window was then shifted forward by one
month, and the process was repeated until the end of the forecasting period (December 2020).
This method allows for a thorough evaluation of the predictive performance of each model,
as it mimics the real-time prediction scenario where only past data is available. The one-
step-ahead forecasts generated by each model were then compared with the actual observed
values, providing insights into the accuracy and robustness of the models in the forecasting
seismic activity in Italy.

5 Conclusion
The study of earthquakes and climate changes is of utmost importance for social disciplines.
This paper aims to assess the performance of four different Poisson models for the forecasts
of earthquakes and high temperature in Italy.
The paper ﬁrst carries out Monte Carlo simulations for the evaluation of the four models
under scrutiny: a standard Poisson model with constant rate; a Poisson autoregressive model,
a Poisson autoregressive model with covariates; and a self-excited threshold Poisson autore-
gression model. This is the ﬁrst contribution of the paper. Then, two empirical applications
on earthquake data for Italy and on temperature data for six Italian cities are offered. More
speciﬁcally, monthly data counts of earthquakes from January 1900 to December 2020 and
monthly counts of high temperatures from January 1973 to June 2024 are considered. The
cities under scrutiny are Turin, Milan, Bologna, Rome, Naples and Palermo. For the evalu-
ation of the forecasts the mean square forecast error and the average forecasting score are
used.
The simulation results show that the Poisson model with covariates outperforms all the
other models in terms of both means quare forecast error and average forecasting score.

From the empirical applications, the following main results emerge. First, for earthquake
data the best forecasting performance is achieved by the Poisson model with covariates, that
is a model with a deterministic trend. Second, in case of temperatures, the results are more
mixed when using the average forecasting score measure. Here, the Poisson autoregressive
model with constant rate prevails against the other models on the majority of the cases. Third,
the Poisson autoregressive model with seasonal dummies outperforms all the other models
in terms of mean square forecast error for the temperatures.
All in all, the empirical results obtained in this paper may have useful utilization for
seismic risk and climate change analyses.
