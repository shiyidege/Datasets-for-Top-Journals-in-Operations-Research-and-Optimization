A Co-Opetitive Game Analysis of Platform Compatibility Strategies Under 
Add-on Services 
Abstract: Large-scale platforms (LSPs) with valuation and awareness advantages have enabled 
competing small-scale platforms (SSPs) to be embedded in their platforms. This compatibility strategy 
creates a new channel, that is, the compatible channel, through which customers can purchase services 
from SSPs via the LSPs. Additionally, numerous platforms have been introducing add-on services to 
enhance their profitability. In this study, we develop stylized game models to characterize the interaction 
between an LSP and an SSP and explore their strategic and operational decisions on platform 
compatibility under add-on services. Our major research findings are as follows: First, compatibility 
has opposite impacts on service pricing. That is, at a low proportion of demand through the compatible 
channel, the two platforms engage in a price war; otherwise, they both raise prices. Second, we identify 
the conditions for platform compatibility: compatibility becomes an equilibrium strategy if the 
proportion of demand through the compatible channel falls within an intermediate range. Third, we find 
that homogeneous add-on services stimulate rather than inhibit compatibility due to different profit foci 
of two platforms. Finally, we conduct extensions to further verify the robustness of the conclusions. Our 
results provide important implications to the burgeoning debate on when platforms should implement 
compatibility to achieve a win-win scenario under a variety of settings. 
Keywords: game theory; co-opetition relationship; two-sided market; platform compatibility; add-on 
services

1. Introduction 
The rapid development of the sharing economy has spawned many two-sided platforms in different 
industries, such as taxi-hailing, food delivery, and hospitality. In a two-sided market, the number of 
users on one side affects the utility of users on the other side (Armstrong and Wright, 2007). For example, 
on a taxi-hailing platform, an abundance of customers increases the likelihood of drivers to join the 
platform and provide services for potential income. Similarly, a sufficient number of drivers attract 
customers to the platform to address their transportation needs. In this case, the platform earns income 
by matching drivers with passengers. 
In recent times, some two-sided platforms have started allowing their competitors to use their 
proprietary interfaces. Typically, large-scale platforms (LSPs) may grant small-scale platforms (SSPs) 
access to their interfaces, enabling SSPs to tap into a large pool of potential customers. This 
compatibility strategy creates a new channel called ŌĆ£compatible channel,ŌĆØ through which customers can 
purchase services from an SSP through the LSP. For instance, Didi has allowed some SSPs, such as 
Dongfeng and Ruqi, to launch their mini apps on its platform (Didi, 2019). If an SSPŌĆÖs service is 

3 
 
transacted in the compatible channel, the SSP (Dongfeng in this case) must pay Didi a commission of 
10% or more. Similarly, Uber has partnered with two taxi companies, Curb Mobility and Creative 
Mobile Technologies, to allow taxi bookings for passengers in New York through the Uber platform 
(New York Times, 2022). Additionally, Apple has permitted the Kindle app to be installed on its iPad 
and iPhone devices (Adner et al., 2019). 
This research examines the scenario wherein the LSP possesses valuation and awareness 
advantages. The awareness advantage means that more consumers are aware of the services offered by 
the LSP than those by the SSP. The valuation advantage indicates that all else being equal, consumers 
prefer to purchase services from the LSP over the SSP potentially because of the formerŌĆÖs superior brand 
reputation or after-sales services. As such, implementing compatibility appears to be a double-edged 
sword for the LSPŌĆÖs profitability. On the one hand, compatibility offers the LSP commission revenue 
for SSP transactions in the compatible channel and helps it achieve a high transaction volume. On the 
other hand, compatibility weakens the LSPŌĆÖs valuation and awareness advantages, thus reducing its 
effective service demand. Nevertheless, numerous cases of platform compatibility have been observed 
in practice, with the case of DidiŌĆÖs compatibility with Dongfeng being a prime example. To understand 
the rationale behind these practices, we aim to explore the conditions under which the LSP and SSP can 
implement compatibility. 
Meanwhile, platforms have been introducing add-on services on top of their core services to 
exploit potential cross-sales opportunities (Zhang et al. 2021); such trend may affect the dynamics when 
the platforms contemplate their compatibility strategies. For instance, Didi (LSP) and Dongfeng (SSP) 
have launched charging services for new energy vehicles on top of their ride-hailing services 
(Guangming.net News, 2019; Sohu News, 2019). Understandably, the revenue of add-on services is 
positively correlated with transaction volume. For example, owing to the huge transaction volume on 
its platform, Didi provided 25 million charging services in 2019, covering nearly one-fifth of electric 
vehicle owners in China (Beijing Business Today, 2020). The offering of add-on services can bring 
considerable additional benefits to platforms, but it inevitably intensifies their competition for 
transaction volume. Given that compatibility shifts transactions between LSPs and SSPs, offering add-
on services on platforms may deter them from adopting the compatibility strategy. Nevertheless, we 
observe that Dongfeng has made its ride-hailing services available on the Didi platform as a third-party 
service. This observation motivates us to investigate the impact of offering add-on services on the two 
membersŌĆÖ compatibility strategy. 
Theoretically, on the one hand, the existing literature on two-sided market focuses on two core 
themes: pricing mechanisms of monopolistic platforms (Chou et al., 2012; Kung and Zhong, 2017; Sun 
et al., 2019) and operations in competitive platforms (Armstrong and Wright, 2007; Cohen and Zhang, 
2022; Bakos and Halaburda, 2020). To the best of the authorsŌĆÖ knowledge, no research has examined 
co-opetitive relations, especially when the compatibility strategy under add-on services is considered 
in a two-sided market. On the other hand, the existing research on co-opetitive relations is mainly 

4 
 
conducted from the perspective of supply chains, including the horizontal co-opetition between peer 
firms (Casadesus-Masanell and Yoffie, 2007; Nagarajan and So┼Īi┬┤, 2007; Yuan et al., 2021; Song et al., 
2020; Adner et al., 2020; Chen and Guo, 2022) and the vertical co-opetition between buyers and sellers 
(Lim and Tan, 2010; Niu et al., 2019). Hence, no study has considered network externality, a key 
characteristic of a two-sided market. Therefore, this research aims to close the gap by investigating the 
increasingly common co-opetition relationship in two-sided platforms. 
Motivated by practical cases and research gaps, we consider a two-sided market consisting of an 
LSP and an SSP with add-on services, with the former holding awareness and valuation advantages 
over the latter. After the two platforms achieve compatibility, a compatible channel will be established 
on the LSP to offer the SSPŌĆÖs core services to customers. As such, more customers in the market become 
aware of the SSP, and the LSPŌĆÖs endorsement of the SSP in the compatible channel helps boost the 
valuation of the SSPŌĆÖs services. To exploit potential cross-sales opportunities, both platforms offer add-
on services. To make the model tractable, add-on services are offered by the platform owners. Under 
this assumption, for transactions via the compatible channel, the SSP offers core services while the LSP 
offers add-on services, if any. Both platforms offer homogenous add-on services in the base model and 
allow heterogeneous add-on services in the extended model (Appendix C-1). Specifically, we aim to 
explore the following research questions: (1) What are the conditions for the two platforms to implement 
the compatibility strategy? (2) How does offering add-on services affect their compatibility strategy? 
Our study yields several major findings. First, we examine the impact of compatibility on pricing, 
demand, and transaction volume. Intuitively, after compatibility, the LSP loses its relative awareness 
advantage, leading to intensified price competition between the two platforms. However, if the 
proportion of the SSPŌĆÖs demand attributed to the compatible channel is at the higher end of the range 
that makes compatibility possible, both platforms share more common interests. This condition buffers 
the competition, allowing them to set higher service prices than those in the incompatible case. Second, 
we find that compatibility becomes an equilibrium strategy if the proportion of the SSPŌĆÖs demand 
attributed to the compatible channel falls within an intermediate range. If the proportion is too low, the 
SSP obtains most of the spillover benefits from compatibility while the LSP refrains from opening the 
compatible channel to the SSP. Conversely, if the proportion is too high, the LSP grabs more spillover 
benefits generated by the compatibility as the SSP has to share more revenue with the LSP, deterring 
the SSP from adopting the compatibility strategy. Finally, we explore the impact of homogeneous add-
on services on compatibility. Surprisingly, we discover that offering homogeneous add-on services 
stimulates rather than inhibits the adoption of compatibility by the two platforms. 
The contributions of this study are as follows. First, to the best of our knowledge, this study is the 
first to focus on co-opetitive relationships in the context of a two-sided market. To this end, we propose 
a new cooperation model wherein the SSP builds the compatible channel on the LSP. In this way, we 
expand the research on channel management and two-sided market. Second, we find that after 
compatibility, the LSP emphasizes the profit brought by transaction volume (i.e., web traffic) while the 

5 
 
SSP focuses more on the profit from demand. Their different profit foci effectively drive cooperation 
between the platforms. Therefore, our study extends the findings of Adner et al. (2020) in the context 
of the two-sided market. Finally, our research provides rich management guidance. For example, 
platforms can offer homogeneous or slightly heterogeneous add-on services to achieve mutually 
beneficial platform compatibility. 
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. The literature is analyzed from three aspects in 
Section 2. Model setup and parameters are described in Section 3. The equilibrium outcomes of the 
compatible and incompatible cases are presented in Section 4. The comparative static analyses of 
pricing decisions, demand, and profits are discussed in Section 5. The impact of add-on services on 
compatibility is detailed in Section 6. The robustness analyses conducted in the study are expounded in 
Section 7. Three model extensions are identified in Section 8. The conclusions and managerial insights 
are summarized in Section 9. All proofs and more extensions can be found in the Appendix. 
 
2. Literature review 
We investigate the compatibility strategy of two platforms under add-on services in a two-sided 
market. Given that compatibility is a subset of the co-opetition relationship (Adner et al., 2019), this 
study is closely related to three streams of literature: (1) co-opetition relationship and compatibility, (2) 
platform operations in the two-sided market, and (3) add-on services. 
2.1 Co-opetition relationship and compatibility 
Co-opetition refers to the intertwining scenario in which two or more firms engage in both 
competition and cooperation. The idea is that the co-opetitive firms strive to increase the size of the 
total pie for them to divide so that a winŌĆōwin result can be achieved (Luo, 2004; Jacob, 2020). 
The co-opetition relationship has received extensive attention in operations management fields. 
For example, Casadesus-Masanell and Yoffie (2007) explore the impact of the partnership between two 
complementary product manufacturers (e.g., Intel and Microsoft) on research and development 
investment, pricing, and new product release time. Nagarajan and So┼Īi─ć (2007) focus on how a dynamic 
alliance of agents in a competitive market affects prices, inventory levels, and market structure. In the 
service field, Yuan et al. (2021) develop a co-opetition model in service clusters with waiting area 
entertainment. They conclude that service providers can only benefit from co-opetition by balancing 
the relationship between efficiency and fairness. In addition to these studies on horizontal co-opetition 
between peer firms, some scholars have focused on vertical co-opetition between buyers and sellers in 
a supply chain setting. For example, Lim and Tan (2010) investigate the interaction between an original 
equipment manufacturer (OEM) and an upstream contract manufacturer (CM) and find that the high 
brand equity of the OEM can prevent the CM from entering the market. Niu et al. (2019) consider 
production decisions in a supply chain consisting of a manufacturer and an OEM. Their results show 
that under the co-opetition relationship, implementing an ex-post production strategy may harm the 

6 
 
supply chain. 
Some scholars have considered the co-opetition relationship in platform operations in which 
platform compatibility can be treated as a specific form of co-opetition. For instance, Maruyama and 
Zennyo (2013) find that compatibility depends on product life cycles: once most users have purchased 
the hardware, platformsŌĆÖ profits accrue mostly from content purchases, incentivizing competing 
platforms to make their devices compatible. Adner et al. (2019) study the compatibility strategy between 
two competing platforms that sell hardware and software simultaneously. Their results indicate that the 
motivation for one-way compatibility may stem from the differences in the platformsŌĆÖ profit foci. Song 
et al. (2021) explore a case in which an online retailer opens its platform and a third-party seller joins 
the platform. Chen and Guo (2022) examine how new media advertising affects retail platform openness. 
They find that the availability of relatively low-cost advertising through new media plays a vital role. 
Whereas these existing studies discuss the compatibility of content platforms (e.g., e-books) and e-
commerce platforms, our research explores the compatibility strategy of two-sided platforms that offer 
core and add-on services. 
2.2 Platform operations in a two-sided market 
A two-sided market serves two groups of agents with a platform as an intermediary. In this market, 
the agents on the two sides transact through a matching mechanism of the platform (Armstrong, 2006; 
Casadesus-Masanell and Zhu, 2010). The emergence of a two-sided market requires three conditions: 
the existence of two distinct groups of users, network externality, and a matching intermediary (Xie et 
al. 2021). Scholars have conducted extensive research on network externality, and their findings indicate 
that the utility of agents on one side is affected by the number of agents on the other side (Mantena and 
Saha, 2012). For example, motivated by the video game industry, Anderson et al. (2014) explore the 
optimum investment in platform performance under strong cross-network externalities. They show that 
contrary to the conventional wisdom in ŌĆ£winners-take-allŌĆØ markets, heavy investment in the core 
performance of a platform does not always yield a competitive edge. Xie et al. (2021) study the impact 
of the same-side and cross-side network effects on multihoming platform competition. 
In terms of research on platforms in a two-sided market, some scholars are concerned about the 
pricing of monopolistic platforms (Chou et al. 2012; Kung and Zhong, 2017; Sun et al. 2019; Birge et 
al. 2021a; Birge et al. 2021b). Some studies have investigated the fact that platforms rely on 
decentralized providers to determine prices. For instance, Birge et al. (2021a) examine how to maximize 
revenue by leveraging commissions and subscriptions for a platform that does not directly control 
pricing. Birge et al. (2021b) study how a platform optimizes its providersŌĆÖ pricing through information 
disclosure and incentives. By contrast, other studies believe that platforms act as ŌĆ£central plannersŌĆØ and 
have complete control over the price setting process. For example, Chou et al. (2012) examine the 
impact of supply chain replenishment costs and demand uncertainty on platform pricing strategies. 
Kung and Zhong (2017) obtain the optimal strategic choice of a platform by comparing membership-
based pricing, transaction-based pricing, and cross-subsidization strategies. Sun et al. (2019) investigate 

7 
 
the optimal pricing strategy for online ride-hailing platforms under two driver-matching policies: first 
to respond and closest to the customer. Different from the above literature, this study considers the 
pricing strategies of two platforms, an LSP and SSP, where the LSP has awareness and valuation 
advantages. 
Some scholars have explored the phenomenon of platform competition in a two-sided market. For 
example, Armstrong and Wright (2007) find that instead of directly competing for sellers, symmetric 
platforms choose to compete indirectly by subsidizing buyers. Bakos and Halaburda (2020) suggest that 
the conventional advice of subsidizing one side to maximize the total profit tends to have limited effect 
or even be counterproductive when both sides choose multihoming, which is an important caveat given 
the increasing prevalence of multihoming in platform markets. Cohen and Zhang (2022) consider the 
competition between an instant ride-hailing platform and a taxi company, and design a revenue sharing 
contract to benefit both parties. Although the competition of two-sided platforms has received 
widespread attention, not much is known about co-opetition, which should arise once LSPs reach a 
consensus with SSPs on compatibility by allowing SSPs to open compatible channels on LSPs. To close 
this research gap, we analyze the compatibility strategy of two co-opetitive platforms in a two-sided 
market. 
2.3 Add-on services 
Add-on or value-added services are widely discussed in many fields (Rivera et al. 2016; Liu et al. 
2019; Zhang et al. 2021). For example, Rivera et al. (2016) empirically investigate the relationship 
between cooperation and value-added services in logistics clusters. Liu et al. (2019) examine an online-
to-offline platformŌĆÖs pricing strategy and explore how it is affected by the service providerŌĆÖs quantity, 
value-added services, and matching capability. Most platform owners have realized that offering add-
on services on top of their core services is an effective approach to attracting customers and expanding 
businesses (Liu et al. 2019). However, very few scholars have focused on add-on services in two-sided 
platforms. Dou et al. (2016) consider a platformŌĆÖs value-added service investment strategy under cross-
network externality and explore the optimal value-added service levels and pricing decisions. Similarly, 
Zhang et al. (2021) study the bilateral value-added services and pricing strategies of a third-party 
manufacturing platform. Although the aforementioned studies have considered monopolistic platforms 
under add-on services, they do not investigate the impact of add-on services on the operations of two 
co-opetitive platforms, which are the focus of this study. 
2.4 Literature summary 
From a co-opetition angle, most scholars are concerned about horizontal or vertical co-opetition 
between traditional enterprises while limited research has been devoted to the compatibility strategy of 
two-sided platforms, which represent a special form of the co-opetition relationship. From the 
perspective of two-sided markets, a large number of studies have explored the different operational 
issues of monopolistic platforms or competitive platforms, but they have largely neglected how add-on 
services affect the co-opetition relationship of two platforms. In Table 1, we list the studies that are

3.3 Utility of supply side 
For providers on the supply side, we consider the single-homing case in which they can register

9. Concluding remarks 
This study explores platform compatibility in a two-sided market consisting of an LSP and SSP 
and investigates the impact of add-on services on their compatibility decisions. The main conclusions 
are as follows: 
First, with a varying proportion of the SSPŌĆÖs demand in the compatible channel, two different 
pricing strategies exist after the two platforms implement compatibility: both platforms dropping prices 
owing to intensified competition or both platforms raising prices. Meanwhile, the impacts of 
compatibility on the demand and transaction volume are contingent upon the valuation enhancement 
and the proportion of the SSPŌĆÖs demand in the compatible channel. Second, we find that compatibility 
is not the dominant strategy. When the proportion of the SSPŌĆÖs demand in the compatible channel is in 

30 
 
an intermediate range, the compatibility strategy arises as an equilibrium for the two platforms. In 
addition, we explore how network externality affects the equilibrium compatibility strategy. The 
simulation results show that a high network externality coefficient reduces the range of ’üó values 
within which compatibility arises as the equilibrium strategy. Finally, we explore the impact of add-on 
services on the equilibrium compatibility strategy and reveal that introducing homogeneous add-on 
services is conducive to the compatibility of the two platforms. Some model extensions further verify 
the robustness of the conclusions. 
This study provides three aspects of insights for managers. First, managers should understand that 
compatibility may hurt their profitability. When the SSPŌĆÖs demand in the compatible channel falls within 
an intermediate range, compatibility allows both platforms to raise their prices to achieve a winŌĆōwin 
situation. When the demand in the compatible channel is too low (high), the LSP (SSP) is better off 
going with the incompatible strategy. Therefore, the SSP and LSP should consider launching promotion 
campaigns or furnishing subsidies to attract customers to the compatible channel to a reasonable degree. 
Second, managers should realize that network externality affects the LSP and SSP differently and should 
thus devise their operations strategies accordingly. More specifically, strong network externality 
strengthens the LSPŌĆÖs valuation and awareness advantages and reduces its willingness toward 
compatibility. Consequently, the SSP should exert extra effort to narrow the gaps between its awareness 
and valuation disadvantages and the LSPŌĆÖs advantages. Doing so would make collective betterment 
possible for both platforms via compatibility. Finally, offering add-on services generally enhances the 
LSPŌĆÖs willingness to implement compatibility. However, according Appendix C-1, under heterogeneous 
add-on services, the platform with a higher marginal benefit is less willing to implement compatibility. 
The implication is that the two platforms should offer homogeneous or slightly heterogeneous add-on 
services to achieve mutually beneficial platform compatibility. 
Although this study conducts a meaningful exploration of platform compatibility strategies under 
add-on services and network externality in a two-sided market, we note several promising opportunities 
for future research. On the one hand, we assume that the SSP sets the same price in the compatible 
channel and on its own platform. In fact, to attract more customers, many SSPs often differentiate their 
pricing between the two channels. Therefore, a worthy research topic is to study the situation of 
differentiated pricing in different channels. On the other hand, future research may apply the bargaining 
model to investigate the price decision in the compatible channel.
