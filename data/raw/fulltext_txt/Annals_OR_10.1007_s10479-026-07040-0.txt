Abstract
Thegrowingprevalenceofonlineretailinghasreshapedhowmarketplacesoperate.Thispaper
considers the case where a platform and a seller both receive signals (private information)
that correlate with uncertain demand and may choose whether to share this information.
The platform can adopt one of two commission schemes: ﬁxed (a given sum for each unit
sold) or proportional (a given percentage of the revenue). Speciﬁcally, this study deals with
pure strategies and focuses on comparing commission schemes under different information
scenarios (common information or information superiority of one of the parties). The main
ﬁndings are: (i) Under the ﬁxed commission scheme and information superiority of the seller,
the platform always prefers a more informative signal. (ii) Under the proportional commission
scheme, it is in the platform’s interest to share its private information with the seller and offer
the seller a constant percentage that does not depend on the platform’s signal. (iii) In contrast
to the proportional commission scheme, under ﬁxed commission, the seller will refuse to
play in some (private-signal determined) cases.
Keywords Supply-chain management · Online marketplace · Revenue-sharing contracts ·
Information sharing · Commission schemes

1 Introduction
In recent years, online marketplaces have surged in importance within global e-commerce.
These marketplaces now account for 53% of total e-commerce sales, equating to $2.8 trillion
in revenue as of 2023 (eMarketer, 2023a). The COVID-19 pandemic played a pivotal role in
accelerating the adoption of online marketplaces, which in turn reshaped consumer behavior
and business strategies. Optimistic forecasts project a 12% annual growth rate in marketplace-
driven e-commerce over the next ﬁve years (eMarketer, 2023b). Leading players such as
Amazon and Alibaba boast massive user bases (of 450 million and 755 million, respectively),
highlighting their global inﬂuence (Statista, 2023). Additionally, virtual-product market-
places, such as Apple’s App Store, continue to thrive. In 2022, the App Store witnessed a
staggering $45 billion in customer spending, reﬂecting a remarkable 35% year-over-year
increase (Apple, 2023). These trends afﬁrm the signiﬁcance of online marketplaces as a cor-
nerstone for businesses seeking to expand their digital sales and foster secure transactions in
an evolving e-commerce landscape.
Marketplaces and sellers commonly interact through consignment contracts with revenue
sharing, in which the seller owns the product until it is sold. Two commission schemes are
typically applied to these contracts: ﬁxed, whereby the platform charges the seller a ﬁxed sum
for each unit sold, and proportional, whereby the platform charges the seller a percentage of
the revenue from each unit sold (Avinadav & Levy, 2023; Perlman, 2023; Zhang et al., 2019).
Apple and Google charge their app developers revenue-based fees, whereas newspapers and
some credit card companies charge ﬁxed per-transaction fees (Armstrong, 2006). In this
study, we investigate a two-echelon supply chain consisting of a platform (which acts as a
marketplace) and a seller (who accesses customers via the platform). The aim is to compare
the beneﬁts and drawbacks of ﬁxed and proportional commission schemes.
In the framework under consideration, the seller and the marketplace operator sign a
contract prior to demand realization. Because each party is exposed to different information
sources, they may form different forecasts about demand. In other words, the availability of
multiple sources of information often leads to information asymmetry between supply-chain
members with regard to demand forecasting (Fu et al., 2016). Online marketplaces typically
have access to sales data that can support demand forecasting, whereas sellers tend to have a
broader understanding of the market and of upcoming trends (Gal-Or et al., 2008). Combining
information from multiple sources, rather than relying on a single source, can substantially
improve the accuracy of the demand forecast. Thus, information sharing between an online
marketplace and its sellers can signiﬁcantly affect the performance of the entire supply
chain (Chen, 2003; Shin & Tunca, 2010). Numerous theoretical studies have documented
the beneﬁts of information sharing among supply-chain members (see the reviews in Eksoz
et al., 2014 and Hill et al., 2018). In practice, information sharing in the mobile app industry
is facilitated through dedicated applications provided by the platforms, such as Google’s
Firebase1 and Apple’s App-Analytics,2 as well as through third-party apps like UXCam App
Analytics3 and Mixpanel.4 In this study, we incorporate the above considerations into our
modeling framework by examining the effect of commission scheme in online marketplaces
under various information scenarios.

In the remainder of this section, we summarize the study’s methods and ﬁndings. We
begin by describing the core concepts of the modeling framework:
Parties The supply chain consists of two parties, a single seller (denoted by subscript s),
who sells a product via a platform (denoted by subscript p).
Demand and quality cost The demand for the product is assumed to be linear in the
selling price p and product quality q. Speciﬁcally, the demand function is D(p, q) = a −
β p +αq, where a is the base demand, β is demand responsiveness to price, and α is demand
responsiveness to product quality. By redeﬁning the units of measurement of price and quality,
it may be assumed, without loss of generality, that α = β = 1 (a convention adopted
henceforth). The base demand a, unknown to the parties, is modeled as a non-negative
random variable a = a + ξ, where a is the mean demand and ξ is the uncertain component
of the demand, with mean zero and variance σ 2. In line with related studies in this ﬁeld
(see, e.g., Avinadav et al., 2014; El Ouardighi & Kim, 2010; Chernonog & Levy, 2023), the
cost of quality follows the quadratic function C(q) = γ q2, where γ is a scale coefﬁcient
representing the seller’s efﬁciency in creating quality (lower values of γ indicate higher
efﬁciency). This speciﬁcation enables mathematical tractability while incorporating the rule
of diminishing returns (Avinadav et al., 2017; Avinadav, 2019; Perlman, 2021).
Commission schemes The platform, as leader of the supply chain, either retains a com-
mission m from each item sold (ﬁxed commission scheme) or pays the seller a proportion η
of the price of each item sold (proportional commission scheme).
Signals We consider information asymmetry regarding demand and investigate whether
thepartiesshouldshareorwithholdtheirprivateinformation(signals).Speciﬁcally,eachparty
obtains a signal about a via market surveys or research. As noted, the platform’s signal f p and
the seller’s signal fs constitute private information, and each party’s decisions are based on the
information it holds. The joint distribution of the triple (a, f p, fs) is assumed to be common
knowledge. In contrast to the prevailing practice of assuming that the triple (a, f p, fs) is
trivariate normally distributed (where a may be negative), our approach uses conditional
expectation and martingale methodology to allow for arbitrarily distributed signals and non-
negative demand. Parametric assumptions are invoked for illustration purposes only. The
action taken by the platform, either m or η, is denoted by (f p).
Information scenarios Three information scenarios are considered:
(i)
Information superiority of the seller: The platform’s signal f p is a function of the
seller’s signal fs.
(ii)
Common information: The parties share the same signal—a scenario referred to in the
literature as “information sharing”.
(iii) Otherwise:Oneofthefollowingtwosub-scenariosapplies:(a)neithersignaldetermines
the other or (b) f p determines fs, but not vice versa.
In the case where private information is shared, the information is assumed to be truthful.
Mathematical remark These three seemingly clear-cut scenarios ignore the stochastic
nature of demand and signals. Formally, the scenarios should be characterized in terms of
the joint distribution of the triple (a, f p, fs), which is assumed to be agreed upon and known
to both parties. Speciﬁcally, depending on the scenario, the joint distribution takes one of
three forms. In the ﬁrst scenario, there is a function G such that P( f p = G( fs)) = 1. In the
second, the function G is invertible. In the third, there exists an event with positive probability
(deﬁned in terms of the signal fs), such that on this event, the conditional distribution of f p
given fs is not concentrated at a single point. Consequently, in the ﬁrst scenario, the seller
can infer the platform’s signal; in the second, each party can infer the other party’s signal;
and in the third, the seller cannot infer the platform’s signal with positive probability.

Results The main ﬁndings of this study are as follows:
• Under the proportional commission scheme, it is in the platform’s interest that the seller has
knowledge of the platform’s signal. Thus, the platform publicizes its signal and, at equi-
librium, offers the seller a constant share of the proﬁts (i.e., independent of the platform’s
signal).
• Under the ﬁxed commission scheme, in contrast to the proportional scheme, there are
instances (in terms of the platform’s stipulated commission and the seller’s private signal)
in which the seller refuses to play once the contract term has been announced.
• Under common information or information superiority of the seller, the ﬁxed commission
m is independent of the cost-of-quality parameter γ , and the proportional commission η
is independent of the platform’s signal.
By offering guidance to platforms and sellers on choosing a commission scheme under
various information scenarios, this study has the potential to advance both the theory and
practice of online-marketplace trading.
2 Literature review
Our study relates to three areas of the OM literature: (i) decisions in online marketplaces; (ii)
revenue-sharingcontracts,and(iii)informationsharing(referredtoas“commoninformation”
in the current study) with regard to demand. In what follows, we survey the literature for
each of these areas.
2.1 Decisions in online marketplaces
In recent years, the growing prevalence of online retailing has given rise to a novel
e-channel—the marketplace—where manufacturers can sell their products directly to con-
sumers by paying fees to e-tailers (Yan et al., 2018). Unlike conventional retailers,
marketplaces give sellers full control over prices and inventory (when physical inventory
exists), such that the consignment contract predominates in this setting (Bart et al., 2020). In
light of the growing importance of online marketplaces, more and more scholars are studying
supply-chain decisions in this framework.
Under the assumption of complete information, Avinadav et al., (2015a, 2015b) inves-
tigated the decisions of a platform and one or more app developers, where the developers
adopt varying risk proﬁles, including risk-averse and risk-seeking. The decisions related to
the app’s selling price and quality level, as well as to the commission rate charged by the plat-
form. Zhao et al. (2018) used a game-theoretic approach to study quality-disclosure strategies
for small business enterprises in a competitive marketplace. Shen et al. (2019) analyzed the
seller’s pricing decisions under three different scenarios: (i) distributing only through a plat-
form retailer, (ii) distributing only through a traditional retailer, and (iii) distributing via both
channels. Perlman (2022) examined pricing decisions and the corresponding market-share
outcomes in a duopolistic market with an e-tailer and a traditional retailer. Du et al. (2019)
studied the pricing decisions of two manufacturers competing in a common platform mar-
ket, who produce goods with different environmental performance. Zhang and Zhang (2020)
focused on pricing and selling-mode decisions in a two-echelon supply chain. Speciﬁcally, the
e-tailer chooses between agency selling and reselling, and also sets the information-sharing
policy (to share or not to share), while the supplier decides whether or not to establish a
brick-and-mortar store. Qin et al. (2020) analyzed the platform’s choice of selling mode

2.2 Revenue-sharing contracts
Platforms and sellers mostly interact via revenue-sharing contracts in which the commission
is either a proportion of the revenue from each unit sold or a ﬁxed fee for each sale (for a
comprehensive literature review, see Bart et al., 2020).
2.2.1 The ﬁxed commission scheme
Revenue sharing with a ﬁxed commission has been studied mainly in the context of con-
signment contracts with a consignment price. Ru and Wang (2010) analyzed a consignment
contract using a price-setting newsvendor model in which the supplier decides upon the con-
signment price he will charge the retailer for each unit sold, and subsequently, the retailer
chooses her retail price for selling the product. Cai et al. (2012) studied a revenue-sharing
contract with ﬁxed commission in a complementary-goods market. Hagiu and Wright (2015)
analyzed the decision making of an intermediary operator regarding whether to act as a
marketplace or as a reseller, where in the ﬁrst case, each supplier pays a ﬁxed fee to the
marketplace for each unit sold. Fang et al. (2016) investigated a consignment contract with
a consignment price in a supply chain consisting of two suppliers who sell their products
via a common retailer. Xiao and Xu (2018) developed a model for online retailing between
a platform and a seller, where the seller has superior information about demand. As a result,
the platform proposes a menu of ﬁxed-commission contracts. While these papers consider
the effect of quality on demand, they do not compare the ﬁxed commission scheme with the
proportional commission scheme.
Adida and Ratisoontorn (2011) studied a consignment contract with a consignment price
in a two-echelon supply chain with two competing retailers. They also studied a consign-
ment contract with revenue sharing (which we term a proportional commission scheme).
They found that, compared with the proportional scheme, the ﬁxed commission scheme
results in higher expected proﬁts for the two retailers and a higher retail price, whereas the
manufacturer’s expected proﬁt may either increase or decrease depending on the values of
demand parameters. Mai et al. (2017) compared ﬁxed and proportional commission schemes
in a two-echelon supply chain for extended warranties. They found that the retailer is better
off with the ﬁxed commission scheme, whereas the manufacturer is either better off with the
proportional commission scheme or is indifferent between the two. Perlman (2022) inves-
tigated whether a platform should offer the seller a proportional commission based on the
revenue or a ﬁxed commission for each unit sold. It was found that in most cases, a propor-
tional commission is preferable, as it yields higher proﬁt for the platform. A limitation of the
above three papers is that they do not consider the effect of quality on demand. In contrast,
we treat quality as a demand accelerator, consistent with its important and recognized role

account for information asymmetry—a situation that arises frequently in practice because
each party may acquire demand information from a different set of sources.
The two papers that are most closely related to the current study are Zhang et al. (2019)
and Avinadav and Levy (2023). The former studied the relationship between a platform’s
contract choice—proportional or ﬁxed commission—and the manufacturer’s decisions with
regard to product quality and pricing. The authors found that compared with the proportional
commission scheme, the ﬁxed commission scheme leads to a higher price, higher quality, and
a (weakly) smaller market size. They also found that the platform prefers the proportional
commission scheme when customer heterogeneity is high and the ﬁxed commission scheme
when customer heterogeneity is low; for medium heterogeneity, a win-win situation for both
the platform and the manufacturer is possible under the ﬁxed commission scheme. Our paper
differs from Zhang et al. (2019) by focusing on information asymmetry and information
exchange rather than assuming complete information. Avinadav and Levy (2023) compared
the two commission schemes in a supply chain of subscription-based apps where the platform
knows the exact demand while the developer has only probabilistic knowledge. The authors
showed that it is more beneﬁcial for the platform to interact via the ﬁxed-fee contract than
via the commission-rate contract, whereas neither contract dominates from the developer’s
or supply chain’s perspective. The study assumed that the platform decides whether or not
to share its information with the developer only after learning the exact demand (in common
with Avinadav et al., 2019 and Avinadav & Levy, 2022). In contrast, in the current study,
we assume that this decision must be made a priori, that is, before each party receives its
signal (in common with Mishra et al., 2007, 2009; Zhu et al., 2011; Yan & Pei, 2015; Bian
et al., 2016; Jha et al., 2017; and Avinadav et al., 2022). Additional differences between the
current study and Avinadav and Levy (2023) include the way in which uncertainty is taken
into account in the model (additive versus multiplicative) and whether the follower has the
ability to infer the leader’s private information from the leader’s action.
2.2.2 The proportional commission scheme
We now review papers in which the proportional commission scheme is applied in a supply
chain under information asymmetry. Gerchak and Khmelnitsky (2003) analyzed a case in
which the supplier is less informed than the retailer about demand and, over time, updates his
beliefs about the demand distribution based on the retailer’s sales reports. Hou et al. (2009)
and Zhao et al. (2016) studied settings in which the downstream party has partial information
about the upstream party’s production cost and inventory holding cost, respectively. Wang
et al. (2012) investigated a supply chain in which the manufacturer (the leader) only has prob-
abilistic knowledge about the demand and the retail cost, whereas the retailer, who is closer
to the market, knows both variables exactly. Kong et al. (2013) considered a supplier-leader
who interacts with both an incumbent and an entrant retailer, where the incumbent retailer
is better informed about demand. Avinadav et al. (2019) studied the voluntary disclosure
strategy of a manufacturer who has private information about demand and whose informa-
tion status is not necessarily transparent to the retailer. Three papers assume that sales are
unobservable to the supplier: Heese and Kemahlioglu-Ziya (2014, 2016) studied this scenario
under a revenue-sharing contract with a proportional commission fee, whereas Gerchak et al.
(2007) studied it under a consignment contract with a proportional commission fee assum-
ing information asymmetry with regard to the demand and production cost. Avinadav et al.
(2020) designed a menu of revenue-sharing contracts for a platform that is less informed

2.3 Information sharing with regard to demand
The following papers examine scenarios in which the demand prediction is updated by
acquiring signals. Yao et al. (2005) and Yue and Liu (2006) investigated information sharing
in the case where sales are carried out through a combination of a traditional retail channel and
a direct channel. Mishra et al. (2007) analyzed incentives for inﬂating or deﬂating forecasts
when sharing demand information, and showed that bargaining-based contracts can eliminate
incentives for parties to engage in such information distortion. A subsequent study by Mishra
et al. (2009) examined the incentives of a manufacturer and a retailer to share their demand
signals. They found that a side-payment contract cannot induce a Pareto-optimal information-
sharing equilibrium, but this can be achieved using a discount-based wholesale-price contract.
Zhu et al. (2011) considered a manufacturer who sells a product to a retailer in a single selling
season, under the assumption that greater forecasting accuracy is associated with a higher
cost. Bian et al. (2016) studied bilateral information sharing between the retailer and the
manufacturer in each of two competing supply chains, and found that sharing demand signals
voluntarily beneﬁts the manufacturer but hurts the retailer. An important difference between
our paper and the above papers is that the latter disregard the case in which the manufacturer
sets the wholesale price too high, causing demand to be negative even when the selling price
equals the wholesale price such that the retailer refuses to play.5 This omission allows those
studies to obtain closed-form solutions in the no-information-sharing case. Our study, by
contrast, does not allow for negative demand and shows how negative estimated proﬁts may
bring the seller to decline the contract. Moreover, while the aforementioned papers assume
that the parties interact via a wholesale-price contract, we study revenue-sharing contracts
(witheitherproportionalorﬁxedcommission),whicharemoreprevalentinrealmarketplaces.
To our knowledge, only Zhang and Zhang (2020) and Wang et al. (2020) combined the
use of a revenue-sharing contract with the ability to update the demand forecast. Zhang
and Zhang (2020) investigated whether an e-tailer should share her demand information
with a supplier who has the option to sell via an ofﬂine channel, where the parties interact
via a revenue-sharing contract. In their model, both parties are initially aware that the base
demand follows a speciﬁc uniform distribution, while at a later stage, the e-tailer reveals the
true value of the base demand. Wang et al. (2020) analyzed a three-echelon supply chain
consisting of a manufacturer, a reseller, and a platform, where the platform acts as both
marketplace and online seller. The reseller and the platform interact via a revenue-sharing

2.4 Inadequate handling of Nash equilibria under the case of no information
sharing
In addition to the gaps in the literature identiﬁed in the previous subsections (relating to
the scope of the model), there is also a methodological gap in how equilibrium is handled
in Stackelberg games with private signals. The seller maximizes, with respect to price and
quality, some function of the signal fs and the leader’s action ( f p), under the standard
assumption that the action function  is common knowledge when deriving the equilibrium
solution. The seller’s estimated proﬁt depends on E[a| fs, ( f p)]. This conditional expec-
tation may equal E[a| fs, f p], may equal E[a| fs], or may take a more complicated form in
which ( f p) provides the seller with partial information about f p, where this information
is not contained in fs. For instance, if  is U-shaped, the seller can infer two possible values
of f p with some distribution.
In Stackelberg games between a retailer and a manufacturer with private signals, it has
become common in the literature (including Avinadav et al., 2022) to set up conjectures (in
the terminology of the current study) regarding whether E[a| fs, ( f p)] is E[a| fs, f p] or
E[a| fs], with the intention of disproving one of these conjectures and accepting the other as
proved by contradiction. However, ignoring the third, more general possibility – that ( f p)
provides the seller with partial information about f p – invalidates this method. Consequently,
identifying a dominant Nash equilibrium under private signals remains an open question. In
general, there is no known method for determining equilibrium behavior when both parties
possess private information. However, in this paper, we show that under the proportional
commission scheme, the problem can be partially solved – a unique Nash equilibrium in
pure strategies can be identiﬁed under certain conditions (Subsections 4.2 and 4.3). For
the case of ﬁxed commission, a unique Nash equilibrium can be identiﬁed under common
information (Subsection 5.2), and all Nash equilibria (unique or multiple) can be identiﬁed
under information superiority of the seller (Subsection 5.1).

Leader and follower Following standard practice in online marketplaces (Avinadav et al.,
2020, 2021; Chernonog, 2024), the relationship between the parties in the supply chain is
modeled as a sequential non-cooperative game in which the platform is the leader and the
seller is the follower. If the seller were to set the price and quality as the leader, then the
platform would be able to keep all the revenue; therefore, this structure is infeasible and is
not considered further in this paper.
Fixed and proportional commission schemes The platform announces the commission
scheme – either ﬁxed or proportional. Under the ﬁxed commission scheme, the platform
keeps an amount m from each unit sold and earns proﬁt m(a −p +q), which leads to a proﬁt
for the seller of (p −m)(a −p + q) −γ q2. Under the proportional commission scheme,
the platform keeps a fraction 1 −η of the selling price and earns proﬁt (1 −η)p(a −p + q),
which leads to a seller proﬁt of ηp(a −p + q) −γ q2.
Private and common information In this study, special attention is paid to the scenario of
information superiority of the seller in which f p is a function of fs and E[a| f p, fs] = E[a| fs]
a.s. Such a scenario may occur in real life when the platform’s signal is obtained from a
public source, such as analysts’ announcements, whereas the seller obtains an additional
private signal, for example, by conducting a customer survey. Another possible mechanism
for this scenario is that the platform discloses f p to the seller ex-ante; as will be shown,
such disclosure is in the platform’s interest under the proportional commission scheme.
Under the scenarios of common information and information superiority of the seller, the
seller’s posterior distribution of a is unaffected by the platform’s action. In contrast, under the
third scenario mentioned in the Introduction (neither common information nor information
superiority of the seller), the conditional distribution of a given fs may (and typically will)
differ from the conditional distribution of a given fs and ( f p).
Transaction dynamics The platform and the seller receive their signals, f p and fs, and
share them if this strategy was agreed upon. The platform then announces the contract term
(m or η), and the seller responds by either declining the contract or setting the quality level
q and selling price p of the product. Finally, the demand is realized.

8 Conclusions
In this work, we studied two commission schemes, ﬁxed and proportional, for a revenue-
sharing contract between a seller and a platform. Because each party receives its own private
signal, we analyze each commission scheme under various information scenarios. The anal-
ysis of the proportional commission scheme applies to the case where, a priori, there is
no common information, but as it turns out, it is in the platform’s interest to share all of its
information with the seller. For the ﬁxed commission scheme, for the reason given in Subsec-
tion 2.4, the analysis is restricted to the case where the platform’s information is available,
a priori, to the seller (Scenarios (i) and (ii)). When the parties have common information
(Scenario (ii)), the following managerial insights (see Corollary 2) are derived: (i) The plat-
form, which is the leader of the supply chain, prefers the ﬁxed commission scheme; (ii) From
the seller’s perspective, when its efﬁciency in creating quality is low (i.e., when γ is high),
the seller prefers the proportional commission scheme to the ﬁxed commission scheme, and
vice versa; (iii) The ﬁxed commission scheme always outperforms the proportional commis-
sion scheme in terms of total channel proﬁt. The numerical example of the Gaussian case
(Figure 3(a)) illustrates that the ﬁxed commission scheme may outperform the proportional
commission scheme in terms of the platform’s expected proﬁt, under Scenarios (i) and (ii)
(the two scenarios yield very similar expected proﬁts for the platform).
A notable innovation of this study—relevant when the platform does not have access to the
seller’s information—is that the model allows for the possibility that the seller will decline
the contract when the platform’s ﬁxed commission fee results in negative estimated proﬁt.
This refusal, which can only occur in the ﬁxed commission scheme, leads to situations where
unique or multiple Nash equilibria may arise.
We propose two directions for future research: (i) extending the analysis to supply chains
with horizontal competition, where multiple sellers and/or platforms operate on the market;
and (ii) considering the production-capacity decisions of the seller.

Conditional expectation as a martingale. If F1 is a sub-σ-ﬁeld of F2, then the pair

X1, X2, ... is a martingale, then (if integrable) ψ(X1), ψ(X2), . . . is a submartingale.
