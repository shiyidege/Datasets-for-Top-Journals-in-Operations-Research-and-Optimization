Abstract
Heart disease remains the leading cause of death in the United States. In this study, we
examined the socio-economic effects on heart-disease related deaths among the elderly in
New Jersey across three time periods: before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic.
We developed statistical learning models to analyze the impact of socio-economic factors on
heart disease deaths in three age groups: 65-74, 75-84, and 85 and older. The variables we
considered included food and health resources, poverty rate, urbanization levels, and popu-
lation density. Our ﬁndings indicate that socio-economic factors signiﬁcantly inﬂuence heart
disease mortality among the elderly. Speciﬁcally, we observed that the death rate decreases
with an increasing number of pharmacies and ﬁtness centers, while it rises with a greater
number of fast-food restaurants. Additionally, these effects vary among the different age
groups across the three study periods. This study could inform public health policies aimed
at reducing mortality due to heart disease.
Keywords Heart disease · COVID-19 · Socio-economic factors · Statistical learning
1 Introduction
Heart disease continues to be the leading cause of death in the United States(Tsao et al. 2022);
it accounts for approximately 22% of all U.S. deaths in 2023 (Centers for Disease Control
and Prevention 2025). It has consistently ranked as the top cause of mortality in New Jersey
over the past two decades (New Jersey Department of Health 2023). Given this persistent
burden, it is crucial to enhance research on potential risk factors associated with heart disease
in order to reduce both morbidity and mortality.
Elderly individuals aged 65 and older are a signiﬁcant focus for understanding the risk
factors associated with heart disease mortality. It is essential to examine the elements that
contribute to heart disease in this age group. These factors can be categorized into self-
regulated physical factors and externally inﬂuenced environmental factors (Noale et al. 2020).
Physical risk factors include hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, and smoking. These
conditions are often worsened by aging, poor dietary choices, and irregular lifestyles. On the
other hand, environmental factors involve the level of social connectivity and the overall social
environment available to older adults (Noale et al. 2020). Higher levels of social connectivity

The socio-economic status of older adults, which is deﬁned by factors such as ﬁnancial
resources, education level, and professional reputation, plays a crucial role in health outcomes
(Krieger 2001). Research shows that individuals from lower socio-economic backgrounds
are more likely to live in environments that contribute to disease risk (Khalatbari-Soltani et
al. 2020). This exposure to unfavorable environmental conditions can lead to higher mor-
tality rates from heart disease. Urbanization signiﬁcantly impacts heart disease mortality
rates. Populations in rural areas consistently experience signiﬁcantly higher cardiovascular
mortality rates than their urban counterparts. This phenomenon was characterized in public
health literature as the “rural mortality penalty” (James 2014; Cosby et al. 2019). Marinacci
et al. (2010) examined that age-adjusted cardiovascular mortality rates for more than 11 mil-
lion U.S. adults, identiﬁed a profound divergence in cardiovascular health outcomes between
rural and urban populations over the last decade. It was identiﬁed that cardiovascular deaths
rose nationwide after 2019 and the mortality rate increase was signiﬁcantly larger in rural
populations compared to urban populations.
The COVID-19 pandemic has had a profound impact on public health worldwide since
its emergence in late 2019. Research has connected the pandemic to a signiﬁcant increase in
cardiovascular fatalities (Chaganty et al. 2023; Song et al. 2024; Marinacci et al. 2010). Some
researchershaveconductedcausalstudiestoinvestigatethemechanismsbehindincreasedcar-
diovascular mortality during the COVID-19 pandemic (Hilser et al. 2024; Pham et al. 2025;
Halvorsen et al. 2023; Shi et al. 2020). Additionally, during the pandemic, the economic
downturn and its collateral effects severely impacted the implementation of healthcare pro-
grams. This situation further weakened healthcare coverage for low socio-economic groups,
which indirectly contributed to an increase in the mortality rate from heart disease (Gopalan
and Misra 2020).
This paper examines the impact of environmental factors and the COVID-19 pandemic
on heart disease mortality among older adults (aged 65 and above) in New Jersey. It analyzes
three distinct periods: before, during, and after the COVID-19 pandemic. The socio-economic
factors explored in this study include access to food and health facilities, poverty rates, urban-
ization levels, and population density. Speciﬁcally, the food and health facilities considered
encompass the number of fast-food restaurants, non-fast-food grocery stores, pharmacies,
ﬁtness centers, nursing homes, and hospitals. The ﬁndings of this research can provide valu-
able insights for public health policies aimed at reducing heart disease mortality among the
elderly population in New Jersey and potentially in other regions with similar socio-economic
contexts.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the data source, explana-
tory variables, preprocessing, outcome variable, and statistical analysis. Section 3 presents
the results of the regression analysis. Finally, Section 4 discusses the ﬁndings and concludes
the paper.

2 Methods
2.1 Data
2.1.1 Data source
Our data collection spans from March 2017 to August 2025, divided into three distinct
periods:
Period 1 (Before COVID): March 2017 to February 2020
Period 2 (During COVID): March 2020 to May 2022
Period 3 (After COVID): June 2022 to August 2025
We selected three years before the COVID-19 pandemic to ensure we have sufﬁcient data to
accurately represent normal conditions prior to the outbreak. The post-COVID period ends
in August 2025, as that is the latest data available. The start and end dates for the COVID-19
period were chosen based on signiﬁcant events: the U.S. declared a national emergency due
to COVID-19 on March 13, 2020 (Federal Emergency Management Agency 2020), and the
U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) announced the conclusion of the
COVID-19 public health emergency on May 11, 2022 (U.S. Department of Health & Human
Services 2022).
We collected age-grouped monthly heart disease death data for each county in New Jersey
from New Jersey Department of Health (1996b). The ICD-10 codes for heart disease include
I00-I09, I11, I13, and I20-I51. The age groups examined in this study are under 5, 5-14,
15-24, 25-34, 35-44, 45-54, 55-64, 65-74, 75-84, and 85 and above. However, this paper
focuses speciﬁcally on the age groups 65-74, 75-84, and 85 and above, as the heart disease
death rate is very low for the other age groups. Additionally, we collected county-level total
population and poverty population data from the U.S. Census (Bureau 2018). For urban data,
we gathered information on the proportion of urban area to total land area in each county
(Stacker 2022). We also obtained the number of hospitals in each county, including compre-
hensive rehabilitation hospitals and general acute care hospitals (New Jersey Department of
Health 1996a).
The data used in this paper regarding county-level access to food and health facilities
includesthenumberoffast-foodrestaurants,non-fast-foodstores,grocerystores,pharmacies,
and ﬁtness centers. For both Period 1 and Period 2, we utilized the food and health facilities
data from (Amaratunga et al. 2022), which was collected in 2020. The same method, speciﬁ-
cally querying the Yelp Places API (https://docs.developer.yelp.com/docs/places-intro), was
used to gather the data for Period 3 in 2023.

2.1.3 Preprocessing
Preprocessing is needed because the explanatory variables are highly correlated, as shown
in Figure 3 in Appendix A. Because the numbers of food and health facilities are highly
relavant to population, we divide each facility variable by population and multiply it by
100,000 to remove the effect, i.e., X ji/Pi ∗100, 000, and denote them as x ji for simplicity
( j = 1, 2, · · · , 7 and i = 1, 2, · · · , 21). The new scatter plot after population adjustment
is shown in Figure 4, which is better than that before adjustment. However, the distribu-
tions of some explanatory variables are still highly skewed, for example, grocery stores and
non-fast-food stores. Hence, we employ data transformation to ensure the variable distri-
bution satisﬁes the assumptions of linear regression. For each explanatory variable, we try
different transformations, including power transformation, logarithmic transformation, and
inverse transformation. We choose the one that makes the variable distribution closest to a
normal distribution based on the Q-Q plot. The transformations for ﬁtness, nursing home,
hospital, urban level, poverty rate, and population are logarithmic transformations. The trans-
formations for non-fast-food stores, grocery, and pharmacy are inverse transformations. The
transformation for fast food is power transformation with power 1. The scatter plot after
population adjustment and transformation is shown in Figure 5 in the Appendix, which looks
much better. We employed the processing procedures for all three periods and all three age
groups.
2.1.4 Outcome variable
The outcome variable in this study was the daily crude death rate due to heart disease for
the three age groups (65-74, 75-84, and 85+) in each county in New Jersey across the three
periods. Since the death data were collected monthly, we ﬁrst calculate the total deaths in
each period for each county and age group by summing the monthly death data. Then we
calculated the average daily death in each period by dividing the total deaths by the number
of days in that period. Finally, to remove the effect of population, we calculated the death
rate per 100,000 people by dividing the average daily deaths by the total population of that
age group in that county, then multiplying by 100,000. The formula is as follows.

TheregressionresultsforthethreeagegroupsbasedonModelIandIIareshowninTables1,2,
and 3 respectively. The results were similar in the two models across the three age groups. For
age group 65-74, Table 1 shows that Urban level and Poverty rate were both highly signiﬁcant
across the three periods. The coefﬁcient of Urban level was negative, indicating that a higher
urban level was associated with a lower death rate due to heart disease. Conversely, the
coefﬁcient of Poverty rate was positive, suggesting that higher poverty rate was linked to
higher death rate. In the model with period effects (Model II), Urban level and Poverty
rate remained signiﬁcant with similar coefﬁcients (p-value< 0.001). Fast-food was also
signiﬁcant with positive coefﬁcient, indicating that a larger number of fast-food restaurants
was associated with a higher death rate due to heart disease in these periods. Contrarily, non-
fast-food was signiﬁcant with a negative coefﬁcient (p-value< 0.05), indicating that a larger
number of grocery stores was associated with a lower death rate due to heart disease. Effect
of Pharmacy was also negative and signiﬁcant. The coefﬁcients of Period 1 and Period 3 were
both negative (p-value< 0.05), indicating that the death rates in these periods were lower
compared to Period 2 (the reference COVID period). However, there were no interaction
terms coming out as signiﬁcant in the Model II, which means the effects of socio-economic
factors did not vary across the three periods.
Table 2 shows the regression results for age group 75-84. Similar to those of age group
65-74, in Model I Urban level was very signiﬁcant across the three periods with similar coef-
ﬁcients. However, in Period 2, the Poverty rate didn’t come out, while more socio-economic
factors did, including Fast-food, Pharmacy, and Fitness center, and Nursing home. The coef-
ﬁcient of Fast-food was positive, indicating the positive effect of Fast-food on death rate.
The coefﬁcient of Nursing home was also positive, indicating that a higher number of nurs-
ing homes was associated with a higher death rate due to heart disease. Conversely, the
coefﬁcients of Pharmacy and Fitness center were negative, indicating that a higher number
of pharmacies and ﬁtness centers were associated with a lower death rate due to heart dis-
ease. The different effects of the socio-economic factors across the three periods were also
investigated in Model II, where the interaction terms of Fast-food and Period, Pharmacy and
Period emerged in the stepwise selection result. This indicated that the effects of Fast-food
and Nursing home on death rate varied across the three periods. Additionally, the coefﬁcient
of Period 3 was negative and signiﬁcant (p−value<0.001), indicating that the death rates in
Period 3 were lower compared to Period 2 (the reference period). However, the coefﬁcient of
Period 1 was not signiﬁcant, indicating that the death rates in Period 1 were similar to those
in Period 2.

4 Discussions and conclusions
The socio-economic effects on heart disease deaths in New Jersey for three periods (before,
during, and after COVID) and three age groups (65-74, 75-84, and 85 and above) were inves-
tigated in this study. The results showed that socio-economic factors signiﬁcantly impacted
elderly heart disease death across the three periods. For example, the death rate decreased
with the number of pharmacies, ﬁtness centers, and hospitals but increased with the number
of fast-food restaurants and nursing home. Furthermore, the effects of some socio-economic
factors varied across the three periods for age group 75-84. For instance, the effects of Phar-
macy and Fast-food were signiﬁcant in Period 3 but not in Period 1 and 2.
Researches have found that urbanization can have a protective effect on cardiovascular
health (Marinacci et al. 2010; Cosby et al. 2019). Different from the existing research, our
study focused on the elderly population and investigated the effect of urbanization using
statistical learning models with urban level as a continuous variable, which provided a more
detailed understanding of the relationship between urbanization and heart disease mortality
among the elderly population. Urban areas may provide better access to healthcare facili-
ties, healthier food options, and opportunities for physical activity, which can contribute to
improved cardiovascular health among the elderly population. However, it is important to note
that urbanization can also have negative effects on health outcomes, such as more fast-food
restaurants, which can increase the risk of heart disease. Also, there would be more potential
exposure to Covid patients due to higher population density in urban areas. Therefore, it is

It is worth noting that Nursing home and Population were signiﬁcant for age group 85
and above, but not for the other two younger age groups. The positive correlation observed
between the number of nursing homes and the heart disease death rate for individuals aged
85 and above may be attributed to the fact that those in this age group are more likely to have
severe health conditions and require long-term care. This increased need for care can elevate
their risk of mortality from heart disease. Furthermore, the positive association between
population size and heart disease death rates for the 85-and-above age group may result
from higher population numbers leading to increased competition for healthcare resources.
This heightened competition can negatively affect the quality of care for elderly individuals
suffering from heart disease.
To further investigate the effects of the death rate and the COVID-19 pandemic, we
conducted a regression analysis using variables selected by stepwise regression for Model 2,
excluding the period variable. Figure 2 shows the boxplot of residuals in the three periods
for each age group. It can be observed that after removing the socio-economic effects, the
death rate in Period 2 is the highest among the three periods for age groups 65-74 and 75-84,
which is more pronounced than the original boxplot in Figure 1 (a-b). This funding suggests
that additional factors during the COVID-19 pandemic may have contributed to the increased
death rate for these two age groups. For instance, the pandemic may have caused delays in
seeking medical care, disruptions in healthcare services, and heightened stress levels, all of
which could have led to higher mortality rates from heart disease during this time. For age
group 85 and above, the death rate of the three periods was decreasing over time, which was
similar to the original boxplot in Figure 1 (c). The difference among the three periods for
all the three age groups became more signiﬁcant after removing the socio-economic effects,
indicating that socio-economic factors had a moderating effect on the increased death rate
during the COVID period.
This study has several limitations. First, the study focused exclusively on New Jersey,
which may limit how widely the ﬁndings can be applied to other areas with different socio-
economic and healthcare contexts. Future research should include multiple states or regions to
improve the relevance and applicability of the results. Second, the study took several socio-
economic factors into account; however, it did not consider other potential confounding
variables, such as lifestyle behaviors (e.g., smoking and physical activity) and environmental
factors (e.g., air pollution). Future research should aim to include a wider range of variables
to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the factors affecting heart disease death rates.
