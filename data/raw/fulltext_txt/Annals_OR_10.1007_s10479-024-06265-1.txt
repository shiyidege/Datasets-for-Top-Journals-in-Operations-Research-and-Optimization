Abstract
Standard solutions for cooperative transferable utility (TU-) games assign to every player
in a TU-game a real number representing the player’s payoff. In this paper, we introduce
interval solutions for TU-games which assign to every player in a game a payoff interval.
Even when the worths of coalitions are known, it might be that the individual payoff of
a player is not known. According to an interval solution, every player knows at least a
lower- and upper bound for its individual payoff. Therefore, interval solutions are useful
when there is uncertainty about the payoff allocation even when the worths that can be
earned by coalitions are known. Speciﬁcally, we consider two interval generalizations of
the famous Shapley value that are based on marginal contributions in terms of intervals.
To determine these marginal interval contributions, we apply the subtraction operator of
Moore. We provide axiomatizations for the class of totally positive TU-games. We also show
how these axiomatizations can be used to extend any linear TU-game solution to an interval
solution. Finally, we illustrate these interval solutions by applying them to sequencing games.

1 Introduction
Standard solutions for TU-games assign to every TU-game a payoff vector. However, if there
is uncertainty about the payoff allocation then we cannot just assign a speciﬁc payoff to
every player. Therefore, in this paper we introduce interval solutions for TU-games which
assign to every totally positive TU-game, being games such that all Harsanyi dividends are
nonnegative as introduced by Vasil’ev (1975), a vector of payoff intervals. Although players
do not know their payoff, they can be assigned a minimal and a maximal payoff they can earn.
The uncertainty comes in by assigning to each player a compact interval with the minimal
and maximal payoff as lower, respetively upper bound of this interval.
To deﬁne this type of solution, we ﬁrst transform a TU-game into an interval game (assign-
ing to every coalition of players an interval worth) and then apply a solution for interval games
that extends the Shapley value for TU-games. Speciﬁcally, to every coalition we assign the
interval worth that has its original worth as lower bound, and its worth in the dual game as
upper bound. In game theoretic tradition, the worth of a coalition is deﬁned by a pessimistic
approach in the sense that its worth is what the coalition can guarantee irrespective of the
behavior of the players outside the coalition. On the other hand, the worth in the dual game
equals the worth of the grand coalition minus the worth of the complement coalition in the
‘pessimistic’ game, and thus can be seen as an optimistic view on the worth of the coalition
in the sense that a coalition earns what is left from the worth of the grand coalition after the
complement coalition receives its guaranteed amount. In our approach, we put in uncertainty
in the game by considering the game and its dual to deﬁne the interval worths, so the lower
and upper bounds of the interval worths are determined by a pessimistic, respectively opti-
mistic, view on what coalitions can earn. Applying a solution for interval games to this game
with uncertainty yields an interval solution for totally positive TU-games.
Cooperative interval games are ﬁrst introduced by Alparslan Gök et al. (2009b) where
the classical theory of two-person cooperative games is extended to two-person cooperative
games with interval uncertainty. Moreover, the core, dominance core and stable sets are
extended to the interval setting in Alparslan Gök et al. (2011). The interval Shapley value is
characterized with the aid of the properties of additivity, efﬁciency, symmetry and dummy
player in Alparslan Gök et al. (2010). In Alparslan Gök (2014) the interval Shapley value
is characterizated by using the properties of efﬁciency, symmetry and strong monotonicity.
Moreover, they provide another characterization by using the interval dividends.
Since the solution we propose uses marginal vectors of the interval game, we need to
apply a difference operator on intervals. In the literature, such difference operators exist, for
example that of Moore (1979) and Alparslan Gök et al. (2009a). It turns out that we cannot
apply the subtraction of Alparslan Gök et al. (2009a). Applying Moore’s subtraction, we
can deﬁne an interval solution for TU-games and we provide an axiomatization. Moore’s
subtraction is also used by Han et al. (2012). However, we argue that their indifference
relation is not suitable when exploring uncertainty in TU-games.1
We provide axiomatizations for the class of totally positive TU-games. Many games
derived from situations in Economics or Operations Research (OR) are totally positive. For

2.1 TU-games
An n-person game in characteristic function form (or shortly TU-game) is a pair (N, v)
where N = {1, 2, ..., n} is the set of players and v : 2N →R is a characteristic function,

2.2 Interval games and interval calculus
Next, we recall some preliminaries from interval calculus and the theory of cooperative
interval games as discussed in Alparslan Gök et al. (2009a, 2010, 2009b).
A cooperative interval game is an ordered pair (N, w) where N = {1, 2, . . . , n} is the set
of players, and w : 2N →I(R), where I(R) is the set of all nonempty, compact intervals in R,

where w(S) is the minimal worth which coalition S could receive on its own, and w(S) is
the maximal worth which coalition S could get. The family of all interval games with player

3 Two interval solutions for TU-games: deﬁnitions and axiomatizations
Until so far, the literature studies interval solutions for interval games, and point solutions
for classical TU-games. In this paper, we consider interval solutions for totally positive
TU-games being mappings f : GN
+ →I(R)N assigning to every totally positive TU-game
an interval payoff vector. These interval payoffs reﬂect uncertainty in the payoffs to the
individual players although there is no uncertainty about the worths of coalitions.

5 An application: Sequencing games
In this section, we illustrate the two extensions of the Shapley value, that are introduced and
axiomatized in Section 3, by a sequencing game. Sequencing games are introduced in Curiel
et al. (1989). They consider a class of one-machine sequencing situations. A (one-machine)
sequencing situation consists of a set of jobs that have to be served on a single machine. Each
job has a waiting cost and a processing time. The jobs are ordered exogenously in an initial
queue. Generally, cost savings are possible if the jobs reorder themselves into an efﬁcient
queue, i.e. a queue with minimal total cost over all jobs, where the cost of a job depends on its
total waiting plus processing time. For constant unit waiting cost, it is well known that a queue
is efﬁcient if the jobs are ordered by non-increasing urgency index, where the urgency index
of a job is its waiting cost divided by its processing time. This reordering in an efﬁcient queue
brings welfare gains/cost savings, and a main question is how to allocate these cost savings
over the jobs. Such an allocation can be obtained by applying solutions to the associated
sequencing game where the jobs are the players and the characteristic function assigns to
every coalition of consecutive jobs the maximal cost saving they can obtain by reordering
only jobs within the coalition, and assigns to other coalitions the sum of the worths of its
maximally consecutive subcoalitions. In case of constant unit costs, the associated games are
totally positive.
We illustrate our interval solutions with a speciﬁc example of a sequencing situation with
three jobs, so consider the set of players/jobs N = {1, 2, 3}. Suppose that they are ordered in
the initial queue σ 0 (1) = 1, σ 0 (2) = 2, and σ 0 (3) = 3, where σ 0(i) gives job i’s position
in the queue, so the queue is 1, 2, 3. Further, suppose that their processing times are given by
the vector p = (7, 3, 5), and their unit waiting cost are given by the vector c = (10, 20, 30) .
The associated urgency indices are given by the vector u = ( 10
7 , 20
3 , 30
5 ), and thus the

6 Concluding remarks
In this paper, interval solutions are obtained for totally positive TU-games. In the literature,
there exist classical solutions for classical TU-games and interval solutions for cooperative
interval games in literature. Different from the existing literature, we have constructed interval
solutions for classical TU-games.
First of all, from classical TU-games, by choosing the coalition value as the lower bound
and the dual coalition value as the upper bound, we have constructed cooperative interval
games. Using these interval games, we have obtained two types of interval Shapley values
and provided axiomatizations. Using the axioms, we also provided a method how to extend
any linear TU-game solution, such as the Banzhaf value, into an interval solution by chosing
an appropriate efﬁciency axiom. Finally, we gave an illustration by applying these interval
solutions to sequencing games. We argued that in our study the special subtraction operator
of Alparslan Gök et al. (2009a), that is often used for cooperative interval games, cannot be
applied here. Therefore, we used Moore’s subtraction operator (Moore, 1979).
Interval solutions for TU-games reﬂect uncertainty about the payoff allocations in situ-
ations where there is no uncertainty in the worths of coalitions. This uncertainty can have
several reasons. One reason can be that there are externalities from payoff allocations. For
example, if a player prefers egalitarian outcomes, then large payoff differences among players
might lead to a negative externality for this player. On the other hand, a ‘competitive’ player
might derive positive externalities from a payoff allocation where it earns more than other
players. When the utility assigned to payoff allocations is not known, this might be modeled
by interval payoffs.14
Notice that our interval solutions allow for negative lower bounds, even when a player is a
veto player. Speciﬁcally, when considering the unanimity game uN of the grand coalition, all
players are veto players and f (uN) and f (uN) coincide in that case and both assign to every
player the interval [ 2−n
n , 1]. The negative lower bounds are, in some sense, the counterpart
of all players taking as possibility that they, as veto player, get the full worth uN(N) = 1.
This is reﬂected by veto upper bound which requires that the upper bound for all players is
uN(N) = 1. With midpoint efﬁciency and symmetry it then follows that the lower bounds
must be 2−n

that a veto player considers the possibility to get the full worth, the lower bound reﬂects that,
if other veto players claim the full worth, it might be that additional worth must be generated,
for example by taxation, and a player might have a negative payoff. If this is not acceptable
then one could require a Nonnegativity axiom stating that the lower bound for veto players

Since, as mentioned above, in this ﬁrst paper on interval solutions for TU-games we want
to focus on how to deal with uncertainty as reﬂected by the length of the interval payoffs,
we required midpoint efﬁciency so that ‘under the veil of ignorance’ (i.e. taking a uniform
distribution over the interval), the expected payoffs add up to v(N). In the future, we want to
extend this study to solutions where both the length and the sum of expected payoffs (sum of
midpoints) are not known. Then weaker versions of efﬁciency can be considered, such as the
axiom that requires that at least v(N) belongs to the sum of the individual interval payoffs.16
For future studies, we want to extend our research to other OR games by using Moore’s
subtraction operator. Similarly, we plan to introduce different linear interval solutions for
classical TU-games. Also, a plan for future research is to extend the axiomatizations to a larger
class of TU-games for which the solutions are deﬁned, such as the class of superadditive TU-
games. Notice that a characterization on the class of superadditive games requires a different
type of proof since positive linearity cannot be used directly for games with negative Harsanyi
dividends.
