Abstract
Optimizing costs and proﬁts while meeting customer demand is a critical challenge in the
development of perishable supply chains. Customer-centric demand forecasting addresses
this challenge by considering customer characteristics when determining inventory levels.
This study proposes a solution framework comprising two steps: (a) segmentation using
customer characteristics and (b) demand forecasting for each segment using transparent
and responsible artiﬁcial intelligence techniques. We employed k-means, hierarchical clus-
tering, and explainable AI (XAI) to segment, model, and compare several machine-learning
techniques for demand forecasting. The results showed that support vector regression outper-
formed the autoregressive models. The results also showed that the two-step segmentation and
demand forecasting process using hierarchical clustering and LSTM outperforms (Weighted
average RMSE across segments = 61.57) the conventional single-step unsegmented fore-
casting process (RMSE overall data = 238.18). The main implication of this study is the
demonstration of XAI in enhancing transparency in machine learning and an improved
method for reducing forecasting errors in practice, which can strengthen the supply chain
resilience for perishable products.

1 Introduction
A supply chain is a network of interconnected operations that produce consumer-beneﬁcial
products or services (Faruquee et al., 2021). Purchasing raw materials, work-in-progress
(involving intermediate forms of ﬁnished items), and distributing ﬁnished goods to end users
comprise the operations involved (Ali & Govindan, 2021). Across the supply chain, there
should be a proper ﬂow of items and information from consumers to suppliers, and vice versa,
to meet the requirement of delivering the correct set of products in the correct quantity and
at the correct time to the correct customer (Oluwaseyi et al., 2017).
Inventory management is one of the most important supply chain tasks and presents
an opportunity to reduce expenses. Therefore, focusing on this work is essential to enhance
supply chain performance (Creazza et al., 2021; Faruquee et al., 2021). With the emergence of
Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) in recent years, there have been enormous opportunities to deploy
such technologies in inventory management (Sharma et al., 2020). Inventory management
is an ongoing inventory planning, organization, and control process to minimize inventory
investment and balance supply and demand (Singh & Verma, 2018).

Inventory management and demand forecasting are closely related because it is essential
to comprehend demand to maintain optimal inventory levels. Demand forecasting is a set of
methods and procedures used to estimate the future demand for a product or service so that
producers can plan the quantity of raw materials to order and manufacture, and retailers can
maintain optimal stock levels (Ali & Govindan, 2021). The rise in digitalization has led to
the production of vast quantities of data. The pattern analysis of data can aid in predicting
future sales. Demand forecasting is crucial in inventory management. This determines a
ﬁrm’s proﬁtability and competitive position. Predicting sales is the most important factor in
determining retailers’ inventory levels and order quantities (Kazancoglu et al., 2021; Kumar
& Singh, 2021).
The term Fast-Moving Consumer Goods (FMCG) refers to products that are inexpensive
and quickly sold. Because consumer purchasing behavior is highly erratic, it is challenging
to predict future sales in this industry (Fosso Wamba et al., 2022; Grover et al., 2022). The
FMCG market is the fourth-largest market segment in India, with primary products in three
categories: household and personal care, healthcare, and food and beverages (FMCG). In
India, revenues have increased by 24.4% over the past decade. The rural market for this
sector is anticipated to grow at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 14.6% and reach
$220 billion by 2025. Because perishable products have a short shelf life, they are classiﬁed
as FMCG. They require specialized transportation, are extremely sensitive to temperature
changes, and require special maintenance; therefore, their inventory management becomes
more complicated (Ali & Govindan, 2021).
The global wine market comprises three categories: still, sparkling, and fortiﬁed wine.
It is the least widely consumed alcoholic beverage in the world. In 2019, wine accounted
for 23% of the alcoholic beverage market and generated worldwide revenue of USD 378.3
billion. From 2012 to 2025, wine income is projected to increase at a CAGR of 5.6%, while
sales volume is projected at a CAGR of 0.8% (Elluru et al., 2019; Katsaliaki et al., 2021).
As a result of the COVID-19 pandemic, the estimated wine production decreased by 14.2%.
In 2019, wine was predicted to generate the highest revenue among all wine categories,
accounting for 85% of the wine segment’s revenue. In 2020, it was found that the United
States, France, and Italy consumed the most wine, while Italy, France, Spain, and the United
States were identiﬁed as the largest wine-producing countries (Zhu, Krikke, Kumar, & Singh,
2021; Kazancoglu et al., 2021).
Owing to price-conscious consumers and a previous ban, wine consumption is lower in
India than in other countries. While India has 123,000 acres of vineyards (as of December
2018), only 1–2% are utilized for wine production. The primary wine-producing regions are
Nashik, Hampi Hills, Pune, Bijapur, Northern Karnataka, and Bangalore, with Nashik being
India’s wine capital (Kazancoglu et al., 2021). Between 2012 and 2013, the wine wholesale
index increased by nearly 18%. In 2014, the quantity of imported wine steadily increased. In
2018, India imported approximately 5.2 million liters of wine, worth approximately $27.5
million. Companies in India may decrease their reliance on imports by improving their
understanding of the market and using that knowledge in demand estimation to build resilient
domestic supply chains (Zhu, Krikke, Kumar, & Singh, 2021).
The literature review identiﬁes the following knowledge gaps: (a) Existing ML models
lack explainability (Huber et al., 2020; Ali et al., 2009). Incorporating explainable AI (XAI)
enhances transparency and trust, aligning it with responsible AI in operations research. (b)
Most studies used single-stage models without segmentation (Huber et al., 2020; Tonbul,
2019). A two-stage approach can improve accuracy and provide granular insights. (c) Previ-
ous studies (Ali et al., 2009; Bala, 2012) used limited attributes for customer segmentation. It

is recommended that all available attributes are employed to enhance transparency and effec-
tiveness. (d) Many studies focused on groceries and perishable goods (Ali et al., 2009; Bala,

The major research questions are as follows: (1) Can transparent machine learning (ML)
techniques be employed for customer-centric demand forecasting in perishable product sup-
ply chains? (2) Does ML enhance forecasting accuracy compared with traditional techniques?

RO1: Hierarchical clustering is performed based on customer characteristics and XAI

RO2: Estimate wine consumption using machine-learning techniques for each identiﬁed

RO3: Compare the efﬁcacy of machine-learning techniques with conventional forecasting

2 Literature review
A company’s inventory management policy can either increase proﬁts or result in losses.
This essential task involves several crucial considerations, particularly concerning perishable
goods. As the life expectancy of items decreases, they become more fragile, increasing chal-
lenges in their management. This study employed data from the wine industry. This section
elaborately describes the winemaking process and concerns about perishable products.
2.1 Studies on perishable supply chains
Perishable supply chains refer to transporting and storing goods with limited shelf lives,
such as fresh produce and dairy products. A study conducted by Cornell University offers
insights into reducing food loss and waste in perishable supply chains (Tata-Cornell Institute,
2023). The study highlights that access to fresh fruits and vegetables is limited in developing
countries such as India due to food loss and waste, among other factors. Another study
examined supply chain design strategies for fresh produce, speciﬁcally using melons and
sweet corn as examples (Blackburn & Scudder, 2009). This study explores the challenges

and opportunities associated with transporting and storing perishable products and provides
recommendations for improving supply chain efﬁciency.
A recent study posited that competition among supply chain networks for perishable
products has grown more intense because of the COVID-19 pandemic (Shaﬁee et al., 2022).
This study provides critical insights for managers and decision-makers to mitigate the risks
associated with perishable product supply chain networks in the current climate. The search
results revealed recent studies on perishable supply chains. One study investigated the impact
of the COVID-19 pandemic on perishable food supply chains across the globe and aimed
to enhance their resilience (Shanker et al., 2022). Another study scrutinized the factors that
inﬂuence perishable goods supply chains and emphasized the signiﬁcance of effective time
management in transporting perishable goods (Sharma et al., 2022). Moreover, a recent study
in the United Kingdom examined the consequences of supply chain traceability and visibility
on supply chain performance, sustainable practices, and velocity (Perishable Goods Supply
Chain, 2023).
Overall, these studies highlight the importance of efﬁcient supply chain management for
perishable products to minimize food waste, increase access to fresh produce, and prevent
disruptions to supply chains during unforeseen events such as pandemics. Our research
examines supply chain design strategies for fresh produce, speciﬁcally using grapes and
utilizing them to produce wine as an example of a perishable product. Wine is a grape-based,
alcoholic beverage. There are numerous varieties of wine, most of which are made from
grapes. Various types of wine are based on the grapes used, the environment in which they
are grown, and the winemaking process. Three types of wine are red, white, and rose (Gupta
et al., 2022).
The wine supply chain comprises four stages: harvest, manufacturing, packaging, and
distribution. Researchers have studied optimizing these stages to fulﬁll wine orders at the
lowest possible cost (Basso et al., 2023). Material shortages and supply chain challenges have
affected the wine industry. Wineries and wine brands have reported shortages in packaging,
production materials, and logistics headaches when trying to get their products to the market.
This has led to increased costs and delays in the supply chain (Forbes, 2023). According
to supply chain analysts, the schedule reliability of global ocean liners hit an all-time low
in August 2021, which has caused shipping delays in the wine industry (Winemag, 2021).
Overall, recent research on wine supply chains has focused on optimizing the different stages
of the supply chain and addressing the challenges related to inventory management, material
shortages, and shipping delays. These studies are important to ensure that the wine industry
continues to operate efﬁciently and effectively.
2.2 Resilient inventory management of perishable product supply chains
Inventory management of perishable goods is complex because of their deterioration or
spoilage, which can cause health problems if consumed later. Fruits and vegetables, meat,
poultry, and other foods require proper storage to prevent the growth of bacteria, which can
cause spoilage (Zhu & Krikke, 2020; Kumar & Singh, 2021; Kazancoglu et al., 2021). They
must be refrigerated at temperatures as low as 40 °F or frozen at 0 °F or lower, necessitating
expensive equipment, substantial initial investment, and substantial maintenance expenses
(Yavari & Zaker, 2019, 2020; Kumar et al., 2021).
The transportation of perishable goods is challenging. For instance, once fruits and veg-
etables are harvested, they ripen by consuming atmospheric oxygen and quickly perish. The
respiration rate must be controlled without causing harm to maintain the proper texture,

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 354:103–142

appearance, and ﬂavor of fruits and vegetables (Zhang et al., 2021). It is essential to ﬁnd
appropriate packaging based on the type of fruit or vegetable and to determine the composi-
tion of respiratory gases to keep fruits and vegetables fresh until they reach the end-user or
a factory where they will be further processed (Yavari & Zaker, 2020).
The COVID-19 pandemic has signiﬁcantly disrupted the operation of perishable sup-
ply chains and exposed the limitations of demand forecasting. Speciﬁcally, the following
issues have arisen: (a) Disruptions: The imposition of lockdowns and travel restrictions has
resulted in a shortage of labor, transportation delays, and border closures, which have neg-
atively affected the transportation of perishable goods. This, in turn, leads to commodity
spoilage and inventory shortages. (b) Fluctuating Demand: Localized incidents of panic buy-
ing and stockpiling have caused unpredictable spikes in demand, followed by a sharp decline.
Conventional forecasting models are inadequate for handling erratic consumer behavior. (c)
Difﬁculties in Forecasting: The sudden and unforeseeable nature of COVID-19 presents chal-
lenges in accurately forecasting the demand. Conventional models have not yet been designed
to address such signiﬁcant disruptions (Masudin et al., 2021; Kumar et al., 2022; Ghafour
et al., 2023).
2.3 AI applications for resilient perishable supply chains
Researchers now have vast amounts of data that allow them to comprehend market dynamics
and develop more accurate methods for predicting demand and enhancing the supply chain
value (Kumar et al., 2021). Owing to the vast amount of data currently available, AI and ML
techniques can be used to improve supply chains. Some of AI’s most crucial supply chain
outcomes are demand forecasting, risk and resilience management, transportation, supplier
selection, and inventory management (Riah et al., 2021). Table 1 summarizes some research
on inventory management and demand forecasting.
Inventory management is divided into three categories: (1) raw materials, (2) work in
progress, and (3) ﬁnished goods ready for shipment. Given the number of actors involved
in each stage, it is essential to establish coordination and deﬁne appropriate supply chain
policies (Gligor et al., 2019; Modgil et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021). This topic relies heavily
on demand forecasting and has unrealized potential.
2.4 Explainable AI
There is a growing concern that, as the underlying AI models have become increasingly com-
plex and powerful, they have also become a “black box” lacking comprehensibility (Meske
et al., 2022). A comprehensible model is necessary to deem AI models fair and trustwor-
thy and foster their usage (Abdul et al., 2018). The ﬁeld of XAI has undergone signiﬁcant
development over the years. Arrieta et al. (2020) comprehensively reviewed the XAI and
discussed related concepts, taxonomies, opportunities, and challenges. Similarly, Adadi and
Berrada (2018) conducted a comprehensive XAI survey to explore various techniques and
methods. Here, we summarize the need for XAI.
AI techniques have evolved from straightforward and interpretable methods, such as linear
models, to increasingly intricate and sophisticated approaches, such as ensembles and deep
learning. This progression led to an increase in the complexity and opacity of the models.
Opaque models such as DL have consistently demonstrated better performance. Hence, there
has been a continuous effort to enhance the interpretability of these opaque models. This
pursuit of XAI aims to ensure that the model’s inner workings are clear to decision-makers.

For instance, in the context of this study, decision-makers may seek to comprehend the
factors that distinguish clusters (segments). Without this understanding, they may hesitate
to apply forecasting techniques at the segment level. We chose XAI because it allows us to
use black-box models for demand forecasting, which are superior to white-box models in
performance.
Dwivedi et al. (2022) compared and contrasted XAI feature-based XAI techniques: feature
importance, partial dependence plots, individual conditional expectation, global surrogate,
LIME, and Shapley values. These techniques differ in the extent to which they are globally or
locally interpretable, model-speciﬁc, or model-agnostic. Global interpretable techniques ana-
lyze the overall decision-making process of a model, whereas local interpretable techniques
analyze the decision-making process at an instance level. Model-speciﬁc interpretation tech-
niques can only work with speciﬁc ML or CL algorithms; model-agnostic techniques do not
have this disadvantage. Among the aforementioned feature-based XAI techniques, Shapley
values have the advantage of being globally interpretable and model-agnostic. We explain
the difference between the Shapley values and LIME in detail in Sect. 4.4.
A few applications of XAI are as follows. Abdollahi and Nasraoui (2018) discussed the
importance of transparency in fair machine learning, speciﬁcally in the context of explainable
recommendation systems. Different approaches have been proposed to achieve explainability
in AI models. Angelov and Soares (2020) introduced the concept of explainable deep neu-
ral networks (xDNN), which aimed to provide interpretable representations in deep learning
models. Al-Shedivat et al. (2020) presented contextual explanation networks, which aimed to
capture the dependencies among explanations. Samek et al. (2019) explored the interpretabil-
ity of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models, speciﬁcally focusing on explaining and
interpreting their behavior. These studies highlight the diversity of methods employed to
achieve explainability in AI.
AI techniques have gained signiﬁcance in the demand forecasting of perishable product
supply chains. A literature review by Mediavilla et al. (2022) analyzed various artiﬁcial
intelligence methods for demand forecasting in supply chain management, including machine
learning and deep learning. Although not speciﬁc to perishable product supply chains, this
review explains how AI techniques can be applied to demand forecasting in various domains.
While the literature on the application of ML to solve supply chain problems is abundant,
there is a paucity of research concerning the incorporation of XAI. Further research is required
to bridge this gap and address the unique challenges of demand forecasting for perishable
products. We address this concern by adopting XAI techniques in this study.
2.5 Research gaps
Huber et al. (2020) investigated the bakery chain case and projected daily demand for var-
ious product categories in each store. In this study, Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN) and
decision trees were among the models employed. The results demonstrate that Machine
Learning (ML) methods are suitable for forecasting because of their superior performance
and capacity to handle larger-scale demands. It was found that classiﬁcation techniques sur-
passed regression techniques in forecasting, which is a noteworthy observation. According
to Ali et al. (2009), the perishable nature of groceries makes demand forecasting particularly
challenging, necessitating advanced models. This study examines demand forecasting during
sales in a European supermarket. The study revealed that pooling data enhanced the perfor-
mance of the model. An interesting ﬁnding was that a simple time-series method worked well
in the absence of promotions; however, a more complex method, such as regression trees
123
Annals of Operations Research (2025) 354:103–142
111

with appropriate features, is required for accurate predictions when promotions are available
(Ivanov, 2021; Liu & Lee, 2018; Mishra et al., 2021).
Following these early studies, ML techniques are generally superior to traditional
approaches because of their ability to adapt to changing patterns, model complex and nonlin-
ear associations, versatility in handling structured and unstructured data, high-dimensional
data, and feature engineering abilities. Hence, we chose ML techniques for the forecasting.
Our research employs machine learning techniques but distinguishes itself from existing
ML-based forecasting studies in several ways. Most prior studies have not adopted a two-
stage approach or incorporated XAI. For instance, Ali et al. (2009) utilized regression trees for
demand forecasting, whereas Praveen et al. (2019) applied artiﬁcial neural networks (ANN).
Lolli et al. (2018) employed support vector machines (SVMs), and Huber et al. (2020) and
Tanizaki (2019) explored multiple ML techniques. Tian et al. (2021) implemented Markov
Chains. However, none of these studies considered customer-centric segmentation, as we
have done. Bala (2012) used segmentation followed by demand forecasting. However, unlike
Bala (2012), who used ﬁve attributes based on rule-based classiﬁcation, our approach is
more ﬂexible and uses all available attributes to create segments. Our approach is lenient and
enhances the likelihood of identifying naturally occurring customer segments.
Following clustering, we also increased comprehensibility and conﬁdence by using XAI
to determine the characteristics that differentiate the segments. Furthermore, we also consid-
ered forecasting techniques such as the support vector method (SVM), vector autoregressive
(VAR), and long short-term memory networks (LSTM). Another difference is that the context
of the product in our case is wine, which varies in consumption patterns from the grocery
products considered in Bala (2012). The contributions of this study are as follows.
• This study proposes a solution framework for perishable supply chains that segments
customers based on their characteristics and uses transparent and responsible artiﬁcial
intelligence techniques for demand forecasting.
• We employed k-means, hierarchical clustering, and XAI for segmentation and modeling
and compared several machine learning techniques for demand forecasting.
• This study demonstrated the use of XAI to enhance transparency in machine learning
and improve forecasting accuracy, which can enhance the resilience of supply chains for
perishable products.
• The main implication of this study is the demonstration of XAI in enhancing transparency
in ML, and an improved method for reducing forecasting errors in practice, which can
enhance the resilience of supply chains for perishable products.
3 Problem statement
Perishable product supply chains are characterized by distinct challenges compared to non-
perishable products, necessitating stricter management practices throughout the process
(Bharti, 202). These challenges can be summarized as follows. (a) Perishability: The lim-
ited shelf life of perishable products mandates precise control over storage, transportation,
and handling to minimize spoilage. This requires careful planning, coordination, and risk-
management strategies (Osman et al., 2023). (b) Temperature sensitivity: Many perishables
require speciﬁc temperature ranges during storage and transportation to maintain quality.
Deviations can result in rapid spoilage and signiﬁcant waste generation (Vrat et al., 2018).
(c) Demand variability: The demand for fresh produce can ﬂuctuate signiﬁcantly depending
on seasonality, weather patterns, and consumer preferences. This unpredictability makes it

challenging to optimize inventory management compared with non-perishables with more
stable demand (Ali et al., 2021). (d) Quality Deterioration: Perishable foods are sensitive
to time and temperature, and quality deterioration increases with increased shipping times.
Demand unpredictability exacerbates this challenge (Taparia et al. 2020).

Inventory management is a crucial aspect of the perishable supply chain; stockouts reduce
customer satisfaction, which results in low revenues, whereas overstocking increases inven-
tory holding costs, which increases inventory costs. Inventory management is closely related
to the demand for products or services. As such, it depends on the precision of the demand
forecasting (Ali & Govindan, 2021). Optimizing inventory levels from beginning to end is
essential for increasing the value of the entire supply chain. Owing to stockouts and the lead
time associated with stock replenishment, retailers frequently struggle to provide customers
with desired products at the right time, negatively affecting customer satisfaction (Ivanov,
2021; Liu & Lee, 2018; Mishra et al., 2021).
Although India produces large quantities of grapes annually, its wine industry is still in
its infancy. Because wine’s primary raw material is grapes, a perishable product, inventory
management becomes even more challenging. As wine companies are new, if they invest
heavily in inventory, they will lose money, and the industry will perish. Therefore, it is
crucial for the company to understand the demand for wine based on the size of its customer
base and to produce sufﬁcient quantities each year to meet those demands. As most of
the wine consumed in India is imported, the industry must embrace technology to achieve
revolutionary growth.
In the conventional method, sales from previous years are used to predict sales for the
next year, increase or decrease production, and plan for raw materials. Unaddressed is the
fact that sales are dependent on customers and that there are customer segments whose
characteristics may affect product sales, thereby inﬂuencing the inventory levels that the
company must maintain. The raw materials used to produce wine are various varieties of
grapes, and the suppliers are farmers and vineyard owners. After harvesting and producing
wine, manufacturers collect the grapes and distribute them to retailers.
4 Solution approach
This section describes the procedure and steps for implementing the model. Figure 2 summa-
rizes the methods used in this study. Initially, extensive research was conducted to identify
the issue of inventory management in perishable products. In this dataset, our primary raw

produced is determined by the quantity sold. Figure 1 illustrates the relationship between the
demand for raw materials and consumer purchases. We then test the robustness of the gen-

from the surrogate model via XAI techniques. Forecasting techniques are then used to predict
the demand for each cluster. The proposed method aligns with standard frameworks such
as CRISP-DM, which suggests sequence – business understanding, data understanding, data
preparation, model development, model evaluation, and deployment. In our approach, we
developed and validated two models: clustering and forecasting, as shown in Fig. 2 (Wang

4.4 XAI
Section 2.4 describes the literature that highlights the need for transparency and interpretabil-
ity in machine learning (Abdollahi & Nasraoui, 2018; Abdul et al., 2018; Meske et al., 2022).
In this study, clustering techniques were used to determine customer segments. However, we
do not know (a) whether the segments are robust and (b) their distinguishing features. This
is because, while clustering techniques are not entirely black-box, they are not as amenable
to interpretation as white-box-supervised models. The role of XAI in this study is to address
these issues by building interpretable surrogate models. Surrogate models are usually white-
box techniques such as linear regression, logistic regression, classiﬁcation, and decision trees.
We choose an ensemble method, namely random forest, as it performs better as a surrogate
model and is interpretable using several XAI techniques. The contribution of XAI is that it
lends credibility to this study by rendering the ML approach transparent.
The XAI process was operationalized in two steps. The ﬁrst step involves building and
evaluating a random-forest-based surrogate classiﬁcation model using the same features as
the clustering model. The clustering process is deemed robust if the random forest model,
as developed, shows high accuracy. The second step involves XAI methods, such as feature

values is that the Shapley value provides both local and global explanations, while LIME
provides only a local explanation (Dwivedi et al., 2023). Global explanation means that
Shapley values can provide insights into the importance of features across the entire dataset,
unlike LIME, which can provide valid interpretations at an instance level. This helps us to
understand the feature’s importance and why an instance’s feature values lead to a speciﬁc
outcome. Unlike LIME, which is less formalized, SHAP has a strong foundation in coopera-
tive game theory (Angelov et al. 2021). LIME interpretations are based on the perturbation of
the input data and may be unreliable if such perturbations are not carefully chosen (Angelov
et al., 2021).
Another method, namely the relative importance method, is speciﬁc to tree-based meth-
ods, and it ranks feature contributions by averaging their contribution to improving the
homogeneity of nodes across several trees. We used three methods for global explanation:
SHAPKernelExplainer (a model-agnostic method), SHAPTreeExplainer (a tree model-
speciﬁc method), and Relative Importance in determining the features with the highest
discriminator power. Using three different approaches for XAI helps us assess whether the
top-ranking features appear consistently across all the approaches.

recorded at regular intervals, with data related to only 662 days indicating sales over three

5.1 k-means clustering
K-means clustering is a partitioning-based approach for clustering. This method generates
and assigns data points to k clusters using the following steps:
Step 1: Starting with a given value of k
Step 2: Randomly identify k points as centroids.
Step 3: Each point is assigned to the centroid closest to it.
Step 4: Recalculating the centroids of k clusters.
Step 5: Repeat Steps 3 and 4 until the centroids do not change in successive iterations.
We applied k-means clustering to the data obtained after standardization. A scree plot
was used to determine the optimal number of clusters. Using a scree plot, we determined
the locations where we achieved the lowest within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS) with the
fewest clusters. We found that the WCSS did not drop appreciably beyond these ﬁve clusters.
We obtained four clusters using k = 4 and analyzed them based on the average age, average
number of children, average education, average family size, average income, and having
children. This process is repeated for k = 5. Table 7 shows a few characteristics of the four
clusters identiﬁed using k-means.

5.4.2 Autoregression (AR) model
AR models assume autocorrelation between the data in the initial and current timestamps.
In the AR models, we used the previous values of the independent variable to predict its
future values and referred to the variables involved as lag variables. The number of terms
in the regression model increased with the number of lags. For simplicity, this model was
constructed using only MntWines, which is the amount spent on wine. We determined the lag
valuestobetwofromtheautocorrelationplotandthenobtainedthemodel.Theautoregression
model obtained is as follows: Li,i = 1,2 lag of 1 and 2, respectively, Mntwines = 35.094843
+ 0.222446* MntWines.L1 + 0.272481* MntWines.L2. The AR model has an RMSE of
43.464, 179.008, 181.506, and 92.666 for Clusters 0, 1, 2, and 3, respectively, and the overall
RMSE was 259.776 in the test dataset.
5.4.3 Vector autoregression (VAR)
While AR deals with a single time series, it can handle two or more time series that inﬂuence
each other. Christopher Sims introduced VAR and used it to model causal relationships
among macroeconomic variables. In VAR, the target variable depends not only on its previous
values but also on the previous values of other variables. We ﬁrst determined the suitability
of the data for VAR and lag order using the Granger causality test. After identifying the
variables that inﬂuence MntWines, we performed cointegration testing to determine whether
the variables had a signiﬁcant relationship. We applied the VAR model to train data after a
test for stationarity (by Augmented Dickey-Fuller test) and identity lag order by selecting
the model with the lowest AIC. The RMSE values for clusters 0, 1, 2, and 3 and the overall
data are 40.681, 173.397, 184.361, 92.573, and 232.069, respectively.

SVR is a regression technique that uses the same principle as SVMs. SVRs are among the
most potent ML techniques for regression, and their advantage is that they are not bound

5.4.5 Univariate LSTM
LSTMs are a Recurrent Neural Network type of RNN used in the context of time-series data.
LSTMs have speciﬁc memory cells in their architecture that store long-term dependencies

typically observed in DNNs used for sequential data. LSTMs also exhibit architectural varia-

Furthermore, the LTSM architecture involves several hyperparameters, and we use the grid-

This study extends the literature on the application of AI to inventory-management problems.
Our study reinforces the application of ML to inventory management problems and is in line

This study has several practical implications. The ﬁrst implication for managers is that

may be accomplished using the most suitable technique. The implication is that considering

ing and forecasting, such as autoencoders and reinforcement learning, future studies may
consider them.

and learn from the results. We used the classiﬁcation model to evaluate and conclude that the
segments from the clustering were robust (classiﬁcation metrics > 90%). Feature importance
and Shapley values are explained and learned by extracting the features that contribute to
distinguishing the segments, thus justifying clustering. Managers must adopt XAI to foster
trust and AI interventions.

Forecasting the demand is signiﬁcant for perishable goods that cannot be consumed after

The wine industry is complex and requires a deep understanding of consumer preferences,

The wine industry encompasses the entire grape cultivation process, wine production,
distribution, and marketing. Consumer preferences play a crucial role in this industry, with
factors such as (a) demographics, age, income, and cultural background; (b) tastes and pref-
erences, including preferences for grape variety, sweetness level, and region; and (c) evolving
trends, including sustainability and ethical sourcing (Fazio et al., 2023; Volpe et al., 2010).

Wine demand forecasting is complex for several reasons, including (a) seasonality, with
sales often surging during holidays and on speciﬁc occasions; (b) vintage variation and
weather conditions signiﬁcantly affecting grape quality and overall vintage appeal (Volpe
et al., 2010); (c) economic factors, with economic downturns leading to decreased spending
on non-essential items such as wine; (d) new product launches, with the introduction of
new varieties or brands disrupting established preferences; and (e) consumer behavior, with
consumer tastes and preferences being complicated and inﬂuenced by various factors such as
marketing and critical reviews. These factors make it challenging to predict future demand
accurately (Fazio et al., 2023).
Consumer behavior in the wine sector is complex and multifaceted, contributing to the
difﬁculty in accurate demand forecasting. This complexity arises from (a) subjective prefer-
ences, with wine appreciation being subjective and inﬂuenced by emotional and social aspects
beyond price and quality; (b) evolving preferences, with consumer preference shifting owing
to exposure to new wines, trends, or recommendations; and (c) external inﬂuences, with unex-
pected events, such as weather disasters or economic crises, signiﬁcantly impacting consumer
buying behavior. Therefore, wine demand forecasting requires a nuanced approach that con-
siders historical sales data and qualitative factors affecting consumer preferences (Mehta &
Bhanja, 2017; Stanco et al., 2020).

than traditional forecasting techniques, which rely solely on sales data and place limited
emphasis on customers. This research focuses on the wine industry, which has received little
attention, particularly in India, and is still in its early stages. However, this study can also
be applied to other industries that produce perishable goods. Furthermore, we demonstrated
a method to incorporate XAI as part of the machine learning process, which can easily
be adopted in any supply chain problem requiring AI intervention. We adopted three XAI
techniques: (a) conducting exploratory data analysis and employing dimensionality reduction
to improve data explainability; (b) using feature importance for feature-based explainability;
and (c) employing kernelShap for example-based explainability to better understand data,
features, and segment characteristics. The outcomes conﬁrm the effectiveness of employing
segment-speciﬁc forecasting and encouraging XAI adoption. Future investigations could
explore other XAI techniques, such as counterfactuals, anchors, and contrastive explanation
methods (Dwivedi et al., 2023).

7.1 Conclusions
This paper presents a framework for optimizing inventory levels and reducing forecasting
errors in perishable supply chains in two steps: customer segmentation using k-means, hier-
archical clustering, and XAI techniques, followed by demand forecasting for each segment
using transparent AI methods. LSTM outperforms autoregressive models and support vector

regression for demand forecasting. The two-step segmentation and demand forecasting pro-
cesses surpassed the traditional methods that did not incorporate segmentation. This study
underscores the use of XAI to enhance transparency in machine learning and improve sup-
ply chain resilience for perishable products. Speciﬁcally, the framework involves customer
segmentation and demand forecasting using XAI techniques, employing k-means cluster-
ing, hierarchical clustering, and XAI to compare machine learning methods for demand
forecasting. The results indicate that hierarchical clustering and LSTM in a two-step process
outperformed conventional unsegmented forecasting. This approach demonstrates XAI’s role
of XAI in improving transparency and reducing forecasting errors, thereby enhancing the
resilience of perishable product supply chains, notably in the Indian wine industry.
The primary novelty of this study is the application of XAI to increase machine learning
transparency and minimize forecasting errors in real-world scenarios, which is especially
important for Operations Research (OR) applications that require explainability and trust in
decision-making. Enhanced demand forecasting can improve the resilience of supply chains

7.2 Limitations and directions for future research
The proposed method presupposes that the sales data contain inherent customer segments.
This study is limited because customer data are required to perform a major task: identifying
customer segments. However, most businesses lack access to this data. Second, the data we
used were from 2015, and the segment characteristics could have changed over the years. The
algorithms used for customer segmentation and demand forecasting may vary depending on
the dataset. Obtaining this information is particularly challenging in developing countries like
India, where most perishable product purchases are made in small brick-and-mortar stores.
Furthermore, forecasting techniques require data collection at regular intervals. However, our
research began with real-world data to create customer segments and realistic data to meet
forecasting requirements. The results underscore the importance of embracing and examining
digital technologies across various domains for enhanced information collection (e.g., IoT),
dissemination (e.g., pervasive networks), processing (e.g., cloud computing and big data),
management (e.g., AI), and security (e.g., blockchain).
Based on our ﬁndings, further research on supply chain resilience is necessary. Small-
and medium-sized enterprises’ perishable product supply chains are critical to the global
economy’s survival, but they face enormous challenges due to the nature of their operations.
Given the importance of perishable product supply chains in sustaining fresh food supply and
the global economy, future research could simultaneously focus on developing and imple-
menting resilient supply chain designs and recovery strategies. The impact of disruptions on

Appendix B: Description of techniques used Hierarchical clustering
Clustering methods belong to the category of unsupervised machine learning. Clustering aims to identify
patterns and group the data using its features. Based on their approach, clustering techniques can be classiﬁed
into partitioning, hierarchical, density, and grid-based methods. This study used k-means (partition method)
and hierarchical clustering (hierarchical method). Partitioning algorithms divide the given “n” data points into
“k” partitions or, in other words, “k” clusters. The objects are clustered such that the objects in a cluster are
similar and dissimilar to objects in other clusters. Hierarchical clustering algorithms create top-down or bottom-
up decompositions of data points. In the top-down approach, a data cluster is progressively decomposed into
smaller clusters, whereas, in the bottom-up approach, individual data points are progressively agglomerated
into a larger cluster. As shown in Appendix A, agglomerative clustering was used in the conﬁguration.
Random forest
Random forests belong to the class of ensemble models used in machine learning. Ensemble models use a
set of weak learners to build a robust model. A random forest is an ensemble of multiple decision trees, where

each decision tree is trained on a randomly chosen subset of data features. The outcomes of the decision trees
were aggregated to arrive at the outcome. The Random Forest model used in this study had a maximum depth
of 11 and 500 estimators.
LSTM
LSTMs is a recurrent neural network (RNN) class that uses memory cells to handle long-term dependen-
cies. LSTM consists of input, output, and forget gates to selectively retain, modify, and discard long-term
information, respectively. The LSTM processes the inputs sequentially at each time step, and the operations
are as follows:

and current inputs. The forget gate vector ft is given by ft = σ(Wf·[ht−1,xt] + bf), where σ, Wf, xt, ht−1, and
bf are the sigmoid activation function, weight matrix of the forget gate, current input, previous hidden state,
and bias term of the forget gate.
Input Gate: Determine the information to update the current cell state. It determines the input gate vector
it and the candidate cell state. 

SVR
Support Vector Machines belong to the family of boundary separation methods used in machine learning.
SVMs can be used for regression (SVR) and classiﬁcation (SVC). SVR aims to identify the optimal hyperplane
within threshold m for any given y. SVR can be applied to linear and nonlinear regressions. SVR supports
nonlinear regression through popular kernel functions, such as RBF, Polynomial, and Sigmoid. In this study,
we applied the RNF kernel while implementing the SVR.
Autoregression
Autoregression (AR) means the regression of a variable measured at different periods. This difference
in period is termed the lag. The equation of AR with lag p, i.e., AR(p), can be represented as Yt+1 =
β1Yt + β2Yt−1 + ...+βpYt−p+1 + ε
t+1. The number of lags was identiﬁed from the partial autocorrelation
plots. In this study, we used an Auto Regression Model with lag 2.
Vector autoregression
Unlike Autoregression, A VAR model uses multiple time series variables. Each variable in the sys-
tem was regressed on past values of the variables in the system. We modeled the VAR with the following
variables: MntWines, MntTotal, NumWebVisitsMonth, NumPurchases, Age, Children, Family_Size, Educa-
tion_Graduate, Education_Postgraduate, Living_With_Alone, and Living_With_Partner.
Feature importance of using shap
Shapley valued quantity as the impact of a coalition of players in a cooperative game. In machine learning,
the model is a coalition of features and the output is the payoff. The Shap values calculate the difference between
the actual and average predicted outputs for different coalitions with and without a feature. The difference
provides the marginal contribution of a feature across all coalitions (subsets of features). A positive/negative
Shap value indicates that the feature positively/negatively affects the prediction. The magnitude of the Shap
value indicates the strength of the impact of a feature.
