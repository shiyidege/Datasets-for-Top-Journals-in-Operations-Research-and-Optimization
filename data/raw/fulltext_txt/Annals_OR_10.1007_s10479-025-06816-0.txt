Abstract
Japan is facing relatively high costs in its transformation from an energy structure domi­
nated by fossil energy to one dominated by renewable energy, which is necessary to 
achieve Sustainable Development Goal 7.2, due to its inferior natural circumstances. The 
present study attempts to approach this issue by shifting focus from the generation seg­
ment to the naturally monopolized segment of the power industry value chain, since the 
latter usually accounts for 30–40% of the whole bills paid by the end-user. Simulation 
results show that the cost-reduction ratios vary from 9.871 to −0.992% according to how 
the regulatory regime is designed. This study aims to add policy tools used to accelerate 
renewables development, and also to bridge academic research with regulatory practice 
by providing insights into the design and implementation of effective incentive regulation.
Keywords  Sustainable development goal 7.2 (SDG 7.2) · StoNED framework · 
Naturally monopolized utilities · Efficiency and productivity
1  Introduction
The purpose of this study is to show by simulation based counterfactual analysis that Japan 
can lower its costs to achieve Sustainable Development Goal 7.2 by implementing incentive 
regulation which aims to improve efficiency levels of power grid sector that is characterized 
by natural monopoly. In fact, the surging costs accompanied by the introduction of renew­

Fig. 1, the levelized costs of electricity (herein after LCOE) for wind power and solar power

wind power and solar power are about US$150.4/MWh and US$95.8/MWh in Japan, about 
3 times and 1.5 times the average cost of sample countries, respectively.
However, lowering the LCOE is not easy because Japan is mountainous and prone to

been increasing rather than decreasing over the past few years in Japan. For example, the 
purchasing cost of renewables through the FIT (Feed-in Tariff) program in 2012 when the 
FIT program began was about 0.22 JPY/kWh and surged to about 3.45 JPY/kWh in 2022.1
On the other hand, from an end-user’s perspective, the cost of using renewables includes 
the cost of electricity generation, namely the LCOE mentioned earlier, the cost of the net­
work to deliver electricity from the power plant to the end-user, and the retail cost incurred 
by the retailer to buy electricity from the wholesale market and sell it to the end-user. Put it 
differently, the value chain of the power industry is composed of generation segment, trans­
portation segment and retailing segment. So theoretically speaking, reducing costs in the 
development of renewables in Japan may be conducted in every segment along this value 
chain, the present study focuses on how to reduce cost in the natural monopolized power 
transmission and distribution segment. This is because: firstly, the generating cost of renew­
ables in Japan can be hardly reduced due to uncontrollable inferior exogenous conditions. 
Secondly, the cost of natural monopolized segment, or the network cost, account for about 
30–40% of the end-users’ bill paid for using power in Japan (The Consumer Commission of

Japan, 2016), experiences in other countries show that such cost may be reduced conditional

The fundamental principle underlying the design of a regulatory regime for the natural 
monopolized segment is that it should aim to replicate the competitive process and reflect 
the discipline found in competitive markets (Kahn, 1970). In a competitive market, the 
ongoing effort by each firm to outperform its rivals is a fundamental aspect of the com­
petitive process. Building on the principles of market competition, Schleifer (1985) intro­
duced the concept of yardstick competition, where a regulator's key task is to establish the 
cost level of the shadow competitor against which a regulated monopolist must compete. 
The functioning mechanism of yardstick competition can be described as follows: from the 
viewpoint of the natural monopolist, if its realized cost is lower than the shadow cost set 
ex-ante by the regulator, then it can achieve extra profits (or economic profits). To earn those 
extra profits, the natural monopolist has an incentive to reduce cost by improving efficiency. 
While from the perspective of the user of the service or product provided by the natural 
monopolist, the lower the cost realized by the natural monopolist, the cheaper the service 
or product. Put it differently, the yardstick competition has two roles: on the one hand, it 
induces the natural monopolist to reduce cost as many as possible. On the other hand, it 
allows consumers to share in the benefits of lower cost realized by the natural monopolist. It 
is in this sense that the regulation based on yardstick competition is called incentive-based 
regulation (or performance-based regulation).
The present research is based on the counterfactual analysis, its logic can be described 
as follows: since the incentive-based regulation which employs the yardstick competition 
to set the shadow cost (or the revenue cap that a monopolist can collect from consumers 
through tariff), was not implemented in Japanese power grid sector in practice (see Appen­
dix 2 for more information on regulatory regime in Japanese power grid sector), it is rational 
to think that the actual network cost includes, more or less, the redundant cost which could 
have been avoided or reduced if more effective regulatory regimes like incentive regulation 
have had been implemented. Based on this fact, we, like regulators usually do in the real 
word regulatory practice, firstly set the shadow cost for each Japanese power grid company 
on the assumption that the incentive-based regulation have had been implemented in the 
sample period, then we calculate the difference between this estimated shadow cost and the 
actual cost which is observable, this difference is exactly the above-mentioned redundant 
cost, or the cost that end-users could have been saved in their usage of renewables if the 
incentive-based regulation have had been implemented. Since the shadow cost is unob­
served and needs to be estimated, the StoNED (stochastic non-parametric envelopment of 
data) framework will be used to estimate the minimum cost and cost efficiency required for 
regulators to set the shadow cost. By changing parameter values in the process of setting 
shadow cost, this study will simulate how regulatory regimes influence the size of the redun­
dant cost, or the degree of the cost-reduction for end- users to use renewables. As expected, 
our simulation results show that, depending on how the regulatory regime was designed, 
the network cost in Japan could have been reduced by about JPY−43.59–433.823 billion 
during the sample period, which means that best regulatory practices targeting naturally 
monopolized power network segment can indeed reduce the cost of a green transformation.

This study makes three contributions. Firstly, from the viewpoint of policy, it adds policy 
tools on how to achieve SDG 7.2 (increasing the share of renewable energy in the energy 
mix and raising per capita generating capacity from renewable sources) by focusing on the 
naturally monopolized segment of the power industry value chain. Although the situation 
varies from country to country, the power network cost usually accounts for about 30–40% 
of the whole bills paid by the end-user (Bu, et al., 2024). So, for countries like Japan that 
encounters a high cost in accelerating its development of renewables, reducing costs in 
segments other than generation of the value chain provides a feasible measure to foster 
renewables.
Secondly, from the perspective of methodology, the study demonstrates, by simulation, 
how the estimation of cost level of individual monopolists as well as regulatory designs 
affect the shadow cost and the resulting cost-reduction. In a real world characterized by 
imperfect and asymmetric information, monopolies such as power grid companies have 
incentives and inherent advantages in strategically leveraging their private information, so 
to what degree that the network cost can be reduced is subject to the quality of the regula­
tory regime.
Thirdly, the study bridges academic research with regulatory practice on how to set the 
shadow cost, providing valuable insights for regulators in both developed and develop­
ing economies that are planning to introduce the yardstick competition-based incentive 
regulation.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Sect. 2 offers a brief review of the develop­
ments of the StoNED method. Section 3 presents efficiency measurements of power grid 
companies. This is followed by a description of the simulation logic in Sects. 4 and 5 pres­
ents and analyses simulation results from various perspectives. Finally, Sect. 6 concludes 
the paper. Supplementary materials are presented in the Appendix.
2  A literature review on developments of the StoNED framework
This section examines important advances in the StoNED framework in order to under­
stand its use in the current study and to highlight its methodological strengths in regulatory 
economics.
As the frontier-based framework to estimate efficiency, the Data Envelopment Analysis 
(DEA) method needs no arbitrary assumption on functional form and consequently reduces 
the risk of violating the regularity properties of production technology, while it suffers from 
inability to separate statistical noise from inefficiency and is sensitive to outliers when 
constructing frontier. On the other hand, the virtues of the parametric Stochastic Frontier 
Analysis (SFA) method lie in its ability to separate inefficiency from statistical noise and 
its inherent compatibility with panel data. However, its arbitrary assumption on functional 
form which may violate the regularity properties of production technology has drawn criti­
cism. While the StoNED framework, first proposed by Kuosmanem (2006), represents an 
advancement in efficiency estimation by integrating the strengths of non-parametric DEA 
method and parametric SFA method.
Since its inception, the StoNED framework has been extended in various ways. Johnson 
and Kuosmanen (2011, 2012) introduced the one-stage estimation method to control for 
external (contextual) impacts, allowing the StoNED model to account for environmental 
1 3

variables that influence a firm’s cost but are beyond managerial control. Kuosmanen et al. 
(2015) improved the StoNED framework by enhancing its ability to separate inefficiency 
from random noise, making efficiency estimates more reliable in stochastic settings. Kuos­
manen and Zhou (2021) introduced a directional distance function approach, making the 
StoNED model more robust to random noise and heteroscedasticity. Rødseth et al. (2023), 
further developed the StoNED methodology for panel data and decompose inefficiency into 
persistent inefficiency and time-varying inefficiency, following the footsteps to the paramet­
ric models previously suggested by Kumbhakar et al. (2014).
These improvements have expanded the framework's applicability, particularly in indus­
tries characterized by fluctuating demand and variable production conditions, such as the 
energy sector. Given the relative advantage of the StoNED framework, the Finnish Energy 
Agency has replaced the SFA and DEA methods with the StoNED framework to estimate 
frontier cost (minimum cost) and cost efficiency of power distribution companies in its regu­
latory practice since 2012 (Kuosmanen, 2012; Kuosmanen & Johnson, 2020).

3.3  Considerations on the selection of the sample period and variables used
The nuclear leakage crisis in Tokyo Power Companies and the resulting regulatory reform 
introduced to electric industry largely changed the operating environment of power grid 
companies in Japan, this shock may make the data between before- and after-2011 incompa­
rable due to changed behavior patterns of both regulators and regulated electric networks.4 
In addition, this study is simulation-focused which is not so demanding on the fresh data. 
Therefore, we restricted our sample period to the before-crisis period, ranging from 1996 
to 2010. More specifically, the data set for this study consists of a balanced panel with 
150 observations from ten incumbent power grid companies in Japan. We gathered data 
mainly from the Electricity Statistics Database, compiled by The Federation of Electric 
Power Companies of Japan which is accessible online.5 All monetary values are converted 
to 1996 values using the Japanese GDP deflator, available from the Cabinet Office of Japan. 
In particular, data for 1996–2006 are used to estimate efficiency levels, whereas data for 
2007–2010 are utilized to simulate outcomes of different regulatory designs.
Econometric methods necessitate making assumptions about the relationship between 
a firm's cost and its cost drivers, which is followed by the estimation of the parameters of 
the function that represents this relationship. Now let us turn to discuss variables used to 
estimate this relationship. In the selection of inputs, outputs, and environmental variables, 
we refer to the studies of Jamasb and Pollitt (2001), Li et al. (2017), and Xie et al. (2021).
(i) Input variable: Broadly speaking, a power grid company’s cost input can be classified 
into various categories, including activity-based cost (such as the cost of tree-cutting 
to protect overhead lines and wire poles), activity-group-based cost (such as repair 
cost which includes both labor and material costs), aggregated cost (such as operating 
expenditure and capital expenditure) and total cost. As far as this research is concerned, 
we select the total cost which is the sum of electric transmission cost, electricity distri­
bution cost and voltage switching cost to serve as input variable.
(ii) Output variables: As noted in Hattori et al. (2005), there is no uniform measure of out­
put for power network services. According to Jamasb and Pollitt (2001), the most com­
monly used output variables in empirical studies include power delivered, number of 
customers, service area and network length. Due to problems such as data accessibility 
and/or compatibility between data and the selected model, most of the studies include 
only some of these variables in their estimations. For example, Hattori et al. (2005) uti­
lized customer number and power delivered, Huang et al. (2010) employed quasi-fixed 
capital and power delivered, whereas Agrell et al. (2014) focused on power delivered 
alone. In this study, data on power delivered, number of customers, maximum load and 
network length are available, but because of high correlation coefficients among these 
four variables, we finally selected power delivered and customer number as the output 
variables.
(iii) Contextual/Environmental variables: Contextual variables, added to either the fron­
tier function or the ancillary function of the inefficiency term, are used to reflect the 
belief that factors influencing efficiency levels but beyond managers' control should be

4  Implementation of the incentive regulation and simulation design
In what follows, we turn to describe how a yardstick competition based incentive regulation 
is usually implemented and then show how to simulate outcomes of incentive regulation by 
counterfactual analysis. More specifically, we suppose that the Japanese regulator have had 
implemented the incentive regulation during the period of 2007–2010, the key tool to which 
is to set, by the Japanese energy regulator, the shadow cost in the period of 2007–2010 
against which the corresponding power grid company is forced to compete. The difference 
between this shadow cost and the actual cost in the period of 2007–2010 which is observed 
is regarded as the cost-reduction effects of incentive regulation.
4.1  A simple description of the implementation of the incentive regulation
As shown in Fig. 3, the implementation process of an incentive regulation can be roughly 
described as follows: firstly, regulators declare guidelines which show the files that the 
regulated monopolists should submit for setting their shadow costs, namely the revenue cap 
that they can collect from users in the upcoming regulatory period. Secondly, the monopo­
lists submit files as well as financial materials on which their files are based. Then, regula­

shadow cost for each monopolist. After several round of revision, negotiation or even law 
suit between regulators and monopolists, the final shadow cost is decided and declared 
before the beginning of the upcoming regulatory period. Thirdly, monopolists provide ser­
vice which should be compliance with the ex-ante set quality and other standards. Further­

they will actively do their best to reduce costs by improving efficiency and innovation. 
With respect to regulators, they will supervise monopolists on the one hand and prepare 
guidelines for the new round of regulatory period on the other hand. Particularly, they may 
set lower than the last round of shadow cost according to degree of extra profits earned by 
monopolists, which means that welfare generated by incentive regulation will be partially 
transferred to users through lower tariffs.

6  Concluding remarks and research limitations
Despite significant progress on the renewables’ targets under SDG 7.2, achievements in 
recent years lag far behind ambitions. The agreement by over 130 countries to triple the 
world’s renewable power capacity by 2030 as well as a global consensus on the need to 
transition away from fossil fuels at COP28 in Dubai in 2023 have significant implications 
for accelerated renewable energy deployment (UNFCCC, 2023). Multiple policy tools 
are required to speed up the necessary transition of energy systems across developed and 
developing countries in line with ambitions (IEA, 2024; IRENA, 2023). Targets need to be 
accompanied by effective policy planning and implementation, including regulatory reform, 
price-based mechanisms, market incentives, fiscal policy, power market design, and stream­
lined permitting procedures. In the present study, we focus on how to lower the cost of the 
naturally monopolized segment of the power industry value chain. This is because the cost 
in this segment, namely the power network cost, accounts for 30 –40% of the end-users’ bill 
paid for using power (Bu, et al., 2024). If the generating cost of renewables can hardly be 
reduced due to uncontrollable exogenous conditions such as situations in Japan, govern­
ments may shift focus to this segment where cost may be reduced conditional upon the qual­
ante by the regulator, then the redundant cost is the possible maximum economic profit. By contrast, if the 
realized cost by regulated monopolists is smaller than the minimum cost estimated ex ante by the regula­
tor, then the economic profit may be greater than the redundant cost. Supposing the difference between the 
minimum cost and the realized cost is π(π > 0), then the possible maximum economic profit in such case 
amounts to the sum of the redundant cost and π.

degree of cost reduction under incentive regulation, this study employs the Japanese power

Our simulation results do show that best regulatory practices such as yardstick competi­
tion based incentive regulation targeting naturally monopolized power network segment can 
indeed reduce the cost of a green transformation. More specifically, the simulation results 
reveal the following key points: Firstly, under incentive regulation, where the power grid 
company is compelled to compete with a shadow competitor, the costs of these monopo­
lized companies can be significantly reduced. For instance, the total cost of the Japanese 
power grid sector could have been reduced by at least JPY 433.823 billion, about 9.871% 
of the actual cost during the same period in Scenario 1, ceteris paribus. Secondly, the size of 
cost saving is mainly determined by the shadow cost and the power of the incentive which 
influences the degree of efforts to improve efficiency by the monopolist. The power of the 
incentive involves regulatory design, where if the monopolist is assured that they are the 
only residual claimant and trusts that the regulators will keep their commitments, the abil­
ity to reduce costs is the strongest. Thirdly, simulation results demonstrate the importance 
of estimates of efficiency and productivity change, both of which are used to determine 
base year efficiency score and to set the TFP change. In theory, any exogenous differences 
between the monopolists must be carefully considered to set a feasible and fair efficiency 
improving target. Neglecting this point may result in either over-compensation or under-
compensation for the utility. Additionally, addressing heterogeneity among monopolists is 
likely to pose a more significant challenge when estimating efficiency levels compared to 
estimating changes in productivity. Notwithstanding, regulators in some countries have set 
different efficiency improving targets (stretch factors) across monopolists and update these 
values in every round of price review.
Although simulation results highlight the merits of incentive regulation, it is compli­
cated to put incentive regulation into practice. Methodologically, efficiency estimation must 
address three key issues: constructing the cost/production frontier, separating inefficiency 
from error terms to obtain accurate efficiency estimates, and mitigating the effects of exog­
enous heterogeneity on costs. In order to lower, as many and accurate as possible, costs of 
naturally monopolized segment of power industry value chain, it is necessary for regulators 
to decide the framework for the frontier-based cost assessment, to select the formula used 
to calculate shadow cost, and so on. There are no unique answers to these questions, as they 
are likely to be specific to each country and may involve a trial-and-error approach with aca­
demic assistance (such as Pollitt, 2005; Sappington, 1994, to name just a few). Furthermore, 
given the vital importance of high-quality data in the efficiency analysis process, regulators 
should collect and compile necessary data, with which to test various efficiency estimation 
models to ensure results align with the consistency conditions proposed by Bauer et al. 
(1998) and Rossi and Ruzzier (2000).
There are some limitations in the empirical part of this study. That is to say, we discussed 
only fundamental aspects of how to set the shadow cost, neglecting other aspects that may 
also affect the amount of it. These factors include: (i) carryover mechanisms used to balance 
the incentive power for monopolists to reduce cost throughout the regulatory period; (ii) 
ratchet effects resulting from setting efficiency improvement targets based on the monopo­
list's performance; (iii) additional mechanisms addressing non-output-producing costs in

nor small firms are treated preferentially by the regulator in our research setting. However, 
if the technology shows economies or diseconomies of scale, the CRS assumption will put 
small or large firms at a disadvantage. In this section, we, therefore, have estimated frontier 
costs by the StoNED method under CRS, non-decreasing returns to scale (NDRS), and vari­
able returns to scale (VRS) assumptions12. We compare the values of R2 estimated from 
these three models, conduct correlation analysis and non-parametric Kolmogorov–Smirnov 
(KS) test to regression residuals under three alternative returns to scale (RTS) assumptions, 
and perform regression analysis between efficiency scores and two outputs. In this way, we 
can assess whether the RTS assumption influences the efficiency estimates and whether the 
impact is statistically significant.

the Japanese electric grid sector.

The March 11, 2011 event highlighted the shortcomings of the Japanese-style partial 
reform to both the opponents and the supporters of the overhaul in the electricity indus­
try. An expert committee on electricity system reform was set up in February 2012. Based 
on the committee's recommendations, the Japanese government implemented a three-step 
deregulation process to achieve three policy objectives: securing a stable electricity sup­
ply, reducing electricity tariffs as much as possible and, increasing choices for consumers 
and business opportunities for industries. The first step of reform, implemented in 2014, 
involved establishing an organization responsible for cross-regional coordination of trans­
mission operators. This was followed by the second step in 2016, which deregulated the 
whole electric retail market. The third step involved unbundling the electric grid depart­
ments from the incumbents through legal separation in 2020. These power grid depart­
ments of the incumbents were incorporated and became subsidiary corporations of newly 
established holding companies, which also owned subsidiaries in the power generation and 
retail sectors.
Furthermore, a task force established in 2011 to address financial issues at Tokyo Power 
Company discovered inaccuracies in accounting information related to network costs. For 
example, The Task Force Report on the Management and Finance of Tokyo Power Com­
pany14 states that in the field of ultra-high voltage, the reported network costs are higher 
than the real costs by about 5.38% in 2007, 1.59% in 2008, 3.98% in 2009, and 5.96% 
in 2010, whereas in the high voltage field, the difference were 2.97%, 10.13%, 1.95%, 
5.25%, and 7.52% (pp.145–146). Since the power network tariff in Japan was then subject 
to a rate-of-return regulation and power companies were vertically integrated, there was a 
potential incentive for companies to manipulate cost assignments between competitive and 
non-competitive activities. The Task Force Report recommended exploring the introduction 
of incentive-based regulation (pp.146–151).
In fact, the network costs in Japan were higher than those in European countries except 
Germany, and even higher than in Denmark where significant investments have been made 
to promote the usage of renewable energy. According to another Task Force15 commissioned

regulatory regime at that time: Firstly, cost savings by network operators may not have been
