Abstract
Sociallyresponsibleinvesting(SRI)hasgainedsigniﬁcantpopularity,withinvestorsandinsti-
tutions increasingly prioritizing sustainability and ethical considerations. However, during
crises such as the COVID-19 pandemic and the Russia–Ukraine conﬂict, investor preferences
often shift toward conventional assets, raising the question of whether SRI is a true prior-
ity or a luxury for stable markets. This paper investigates the relationship between socially
responsible funds (SRFs) and conventional indices (CIs) across different market conditions,
assessing whether SRFs maintain their appeal during ﬁnancial distress. Using data from 2014
to 2024, we identify two volatility regimes via a Hidden Markov model and exploit copula
models to assess tail dependence, cointegration tests to examine long-term price relation-
ships, fuzzy clustering to classify assets by risk dynamics, and spillover analysis to measure
asset interconnectedness. Our ﬁndings show that SRF and CI performance converge in perfor-
mance during market turmoil, suggesting that SRI loses prominence in unstable conditions.
Moreover, systemic, geographic, and issuer-speciﬁc risks overshadow ESG-related factors,
challenging the notion of SRI as a resilient investment strategy in turbulent markets.
Keywords Socially responsible investing · Hidden Markov model · Copulas · Fuzzy cluster
analysis · Volatility spillover
1 Introduction
The growing emphasis on sustainability shifts market dynamics, positioning socially respon-
sible investing (SRI) as a key focus for long-term ﬁnancial strategies. However, periods of
instability, such as the COVID-19 pandemic or geopolitical disruptions such as the Russia–
Ukraine conﬂict, often redirect attention toward immediate economic concerns. This contrast
underscores the tension between sustainable objectives and traditional market imperatives
during times of crisis.
The global economy in the last twenty years has faced signiﬁcant turbulence, driven by a
series of crises arising from ﬁnancial instabilities, sanitary emergencies, geopolitical tensions,
and armed conﬂicts. These events entailed periods of elevated levels of market volatility, when
the traditional investment strategies require a reevaluation of risk management practices
(see, e.g., Fatica & Panzica, 2024; Gianfrate et al., 2021; Lei et al., 2023; Naeem et al.,

over time, showing consistently lower downside deviations compared to traditional ones. In
addition, sustainable bond funds exceed their traditional counterpart in median annual returns
(10% vs 6.4%).2
SRI is often promoted as ethically and ﬁnancially superior to conventional investing,
but the performance analysis under different market conditions leads to mixed conclusions
(Bauer et al., 2021; Bolton & Kacperczyk, 2021; Bilbao-Terol et al., 2023; Hornuf & Yüksel,
2024; Riedl & Smeets, 2017). For example, Renneboog et al. (2008) suggest that socially
responsible funds (SRFs) tend to outperform conventional funds (CFs) during periods of
low volatility and less susceptible to market ﬂuctuations. Similarly, Leite and Cortez (2014,
2015) ﬁnd that SRFs provide competitive returns under stable market conditions, suggesting
their potential as long-term investment alternatives. However, other studies, such as Cortez
et al. (2009); Becchetti et al. (2015), argue that CFs generally offer superior returns during
market downturns or periods of economic contraction. The ethical screening in SRFs can
limit their diversiﬁcation, potentially exposing them to large negative market shocks. This
ﬁnding aligns with the notion that CFs better adapt to volatile market environments delivering
greater ﬁnancial performance during crises (Pedersen et al., 2021).
In this paper, we examine whether the pursuit of sustainable ﬁnancial products retains
its prominence during phases of market distress. By analyzing the interplay between con-
ventional and socially responsible assets in both stable and volatile market regimes, we
investigate the hypothesis that sustainability objectives take a backseat when ﬁnancial mar-
kets face extraordinary challenges, such as geopolitical crises or global economic disruptions.
We select a sample of conventional indices (CIs) and SRFs, observing their daily quotes from
January 1, 2014, to December 1, 2024. We identify two volatility regimes (low and high)
using a Hidden Markov model (HMM) ﬁtted to the daily prices of the Chicago Board Options
Exchange Volatility Index (VIX). We then model the relationship between the two asset
classes (i.e., CIs and SRFs) in each identiﬁed market phase. In particular, we ﬁrst estimate a
list of copula models to examine the dependence structure between each pair of asset returns
and compute Kendall’s τ to quantify the strength and direction of the relationship. We fur-
ther investigate the issue by performing cointegration tests to assess possible comovements
in asset prices. We group assets according to their volatilities, a proxy for the market risk,
using a fuzzy cluster analysis (FCA), exploiting the estimates obtained from a generalized
autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity (GARCH) model. By analyzing the variation in
the clusters’ membership probabilities, we measure how risk associations adjust to different
market conditions. Finally, a spillover analysis sheds light on how risk transmission among
assets vary according to the volatility regime.

The reasons why we adopt these methodologies in conducting our analysis are as follows.
HMMs are widely employed to identify different volatility regimes in ﬁnancial markets (see,
e.g., Hamilton, 1989; Rabiner, 1989). These models are particularly effective in uncovering
latent states and are well-suited for detecting the unique characteristics of ﬁnancial markets.
For instance, after detecting volatility regimes using an HMM, Ryou et al. (2020) adapt a
momentum strategy to improve the short-term trading performance, while Nystrup et al.
(2018) optimize portfolio allocation by calibrating weights based on time-varying return
distribution parameters. Similarly, Majumder et al. (2023) examine regime-dependent varia-
tions in interconnected sectoral dynamics within SP500 index components using a Gaussian
copula. Moreover, studies such as Ang and Timmermann (2012) and Elliott and Siu (2010)
leverage a HMM to predict market phase transitions, improving stock selection and portfolio
performance.
We model the dependence between ﬁnancial asset returns using copulas, which provide a
robust framework for capturing both linear and non-linear relationships (see, e.g., Cerqueti
& Rotundo, 2023; Cherubini et al., 2004; McNeil et al., 2015). This approach allows for
a comprehensive understanding of asset interactions across different market environments.
While Gaussian copulas are widely employed in the literature to model joint movements
of asset returns (see, e.g., Czado et al., 2022; Patton, 2006), we also utilize Archimedean
copulas to speciﬁcally capture joint occurrences of extreme negative returns during crises
(see, e.g., Czado et al., 2022; Demarta & McNeil, 2005) and extreme positive returns during
market upswings (see, e.g., Czado et al., 2022; Embrechts et al., 2002). In the same vein, we
adopt cointegration analysis as it is extensively used to identify long-term comovements of
asset prices (see, e.g., Gu et al., 2024; Kuck & Schweikert, 2023; Laborda et al., 2024; Rad
et al., 2016).
We model time-varying volatility in a GARCH framework, which is particularly effective
in capturing volatility clustering, a stylized fact of ﬁnancial markets where periods of high
(low) volatility tend to persist (see, e.g., Bollerslev, 1986; Cont, 2001; Nelson, 1991). The
adoption of this well-established, ﬂexible, and robust methodology enhances the reliability
of our analysis (see, e.g., Otranto, 2008; Piccolo, 1990).
Among clustering procedures, we choose the FCA to group assets according to their
risk, as it emerges as a powerful tool in ﬁnancial econometrics application (for comparison
among cluster algorithms see Kou et al. (2014)). This methodology gains popularity due
to its ability to capture the asset relationship structures by allowing overlapping cluster
memberships (see, e.g., , Cerqueti et al., 2023; Lorenzo & Arroyo, 2023; Mattera et al., 2021).
For example, studies like (D’Urso et al., 2013) and D’Urso et al. (2016) exploit model-based
fuzzy clustering with time series-speciﬁc distance metrics. More recently, Cerqueti et al.
(2021, 2022) introduced advanced FCA methods using conditional moments, optimizing
clustering precision.
Finally, we adopt the well established volatility spillover technique to assess whether the
connectedness among assets varies across different market phases. We can mention a wide
range of scholars that resort to this approach to model such relationships in ﬁnance (see, e.g.,
Baele, 2005; Christiansen, 2007; Gelos & Sahay, 2001; Joo et al., 2023; Liow, 2015; Strohsal
& Weber, 2015; Wu et al., 2024). Furthermore, several studies compare the spillover effects
across different volatility regimes (see, e.g., BenSaïda et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2021). Authors
such as Park et al. (2020); Iqbal et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022) measure spillovers in sus-
tainable investments, including green bonds and ESG stock indices. Furthermore, Cerqueti et
al. (2023) combine FCA and volatility spillovers to assess connectedness within the ﬁnancial
Italian stock market. However, to the best of our knowledge, no existing study investigates

2 Methodology
In this section, we outline the methodology used in this study. We work with a series of daily
prices and returns for K assets, as well as the daily quotes of the Chicago Board Options
Exchange Volatility Index (VIX), xV ,t, which serves as a proxy for daily market volatility. We
label the return and price of the k-th asset by yk = (yk,1, . . . , yk,T ) and xk = (xk,1, . . . , xk,T ),
respectively, with yk,t = log(xk,t/xk,t−1) for each day t and asset k.
2.1 Hidden Markov model
We employ a Hidden Markov model (HMM) to identify different volatility regimes in the
VIX price time series. Within the HMM framework, we assume that the VIX time series,
xV , is generated by S unobserved (hidden) states. The transition from one to another state
at every step t is governed by a Markov process, and each hidden state is characterized by
a different probability distribution. This model is widely used in ﬁnance to detect volatility
regimes (see, e.g., Kabašinskas et al., 2024; Mamon & Elliott, 2007; Rossi & Gallo, 2006;
Soltani & Abbes, 2023; Zheng et al., 2021) and stock selection within an asset allocation
problem (Nguyen & Nguyen, 2015). We denote hereafter the state η at time t by ηt and the
probability of state η at time t by πη,t, for η = 1, . . . , S and t ≥0. The HMM takes a vector
of initial values that deﬁnes the probability, πη,0, that the time series starts (t = 0) in each
hidden state η. At each time t = 1, 2, . . . , T , the probability of transitioning from a generic
state ηi,t−1 to a generic state η j,t is governed by a probability matrix At = {ai j,t}, where
ai j,t = Pr(it| jt−1), for each i, j = 1, . . . , S. The effect of hidden states on observed data is
summarized in the emission probability matrix Bt, which contains the probabilities bη(xV ,t),
that each observation xV ,t is generated from the state η:
bη(xV ,t) = P(xV ,t | ηt).

2.3 Cointegration
We investigate the long-term equilibrium relationships between asset prices by applying
cointegration analysis, aiming to identify persistent connections over time (see, e.g., Dickey
& Fuller, 1979; Granger, 1981; Johansen, 1991). Given two price series xk and xl, with
k,l = 1, . . . , K and k ̸= j, that are integrated of order one, I(1), we assess for cointegration
by estimating the residuals, ˆϵk,l, from the following linear regression:
xk = μ + φxl + ϵk,l.
(7)
The estimated residuals are given by:
ˆϵk,l = xk −ˆμ −ˆφxl,
(8)
where ˆμ and ˆφ are OLS parameters. If ˆϵk,l is stationary, then the series xk and xl are said to be
cointegrated. This implies that any deviations from the equilibrium relationship are temporary
and tend to revert over time. We test for stationarity of ˆϵk,l using the Engle-Granger two-
step procedure, which consists of applying the Augmented Dickey–Fuller (ADF) test on the
estimated residuals. The null hypothesis of the ADF test is that ˆϵk,l has a unit root (i.e., it
is non-stationary, implying no cointegration). If the null hypothesis is rejected, we conclude
that the series are cointegrated.

3 Empirical analysis
In this section, we present the results of the empirical analysis conducted to investigate the
dependence of socially responsible asset proﬁtability on market health conditions. We begin
by describing the data and identifying the market volatility regime using a HMM approach.
123

Annals of Operations Research
Next, a copula analysis is performed to capture the time-varying relationship between the
returnsofsociallyresponsiblefunds(SRFs)andconventionalindices(CIs).Tofurthersupport
our ﬁndings, we conduct a cointegration test on the daily prices. Finally, we estimate the
volatility of each asset to cluster them according to their risk and evaluate their volatility
connectedness through a spillover analysis.
3.1 Data
We collect daily quotes of a selection of CIs and SRFs observed from January 1, 2014,
to December 1, 2024. We retrieve data from the London Stock Exchange Group (LSEG)
database (LSEG, 2024). Table 1 provides a description of the assets included in the analysis.
In the data collection process, we construct a sample of CIs that represents both speciﬁc
market segments (e.g., FTSE Emerging Index and Nasdaq 100 Index) and the market as a
whole (e.g., FTSE All-Share Index). The selected sample includes indices that invest both
globally and regionally. For instance, we retrieved prices for the MSCI All Country World
Price Index, representing global equity market performance, and the STOXX Europe 600
Index, which focuses on the region most dedicated to ESG practices worldwide. By analyzing
the relationship between SRFs and CIs, we aim to uncover discrepancies driven by varying
social policies across regions. Among the SRFs, we select those with sufﬁcient historical
depth and high trading volume. This naturally results in a relatively small sample of SRFs,
as most of them were launched recently, particularly following the Paris Agreement. To
enhance comparability, we include funds issued by the same CI managers (e.g., FTSE4Good
Developed 100 Index) and focused on similar geographical regions (e.g., Amundi STOXX
Europe 600 UCITS ETF).
Table 2 reports the descriptive statistics of the log-returns computed from the asset prices.
All series are characterized by signiﬁcant outliers, primarily due to the shock caused by
the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to large troughs and spikes. The log-returns exhibit a
non-Gaussian distribution, as evidenced by the statistically signiﬁcant results of the Jarque-
Bera (JB) test. Additionally, the price series are found to be integrated of order one, I(1), as
the ADF test rejects the null hypothesis of a unit root for all ﬁnancial returns. The Nasdaq
100 Index records the highest risk weighted performance (i.e., Sharpe Ratio (ShR), Sortino
Ratio (SoR), and Calmar Ratio (CR)), highlighting the signiﬁcant ﬂuctuations, often positive,
observed in the U.S. technology market in recent years. The ﬁnancial performance of SRFs

3.2 Individuating market regimes
We collect the open-access daily historical quotes from January 1, 2014, to December 1,
2024, of the Volatility Index (VIX) provided by the CBOE.3 This index is widely considered
a key measure of market conditions and is often referred to as the fear index. It is calculated
based on the prices of options traded on the SP500 Index, while alternative versions provide
measures of risk for different markets. According to the leverage effect, periods of high
volatility are often associated with negative returns. Therefore, high values of the fear index
effectively indicate situations of market distress.
To identify distinct market phases, we estimate a HMM on VIX quotes. By doing so,
we aim to separate normal market conditions and periods of ﬁnancial distress. The choice
of employing a HMM is motivated by two main reasons. First, the model is widely used
in ﬁnancial applications to estimate volatility regimes (see, e.g., Kabašinskas et al., 2024;
Nguyen & Nguyem, 2015; Rossi & Gallo, 2006; Zheng et al., 2021). Second, it provides a
probabilistic framework for classifying observations into latent states, making it well-suited
for capturing the dynamics of ﬁnancial market conditions.
The results of the HMM application are reported in Table 3. The emission matrix,
which summarizes the characteristics of each regime, clearly identiﬁes two distinct volatility
regimes. The ﬁrst regime, labeled as low, exhibits an average VIX value of approximately
13, which is nearly half of the average value observed in the second regime (≈23), labeled
as high. The high volatility regime is not only associated with a higher mean, but is also
characterized by a standard deviation that is roughly four times larger (7.33 vs 1.84). These
features reﬂect two distinct market conditions: periods of relatively low and stable volatility,
corresponding to normal market phases, and others of high and erratic volatility, indicative of

market, elevating risk and volatility. But it also underscores the HMM’s capacity to accurately
identify these volatility regimes.
We exploit the HMM results to differentiate between normal and distress market phases.
Each trading day t is classiﬁed as belonging to either a low or high volatility phase, allowing us
to analyze the behaviour of our assets and their relationships under these distinct conditions.
This approach involves conducting two parallel analyses, separating trading days into high-
and low-volatility regimes, and comparing the ﬁndings across these two phases. For each
asset, we distinguish two segmented time series, where each day t is assigned to one of the
regimes identiﬁed by the HMM.

as proxies for the performance of the general market or speciﬁc segments (e.g., the SP500
for the US market). In contrast, the SRF components are inﬂuenced by both endogenous and
exogenous market shocks, although they exhibit resilience during unexpected market phases

Table 4 presents the results of the copula estimations separated by the two volatility
regimes. While the relationships between CIs and SRFs are generally consistent across
regimes, they tend to strengthen during periods of high volatility. This trend is evident from
the Gaussian copula parameters, which are slightly lower during low-volatility phases and
increase in times of market distress. However, the differences become more pronounced when
analyzing tail dependence through the parameters of the other copulas. Interestingly, high
copula values do not appear to be strongly inﬂuenced by the issuer or the geographic focus
of the assets. However, the greater attention to sustainability that characterizes the European
markets compared to the US one has an impact, as the association between SRFs and the
STOXX index is, on average, higher than that with the SP500. To further illustrate these
relationships, we report in Table 5 the Kendall’s τ values derived from the copula parame-
ters. For simplicity and to avoid redundancy, we focus our commentary on Kendall’s τ, as it
provides a comprehensive and interpretable measure of the relationships between the assets.
In the low-volatility regime, Gaussian copula Kendall’s τ values range from 0.21 (NAS-
DAQ vs FTSEASESG) to 0.91 (FTSEAS vs FTSEASESG, MSCIACWI vs SPGESG, and
STOXX vs ASMEESG). During market distress, these values rise signiﬁcantly, reaching up to
1 (MSCIACWI vs SPGESG), signaling a stronger convergence between CIs and SRFs under
heightened volatility. This empirical evidence suggests that systemic risk dominates all other
factors, including ESG considerations, as investors shift their focus to liquidity and capital
preservation. Periods of market distress, characterized by increased risk aversion and rapid
portfolio adjustments, drive synchronized movements across asset classes, effectively erod-
ing the distinctiveness of SRFs. As volatility surges, sustainability concerns lose relevance,
and SRFs, despite their mandates, exhibit behavior closely tied to broader market shocks.
This dynamic underscores a market reality where, in times of crisis, ﬁnancial imperatives
override long-term ESG commitments, implying a possible underestimation of ESG-speciﬁc
risks in extreme conditions.

Discrepancies between market phases become even more pronounced when examining
tail dependence through Archimedean copulas. The Clayton copula, which captures lower
tail dependence, measures the likelihood of simultaneous losses. During low-volatility peri-
ods, the probability of extreme negative co-movements remains moderate, becoming large
only for assets with similar objectives or investment areas, such as FTSEAS-FTSEASESG
(89%), MSCIACWI-SPGESG (92%), and STOXX-AMSESG (93%). For other asset pairs,
however, nearly half of Kendall’s τ values remain below 0.50, indicating a meaningful yet
relatively weak dependence. In periods of market distress, this dependence increases sharply,
with tail associations shifting to higher values. When the VIX, and consequently the market,
experiences stress, the tail dependence between CIs and SRFs strengthens further, reinforc-
ing the ﬁndings from the Gaussian copula and highlighting the alignment of these assets’
downside risk.
The right-tail relationship follows a similar, albeit slightly weaker, pattern, as reﬂected in
Kendall’s τ values derived from the Gumbel copula. Still, asset connections intensify under
market stress, mirroring the behavior observed in the left tail. This may suggest that ESG-
aligned assets diverge from the broader market during stable periods. In normal conditions,
the left-tail results could imply that demand for SRIs drives price appreciation, leading to a
degree of right-tail misalignment. However, within the copula framework, there is a limited
evidence to conclusively support a consistent and signiﬁcant divergence.
The Frank copula, which accounts for dependence across both tails, yields conclusions
consistent with those drawn from the Clayton and Gumbel copulas. However, tail asymmetry
and misalignment become more interesting when comparing copula parameters, as shown
in Table 4. While Kendall’s τ values appear similar across copulas, the discrepancy between
regimes is most pronounced in the Clayton copula, emphasizing a stronger association during
market distress compared to stable conditions.
ThecopulaanalysisrevealsacomplexrelationshipbetweenCIsandSRFs.Inlow-volatility
conditions, SRFs exhibit a strong association with CIs, but this dependence intensiﬁes in
turbulent markets, with heightened synchronization in both extreme losses and gains. The
asymmetric effects observed raise questions about the resilience of SRFs during market
distress, suggesting that their perceived defensive properties may be conditional on broader
market dynamics. The hypothesis that ESG investments receive stronger market support
primarily in favourable conditions remains a plausible interpretation of the empirical ﬁndings,
though it cannot be conclusively veriﬁed within the current methodological framework.

3.5 Volatility connectedness
In this section, we extend our analysis beyond returns and long-term price dependence by
examining asset risk across market regimes through volatility dynamics. To this end, we ﬁrst
estimate conditional volatilities using a GARCH model, then group assets based on their
average volatility in each regime using FCA. Finally, we apply a spillover methodology to
assess whether asset interconnectedness varies with market conditions.
We estimate conditional variances using a GARCH(1,1) model for each return series rk,t,
segmented by market regime, as described in Eq. (9). A rolling calibration window of 252
trading days, equivalent to one ﬁnancial year, is used to forecast one-step-ahead volatility
estimates for each day t. This approach ensures that estimates remain responsive to market
dynamics while accounting for potential structural shifts in the data. As a result, volatility
estimates begin on January 1, 2015.
Table 7 reports descriptive statistics for conditional variance distributions across the two
regimes. Average volatility nearly doubles in the high-volatility regime compared to the
low-volatility one (e.g., FTSEDEVEU increases from 9% to 16%, FTSEASESG from 13%
to 20%). While minimum variance values remain stable across regimes, maximum values
rise substantially, highlighting how extreme market ﬂuctuations disproportionately expand
the upper bound of the variance distribution. This effect is further reﬂected in the standard
deviations of conditional variance estimates, which are more than double-and in some cases
triple-in the high-volatility regime across all assets.

ﬁnancial system or if their performance is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by conventional markets,
particularly during periods of market distress.

a global benchmark. Conversely, the FTSEEM is the least affected by shocks from other

than the SP500, highlighting their increasing integration into global market dynamics. This
inﬂuence can be attributed to their diversiﬁed composition and alignment with major market
indices, reinforcing their relevance even during periods of low volatility.

4 Conclusion
The popularity of ESG funds is partly driven by the narrative that SRI not only delivers
competitive ﬁnancial returns but also provides resilience during periods of market distress.
However, this perception is often contradicted by investor behavior during extraordinary
market conditions, such as the COVID-19 pandemic or the commodity shortages triggered
by geopolitical crises, including the Russian invasion of Ukraine. In such periods, market
attention shifts decisively toward conventional investments, pushing the UN Sustainable
Development Goals agenda into the background.
This paper examines whether SRI remains a priority for ﬁnancial agents during periods of
market distress or if its appeal is predominantly linked to stable market conditions. To address
this question, we analyze daily quotes from a sample of CIs, including global benchmarks,
as well as regional and sector-speciﬁc indices, and SRFs with comparable characteristics to
ensure meaningful comparisons. Using the CBOE VIX as a proxy for market conditions,
we segment the sample period (January 2014–December 2024) into two volatility regimes,
identiﬁed via a HMM. We then analyze return dependence through copulas, investigate poten-
tial cointegration in prices, and assess risk relationships by examining conditional variances
within a GARCH framework. To explore similarities in asset risk proﬁles, we employ a FCA
to track shifts in variance-based cluster membership across regimes. Finally, we conduct a
spillover analysis to evaluate how market inﬂuence propagates between the two asset classes
under different market conditions.
The empirical results indicate a strong dependence structure between CIs and SRFs, which
becomes particularly pronounced in extreme market conditions. The dependence observed
in the tails of return distributions highlights that SRFs are not immune to market shocks.
Furthermore, systemic, geographic, and issuer-speciﬁc risks dominate over ESG-speciﬁc
risk. In periods of high volatility, the dependence between SRFs and CIs intensiﬁes signiﬁ-
cantly, challenging the claim that sustainable assets provide greater resilience during market
distress. Far from operating independently, SRFs are deeply embedded in broader market
123

Annals of Operations Research
dynamics. The FCA results further support this conclusion, as the distinction between the
two asset classes tends to blur during downturns. The risk proﬁles of SRFs and CIs converge
in high-volatility phases, undermining the perceived stability of ESG assets as a distinct
class. Similarly, the spillover analysis provides additional evidence of SRFs and CIs inter-
connectedness. In stable market conditions, SRFs act as signiﬁcant spillover transmitters,
underscoring their growing inﬂuence. However, during high-volatility regimes, their ability
to inﬂuence the market declines, overshadowed by dominant CIs such as the SP500 and
NASDAQ indices. At the same time, the spillovers received by SRFs increase, indicating
that these funds are not isolated from market instability but are instead highly susceptible to
it. Thus, during periods of stress, SRFs lose prominence, overwhelmed by the inﬂuence of
larger, more established benchmarks.
A limitation of this study is the relatively recent emergence of many SRFs, which restricts
the analysis to assets with sufﬁcient historical depth. However, we consider the selected
sample to be representative of the broader SRF market. Future research could deepen this
analysis by examining fund components at a more granular level, segmented by business
sector and region, to further reﬁne our understanding of SRI dynamics.
Our ﬁndings highlight that SRFs may not deliver the stability and independence often
attributed to them. Also, they support the notion that SRI is primarily a consideration during
favorable market conditions, whereas in times of turbulence, other concerns take precedence.
By revealing the vulnerability of SRFs during extraordinary market conditions, this paper
contributes to a broader understanding of their role within the ﬁnancial system. Finally, our
results challenge the assumption that ESG investments offer a safe haven in periods of market
distress, suggesting instead that their appeal is closely tied to prevailing market stability.
