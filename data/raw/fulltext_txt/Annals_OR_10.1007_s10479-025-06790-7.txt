Abstract
In the dynamic and uncertain landscape of modern supply chains, a significant challenge 
for supply chain network (SCN) architects is ensuring that their designs consistently de­
liver reliable performance amidst variable traffic demands and unpredictable failures. This 
assurance is particularly difficult due to the exponential and potentially uncountable set of 
scenarios that must be considered for evaluation. In response to this challenge, we propose 
an optimization-theoretic framework to assess the performance of supply chain networks 
by incorporating flexible routing strategies amid system uncertainties. By modeling net­
work failures and uncertain demand requirements, our methodology tackles the inherent 
intractability of these certification problems and provides valid upper bounds for the link 
utilization of the most congested link and the total unmet demand, essential metrics for 
maintaining reliable network operations. We leverage optimization and convexification 
techniques to optimize the SCN design under disruptions and demand variation. Through 
rigorous evaluations on various supply chain network topologies, our results demonstrate 
the efficacy and promise of our approach. Our framework not only provides a systematic 
way to certify SCN performance but also serves as a tool for supply chain architects to 
enhance resilience against unforeseen disruptions. Ultimately, our research contributes to 
the development of more reliable and resilient supply chains in an increasingly volatile 
global environment.

1  Introduction
As supply chain networks (SCNs) play an increasingly critical role in global operations, 
the demand on their underlying infrastructure continues to rise. Given the growing reliance 
on SCNs, it is essential to ensure their seamless operation with acceptable performance, 
even in the face of significant uncertainties (Qiang and Nagurney 2012; Ramaa et al. 2009; 
Chen and Gong 2013). To remain effective, SCNs must achieve their performance objec­
tives while adapting to dynamic and unpredictable operational challenges. The global scale 
and rapid evolution of networks imply that failures and uncertainties in demands are the 
norms, and the complexity of uncertainties is also on the rise [see, for instance (Hollmann 
2011; Rezapour et al. 2015; Salem and Haouari 2017; Gupta and Maranas 2003; Chopra 
and Sodhi 2004; Miles and Snow 1992; Govindan et al. 2017)]. When developing SCNs, 
maintaining consistent performance while keeping costs manageable is essential. However, 
this objective is difficult to achieve due to the inherent instability of SCN links, which can 
be disrupted by both anticipated/ planned and unforeseen events (Adenso-Díaz et al. 2018; 
Yang et al. 2021; Peng et al. 2011; Hosseinalipour et al. 2019). Additionally, fluctuations in 
demand contribute to varying network traffic, further complicating performance evaluation 
and its optimization (Tsiakis et al. 2001; Gupta and Maranas 2003; Tolooie et al. 2020; Hsu 
and Li 2011; Davizon et al. 2023). The practical significance of these challenges is further 
underscored by case studies and data presented in Baghalian et al. (2013); Govindan and 
Fattahi (2017); Torabzadeh et al. (2022); Moosavi and Seifbarghy (2021), which highlight 
the tangible impact of failures and demand uncertainty in real-world SCN operations.
Certifying that the performance of a SCN is reliable or acceptable amidst a range of fail­
ure scenarios and uncertain demand requirements is challenging because the number of sce­
narios to consider are typically exponentially many, and may even be non-enumerable. For 
example, a critical requirement is to verify whether a service network with |E| links can sus­
tain uncertain demand requirements under any combination of f-simultaneous link failures 
(Ramaekers et al. 2007; Chang et al. 2019; Chandra and Tawarmalani 2022). The number 
of possible failure scenarios to consider in this case is given by

disruption and demand fluctuation becomes computationally prohibitive.
There is a large gap between practice and the existing theoretical tools to address the 
SCN performance certification problem. Traditional robust optimization (RO) (Bertsimas 
et al. 2011; Ben-Tal et al. 2009) allows estimating the worst-case performance of the SCN 
amidst multiple scenarios of interest [see, for example (Bertsimas and Thiele 2006; Tang 
2006; You and Grossmann 2008; Qiu and Wang 2016)]. However, these RO approaches 
ensure their tractability by making a conservative assumption that the SCNs can not adapt to 
changes in demand or link states (Zokaee et al. 2017; Bertsimas and Thiele 2004) or admit 
limited forms of adaptation (Bertsimas et al. 2009; Bertsimas and Goyal 2012; Feige et al. 
2007). However, in practice, SCNs do adapt by re-routing traffic as demands shift or failures 
occur, and such adaptation can make SCN operations much more realistic and efficient.
Given the large gap between theory and practice, the process of SCN performance certifi­
cation today is very ad-hoc, often requiring extensive simulations, which are not only highly

in the service network literature [see, for instance, (Adenso-Díaz et al. 2018; Ramaekers 
et al. 2007)]. In addition, we address the challenge of certifying SCN performance under 
uncertain future demand requirements, which may be projected using predictive models 
based on available historical demand data. This direction is particularly motivated by the 
pressing need to design service networks that remain effective under demand uncertainty–a 
topic that has received considerable attention in the literature [see, for example, (Baghalian 
et al. 2013; Pan and Nagi 2010; You and Grossmann 2008; Mo and Harrison 2005)]. The 
key novelty in our framework is that it can accommodate a richer set of SCN adaptation 
mechanisms by allowing flexible routing strategies for re-routing SCN traffic amidst link 
failures and changes in demands. When flexible routing strategies are considered, certifying 
the performance of a SCN amidst uncertain failures and demands typically requires solv­
ing intractable non-convex (and often non-linear) optimization problems. We address these 
difficulties by leveraging cutting-edge techniques in non-linear optimization literature (Lee 
and Leyffer 2011). These techniques, notable for their generality, apply to a wide range of 
SCN performance metrics and uncertain network disruptions.
Our paper offers several important contributions. We develop a tractable and provably 
sound framework for SCN performance certification that accounts for adaptive routing deci­
sions–addressing a core limitation of traditional robust optimization methods. Beyond its 
theoretical innovations, the framework is intended to support SCN architects in making 
informed design and managerial decisions by quantifying the interplay between SCN per­
formance and link capacity augmentation. The proposed framework will serve as both a 
diagnostic and prescriptive tool: diagnostically, by identifying critical failure scenarios that 
compromise network performance; and prescriptively, by informing strategic link capacity 
enhancements to bolster network resilience. Another key contribution of the paper lies in 
the generality of the proposed certification framework. Its provable guarantees and com­
putational scalability also suggest potential for broader applicability across other service 
infrastructure networks operating under uncertainty, such as electrical power networks (Yeh 
et al. 2024), telecommunication networks (Chang et al. 2019; Chandra and Tawarmalani 
2022), and medical services (Shishebori and Babadi 2015).

2  Reliability certification problem
A SCN consists of (i) constant parameters like the network topology itself, which cannot be 
changed (or is expensive to change) across uncertain network disruptions; and (ii) adaptive 
parameters, like the traffic/flow routed on the links of the SCN, that may be chosen flexibly 
depending on the state of the SCN. For our work, certifying that the chosen constant param­
eter i.e., the SCN topology, performs reliably/ acceptably across a set of network-related 
uncertainties is referred to as the SCN performance reliability certification problem. For 
ease of reference, we also refer to this problem as the reliability certification problem or 
simply the certification problem.
Let X denote the uncertainty set of demands or failures over which a given SCN design 
must be certified. The design includes all parameters that must remain constant/invariant 
with changes in demands and the occurrence of failures. The SCNs respond to failures, and 
changes in demand by rerouting flows in the best possible way to minimize/maximize a 
given objective function. Let y denote the parameters determined by the SCN when adapting 
to a scenario x ∈X. Formally, the SCN reliability certification problem may be written as:

The inner minimization captures that for any given x ∈X, the SCN determines y in a man­
ner that minimizes an objective function g(x, y) from a set of permissible strategies Y(x). The 
outer maximization robustly captures the worst-case performance across the set of scenarios 
in X, assuming that the network adapts in the best possible manner for each x. In our work, 
we focus on minimizing: (i) link utilization of the most congested link in the SCN (referred 
to as the maximum link utilization hereafter), and (ii) total unmet demand, as described in 
Sect. 2.1. We refer to (1) as the reliability certification problem since it can be used to verify 
if a chosen SCN design meets a desired performance objective threshold. For instance, 
when applied to a SCN design, if g(x, y) models the maximum link utilization in the SCN, 
then g∗> 1 indicates that the SCN is not sufficiently provisioned to handle all failure sce­
narios and changing demands of interest. Given scenario ¯x ∈X, the inner minimization 
problem miny∈Y (¯x) g(¯x, y) is generally easy to solve (a linear program) since the network 
must quickly compute y to adapt to any network state resulting from the uncertainty set. 
The certification problem is however challenging since X could be a continuous set or may 
contain exponentially many scenarios.

2.2  Non-linear formulation
The certification problem in (1) has been represented in a form referred to as a two-stage 
formulation [see e.g., (Bertsimas and Goyal 2012; Bertsimas and Sim 2004; Ben-Tal 
and Nemirovski 1999; Ben-Tal et al. 2009)]. Here, the optimal second-stage variables 
(y ∈Y (x)) depend on the first-stage variables (x ∈X). We simplify this problem by re-
expressing it as a single-stage problem, where all the variables are determined simultane­
ously. In many certification problems, including ours, given ¯x ∈X, the inner optimization 
problem miny∈Y (¯x) g(¯x, y) is a linear program in variables y ∈Y (¯x) for a fixed ¯x, as is seen 
in formulations Mf(·), Lf(·), Md(·), and Ld(·) from Sects. 2.1.1 and 2.1.2. It is well known 
that every LP (referred to as a primal form) involving a minimization objective may be con­
verted into an equivalent maximization LP (referred to as a dual form) which achieves the 
same objective (assuming the dual is feasible) (Dantzig 1991). The certification problem in 
(1), can then be expressed as a single-stage formulation by, first rewriting miny∈Y (x) g(x, y) 
as an equivalent maximization problem using LP duality for a given x, and then adding the 
constraints x ∈X to the dual form to capture the uncertainty set of interest. We leverage the 
dual formulations of Mf(·), Lf(·), Md(·), and Ld(·) to rewrite their corresponding SCN cer­
tification problems (in particular maxxf ∈X Mf(xf), maxxf ∈X Lf(xf), maxxd∈X Md(xd), 
and maxxd∈X Ld(xd)) as a single-stage formulation in Sects. 2.2.1 and 2.2.2.

3  Making SCN certification problems tractable
In Sect. 2.2, we constructed the single-stage formulation for the SCN certification problem 
in (1). We observe that these problems are typically intractable. Given the intractable and 
NP-hard nature of these certification problems, we do not solve them to optimality but rather 
seek ways to obtain quick upper bounds on the true optimal value of (1). Moreover, since 
our purpose in certifying a SCN design is to ensure that the design is acceptable, an upper 
bound that satisfies the design criteria is sufficient. We will be leveraging non-linear pro­
gramming techniques to come up with such upper bounds. Next, in Sect. 3.1, we introduce 
one such upper bounding approach. We then discuss how it applies to our SCN certification 
cases involving uncertain failures (A1) and demands (A2) in Sects. 3.2 and 3.3 respectively.
3.1  Relaxing certification problem
Our approach relaxes the certification problem into a tractable linear programming problem. 
This relaxation then allows us to obtain an upper bound on the SCN performance across all 
scenarios in the uncertainty set X. An optimization problem L is a relaxation of a problem 
N if (i) every feasible solution in N can be mapped to a feasible solution in L , and (ii) 
the mapped solution’s objective value in N is no better than that of its mapping in L . We 
exploit the Reformulation-Linearization Technique (RLT) (Sherali and Adams 2013), which 
is a general approach to relax non-linear optimization problems. The technique reformu­
lates the non-linear problem by first adding new constraints obtained by taking products 
of existing constraints and then linearizing the resulting formulation by replacing mono­
mials with new variables. For instance, let us consider a non-linear optimization problem 
where the objective is to maximize xy + x −y over the variables (x, y), subject to con­
straints (i) xy + x ≥0, (ii) 8 −x ≥0, (iii) y −2 ≥0, and (iv) 5 −y ≥0. The RLT relax­
ation for the above problem is obtained by first taking the products of pairs of constraints. 
For example, the product of constraints (ii) and (iii) results in a newly derived constraint 
(8 −x)(y −2) ≥0, i.e., 8y −16 −xy + 2x ≥0. The product term xy is then replaced by 
a new variable z. The objective function and the constraint (i) xy + x ≥0 are also rewritten 
1 3
1160

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 361:1151–1181
as z + x −y and z + x ≥0 respectively. The resulting problem is a linear programming 
problem since it does not have product terms, however, it is a relaxation of the original non-
linear problem, in the sense that constraint (e.g., z = xy) that must be present to accurately 
capture the original problem are not included in the new problem.
The formulation obtained by the above explanation represents the first step in the hier­
archy of the RLT relaxations and to go higher in the hierarchy, we multiply more than two 
constraints and linearize the nonlinear terms as discussed above. Going higher in the hier­
archy guarantees tighter bounds, but the corresponding LPs generated become very large 
(more constraints and variables are introduced), see for reference (Handelman 1988; Sherali 
and Adams 2013). Thus, we restrict ourselves to the first level of the RLT relaxation. Fur­
thermore, in practice, it is often sufficient to consider a subset of the product of constraints 
even in the first level of hierarchy, which keeps the complexity of the resulting relaxation 
problem manageable. In Sects. 3.2 and 3.3, we apply the above RLT relaxation technique to 
our SCN certification cases A1 and A2 respectively.

4.3  Operational implications of solving certification problem
The proposed approach in this paper, not only aims to solve the SCN certification problem 
but in Sect. 4.3.1, we show that the approach also assists in identifying critical scenarios 
within the uncertainty set that significantly degrade the performance of the SCN. Gaining 
prior knowledge of these adverse scenarios enables network architects to implement proac­
tive measures to mitigate their effects, thereby offering substantial financial and operational 
benefits. Additionally, in Sect. 4.3.2, we show that our methodology contributes to solv­
ing the capacity augmentation problem by identifying key links where capacity expansion 
would enhance the SCN’s ability to accommodate failures. Strengthening these critical links 
improves the overall robustness of the SCN, ensuring greater resilience against disruptions.

5  Conclusion
This paper presents a novel reliability certification framework for SCN design under uncer­
tainty. The proposed framework enables SCN designers to evaluate whether a given config­
uration can sustain acceptable performance across exponentially large and non-enumerable 
sets of potential link failures and demand fluctuations. By employing first-level Reformula­
tion-Linearization Technique relaxations, we offer a tractable linear programming approach 
to approximate these inherently NP-hard certification problems and provably solve the cer­
tification problem for predicted demands. These relaxations not only yield reliable upper 
bounds on performance degradation due to uncertain failures and demands but also under­
pin an efficient branch-and-bound (B-B) algorithm for identifying critical failure scenarios.
From a theoretical standpoint, our work contributes to the literature by demonstrating how 
RLT relaxations can effectively bound large-scale certification problems in SCNs. Addition­
ally, we show that identifying unfavorable failure scenarios–another NP-hard problem–can 
be addressed efficiently within our integrated framework. Together, these results enhance 
the methodological toolkit available for robust SCN design and analysis. On the practical 
side, our approach equips SCN designers with actionable insights to strengthen network 
resilience. The framework facilitates the identification of performance-critical components, 
supports targeted capacity augmentation, and enables proactive mitigation of high-risk fail­
ure scenarios. Combined with our capacity augmentation scheme, the B-B algorithm helps 
improve the overall performance of the SCN in a cost-efficient manner. In contrast to tra­
ditional enumeration-based techniques, our method is both computationally efficient and
