Abstract
In this paper, we solve a continuous-time dynamic optimal portfolio allocation problem 
in a complete market setup, by considering non-affine dynamics for the state variables, 
since we model precision instead of volatility, intended as the reciprocal of volatility, 
where both the stock and variance processes are subject to discontinuities. Such non-affine 
framework ensures a closed-form expression for the optimal weights of all the financial 
assets involved, namely a riskless security, an equity and traded derivatives on such equity. 
The solution to the optimal allocation is genuinely dynamic, consistently with the well-
known Markowitz economic intuition that an inverse dependence of the portfolio expo­
sures with respect to the variability parameters should be observed. In order to uncover 
the role of jumps in the optimal allocation, we numerically investigate some instances of 
the non-affine model proposed and analyze the corresponding risk profile. Furthermore, 
we perform a calibration procedure on real data, revealing that the performances of our 
investment strategy greatly benefits from the presence of both derivatives and co-jumps.

1  Introduction
Two factors largely influence the choices in financial investments, namely the investors’ 
willingness to withstand risks and the market structure. Therefore, an important point is 
to choose an appropriate model, able to correctly include some notable stylized facts, such 
as the time-dependence of volatility, see e.g. Heston (1973), and the discontinuity of pro­
cesses’ trajectories, see e.g. Duffie et al. (2000). More recently, a popular strain of litera­
ture has focused on modelling another well-known stylized fact, the so called leverage 
effect, whereby surges in volatility correspond to downturns in financial asset prices, see e.g. 
Eraker (2004), Eraker et al. (2003), Bandi and Renò (2016), Todorov and Tauchen (2011), 
and references therein.
Both volatility and event risks play a prominent role within the dynamic optimal alloca­
tion framework, where it is crucial to furnish a consistent procedure, both from an economic 
and a mathematical perspective. The financial literature delivers a large amount of solutions, 
especially focusing on the importance of including jumps when assuming a CRRA utility 
function to describe the satisfaction of decision-makers. For incomplete markets, Liu et al. 
(2003) consider a stochastic volatility model with simultaneous jumps, and provide closed-
form solutions to the dynamic optimal allocation problem, while Das and Uppal (2004) 
focus on systemic risk in a multivariate jump-diffusion model with constant volatility. The 
same framework has been further investigated in Aït-Sahalia et al. (2009), where a con­
sumption-investment problem is solved by providing a suitable orthogonal decomposition 
for Brownian risks, and generalized in Sbuelz (2018), where also default risk is involved. A 
dynamic portfolio allocation problem with time-varying jump risk is analyzed in Maheu and 
McCurdy (2004) and Zhou et al. (2002, 2019) under a GARCH-ARJI model. Postulating 
geometric Levy processes for risky assets, Corcuera et al. (2006) solve an optimal portfolio 
allocation problem in incomplete markets, showing that selecting power-jump discontinui­
ties is sufficient to make the market complete.
Typically, when there is only one source of noise associated with the asset price evo­
lution, the literature on optimal allocation problems tends to exclude derivative contracts 
from the portfolio, as such securities can be perfectly replicated through the underlying, 
thus being redundant. However, when more risk factors are introduced to keep the market 
scenario as realistic as possible, the value of exchange-traded derivatives may show sensi­
tivity to a number of risk factors which the underlying cannot simultaneously hedge, and 
such sensitivity must be exploited in the portfolio strategy: doing otherwise would result in 
a greater opportunity cost for the asset manager.
Therefore, additional financial securities susceptible to the whole range of risk compo­
nents must be included in the picture to complete the market. A natural choice is thus the 
inclusion of derivative contracts on the underlying portfolio equity. Liu and Pan (2003) 
study the impact of options on wealth allocation by considering only jumps in price. Hsuku 
(2007) exploits non-separable preferences without allowing jumps, while Branger et al. 
(2008) enrich the framework by assuming the presence of co-jumps and CRRA utility. More 
recently, Cheng and Escobar-Anel (2021) consider an optimal allocation problem with both 
risk and ambiguity aversion under a 4/2 model. However, the derivatives market for options 
can become particularly illiquid and difficult to rebalance during periods of market stress. 
As a result, a more recent strand of the literature has shifted attention toward volatility

Annals of Operations Research
derivatives as alternative hedging instruments, see e.g. Bahaji and Aberkane (2016) and 
references therein, particularly in the context of portfolio insurance strategies.
Although far from exhaustive, the foregoing literature reveals a common approach, 
namely the use of an affine structure for the model. Moreover, as noted in Chacko and 
Viceira (2005) and detailed in Oliva and Renò (2018), such a choice has strong repercussions 
on the economic aspects of the problem, although it is perfectly sound from a mathematical 
point of view. The difficulty resides in the fact that in affine models the optimal allocation 
does not depend on the market variability, contradicting the foundations of the Markowitz 
portfolio theory, which theorizes and demonstrates a relationship of inverse proportionality 
between the invested wealth and the synthetic indicators of variability. Moreover, this also 
contradicts the empirical evidence, according to which, in case of an unexpected increase 
in volatility, the investor will rebalance the portfolio by reducing the allocation to the risky 
asset, see e.g. Moreira and Muir (2017), for further details. To overcome such a drawback, 
one solution is to resort to non-affine models.
This paper contributes to the existing literature along the following lines. First, the risky 
asset is assumed to evolve according to jump-diffusion dynamics, and the state of the econ­
omy is defined in terms of the precision, i.e. the stochastic process reciprocal to the variance; 
in this sense, we generalize the non-affine model introduced in Chacko and Viceira (2005). 
Second, we assume simultaneous jumps in price and precision, namely jumps are driven 
by single Poisson process; in this regard, we extend the work of Branger et al. (2008) to 
non-affine cases. Third, we complete the market by adding a fixed number of non-redundant 
derivatives; in this way, we enhance the contribution of Liu and Pan (2003) in a non-affine 
framework. Fourth, we aim to maximize the expected power utility of investors’ terminal 
wealth without considering intermediate consumption including derivatives, precision, and 
co-jumps, expanding the findings provided in Das and Uppal (2004) and Liu et al. (2003). 
Fifth, we can provide closed-form formulae for the optimal allocation in stock and deriva­
tives, as well as in the exposures to risk factors governing the market, by using dynamic 
programming techniques, without resorting to numerical procedures, continuing the work 
of Oliva and Renò (2018).
Our main theoretical result ensures that the optimal weights are state-dependent and 
inversely proportional to the instantaneous volatility, in line with the well known Markowitz 
intuition. Hence, the optimal investment results to be genuinely dynamic, since the myopic 
component depends on the volatility level. This is at odds with the affine literature, where 
the myopic component is always constant. We also show that derivatives represent a way 
to manage the additional sources of risk that intervene when a stochastic model with co-
jumps is used. The optimal exposures to diffusive, volatility and jump risks for long-horizon 
investors consist of two terms. The first one is a myopic component, that guarantees the 
risk-and-return trade-off à la Merton. The second one is a hedging component, required 
by investors who want to exploit the time dependence of the investment. Such a hedging 
component is responsible for balancing allocation in presence of sudden and unfavourable 
changes in volatility and returns. Consequently, derivatives allow to avoid any overestima­
tion of diffusive risk. As expected, diffusive and volatility exposures are proportional to the 
corresponding risk premia, and are inversely proportional to volatility, in contrast with the 
portfolio allocation provided by affine models.
By calibrating the model on real data, we investigate two particular cases, to highlight 
the main features of our findings. When assuming no jumps in the dynamics, we show that:

(i) the demand for derivatives is mainly driven by the myopic demand; (ii) as the stock 
attractiveness increases, the corresponding allocation swells as well; (iii) when investors 
become more risk averse, they reduce their allocation to both stock and derivatives; (iv) 
when the initial volatility raises, the position in derivatives and equities goes down. On 
the other hand, when assuming constant volatility, we obtain that jumps in price affect the 
overall optimal portfolio allocation, since investors take a larger position in both stocks and 
derivatives in case of (absolute) jump amplitude reduction. Furthermore, we quantify the 
role played by derivatives in portfolio management in terms of Certainty Equivalent Wealth 
(CEW).
The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the model we use, and motivates 
the introduction of additional financial instruments in the market. In Sect. 3 we explicitly 
solve the optimization problem and explain in detail the economic aspects emerging from 
our theoretical results. The role of derivatives, both in the presence and absence of discon­
tinuities in the state variables’ dynamics, is detailed and empirically illustrated in Sect. 4. 
Section 5 concludes. Technical details are provided in Appendices.

Motivated by empirical evidence, see e.g. Bandi and Renò (2016), we assume that jumps 
in price are simultaneous to jumps in precision, meaning that the jump risk is driven by a 
single jump process. Moreover, jumps in price differ from jumps in precision only in terms 
of the corresponding amplitude. In general, the jump sizes J and ξ are random variables with 
suitable distributions, and they can be considered independent across jump times and inde­
pendent of B, Z, and N(λ). We may also consider the random variables J and ξ to be jointly 
distributed with non-zero correlation. However, in order to present a complete market setup, 
we will need the assumption that both J and ξ attain at most a finite number of values.
The model introduced above encompasses several sources of risk, namely the diffusive 
price shocks, diffusive precision shocks, and the presence of jumps, both in the asset price 
and in the state variable dynamics. In this setting, the optimal portfolio must then comprise 
a set of assets that allow to gain exposure to all these risk factors and access the premia they 
bear, something which an allocation in the stock alone is not able to achieve: a pure equity 
portfolio cannot allow for example to take advantage of volatility fluctuation, or disentangle 
the jump risk from the diffusive return risk.
The market must be completed with a non-redundant set of instruments, any of which 
is sensitive to the whole range of market risks. Following Liu and Pan (2003), by non-
redundant we mean that the set of additional securities considering total price sensitivity, 
once all the risk factors are considered, is not the same for any two assets. Effectively, this 
makes possible to build portfolios that are sensitive to each of the individual risks across the 
whole holding period.

contingent claims maturing at time T are traded in the market, and defined in terms of their 
price at time t ∈[0, T], say O(i)(t), i = 1, . . . , n. In general, the prices of these claims will

3.1  Analysis of the optimal allocation
We first note that Proposition 1 can be both read in terms of (optimal) allocation of market 
components, i.e. stocks and derivatives, as well as with regards to the (optimal) exposure to 
the sources of risk supporting the wealth dynamics, as in Branger et al. (2008) and Liu and 
Pan (2003). In order to provide additional economic insights to our result, we first look at 
the exposures. By inspecting (26a) and (26b), we distinguish two terms, which respectively 
represent the myopic and the intertemporal hedging components for optimal allocation. 
As expected, for θB,⋆(t), the former is proportional to the premium η for diffusive risk, 
as it increases when the attractiveness of the stock raises. The same holds for θZ,⋆(t) : a 
greater myopic risk exposure to the precision risk is due either to an increase of the premium 
demand β or to a smaller risk aversion γ. Interestingly, our optimal portfolio exposures cor­
responding to the diffusive price stock B and the additional precision risk Z are inversely 
proportional to instantaneous volatility. This is the crucial difference with the affine models 
literature. Specifically, a myopic component proportional to the inverse of volatility reflects

the static mean-variance allocation, reconciling the dynamic asset framework with Mar­
kowitz theory.

5  Conclusions
We studied a dynamic optimal portfolio allocation problem in a non-affine jump diffusion 
model, where the investor can access not only to a bond and an equity, but also to derivatives 
written on such an equity, and we assumed the underlying price to be subject to simultane­
ous jumps in price and volatility. We modeled the instantaneous volatility as the process 
reciprocal to that of a familiar square-root diffusion, and were able to solve the problem in 
a closed form. By analyzing the risk exposures and optimal portfolio allocation, we showed 
that the optimal allocation depends on the stochastic determinants of the jump process, and 
as a consequence of the non-affine framework we assumed is strongly affected by the preci­
sion process. Our results suggest that investors need to rebalance their portfolios after a mar­
ket crash, the latter being mathematically depicted via the discontinuous component driven 
by a non-compensated Poisson process. Our findings suggest that portfolio rebalancing fol­
lowing a large market drop can significantly enhance investor utility when co-jumps are 
taken into account. However, in real markets, rebalancing through equity options might be 
constrained during periods of turmoil. A natural extension of this work would be to explore 
volatility-linked instruments, such as VIX futures, which remain more liquid during stressed 
conditions and often exhibit predictable patterns around market crashes. Incorporating these 
instruments into the allocation problem could offer more robust hedging opportunities in 
crisis periods.
In line with suggestions from previous literature, we consider a crash to be a jump of 
sufficiently large magnitude in the equity price, such as one triggered by macroeconomic 
shocks or extreme volatility spikes. As shown in our empirical results, following such a 
jump, the optimal strategy shifts toward a lower allocation to risky assets and a lesser use 
of derivatives, particularly when they offer downside protection. Moreover, we experienced 
a significant portfolio improvement in terms of wealth for the investor when she has access 
to traded derivatives.
Finally, we demonstrated a significant reduction of wealth when investors do not include 
jumps in their model, while supporting the opportunity of inclusion of jumps when the 
dynamics of wealth are based on an inverse square-root diffusion model.
The framework presented in this paper is quite general, but can be further extended 
by relaxing the assumptions on deterministic jump amplitude and frequency. A further 
promising route of research concerns the introduction of ESG-like factors within the util­
ity function to handle the sustainability aspects of agents’ preferences. Moreover, to more 
clearly highlight the role of volatility as an investable asset, one possible direction would 
be to replace equity derivatives with volatility-linked derivatives, thereby extending the
