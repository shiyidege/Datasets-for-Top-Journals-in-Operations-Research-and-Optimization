Abstract
Many real-world systems, including supply chains and manufacturing, have been modeled as
multi-state networks (MSNs) to assess network reliability performance. This paper introduces
an arc-wise architecture designed to enhance the efﬁciency of a multilayer perceptron (MLP)
model for MSN reliability estimation. By representing each arc with a dedicated subnetwork
and incorporating system-level requirements directly into the input layer, the proposed MLP
effectively maps complex dependencies between arc capacities and system-level reliability.
Integrated with Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, the proposed approach
is validated through comprehensive numerical experiments. The results demonstrate high
estimation accuracy and efﬁciency, highlighting the efﬁcacy of deep learning for reliability
analysis in complex, large-scale MSNs.

1 Introduction
In the real world, many systems including supply chains, manufacturing, and communication
systems, are frequently modeled as networks composed of arcs and nodes for performance
evaluation. Multi-state networks (MSNs) further capture real-world complexity by allowing
123

Annals of Operations Research
arcs and nodes to have varying operational states rather than a binary state (Huang et al.,
2025; Kuo et al., 2026; Nguyen et al. 2023). Speciﬁcally, in an emergency response system,
the MSN can effectively represent rescue routes, with each arc corresponding to a road’s
accessibility level, ranging from fully operational to entirely blocked (Huang et al., 2025).
Evaluated widely as a measure of performance, network reliability is able to indicate the
capacity of MSNs to deliver required demand between origin and destination nodes under
speciﬁed constraints (Niu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). For instance, logistics network
reliability represents the likelihood of completing order deliveries within a speciﬁed spoilage
rate (Niu et al., 2025). In computer networks, reliability refers to the probability of successful
datatransmissiontoserversbeforeagivendeadline(Huangetal.,2022a;Yeh,2020).Inhybrid
ﬂow-shops with stochastic capacity, reliability typically denotes the probability of meeting
production targets within time and resource constraints (Lin et al., 2016).
NetworkreliabilityforMSNsisusuallyassessedbasedonminimalpaths(MPs).MP-based
approaches typically identify all MPs, generate demand boundary vectors, and compute
network reliability based on the corresponding probabilities. Chen and Lin (Chen & Lin,
2016) developed a path reordering strategy to reduce the production of infeasible vectors.
After determining all lower boundary vectors, network reliability can be assessed using
various computational methodologies, such as state-space decomposition approaches (Bai
et al., 2021), the recursive sum of disjoint products (RSDP) (Bai et al., 2015; Zuo et al.,
2007), and inclusion–exclusion principle (Hao et al., 2019). For instance, Kozyra introduced
a parallel algorithm that simultaneously identiﬁes multi-state minimal paths and performs
state-space decomposition for exact reliability assessment (Kozyra, 2024).
However, it becomes computationally challenging for large-scale MSNs due to exponen-
tial growth in paths and boundary vectors because identifying all lower boundary points
is NP-hard. Hence, some studies focus on estimating network reliability instead of exact
value (Kovur et al., 2025; Shekhar et al., 2020). A space decomposition method has been
employed to efﬁciently calculate lower and upper bounds for interval estimates when exact
computation is not feasible (Jane & Laih, 2010). Liu et al. developed an enhanced bounding
algorithm based on state-space decomposition to provide tight reliability approximations for
multi-state networks while mitigating the state-space explosion inherent in large-scale sys-
tems (Liu et al., 2021). Using Monte Carlo method, Ramirez-Marquez and Coit simulated
network capacity states for probabilistic reliability estimates and Behrensdorf et al. employed
percolation theory to exclude low-priority conﬁgurations and estimated the survival signature
through Monte Carlo simulation (Behrensdorf et al., 2021; Ramirez-Marquez & Coit, 2005).
This approach provides an accurate approximation with negligible error while signiﬁcantly
reducing computational requirements. To efﬁciently estimate multi-state network reliability
across varying demand levels, Chang et al. employed minimal path enumeration and opti-
mized state sampling to outperform prior simulation techniques (Chang et al., 2024). Their
methodology represents a signiﬁcant advancement in stochastic analysis for logistics and
computing applications.
The above methods typically depend on structural details like MPs, boundary points, or
set probability distributions, which may lead to inaccuracy and re-execution if any changes
occur. Deep learning and active learning offer alternative approachs to overcome the compu-
tational burdens of exact reliability evaluations. Deep neural networks (DNNs) have proven
effective in capturing complex, non-linear interactions to ensure prediction reliability in soft-
ware environments (Deng et al., 2023). For dynamic physical networks, utilizing DNNs
alongside semi-Markov processes (Bo et al., 2024) and deploying approximated artiﬁcial
neural networks (Kaushik & Banka, 2015) provided powerful computational substitutes that
drastically reduce estimation time while maintaining high predictive accuracy. Furthermore,
123

Annals of Operations Research
artiﬁcial neural network techniques have been utilized to model software reliability and
assess failure probabilities (Wu & Huang, 2021), and long short-term memory architectures
and recurrent neural networks have been successfully employed for the reliability evaluation
of power converters (Baharani et al., 2019). Huang et al. introduced a DNN for network reli-
ability estimation but failed to incorporate essential MSN features like arc independence and
ﬂow-based structure (Huang et al., 2022b). To address this, the study proposes an arc-wise
multilayer perceptron (MLP) model that captures crucial elements of network reliability,
providing interpretability and accurate predictions without requiring prior information like
minimal paths or boundary vectors.
Compared to exact reliability evaluation methods, the proposed algorithm shifts the com-
putational burden entirely to the ofﬂine training phase, delivering instantaneous reliability
estimations for real-time applications with hyperparameters efﬁciently tuned via optimiza-
tion. Furthermore, unlike standard deep learning approaches, the proposed arc-wise MLP
model prevents over-regularization and weight underutilization, thereby yielding higher esti-
mation accuracy. The rest of the article proceeds by ﬁrst reviewing the MP-based approach
to network reliability and the conversion of network data into an MLP format in the second
section. The fundamental concepts of the arc-wise MLP model, including its architecture,
training process, and evaluation metrics, are provided in Sect. 3. Following this, Sect. 4
presents experimental results that validate both the efﬁciency and computational accuracy
of the proposed approach. To conclude, Sect. 5 outlines the primary results and suggests
potential future research.
2 Network reliability and network information transformed
This section reviews an existing MP-based method. Subsequently, we describe the process
of converting network data into the speciﬁc training format required for network reliability
evaluation.
2.1 MP-based network reliability evaluation
An MSN in this study is constructed as a directed network, denoted as G ≡(N, A, M), where
N and A = {ai | i = 1, 2, …, n} denote the set of nodes and arcs, respectively. M contains the
maximal capacity (Mi) of all arcs, {Mi | i = 1, 2, …, n}. A current capacity vector X = (x1,

taking values in the set {0, 1, 2, …, Mi}. Such an MSN G follows the following assumptions
(Huang et al., 2022a; Lin et al., 2016; Yeh, 2020):
(1) Perfect reliability is assumed for all nodes in MSN.
(2) All ﬂows throughout the MSN G follow ﬂow conservation law.
(3) Arc capacities are integer and statistically independent.

2.2 Network information transformation
Within a given MSN G, the capacity of every arc is modeled as a discrete random variable.
Speciﬁcally, Piβ deﬁnes the probability that arc ai functions at a given capacity level β, i = 1,
2, …, n, and β ∈{0, 1, 2, …, Mi}. Derived from historical data, these capacity probabilities
are expressed as an n × v matrix (Pk), where the number of columns (v) is deﬁned as the
maximum capacity of any arc plus one. In this study, an MLP is employed to approximate
the relationship between arc capacity probabilities, system demand, and network reliability
Rd. The network reliability, regarded as the target output used for MLP training is calculated
using well-established analytical methods (Chen & Lin, 2012; Lin et al., 1995), and serves
as the ground truth.
Unlike these approaches mentioned, the MLP does not require MPs or d-MPs in advance.
Instead, the capacities and their corresponding probabilities of each arc, together with demand
d, are directly used as input features. These inputs are concatenated and transformed into a
c-dimensional vector Y = (y1, y2, …, yc), where yo represents the oth feature value and c is
the number of neurons in Y.
To construct training datasets, multiple combinations of arc capacity probability distri-
butions and demand levels are generated, where the demand d varies within a predeﬁned
range [dmin, dmax]. For each data point, the corresponding network reliability is calculated
using the MP-based method described in Sect. 2.1. The overall data generation procedure is
summarized in Algorithm I. For model evaluation, a total of g data points are generated and
subsequently partitioned into training and validation sets at a ratio of p and 1 −p, respectively.

3 MLP model with arc-wise architecture
This section illustrates the arc-wise MLP model, detailing its architecture, activation func-

An MLP, composed of input, hidden, and output layers, is a feedforward DNN that can
approximate complex non-linear functions. For each neuron, the input feature values are
linearly combined using learned weights, and the resulting value is subsequently passed
through a nonlinear activation function. The MLP operates through forward and backward
propagations. During forward propagation, neuron outputs are computed systematically layer
by layer, beginning with the initial data features and advancing past the internal network
structures. Each neuron receives an input value (yo) and weight (wo), computes their products
and adds bias if needed. Let W = (w1, w2, …, wc) be the corresponding weights of input
vector Y and u be the bias term, the output of a neuron before activation is computed as:

Additionally, the performance of the proposed arc-wise MLP model for network reliability
estimation depends greatly on hyperparameters, such as the batch size, the learning rate, and
the number of training epochs, hidden layers, and neurons in the ﬁrst hidden layer (Xiao et al.,

Shami, 2020), whereas the batch size balances computational efﬁciency against gradient
noise (Kandel & Castelli, 2020). Additionally, the number of epochs deﬁnes the total dataset
iterations, requiring precisely setting to avoid underﬁtting and overﬁtting (Sabiri et al., 2023).
Due to the complex interactions among these hyperparameters, manual tuning is inefﬁcient.
To balance model capacity with generalizability, this study utilizes Bayesian Optimization
(BO) for hyperparameter selection (Zhang et al., 2019; Madani et al., 2025; Paladaechanan
et al., 2026). Additionally, large number of input neurons coupled with too few hidden-layer
neurons can hinder model training. Consequently, this study adopts a strategy that gradually
decreases the size (nr) of the rth hidden layer (Bergstra et al., 2011).

5 Conclusions and future research
In conclusion, a novel arc-wise MLP model, optimized via Bayesian optimization, is pro-
posed for estimating the reliability of multi-state networks. The high accuracy of this model is
validated through comparative analyses of demand integration strategies and activation func-
tions. A signiﬁcant reduction in computational burden is also achieved compared to exact
evaluations. Once trained, the model provides instantaneous reliability assessments, facil-
itating rapid, real-time decision-making even amidst ﬂuctuations in demand, capacity, or
probability distributions. These ﬁndings emphasize the potential of deep learning techniques
to advance reliability analysis for complex networks. Furthermore, the proposed architecture
outperforms conventional fully connected networks from existing studies.
