Abstract. In the context of the Amazon Last-Mile Routing Research Challenge, this paper 
presents a machine-learning framework for optimizing last-mile delivery routes. Contrary 
to most routing problems where an objective function is clearly defined, in the real-world 
setting considered in the challenge, an objective is not explicitly specified and must be 
inferred from data. Leveraging techniques from machine learning and classical traveling 
salesman problem heuristics, we propose a “pool and select” algorithm to prescribe high- 
quality last-mile delivery sequences. In the pooling phase, we exploit structural knowledge 
acquired from data, such as common entry and exit regions observed in training routes. In 
the selection phase, we predict the scores of candidate sequences with a high-dimensional, 
pretrained, and regularized regression model. The score prediction model, which includes 
a large number of predictor variables such as sequence duration, compliance with time 
windows, earliness, lateness, and structural similarity to training data, displays good pre­
diction accuracy and guides the selection of efficient delivery sequences. Overall, the 
framework is able to prescribe competitive delivery routes, as measured on out-of-sample 
routes across several data sets. Given that desired characteristics of high-quality sequences 
are learned and not assumed, the proposed framework is expected to generalize well to 
last-mile applications beyond those immediately foreseen in the challenge. Moreover, the 
method requires less than three seconds to prescribe a sequence given an instance and, 
thus, is suitable for very large-scale applications.

1. Introduction
The design of efficient last-mile delivery routes must 
consider several factors, such as customer availability 
and time windows, spatiotemporal congestion pat­
terns, parking availability, and turn restrictions, among 
many others. For tractability, most vehicle routing 
models and corresponding solution methods typically 
assume a single optimization direction (e.g., route 
duration) with eventual side constraints (e.g., time win­
dows). Not surprisingly, experience often shows a sig­
nificant deviation between routing plans prescribed by 
decision support tools and actual routes executed by 
drivers who possess a practical knowledge on delivery 
operations that is not contemplated in traditional opti­
mization models. Unquestionably, being able to elicit 
such tacit knowledge is crucial for the planning of effi­
cient last-mile routes.
Given the difficulty of translating all factors behind 
efficient delivery routes into a tractable optimization 
model, the Amazon Last-Mile Routing Research Chal­
lenge (Challenge 2021, hereafter, “Routing Challenge”) 
called for a data-driven method for designing high- 
quality last-mile routes. In this perspective, relevant 
characteristics of efficient routes must be inferred from 
a training data set with a large number of actual deliv­
ery sequences, which are preclassified by domain experts 
(e.g., drivers) with respect to their quality. This paper pre­
sents a prescriptive analytics framework to acquire and 
learn essential information about such training data and 
prescribe high-quality delivery sequences. Our model is 
similar to the two-stage model discussed in Ben-Akiva, 
Ramming, and Bekhor (2004), where the first stage focuses 
on the generation of a set of alternative routes, and in the 
second stage, a route among the alternatives is chosen. In

Our work is related to inverse optimization for route 
choice modeling where multiple observations of past 
solutions are available, and the goal is to use data- 
driven machine learning (ML) tools to estimate the 
objective function of drivers and their constraints. We 
make use of the observable parameters of the problem 
that are already available from the historical data and 
the decisions made in the past according to these para­

able to suggest new sequences that exhibit similar pat­
terns captured from the data. Moreover, it learns a scor­
ing function to pick the best sequence from the pool of 
solutions. Unlike the traditional static solution methods, 
these two features allow our model to update itself 
dynamically from the new observations in case paradigm 
shifts in driver decisions or preferences occur over time.
We also propose a general mechanism to capture deci­
sion makers’ preferences, and, in the most general setting, 
our idea is applicable to last-mile transportation problems 
that transcend the specific setting faced by Amazon. We 
apply our framework to the Routing Challenge, and the 
results show that our algorithm can learn the hidden 
objective metrics and the drivers’ tacit knowledge to pre­
scribe high-quality last-mile delivery sequences.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: 
in Section 2, we review the relevant literature on last- 
mile delivery, inverse optimization, and ML methods 
for combinatorial optimization; in Section 3, we describe 
in detail our learning framework on the Routing Chal­

driven inverse optimization problem and propose our 
framework to solve it; in Section 5, we present and dis­

we conclude this paper by discussing the key takeaways 
and by providing further research directions.
2. Literature Review
We identify three streams of relevant literature. In Sec­
tion 2.1, we discuss last-mile routing optimization and 
connect the general problem with the framework of 
inverse optimization. In Section 2.2, we review the 
recent but fast-expanding research area of machine 
learning for combinatorial optimization.
2.1. Last-Mile Routing and Inverse Optimization
Last-mile delivery refers to the logistics of freight trans­
portation in the final leg of the journey to the customer. 
Because of the large increase in e-commerce orders, 
logistics service providers face challenges. The pro­
blems of last-mile delivery are typically caused by the 
fact that deliveries are composed of individual orders 
that must be delivered to several different addresses. 
Despite increasingly growing research on optimization 
methods applied to city logistics, there remains a gap 
in the literature concerning the real-world applicability 
of those methods to improve efficiency in the last mile 
(Savelsbergh and Van Woensel 2016).
In the most general setting, route planning constitu­
tes a great proportion of the well-known vehicle rout­
ing problem (VRP; Toth and Vigo 2014). The canonical 
VRP has a known objective and seeks cost-minimal 
routes. In recent decades, however, the number of VRP 
variants has grown quite substantially, as a result of 
the diverse set of applications, especially in last-mile 
delivery (Cattaruzza et al. 2017). A large proportion of 
VRP studies incorporates hard time windows (Savels­
bergh 1985, Gendreau et al. 1998). Nonetheless, as 
also observed from the Routing Challenge data, only a 
relatively small percentage of customers have time 
windows. As a result, innovative solutions based on 
customer-related data available from various sources 
are proposed to achieve high success rates and low 
costs in the last mile. Van Duin et al. (2016) show that 
the delivery efficiency is closely related to (demo­
graphic) characteristics of an area. Florio, Feillet, and 
Hartl (2018) introduce customer availability profiles 
(CAPs) to model customer presence throughout the 
delivery period and improve hit rates. ¨Ozarik et al. 
(2021) and Voigt et al. (2022) use CAPs to solve a rout­
ing and scheduling problem for last-mile deliveries 
with uncertain customer presence.
Contrary to most VRP settings, when real-life cases 
are considered as in the Routing Challenge, an optimi­
zation direction is not explicitly specified because the 
utility of a route is usually latent and probabilistic. 
Therefore, route choices of the drivers must be inferred 
from available data. To this end, there have been stud­
ies that focus on learning drivers’ route choice behavior 
to improve the planning of routes. A great majority of 
the studies collect data on drivers’ route choice behav­
ior from a preference survey, in which several alterna­
tives are shown to participants, and they are asked to 
choose one route based on their preferences. Although 
some studies collect information only on route attri­
butes, such as travel time, travel time fluctuations, 
and fuel costs (Sun and Park 2017), some others also 
include shipping and driver employment terms, such 
as the method of calculating pay and the bearing of 
fuel costs and tolls (Sun et al. 2013). Ben-Akiva et al. 
(2016) and Ding-Mastera et al. (2019) provide insights 
on how to leverage GPS trackers and web-based sur­
veys while collecting data on freight route choices. To 
understand route choices, Agrawal and Peeta (2021)

delivery data. In that regard, the problem at hand is 
most related to data-driven inverse optimization, where 
the goal is to elicit an objective function that explains the 
observed data.
Inverse optimization literature can be discussed under 
two main streams: classical inverse optimization and 
data-driven inverse optimization. The work in classi­
cal inverse optimization has primarily focused on sin­
gle observation cases, whereas data-driven inverse 
optimization models consider more than one observa­
tion. The main difference between these streams lies 
in the assumption of inverse feasibility since, for 
classical inverse optimization, inverse feasibility must 
be satisfied. As real data are usually imperfect and 
noisy, there is no guarantee that this assumption will 
be satisfied (Chan, Lee, and Terekhov 2019). More­
over, working with noisy data is challenging because 
the noise may prevent finding a single set of model 
parameters that satisfies optimality conditions. Data- 
driven inverse optimization models overcome these 
issues by minimizing loss functions and penalizing 
the degree of infeasibility. Aswani, Shen, and Siddiq 
(2018) provide solution frameworks for solving inverse 
optimization models where the measurements of the 
optimal solutions are corrupted by noise. Chan, Mah­
mood, and Zhu (2021) provides a detailed review of the 
methodological and application-oriented literature in 
inverse optimization. Although, as we show in Section 
4, the problem at hand can be cast in the general frame­
work of inverse optimization, our solution method does 
not employ inverse optimization-specific tools and, instead, 
hybridizes classical heuristics with ML, in particular, super­
vised learning methods.
2.2. Machine Learning for Combinatorial 
Optimization
To take advantage of real-life data, reduce the reliance 
on expert heuristics, and exploit similarities between 
instance characteristics, ML methods have been pro­
posed to solve combinatorial optimization problems 
(Bengio, Lodi, and Prouvost 2021). Many works have 
focused on routing problems, such as the VRP and 
the TSP. With the current advances in deep learning, 
several methods have achieved impressive results in 
learning construction and improvement heuristics. In 
particular, Vinyals, Fortunato, and Jaitly (2015) revisited 
the problem and proposed Pointer Networks (PtrNet) to 
learn optimal TSP sequences generated with Con­
corde (Applegate et al. 2011). Following this work, 
Bello et al. (2017) extended the PtrNet to learn tour 
construction policies without requiring optimal solu­
tions via reinforcement learning. Later, other works 
extended the deep learning and deep reinforcement 
learning approaches (Deudon et al. 2018, Joshi, Laurent, 
and Bresson 2019, Kool, van Hoof, and Welling 2019, 
Kool et al. 2021), resulting in lower optimality gaps. 
Recently, other methods considered the task of learning 
improvement policies for the TSP, that is, learning to 
generate new solutions starting from a poor one (da 
Costa et al. 2020, Wu et al. 2021). These deep learning 
methods have achieved promising results for small 
instances, surpassing the solution quality of specialized 
TSP heuristics.
Although these methods achieve impressive results 
in terms of solution quality, they are limited to small 
instances and require long training times and special­
ized hardware to be able to learn effective policies that 
are competitive with simple heuristics such as the Lin- 
Kernighan-Heulsgaun (Helsgaun 2017). Thus, we focus 
our attention on methods that do not require many 
hours of batched computation and specialized hard­
ware. For this reason, to select good TSP tours, our 
proposed method combines expert-designed search 
heuristics from operations research with a classical 
regression model. In the proposed ML approach, the 
objective is to learn a relationship between sequence 
features and solution quality, given a fixed data set of 
previous sequences.
Our method is similar to search algorithms that use 
ML to make informed decisions about promising search 
regions. Previous work on combining search and ML 
considered algorithm selection methods, in which an 
ML model is used to choose the best algorithm out of a 
portfolio of options for a problem instance (Bischl et al. 
2016). Other methods considered ML-guided large 
neighborhood search by learning the decisions about 
destroy and repair operators (Hottung and Tierney 
2020). Closely related methods considered learning 
metrics of the variable-value selections and estimates of 
the objective using an ML model trained offline over 
available solutions (Hottung, Tanaka, and Tierney 2020). 
Although our “pool and select” method generates good 
quality sequences, we point out that one drawback is 
the reliance on an offline ML model that may fail to gen­
eralize to instances outside the training data distribution, 
a common issue in this class of methods (Lombardi and 
Milano 2018).

In this section, we consider the route sequencing prob­
lem (RSP), the specific problem for which a solution is 
sought for in the Routing Challenge. The RSP considers

into consideration. Unlike traditional route optimiza­

indicated by an explicit optimization direction, such as 
total travel time. Rather, it is indicated by domain 
experts (i.e., drivers) who are experienced in their 
delivery zones and have an intuitive understanding of 
the area, infrastructure, and customers they serve. On 
their delivery network, drivers have tacit knowledge of 
decisive information such as traffic conditions, parking 
possibilities, delivery stops to be grouped conveniently, 
or streets that are harder to traverse. To grasp the per­
ception of the experts, it is crucial to learn the charac­
teristics of the preferred routes by using available data 
on traffic conditions of the region, parking possibilities, 
customer preferences, and time windows.
3.1. General Definitions for the RSP
In the RSP, a set S  {1,:::,n} of stops belonging to a 
given route needs to be sequenced. (In the Routing 
Challenge jargon, a route represents an instance of the 
RSP, and not a particular sequence of stops.) We repre­
sent the characteristics of the route by a vector θ. More 
specifically, vector θ corresponds to a specific instance 
of the RSP and holds information related to it (e.g., 
number of stops, delivery zones, time windows, and 
package-level data). Assignment of packages to the 
routes is done prior to route sequencing considering 
the capacity restrictions, and thus, the vehicle capacity 
is not a constraint in the RSP. We are concerned with 
finding a sequence x  (x0,:::,xn) that visits each cus­
tomer node exactly once for the given route. The spe­
cial stop s ∈S indicates the station of the route such 
that x0  xn  s. We denote by X(θ) the set of feasible 
sequences to this route. Furthermore, we let x∗be the 
preferred or optimal sequence associated with the route.
3.2. Available Data
In the Routing Challenge, Amazon provided partici­
pants with data on its last-mile delivery operations con­
taining more than 6,000 historical route sequences from 
multiple delivery stations in the United States. The 
data includes shipment-level information (i.e., delivery 
locations and physical shipment characteristics), transit 
information (i.e., travel distances and times), and route- 
level information (i.e., date of the route, route origin, 
route quality, and vehicle characteristics). Merch´an 
et al. (2022) describe the data set released for the chal­
lenge in great detail. In Table 1 we provide descriptive 
statistics explaining the original data shared for the 
Routing Challenge. For each route in the data set, there 
is a sample route sequence provided with the corre­
sponding categorical quality measure (i.e., high, medium, 
or low). The aim of the challenge is to find sequences of 
stops that are similar to high-quality sequences followed 
by real drivers without any explicit knowledge of objec­
tive functions or driver considerations.
For our learning framework, we discard the medium- 
and low-quality historical sequences. Thus, the training 
and test sets consist only of routes with high-quality 
sequences. We denote by ΘTR and ΘTE the sets of training 
and test routes, respectively, and define Θ  ΘTR ∪ΘTE. 
For the rest of the paper, the superscripts “TR” and “TE” 
denote sets related to training data and test routes, respec­
tively. The participants were asked to develop models 
that use the training data on historically observed routes 
and their stop sequences to find solutions to the RSP for 
new sets of orders in previously unseen routes. These 
new historical stop sequences of the test data set are 
unknown to the model.

3.3. Structural Information
In the actual route sequences provided in the Routing 
Challenge data set, the zones of any two consecutive 
stops were found to be the same around 85% of the 
time. For this reason, macro, micro, and nano zones 
play an important role in our framework. In particular, 
we leverage the structural information conveyed by 
macro, micro, and nano zone transitions observed in 
the training data to generate candidate sequences for 
test routes and to evaluate the quality of proposed 
sequences. This structural information is collected by 
processing training routes as described in Algorithm 1. 
First, procedure COLECTINFO initializes macro, micro, 
and nano zone transition counters (lines 1–3). Then, for 
each route in the set of training routes passed as input, 
the procedure increments the counters according to the 
transitions observed in the actual sequences (lines 
7–13). Stops with unspecified zones are ignored when 
processing the training data (line 5).

The method used for pooling candidate sequences is 
based on the random insertion TSP heuristic (Rosenk­
rantz, Stearns, and Lewis 1977). Random insertion 
(RI) is an “insertion method,” which iteratively builds 
routes by inserting a randomly selected node into the 
position where the incremental travel time is mini­
mized. The difference between RI and the more popu­
lar cheapest insertion heuristic is that, in the former, 
the node to be included in the tour is chosen randomly 
at each iteration. For our purposes, RI is particularly 
advantageous because it generates a diverse pool of 
sequences, is highly efficient, and performs well in 
asymmetric instances (Glover et al. 2001).
The pooling procedure is fully described in Algo­
rithm 2. First, we determine the set of candidate entry 
and exit stops (lines 1–6). A candidate entry (exit) stop 
is a stop whose nano region appears as an entry (exit) 
region in the training data. From the depot (or station), 
the driver must proceed to the entry stop e+, and the 
last node visited must be the exit stop e–. Next, until we 
reach the desired pool size, we randomly select an 
entry and an exit stop from the sets of candidate stops 
(line 9). Procedure SELECTRANDOM biases the random 
selection by favoring stops from nano regions that 
appear more frequently as entry and exit regions in the 
training data. Then, we compute by RI a sequence 
where the first and last stops are fixed to the selected 
entry and exit stops, respectively (line 10). Finally, we 
add the new sequence to the pool (line 11).
After analyzing the structure of sequences in the 
training data, we noticed that high-quality sequences 
tend to serve all stops in a zone before moving to the 
next zone, both at the nano and micro levels. To gener­
ate candidate sequences that follow this same pattern, 
we apply RI on a modified travel time matrix, in which 
travel times between stops from different nano and 
micro zones are penalized by multiplicative factors 1 +
πn and 1 + πm, respectively, where πn,πm > 0 are para­
meters of the framework (see Section 5.1).

3.6. Learning Framework
The main purpose of the learning framework is to train 
and output a model that can accurately predict the 
score of any given sequence to any given route. To 
achieve this, we expand the original set of sequences 
by generating a pool of candidate sequences for each 
route. The candidate sequences are generated based on 
structural information of historical high-quality sequences, 
as explained in Section 3.4. A numerical score is assigned 
to each sequence generated. This score corresponds to the 
deviation between the sequence and the optimal sequence 
for the same route, which is known at training phase. 
Then, a regression model is trained to predict the score of 
arbitrary sequences. The features of the model represent 
certain characteristics of the sequence and the route, and 
the target variables are the scores based on the normalized 
edit distance similarity (see Section 3.5.1) with respect to 
the optimal sequences. The features of the regression 
model hold information on both the route and the 
sequence itself, as well as statistics on the other sequences 
created for the same route, such as the duration of the 
shortest sequence in the pool. The score prediction model 
trained by the learning framework is instrumental to our 
selection algorithm, which picks the sequence with the 
best predicted score from a candidate sequences pool, as 
detailed in Section 3.7.

3.7. Sequence Selection
Given a test route r ∈ΘTE, the procedure to prescribe a 
sequence x ∈X(θr) is described in Algorithm 4. Consid­
ering the structural information acquired from training 
sequences, we first generate a pool of candidate sequences 
(line 1). Then, we compute statistics on the generated pool 
(line 2), as some sequence features are better assessed rela­
tive to other features. For example, instead of taking the 
sequence duration in isolation, it is more sensible to use as 
a score predictor variable the ratio between the sequence 
duration and the minimum duration among the sequences 
in the pool. Next, we remove from the pool sequences that 
are not likely to yield a good score (lines 3–4). The criteria 
used in this step is the ratio between the sequence similar­
ity (to training data) at the nano zone level and the number 
of nano zone transitions in the sequence. The main motiva­
tion for the heuristic pruning step (line 4) is to decrease 
sequence selection runtimes, which are quite constrained 
in the Routing Challenge. Besides, the pruning step isolates 
a promising region of the search space, effectively increas­
ing the average quality of the sequences left in the pool. 
This further decreases the risk that a bad sequence is 
returned as a result of a potentially noisy score prediction 
model. By keeping only promising sequences in the pool, 
we speed up the last step (line 5), which requires extracting 
features with F(·) and evaluating the score prediction 
model Φ(·) for each sequence left in the pool. Finally, we 
return the sequence that has the best predicted score.

more general data-driven inverse optimization pro­

and must be inferred from data. Throughout this sec­
tion, we describe the components of the framework in 
more general terms and relate those with the algo­

4.3. Limitations and Generalizations
We first point out some limitations of the method. In 
general, there are two main sources of bias in our 
framework. First, the bias from the score prediction 
model. That is, even if the pooling heuristic always 
generated the optimal solution, the “select” Step 7 
could still prescribe a suboptimal solution because of 
prediction errors. The second source of bias is induced 
by the pooling heuristic itself. In case the generated 
candidate solutions are consistently “far” from the 
optimal solutions, the framework would perform sub­
optimally even under the assumption of an unbiased 
score prediction model.
The framework generalizes to other problem classes 
as long as some specific ingredients are implemented. 
First, a domain-specific solution dissimilarity metric 
needs to be defined. Specialized domain knowledge is 
also required to gather potentially relevant characteris­
tics of instances and solutions that could affect the solu­
tion quality (Step 1). Second, a specialized solution 
pooling heuristic is required. We believe that this 
requirement is easily attainable in many problem clas­
ses. In particular, any randomized heuristic can serve 
as a generation method. Most metaheuristics for combi­
natorial optimization apply some level of randomization 
for solution construction or improvement (Gendreau 
and Potvin 2005), so they could be readily used as gen­
eration methods within our framework.

5.2. Test Data Sets
To validate the performance of the learning framework 
and the sequence selection algorithm, we partition the 
high score routes from the original data set made avail­

test routes. Then, the learning framework (Algorithm 
3) is applied on the training routes, and the sequence 
selection (Algorithm 4) is applied on the test routes; 
thus, guaranteeing no data leakage. Five such parti­
tions are created, which are referred to as test data sets 
A–E. Considering initially a set ΘTR with all high score 
routes, each data set is obtained by sampling and mov­
ing high-score routes from ΘTR to a test set ΘTE. To this 
end, we apply a stratified sampling method. First, we 
partition all high score routes from ΘTR into a set of 
macro zone buckets Bz, such that Bz  {r ∈ΘTR : P

sets are provided in Online Appendix B. Because our 
framework relies on structural information about entry 
and exit regions and zone transitions, this stratified 
sampling strategy ensures that most zones are well 
represented in both training and test sets.
5.3. ML Models for Predicting Sequence Scores
As anticipated in Section 3.6, the score prediction 
model is trained by Lasso regression. To confirm that 
the ℓ1 norm-based regularized regression leads to accu­
rate score predictions, we compare Lasso with other 
regression methods on the same data sets.
Table 4 reports the out-of-sample mean squared 
error (MSE) obtained from seven regression methods: 
Lasso regression (Lasso; Tibshirani 1996), ridge regres­
sion (Ridge; Friedman et al. 2001), decision trees (DT; 
Breiman et al. 1984), multilayer perceptron (NN; 
Rosenblatt 1961), linear support vector (SVR; Drucker 
et al. 1997), random decision forests (RF; Ho 1995), and 
gradient boost (GB; Friedman 2002). As observed from 
the results, Lasso is the most competitive method for 
predicting sequence scores. In our case, the advantage 
of Lasso goes beyond accuracy. The final (regularized) 
Lasso model contains from 60 to 90 active (i.e., non­
zero) coefficients, out of the more than 1,000 model 
variables. By promoting sparsity in the learned coeffi­
cients, we are able to evaluate faster the predicted 
scores of sequences and therefore increase the number 
of candidate sequences (poolsize) that we are able to 
evaluate in a given computational time.

6. Conclusions
We conclude this paper by summarizing our key contri­
butions and takeaways and by providing directions for 
further improving the proposed solution framework.
6.1. Main Contributions and Takeaways
Real-world last-mile delivery routing is a complex oper­
ational task in which several potentially conflicting 
objectives must be considered. These include, for exam­
ple, route duration, time window compliance, and con­
sistency considerations. We proposed a data-driven, 
inverse optimization-based solution method to answer 
the general problem of optimizing last-mile delivery 
under an unknown optimal “balance” among all objec­
tives. We tested the framework on real-world data from 
the Routing Challenge and verified empirically that our 
method improves on the most sensible baseline in last- 
mile routing, namely, minimizing route duration.
Our solution framework arises from a characterization 
of the route sequencing problem (RSP) as a data-driven 
inverse optimization problem. We begin by leveraging 
data to elicit drivers’ tacit knowledge on efficient delivery 
routes and then train a high-dimensional score prediction 
model to evaluate the quality of arbitrary delivery sequences. 
To avoid the well-known difficulty of learning combina­
torial solutions directly from data, we proposed a two- 
stage procedure to prescribe solutions to the RSP. First, 
we use classical, highly efficient heuristics to generate a 
pool of candidate sequences. Then, we evaluate the pool 
of sequences under the score prediction model and select 
the one sequence with the best predicted score. This 
“pool and select” paradigm is of independent interest 
and may also be useful to solve other hard combinatorial 
problems with multiple objectives with unknown coeffi­
cients. For example, the framework extends readily to 
VRP variants with several objectives.
The empirical results obtained on the real-world 
data confirmed the importance of the optimization 
metrics traditionally adopted in vehicle routing such as 
total travel time, time window performance and rout­
ing consistency. However, they also brought to light 
more subtle metrics that are not as heavily studied. For 
example, the relevance of adopting zone-based routing 
to avoid crossing neighborhoods or subregions too 
often. Perhaps more importantly, the results showed 
that sequence features should not be evaluated in 
isolation, but in combination with instance features. 
For example, consistency may be relatively more 
important in regions that are difficult to navigate. 
Hence, a sensible model to evaluate the quality of solu­
tions should take account of nonlinear relationships 
between solution and instance features.
6.2. Further Improvements
Finally, we provide some avenues for further improv­
ing the performance of the proposed framework. First, 
we note that, although effective, the random insertion 
pooling algorithm used in our framework does not 
explicitly take into account time windows. Given the 
relatively worse performance of our method in routes 
with many time windows, it may be beneficial to use, 
in combination with random insertion, other pooling 
algorithms that exploit time window information to 
generate a higher-quality sequence pool. In most cases, 
feasibility of insertion can be checked easily. One way 
to improve the pool quality in terms of compatibility 
with time windows or other complicating constraints is 
to enforce a minimum share of (time window) feasible 
routes in the pool. To this end, insertions that violate 
the feasibility of a sequence can be avoided when creat­
ing new sequences for the pool. Although considering 
the time windows has a significant impact on the feasi­
bility of a schedule, it also increases the complexity of 
insertion heuristics. Campbell and Savelsbergh (2004) 
provide efficient insertion heuristics that can be useful 
to handle the complexity due to the complicating 
constraints.
Second, our method does not leverage temporal infor­
mation (e.g., day of the week and time of the day of 
routes), physical package information (e.g., dimensions), 
or executor capacity, mostly because in the exploratory 
data analysis no clear relationship between sequence 
scores and such information could be identified. Never­
theless, there might be relevant relationships that our 
model fails to capture because those features are not 
included. Temporal information can be more useful 
when it is combined with external data such as weather 
reports or traffic information for a given day. These data 
can be collected, processed, and analyzed for possible 
correlations with the other features that are considered 
in this study to improve the quality of the solutions and 
better understand route choices. Finally, as discussed in 
Ben-Akiva et al. (2016), driver surveys can also be useful 
to understand routing decisions, especially when cou­
pled with external data such as GPS traces.
