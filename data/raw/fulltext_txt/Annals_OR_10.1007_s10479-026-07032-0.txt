Abstract
End-of-life packaging management has driven circular economy (CE) initiatives world­
wide, such as Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR). This policy establishes metrics for 
producer responsibility in the post-consumer stage, which can be managed through indi­
vidual or collective models, the latter known as Producer Responsibility Organization. The 
implementation of packaging returnability strategies within the framework of EPR, and 
their management through a producers’ organizations, implies several challenges at the 
level of the design and configuration of the supply chain. These challenges include com­
pliance with environmental regulations, system definition, and facility location, among 
others. The literature review highlights a gap in studies addressing this design problem 
with a focus on primary packaging, which is the packaging directly delivered to the end 
customer, and the relation of the actors within the system. Therefore, this article proposes 
a multi-period, multi-product Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model for the 
design of reverse logistics network in returnable packaging systems, implemented in Gu­
robiPy and solved using the Gurobi 10.0 solver. The model supports strategic and tactical 
decision-making for the design and operation of a returnable packaging system, such as 
those for beer bottles, managed by a producer organization within the framework of EPR 
policies. The formulated model is validated through a practical case study conducted in 
Colombia. The results show the model’s ability to generate information that supports de­
cision-making and clarifies the relationships between operational parameters and external 
variables, and among the actors in the system.

1  Introduction
The generation of waste is increasing globally, and by 2050, its volume is expected to rise 
by 70% compared to current figures. The most pressing issue lies in the accumulation of 
plastic and its impact on the environment and marine ecosystems. Approximately two-
thirds of plastic waste originates from materials with a useful life of less than five years, 
of which 40% corresponds to packaging, 12% to consumer products, and 11% to clothing 
and textiles. However, only 9% of this waste is recycled, while 19% is incinerated, 50% is 
landfilled, and the remaining 22% escapes formal waste management systems, eventually 
reaching terrestrial or aquatic ecosystems or ending up in uncontrolled landfilling or incin­
eration (OECD, 2022).
To address this issue, governments have begun to implement waste management policies 
based on CE strategies that are restorative and regenerative by design. These strategies aim 
to keep products circulating within the production chain at their highest possible value, or to 
return them to the environment with minimal impact (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017). 
As an environmental policy instrument, the EPR program is defined as an initiative that 
shifts the responsibility for managing the post-consumer phase of a product’s life cycle—
including single-use packaging—onto manufacturers or producers (OECD, 2016).
One of the strategies implemented under the EPR framework is returnability. This strat­
egy assigns producers the responsibility of collecting post-consumer packaging for reinte­
gration into the production cycle after appropriate inspection and cleaning, thus enabling 
reuse by consumers and reducing new packaging production and waste generation. Once 
the packaging reaches the end of its useful life and shows visible damage, it is removed 
from circulation and redirected—by the same or a different company—into other produc­
tion processes such as recycling or material recovery (Marín Ortiz et al., 2024). Within the 
returnability strategy, economic incentives have been implemented to ensure the return of 
packaging. One such mechanism is the deposit-refund system, in which an initial payment 
(the deposit) is added to the product’s price. This deposit is fully or partially refunded once 
the packaging is returned by the consumer (OECD, 2016).
EPR, and specifically return systems within it, includes several key components: the 
obligated producers (i.e., manufacturers, bottlers, or distributors); the products and materi­
als covered (i.e., packaging and containers); the specific collection and treatment targets 
(i.e., annual recovery percentages); and the mechanisms to ensure compliance (i.e., certified 
annual reports). Producers can assume this responsibility either individually or collectively. 
In the individual model, the producer is directly responsible for meeting the established 
targets, implementing the necessary processes to collect, manage, and recover the materi­
als (OECD, 2016). In the collective model, a Producer Responsibility Organization (PRO) 
acts on behalf of multiple producers to coordinate waste collection and management. This 
organization engages both waste collectors and processors and oversees compliance with 
the established collection and treatment goals as set forth by regulation.
The implementation of the returnability process through a collective model under an 
EPR policy for packaging is illustrated in Fig. 1. The system is composed of two internal 
actors and seven external actors.
The internal actors are the evaluation centers and washing plants, which are manage 
by the PRO. These facilities sort the material and determine whether the packaging is suit­
able for return. The evaluation center sends returnable packaging, along with data on the

The external actors include the consumer, who purchases the product, and the collec­
tion centers, which are consumption establishments such as bars, restaurants, or stores. 
These collection points are responsible for applying the most convenient deposit strategy 
and providing the PRO with information on the amount of material collected. Another rel­

collect material from households and businesses and then sell it to recycling companies. 
Recycling companies collect materials and sell them either to evaluation centers as return­
able packaging or, if defective, to processors. Transformers purchase and trade materials to 
treat packaging that is no longer suitable for reuse, repurposing it for other production pro­
cesses. Governmental authorities are responsible for verifying that the data reported by the 
PRO matches the returnability targets established under EPR policies. Lastly, producers pay 
the PRO for managing the returnable packaging process and for delivering the quantities 
needed to keep their products in circulation. The volume of returnable packaging demanded 
by producers is subject to current regulations.
The design of the returnability system enables the planning and execution of a logistics 
network for retrieving packaging after its initial use. Its implementation under the collective 
model faces several challenges, particularly in improving operational efficiency and sup­
ply chain management. Overcoming these challenges requires developing network models 
that incorporate CE principles, considering material and product flows, demand planning, 
and the establishment of effective return and collection systems, including decisions on 
the location of evaluation centers and washing plants. The development of strategies and 
models in this context supports decision-making in logistics and operations, contributing 
to the enhanced circularity of packaging throughout the supply chain and fostering a more 
sustainable and efficient system.

Despite the growing implementation of EPR policies worldwide, there remains a sig­
nificant gap in understanding how to design and optimize returnability systems when man­
aged collectively through PROs. Most existing studies on return logistics and EC models 
focus on individual producer schemes or on tertiary packaging (e.g., pallets or industrial 
containers), where the logistics network is relatively centralized and involves fewer actors. 
However, the collective return of primary packaging introduces greater complexity due to 
dispersed collection points, heterogeneous packaging types, and the participation of both 
formal and informal actors. These characteristics make it difficult to design efficient, scal­
able, and sustainable returnability networks under the EPR framework—particularly in 
developing countries, where institutional and infrastructural limitations further constrain 
system performance.
In this context, this study proposes a systemic model to support decision-making for the 
implementation of returnability strategies by PROs within the framework of EPR policies. 
By addressing the design and optimization of collective return networks for primary pack­
aging, this research contributes to narrowing the existing gap in the literature and practice. 
Unlike most existing studies, which analyze the issue from the perspective of a single pro­
ducer or a simplified supply chain, this work considers the collective dynamics of multiple 
producers operating under regulatory EPR schemes. Finally, the proposed model is vali­
dated through a case study inspired by the EPR system for packaging in Colombia, bringing 
to light specific features found in developing countries, such as the existence of multiple 
PROs (Castrillón-Ocampo et al., 2025).
The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents a literature review, outlining the 
challenges associated with the design of returnability systems and the analytical tools used 
to address them. Section 3 describes the proposed methodology for the case study and the 
mathematical model. Section  4 presents the results obtained from the experiments per­
formed. Section 5 discusses the most relevant findings. Finally, Sect. 6 concludes the paper 
and outlines future lines of research.
2  Theoretical background
The literature review identifies studies on the use of analytical tools in supply chain manage­
ment, specifically in the context of returnability strategy for packaging. The identified tools 
range from descriptive to prescriptive approaches, with the latter including methods such as 
optimization, simulation, and statistics.
2.1  Classification of supply chain challenges for returnability
The efficient management of supply chains is analyzed across three decision-making levels 
(i.e., strategic, tactical, and operational) and through three main supply chain processes: 
network design, transportation, and inventory, as proposed by Chopra and Meindl (2000). 
Figure 2 presents the classification of the challenges identified in literature. At the network 
design process, key challenges for the proposed returnability system include the imple­
mentation of regulations and performance indicators, which require producers or PROs to 
configure the logistics network to enable material traceability and monitor operational effi­
ciency throughout each stage of the return process (Popovic et al., 2018).

Regarding system considerations, it is essential for PROs to define the entry and exit 
barriers for each actor involved in the system, establish the work plan needed to meet the 
returnability targets and requirements, and set the pricing schemes for the products and 
services offered (Glock, 2017). In terms of facility location, it is necessary to define the 
potential sites for infrastructure deployment, including both evaluation centers for the 
collection and sorting of large volumes of materials and washing plants for cleaning and 
maintenance processes. As highlighted by Marampoutis et al. (2022), the challenge lies in 
determining the number, location, and capacity of these facilities, as well as ensuring the 
robustness of the network. At the inventory process, the network design must incorporate 
inventory models that consider uncertainty and the return lead time of packaging materials 
to ensure a constant material flow (Yang et al., 2018). Furthermore, several challenges must 
be addressed regarding the maintenance of packaging, such as processing, sorting, and sepa­
rating containers for subsequent treatment (Accorsi et al., 2022), as well as the integration 
of technologies that support the cleaning processes.

In sum, the literature review shows that various supply chain challenges have been ana­
lyzed and addressed using prescriptive analytics tools. Specifically, to tackle challenges 
related to facility location, transportation planning and scheduling, inventory management, 
and packaging traceability, studies have employed optimization, simulation, and statistical 
models. The most prominent challenges, with the highest number of associated studies, 
are facility location and transportation planning and scheduling, with five and four arti­
cles respectively, each focusing specifically on tertiary packaging. Moreover, the models 
proposed in the literature overlook key challenges such as regulatory compliance, system 
design, implementation of incentive strategies, and maintenance processes required to 
enable the reuse of packaging—factors that are crucial for defining an effective returnable 
packaging system.
To address these gaps, this study proposes a multi-period, multi-product Mixed-Integer 
Linear Programming (MILP) model. Unlike most previous optimization models, which typ­
ically focus on single-echelon or single-period problems (Chandoul et al., 2009; Duhaime et 
al., 2001; Misra et al., 2019), the proposed MILP simultaneously captures strategic and tac­
tical decisions over multiple periods, including facility adaptation and use, material flows, 
inventory levels, and operational costs. This approach allows for a more comprehensive 
representation of the returnable packaging system by explicitly incorporating regulatory 
compliance, incentive strategies, maintenance processes, and the recovery and reuse of 
packaging—elements often overlooked in the existing literature. Compared to simulation- 
or statistical-based methods, the MILP provides globally optimal solutions under capacity 
and operational constraints, enabling more informed decision-making for the design and 
operation of returnable packaging networks.

3  Methodology
This section describes the optimization problem associated with supply chain network 
design, presents the proposed mathematical formulation, and outlines the case study used 
to validate the model.
3.1  Description of the problem
In the context of an EPR policy, a collective scheme (PRO) consists of a group of producers 
that place their packaged products on the market. The PRO aims to meet packaging return 
targets by establishing a logistics and operations network. Once the products are consumed, 
a proportion of the packaging is collected at designated collection points through various 
strategies, such as direct return by consumers or recovery by waste pickers who gather and 
transport the materials to these sites. Periodically, the collected packaging is transferred to 
evaluation centers, where its condition is assessed. Packaging that does not meet returnabil­
ity criteria is sent to material transformers for use in other production processes. Packag­
ing that complies with the returnability requirements is sent to a washing facility, where it 
undergoes cleaning and additional material inspections. Finally, the packaging that success­
fully completes the preparation process is sold back to producers for reuse in placing new 
products on the market.
The network design for operating the returnable packaging system through the PRO 
involves both strategic and tactical decision-making. At the strategic level, the system’s 
coverage and capacity are defined through the location and sizing of facilities. At the tacti­
cal level, the required flows within the network are determined to maximize system utility 
while meeting established returnability targets. Network design must be considered within 
a multi-product, multi-period framework that captures the limited capacity usage across dif­
ferent packaging types, as well as the long-term effects of facility location decisions and the 
enduring relationships among stakeholders.
3.2  Mathematical model
Let A be the set of collection centers where returnable containers are collected, V the set of 
potential evaluation centers, L the set of potential washing plants to be used, P the set of pro­
ducers to be served by the collective scheme, E the set of types of returnable packaging to 
be marketed, and T the set of time periods considered in the planning horizon. The decision 
variables are classified as strategic and operational levels. Regarding strategic decisions, the 
variables yjt and xjt indicate whether the evaluation center j is adapted and used, respec­
tively, during period t. Note that a facility must be adapted in a given period t, incurring a 
cost, in order to be used in that period or in any subsequent ones. Similarly, the variables 
wkt and zkt indicate whether the washing plant k is adapted and used, respectively, dur­
ing period t. Concerning tactical-level variables, the variables qpijt, rpjkt, upklt represent, 
respectively, the flows of each type of packaging in each time period between collection and 
evaluation centers, valorization centers and washing plants and washing plants and produc­
ers. Finally, the variables icpjt, ippkt define the inventory levels of each packaging type p in 
each period t at the evaluation centers and washing plants, respectively. Figure 3 represents 
the elements of the decision model.

The network design must satisfy the following constraints. Constraint (11) states that, for 
each collection center, the quantity transferred to the evaluation centers cannot exceed the 
quantity collected at that point. Constraints (12) and (13) ensure that the processing capaci­
ties of the evaluation centers and washing plants, respectively, are not exceeded. Constraint 
(14) guarantees that, in each period, the maximum demand of each producer is not sur­
passed. Constraints (15) and (16) limit the maximum number of packaging that can be 
received by the evaluation centers and washing plants, respectively, in each period, based 
on the maximum quantity of material generated by the preceding node—namely, collection 
centers and evaluation centers. The inclusion of Constraint (15) tightens the relaxation of 
the model, thereby reducing computation times, as stated in Cornuéjols et al. (1991). Con­
straint (17) determines the period in which an evaluation center is set up, while constraint 
(18) ensures that the center remains operational from that period onward. Constraint (19) 
addresses the particular case in which the center is set up from the first period. Constraints 
(20), (21), and (22) serve the same purpose for the set of washing plants. Constraint (23) is 
a flow balance constraint that manages inventory over time at the evaluation centers. In this 
equation, for period 1, inventory from the previous period must be considered as the initial 
inventory parameter IVpj. Additionally, the inventory is bounded by the storage capacity of 
the evaluation center, as indicated in constraint (24). Similarly, flow balance and inventory

The model is developed under several assumptions to ensure tractability and general appli­
cability. It assumes that all types of packaging are treated as homogeneous units, regardless 
of their source or characteristics. The capacities of the washing plants and evaluation cen­
ters are considered independent from each other. Additionally, operational costs, including 
rental, adaptation, transportation, and inventory-holding expenses, are assumed to be uni­
form across all facilities.
3.3  Description of case study
Colombia generates approximately 700,000 tons of plastic containers and packaging annu­
ally. Only 32% of this volume is recovered, and a mere 3% undergoes closed-loop recycling, 
in which the material is reused for its original purpose, that is, as containers and packaging. 
The remaining volume is discarded as waste (Rojas, 2023). In contrast, the recovery rate for 
glass packaging has shown substantial improvement, increasing from 12 to 70% as of 2022. 
Nonetheless, the recycling process continues to incur a material loss of approximately 15% 
(Socya, 2023).
In response to these challenges, the Colombian Ministry of Environment issued Resolu­
tion 1407 (2018), which mandates that producers develop and maintain an Environmen­
tal Management Plan for Packaging Waste under the framework of EPR. This regulation 
establishes specific performance metrics and reporting requirements for strategies aimed 
at reducing packaging material volumes, facilitating reuse, extending product lifecycles, 
and minimizing waste generation. Resolution 1342 (2020) subsequently amended this regu­
lation by introducing returnability models, wherein packaging is defined as “conceived, 
designed, and marketed to perform multiple circuits or rotations throughout its life cycle” 
(MMADS, 2020).
To operationalize EPR obligations, several PROs, commonly referred to as collective 
management schemes, have been established. This study presents a case-based application 
focusing on one such PRO, which oversees the reverse logistics and reuse of beer glass 
bottles across multiple Colombian regions. The size of the modeled instance is delineated by 
the input sets provided in Table 3. The system parameters and their corresponding justifica­
tion can be found in the Appendix (see table included).
The calibration of the parameters was conducted by considering several key aspects. First, 
it was assumed that one metric ton corresponds to approximately 2,597 bottles. According 
to information provided by the PRO, the total processing and logistics cost of a return­
able bottle is approximately $0.19, distributed across the different operational processes. 
Additionally, the characteristics of the type 1 bottle were used as a reference to validate the 
operational capacities of the evaluation centers and washing facilities.
4  Computational experiments and results
The model described in Sect. 3.2 was implemented using GurobiPy and solved with the 
Gurobi 10.0 solver. Computational experiments were carried out on a system running 
Ubuntu 24.04.2 LTS, equipped with an Intel (R) Xeon (R) Platinum 8160 processor at 
2.10 GHz and 96 processing cores. Three types of analyses were conducted: first, an assess­
ment of the model’s capability to solve real-world instances; second, a sensitivity analysis to

Although the development of a full stochastic optimization model lies beyond the scope 
of this work, it is important to note that the proposed formulation can be used to capture 
variability in selected parameters through scenario-based analyses, following the principles

A realistic instance is considered for a PRO involving 15 producers that share three types 
of returnable containers, which are collected after use at 25 collection points. It is assumed 
that there are 15 potential locations available for both evaluation centers and washing plants. 
The focus of this analysis is to understand specific system design parameters affect the prof­
its of the PRO, particularly in interaction with exogenous factors such as demand. Specifi­
cally, four parameter groups are analyzed: (i) transportation costs, (ii) operational costs, (iii) 
recovery rate, and (iv) planning horizon of PRO.

4.2.2  Variation in operational costs involved in returnable packaging systems
Returned bottles must undergo a series of processes to ensure their suitability for reuse. 
Accordingly, this study analyzes the variation in unit costs associated with key stages in 
the reverse logistics of returnable packaging, specifically sorting (QC), washing (QL), 
laboratory testing (QB), and deposit (QD). The cost levels considered for the experiment, 
expressed in USD per unit, are as follows: QC = [0.0085, 0.0341, 0.1366], QL = [0.0127, 
0.0512, 0.2049], QB = [0.0085, 0.0341, 0.1366], and QD = [0.0041, 0.0171, 0.0683]. These 
values represent the low, medium, and high-cost scenarios, respectively, for each process.
An analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed to analyze the impact of costs and their 
interactions on total utility. The 98.2% of the variance in the response variable is explained 
by the model. Table 6 shows the variables and interactions that are statistically significant 
(p-value < 0.05).
The results show that all process costs individually have a significant effect on total 
profit. In particular, the washing cost is significant in all interactions and represents the key 
process within the system, where improvements can significantly enhance the profitability 
of the process. Based on the experiments, the critical cost values above which the profit­
ability is zero are analyzed. Table 7 shows that the combination of cost values QC = 0.0439, 
QL = 0.0654, QB = 0.0439, and QD = 0.0217 defines an economic cut-off point, where the 
operating costs associated with the returnable containers surpass the income obtained, mak­
ing the system no longer profitable.

with high adoption levels. Likewise, five demand levels are defined: 10,000, 40,000, 70,000, 
100,000, and 130,000 units. Figure  4 presents the utility obtained for each experiment

The PRO operates within the framework of an existing EPR policy. In the case study, this 
policy sets goals and territorial coverage requirements that increase periodically. The impact 
of considering facilities with different capacities, as well as increasing the dispersion of 
potential facility locations, is to be evaluated in the network design.
In the first scenario, different processing and inventory capacities are considered for the 
evaluation centers and washing plants. Three facility sizes are assumed: small facilities, 
which correspond to the current size used, and medium and large facilities. According to 
the size, adjustments are made to the leasing and adaptation costs using the scaling relation

5  Discussion
The design of a return system for packaging within a collective plan operating under an 
EPR framework requires both tactical and strategic decisions. A mathematical model that 
formulates the associated decision-making problem must not only incorporate these deci­
sion variables but also consider extended time horizons to capture the long-term effects of 
facility location decisions and sustained relationships among stakeholders. This approach 
increases the complexity and difficulty of solving the optimization problem. However, mod­
eling flows as continuous rather than integer variables enables the resolution of decision 
problem instances for real-world scales and sufficiently long planning horizons.
The results demonstrate that the model captures the system’s sensitivity to changes in 
operational parameters, such as transportation and processing costs, and to exogenous vari­
ables and conditions, including demand, recovery rate, and the system’s planning horizon. 
In particular, transportation costs have a significant impact on the system’s profitability, 
strongly influencing both the number of facilities used and their location. This highlights the

6  Conclusions and future work
The proposed model addresses the design of the logistics network for a collective producer 
plan operating a packaging return system within the framework of EPR policies. Unlike 
most previous studies in literature, the returnability problem is approached from a systemic 
perspective rather than from the standpoint of a single company, which more accurately 
reflects the context generated by EPR regulations. Additionally, while most existing works 
focus on tertiary and secondary packaging, only about 7% address primary packaging, 
which is the focus of this study.
The design of the logistics network for the operation of PROs in return systems benefits 
from the use of decision-support models for tactical and strategic planning. These tools 
help identify and understand the relationships and effects of design variables, operational 
parameters, and exogenous conditions that influence system performance. Issues such as 
determining the required infrastructure size and location, as well as the planning horizons 
needed for profitability, are common concerns for decision-makers who require objective 
information for evaluation.
6.1  Recommendations for future work
This work points to several avenues for future research. First, integrating operational deci­
sions such as routing for collection and distribution could help better assess the impact of 
transportation costs on the system’s profitability and feasibility. Second, quantifying envi­
ronmental impacts would enrich the sustainability perspective of the system, extending the 
analysis to include environmental and social dimensions in addition to the economic dimen­
sion discussed in this study. Third, an explicit treatment of uncertainty through stochastic 
programming represents a promising direction, as uncertainty plays a key role in network 
design decisions. While this study shows that scenario-based analyses allow representing 
variability in parameters such as demand, capacity, or operational costs, developing a fully 
stochastic framework—following unified approaches to stochastic optimization—would 
provide deeper insights into decision-making under uncertainty. Finally, the use of heu­
ristics or metaheuristics to solve the model is a promising area for addressing larger-scale 
instances in countries where collective plans include a greater number of producers.
