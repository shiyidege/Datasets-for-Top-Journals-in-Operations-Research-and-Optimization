Abstract
The increasing frequency of supply chain disruptions has made resilience and digital integra-
tion essential. Indeed, ﬁrms that succeeded during the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated
advanced digital and supply chain resilience capabilities, enabling them to mitigate adverse
consequences. Addressing this forefront topic in operations management, the article intro-
duces an innovative max–min best–worst method to guide digital transformation in global
supply chains. This approach aims to maximize resilience capabilities and minimize the
impact of supply chain disruptions. The method is examined through an empirical case study
of a highly digitalized furniture company. The ﬁndings emphasize the importance of redun-
dancy, a robust risk management culture, and adaptability, with raw material delays and
cyberattacks being the most signiﬁcant threats. Moreover, prioritizing the adoption of big
data, digital platforms, blockchain, and advanced control systems is crucial for addressing
ongoing geopolitical disruptions. Finally, the study discusses both theoretical and managerial
implications and suggests avenues for future research.

disruptions · Best–worst method

1 Introduction
Global supply chain operations are undergoing an unprecedented digital transformation. The
process began with the emergence of Industry 4.0 in the last decade (Kagermann et al.,
2013), which has triggered the Fourth Industrial Revolution (Ghobakhloo, 2018; Liao et al.,
2017). During this phase, ﬁrms worldwide are transforming their traditional operations and
gradually evolving into autonomous factories with interconnected smart supply chains.
In this context, innovative technologies such as Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS), the Inter-
net of Things (IoT), and the Internet of Services (IoS) have been adopted to synchronize
both virtual (e.g., digital twins) and physical (e.g., robotic machines) dimensions in real time
within any smart factory (Hofmann & Rüsch, 2017; Lizarralde Dorronsoro et al., 2020). This
means machines, robots, devices, and systems operate autonomously and communicate with
each other with minimal human intervention, enabling the manufacturing of increasingly
customized goods and services. Technologies such as digital platforms, cloud computing,
and Big Data Analytics (BDA) are essential for capturing, processing, storing, and analyz-
ing the large volumes of data generated in smart manufacturing processes (Francia et al.,
2025; Wang et al., 2022; Yang & Zhen, 2025). Simultaneously, the integration of artiﬁcial
intelligence supports operational and strategic decision-making through real-time data anal-
ysis, simulations and process optimization (Culot et al., 2024; Soori et al., 2026). Industry
4.0 also encompasses many other interrelated technological innovations adopted in global
manufacturing operations (Oztemel & Gursev, 2020; Zheng et al., 2021).
During the COVID-19 crisis, the adoption of technologies 4.0 in business operations
helped ﬁrms not only to be resilient against the constant and unpredictable disturbances
generated by the pandemic (Burgos & Ivanov, 2021; Spieske & Birkel, 2021) but also to
ensure the survivability of the intertwined supply chain facing this large-scale disruption
(Ivanov & Dolgui, 2020a; Sarkar et al., 2025). A report revealed that the main technology-
enabled capabilities provided by industry 4.0 technologies during COVID-19 were: i. Remote
work; ii. Supply chain connectivity among the focal ﬁrm, its suppliers and clients for better
visibility, planning and control; iii. Transparency in manufacturing operations and supply
chains (Agrawal et al., 2021). Consequently, ﬁrms are currently aware of the importance of
advancing their digital transformation process by extending it across their supply chain.
This implies the evolution of traditional supply chains in an advanced framework known
as Supply Chain Management (SCM) 4.0 (Frederico et al., 2020; Zekhnini et al., 2021).
Its technologies enable real-time interconnection between a ﬁrm’s manufacturing operations
and the procedures developed at each stage of its supply chain. It thus uniﬁes Procurement
4.0, Manufacturing 4.0, Logistics 4.0, and Warehousing 4.0 into an integrated framework
(Govindan et al., 2022).
The interoperability of these SCM 4.0 technologies enhances speciﬁc resilience capabili-
ties, such as collaboration, responsiveness, and transparency in supply chains, making them
more resilient to long-term disruptions (Frederico et al., 2023). Notwithstanding, the digital
transformation of the supply chain does not replace the need to develop resilience capabili-
ties (Birkel & Müller, 2025). Instead, the adoption of SCM 4.0 technologies should focus on
maximizing the development of critical resilience capabilities among supply chain agents.
In this way, the earlier literature has already veriﬁed that speciﬁc resilience capabilities play
a mediating role between digital transformation and improved supply chain performance in
disruptive scenarios (Li et al., 2025; Qader et al., 2022; Tortorella et al., 2024).
Nowadays, the most disruptive scenarios threatening global supply chains arise from
geopolitical tensions among countries. The outbreak of armed conﬂicts, trade wars, or the

rise of extreme nationalism triggers a series of disruptive effects on global supply chains
that must be minimized (Bednarski et al., 2023; López et al., 2025). The adoption of BDA,
blockchain, artiﬁcial intelligence, additive manufacturing and digital twins has proven to be
effective in mitigating geopolitical uncertainties (Belhadi et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2025),
supply–demand mismatch and process risks (Ali et al., 2024), and even in controlling the
ripple effects originating from supply chain disruptions (Ivanov et al., 2018).
Consequently, there is an imperative need to boost the digital transformation of global
supply chains, considering both resilience and disruption management. Existing research has
overlooked the simultaneous consideration of these issues. On the resilience management
side, a framework of Industry 4.0 technologies has been recently proposed for developing
required capabilities in the supply chain (Ismail et al., 2025). The interactions between the
opportunities provided by Industry 4.0 and the resilience management of supply chains
have been modeled (Ghobakhloo et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2025). Other studies offer
novel hybrid fuzzy Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) methods to prioritize digital
transformation strategies (Alkan & Kahraman, 2023) or assess the critical success factors
in the adoption of SCM 4.0 (Mutambik, 2025), considering resilience capabilities. On the
disruption management side, no research has been found that addresses the disruptive effects
of digital transformation on supply chains.
Therefore, a notable gap exists in the literature regarding the consideration of disrup-
tive effects and resilience capabilities in tandem during digital supply chain transformation.
Indeed, a recent research call has been issued for the development of novel MCDM methods
to determine key technological applications in supply chains in the context of geopolitical
disruptive scenarios (López et al., 2025).
Over the past decade, one of the most frequently applied MCDM methods in the literature
has been the Best–Worst Method (BWM) (Mi et al., 2019; Rezaei, 2015). Its appeal lies
in its ability to yield consistent results while requiring fewer comparisons and allowing for
seamless integration. To the best of our knowledge, this method has not yet been adapted to
optimize criteria in different directions.
Accordingly, the primary objective of this study is to propose a novel max–min BWM that
boosts the digital transformation of global supply chains. These include best factors, such as
supply chain resilience (SCRES) capabilities, whose impact should be maximized, and worst
factors, such as supply chain disruptions, whose impact should be minimized. Additionally,
SCM 4.0 technologies are considered alternatives for ranking in the digital transformation.
The proposed max–min Best–Worst Method (BWM) was applied in a revelatory case
study (Yin, 2013) of a highly digitalized global furniture supply chain, which successfully
managed disruptions arising from the Ukraine war. The ﬁndings identiﬁed the most and
least critical disruptive effects, as well as the most and least powerful resilience capabilities.
Ultimately, these revealed the priority that each SCM 4.0 technology should have within the
digital transformation process.
The rest of the article is structured as follows. Section 2 presents a literature review on
the severe supply chain disruptions, SCRES and SCM 4.0. Moreover, this section exam-
ines previous MCDM methods employed in the literature to enhance digital transformation.
Section 3 introduces the BWM and the extensions developed in the literature, thereby formu-
lating the proposed max–min BWM. Section 4 describes the case study, the ﬁndings reached
in the application of the proposed method, as well as the results of the sensitivity analy-
ses. Section 5 discusses the theoretical and managerial implications of the research. Finally,
Sect. 6 summarizes the conclusions, research limitations, and proposes future research lines.

2 Literature review
2.1 Minimising the effects of supply chain disruptions with SCM 4.0
The disruption occurrence has caused several negative consequences and exposed risks asso-
ciated with supply chain operations, arising from both external and internal factors (Shefﬁ
and Rice, 2005; Wagner & Bode, 2008). Diverse supply chain disruptions have been explored
in the literature, including ﬁnancial crises (Moretto & Caniato, 2021), cybersecurity attacks
(Jeong et al., 2025), pandemic crises (Pujawan & Bah, 2022), natural disasters (Matsuo,
2015), geopolitical tensions (Bednarski et al., 2023), and many others. What is more, mul-
tiple taxonomies have been proposed to classify them (Katsaliaki et al., 2022; Manuj &
Mentzer, 2008; Shekarian & Mellat Parast, 2021).
Their effects are increasing due to the higher vulnerability of supply chains, which become
more pronounced in global supply chain conﬁgurations (Holgado & Niess, 2023; López et al.,
2023). Furthermore, companies are becoming increasingly vulnerable to high-impact, low-
probability events due to factors such as globalization, shifting customer demand patterns,
and political instability (Bednarski et al., 2023; Maryniak et al., 2021; Pettit et al., 2013).
Severe disruptions originating from one node may even lead to a ripple effect that propagates
throughout the rest of the supply chain (Dolgui & Ivanov, 2021). Hence, numerous studies
were conducted to examine the impact of large-scale disruptions in supply chains (Ardolino
et al., 2022; Baghersad & Zobel, 2021).
Initially, Blackhurst et al. (2005) found that the severity of supply chain disruptions is
inﬂuenced by the structure of the supply chain itself and its mitigation capabilities. With this
in mind, mathematical models have been proposed to proactively and/or reactively mitigate
the effects of these disruptions on supply chains (Ivanov et al., 2017; Kamalahmadi & Parast,
2017; Paul et al., 2017; Schmitt et al., 2015). This has brought together methods for mitigating
disruption events considering the structure of the supply network (Bier et al., 2020). The
earlier literature also provides a quantitative index to measure the impacts of disruptions on
the performance of focal ﬁrms (Baghersad & Zobel, 2021) and SCRES (Gao et al., 2019).
During the COVID-19 crisis, the importance of SCM 4.0 in minimizing supply chain
disruptions became speciﬁcally clear. This term refers to the physical and technological
integration of systems across supply chain networks, enabling organizations to enhance prof-
itability, efﬁciency, and production performance (Sobb et al., 2020).
The use of artiﬁcial intelligence in SCM offsets the risk cost associated with uncertainty
by developing robust commercial agreements that withstand geopolitical disruptions (Dong
etal.,2025).Thecombinedadoptionofothertechnologies,suchascloudcomputing,robotics,
BDA and the IoT, has proven to minimize disruptions arising from supply–demand mis-
matches and process risks in food manufacturing supply chains (Ali et al., 2024). Moreover,
the implementation of cyber supply chains enables the execution of supply chain risk ana-
lytics, making it possible to detect, model, control, and learn about the ripple effects of
disruptions (Ivanov et al., 2018).
Despite the growing body of research on the digital transformation of the supply chain, no
methods have been found for prioritizing the adoption of SCM 4.0 technologies, considering
supply chain disruptions. This literature gap will be addressed in the present study.

2.2 Maximizing the power of resilience capabilities with SCM 4.0
The effectiveness of SCRES has been extensively studied, particularly in response to recent
major disruptions such as COVID-19 (Ivanov et al., 2021; Shen & Sun, 2023) and trade
wars (Altuntas Vural et al., 2024; Blessley & Mudambi, 2022; López et al., 2025). Resilient
supply chains are capable not only of preparing for and recovering from disruptions but also
of emerging in an improved state afterwards (Ponomarov & Holcomb, 2009). This is due to
the fact that they possess concrete capabilities such as adaptability, robustness, and ﬂexibility
(Han et al., 2020; López & Ishizaka, 2019), among many others.
Resilience capabilities are described as organizational abilities, skills, competencies, and
knowledge that enable anticipating, resisting, reacting, mitigating, and recovering supply
chain operations from disruptive effects (López et al., 2023; Ponomarov, 2012). The perfor-
mance level of each resilience capability can be measured using a speciﬁc set of indicators
(Han et al., 2020; Singh et al., 2019; Werner et al., 2021). These indicators should be maxi-
mized to reinforce the power of resilience capabilities facing disruptions.
Recent studies have examined the positive impacts of SCM 4.0 on SCRES (Ahmed et al.,
2023; Küffner et al., 2022; Sarkar et al., 2025; Spieske et al., 2023). Various technologies
play a crucial role in the digital transformation of a resilient supply chain (Sharma et al.,
2025).
BDA in SCRES allows for effectively managing the data generated by various entities
in the supply chain, such as suppliers, customers, production, and inventory. This provides
detailed insights and helps identify potential risks. The integration of BDA and artiﬁcial
intelligence in SCM enables the development of resilience strategies for readiness, response,
recovery, and adaptability (Zamani et al., 2023).
Digital twin technology enables supply chain managers to create a virtual replica of the
entire supply chain process. This enables effective scenario simulation by analyzing data
acquired from BDA and different sensors, ensuring visibility, agility and adaptability in the
supply chain to proactively develop resilience strategies (Ivanov & Dolgui, 2020b). What is
more, a bibliometric analysis (2017–2024) on the application of digital twins in SCM revealed
that they enhance visibility, improve resilience, and optimize decision-making within supply
chain systems (Kapil et al., 2025).
Similarly, the integration of blockchain technology in the supply chain improves trans-
parency and traceability. This ultimately enhances visibility, strengthens collaboration,
integration, risk management, and knowledge management to tackle any disruptions (Bayra-
mova et al., 2021).
Furthermore, additive manufacturing technology in SCRES promotes on-demand produc-
tion using advanced technologies, such as 3D printing, instead of traditional manufacturing
processes. The on-demand production enhances ﬂexibility, collaboration, knowledge man-
agement, responsiveness, and agility within a ﬁrm’s supply chain while reducing lead times
and inventory costs (Belhadi et al., 2022). Another study evaluating the impact of additive
manufacturing on SCRES, using cost, lead time, and response time as key performance fac-
tors, revealed that this technology is highly effective in optimizing supply chain operations
(Ekren et al., 2023).
Similarly, the integration of digital platforms enhances SCRES by utilizing real-time data
from cloud computing systems, IoT, and artiﬁcial intelligence. This enhances visibility, risk
management, information sharing, and decision-making while enabling seamless collabora-
tion across different levels of the supply chain (Suali et al., 2024).
Therefore, the integration of advanced SCM 4.0 technologies will enable the required
interconnectivity,large-scaledatacollection,real-timecommunicationatalllevels,intelligent

2.3 MCDM methods for supporting digital transformation
In the past few decades, various MCDM models have been utilized for technology selection.
The Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) was the ﬁrst MCDM method used for selecting
technologies in developing countries (Ramanujam & Saaty, 1981). Its application was later
extended to include knowledge management (Ngai & Chan, 2005), open source software
selection (Yah Jusoh, 2014) and other technology selection challenges (Hamzeh & Xu, 2019).
AHP has been integrated with other methods, such as goal programming for evaluating
e-commerce software selection (Sarkis & Talluri, 2004), TOPSIS for ETL software selection
(Hanine et al., 2016), SAW for technology selection to improve manufacturing operations
and supply chain collaboration (Farooq & Obrien, 2012), and Quality Function Deployment
(QFD) and Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP) to select a digital transformation model for
manufacturing (Erbay & Ylldlrlm, 2022). Mian and Al-Ahmari (2019) also combined differ-
ent techniques: AHP was used to determine criteria weights, while TOPSIS, ELECTRE, and
SAW were applied to rank different digitization systems. In addition, various AHP variants,
such as Fuzzy AHP, have been combined with TOPSIS to rank digital technologies (Aliak-
bari Nouri et al., 2015; Yıldırım et al., 2022) and AHPSort for selecting a cloud-based ERP
system (López & Ishizaka, 2017).
AnalyticNetworkProcess(ANP),anextensionofAHPthatconsidersinteractionsbetween
criteria and alternatives, ranks telecommunication technologies (Lee et al., 2009). Later on,
Fuzzy Analytic Network Process (FANP), combined with fuzzy TOPSIS, has been applied to
rank operating systems for smart TVs (Lee & Kang, 2023) and various digital technologies
(Pour et al., 2023).
The BWM, a novel pairwise comparison approach, has recently been applied in evaluating
technologies 4.0 for optimizing the supply chain (Mahmah et al., 2025). However, it does
not consider maximizing and minimizing criteria together. Since comparing criteria with
different optimization directions can be challenging, this study will focus on developing a
novel max–min BWM.
3 The developments of BWM
3.1 An introduction to the BWM
The BWM is one of the recent MCDM method, developed by (Rezaei, 2015). It belongs
to the family of the pairwise comparison methods, which often requires completing a full
pairwise comparison matrix as in AHP (Saaty, 1980). The main problem is that the number
of pairwise comparisons is growing exponentially with the number of criteria/alternatives. It
is time-consuming and the burden to ﬁll a full matrix can lead to errors due to tiredness and
inattention. For large matrices, it also becomes difﬁcult to be consistent.
123

Annals of Operations Research
BWM does not use a complete pairwise comparison matrix. It requires only a comparison
with the best and worst criterion/alternative. In this context, the BWM method is superior to
other pairwise methods (Rezaei, 2020).
Two matrices are used in the BWM. The ﬁrst matrix is the “Best to other criteria”
vector, collecting the comparisons between the best criterion/alternative with other crite-
ria/alternatives. The second vector is the “Other criteria to worst” vector, which collects
the comparisons between the worst criterion with other criteria (Rezaei, 2015, 2016). The
weights of decision criteria are obtained by solving a max–min optimization model.
In inconsistent matrices, there exists several optimums. To solve this problem, Rezaei
(2016) presented two ways to tackle this issue: one is based on interval analysis, and the other
is to convert the min–max non-linear model into a linear model. Kheybari and Ishizaka (2022)
propose selecting the weights among all possible solutions based on the decision-maker’s
behavior (i.e., balanced, risk-taking, and risk-averse). Tu et al. (2023) propose completing
the comparison matrix and then use a derivation method, as in AHP (e.g., eigenvalue method,
or the logarithmic least squares method) to calculate the weights.
The BWM has also been extended to incorporate uncertainty and vagueness in judgments.
The fuzzy BWM was developed by Guo and Zhao (2017), the Hesitant fuzzy BWM method
by Ali and Rashid (2019), and the intuitionistic fuzzy BWM by Qiong and Zeshui (2017)
and improved by Liu et al., (2023), Wan and Dong (2022), the probabilistic hesitant fuzzy
BWM by Li et al., (2019), the generalized interval-valued trapezoidal fuzzy BWM by Ali
and Rashid (2020), the Best–Worst method based on 2-tuple linguistic model by Labella
et al., (2021), the interval type-2 hesitant fuzzy BWM by Norouzi and Hajiagha (2021) and
the Belief BWM by Liang et al., (2021). López et al. (2023) developed a method to deﬁne
the membership function in the fuzzy BWM.
BWM has also been combined with GAIA to have a descriptive view of the problem
(Ishizaka et al., 2023). For large problems, Moslem (2023) has proposed the Parsimonious
Best Worst Method, based on the idea of the Parsimonious AHP (Abastante et al., 2019).
Only reference alternatives are compared, and a piecewise linear preference function is then
constructed (Abastante et al., 2018). The BWM has been applied to various areas. Mi et al.
(2019) have compiled a review of successful case studies. Although BWM has been useful,
a unique model is confusing when the criteria to maximize and to minimize are mixed.
Therefore, in this paper, we propose a max–min BWM where disruption and resilience
capability criteria are separated into two distinct models.
3.2 The new max–min BWM
Technological decisions are often based on maximizing the positive criteria or minimizing the
negative criteria. The problem lies in the criteria that are often intangible and not expressed
in units. It is not easy to compare them. A beneﬁt/cost analysis has been proposed in AHP
(Azis, 1990) and also in AHPSort (Ishizaka & López, 2019). However, other approaches are
necessary to assess the opposing forces that affect resilient management of supply chains
in the current business context. With the intention of supporting managers in the digital
transformation of supply chains in mind, the idea is to rank SCM 4.0 technologies, analyz-
ing separately the disruptions (negative criteria to be minimized or minimum criteria) and
resilience capabilities (positive criteria to be maximized or maximum criteria), and ﬁnally
deriving priorities for each model. A max-min score is then calculated for ﬁnding the global
priorities.

4 A case study
4.1 Case description
The case study was conducted within a furniture manufacturing supply chain. The focal ﬁrm’s
activities encompass the design, assembly, and manufacture of various house spaces, primar-
ily kitchens. Regarding size, we encounter a large ﬁrm with revenues exceeding e500M,
employing more than 1500 staff members. This company operates in over 20 countries,
maintaining a signiﬁcant market share. Regarding this company’s supply chain, we observe
that it is a global, international value chain. For this reason, they have been made aware that
dealing with signiﬁcant risks and disruptions is critical for the company’s well-functioning.
The data collection occurred in April 2022, coinciding with the beginning of the geopolitical
conﬂict between Russia and Ukraine, as well as the ongoing COVID-19-related disruptions.
The supply chain was highly vulnerable to these disruptions, as one of its leading timber

suppliers was located in Ukraine at the time the war began. However, we found that their dig-
italization level, combined with a resilience approach to the supply chain, has helped them
resist and overcome the aforementioned disruptions. For this reason, they have achieved
a competitive advantage in terms of production time, lead time, or customer satisfaction.
Despite the critical disruption suffered by the supply chain, this was not altered due to the
competitive advantage reached through its digitalization.
Their production system operates on a request basis, as all orders are unique. It is com-
pletely digitalized. Customers can design their indoor solutions speciﬁcally to approach them
in the best possible way, allowing the company to provide the best option. In that way it works
without inventories, given that all orders are different. To that end, they have trustworthy sup-
pliers that require practical and fast communication and delivery for the company to operate
efﬁciently. Their work schedule is structured into 12 days, with a maximum starting from
the production start date. First, there is an automated system that organizes orders in such
a way that they are not processed until a full truck is completed. In this way, if an order
does not ﬁll the entire space within a loaded truck, the programme pairs it with another order
with the objective of maximizing efﬁciency. Once the program sets the truck to be ready, the
process is initiated. First, they start to do simple things, such as painting, while also order-
ing electronic appliances, furniture, and all the items they purchase from their suppliers.
This process requires a great deal of coordination and planning once the orders are placed.
Once the raw materials arrive, they assemble the indoor space according to the request, as
previously mentioned. This process continues until day −1, at which point they place the
electronic appliances if required. Finally, on day 0, truck J0 arrives at the company, and they
deliver the fully completed order. The company emphasizes that every order is unique. In this
way, they require the Chief Operating Ofﬁcer (COO) to maintain close communication with
the Data Manager, as the process is fully automated and requires effective coordination. This
production process allows the company to assemble and manufacture an entire indoor space
in just 11 days. The company’s goal is to reduce the lead time from 11 days to a signiﬁcantly
lower level, ideally 5 or 6 days.
The analysis of this single case allowed us to explore how an international furniture man-
ufacturing supply chain, seriously threatened by disruptions, could very successfully manage
its operations, thanks to the digital transformation previously carried out. Additionally, the
difﬁculty for researchers in accessing real-time information about this phenomenon during
crisis management makes the selected case study considered revelatory (Yin, 2013) and jus-
tiﬁes the selection of a single case in this research (Demeter et al., 2021; Dremel et al., 2020;
Gaur et al., 2025).

4.2.1 Stage A. Deﬁning the max model and the min model
This stage focused on deﬁning the criteria and alternatives in both the max and min models.
Before starting data collection, we held an informal meeting with the decision-maker (DM),
the Industrial Development Director of the focal ﬁrm. Speciﬁcally, the DM has more than
8 years of experience in this role. Moreover, the DM possessed a profound understanding of
the digital transformation undertaken by the focal ﬁrm and its extension to the supply chain.
After explaining the purpose of the research, the DM agreed to participate.
We had two virtual meetings with the DM. During the ﬁrst virtual meeting, an inter-
view guideline with open-ended questions was conducted to gather information about the

Once a big picture of the case had been drawn, a semi-structured questionnaire with three
parts was designed for the second virtual meeting. Each part began by clarifying the key

4.2.2 Stage B. Results of computing the global priorities in the max model and the min

This stage focused on computing the local and global priorities in the max model and min
model. We had a third virtual meeting with the DM to collect data required for applying the
proposed max–min BWM in Sect. 3. Accordingly, we designed a structured questionnaire
with two parts. The ﬁrst part focused on determining the importance of SCM 4.0 technologies
in minimizing the effects of supply chain disruptions. It began by asking about the most and
least critical disruptions that had jeopardized the supply chain under study, and comparing
those to the other disruptions using numbers 1 (equally critical) to 9 (extremely more critical).
Subsequently, the DM pairwise compared the importance of the most and least effective SCM
4.0 technologies for minimizing the impact of each disruption in relation to the other SCM
4.0 technologies. The second part contained similar pairwise comparisons to ascertain the
importance of SCM 4.0 technologies in maximizing the effects on SCRES capabilities. The
results were computed by applying steps 7–12 (Sect. 3.2). The results are shown in the
following subsections. All comparison matrices in the max model and min model yielded
low values of the consistency index (ξ∗), indicating a satisfactory level of internal consistency
(Rezaei, 2015, 2016). The corresponding matrices and their consistency indices are provided
in the supplementary material.

4.3 Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis was conducted to evaluate the robustness of the max and min criteria
weights and the resulting SCM 4.0 technology ranking. The weights of the most important
criteria in the max and min model, as well as the α value, were varied within ± 10% of their
original values, whilst preserving the normalisation condition. Tables 8, 9, and 10 depict the
results of the sensitivity analysis on SCRES capabilities, supply chain disruptions and α value,
respectively. The results of these analyses demonstrate that the SCM 4.0 technologies ranking
remains stable across all tested cases, thereby conﬁrming the robustness and reliability of the
proposed max–min BWM approach.

and adaptation. This is achieved through effective and timely coordination and execution of

Thus, our study contributes to improving SCRES capabilities and operational performance
by enabling digitalisation across the supply chain. In doing so, it extends recent studies that
have predominantly focused on the adoption of Industry 4.0 technologies within focal ﬁrms
to enhance their supply chain dynamic capabilities (Dixit et al., 2026; Stentoft et al., 2025).
Our ﬁndings even highlight the importance of adopting a supply chain-wide perspective in the
digital transformation process to achieve superior resilience and supply chain performance.
Drawing on this perspective, Gomes and Silva (2025) reveal that supply chain digitalisation
enhances resilience by improving Triple-A Capabilities. In contrast, our study addresses this
perspective innovatively by simultaneously considering both supply chain capabilities and
disruptions as an integrated tandem in the digital transformation process.
Additionally, in traditional approaches, ﬁrms strive to strike a balance between efﬁciency
and resilience (de Arquer et al., 2022; Lücker et al., 2025). However, the proposed study
demonstrates that effectively integrating SCM 4.0 technologies can simultaneously enhance
both by eliminating inventory waste, reducing lead times, and safeguarding against undesir-
able events—thereby offering a vital paradigm shift.
Finally, the proposed max–min BWM represents a signiﬁcant contribution, offering a
systematic approach to decision-making by evaluating different digital technologies and
addressing the competing objectives of ﬁrms—minimising the impacts of disruptions whilst
maximising resilience capabilities in the supply chain. Indeed, recent articles have proposed
novel MCDM approaches that prioritise digital transformation strategies or assess the critical
success factors in the adoption of SCM 4.0 (Mutambik, 2025) based solely on resilience
capabilities(Alkan&Kahraman,2023),withoutconsideringtheminimisationofsupplychain
disruptions. Moreover, the proposed max–min BWM is preferable to beneﬁt–cost analysis
as the former: i) requires fewer pairwise comparisons; ii) requires only integer numbers; iii)
provides more consistent comparisons; and iv) provides more reliable ﬁnal weights than the
latter. Furthermore, SubSect. 5.1.1 describes the innovative analysis provided by the max–min
BWM to support decision-making, compared with applying the traditional single-hierarchy
BWM (Kheybari et al., 2023; Rezaei, 2015). Hence, the proposed max–min BWM method
lays a vital foundation for future research, particularly in advancing multi-criteria approaches
and conducting effective studies on SCRES using multiple methods.
5.1.1 Comparison with standard single-hierarchy
In the traditional BWM, all criteria share the same optimization direction and are integrated
within a single hierarchy. This compels decision-makers to simultaneously trade off beneﬁt-
typeandcost-typecriteria,whichcanreduceclarityandobscuretheirmanagerialsigniﬁcance.
In contrast, the proposed max–min BWM clearly distinguishes resilience capabilities, which
are to be maximized, from disruptions, which are to be minimized, in order to achieve a
more resilient supply chain. This clear separation allows decision-makers to express their
preferences more naturally and consistently, avoids combining conﬂicting criteria within a
single comparison framework, and preserves conceptual integrity throughout the evaluation
process.
Methodologically, the conventional BWM deals with a single weight vector obtained from
one min–max optimization model. In contrast, the proposed model employs two separate

min–max models for resilience enhancement and disruption reduction, which are subse-
quently integrated through a strategic aggregation parameter. This approach allows sensitivity
to managerial preferences and safeguards against the risk of dominance by highly severe dis-

5.2 Managerial implications
This study offers signiﬁcant managerial implications. First, the proposed approach can help
managers prioritize investments in digital technologies more effectively, thereby maximizing
the beneﬁts of integration. What is more, such investments should not be limited to the
internal operations of individual ﬁrms but extended across the entire supply chain—including
suppliers, distributors, retailers, logistics partners, and customers.
The study also emphasizes the importance of embedding a risk management culture into
daily operations. Managers should ensure proper training, clear risk procedures, and cross-
functional collaboration to support the smooth adoption of digitalization, as well as the
effective collection and interpretation of data for actionable insights.
Integrating digital platforms enhances visibility across the supply chain, enabling early
detection of potential disruptions. Therefore, managers should invest in shared dashboards
and mapping tools to improve transparency and build trust with investors and other stake-
holders.
Althoughdigitaltwinswererankedlowerintheproposedcase,theyremainhighlyeffective
for simulating disruption scenarios and reconﬁguring plans. These tools allow managers to
virtually replicate processes, helping them anticipate and mitigate risks more effectively.
Concerning disruption risks, cyber-attacks were ranked in a high position. Moreover,
they remain themselves a serious threat in digitalization. Managers must address this risk by
implementing robust cybersecurity measures such as multi-factor authentication, blockchain-
based tamper-proof transactions, and secure data-sharing protocols.
Additionally, the study underscores the importance of cross-functional collaboration
among supply chain planners, IT teams, and operations to align technologies with opera-
tional goals. Managers should actively support such a collaboration, given its critical role in
enhancing ﬁrm performance.
Furthermore, the integration of digital technologies can signiﬁcantly reduce lead times,
as shown in the case examined in this study, where lead time was reduced from 12 days to
just 5. Such practices enable managers and strategic decision-makers to leverage predictive
analytics and automated scheduling systems better to coordinate activities across suppliers,
production, and logistics.
Finally, since the ﬁrm examined in this study operates in more than 20 countries, it is
crucial for managers to develop a scalable digital infrastructure to effectively manage its
global supply chain while ensuring the onboarding of local suppliers. Integrating digital
technologies is essential for building efﬁcient resilience models, which can also be adapted
and applied by other ﬁrms.

6 Conclusions, limitations, and future work
6.1 Conclusion
This study examines the integration of SCM 4.0 technologies in a furniture ﬁrm that faced
severe disruptions from the COVID-19 pandemic and the ongoing Russia–Ukraine conﬂict.
The ﬁndings reveal that the ﬁrm’s proactive adoption of digital technologies and resilience
strategiesprovidedacompetitiveedge,enablingittosustainproductionandmanageinventory
more effectively.
The max–min BWM methodology offers a systematic approach to evaluating the con-
ﬂicting objectives of maximizing resilience capabilities while minimizing the effects of
disruptions. The outcomes yield the following key insights:
• Technology prioritization Blockchain (TEC3), digital platforms (TEC6), and BDA (TEC1)
emerge as the most critical technologies, together accounting for more than 77% of the pri-
ority weight using the max-min method. These technologies play a vital role in enhancing
visibility and risk anticipation, thereby strengthening timely decision-making and adapt-
ability.
• Key supply chain disruptions The disruptive scenario under study presented two critical
disruptions: delays in the delivery of raw materials and cyber-attacks. The integration of
BDA, digital platforms, and blockchain mitigates them by supporting timely, data-driven
decision-making, secure information exchange, and the automated execution of contractual
obligations.
• Key resilience capabilities In the proposed case study, redundancy, adaptability, and risk
management culture accounted for more than 50% of overall resilience. Redundancy was
reinforced through digital solutions such as smart subcontracting, optimized stock alloca-
tion, and automated inventory management policies, which effectively balanced efﬁciency
and resilience objectives.
• Strategic role of digitalization The integration of SCM 4.0 technologies in the focal ﬁrm
enabled the implementation of a just-in-time model while still enduring supply chain
interruptions. This demonstrates that adopting SCM 4.0 not only enhances efﬁciency but
also strengthens resilience capabilities, mitigates disruption risks, and ultimately provides
a sustainable competitive advantage.
Therefore, the proposed study empirically conﬁrms that SCM 4.0 serves as a key driver
for enhancing both resilience and disruption management in today’s complex supply chains,
facilitating a shift in business operations from preventive disruption management to more
sophisticated, proactive designs.
6.2 Limitations
Although the proposed research makes signiﬁcant contributions to scholarly work, certain
limitations must still be acknowledged:
• Single-case study design The ﬁndings of this research are based on data from the single
furniture industry, which limits their generalizability. Supply chains in other sectors, such
as healthcare or construction, may face different types of disruptions, potentially altering
the ranking of digital technology adoption and resilience priorities

• Decision-maker Bias The data for this research was obtained from a single decision-maker
(the Industrial Development Director), which may introduce biasness in data, as MCDM
methods are inherently subjective and reﬂect the preferences of the decision-maker.
• Time-speciﬁc context The data for this research was collected in April 2022, during the
Russia–Ukraineconﬂictandthepost-pandemicperiod.Thesituationmaydifferunderother
disruptive events, which could require different technological and resilience capabilities
depending on macroeconomic or geopolitical conditions.
• Technology scope This research primarily focuses on six SCM 4.0 technologies to evaluate
their impact. However, many other emerging digital technologies—such as IoT sensors,
autonomous logistics, and AI-driven predictive analytics—also warrant evaluation to better
understand the insights and advantages they can provide when integrated into supply
chains.
6.3 Future work
Building on the research ﬁndings and acknowledging its limitations, this study opens several
promising avenues for future research:
• Multi-case or cross-industry studies It is important to extend this study by conducting
comparative analyses across different industries to validate the generalizability of the
proposed max–min methodology and its results.
• Extensions of max–min BWM with aggregation or consensus mechanisms the proposed
method can be easily extended to support group decision-making based on an expert
panel. On the one hand, future extensions of the max–min BWM should incorporate
mechanisms to aggregate individual experts’ pairwise comparisons or global weight of
max and min criteria. On the other hand, the max–min BWM can be combined with
consensus mechanisms, such as the fuzzy Delphi or Nominal Group Technique (NGT),
enabling the determination of expert preferences regarding the best and worst criteria in
both the max and min models.
• Longitudinal analysis Although this study revealed that lead time was drastically reduced
through the integration of SCM 4.0 technologies, it should be noted that the data were
collected in April 2022. Given the dynamic nature of geopolitical disruptions, the ranking
of SCM 4.0 technologies might change over time. To assess the temporal robustness of
the max–min BWM, it can be applied longitudinally, with data updated periodically to
determine whether digital transformation maintains a sustained competitive advantage
over time.
• Inclusion of additional technologies The proposed model can be further extended by incor-
porating additional digital technologies—such as robotics, IoT, and cloud computing—to
more comprehensively evaluate available alternatives and identify the most proﬁtable
options that strengthen SCRES.
• Behavioral and organizational perspectives For future research, a promising direction
would be to examine the change management policies required for the effective integration
of SCM 4.0 technologies, and to explore how employee adoption and organizational culture
are inﬂuenced by bringing about the technological changes in the ﬁrms.
• Quantitative performance impact Future research could be extended to investigate the
impact of SCM 4.0 adoption on qualitative metrics, such as carbon emissions, service level
improvements, and the optimal utilization of human, ﬁnancial, and material resources to
enhance business operations.
