Abstract
Repeat sales index methodologies are prevalent for computing indices, largely owing to
their simplicity, minimal data requirements, and reliable approach to addressing real estate
heterogeneity. However, this method is not without ﬂaws, being restricted to properties sold
at least twice within the sample period and demonstrating high sensitivity to the size of the
dataset. In this paper, we introduce a stable and robust factorial approach that holds ﬁrm even
when the data quantity is sparse due to factors such as frequent publications, thin markets,
data unavailability, or a drastic drop in the number of transactions. We demonstrate that
trend and volatility estimates tend to be biased with the standard repeat sales approach, but
remain unbiased with our factorial method, regardless of the size of the dataset. The factorial
approach exhibits greater robustness in relation to transaction numbers.

1 Introduction
The dynamics of real estate prices are a prevalent topic in news outlets, economic forums, and
societal discourse. Most of these discussions are rooted in indices provided by governmental
agencies, brokerage ﬁrms, or private index publishers, placing the real estate sector squarely
in the public eye. This sector’s signiﬁcant impact on the ﬁnancial sphere, the wider economy,
monetary policy decisions, and ﬁnancial stability underpins its importance. However, the
methodologies used to analyse price ﬂuctuations and compute these real estate indices are
rarely deliberated in public, despite their critical impact on the published analyses and indices.

terms of location, as no two properties can occupy the same spot. This diversity makes it

Creating real estate indices that accurately reﬂect the market in a speciﬁc location and segment
is no easy task. The construction of real estate indices grapples with challenges mainly stem-
ming from the unique nature of these assets. As real estate is an illiquid asset of varied nature,
the implementation of a uniform approach is near impossible due to property-speciﬁc factors
such as size, location, condition, and the presence of amenities like parking. Moreover, trans-
actions are relatively rare. Despite these complexities, real estate indices are indispensable
tools for enhancing transparency, enforcing regulations, conducting market analysis, manag-
ing portfolio allocation, evaluating performance, and monitoring real estate risk within the
economic system.1 Hence, the need for reliable real estate indices to monitor this crucial
sector cannot be overstated.
Various methodologies exist to reproduce the evolution of prices over time, and they are
more or less sensitive to changes in data over time, as underlined by Wallace and Meese
(1997). Internationally, several approaches have been adopted, which can be classiﬁed into
four main categories: simple techniques (simple or weighted average and median prices),
hedonic methods, repeat sales approaches, and appraisal-based indicess.
(i) Median price indices simply track the median sale price of homes in a speciﬁc region
(see (Knight et al.,1995; Prasad & Richards, 2008). Regarded as the simplest approach,
it offers a general market overview. A key drawback, however, is the susceptibility to
changes in the mix of properties sold, thereby failing to accurately reﬂect value changes in
individual properties. Consequently, such indices may present noisy estimates of housing
cost ﬂuctuations. The merits of median indices lie in their requirement for less data, ease
of computation, and straightforward interpretation.
(ii) Hedonic price indices are based on models that identify price factors (Hill, 2011; Kestens
et al., 2006; Tchuente & Nyawa, 2022). In real estate, for instance, the price of a build-
ing’s price is determined by the property’s features (surface area, condition, number of
rooms, etc.) as well as its surrounding environment (proximity to schools, air pollution
levels, value of nearby homes, etc.). Hedonic price indices account for variations in the
characteristics of the sold properties, thereby offering a more accurate picture of individ-
ual property value changes over time. Yet, they can be complex to calculate. Despite their
popularity, they have attracted criticisms, including sample selection bias, omitted vari-
ables bias, and functional form misspeciﬁcation, and they can also be computationally
demanding.
(iii) Repeat sales indices are used to track the price changes of properties that have been sold
more than once (Nagaraja et al., 2014). This index is computed by comparing a particular
property’s sale prices at different moments in time, necessitating at least two transactions
on the same property. Because they control for factors such as location, property type, and
size, repeat sales indices are deemed a reliable way to measure real estate value changes.
The estimation of a standard repeat sales regression equation requires only price, sales
date, and property address, thereby rendering this method non-data-intensive as it does
not necessitate individual property characteristics. This approach is discussed in greater
detail later in this study.

(iv) Appraisal-based indices rely on the estimates of the values of individual properties based
on individual appraisals or automated valuation models. These indices can provide highly
detailed and accurate information about changes in the values of individual properties;
however, they may be affected by errors in the appraisal process, such as temporal lag
or smoothing. These indices are often used in commercial real estate Geltner and Ling
(2006); Crosby and Devaney (2018).
In general, hedonic and repeat sales indices are often deemed more reliable than median
price indices as they control for changes in the mix of properties sold, and they offer greater
transparency (and reliability) than appraisal-based indices.
Repeat sales indices are widely used in the residential real estate industry. These indices are
used in many countries around the world, including the United States, Canada, Australia, and
the United Kingdom. In the US, the most well-known repeat sales index is the S&P Case-
Shiller Home Price Indices, which track changes in the value of residential real estate in 20
metropolitan areas across the country. They are particularly useful for investors, appraisers,
and others who need to make decisions based on changes in real estate prices because they
provide a reliable way to track changes in the value of speciﬁc types of real estate over time.
Repeat sales indices are also used by policymakers and government agencies to track changes
in the housing market and make decisions related to monetary or other economic policies. For
example, the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) publishes many house price indices
based on weighted repeat sales statistical technique for over 400 American cities.2
The primary advantage of the repeat sales approach is its independence from any presupposed
form of behaviour for the underlying real estate index. Since the seminal paper by Case &
Shiller in 1987, the model has garnered substantial attention and given rise to numerous
reﬁnements and criticisms. While repeat sales indexes are popular and provide valuable
insights into the real estate market, they do have some limitations that have been thoroughly
discussed in the literature (see Section 2):
– However, the sample size of this study is limited. Repeat sales indices are based on
properties that have been sold multiple times (at least twice) which can limit the accuracy
of the index, particularly in smaller real estate markets where there may be fewer sales.
– These indices may be subject to time-lag effects. Indeed, the data used to calculate repeat
sales indices can be many years old, depending on how frequently the properties in
the sample are sold (some properties are exchanged every 30 years). This can limit the
timeliness of the information provided by an index.
– Repeat sales indices may not be representative of the broader real estate market if they are
based on a sample biased towards certain types of properties (some properties are almost
never sold). For example, if a sample is dominated by small units (such as studios), the
index may not accurately reﬂect changes in the broader real estate market.
– The repeat sales approach is a complex econometric method that may be difﬁcult for
those who are unfamiliar with the methodology. This results in lack of transparency,
particularly among the public (the simple median approach is easier to understand).
– The repeat sales index assumes constant quality. However, a dwelling unit sold at two
different points in time is not necessarily identical, owing to factors such as depreciation

epeat sales indices are based on a technique called repeat sales regression. This technique
involves analysing the prices of properties that have been sold multiple times over a period
and uses statistical analysis to isolate the changes in value that can be attributed to factors
such as inﬂation, location, and the condition of the property. Repeat-sales methods calculate
changes in home prices based on sales of the same property, thereby avoiding the problem
of accounting for price differences in homes with varying characteristics. To create a repeat
sales index, researchers typically begin with a sample of properties sold multiple times over
a period. They use regression analysis to estimate the change in the value of each property
between the time it was ﬁrst sold and the time it was most recently sold. Once the regression
analysis is complete, researchers can use the estimated changes in the value of each property
to create an index that tracks changes in the value of the properties over time. This index can
be used to compare the changes in the value of similar properties over time and track the
overall trends in the real estate market. This approach is discussed in greater detail in Section
3.
The repeat sales index is efﬁcient in large markets, where the main pitfalls (heteroskedasticity,
sample selection bias, inefﬁciency and revision, quality change, dataset size, etc.) are over-
come by the quantity of transactions. However, when limited data are available, the repeat
sales index cannot be computed reliably. This has long been underlined by various authors,
such as Haurin and Hendershott (1991), Schwann (1998) and Baroni et al. (2011). (Haurin
and Hendershott, 1991, p.260) explain that “a large number of sales are required before a
reasonable repeat-sales sample is obtained.”
One solution to this pitfall is to increase the temporal dimension, as Schwann (1998) under-
lines. For instance, we estimate longer temporally aggregated price indices, such as annual
indices, instead of quarterly or semi-annual indices. However, this solution comes with draw-
backs. First, a lower publication frequency reduces the utility of the indexes for market
monitoring and analysis. Second, a smoothing effect may appear, as discussed by Geltner
(1991), and Barkham and Geltner (1995). Other ways to overcome data scarcity must be
explored.
In this study, we show how to compute real estate indices in situations where data are scarce
(small markets, conﬁdentiality of transactions, and lack of transactions due to an event). Local
markets, neighbourhood markets and small cities are examples of these markets. Therefore,
it is important to understand how price movements can be measured in these markets. In
particular, these small markets attract increasing interest, especially from urban planning,
property developers, and lenders who ask for more information, such as indices, to make
their decisions.
In particular, small cities can beneﬁt from having real estate information and indices, even if
they have a relatively smaller amount of data than larger cities. Real estate information and
indices provide valuable insights into the local market dynamics in small cities. They can
help homeowners, buyers, sellers, and investors understand the trends, pricing patterns, and
supply- demand dynamics speciﬁc to their area.
Furthermore, in smaller cities, real estate is generally a signiﬁcant asset and often the only
source of revenue for the municipality. Access to information and indices allows munic-

Small or thin real estate markets (housing or commercial) in the context of repeat sales
have been discussed in the literature (Francke, 2010; Schwann, 1998). This also relates to
periods of crisis when the volume of transactions decreased. In this study, we show how a
modiﬁed methodology (originally developed by Baroni et al. (2008)) based on ﬁnancial and
economic factors leads to a more robust index estimation, even in the case of small transaction
volumes, compared to the classical standard repeat sales methodology. This is demonstrated
using simulations and an empirical dataset for Paris. The robustness is considered in two
ways: ﬁrst, for the index itself and second, for the risk and return measures induced by the
index.
Conceptually, the factorial approach occupies an intermediate position between hedonic and
appraisal-based techniques on one hand and traditional repeat-sales models on the other.
Hedonic indices-as discussed by Hill (2011), Kestens et al. (2006) and Tchuente and Nyawa
(2022) offer detailed explanations of price formation by explicitly modelling property charac-
teristicsandlocationalattributes.Theydeliverstrongexplanatorypowerbutrequireextensive,
high-quality micro-data and a stable functional speciﬁcation, conditions that are rarely met
when transaction volumes are thin or publication frequency is high. Appraisal-based indices,
such as those analysed by Crosby and Devaney (2018) and Geltner and Ling (2006), rely
on professional valuations and therefore tend to produce smoother price paths, yet they suf-
fer from appraisal lags and serial correlation that dampen measured volatility. In contrast,
the factorial index retains the parsimony and transparency of repeat-sales models (Baroni et
al., 2011; Case & Shiller, 1987) while anchoring price dynamics to macro-ﬁnancial factors-
interest rates, rents, equity and REIT returns, unemployment, and income growth. This hybrid
structure enables it to capture systematic market behaviour without heavy data requirements,
thereby bridging the gap between the precision of hedonic methods and the operational sim-
plicity of repeat-sales indices. Similar motivations for combining parsimony and robustness
can be found in Bourassa and Hoesli (2017), who emphasise the importance of reliable
indices when data are scarce.
In the next four sections, we examine the robustness of our proposed real estate price indices
for markets with a small dataset (either a thin market or a lack of data) when estimated using
both a conventional repeat sales price index and our proposed approach derived from Baroni
et al. (2008). To demonstrate how our approach overcomes the issues arising from a lack of
data (small or thin markets), we review the literature related to repeat sales approaches in
the next section. In particular, we highlight how this approach has attracted (and continues
to raise) attention. In Section 3, we present the repeat sales approach and methodology.
In Section 4, we demonstrate the superiority of our approach over traditional approaches.
Section 5 concludes the study by summarising the advantages of the proposed approach.

2 Literature review
Bailey et al. (1963) initially proposed the repeat sales approach for constructing real estate
indices. They describe their procedure as a generalisation of the chained-matched model
methodology. Earlier versions of this approach to the development of real estate indices can
be found in Wyngarden (1927) and Wenzlick (1952). This method uses a set of repeated sales
of the same properties over time to estimate changes in property values. The method starts
by stating that the price of good i at date t is a function of four terms: good quality at date t;
the value of the underlying global real estate index at date t; a random walk variable linked
to good i at date t and an error term, which is again linked to good i at date t (modelled as
white noise).
Case and Shiller (1987) generalised the work of Bailey et al. (1963) and popularised the
approach by proposing the ﬁrst comprehensible approach of repeat measure methods avail-
able to build real estate indices. Their work formed the basis of a large trend in real estate
economics, real estate ﬁnance, and operational research. Many good literature reviews are
available, including Wang and Zorn (1997); Yiu and Tam (2004); Baroni et al. (2005);
Nagaraja et al. (2014); Bourassa and Hoesli (2017).
Nevertheless, the repeat sales indices present numerous discrepancies. Below, we list the
most important pitfalls of repeat sales indices and discuss them in the academic literature.
1. Constant Quality Assumption. Repeat sales indices are estimated on the premise that the
quality of individual properties (as measured by their characteristics) does not change
over time. The rationale for this idea is that the value of renovation is approximately equal
to that of depreciation. Information regarding renovations is generally lacking.
To account for this, Abraham and Schauman (1991) adjust the repeat sales index from
aggregate data on renovation expenditures and make an adjustment for depreciated struc-
tures. This approach has not reached a consensus in the literature and has not been used
since then. Other approaches have also been proposed for this purpose. Goetzmann and
Spiegel (1995) ﬁnd a consistent bias in the index resulting from repeat sales regressions,
and suggest that this bias is caused by incremental home improvements. They propose a
maximum likelihood method to estimate the ﬁrst and second moments of the ﬁxed and
temporal components of real estate returns, which relies upon relatively small samples.
Shiller (1994) selects the constant quality assumption and focuses on market heterogene-
ity to explain the bias introduced in the regression coefﬁcients. Recently, Shimizu et
al. (2010) propose a repeat sales method that considers net depreciation. Their method
relies on the parameters for which an estimate must be made. The adjustment improves
the approach (compared to completely ignoring the depreciation problem), but is not a
panacea, particularly because of the estimate that must be guessed.
2. The Selection Bias. The repeat sales approach considers only assets sold at least twice,
creating an important sample selection bias. Indeed, some types of houses may trade more
frequently in the market than others, so they will be over-represented in the repeat sales
sample (compared to the total stock of houses or sales during some period). Case et al.
(1997); Gatzlaff and Haurin (1997); Quigley (1995); Wallace and Meese (1997); Englund
et al. (1998); Hwang and Quigley (2004) emphasise the bias caused by the selection of
properties induced by the methodology itself. The properties sold more frequently are
overrepresented and can introduce a bias that may be more impactful because the samples
are small.
One method for overcoming this issue is to stratify the dataset. However, subsamples may
become very small and produce either very volatile or smooth indices (see Balk et al.,

2014). This is exactly where the approach proposed in this study becomes interesting, as
it is much less sensitive to the size of the dataset.
3. The Revision Issue. Shiller (1994); Clapp and Giaccotto (1999); Clapham et al. (2006)
consider that a revise by adding new data to the dataset may cause substantial concern
for the stability of the index. Indeed, when additional repeat sales information becomes
available, re-estimation results in changes to the estimated coefﬁcients and thus in the
price indices inferred. This is empirically measured using various datasets. In particular,
the longer the sample period, the more data will be available using the repeat sales method
(as more houses will have been resold). Baroni et al. (2008) note that this impact is more
signiﬁcant for periods when the number of observations is smaller.
4. Presence of Heteroscedasticity. Abraham and Schauman (1991); Case and Shiller (1988);
Clapp and Giaccotto (1999); Englund et al. (1998); Goodman and Thibodeau (1995,
1998); Wallace and Meese (1997) reveal the presence of heteroscedasticity in the regres-
sion error terms. Indeed, changes in house prices include components whose variance
increases with the interval of sales; therefore, the assumption of a constant error variance
is violated. This heteroscedasticity may be reduced using various methods, depending on
the factors that explain it.
To solve this issue, the main approach (see K. Case & Shiller, 1988) is to use the weighted
least-squares approach to correct for this type of heteroscedasticity. The weights are com-
puted by regressing the squared residuals from the standard (OLS) repeat sales regression
on the intercept and time interval between sales. Many studies argue that this method of
accounting for heteroscedasticity is useless (Jansen et al., 2008; Leishman et al., 2002).
5. Small Transaction Volume Issue. The repeat sales approach is data intensive, and thus a
small dataset is a classical problem for the repeat sales approach. This can be addressed in
various ways, but today, it remains a concern in the classical repeat-sales index (Gatzlaff
& Haurin, 1998; Guo et al., 2014; van de Minne et al., 2020; Wallace & Meese, 1997).
Goetzmann (1992) analyses the estimator’s behavior when the number of observations is
small relative to the length of the return series to be estimated. In this case, the columns of
the explanatory variable matrix tend to be nearly identical. Goetzmann suggests using the
Bayes and Stein method to minimise the bias induced by multicollinearity, and compares
it to the repeat sales Bayes estimator (which encompasses traditional ridge regression).
Moreover,heunderlinesthatevenifthiseconometricapproachisconsistent(anasymptotic
concept), it leads to a negative autocorrelation in the estimated return series. More recently,
Francke and Van de Minne (2017) develop an elegant approach using a hierarchical
repeat sales model with multiple stochastic log price trends and a hierarchical additive
structure: one common log price trend and cluster-speciﬁc log price trends deviating from
the common trend. Their approach provides reliable indices at a very detailed level based
on a small number of observations.
This last issue is particularly appealing in periods of crisis when the volumes of transactions
are decreasing, but also for submarkets, thin markets, or when the periodicity of publication
increases. The lack of data (or small datasets) is a salient feature of real estate markets. Indeed,
the trading volume is highly variable and procyclical; volume and price move together. In
real estate markets, the trading volume is viewed as an indicator of liquidity; the ease or speed
with which properties can be sold reﬂects the state of the market. Thus, volume (quantity of
data) is an indicator of market risk. This has been discussed by Fisher et al. (2007) or van
Dijk et al. (2020). The aim of this study is to show that another repeat sales method based on
the factors proposed by Baroni et al. (2008) leads to a more robust index estimation, even in
the case of a small dataset.

Recent work published in the Annals of Operations Research has extended these econometric
foundations using data-driven or dynamic modeling approaches. For example, Tchuente and
Nyawa (2022) applied machine-learning and geocoding methods to improve price estimation
in French cities under data scarcity, while Bhar et al. (2024) modelled U.S. housing-price
cycles through dynamic macro-ﬁnancial factors. These studies underscore the growing role
of operational-research frameworks in real-estate index construction and motivate our own
factorial approach.
In this study, we concentrate on situations (common in real estate but also in many illiquid
assets such as infrastructure, wine, and private equity) where data are scarce. In these markets,
the availability of data is low. Transaction volume is one of the most important criteria in
explaining the determinants of a thin market. This can be related to other macro factors such
as demographics, population, income level, or economic base. Thin markets, submarkets, or
a lack of availability can lead to potential pricing problems in the repeat sale index. This has
been widely discussed in the real estate and ﬁnancial literature (see for instance Junainah
et al., 2011). There are a few potential implications of thin markets, including price bias,
market manipulation, instability of price, inability of data to cooperate quickly, difﬁculty in
gathering the required information, and inconsistent assessment.
3 Methodology and dataset
3.1 Methodology
In this study, we compare two methodologies: the classical standard weighted repeat sales
method, derived from Case and Shiller (1987), and the factorial repeat sales index constructed
from economic and ﬁnancial variables, derived from Baroni et al. (2008):
1. The standard repeat sales methodology assumes that the price of asset i at date t is a
function of four terms: the asset’s quality at date t, the value of the underlying global
real estate index at date t, a random walk variable linked to asset i at date t and an error
term, here again linked to asset i at date t (modelled as white noise). Case and Shiller
(1987) generalise the work of Bailey et al. (1963) and thus provide the ﬁrst approach to
repeat measure methods for the construction of real estate indices. The main merit of this
model based on repeat sales, is that it does not presuppose any mechanical form for the
behaviour of the underlying real estate index.
2. Thefactorialrepeatsalesindexisconstructedfromtheeconomicandﬁnancialvariables,as
described by Baroni et al. (2008). Real estate price returns are computed from repeat sales
transactions and associated with their corresponding returns for economic and ﬁnancial
variables. Hence, a return vector is constructed for each observation comprising two
transactions of the same asset. Then, each real estate return is explained by the other returns
using linear regression. Finally, an index is constructed from the factor time series, which
is determined using principal component analysis as a linear combination of variables.
For both methodologies, the dates t and τ are given based on a theoretical point of view. The
overall period of analysis may be divided into S sub-periods according to publication needs
(monthly, quarterly, biannually, annually, etc.).
Date τ refers to the acquisition date, denoted T1(i) for observation i, and t refers to
the resell date, denoted T2(i) for observation i. By aggregating the observations (a buy or
sell transaction) by subperiod, we may construct a discontinuous series. The interval

Heteroscedasticity refers to a situation in which the spread of the residuals in a regression
model is not constant across the range of independent variables. In simpler terms, this means
that the variability of the data points differs at different levels of the predictor variable. In
the context of real estate data, heteroscedasticity indicates that the dispersion of observed
values around predicted values changes systematically as the values of the independent
variables change. This violation of the assumption of homoscedasticity (constant variance)
in regression analysis can result in implications for accuracy and reliability of statistical
inferences and predictions. This can be easily visualised using the scatter plots shown in
Figure 5a.
To address heteroscedasticity, we transform dependent variables. We multiply the returns
by the holding period. This can be observed from the ﬁgure 5b. The patterns and shapes of the

scatter plots are much more clustered and evenly distributed. Addressing heteroscedasticity
by incorporating the holding period is a practical approach that acknowledges the empirical
observation that variability in returns tend to be inversely proportional to the length of the
real estate investment period. By multiplying the returns by the holding period, we effectively
scale the returns to account for this relationship and align them with the investment duration.
This correction ensures that the analysis considers the varying levels of risk and volatility
associated with the different holding periods. In real estate, it is often observed that shorter-
term investments exhibit higher levels of volatility, whereas longer-term investments tend to
have more stable returns (proﬁt is already achieved). By adjusting the returns based on the
holding period, we accountfor these dynamics, and we provide a more accurate representation
of the true risk and return proﬁle for each investment duration. Furthermore, incorporating
the holding period in this manner allows for better comparability of returns across invest-
ment horizons. This enables more meaningful assessments of performance and risk, as the
adjustment helps to normalise the data and remove the bias introduced by heteroscedasticity.6

4.2 Sample size
Sample size is an important consideration when interpreting information. Sample size refers
to the number of data points or observations used to compute the indices (and conﬁdence
interval).8 Sample size can affect their interpretation. The sample size for the indices depends
on their construction methodology and frequency with which they are are updated or rebal-
anced.9
A larger sample size is generally preferred to compute this index. A larger sample size
provides greater conﬁdence in the estimated index. In addition, with a larger sample, the
conﬁdence interval is more likely to capture the true population parameters, leading to higher
conﬁdence in the accuracy of the estimate. Indeed, a larger sample size generally leads
to narrower conﬁdence intervals. With more data points, more information is available to
estimate the true population parameters, resulting in increased precision.
In this section, we analyse what happens for the two indices when the size of the sample
used to compute these indices decrease. Sample size inﬂuences the statistical signiﬁcance of
the estimates. Larger samples can provide more statistical power, allowing for a more accu-
rate assessment of relationships, trends, and differences between variables. Smaller sample
sizes may result in wider conﬁdence intervals, making it challenging to draw statistically
signiﬁcant indices. However, a desirable index methodology should remain stable, accurate,
and representative even with a smaller dataset. Indeed, the sample size may decrease for
various reasons, such as crisis, market movement, and pandemics.
Here, we decrease the size of the database used to compute the two indices (with bootstrap
and sampling procedures) to analyse the implications for the accuracy, representativeness,
and reliability of the index. Figure 7 presents the 1991-2001 indices for various sample sizes
ranging from 100% (Figure 7a) to 0.1% (Figure 7f).
Figure 7 shows that a smaller database introduces higher volatility or instability into
the index calculations for both approaches. Indeed, the conﬁdence interval widens for both
approaches. However, the factorial approach appears to be much more robust, with a narrower
conﬁdence interval. This difference is particularly obvious for very small datasets, such as
those shown in Figures 7e and 7f. The standard repeat sales approach seems to lead to reduced
statistical conﬁdence in index calculations and a decrease in the precision and reliability of
index estimates such as trends or volatility. We have conﬁrmed these observations in Table

4.3 Reducing estimation frequency
The frequency with which an index is constructed or calculated also inﬂuences the sample
size. When indices are published more frequently, there is often a trade-off between the
frequency of publication and the available data to compute the indices. This trade-off arises
because the shorter the time period between publications, the fewer transactions or data points
typically available for each publication.
Increasingthefrequencyofindexpublicationsimplieslesstimetoaccumulateasubstantial
amount of data for each publication. This reduced timeframe limits the number of transactions
or data points that can be included in the calculations. Consequently, the sample size for each
prediction may have been small, potentially affecting the representativeness and reliability of
the index. Furthermore, given the small sample size of each publication, the resulting indices
may exhibit increased volatility. A limited number of transactions or data points can amplify
the impact of individual transactions on the calculated index values.
123

Annals of Operations Research
The decision to publish indices more frequently is often driven by a desire to provide more
up-to-date information to market participants. Although increased frequency offers timely
insights into market conditions, it comes at the cost of less data available for each publication.
Market participants must balance the need for timely information with potential trade-offs
in terms of data quality and reliability.
Finally, increasing the frequency of index publications often means working with smaller
datasets, which can lead to challenges in the index methodology. Balancing the trade-off
between frequency and data size is essential for providing timely information while main-
taining the integrity of the indices. When data availability is limited owing to more frequent
publications, it becomes even more crucial to employ robust and reliable methodologies for
index computation. This methodology should account for small sample sizes and potential
data limitations to ensure that the resulting indices are as accurate and representative as
possible. Here, we analyse the ability of the two approaches to remain efﬁcient when the
frequency of publications increases. This is illustrated in Figure 8.
The two graphs in Figure 8 demonstrate the contrasting behavior of the standard repeat
sales approach and factorial approach in estimating returns and volatility.
– In the graph depicting the standard repeat sales approach (Figure 8a), we observe con-
siderable variability across different periodicities. This high variation suggests that the
return and volatility estimates derived using this approach are prone to ﬂuctuations and
inconsistencies. Such volatility can be problematic when attempting to make accurate
and reliable predictions or draw meaningful conclusions from data.
– Conversely, the graph representing the factorial approach exhibits a markedly different
pattern. Here, we observe a much higher concentration with signiﬁcantly fewer variations
across the different periodicities. This indicates that the return and volatility estimates
derived using the factorial approach are more stable and consistent. This consistency is
crucial for making reliable comparisons and informed decisions based on the data.
The contrasting behavior of the two graphs strongly supports the argument that the factorial
approach is superior to the standard repeat sales approach, particularly when estimating
returns and volatility. The concentrated nature of the factorial approach graph implies that
the estimates generated by this methodology exhibit higher precision and reliability, even
when the dataset is limited.
By utilising the factorial approach, the inherent variability and inconsistencies associated
with the standard repeat sales approach can be overcome, particularly in scenarios in which
data availability is reduced by more frequent computations. The graph’s clear visualisation
of the factorial approach’s reduced variations underscores its efﬁciency in providing robust
estimates of returns and volatility, even for small periodicities, making it a recommended
choice for accurate real estate analysis.
The factorial approach also demonstrates adaptability to distinct market conﬁgurations.
In highly dynamic markets, where transactions are frequent and information is abundant,
the factorial index converges toward the traditional repeat-sales index since both exploit suf-
ﬁciently rich price signals. However, in markets subject to abrupt shifts-such as ﬁnancial
crises, credit contractions, or periods of exceptional price acceleration-the factorial approach
remains stable because it anchors property returns to the evolution of macro-ﬁnancial fac-
tors (interest rates, rents, equity and REIT performance, unemployment, etc.). These factors
act as latent drivers of market behavior and provide an external reference when transaction-
based information becomes unreliable or discontinuous. As a result, the factorial index can
continue to reﬂect economic reality during turbulent phases, while the repeat-sales index
becomes excessively volatile or even inestimable. This property makes the factorial frame-

5 Conclusion
In this study, we examine one of the critical pitfalls and limitations of the traditional repeat
sales index: the inability of the approach to accurately replicate the true variations in the
market when the number of available transactions is small. This limitation arises in situa-
tions characterized by a thin market, limited data availability, frequent computations (shorter
periodicity), or weak market conditions. The consequences of this limitation have signiﬁcant
implications for the estimation of the index itself as well as for the market trends and volatility
derived from the index.

index, posing challenges in accurately estimating crucial parameters. This limitation becomes
particularly pronounced when the number of transactions decreases, making it an important
factor to consider in standard approaches, especially in scenarios involving short periodicity
analyses, crisis periods, small cities, and speciﬁc neighbourhoods.
To address this limitation, our study introduces a factorial methodology (see Baroni et
al., 2011) that leads to a much more robust and reliable index, even in situations where the
quantity of available data is inherently small. The factorial approach demonstrates its ability
and stability for trend and volatility measurements regardless of the quantity of data, with a
minimum threshold necessary to ensure accurate estimation.
Furthermore, our empirical analysis highlights the inadequacy of the traditional approach
in capturing market movements in small markets with a limited number of transactions or
during periods of decreased transaction volume, such as more frequent index publications,
thin markets, submarkets, the impact of Covid-19 restrictions or and housing market crashes.
Although the differences between the two models are minimal when dealing with large
samples, the factorial approach consistently exhibits superior efﬁciency in terms of robust
index, stable trend, and reliable volatility estimations.
From an operational standpoint, the factorial methodology can be implemented by national
statisticalagenciesorprofessionalindexproviderswithminimaladaptationofexistingrepeat-
sales frameworks. In particular, it can stabilize index publication during market crises or for

small municipalities where transaction volumes are low. Similar challenges in thin mar-
kets have been emphasized by Francke (2010) and Francke and Van de Minne (2017), who
show that robust index construction is critical when observations are sparse or clustered. By
integrating macro-ﬁnancial variables-rents, interest rates, REIT returns, or unemployment
rates-the factorial approach provides a bridge between classical econometric techniques and
applied decision-making contexts, enabling regulators, lenders, and investors to monitor price
dynamics even when direct market evidence is scarce. This operational ﬂexibility aligns with
the needs of applied econometrics and urban-ﬁnance practice, where reliable indicators must
be produced despite data scarcity.
From a ﬁnancial perspective, the factorial approach implicitly decomposes real-estate
returns into macroeconomic risk factors similar to those used in multi-factor asset-pricing
models (see, for instance Baroni et al., 2008, 2011). In this framework, the factorial index
behaves analogously to a portfolio exposed to systematic sources of risk-interest-rate move-
ments, rent growth, and equity-market performance-rather than to idiosyncratic transaction
noise. This structure helps interpret the stability of the factorial index as a manifestation of
diversiﬁcation across correlated macro-drivers. It also connects with the empirical ﬁndings
of Amédée-Manesme et al. (2019), who show that macroeconomic fundamentals largely
explain medium-term real-estate dynamics in the Paris market. In this sense, the factorial
approach provides not only a statistical advantage but also a clearer economic intuition for
practitioners accustomed to factor-based ﬁnancial modelling, where robustness arises from
the link between market performance and underlying risk factors.
Based on the robust evidence and ﬁndings presented in this study, we strongly recommend
adopting a factorial approach for computing repeat sales indices, regardless of the size of the
dataset. By leveraging the proposed methodology, researchers and practitioners can beneﬁt
from the enhanced reliability, stability, and accuracy in capturing the intricate dynamics of
the real estate market. The factorial approach is a valuable tool for real estate market analysis,
empowering stakeholders to make informed decisions and gain deeper insights into market
trends even in scenarios where data availability is limited. This interpretation is consistent
with Bhar et al. (2024), who identify macro-ﬁnancial cycles underlying U.S. home-price
dynamics, reinforcing the idea that real-estate returns can be decomposed into systematic
economic components.
Future work could extend this comparison empirically by assessing how factorial, hedonic,
and appraisal-based indices co-move over identical samples, providing further evidence on
their relative responsiveness to market shocks.
