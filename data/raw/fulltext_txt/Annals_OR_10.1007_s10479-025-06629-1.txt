Abstract
Intoday’sacademiclandscape,itisessentialtocontinuouslyevaluateresearchcenters,includ-
ing assessing scientiﬁc output and inter-researcher collaboration. This paper delves into the
intricate dynamics of collaboration among researchers at the Italian Institute of Technol-
ogy, one of Italy’s leading research organizations, employing an Exponential Random Graph
Model (ERGM) approach. Our analysis enabled the identiﬁcation of speciﬁc network statis-
tics and the social and demographic characteristics of authors that signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the
structure of the scientiﬁc collaboration network. The analysis encompasses 1,469 researchers
based at the Institute’s headquarters in 2020, examining collaborative connections established
through joint publications. Speciﬁcally, through the lens of an ERGM, this study examines
the interplay between sex and research areas - Computational Sciences, Life Technologies,
Nanomaterials, and Robotics - shedding light on how these factors shape collaborative rela-
tionships.Bybridgingthegapbetweennetworkandnon-networkcharacteristics,thisresearch
contributes to the literature on network formation, one of the most promising areas within
knowledge and innovation networks. The ﬁndings hold potential implications for the for-
mulation of research policies aimed at promoting equity and fostering a more inclusive and
synergistic research community.

seeking funding and navigating an increasingly segmented and specialized landscape of sci-
entiﬁc knowledge, driven by the complexity of research problems. The interdisciplinary and
resource-intensive nature of modern science necessitates collaborative efforts, as evidenced
by the rise in scientiﬁc collaborations over recent decades (Drenth, 1998; Glänzel, 2002;

Barnett et al. (1988) identify specialization opportunities as a key factor driving co-
authorship, with collaboration often resulting from the synergy of complementary skills
(Hudson, 1996). This specialization supports labor division (Hauptman, 2005), knowledge
dissemination (Söderbaum, 2001), and resource sharing (Bammer, 2008), enhancing pro-
ductivity and innovation at both individual and organizational levels. Multi-authored papers,
stemming from synergistic interactions, tend to have higher impact and citation rates (Taham-
tan et al., 2016), reﬂecting improved scientiﬁc quality (Laband, 1987; Chung et al., 2009;
Johnson, 1997).
The analysis of scientiﬁc collaboration spans both social and natural sciences, revealing
a positive trend in interdisciplinary cooperation (Babchuk et al., 1999; Moody, 2004). This
trend varies across disciplines, with natural sciences showing more collaborations, potentially
due to larger laboratory environments and complex quantitative methodologies. Advances
in communication technologies have further facilitated collaboration, making it easier and
faster for researchers to work together.
Quantifying the beneﬁts of scientiﬁc cooperation has become possible with advancements
in data collection and computational capacities. Collaborative research networks provide
valuable resources such as equipment, expertise, and social capital, deﬁned as the beneﬁts
derived from social relationships within the network (Coleman, 1988). Co-authorship, as a
formal manifestation of intellectual cooperation, plays a crucial role in building social capital
(Acedo et al., 2006).
This paper builds on a previous study by di Bella et al. (2021), which provided an in-depth
analysis of the coauthorship network of researchers at the Italian Institute of Technology
(IIT), one of the leading research centres in Italy1. By conducting both macro and micro-
level analyses, the authors examined the overall structure and characteristics of the network,
its evolution over the years, and identiﬁed the individuals who play pivotal roles in the
scientiﬁc collaboration network. They also pinpointed practices that lead to higher research
performance.
Building upon this foundation, this paper extends the previous research by conducting a
meso-level analysis of collaboration dynamics within the IIT research community. Specif-
ically, we examine how network statistics and socio-demographic characteristics inﬂuence
researchers’ propensity to form collaborative relationships. This approach provides a nuanced
understanding of the social dynamics within IIT and identiﬁes potential areas for fostering
more inclusive and effective collaborative practices.

features, either endogenous (structural network variables) and exogenous (node character-

To explore these questions, we employ an Exponential Random Graph Model (ERGM),
which models the probability of link formation between nodes, by studying the conﬁguration
of current connections between authors. Speciﬁcally, we investigate how individual attributes
(e.g., sex, nationality, seniority and research ﬁeld) and network structures (e.g., transitivity)

of link formation represents one of the most promising areas within knowledge and inno-
vation networks2 (Resce et al., 2022). Despite extensive research on scientiﬁc collaboration
networks, few studies have speciﬁcally analyzed internal collaboration dynamics within a
single research center using social network analysis (SNA) techniques. Unlike prior studies
focusing on national or international collaborations, this research provides an institutional-
level perspective, leveraging bibliometric data at the individual level.
Furthermore, few studies examine the structure of collaborations based on gender3
(Koseoglu, 2016). Existing research has primarily focused on gender disparities in scien-
tiﬁc productivity (Toutkoushian & Bellas, 1999; Lee & Bozeman, 2005; Rørstad & Aksnes,
2015; Lavelle, 2023), often concluding that women tend to have lower publication rates than
men. However, limited attention has been given to whether gender disparities extend to col-
laboration patterns within research institutions. By examining the IIT network, this study
seeks to determine whether gender inﬂuences collaboration tendencies and whether these
disparities vary across different research ﬁelds.
Additionally, the ERGM incorporates network-based mechanisms such as triadic closure
for a deeper understanding of how collaboration tendencies emerge within research insti-
tutions. By focusing on these factors, our research contributes to the broader literature on
knowledge and innovation networks while offering insights for policy-makers aiming to
promote gender equity in research collaboration.
The paper is structured as follows. It begins with a description of the data collected
from Scopus and provided by the research center, followed by a detailed explanation of the
methodology. It then examines the collaboration network among IIT researchers in 2020
from a meso perspective. Finally, the paper concludes with interpretations and conclusions
based on the ﬁndings.

2.2 Methodology
As mentioned in the Introduction, we perform an Exponential Random Graph Model
(ERGM), which represent one of the central approaches in analyzing social networks (graphs)
introduced by Holland and Leinhardt (1981), and further developed by Frank and Strauss
(1986) and 7 Wasserman and Pattison (1996). We decided to implement ERGMs because,
rather than assuming that network ties are independent as most traditional statistical models,
they explicitly account for structural dependencies within the network (Lusher et al., 2020).
ERGMs are particularly well-suited for capturing endogenous (structural) network charac-
teristics (Zinilli et al., 2024), such as transitivity, which play a crucial role in predicting
scientiﬁc collaboration between authors. At the same time, they also allow for the inclusion
of control variables (node level attributes) - such as sex, nationality or seniority - commonly
considered in previous studies, providing a more comprehensive analysis of collaboration
dynamics. These models postulate an exponential family over the graph space, deﬁned by
their sufﬁcient statistics represented by the graph’s features deemed pertinent to the social
process that had produced it. ERGMs treat each network link (tie, edge) as a random variable
and can explain why a given observed network presents a particular structure, in contrast to
alternative conﬁgurations. In essence, these models enable the identiﬁcation of the underlying
processes inﬂuencing link creation (van Der Pol, 2019). ERG models may encompass two
categories of explanatory variables: the ﬁrst type pertains to node or edge-level covariates,
such as sex, nationality, seniority, and so forth, while the second type considers structural
characteristics of the network, typically subgraphs, triadic closure, and degree distribution
statistics.
In this study, ERGM is employed to model a binary output concerning collaborative
relationships among actors; speciﬁcally, it models the presence or absence of a collaborative
relationship between a given actor i and a given actor j. It was decided to include in the
model key statistics chosen to represent collaborative relationship selection rules operating
at a local level. Initially, a simpliﬁed form of ERGMs is performed by exclusively considering
the node-level attributes, i.e., the socio-demographic characteristics of each author. These
models assume the form of a Bernoullirandom graph8, where the probability of a link between
two vertices cannot be explained by the existence of another link within the network; in other
words, all links occur independently with the same probability. Consequently, the terms of
these models are referred to as “dyad independent”.
Then, we also incorporated network statistics among the covariates to estimate the proba-
bility of a link. Since the presence or absence of a link leads to changes in network statistics,
the inclusion of these terms results in cascading effects that render the model more complex
and necessitate a different estimation algorithm, as will be explained below. These terms are
referred to as “dyad dependent”.

Thedecisiontoexpandthemodelstemmedfromthenaturalexpectationthattheprobability

7 nodes has 2,097,152 possible conﬁgurations. To address this, Geyer and Thompson (1992)
proposed a technique called Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) maximum likelihood
estimation18, in which a stochastic approximation to the likelihood function is built and
then maximized (Hunter et al., 2008). This technique leverages the law of large numbers,
estimating the model by sampling a very large number of possible networks through an
MCMC algorithm. Thus, the maximum likelihood of the MCMC sample approximates the
maximum likelihood estimate of the model, shown in Equation 1. In essence, an ERGM
assesses an observed network by comparing it to the various ﬁnite ways it could be arranged.
MCMC simulations are then used to conduct a goodness of ﬁt test for the model.
The ERGM is based on two straightforward assumptions. First, the model is completely
and correctly speciﬁed: given a set of sufﬁcient statistics deﬁned on the graph, there is
an equal probability of observing any two graphs with the same value of those statistics
(Desmarais & Cranmer, 2012). Second, the identiﬁcation of the model parameters assumes
that a network realization presents the average value of those statistics over the possible
network conﬁgurations that could have been observed.
3 Results
We may anticipate a scenario where network ties are not only self-organized but also inﬂu-
enced by the attributes of individuals representing the vertices in the graph. Each individual
brings their own capacities, capabilities, and predispositions to a social system (Lusher et
al., 2013). The qualities of individuals within networks play a signiﬁcant role in determining
the formation of these network ties. Furthermore, the probability of a tie’s existence could
be inﬂuenced by the graph’s structural features. To empirically test these hypotheses and
identify the extent to which each variable affects the probability of a relationship between
two authors, we perform an Exponential Random Graph Model (ERGM).
First, a simpliﬁed form of the ERGM is performed by considering only the sociodemo-
graphic attributes of each author. As previously mentioned, this model takes the form of a

Second, network statistics are included as predictor variables to estimate the likelihood of

The present application used the R package ergm, which is part of the statnet suite

Table 2 reports the results of the ﬁrst two models, which include a term for the overall
number of edges in y and several terms related to nodal properties. These nodal covariates
represent homophily terms20, which measure the tendency of nodes to connect with others
who are similar to themselves. A substantial and growing body of empirical research high-
lights the inﬂuence of node similarity on collaborative relationship formation (Leicht et al.,
2006; Lü et al., 2009). Mathematically, for each class of a categorical variable (Sex, Nation-
ality, and Research Domains), a term is introduced that equals 1 if the two incident nodes
share the class value and 0 otherwise (e.g., the term for Sex = F is equal to 1 if and only if
both nodes are female). Additionally, a homophily term for the continuous variable Seniority
is introduced, based on the assumption that the propensity to form collaborative relationships
also depends on the difference between two actors’ seniority values (e.g., researchers with
the same career length may be more likely to collaborate). The absdiff function computes
the absolute difference in the Seniority (number of years from ﬁrst publication) between
incident nodes for a link, summed across all links.

According to these models, two actors can match based on exogenous attributes. How-
ever, a further source for local clustering could arise from the tendency of actors to form
collaborative relationships based on shared partners. To represent the network structure more
accurately, and since triangles are prevalent in social networks, Models 3 and 4 include a
term for transitivity in addition to the variables of Models 1 and 2.
Model 3 and Model 4 introduce an additional dyad-dependent term called the Geometri-
cally Weighted Edgewise Shared Partnership (GWESP). This statistic, detailed in “Material
and Methods” Section, employs a curved exponential family form to represent shared part-
ner distributions22. It represents a term for triadic closure or network transitivity, where the
presence of a link between actors i and j (Yi j = 1), and between actors j and k (Y jk = 1)
increases the probability of a social relationship between actors i and k (Yik = 1) (Levy
& Lubell, 2018), as previous relationships can inﬂuence link formation within the cluster
(Capone & Zampi, 2020). In other words, triadic closure refers to the tendency of authors
who share a common collaborator to establish direct collaboration themselves.
In this setting, the dyadic dependence property applies23, wherein the triadic closure
effect stems from an intrinsic feature of network formation rather than merely a side effect of
homophily (Hunter et al., 2008). Besides assessing the effect of by sex, nationality, seniority,
and research ﬁeld on collaborative relationship formation among researchers, Models 3 and
4 allow us to control for some effects of transitivity.

Assessing goodness of ﬁt in an ordinary least squares regression typically involves exam-
ining residuals from the model. However, in an ERGM, there is no direct analog to residuals.
Instead, to test the goodness-of-ﬁt, simulated networks are generated using estimated model
parameters via an MCMC algorithm. Subsequently, various structural measures are computed
from these simulated graphs. This approach facilitates comparison of the network statistics
of the simulated graphs with those of the observed graph, as illustrated in Figures 3 - 4,
which display boxplots for statistics over 100 simulations from the underlying ERGM, with
observed network statistics represented by heavy black lines. Speciﬁcally, the distributions
encompass degree counts, edgewise shared partner counts, and minimum geodesic distances,
as these measures phenomena such as sociality, clustering, transitivity, and centrality.
A comparison of these graphs reveals that Model 3, which incorporates edgewise shared
partners, exhibits a superior ﬁt compared to models solely focusing on actor heterogene-
ity through nodal covariates, although these variables remain highly relevant variables for
predicting collaborative relationships among researchers. Given that social relationships typ-
ically exhibit local clustering, it’s evident that the simple dyad independent model (Figure
3) underestimates the number of shared partners individuals should have. Model 3 appears
to capture the full behavior of the network.

3.1 Supplementary ERGMs
To further advance this research, we conducted four additional models by introducing a new
variable into the regressors of the ERGM: a control variable for the job positions held by
the authors within the research organization. This addition aims to explore the relationship
between job positions and the propensity to collaborate among authors. Speciﬁcally, the
objective is to validate the hypothesis that collaborative relationships are more likely between
authors holding different job positions rather than between those in either top or lower
positions.
This hypothesis is based on ﬁndings from previously implemented models, particularly
the best-performing Model 3. In this model, the variable “seniority” (measured as the number
of years since the ﬁrst publication) partially captured job position information. The estimated
coefﬁcient for seniority suggested that a greater difference in seniority increased the like-
lihood of collaboration. However, despite its signiﬁcance, the coefﬁcient’s magnitude was
relatively low. Additionally, it was veriﬁed that there is not a perfect correlation between the
job position and seniority variables. The data revealed instances of authors in top positions
who had not published for many years (e.g., authors in the Research Direction category who
had not published for 4, 5, 7, and 10 years) and conversely, authors in lower positions (e.g.,
Postdocs and Researchers) with higher seniority. Furthermore, seniority values varied even
within the same job position. Given these observations, we included the job roles of authors
as a regressor to account for these dynamics.
Additionally, we aim to demonstrate that collaborative triangles are not exclusively formed
by male authors in higher-ranking positions.

notably larger than the other coefﬁcients, highlighting the inﬂuential role of transitivity.
In conclusion, these ﬁndings indicate that triangles in the collaboration network are not
exclusively comprised of male authors in high-ranking positions. Nevertheless, the occur-
rence of triangles is more prevalent among individuals in senior roles compared to those
in other job positions. Within the Research Direction category, triangles are slightly more
common among males than females.
It is noteworthy that the Akaike’s Information Criterion (AIC) value for the models that
include the triangle statistic (Model 7 and Model 8) is lower, indicating a better-ﬁt model.
To conﬁrm this preference, the AIC values were compared with the results of goodness-of-
ﬁt diagnostics for ERGMs. Figures 5 and 6 report the goodness-of-ﬁt plots of Model 7 and
Model 8, demonstrating that both models effectively capture the full behavior of the network.

4 Discussions and Conclusions
In today’s academic landscape, it is essential to continuously assess research centers, includ-
ing evaluating scientiﬁc output, inter-researcher collaboration, and openness to national and
international peers. This study focuses on the Italian Institute of Technology (IIT), exam-
ining collaboration within the center and across its various scientiﬁc disciplines, known as
research domains. By employing an Exponential Random Graph Model (ERGM), this paper
contributes to the literature on network formation, with a speciﬁc focus on local clustering.
Our analysis identiﬁes speciﬁc network statistics and socio-demographic attributes of
authors that signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the structure of the scientiﬁc collaboration network.
Notably, we ﬁnd gender differences in the propensity to collaborate, with males more likely
to engage in cooperative relationships than females, and we explore potential differences

partly attributed to women’s lower collaboration propensity or less-developed collaboration
networks. This is in addition to well-documented barriers in the literature, such as various

Overall, results indicate that collaboration is more likely between authors with similar
characteristics across all variables considered, except for seniority, where collaborations are
more likely between junior and senior researchers than between two juniors or two seniors.
Additionally, the presence of the GWESP statistic highlights a non-trivial transitivity effect,
underscoring the importance of trust and previous relationships in forming collaborative ties.
The insights gained from this study can help foster inclusivity and diversity within research
communities. Our results indicate that collaboration tendencies increase among authors with
similar job positions at higher hierarchical levels. This pattern, observed in other studies (for
instance, in Rørstad and Aksnes, 2015), suggests the existence of ‘closed clubs’, primarily

composed of male actors in higher positions, as women are underrepresented in leadership
roles. To mitigate these barriers and cultivate a more inclusive research culture, the Institute
could implement targeted strategies to promote collaboration between actors with different
job positions. For instance, promoting networking events or implementing sponsorship pro-
grams may facilitate collaborations between newcomers (novice or less established within
the network) and established researchers (actively engaged in the network for a signiﬁ-
cant period), mitigating the dominance of ‘closed clubs’ (Enger, 2018; Zinilli & Cerulli,
2023). More precisely, implementing structured mentoring initiatives that pair newcomers
and established researchers could enhance their professional networks and increase collabo-
ration opportunities. Another effective approach would be to organize internal workshops and
seminars, where early-career researchers can present their work and receive feedback from
more experienced scientists. This would not only strengthen research networks within the
institution but also encourage interdisciplinary and cross-hierarchical collaboration, fostering
a more equitable and dynamic academic environment.
By addressing systemic barriers to collaboration, such as gender disparities, policymakers
and research administrators can create more equitable and productive research environments.
Although this study provides insights into the dynamics of scientiﬁc collaboration within IIT,
it is crucial to recognize the variations that may exist in different organizational contexts and
cultural landscapes. The principles underlying collaborative networks can offer valuable
insights applicable to various research environments.
It is important to acknowledge certain limitations of this study. First, our analysis focuses
exclusively on co-authorship links among authors afﬁliated with the Italian Institute of Tech-
nology, resulting in a partial view of the network. This approach was guided by two main
considerations: the lack of socio-demographic data for external co-authors and the aim to
concentrate on internal scientiﬁc collaboration within IIT. While this focus enables a tar-
geted analysis, it excludes external partnerships - where IIT researchers co-author papers
with external colleagues - thereby omitting signiﬁcant aspects of the broader knowledge net-
work. Including external collaborators would have expanded the study’s scope considerably,
introducing edge effects akin to those in geographic studies and complicating the modeling
of socio-demographic attributes essential for ERGMs. Second, co-authorship represents only
one form of joint collaboration, and not all collaborations result in publications (Fonseca et
al., 2016; Zinilli et al., 2024). Third, our dataset, based solely on a bibliometric source, does
not capture the entirety of IIT’s collaborative network. Other databases may include publi-
cations in different journals or languages, providing a more comprehensive representation
(Abbasi et al., 2011).
To extend our research, we plan to explore advanced methodologies, including machine
learning techniques, to predict link formation between authors. Additionally, it would be
worthwhile to conduct national or international comparisons among different research orga-
nizations, particularly those in countries with different cultural contexts. Such analyses would
provide a broader understanding of collaboration styles across various research environments,
further enriching the insights gained from our study.

We conducted a community detection analysis to further validate and complement the ERG
models results.
The analysis was performed on the giant component of the co-authorship network that
consists of 1,377 authors26.
According to the ERGMs results, collaboration is more likely to occur between researchers
in the same ﬁeld. The institute is organized into four primary domains, allowing researchers
to be grouped into four distinct clusters: Computational Sciences, Technologies for Life
Science (Life Tech), Nanomaterials, and Robotics, along with Facilities and Internal Staff.
Beyond this domain-based classiﬁcation, the network structure itself can be leveraged to
identify an alternative clustering. This is achieved using a divisive hierarchical clustering
approach, speciﬁcally the Girvan-Newman betweenness algorithm, introduced by Michelle
Girvan and Mark Newman (2002) Girvan and Newman (2002). The core principle of this
algorithm is that edges connecting distinct modules are likely to have high edge betweenness,
as all geodesic paths between separate modules must pass through them. The algorithm
progressively removes edges with the highest edge betweenness values to reveal clusters.
When applied to the network, the Girvan-Newman method identiﬁes 35 research com-
munities (or clusters). These range from small clusters with fewer than ten researchers to
large ones with over 100 authors, as well as mid-sized clusters containing between 12 and
78 authors. Figure 7 showcases the largest communities in the IIT co-authorship network, as
identiﬁed by the hierarchical clustering algorithm.
A contingency table was built to examine patterns of collaboration between research
domains and to assess the relationship between the two identiﬁed partitions. Table 8 focuses
on clusters with at least 10 authors, collectively accounting for 95% of the total observations.

Each row in the table corresponds to a cluster, while each column represents a research

The data in Table 8 conﬁrms a strong tendency for authors to collaborate with others within
the same domain. For example, the largest cluster predominantly comprises researchers from
the Nanomaterials domain (83%), the second-largest cluster involves researchers from Life
Tech (73%), and the third is primarily composed of Robotics researchers (96%).
Interestingly, authors from Computational Sciences show a broader distribution across
domains, yet a notable tendency to collaborate with the Life Tech domain is observed, as
reﬂected in clusters no. 3 and no. 6. Facilities staff, due to their support role for the institute’s
primary research lines, tend to collaborate with all domains. Smaller clusters, by contrast,
are often composed predominantly of researchers from a single RD or present a dominant
domain.
The ﬁnal column of Table 8 presents the normalized Gini heterogeneity index values
for each cluster27. The index ranges from 0 to 1, where a value closer to 0 indicates a
homogeneous cluster with members from a single research domain, while a value closer to
1 indicates a more heterogeneous distribution of domains within a cluster.
For instance, cluster no. 14, no. 22, and no. 28 have a Gini heterogeneity index of 0.00, as
they consist entirely of authors from a single domain. In contrast, cluster no. 10 has a Gini
index of 0.83, reﬂecting a more diverse composition with authors from multiple domains.
Since communities are largely dominated by single RDs, this strongly supports ERGM
ﬁnding of RD homophily. Additionally, these results provide valuable insights into the
degreeofinterdisciplinarycollaborationamongpersonnel.Thisinformationcanguidepolicy-
making efforts aimed at enhancing the impact of scientiﬁc collaborations by strengthening
existing partnership and fostering the development of new ones, ultimately improving col-
laborative outcomes.
ERGMs include authors’ sex and institutional roles as control variables. Regarding sex, the
models reveal gender differences in collaboration tendencies, with males being more likely to
engage in cooperative relationships than females. The community detection analysis reﬂects
a mix of genders within clusters, with no strong evidence of female-dominated groups.
This partially aligns with the ERGMs results, although the gender effect is less pronounced
compared to the patterns observed for research domains.
In terms of institutional roles, ERGMs indicate that collaboration tends to increase among
authors with similar job positions, particularly at higher hierarchical levels. This ﬁnding is
supported by the community detection analysis, which shows that 60% of Research Direction
members are concentrated within just ﬁve of the 35 identiﬁed clusters, underscoring the
tendency of hierarchical roles to shape collaboration patterns.
Both methods underscore the importance of shared RDs or job positions in shaping collab-
oration patterns. However, it is important to recognize that ERGMs and community detection
capture distinct aspects of collaboration. ERGMs explicitly model the likelihood of tie for-
mation based on speciﬁc attributes, offering a detailed view of individual-level collaboration
dynamics. In contrast, community detection reveals broader structural patterns, identify-
ing clusters of researchers within the network. Together, these complementary approaches
enhance our understanding of collaboration dynamics from different analytical perspectives.
