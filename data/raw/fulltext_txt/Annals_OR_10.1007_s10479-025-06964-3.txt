Abstract
Remanufacturing-as-a-Service promises scalable, lower-cost repair of metal components 
by coordinating distributed capabilities through a cloud platform. This paper formulates 
a quantitative, supply-chain optimization of cloud-enabled remanufacturing built on a hy¬≠
brid additive‚Äďsubtractive (ASM) repair cost model and evaluates three delivery scenarios: 
(i) centralized national hubs with hybrid ASM; (ii) collaborations with tier-one local fa¬≠
cilities that own separate AM and SM equipment; and (iii) collaborations with tier-two 
local facilities that provide only partial capability, requiring multi-facility routing. Using 
a U.S. tooling and machinery case study, this paper quantitatively fuses stepwise ASM 
repair economics with strategic facility location under an explicit quality‚Äďcost trade-off, 
closing a gap between process modeling and supply-chain design in remanufacturing. 
Results show that across plausible freights and capital costs, decentralized collaborations 
remain within 5% of the cost optimum while improving regional responsiveness; longer 
planning horizons shift the near-optimal set toward 5‚Äď8 hubs, rather than a single national 
site. Sensitivity tests confirm that (a) higher transportation costs and longer horizons favor 
additional hubs, while (b) tighter accuracy thresholds steer volume to hybrid hubs or vet¬≠
ted tier-one partners. The model provides clear trigger points‚ÄĒbased on accuracy targets, 
capital expenditures, and freight conditions‚ÄĒfor deciding when to build new hubs versus 
contracting with local partners. It offers managers a robust tool to navigate the critical 
trade-offs between cost efficiency, service reach, and quality.

1‚Äā Introduction
Driven by the need for industrial sustainability and resource efficiency, remanufacturing has 
become a vital strategy for extending product life and reducing material consumption (Aziz 
et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). The global market for remanufactured goods is substantial 
and growing, with the U.S. tooling and machinery sector alone valued at nearly $6 billion 
in 2011 (Lee et al., 2017). Concurrent with this trend, the rise of Cloud Manufacturing 
(CMfg) (Wu et al., 2013) and advanced repair technologies like hybrid additive‚Äďsubtractive 
manufacturing (ASM) (Li et al., 2018) has created a powerful new paradigm: Remanufac¬≠
1‚ÄĮ3

turing-as-a-Service (RaaS) (Wang & Wang, 2019). This model promises to create scalable, 
on-demand repair networks by coordinating distributed manufacturing resources through a 
central cloud platform, potentially lowering costs and increasing service reach (Andrews, 
2015; Lee et al., 2017).
However, while the conceptual framework for RaaS is established, a critical gap exists 
between its theoretical promise and practical implementation. The majority of academic 
literature on cloud remanufacturing focuses on operational-level problems, such as task 
scheduling and resource assignment within a pre-defined network (Caterino et al., 2022; 
Yu, 2022). These studies inherently assume a fixed supply-chain configuration and do not 
address the foundational strategic question: What is the most economically feasible network 
design for a RaaS platform? Moreover, they do not consider the recently emerging appli¬≠
cations of hybrid ASM processes for metal component repair and their associated param¬≠
eters. Furthermore, supply-chain models often lack detailed, process-level cost inputs for 
advanced technologies like hybrid ASM. This prevents a realistic assessment of the trade-
offs between centralized, high-tech hubs and decentralized local partners. Current studies 
rarely combine process-level repair economics for hybrid ASM (pre-/post-processing, addi¬≠
tive deposition, subtractive cleanup, consumables, energy) with transportation and platform 
costs in a single quantitative model, nor do they embed provider quality differences so that 
accuracy requirements can meaningfully influence facility count, partner choice, and rout¬≠
ing. Without this integration, it isn‚Äôt easy to judge when decentralized collaborations with 
local providers are economically near-optimal relative to centralized, high-accuracy hubs, 
or how planning horizons and logistics conditions reshape the feasible network.
To address this gap, we develop a network-design model for cloud-enabled remanu¬≠
facturing of metallic components. This work formulates the RaaS in the context of addi¬≠
tive manufacturing-enabled remanufacturing of metallic components under three different 
novel remanufacturing scenarios. It discusses the impacts of various parameters associated 
with the repair process or remanufacturing service on the obtained SC configuration and 
its economic feasibility. The base remanufacturing scenario realizes the remanufacturing 
service through national-level remanufacturing hubs, centralized national hubs with a whole 
hybrid ASM. The first scenario extends the base scenario to collaboration with tier-one 
local facilities that have full remanufacturing capabilities, with assumed relatively lower 
accuracy due to the decoupling of the additive and subtractive manufacturing processes, 
and creating a multi-machine route for the remanufacturing within their facilities. The sec¬≠
ond scenario extends the base scenario to collaboration with tier-two local facilities with 
partial remanufacturing capabilities, with assumed relatively lowest accuracy due to creat¬≠
ing a multi-machine and multi-facility route for the remanufacturing process. The model 
links stepwise ASM repair costs with transportation and platform costs and incorporates a 
calibrated accuracy penalty to reflect quality differences among provider configurations. In 
addition to evaluating the resultant SC configuration and sensitivity analysis with respect to 
various remanufacturing process-specific or remanufacturing service-specific parameters, 
the SC cost breakdown in each of these remanufacturing scenarios is also presented based 
on a case study for the tooling and machinery sector within the United States. The prob¬≠
lem is formulated as a mixed-integer optimization that selects facility locations and counts, 
assigns demand, and routes multi-step remanufacturing subject to capacity. A U.S. tooling/
machinery case over a discounted 10-year horizon anchors the analysis. The findings in this 
work bridge process-level ASM economics and strategic facility location under an explicit

quality‚Äďcost trade-off, offering a decision-ready basis for when to add hubs versus contract

2‚Äā Literature review
The general remanufacturing processes include disassembly, inspection, cleaning, restora¬≠
tion, reassembly, and testing (Dias et al., 2013; Gan & Pujawan, 2014) and remanufacturing 
is one of the best product recovery techniques, considering the product‚Äôs quality, service 
life, and warranty (Sharma et al., 2016; Wits et al., 2016). The restoration is the critical step 
in the entire remanufacturing process, and it can be achieved through various manufacturing 
techniques such as welding, AM, or hybrid ASM. According to Schniederjans et al. (2017) 
The remanufacturing process is considered to have a disruptive impact on the existing man¬≠
ufacturing-based SC network with an unexploited potential, as its implementation is still far 
from common. Promising and emerging applications for AM are the fields of maintenance 
and repair, as it presents many advantages compared to traditional techniques (Svetlizky et 
al., 2021). AM is a critical technology enabler for implementing a circular economy, shifting 
the paradigm from a linear ‚Äútake-make-dispose‚ÄĚ model to a circular ‚Äúrepair-reuse-remanu¬≠
facture‚ÄĚ model, especially for high-value metallic components (Ponis et al., 2021). This is in 
line with the global trend of moving towards more sustainable business models, as pursuing 
a lower raw material usage tackles problems such as non-renewable resources, environmen¬≠
tal overload, and supply deficiencies (Rahito et al., 2019). This reflects in a strong growth 
of the market for remanufactured products, as these emerging restoration technologies are 
advancing from mechanical and economic standpoints (Delic et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2017).
Rahito et al. (2019) collected and reviewed a multitude of published case studies of appli¬≠
cations of AM in metal component remanufacturing. For high-value components that would 
otherwise be scrapped, AM processes‚ÄĒparticularly Directed Energy Deposition (DED)‚ÄĒ
allow for the precise addition of material only to worn or damaged areas (Shrestha et al., 
2021; Svetlizky et al., 2021; Wilson et al., 2014). In a remanufacturing context, the fusion of 
additive and subtractive processes into a hybrid ASM system is particularly powerful. After 
the AM process restores the lost material, subsequent CNC machining finishes the part to 
its original dimensional tolerances and surface specifications, creating a component with 
a service life and performance comparable to a new one. This approach typically replaces 
only a small fraction of the component‚Äôs original mass, drastically reducing the energy, cost, 
and lead time associated with manufacturing a full replacement part (Rahito et al., 2019). 
The remanufacturing of high-performance turbine blades is a classic example. DED is used 
to repair worn blade tips and seals using nickel-based superalloys, restoring complex geom¬≠
etries and critical functionalities that would be impossible to fix with conventional methods. 
In the tooling sector, expensive stamping dies made from hardened steels are repaired using 
DED (Ponis et al., 2021). Studies have shown that the lifetime of a repaired automotive 
die can be equal to or even exceed that of a brand-new one, at a lower cost (Bennett et al.,

2019). Other literature studies (Saboori et al., 2019; Sames et al., 2016) report the supposed 
economic convenience of AM-based remanufacturing without quantitatively assessing it in 
a systematic way.
Among different areas for remanufacturing SC, quantitative models for production plan¬≠
ning and scheduling are the most researched area (Ansari & Daxini, 2022). The produc¬≠
tion planning and scheduling models have single and multi-objective models with objective 
functions to minimize costs (Yu, 2022), maximize matching efficiency (Lei, 2021) etc. 
Reverse logistics is usually linked to remanufacturing SCs. Reverse logistics is defined as 
the process of implementing, controlling, and planning the cost-effective flow of finished 
goods, raw materials, and in-process inventory from the point of consumption (e.g., cus¬≠
tomer) to the point of origin (e.g., manufacturer) to properly dispose of these or to recap¬≠
ture value (Cannas et al., 2020; Rossi et al., 2021). Such integration and attention on the 
remanufacturing problem in LDN modeling has only been shown from Strong et al. (2020) 
who addressed the specific case of small and medium enterprises (SMEs) accessing AM 
repair services without explicating the mathematical formulation. Zhang et al. (2019) con¬≠
sider AM-based repairing technologies to be crucial catalyzers for circular economies, and 
such a problem has been later modeled also by Santander et al. (2020) in the case of plastic 
recycling.
A paradigm shift from mass production to mass customization and from product-oriented 
to service-oriented manufacturing systems has been taking place (Aghamohammadzadeh et 
al., 2020). As CMfg has been described as a service-oriented, knowledge-based smart man¬≠
ufacturing system with high efficiency and low energy consumption (Bohu et al., 2010), it is 
facilitating this transition. The challenges in the SC network for manufacturing industries to 
implement CMfg have been discussed by He and Xu (2015). Research for cloud remanufac¬≠
turing is in the initial phase of introducing the cloud platform and qualitatively explaining 
its capabilities (Wang & Wang, 2014; Xia et al., 2015). Wang and Xu (2014) focused on how 
CMfg can enable more sustainable manufacturing practices, indicating that it is particularly 
useful for product end-of-life strategies such as remanufacturing, reconditioning, reuse, and 
repurposing. The cloud-enabled systems can, in fact, better support the remanufacturers in 
rebuilding the necessary knowledge for the product (Debnath et al., 2022) and can achieve 
the alignment between the supply chain partners by improving the optimization of the sup¬≠
ply chain information flow (Ali et al. 2020).
Mancusi et al. (2022) presented a cloud-based framework for complexity measurement 
for the assembly/disassembly step in the remanufacturing process. Haghnegahdar et al., 
(2022) suggest a network configuration made of distributed resources but managed in a 
centralized way. Very few studies have been published on the quantitative models for SC 
in cloud remanufacturing and those published are mainly solving production planning and 
scheduling issues. Their focus is on the products being realized as decentralized/distrib¬≠
uted resources managed in a centralized way, creating complex problems for assigning and 
scheduling of resources and tasks (Caterino et al., 2022; L. Zhou et al., 2018). In production 
planning and scheduling, most of these always make an assumption about the number and 
location of SC entities, as the aim of these studies is to match the customer with the right 
service with optimal cost, time, and quality (Yu, 2022). These studies also account for the 
number and unit cost of tasks/sub-tasks for the remanufacturing process, and they lack the 
impacts of remanufacturing process parameters on SC. They cannot evaluate the impact and 
economic feasibility of various remanufacturing scenarios at the supply chain level. This

literature to investigate a possible temporal evolution of the quantitative models. It can be 
seen that no assessment for the economic feasibility of cloud remanufacturing at the supply 
chain level, considering hybrid ASM repair cost components, exists. Moreover, literature 
lacks quantitative models based on reparation costs. The original contribution of this work is 
the quantitative modeling and assessment of SC cost for cloud remanufacturing via a hybrid 
ASM process and the study of the economic long-term feasibility of a decentralized SC for 
large-scale remanufacturing services with three different scenarios for CMfg in the tooling 
and machinery sector.
The classification of literature reported in Table¬†1 revolves around six main dimensions. 
The first one addresses the methodological approach followed by the authors, which may 
be either qualitative or quantitative. Here, you do not see any dominant pattern or specific 
school of thought, with an overlap of qualitative and quantitative studies over the years. The 
second dimension focuses on the circular economy and should be analyzed, followed by 
the third one devoted to SCs. While a circular SC is a prerequisite for the circular economy, 
besides our study, just two other papers (see Fisher et al., 2018; Santander et al., 2020) com¬≠
bine the notion of return flow in SCs with circularity. On the other hand, a non-negligible 
number of literature contributions do not set a reference at all to SCs or circularity. How¬≠
ever, all of them follow a qualitative approach.
Moving now to the cost-related dimension, you can see that some papers‚ÄĒall qualita¬≠
tive‚ÄĒdo not consider the notion of cost relevant. Only two authors (Bennett et al., 2019; 
and Sefene et al., 2022) consider the sole reparation cost, while the majority are sensitive to 
the point of costs in various ways. Finally, three contributions‚ÄĒincluding ours‚ÄĒkeep the 
reparation cost separate from the others.
The final portion of the taxonomy reported here addresses the area of technology through 
two separate dimensions. The former one addresses the role of cloud manufacturing (CMfg). 
Our study is the only quantitative one considering CMfg, while all the others are qualitative 
and relatively recent. The latter technology-related dimension provides insights into the 
specific manufacturing technology considered: AM, hybrid ASM, or ‚Äėothers‚Äô. Our study 
is centered on hybrid ASM, even though a relevant portion of the literature addresses AM, 
often combined with the circular economy. Debnath et al., 2022 consider both AM and 
hybrid ASM, and the seminal paper by Chen et al. (2014) and Svetlizky et al. (2021) con¬≠
sider AM, hybrid ASM, and other manufacturing technologies. However, all of them follow 
a qualitative approach.
In conclusion, our paper differentiates from the rest of the literature contributions since 
it follows a quantitative approach, considers the return flow along SCs to implement the 
circular economy pattern, is sensitive to costs by also keeping separate the reparation cost, 
and, from the technology-related viewpoint, it leverages CMfg by focusing on hybrid ASM.

Scenario 2 (collaboration with tier-two facilities): In this scenario, the base scenario is 
extended to collaboration with small local facilities with partial remanufacturing capabili¬≠

strategy for achieving maximum market penetration by unbundling the repair process and 
engaging a wider, more specialized set of partners. While most existing local facilities are 
equipped with some subtractive machines, they are rarely equipped with metal additive 
manufacturing processes. The additive manufacturing technology for the production of end-
use products (and not prototypes) is not fully mature, which, as a result, has limited its 
adoption specifically by small facilities as they are less capable of tolerating market uncer¬≠
tainties. In addition, most metal AM processes and machines are considered expensive both 
in terms of capital and maintenance. This approach is grounded in the economic reality of 
the broader industrial market: while thousands of local machine shops possess affordable 
subtractive (CNC machining) capabilities, very few have invested in expensive, specialized 
metal AM technology. Scenario 2 is designed to leverage this vast, fragmented network 
of common machine shops. The RaaS platform‚Äôs key role is to coordinate a multi-facility 
workflow, sending a component first to a local SM partner for preparation and then to a 
separate, specialized AM partner for the deposition repair. It is assumed that adopting such 
a scenario for the remanufacturing task would further lower the repair quality due to both 
the risk of stacked tolerances (caused by relocation among machines) as well as the differ¬≠
ent skill levels of operators and machine characteristics at various facilities. The connection 
to Scenario 1 is that it represents a further step down the investment ladder in exchange for 
a massive increase in potential network size. The trade-off is more pronounced: the RaaS 
platform can offer services in nearly any industrial area, but it must manage increased logis¬≠
tical complexity and accept the lowest potential repair accuracy. The quality is impacted not 
only by the multi-machine route but also by the added risk of moving a component between 
two entirely different facilities, with different operators, equipment, and quality standards.

3.3‚Äā Remanufacturing supply chain and its cost components
This paper considers a distribution network of a three-stage SC made of suppliers, remanu¬≠
facturers, and customers. The RaaS offers a collaborative environment for the customers, 
suppliers, and remanufacturers to share crucial information like the component‚Äôs material 
composition (Wang & Wang, 2019). Customers are assumed to have a variety of industrial 
facilities with a demand for the repair of their tools and components. The damaged parts are 
shipped to the remanufacturer(s), refurbished, and sent back to the customer, while metal 
powder is sourced from suppliers in batches. The remanufacturing supply chain cost com¬≠
ponents are evaluated, and an optimization problem is formulated to minimize the overall 
supply chain cost by identifying the optimal supply chain configuration. The capacity of 
the remanufacturing facility is a variable to be optimized, as the equipment needed for 
the repair process described represents an investment cost. As the damaged parts are high-
value, non-perishable goods with low volume and high turnover, the inventory holding costs 
would be negligible and can be assumed to be ignored in the SC cost structure. The costing 
structure for RaaS consists of the remanufacturing cost (previously formulated in Sect.¬†3.2), 
hardware cost, software cost, cloud core services cost, and transportation cost (Cheng et al., 
2011). For running the cloud platform service, hardware such as computers, servers, storage 
devices, as well as software such as a portal website, a user interface are necessary. Both 
the hardware and software would be a one-time investment and would require maintenance 
until the end of their lifetime. So, these costs will be perceived as investment costs in estab¬≠
lishing the facility. The remanufacturing cost, cloud core services cost, and transportation 
cost are the variable costs in the logistics modeling. The transportation cost exists between 
the supplier and remanufacturer(s) as well as between the customer and remanufacturer(s).

4‚Äā Case study parameters and assumptions
A case study for remanufacturing services in the tooling and machinery sector within the 
United States is considered. Most of data and model parameters to perform the study are 
real world data from publicly available sites and literature. One of the main assumptions 
in the case study is that process and parameter validation are assumed to be already done 
and every component is considered restorable. The equipment that is presented as suit¬≠

PLM/CAD/CAM software‚ÄČ+‚ÄČmetrology software, and safety systems). The cloud operation 
and maintenance cost for a facility is assumed to be $200,000. This $200,000 annual cost is 
a comprehensive estimate for the ongoing operational expenses related to the facility‚Äôs digi¬≠
tal and physical infrastructure, considering license renewals and cloud‚ÄČ+‚ÄČplatform operation

5.4‚Äā Managerial implications and real-world applicability
The findings of this study provide actionable insights for decision-makers in the manufactur¬≠
ing and remanufacturing industries. From a managerial perspective, the trade-offs between 
centralization and decentralization of remanufacturing facilities represent a crucial strategic 
decision. While a centralized configuration appears cost-optimal in the short term, decen¬≠
tralization offers higher service levels, improved responsiveness, and potentially greater 
customer satisfaction in the long run. Managers must therefore balance cost minimization 
against service-level considerations, recognizing that customer proximity can provide a 
competitive advantage in markets where lead time and service reliability are critical. In our 
case study, tiered local collaborations are within 5% of the cost optimum while improving 
regional responsiveness; under a 10-year horizon, the near-optimal set shifts toward 5‚Äď8 
hubs.
The findings also highlight the importance of transportation costs and facility accuracy 
as levers for cost control. Scenario 1 demonstrates how collaboration with tier-one facili¬≠
ties can reduce capital investment requirements but introduces quality and transportation 
challenges that must be actively managed. For managers, this indicates the need for careful 
partner selection and performance monitoring to ensure consistent repair outcomes. Simi¬≠
larly, Scenario 2 underscores the risk of increased logistics complexity when relying on 
facilities with only partial capabilities, emphasizing the importance of integrated supply 
chain coordination.
In terms of real-world applicability, the results validate that cloud-based remanufacturing 
models can support a viable distributed repair service network for high-value components. 
1‚ÄĮ3
972

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 357:945‚Äď977
By leveraging hybrid ASM technologies, firms can expand their remanufacturing capabili¬≠
ties while using the cloud platform to maintain visibility and ensure efficient routing of 
repair tasks. The model provides clear trigger points that show when market conditions 
justify building a new hub versus contracting with a local partner. This implies that in prac¬≠
tice, decision-makers can deploy such models to strategically locate facilities, minimize 
costs, and simultaneously expand service coverage, thereby aligning operational choices 
with broader sustainability and circular economy objectives.
6‚Äā Conclusion
This study presents an outlook of the state-of-the-art research regarding the adoption of 
hybrid ASM technologies for Remanufacturing-as-a-Service and the implications on the SC 
network of this service industry. Three different remanufacturing scenarios are presented 
and their implications on the supply chain dynamics and configuration are quantitatively 
analyzed by formulating both the remanufacturing process and cloud-enabled supply chain 
costs. The performed sensitivity analyses offer a quantitative assessment of the most impact¬≠
ful cost drivers that need to be carefully considered when building a business around AM 
reparation and those that are neglectable. Through the SC modeling and analysis formulated 
for the tooling and machinery sector in the U.S. continental territory, the cost structure of the 
whole SC has been determined as well. The configuration analysis disproved the common 
belief in the literature that AM technologies for remanufacturing are infeasible for large-
scale distributed networks due to their capital-intensive nature. However, it was found that 
the lower the dimensions of repair components, the higher the SC configuration is pushed 
towards centralization. The cost for cloud core services was found to have a minor impact 
on overall SC cost and implementing it enables a better communication among the SC 
entities.
The findings of this study offer actionable managerial insights. Managers considering 
investments in hybrid ASM-enabled remanufacturing must view the decision not only 
through the lens of cost minimization but also in terms of long-term service-level improve¬≠
ments and strategic positioning. A centralized approach may optimize cost structures in the 
short run, but a more decentralized configuration enhances flexibility, customer respon¬≠
siveness, and competitiveness over time. For decision-makers, this indicates that strategic 
facility location and collaboration with local partners should be approached as a long-term 
business development choice rather than a purely operational cost decision. The results also 
highlight the strategic value of cloud-based RaaS platforms. These platforms enable SMEs 
to participate in remanufacturing ecosystems without bearing the full burden of capital-
intensive investments. For managers, this opens new opportunities to adopt innovative busi¬≠
ness models and partnerships, leveraging shared resources while maintaining control over 
quality and logistics. Furthermore, the results underline the importance of carefully manag¬≠
ing trade-offs in collaboration: while local facilities lower barriers to entry, they introduce 
variability in quality and logistics complexity that must be actively monitored.
From a real-world applicability standpoint, the presented model serves as a practical 
blueprint for industries dealing with high-value, repairable components such as aerospace, 
heavy machinery, and automotive dies. It provides concrete guidance on how to structure 
investments, balance costs, and design supply chain configurations that align with both 
1‚ÄĮ3
973

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 357:945‚Äď977
profitability goals and sustainability imperatives. By quantifying the impacts of various 
scenarios, the study equips managers and policymakers with evidence-based insights to 
guide the adoption of hybrid ASM processes in remanufacturing supply chains. Ultimately, 
the findings underscore that the shift toward cloud-enabled, service-based remanufacturing 
is both feasible and strategically advantageous, offering firms a pathway to more resilient, 
sustainable, and customer-centric operations.
It is important to note that this work only includes the costs of processes without includ¬≠
ing the measures of potential economic profitability. In the future, the SC model will con¬≠
sider work to prove the hypothesis that the revenue opportunities that come from a more 
widespread and faster reach of the remanufacturing services would further incentivize a 
decentralized SC configuration. Moreover, the future work will incorporate a coverage con¬≠
straint and report the cost‚Äďcoverage frontier. Finally, the SC configuration and economic 
feasibility based on factory-in-a-box mobile remanufacturing facilities can be explored.
