Abstract
Advantages of electric buses (E-bus) in emission and air pollution reduction motivate local
governments to replace conventionally fuelled public transit buses; however, the main draw-
backs of the E-buses are range and charging duration. Estimation of daily energy consumption
(EC) of E-buses is critical, which depends on various factors. This study proposes a multi-
factor EC methodology considering public transit bus routes and operational characteristics
(i.e., grade, journey speed, passenger load, etc.). After the analyses of complex bi-factor
interactions among the selected parameters, the results lead to the development of a multi-
factor E-bus EC estimation matrix, which is later used as a guide for EC estimation of PT
E-bus routes. To better represent the EC of auxiliary system, the ambient temperature is also
included as a factor. The methodology is tested along the two E-bus routes in Ankara, Türkiye.
A total of 7 round-trip ﬁeld data collection also included the observed state-of-charge (SOC)
values at every bus stop, which was later used as a control value for the estimated E-bus
route EC values; the slight difference between the estimated and observed SOC is assumed
to stem from integer values of displayed SOC values. Analysis of auxiliary energy separately
provides a more accurate evaluation of E-bus EC within a day, where night-time and daytime
temperatures may vary signiﬁcantly, as well as for different seasons (winter versus summer).
The effect of passenger number, which can change dramatically during the peak and off-peak
periods, can also be captured, as well.

1.1 Scope of the study
The main focus of this work lies in the development of a multi-factor total EC estimation
methodology for E-bus routes that can be realistically calculated considering route and oper-
ational characteristics. Unlike other studies that focus solely on singular factors such as speed
or passenger load (Fiori et al., 2019; Perugu et al., 2023; Yang & Liu, 2022), our approach
integrates speed, grade, passenger load, and inter-stop distance. Complex interactions among
these factors prevent the use of simple factor-based coefﬁcients and simple interpolations,
as can be seen in bi-factor analyses. Further analysis is used to develop a multi-factor EC
estimation matrix, which is later used as a guide for the more realistic estimation of E-bus
123

Annals of Operations Research
route EC for PT services. This matrix provides the value of the mechanical EC per km for
different scenarios based on different levels of i) journey speed, ii) grade, iii) passenger load,
and iv) stop distance. Furthermore, the auxiliary energy (Eaux) requirements based on ambi-
ent temperature variations are incorporated in the estimation of the total EC, which are rarely
addressed in existing studies. The Eaux component is calculated based on the ambient temper-
ature of the E-bus service time, which may change during daytime versus night-time services,
as well as summer versus winter conditions. Total EC estimation is used to determine the
state-of-change (SOC) of the E-bus battery.
The proposed methodology is tested along the two E-bus routes in Ankara, Türkiye. A
total of 7 round-trip ﬁeld data collection, which included observed SOC values at every bus
stop, provided the evaluation of the estimated E-bus Route EC and methodology. Analysis
of Eaux separately provides a more accurate evaluation of E-bus use within a day, where
night-time and daytime temperatures may vary signiﬁcantly, as well as for different seasons
(winter versus summer). The effect of passenger load, which can change dramatically during
the peak and off-peak periods, can also be captured, as well.
The proposed E-bus route EC estimation method can be used to support the planning of
deployment of E-buses in PT use by municipalities and planning agencies. It does not require
extensive bus GPS track data, yet it can provide realistic results in terms of EC (or SOC)
by considering multiple factors simultaneously, as well as their non-linear complex interac-
tions; furthermore, the regenerative braking phenomenon is included to improve the realistic
estimation capability of the method. The simplicity of the method is provided by the created
EC estimation matrix, which can be used with basic PT route and travel characteristics data.
The visual support of the matrix for the EC levels estimation makes it easily understood and
used by non-engineering people (such as planners, PT analysts, decision-makers, etc.), who
do not necessarily solve mechanical energy equations with advanced calculus requirements.
2 Factors aﬀecting E-bus energy consumption (EC)
The battery technologies and the bus operation planning studies focus on maximizing the
performance of the buses and optimizing the bus network system. The range of the E-buses
is mainly dependent on battery size. However, although higher battery capacity provides
more power, it also creates a signiﬁcant extra load on the bus, which also increases energy
consumption. Therefore, in terms of battery technology, the increase in range depends on the
improvement of the power systems. On the other hand, in terms of bus operation planning, the
adequacy of charging stations and the electricity grid system, as well as the number of buses
operating and the ﬂeet scheduling, depend on the performance of the e-buses. According to
Gallet et al. (2018a) when the bus ﬂeet is fully electriﬁed, the heterogeneity of the driving
proﬁles creates a burden on the electricity grid, causing high variance in energy requirements
between different lines and at different times of day. Therefore, an energy consumption
estimation is crucial for planning and deployment purposes.

2.3 Regenerative braking
Regenerative braking is the process in which the vehicle regains some of the lost energy from
deceleration back into the system. The regenerative braking occurs when the tractive force
(FLDM) is negative. This occurs when the vehicle is braking. Under these circumstances, the
electric vehicles are able to convert this energy and store it back in the battery. The efﬁciency
of the regenerative braking is important when calculating the EC of the vehicles. Gallet et al.
(2018a), deﬁned a regeneration factor that determines how much of the kinetic energy can be
regenerated, and this factor is applicable only when the tractive force is negative. Hjelkrem
et al. (2021) proposed that when calculating the EC, if PLDM has a negative value and the
speed is below 15 km/h, the mechanical energy (ELDM) consumption is equal to zero. The
authors suggest that for the regenerative braking to occur, a minimum speed should be gained,
and that value of speed is 1 km/h. The regenerative braking is considered, but the efﬁciency
of the energy gain is not stated in their study.
2.4 Auxiliary energy requirement

governed by heating and ventilation. Hjelkrem et al. (Hjelkrem et al., 2021) estimated the
EC based on LDM. The formulation of the auxiliary load on the battery was calculated
by summing the heating, ventilation, and other loads. The energy required for heating was
estimated considering the number of doors, ﬂow rates of air exchange, and the ambient
temperature. Comparing the estimated values with the actual EC values for 3266 trips, they

2.6 Energy consumption (EC) models
There are two common analyses used in the literature to study the association between
the parameters and e-bus energy consumption: sensitivity analysis and regression analysis.
Sensitivity analysis describes the importance of each input parameter in determining the
variability of the model response, whereas the regression models determine the signiﬁcance
or the lack of the independent input parameters on the energy consumption (Abdelaty &
Mohamed, 2021). Various models are formed based on different parameters, and these models
are mainly derived based on real-life data. Liu et al. (2020) proposed a multi-objective index
system to evaluate the operating performance of the electric buses. The factors are classiﬁed
into three categories: i) operational, ii) topological, and iii) external.
It can be seen that the most frequently used factors are speed and acceleration, followed by
number of stops, grade, and passenger load. Teoh et al. (2018) used a linear regression model
123

Annals of Operations Research
to estimate the impact of route length and passenger loading on e-bus EC. The route length
and passenger loading are found as the signiﬁcant operational parameters; however, it is also
argued that the operational parameters are greatly affected by various external parameters,
including the headway and the charging type. Vepsäläinen (2019) conducted a sensitivity
analysis based on the results of multiple linear regression analysis to interpret the variation in
the EC rates due to the operation and environmental parameters. A linear regression analysis
was used to determine the correlated and uncorrelated operation parameters and the param-
eters affecting the EC, also concluding the signiﬁcance of the driver’s aggressiveness and
the stops per km. Galvin (2017) developed a multivariate linear regression model to study
the impact of driving behaviour parameters, such as acceleration/deceleration rates and aver-
age speed on EV EC, while ordinary least squares regression and multilevel mixed-effects
regression models were used to assess the impact of the external parameters, such as tem-
perature and HVAC. Furthermore, several multiple linear regression models were developed
to predict the EC based on vehicular parameters (i.e. rolling resistance and drag coefﬁcient),
topological parameters (i.e. road grade and route length), operational parameters (i.e. average
speed and acceleration/deceleration rates), and external parameters including temperature,
auxiliary power, and HVAC (Liu et al., 2017; De Cauwer et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2018; De
Cauwer et al., 2015; Kunith et al., 2017; Neaimeh et al., 2013). Ma et al. (2021) estimated
the effect of route and driving proﬁles for E-buses and conventional diesel buses. The factors
investigated included average journey speed, route grade, number of stops, average stop dis-
tance, average stop-level dwell time, route directness, and time of day. It is concluded that
the parameters have non-linear effects on the EC and that the route characteristics (number
of stops and stop spacing) have a larger impact on e-bus EC.
3 Methodology
3.1 Estimation of E-bus energy consumption (EC) for public transit bus routes
The framework to construct an E-bus route EC proﬁle based on major route and operational
factors affecting E-bus performance is shown in Fig. 3. The studied factors are listed as
follows;
• Speed and Acceleration: The average speed calculated in between stops (km/h) and
assumed constant acceleration (m/s2)
• Road Grade: The average road grade calculated between stop points (%)
• Passenger Load: The number of passengers in the E-bus
• Stop distance: distance between stop points (m)
In the ﬁrst step, for each route segment between stops, speed proﬁle, acceleration, total
mass(basedonpassengernumberandbusmass),androadgradientaredeterminedtocalculate
the mechanical EC (ELDM). To do so, a speed proﬁle is constructed assuming a constant inter-
stop speed value and constant acceleration/deceleration value before and after stop dwellings.
A bi-factor analysis of the selected factors is conducted to analyse the effect of each parameter
in combination with another. This provided the relationship between factors and how they
affect the ELDM characteristics. By combining the effect of the selected factors and the
ambient temperature, the change in mechanical EC is analysed in combination, enabling the
creation of a mechanical EC estimation matrix which provides a unit EC/km value in kWh
that accounts for the energy required for motion. The effect of ambient temperature is added
separately as kWh/km as suggested by Basma et al. (2020). Finally, to estimate EC along a

route for an E-bus operated for PT services, a piecewise linear cumulative energy usage is
accepted for a given route, which can be simply calculated as the sum of estimated EC in
segments between stop points.
It should be noted here that the proposed methodology excludes factors related to vehicle
design characteristics (such as frontal area, battery efﬁciency, and drag coefﬁcient) and road
conditions (such as coefﬁcient of rolling resistance), which are more important for ﬂeet
management and road asset management decisions.
3.2 Factors for E-bus EC estimation
The assumed values for the matrix generation are summarized in Table 3. As urban PT
services are not expected to operate at speeds higher than 50 km/hr, the average speed values
are assumed to change between 10 km/hr (congested trafﬁc conditions) and 50 km/hr. A
constant acceleration/deceleration value of 1.8 m/s2 is assumed as the LDM formulation is
not sensitive to it. All the parameters are inputted to the analysis as provided in the table, but
these values can be altered for speciﬁc purposes. For road grades, a range of [−5%, 5%]
is assumed, which covers level and rolling terrain conditions. The E-bus-related parameters,
such as bus weight and frontal area, are inputted by assuming a 12 m standard E-bus.

3.5 E-bus route total EC estimation using field data
To evaluate the performance of the proposed methodology, the total EC of E-bus routes is
estimated by using ﬁeld observations. For this purpose, E-bus routes are divided into road
segments based on stop locations. The data required for the analysis include the distance
between stops, time difference between stop locations, passenger load throughout the jour-
ney, and the altitude of each stop. For each route, the analysis is based on the stop locations,
assuming that stopping only occurs at stop points (Fig. 6). The average speed between stops

4.3 E-bus route EC estimation
The parameters used in E-bus EC calculation and the resulting cumulative EC proﬁle are
shown in Fig. 9 for BR-111-1. The mechanical EC is obtained from the E-bus EC estimation
matrix using the grade, speed, passenger load, and stop distance values. The values are cal-
culated for each inter-stop distance. The speed value is calculated based on time and distance
difference between stop points, making use of the gathered data. The speed and accelera-
tion proﬁling is conducted assuming 1.8 m/s2 acceleration and deceleration as explained in
Sect. 3.5. The parameters are inputted to the E-bus EC estimation matrix, and the EC estima-
tion per km is obtained. Finally, the EC per km value is proportioned to the distance between
stops. The auxiliary energy requirement is calculated based on the ambient temperature, and
the total EC is determined as the sum of ELDM and Eaux.
The obtained EC results for each inter-stop distance are shown in Fig. 9d, and the cumu-
lative EC proﬁle for BR-111-1 is shown in Fig. 9e. High speeds are observed for sections P,
S, T, and U, and the altitude increases from section P to R and decreases from point R to U.
The changes in the EC can be discussed based on the changes in these parameters. At point
P, the speed is high, and the grade is high; therefore, an EC that reaches above 2.5 kWh is
estimated. With the decreasing grade between point R and U, even though high speeds are
observed, the EC is estimated to be below 1.5 kWh and mostly around 0.5 kWh. Finally, at
point U, where the grade is almost zero, the high speed caused an increase in EC reaching
up to almost 2.5 kWh.

5 Evaluation of the model performance
The observed percent remaining battery is recorded to account for the observed energy
consumption. Figure 9e shows the SOC data gathered through ﬁeld observations for BR-111-
1. In order to compare the estimated energy consumption and the observed results accurately,
the cumulative EC is used in comparisons. The observed SOC values are normalized to start
with zero energy consumption using the following formula. Where BC is the battery capacity,
%SOCstart is the remaining percent battery at the start of the journey, and the %SOC is the

6 Conclusion and further recommendations
Planning the use of E-buses in public transportation systems can be challenging. The results
show that even in the same day, with the same driver and bus, the EC can show signiﬁcant
differences. These ﬂuctuations in the EC of an E-bus creates challenges in E-bus operation
planning since the charging infrastructure is still not adequate and the battery capacity of
the E-buses still need improvement. Therefore, until these infrastructural and technological
advancements are completed, the E-bus deployment should be planned carefully, which is
addressed by the proposed methodology in this study.
Factors affecting the performance of E-buses are stated in the literature, but these factors
also include parameters that cannot be changed at the planning stage, such as parameters of
the mechanical parameters of the bus or road infrastructure parameters. This study focuses
on factors that can be taken into consideration during the planning phase and improved
when necessary. These are determined as speed, road grade, stop distance and number of
passengers. The effect of these factors were calculated for each variable and an electric bus
energy consumption matrix was created. The impact of the selected factors is shown in this
matrix. During these calculations, weight and front area values were used for the selected
bus. When calculations need to be made for another bus, these values must be changed.
The ambient temperature highly affects the EC especially for hot summer days and cold
winter days. This study focused on the summer results because the literature is limited for
hot days. The effect of road gradient is inspected by considering two different routes with
opposite route gradient properties. The results show that the EC estimation for an E-bus can
be estimated by summing the values obtained from the E-bus EC estimation matrix and the
auxiliary energy requirement. This tool can be of signiﬁcant use in operation planning since
the estimation provides acceptable results with only limited available data. The novelty of
this study lies in the development of a ﬂexible EC estimation framework that.
• Captures the effect of multiple operational and environmental factors.
• Provides practical insights into route and energy optimization for E-bus deployment in
urban transit systems.
• Demonstrates a novel application of the bi-factor analysis to isolate and analyse the impact
of factors like road gradient and passenger load.
Additionally, while many existing studies utilize longitudinal dynamic models, our work
goes further by integrating these results into an actionable EC matrix. This matrix can be
used for operational planning without requiring extensive computational resources.
The results obtained from this study provide useful information on the operation and route
planning of E-buses include
• E-buses should be operated with minimum dwell time so that the auxiliary energy require-
ment is minimized.
• The distance between stops should be kept maximum and if possible the stops should
be arranged such that the distance between stops is deﬁnitely greater than 250 m and
preferably 500 m.
• High trafﬁc has a signiﬁcant effect on the EC for E-buses. Therefore, introduction of actions
such as bus lanes to the network will be highly affective to minimize the EC.
• By monitoring the passenger load, the operating bus size can be optimized such that empty
buses are eliminated.
• The energy contribution of auxiliary loads can surpass 50% of total EC on hot days.
• The EC estimation matrix we developed provides accurate predictions (average RMSE:
1.2%) when tested against ﬁeld data.

• And ﬁnally, although altering the grade factor in cities by changing the bus route is not
very likely, when new routes are created the grade factor can be considered. For the initial
deployment stage, the routes with high route grades can be kept in background and other
routes can be prioritized for E-bus deployment.
The limitations and further improvement directions of the study can be listed as;
• The E-bus EC estimation matrix is prepared for a single type of standard 12 m E-bus, but
can be easily modiﬁed to be used with other buses
• Currently, there are limited studies that test the effect hot summer days (almost 40 °C) for
the e-bus operation.
• The winter conditions in terms of ambient temperatures is not tested
• The high trafﬁc condition is not observed during the tests; therefore, the effect of high
trafﬁc should also be tested.
• By evaluating the energy consumption of the bus with only one driver, the variability is
minimized, ensuring that any observed differences in EC is due to external factors rather
than driver behaviour. However, this setup limits real-world applicability by not account-
ing for variations in driving styles and driver-induced energy consumption differences,
reducing the generalizability of the results.
