Abstract
Since the beginning of the financial crisis in 2007-2008, several mitigation policies have 
been considered to monitor and stabilize the financial system in the event of a shock. We 
examine the financial network of systemically important banks as a cooperative game. 
Governments can act as facilitators, enforcing incentives for banks to cooperate in volunВӯ
tary bail-ins and prevent the escalation of the financial crisis. To incentivize the formation 
of a cooperative consortium, we consider (i) a threat tax on equity in case a cooperative 
rescue fails and (ii) assisted bail-ins, in which the regulator promises some financial supВӯ
port. To evaluate the characteristic function of the cooperative game, we employ a clearВӯ
ing payment model for alternative coalitions competing in the market. As a fair division 
concept, we use the proportional nucleolus which implies a possible subsidizing pattern 
among the banks. Group rationality requires the tax to exceed a minimum tax level that we 
introduce. While the tax is assumed fixed before the crisis starts, the regulator may find a 
minimal public subsidy to meet such group rationality requirement. For a demonstration, 
we used major European banks and scenarios that are linked to the adverse economic 
scenario used in the EU-wide stress testing of 2016.

1вҖӮ Introduction
Financial institutions share risks with each other, for instance, they utilize intra-financial 
system lending money and exercise bi- or multi-lateral derivative contracts. They hold simiВӯ
lar portfolios interconnecting them indirectly. These interconnections can provide a link 
through which the failure of a single or multiple institutions can spill over the entire system

and may lead to a catastrophic disaster. This is called systemic risk in a financial system. 
Such a catastrophic consequences can be avoided or alleviated through some mitigation 
policies. Recent examples include the regulatory responses established by the Financial StaВӯ
bility Board (FSB), like adoption of Basel III capital requirements for globally systemically 
important financial institutions, and agreement reached on one of two envisioned liquidity 
standards - the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR); seeВ Claessens and Kodres (2014).
The recent financial crisis prompted extensive studies in many fields, including OperaВӯ
tions Research/Management Science (OR/MS), to investigate the contributing factors of the 
crises, study contagion in financial networks, and develop mitigation policies to increase the 
resilience of financial networks. One strand of literature has been established in the developВӯ
ment of various risk models and measurements. For example, in a seminal paper, Eisenberg 
and Noe (2001) present the clearing payment system framework considering bankruptcy 
law, and study the cascading effects of failures originating from a single institution. They 
also show that the clearing payment vector exists and is unique. Large number of literature 
focused on the impact of asset distribution and market fluctuation on the stability of the 
system such as Elsinger et al. (2005, 2006), Elsinger (2009), Liu and Staum (2010), Chen 
et al. (2013), Elsinger et al. (2013), Chen et al. (2016), and Khabazian and Peng (2018). 
For surveys, see Glasserman and Young (2016) and Jackson and Pernoud (2021). Several 
works have been devoted to discovering the impact of network topology on risk exposure; 
Allen and Gale (2000) point out that the extent of contagion differs significantly between 
network structure patterns. Gai and Kapadia (2010) underline that financial systems tend to 
be robust-yet-fragile, hence, financial crises are low probability-high-consequence events. 
Acemoglu et al. (2013) show how tightly linked networks increase financial stability but 
can accelerate the propagation of shocks that contribute to a more fragile financial system. 
The relation of network topology and cascading failures in financial systems was studied 
by Elliott et al. (2014). Cinelli et al. (2021) study the impact of incomplete information on 
robustness assessments of financial networks. They find missing network information may 
lead to biased actions due to underestimation of potential cascading defaults.
Another strand of literature has focused on developing and analyzing the efficacy of difВӯ
ferent mitigation policies. For example, Pokutta et al. (2011) extend the Eisenberg-NoeвҖҷs 
model as a network flow problem and analyze the impact of the bailout strategy in this 
paper. Capponi and Chen (2015) and Capponi et al. (2016) investigate the influence of 
regulatory and preventive policies to limit the loss exposure towards financial institutions 
or increase the financial systemsвҖҷ resiliency against financial losses.В Bernard et al. (2022) 
use three stage sequential game to study the performance of intervention policies including 
bail-outs and assisted bail-ins.
Different from the above-mentioned non-cooperative frameworks, to the best of our 
knowledge, we are the first to propose a formal cooperative game-theoretic model to study 
financial crisis resolution. Our intention is to employ incentives for banks to collaborate in 
mitigating crises in financial networks. This is important because banks are best informed 
to resolve their crisis. Incentives can be created such that it is in the interest of the banks to 
cooperate in private bail-ins and to prevent the escalation of a financial crisis which would 
hurt everyone.
For such cooperation to take place, governments play a strategic role. First, the set of 
systemically important banks (SIBs) considered in the rescue consortium is defined by the 
regulator, a governmental or inter-governmental agency. Second, if the threat of escalatВӯ

ing crisis is not sufficient to incentivize the formation of cooperation, then the regulator 
can enforce additional incentives by providing credible threats and supportive promises: 
(i) a threat tax on equity may be installed if the cooperative rescue fails, and (ii) some 
public subsidies may be offered for assisted private bail-ins. Credibility of a threat tax is 
achieved, by legislation and law enforcement. Although the threat tax is assumed to be fixed 
already before the crisis starts, the regulator may find the minimal subsidy to meet the group 
rationality requirement. To study the relationship between the subsidy and a threat tax rate 
necessary to meet the group rationality requirement, we propose a minimum tax which is 
decreasing with the level of subsidy. This can be useful at the time before a crisis when 
alternative tax rates are considered.
We should point out that some literature on banksвҖҷ cooperative mitigation of systemic 
risk exists. For example,В Elliott et al. (2014) (in the on-line appendix A.3.2) consider a 
numerical example with two banks in the system, and show that the collaboration between 
the banks might reduce the systemic risk in a network of banks. Rogers and Veraart (2013), 
analyze voluntary rescues of distressed banks by other banks of an exogenous network. In 
their paper, they consider rescue incentives and rescue abilities without employing cooperaВӯ
tive game theory nor considering the imposition of a tax incentive.
A motivation for the tax incentive arises from political trends, for instance, in the US and 
EU, which tend to prevent the use of taxpayersвҖҷ funds to cover financial losses of banks. 
We assume such threat tax is installed in legislation in advance; not after the crisis already 
has started. If the tax rate is low, then it may happen that the threat is insufficient to satisfy 
group rationality needed for a cooperative rescue; i.e., there can be an incentive for a coaliВӯ
tion of banks to withdraw from the consortium. In such an event, given the pre-specified tax 
rate is fixed, the level of public subsidy needs to be increased to meet the group rationality 
requirement. Given an initial motivation for cooperation exists, banks consider collectively 
subsidizing distressed banks. We use fair division principles to determine the amount of 
subsidy each bank contributes or receives. Payments are chosen so that no coalition can gain 
by separating from the consortium.
For mathematical modeling of solvency analysis, we rely on the seminal paper by EisenВӯ
berg and Noe (2001) appended by default costs similarly as proposed by Rogers and Veraart 
(2013), and considering various seniorities of debt. For evaluating the characteristic funcВӯ
tion of the cooperative game, we adopt the clearing payment model of Kallio and Khabazian 
(2025) for alternative coalitions competing in the market.
Our companion paper Kallio and Khabazian (2025) concerns stabilizing financial netВӯ
works through mergers and acquisitions, where we propose a principalвҖ“agent model to 
explore efficient policies to resolve financial crises.
This model minimizes the welfare loss function of a central authority (social planner, 
SP) through simultaneous choices of subsidy levels and potential pairs of banks to merge. 
The SPвҖҷs choice of mergers needs to be incentive-compatible with the autonomous choices 
of banks.
Incentive compatibility is enforced by two options for conditions, based on stable matchВӯ
ing or competitive bidding. The bi-level principal-agent model specifies conditions both for 
matching banks for mergers and for the clearing payment equilibrium.1

We use data employed by EBA2 in the 2016 EU-wide stress testing to demonstrate the 
proposed cooperative framework. Under two scenarios linked to the adverse scenario used 
in the EU stress tests, without cooperation some banks default, non-banking sectors face 
large credit losses and surviving banks need to pay the threat tax. However, under cooperaВӯ
tion within the consortium, there are no defaults, no credit losses, banks avoid the threat tax 
and receive the promised public subsidy. Consequently, each bank is better off by cooperatВӯ
ing within the consortium in comparison with the alternative.
In practice, such cooperative resolution is inherently a negotiation (see, e.g., Raiffa 
(1985)). In non-cooperative models, the equilibrium depends intricately on the bargaining 
protocol - the order in which the banks or the regulator get to make proposals and counterВӯ
proposals. Instead, in cooperative game models of negotiation processes the core characterВӯ
izes all reasonable outcomes that do not depend on the specifics of the bargaining protocol 
because they satisfy the minimal requirements of efficiency and group rationality.
As an example for the real banking cases of cooperative approaches, we may refer to the 
$ 3.6 billion recapitalization (bailout) of Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) in the 
US which was exercised by 14 financial institutions, a group of major creditors, under the 
supervision of the Federal Reserve in the late 1990В s. Recognizing the potential for systemic 
risk, this intervention aimed to prevent the collapse of LTCM from destabilizing financial 
markets. The threat of credit losses appeared to be an important incentive for participation. 
Moreover, prior to the bankruptcy of Lehman Brothers in 2008, the bank held talks with 
Bank of America and Barclays for the companyвҖҷs possible sale. The deal was vetoed by 
financial authorities of the UK and subsequently Bank of AmericaвҖҷs involvement ended. 
Thereafter, major US banks continued negotiations to prevent LehmanвҖҷs rapid failure - withВӯ
out success.
The key issue is how to deal with a negotiation problem of the banks in a consortium. 
Naturally, a cooperative game solution cannot dictate the outcome of negotiations; however, 
it can help in finding a negotiated agreement. In other words, our approach can serve as a 
decision support mechanism for such negotiations. Cooperative game theory can also proВӯ
vide an initiative for the negotiation process which aims to an agreement on private bail-ins. 
We also point out that to help avoid moral hazard, a negotiated agreement would set rules 
for acquisitions and/or bail-in procedures within the consortium in the case where a bank 
would default without help. Such rules aim at preventing the bank managers from excessive 
risk taking; realization of major risks could lead to an acquisition or supervision by other 
banks in the consortium.

3вҖӮ Cooperative assisted bail-ins
Next, we study the case where the n banks in N consider cooperation in assisted bail-ins of 
distressed banks. There are three sources creating an incentive for cooperation: (i) the threat 
of credit and deadweight losses if the rescue fails, (ii) a threat tax set by an authorized govВӯ
ernmental body, and (iii) a public subsidy to the rescue consortium. To enhance the incenВӯ
tives for cooperation, a threat tax П„0 on equity may be charged if the cooperative rescue fails, 
and a public subsidy Оё may be offered to the consortium if their cooperative rescue succeeds. 
We assume the threat tax is levied by the legislation before the financial crisis begins, while 
the level Оё of public subsidy is chosen after the extent of the crisis is revealed. In summary, 
the steps of cooperative assisted bail-ins are as follows:

Before a crisis starts. The rules that determine the banks included in the consortium N 
are declared. The regulator selects and commits to a threat tax rate П„0 applied on the 
equity of each bank in N for not participating in a rescue.
 
в—Ҹ
After the crisis begins. The set N of participating banks is declared. The regulator anВӯ
nounces a possible subsidy Оё to incentivize private bail-ins.

4вҖӮ A case study on European banks
In this section we demonstrate the assisted cooperative bail-ins using a network of major 
European banks for which data is available from the EBA; see Sect.В 4.1. This allows us 
to develop real-world-like test cases. In our case study, systemic risks arise from adverse 
developments in the economy. An economic downturn tends to lead in unfavorable capital 
market developments, for instance, in the real estate market, and in increased credit losses 
from firms and households. Furthermore, securitization (such as subprime loans) has an 
impact on asset values and realization of derivatives has a bearing in balance sheets. In our 
1вҖҜ3

Annals of Operations Research
scenario development, this leads to adjustments in the values of banks outside assets and 
thereby a systemic risk arises in the network of banks. Subsequently, in Sect.В 4.2 we develop 
two adverse scenarios linked to EU-wide stress testing by EBA: Case 1 with four fundamenВӯ
tally defaulting banks plus six cascading defaults, and Case 2 with two fundamental defaults 
plus three cascading defaults. As mentioned, we consider three incentives for cooperation: 
(i) cascading defaults, (ii) a pre-specified threat tax П„0 on equity if the rescue fails, and (iii) 
a public subsidy Оё if the cooperative rescue succeeds. For a cooperative solution, Оё needs to 
be large enough for the threat tax rate П„0 to exceed the minimal tax rate П„(Оё) in (19). Given 
the tax rate П„0, a natural way to find a minimal subsidy Оё(П„0) for full rescue is to use the 
model (15).
The analysis in Sect.В 4.3 is based on our adverse scenarios. First, we demonstrate that if 
cooperation fails then many banks default, non-banking sectors face large credit losses and 
surviving banks need to pay the threat tax. Second, under assisted cooperative bail-ins, there 
are no defaults, no credit losses, the banks avoid the threat tax and receive the promised 
public subsidy. Consequently, each bank is better off by cooperating within the consortium 
in comparison with the alternative.

5вҖӮ Considerations for implementation
Based on cooperative game theory, Sect.В 3 lays out a model supporting negotiations to 
resolve financial crises. For practical implementation, we address the following issues of 
(a) which are the parties involved in a rescue operation, (b) how is a credible threat tax rate 
enforced, (c) how is the level of subsidy chosen for assisted bail-ins. For all items, instituВӯ
tional setting matters, and for brevity, here we only consider a hypothetical case.
(a) The regulator is an agent authorized by the government. The authority to choose the 
parties in the rescue operation is given to the regulator. (b) Tax legislation and law enforceВӯ
ment implies the credibility of a threat tax. For example, the tax can be based on a governВӯ
ment proposal that the parliament can decide on. This proposal, including justifications, 
is prepared by the regulator supported by civil servants. We discuss such preparation in 
Sects.В 3.1, 4.3 and 4.4. The example in Sect.В 4.4 using a sample of European data indicates 
that the threat tax rate П„0 is expected to be in the range between 0% and 10%. A large tax 
can be considered politically infeasible to implement. (c) Choosing the level of subsidy, the 
model (15) with some alternatives for scenario assumptions, and suggestions in Sects.В 4.3 
and 4.4 can be used.
An important question can be how a government can collect a threat tax on the equity 
value of a bank when they refuse to cooperate, in particular, if the systemic instability was 
not the bankвҖҷs fault. Here again, we turn to legislation: the authority responsible for crisis 
resolution could also be assigned the responsibility of collecting the threat tax. Although 
existing institutions may not perfectly fit our model, adaptations can be made. An analogy 
can be found in the Single Resolution Board (SRB) of the European Union. The SRB was 
established to resolve financial crises within the Banking Union. It manages the Single 
Resolution Fund (SRF), which is financed by the banking sector. The SRF was created to 
ensure that financial support is available as a last resort - after private solutions have been 
exhausted and after shareholders and creditors have absorbed losses. The SRF was launched 
in 2016 with initial seed funding of вӮ¬7 billion; today, the fund is approximately вӮ¬80 billion, 
all collected from banks.
In the United States, similar financing arrangements are in place, guided by the principle 
that the banking sector should ultimately serve as the source of financing - even though the 
FDIC as the authority or the government may initially provide financial and other support to 
accelerate the restoration of financial stability, see e.g. LГ¶yttyniemi (2025).
6вҖӮ Concluding discussion
Our contribution two-fold. First, as a novel contribution to the literature, we propose assisted 
private bail-in strategies based on cooperative game theory. In the spirit of the core of the 
game, rescuing banks in a network of systemically important banks (SIBs), participate out 
of self-interest, there is no incentive to withdraw. This can be important because in a finanВӯ
cial crisis banks are best informed to resolve their crisis. Second, we demonstrate the coopВӯ
erative approach using real-like cases of a network of large European banks.
The systemically important banks considered in the rescue consortium is defined by the 
regulator, an authorized governmental or inter-governmental agency. Cascading defaults 
may not provide sufficient incentive for cooperation. Therefore, a threat tax and a public 
1вҖҜ3

Annals of Operations Research
subsidy may be used additionally to incentivize the formation of cooperative bail-ins. The 
government may install a threat tax which applies in case the rescue cooperation fails. PreВӯ
ceding a crisis, legislation to determine the threat tax rate is essential for the threat to be 
credible. After the economic crisis has started, if the tax turns out to be insufficient to ensure 
incentives for banks to participate in the rescue consortium, then s sufficient public subsidy 
can resolve the situation.
We study contagious effect of failures in the financial network in the spirit of the EisenВӯ
berg-Noe model which is extended by considering debt seniorities and the liquidation costs 
in the case of default. We first consider the competitive case, where banks do not cooperate 
and clear their liabilities. Given an initial motivation for cooperation exists, fair division 
principles of cooperative game theory are employed to create an incentive for banks to 
participate in the consortium. Such fair division results in side payments received and paid 
by each bank, and thereby it determines how bailout costs are shared among banks. For the 
evaluation of the characteristic function of the cooperative game, we develop clearing payВӯ
ment models for alternative coalitions competing in the market.
For real-world-like demonstrations of the cooperative bailout, we employ data of 22 
large European banks used by EBA in 2016 EU-wide stress testing. We choose adverse ecoВӯ
nomic scenarios which are notably more severe that the one used in the stress tests. Given 
a modest (or zero) threat tax and a subsidy for successful assisted bail-ins, the results show 
that all the banks benefit by joining the consortium where no bank defaults.
While preventing a crisis in the short run, long run incentives are a concern as well. A 
system which always would rescue failing banks may create a risk of moral hazard. Some 
banks might take excessive risks if they get rewarded with bailouts, and this can lead to a 
buildup of systemic risk in the future. However, in practice such cooperative decisions are 
based on negotiations which may fail. Therefore, banks cannot necessarily count on becomВӯ
ing rescued. Additionally, to help avoid moral hazard, banks may agree on rules for acquisiВӯ
tions and/or bail-in procedures in the case of rescue. Under such rules, banks are reluctant 
to excessive risk taking because realization of major risks can lead to acquisition or superviВӯ
sion by competing banks. Of course, the moral hazard problems are also traditional subjects 
of banking regulation; e.g., the moral hazard related to banks regarded as вҖҳtoo big to failвҖҷ.
