Abstract
In radiation therapy (RT), radiation from linear accelerators is used to kill malignant tumor
cells. Scheduling patients for RT is difﬁcult both due to the numerous medical and technical
constraints, and because of the stochastic inﬂow of patients with different urgency levels. In
this paper, we present a Column Generation (CG) approach for the RT scheduling problem.
The model includes constraints such as different machine compatibilities and individual-
ized patient protocols, as well as planned interruptions in treatments due to maintenance
on machines. Data from Iridium Netwerk, the largest cancer center in Belgium, is used to
evaluate the CG approach. The results show that using a dynamic time reservation method
to handle uncertainty in future urgent patients works very well. Furthermore, the schedules
generated by the CG algorithm are clinically validated and compared to historical clinical
schedules for a time period of one year. The CG generated schedules are shown to decrease
the average patient waiting time by 80%, improve the average consistency in appointment
times by 80%, and increase the number of treatments scheduled on the best suited machine
by more than 90% compared to the manually constructed clinical schedules. Thus, the CG
approach to automatically generate schedules for RT can improve the quality of the schedules
signiﬁcantly while saving the clinic many hours of administrative work every week.

a 40% increase compared to the estimated 19.3 million cancer cases in 2020 (Ferlay et al.,
2021). Radiation therapy (RT) is a cancer treatment that uses high doses of radiation to destroy
or damage cancer cells. As a consequence of the rising cancer incidents, the need for RT

will increase (Borras et al., 2016). In external beam RT, a machine called linear accelerator
(linac) is used to direct radiation from outside the body into the tumor. Each treatment is
either curative, with intent to cure the patient, or palliative, with intent to improve quality of
life by providing symptom control. Cancer patients are often divided into different urgency
levels depending mainly on the site of the cancer and treatment intent (Ebert et al., 2013; Lim
et al., 2005; Scoccianti et al., 2012; Thomsen and Nørrevang, 2009). High priority patients
should start treatment as soon as possible after admission, while lower prioritized patients
can wait a few days or weeks.
RT treatments are normally divided into a number of sessions (fractions) that are delivered
daily over several weeks. The duration of a fraction is most often 10 to 30 minutes, depending
mainly on treatment technique and tumor location. By delivering a small fraction of the total
radiation dose, normal cells have time to repair between the treatments, which reduces the
side effects (Devita et al., 2018). However, the overall treatment time should be kept as
short as possible, as longer gaps between fractions can enable the repopulation of cancer
cells to accelerate, leading to potentially lower cure rates (RCR, 2019). Patients usually
receive treatment ﬁve days per week, but weekly and every-other-day schedules are also
used clinically.
In the RT scheduling problem (RTSP), the aim is to schedule patients for RT, given a
set of linacs, for a certain planning horizon. One of the main challenges in the RTSP is that
there is uncertainty in demand. Since the patients are of different priority, it is important that
there are resources available for urgent patients at arrival. In practice, this is often handled
by reserving a percentage of the machine capacity for high priority patients. This can cause
delays in treatments, as well as unnecessary idle time on the machines. Long waiting times
for RT negatively impacts clinical outcomes. For example, it can cause prolonged symptoms,
tumor growth, and psychological distress for the patients (Chen et al., 2008; Fortin et al.,
2002; Gomez et al., 2015; Van Harten et al., 2015; Žumer et al., 2020). Long waiting times
has also been identiﬁed to cause stress for RT staff, which can compromise quality and safety
of the treatments (French, 2004).
To manually schedule patients on the linacs delivering RT is both time-consuming and
demanding. Moreover, efﬁcient resource planning can play a crucial role in minimizing
waiting times. To solve the RTSP using real-life size instances has been shown difﬁcult
(Frimodig et al., 2023; Sauré et al., 2012; Vieira et al., 2016). This paper aims to address
these challenges through the following contributions:
• We present an automatic scheduling algorithm for the RTSP. To the best of our knowledge,
this is the ﬁrst model to simultaneously assign all fractions of the patients to both linacs
and speciﬁc time windows, while also considering planned machine unavailability and
the constraints and objectives related to the resulting gaps in the schedules. It is also the
ﬁrst model to include consecutive treatments, where a primary treatment is followed by
a secondary treatment, and non-conventional treatments, such as treatments that should
be delivered every-other-day. The model also supports multiple hospital locations and
allows the patients to have hospital site preferences.
• Themaintechnicalnovelty liesinthecolumngeneration(CG)approach.Anovelheuristic
is used to generate initial columns, and dynamic programming is used to model and
solve the subproblems. We also present a method for dynamic time reservation to handle
uncertainty in future patient arrivals.
• The CG approach is evaluated from a clinical point of view by Iridium Netwerk, Bel-
gium’s largest RT center.

2 Related work
The literature on scheduling in healthcare has been summarized in several extensive literature
reviews, such as Cayirli and Veral (2003); Gupta and Denton (2008); Ahmadi-Javid et al.
(2017); Marynissen and Demeulemeester (2019). In the review by Vieira et al. (Vieira et
al., 2016), the authors found 12 papers addressing the problem of scheduling RT patients on
linacs. The solution methods have varied; exact methods such as integer programming (IP)
have been used to solve small instances and metaheuristics have been used for larger ones.
Patient scheduling consists of allocation scheduling and appointment scheduling. Allo-
cation scheduling refers to methodologies for allocating patients to resources in advance of
the service date, when future demand is still unknown, without assigning speciﬁc appoint-
ment times. In contrast, in appointment scheduling all patients for a given service day are
assumed to be known, and speciﬁc starting times are assigned. Appointment scheduling for
example aim to minimize machine idle time, maximize preference satisfaction regarding
treatment time, or handle uncertain treatment durations or delays. On the other hand, alloca-
tion scheduling must fulﬁll capacity constraints, and usually deals with patients of different
types and priorities. Most allocation scheduling algorithms are intended to dynamically allo-
cate resources to patients using a rolling time horizon. In this paper, we aim to perform these
two scheduling tasks simultaneously.
2.1 Radiation therapy scheduling
Patient scheduling is done either in an online fashion, where each request is handled imme-
diately, or in batches, where the scheduling is done at certain time intervals. Using batch
scheduling, Conforti et al. (2008) present the ﬁrst IP model for optimizing RT appoint-
ments using treatment slots of equal length (block scheduling), a model that was later
developed to allow for different fraction durations (non-block scheduling) (Conforti et al.,
2010). Jacquemin et al. (2011) introduce the notion of treatment patterns in an IP model to
allow non-consecutive treatment days. These early works established the foundation for RT
scheduling optimization but used simpliﬁed constraints that do not reﬂect the complexity
of real-world clinical settings. Speciﬁcally, they assume identical machines, ignore planned
machine unavailability, and do not consider multi-site operations or specialized treatment
protocols, limiting their practical applicability.

For a more realistic setup, Petrovic et al. (2006, 2013) propose heuristic and metaheuristic
approaches for block scheduling based on prioritized rules, where the latter considers both
scheduling of the pretreatment phase and the treatment phase. While these approaches handle
more realistic constraints, they still rely on simpliﬁed block scheduling assumptions and do
not provide optimality guarantees. The ﬁrst method for dynamic RT scheduling taking future
events into account was presented by Sauré et al. (2012). They model the problem as a
discounted inﬁnite-horizon Markov decision process that ﬁnds an approximately optimal
scheduling policy, which is shown to increase the treatments initiated within 10 days from
73% to 96% compared to a myopic policy (i.e., not taking future patients into account).
Gocgun (2018) extended the same problem setup to also include patient cancellations. These
papers assume there are three identical machines, 8.25 requests per day and scheduling done
in batches. While notable for considering future arrivals, these approaches are limited by
their assumptions of identical machines and simpliﬁed clinical constraints.
In contrast, Legrain et al. (2015) propose a hybrid method combining stochastic and online
optimization to dynamically schedule patients as they arrive. They consider block scheduling
with two linacs and less than 3.5 requests per day. Aringhieri et al. (Aringhieri et al.) also
present methods for online RT scheduling, and develop three online optimization algorithms
for a block-scheduling formulation and one machine. These methods all allocate a start day
to each patient, with no sequencing of patients throughout the day, and are difﬁcult to scale
to realistic instances. A critical limitation is that none of these approaches consider planned
machine unavailability, which occurs on 34% of weekdays in real clinical settings as observed
in our study.
In appointment scheduling, the main task is the sequencing of patients throughout the
day. For this problem, Vieira et al. (2020) create weekly schedules using a mixed-integer
programming (MIP) model combined with a pre-processing heuristic that divides the problem
into subproblems for clusters of machines. Their objective is to maximize the fulﬁllment of
the patients’ time window preferences. In (Vieira et al., 2021), the same authors evaluate
their method in two Dutch clinics, showing that the weekly schedule was improved in both
centers; for example, the time variations between appointment times was reduced by 51%.
The method is evaluated for a time period of one week, which makes it difﬁcult to assess
the dynamics in a treatment schedule, since it does not capture the uncertainty in patient
arrivals or the fact that treatments span over multiple weeks. Importantly, this is one of the
few studies that validates results in real clinical settings, though only for weekly rather than
multi-week scheduling horizons.
Another paper that focuses on patient sequencing is Moradi et al. (Moradi et al.), where the
authors present a data-driven approach that uses the patient information to improve the weekly
schedules. The predictions are utilized in a MIP model to determine the optimal sequence
of patients. The model is simpliﬁed; all patient durations are equal and all machines are
identical and independent, assumptions that do not hold in multi-machine, multi-site clinical
environments. The results show it is favorable to schedule reliable patients early on to reduce
idle time on machines caused by delayed patients or no-shows.
Pham et al. (2022) solves the RTSP using a two-stage approach. In the ﬁrst phase, an
IP model allocates patients to linacs and days, with a certain percentage of linac capacity
saved for urgent patients. The second phase assigns speciﬁc appointment times using either
a MIP or a constraint programming (CP) model. The algorithm is evaluated dynamically on
a rolling time horizon using generated data with seven linacs and a time horizon of 60 days.
The results show that the CP model ﬁnds good solutions sooner, while the MIP model proves
optimality faster. In a later work by the same group (Pham et al., 2023), the authors develop
a prediction-based approach to decide how much capacity to reserve for urgent patients. A

validated with real clinical data.
2.2 Optimization methods
CG is a decomposition technique that if often successfully used for solving huge linear pro-
grams. The method was ﬁrst presented by Ford and Fulkerson (1958), and has the advantage
that it does not consider all variables explicitly, but instead only generates the variables
that have the potential to improve the objective function. The method alternates between a
restricted master problem (RMP) and one or more subproblems used to generate new vari-
ables to the RMP. To get an integer solution, the column generation procedure is embedded
in a branch-and-bound algorithm, known as branch-and-price, which was ﬁrst introduced by
Barnhart et al. (1998). When applying CG in scheduling problems, the decision variable in
the master problem is most often a schedule for one day, or one shift, or one person, and
the master problem is a set partitioning problem used to ﬁnd the optimal overall schedule.
The subproblems are used to ﬁnd the variables and can be solved in various ways. A com-
mon method is dynamic programming (DP) (Bellman, 1954), where the problem is solved
in a recursive manner. Other medical ﬁelds where CG has been applied is for surgeon and
surgery scheduling (Wang et al., 2018), for patient admission (Range et al., 2014), and for
nurse scheduling (Bard and Purnomo, 2005). In cancer treatments, CG has also been used
for brachytherapy scheduling using deteriorating treatment times (Shao et al., 2022), and for
intraday scheduling in chemotherapy (Lyon et al., 2022).
Operations research (OR) methodologies have been widely applied to healthcare problems
(Brailsford and Vissers, 2011; Rais and Viana, 2011; Saville et al., 2019; Vieira et al., 2016),
but there is limited evidence of successful implementation in clinical practice (Brailsford et
al., 2009; Carter and Busby, 2022). This implementation gap is partly due to the simpliﬁed
modeling assumptions used in most studies, which fail to capture the full complexity of
real clinical environments. Our work addresses this gap by developing a model that incor-
porates all essential real-world constraints and validating it against actual clinical practice,
demonstrating both improved solution quality and practical implementability.

2.3 Research gaps and contributions of this work
Despite the substantial body of work on RT scheduling, several critical gaps remain that limit
the practical applicability of existing approaches. This paper addresses these gaps through
the following contributions:
1. Comprehensive Real-World Constraint Modeling: Most existing approaches use
simpliﬁed assumptions (identical machines, no planned unavailability, single-site operations)
that do not reﬂect the complexity of modern cancer centers. Our analysis shows that 34% of
weekdaysinvolveplannedmachineunavailability,yetthiscriticalconstraintislargelyignored
in the literature. Our contribution: We model planned machine unavailability, specialized
treatment protocols (consecutive treatments, non-conventional fractionation), and multi-site
operations with partial machine matching. These constraints are essential in practice, but
largely ignored in existing literature.
2. Clinical Validation with Real Data: With the exception of Vieira et al. (2021), virtu-
ally all RT scheduling studies evaluate their methods using generated data rather than real
clinical schedules. This makes it impossible to assess the true clinical impact and practical
feasibility of these approaches. Our contribution: Unlike previous studies that rely on gen-
erated data, we validate our approach using a full year of real clinical data from Iridium
Netwerk, enabling direct comparison with manually constructed schedules and assessment
of practical implementation feasibility.
3. Integrated Optimization and Dynamic Time Reservation: Most approaches address
either allocation or appointment scheduling separately, missing opportunities for global opti-
mization across both decision levels. Additionally, current methods do not adequately handle
specialized treatment requirements such as consecutive treatments (17% of patients in our
study), non-conventional fractionation schemes (9% of patients), or effective capacity reser-
vation for uncertain urgent arrivals. Our contribution: Our method simultaneously optimizes
allocation and appointment scheduling decisions, capturing interdependencies that separate
approachesmiss.Wealsodevelopmethodstodynamicallyreservecapacityforurgentpatients
with uncertain arrival times, using placeholder patients and adaptive reservation strategies
that balance current optimization with future ﬂexibility.
4. Multi-Site Operations: Few approaches consider the complexities of multi-hospital
networks with partial machine matching, despite this being common in modern cancer care
delivery. Our contribution: We address multi-site scheduling with different machine types
and partial matching constraints across hospital locations.
5. Dynamic Long-Term Evaluation: Most studies evaluate methods over short time
horizons (1-4 weeks) using static scenarios, failing to capture the complexity of real-world
operations where patients arrive continuously and schedules must adapt to changing con-
ditions. Our contribution: We evaluate the approach dynamically on a rolling time horizon
over 12+ weeks, capturing the complexity of real-world operations where patients arrive over
time and schedules must adapt to changing conditions.

3 Problem description
In the RT scheduling problem, the task is to assign patients to linacs for each treatment
fraction. This section presents the RT center whose real-world constraints and objectives are
considered in the CG approach, and information about the scheduling strategy.
The input from medical physicists, booking administrators, and radiation oncologists has
been used for data acquisition and deﬁning schedule quality metrics. The most common
treatments are carried out in ﬁve fractions per week, with pauses during the weekends, and
span over multiple weeks.
3.1 The RT center: iridium netwerk
Iridium Netwerk is a large RT center with four different hospitals located in Antwerp, Bel-
gium. In 2020, approximately 5500 RT treatments were delivered to 4000 patients. The main
location has four linacs, and the three satellite locations operate two linacs each. In 2020,
three different linac types were operational. Figure 1 shows the network structure of Iridium
Netwerk. Two linacs of the same type are called beam-matched; both machines can deliver
the same treatment plan, and a machine switch between fractions is possible. If the matched
machines are at the same hospital, they are completely matched, otherwise the match is
partial. A switch between partially matched machines is allowed only in special cases. In
2020, 34% of all weekdays had some type of machine unavailability planned due to holidays,
planned maintenance or other quality assurance activities. Since the treatments are delivered
in fractions, these unavailabilities cause disruptions in the treatments. The fractions affected
by machine unavailability can either be scheduled on a beam-matched machine the same day
or postponed. For the latter, a fraction can be added at the end of the treatment or the patient
can be treated twice on a day if the time between two fractions is at least 6 hours.
Each patient is appointed to a treatment protocol that contains the scheduling instructions,
such as required linac type, dose prescription, and the minimum number of fractions to be
treated each week. Since time is needed to prepare the treatment, the protocol deﬁnes the
minimum number of days between CT simulation and treatment start. The protocol also
states the allowed and preferred machines for the treatment, as well as different allowed start
days. The most common treatment protocols at Iridium in 2020 can be seen in Table 2. In
addition to the treatment protocol, there is also patient speciﬁc information: the number of
fractions during which the patient will be treated, the dose prescription for each fraction,

and the duration of the ﬁrst and subsequent fractions, where the ﬁrst is longer than the rest
for each patient because of initial setup and quality checks, as well as extra time for patient
education and reassurance.
In case a patient needs two different consecutive treatments, a secondary plan should ide-
ally start the day after the primary plan has ended. These treatments are most common for
breast cancer patients when the primary plan is followed by a boost plan. The secondary plan
often has different machine requirements and durations, and must therefore be handled sepa-
rately in the scheduling. Approximately 17% of all patients at Iridium Netwerk in 2020 were
treated with two consecutive treatments. Furthermore, there are so called non-conventional
treatments, meaning treatments that do not follow the regular ﬁve fractions per week sched-
ule. The most common are treatments that should be delivered three times per week, with
a pause day between each fraction. At Iridium in 2020, approximately 9% of all treatments
were non-conventional.
Each treatment protocol is associated with a priority as seen in Table 2. The purpose of
the priority is to estimate the urgency for treatment; priority A-patients should start treatment
as soon as possible, whereas priority B-patients can wait a few days and priority C-patients a
few weeks. In 2020 at Iridium, 37% of the patients were priority A, 16% were priority B, and
46% were priority C. Since the treatments often span multiple weeks (often 5 −7 weeks),
there is a need to ensure that capacity is reserved for priority A-patients that will arrive in the
future. At Iridium Netwerk, the booking administrator reserves empty timeslots for urgent
patients on each machine based on historical data.
When scheduling treatment appointments, patient wishes are taken into consideration,
such as preferred location and time. The booking administrators have estimated that 80% of
the patients have a preference for treatment time, of which 65% prefer a treatment before
noon and 35% prefer the afternoon. Most patients prefer to be treated in the hospital closest to
where they live. Each patient is notiﬁed of their treatment schedule soon after admission and
all fractions are communicated at once, and will only be re-planned in case of an unforeseen
event such as a machine failure. The booking administrators estimate it is approximately one
full time job to schedule the patients in all hospital sites.
The scheduling approach in this paper assigns each fraction to a machine, day, and time
window, where a day is divided into four time windows. Assigning patients to time windows
insteadofexactstartingtimesleadstoamoreefﬁcientmodel,whileitenablesalltheobjectives
and constraints needed to follow the instructions from the scheduling staff at Iridium Netwerk.
The speciﬁc appointment times are decided in a post-processing step.
The scheduling process assumes that priority B and C patients are notiﬁed of their starting
day one week in advance, which the majority of patients ﬁnd reasonable (Olivotto et al.,
2015). Priority A patients are notiﬁed immediately. All fractions are communicated at once,
as this is the current practice at Iridium. Each patient’s schedule can change until it has been
communicated, i.e., booking decisions are postponed to the next day if patients are scheduled
after the notiﬁcation period. A daily batch scheduling strategy is applied, using information
accumulated throughout the day.

the waiting time (objective i) is shorter on another machine, that is always preferred as that
objective has a higher priority.
4 Column generation model
The RTSP is formulated as a binary set partitioning model, where each decision variable is
a schedule for a patient, and the aim is to choose the optimal set of patient schedules. Since
it would be too expensive to generate all feasible patient schedules, a CG approach is used.
It consists of a (restricted) master problem and one subproblem for each patient. The master
problem is used for schedule selection: it is solved to choose a schedule for each patient
to make the overall schedule feasible and optimal. The subproblems are used for schedule
generation: for each patient, a new schedule is generated that fulﬁlls all medical and technical
constraints, and if the schedule has a negative reduced cost, the variable is added to the master
problem. The CG algorithm is presented in Figure 2. The notations are introduced in Table 4.
Building on our previous CG model (Frimodig et al., 2023), this paper presents signiﬁcant
extensions that enable clinical implementation at a large cancer center. The key innovations
include: (1) comprehensive modeling of planned machine unavailability, a critical real-world
constraint that creates scheduling bottlenecks and requires complex gap-ﬁlling strategies; (2)
support for specialized treatment protocols, including consecutive treatments (e.g., breast
cancer boost plans) and non-conventional treatments (e.g., three-times-per-week schedules,
total body irradiation); (3) multi-hospital site scheduling with partial machine matching
across locations; (4) dynamic time reservation methods to handle uncertain future urgent
patient arrivals; and (5) validation using real clinical data from a full year of operations,
enabling direct comparison with manually constructed schedules. Unlike our previous work
that focused on algorithmic comparisons using generated data, this model addresses all con-
straints necessary for clinical deployment in a complex multi-site cancer center.
Two different methods for solving the subproblems are developed in this paper; an IP
model and a dynamic programming (DP) model. These are presented in Section 4.2. To
improve the speed of the CG algorithm, all subproblems are run in the ﬁrst iteration, and
henceforth in every third iteration. In the two intermediate iterations, only the subproblems
that previously have had a negative reduced cost are run, since experiments show these are

4.3 Heuristic to create initial schedules
When solving a CG formulation, it is often beneﬁcial to have a heuristically generated set of
initial columns (Vanderbeck, 2005), which is true also for the CG algorithm as described in
Figure 2. For the algorithm to perform well, it is essential that the set of initial schedules are
of good quality, and that there is diversity in the initial schedules for each patient (i.e., not
123

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2869–2915
2893
Fig. 4 Daily arrivals in 2020 at Iridium Netwerk, excluding weekends and holidays
the same column generated over again). Therefore, there must be randomization included
in the heuristic. The number of initial schedules is set to 50, because for a larger number it
becomes difﬁcult to create new schedules that are not duplicates of the ones already created
without sacriﬁcing too much in quality, which in general increases solution times.
The overarching idea with the schedule construction algorithm is that it will create 50
complete schedules with all patients, that are varying in quality but that all fulﬁll the capacity
constraints. In other words, for i = 1, . . . , 50 the capacity constraint in the master problem
(3) can be fulﬁlled by setting ai
p = 1 for all p ∈P for one i at a time. This is achieved
in the algorithm by looping through all patients in a randomized order, and assigning them
to machines, days and time windows, while making sure that the maximum capacity of the
resources is not exceeded, and repeating this from scratch for each i. The time horizon is
chosen long enough so that it will always be possible to ﬁnd feasible schedules on some
machine. The schedule construction heuristic is presented in pseudocode in Appendix A.
5 Uncertainty in future arrivals
A major challenge in RT patient scheduling is the stochastic patient inﬂow, and that patients
of high priority should start treatment as soon as possible after arrival. Figure 4 shows the
daily patient arrivals at Iridium in 2020. The two-week rolling average varies between 13.9
and 26.4 patients per day, and between 4.1 and 10.1 priority A-patients per day. Most clinics,
including Iridium Netwerk, reserve a proportion of machine capacity for high priority patients
each day. This solution can result in poor quality schedules; urgent patients may have to wait
for treatment if not enough capacity was reserved, or the treatments for low-priority patients
may be delayed as a result of poor machine utilization.
To account for uncertainties in future arrivals, three different methods to reserve time for
high priority patients are investigated. These include to not save time for future arrivals at
all (no reservation), to reserve time on the machines based on average utilization rates for
high priority patients, which should mimic the current practices (static time reservation), and
to include expected future patients arrivals in the problem as placeholder (dummy) patients,
thereby allowing for trade-offs with the actual patients (dynamic time reservation). These
strategies are compared to an ofﬂine solution, which is achieved by using the actual future
arrivals for the coming weeks, and including them each day when creating the schedule. This
is of course not possible in reality, but is used to get a best case schedule.
123

The static time reservation method uses the historical patient arrivals, and distributes them
over the preferred machines for each protocol based on the average number of fractions and
average session time for each protocol according to Figure 5. The time is reserved for priority
A patients by blocking it for priority B and C patients; the input schedule Sm,d,w is adjusted
to include the blocked time when schedules for priority B and C patients are generated, both
in Algorithm 3 and constraint (14).
The dynamic time reservation method adds the expected future priority A patients as
placeholder patients and includes them in the scheduling. Based on historical arrival rates,
36 priority A patients are expected to arrive each week on average. In the dynamic reservation
method, these are distributed over the protocols according to the protocol probabilities, where
protocols with similar characteristics are grouped to simplify the problem. An overview of
the placeholder patients for each week can be seen in Table 7. In each daily scheduling
problem, the placeholder patients are added for each week in the planning horizon where
the actual patients are expected to start their treatments, which is usually around 8 weeks.
The cost function ci
p in (36) for the future dummy patients does not include costs for time
window switches, time window preferences, or machine switches, but it does include waiting
time, preferred machines, overall treatment time and site preferences. The site preferences
are generated for each placeholder patient based on the historical distribution between the
hospitals. To include the expected future patients as placeholder patients in the scheduling
will reserve capacity on machines for future patients in a dynamic way, since it allows for
trade-offs with the actual patients. If a low-priority patient has already waited a very long
time for treatment, there are cases where it is beneﬁcial for the overall schedule to let that
patient start before a patient of higher priority.

6.1 Data acquisition and problem setup
Information of all treatments in 2020 were extracted from Iridium Netwerk’s oncology infor-
mation system (OIS) ARIA (Varian Medical Systems, Inc., 2020). The patient data was
anonymized to comply with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).
A breakdown of the cancer categories at Iridium Netwerk in 2020 can be seen in Figure 6.
The palliative patients are grouped as one category. Each treatment protocol is assigned a
priority by the medical staff at Iridium Netwerk. Furthermore, each treatment is assigned a
CourseID based on PatientID, treatment protocol and the times for the delivered fractions.
This is used to separate the treatments for patients that were treated with multiple treatment
courses in 2020. Consecutive treatments are linked together based on the delivery of the
fractions. All patients that arrived to Iridium Netwerk in 2019, but had treatments scheduled
in 2020, are seen as ﬁxed in the input schedule for the 2020 scheduling. On the ﬁrst day of
2020, this occupancy will make up the input schedule Sm,d,w in the CG model.
The patients’ hospital site preferences are communicated verbally and not recorded in the
data. According to the Iridium staff, the majority of patients want to be treated on the hospital
closest to where they live. To keep the data anonymous, instead of using the patient’s home
address, a script assigns a site preference to each patient based on the patient’s zip code and
measured distance to each hospital.
The dates for planned unavailability in the yearly schedule are given as input to the
algorithm. Moreover, the extracted clinical data has information about machine failures and
other unplanned interruptions, and the durations of these interruptions are computed and
given as input at the day of the interruption.

treatment has a PatientID, CourseID, creation date, treatment protocol, number of fractions,
the length of the ﬁrst and subsequent fractions, and site preference. For secondary treatments,

6.2.1 Planned machine unavailability
To the best of our knowledge, the RTSP model presented in this paper is the ﬁrst to consider
planned unavailability on machines. Since gaps in the schedules induced by the scheduling
procedure are only allowed when there is machine unavailability, this also introduces the
minimum fractions per week requirements. Without the planned unavailability, the subprob-
lem IP constraints (15) to (31) can be replaced by a single constraint forcing fractions to be on
consecutive days, as done in (Frimodig et al., 2023). Figure 7 shows the solution times for the
different methods to handle future uncertainty using the IP model to solve the subproblems,
with time limit set to one hour. It is shown that including the planned machine unavailabil-
ity in the CG model increases the solution times considerably: the average solution time is
3.8 times as high when including unavailability ("DynamicRes") as when not including it
("Without unavailability (DynamicRes)"). Furthermore, when including the unavailability,
11.8% of the 254 instances time out, whereas only 0.8% of the instances time out when not
including the unavailability.
Each machine had between 16 and 18 unavailable days in 2020, excluding the weekends.
When not including the unavailability in the CG model, the number of fractions scheduled
on unavailable days is on average 375.1 per machine during 2020. It could be an option to not
include the unavailability in the model, and to instead manually reschedule the fractions for
the patients on these days. However, the number of fractions to manually reschedule is very
large, and there is no way to ensure that the minimum fractions per week requirements can be
fulﬁlled. In the UK guidelines for the management of treatment interruptions (RCR, 2019),
it is stated that for Category 1 patients (i.e., patients treated radically with curative intent
for tumor types where there is evidence that prolongation of treatment affects outcome),

not allowed in the node states. Thus, the solution times with or without machine unavailability
are approximately the same.

6.3.1 Schedule validation
To ensure the schedules generated by the CG algorithm can be implemented in practice, two
different types of validation have been done by the staff at Iridium Netwerk. The patient
schedule validation checks that the treatment is scheduled correctly for each patient, and the
machine schedule validation controls that the schedule is valid on each machine. Since it
would be too time consuming to manually conﬁrm the fulﬁllment of all medical and technical
constraints for all ten machines and all 4000 treated patients at Iridium Netwerk in 2020, the
schedule validation is done for a subset of patients and machines. First, a set of 15 randomly
selected patient schedules are validated, covering a range of different treatment protocols. The
patient information is reviewed together with the resource information data to conﬁrm that
the schedule would work in practice. The reviewed patient information includes fractionation
schemes, number of fractions, fraction duration, time for pre-treatment and minimum number
of fractions per week. Secondly, a random machine is selected for a time period of two weeks.
The machine is inspected for double bookings, machine unavailability, machine validity for
each patient’s protocol and that the machine capacity is not exceeded.
In addition to the manual validation performed by the medical staff at Iridium Netwerk,
an automatic framework for validating the schedules has also been developed. It tests all the
automatically generated schedules, both machine schedules (capacity, planned and unplanned
unavailability), and patient schedules. For the patient schedules, every medical and technical
constraint is tested for every patient and their treatment protocols.

that site were not fully operational in 2020. Therefore, machines M7 and M8, together with
all patients that were scheduled on those machines in the clinical schedule, are not included
in the comparison between the schedules.

When analyzing the manual schedules, a number of patients were identiﬁed to have unusually

Figure 12 shows that the schedule generated by the CG algorithm fulﬁlls the goals better than
the clinical schedules, both if including the 1% outliers or when excluding them. The number

the only objective where the manual schedule outperforms the CG generated one; the average
number of switches to partially beam-matched machines is 0.003 in the manual schedule,
and 0.14 is the optimized schedule, which is still very low.
Finally, Figure 12 illustrates that for objective i, to minimize the waiting times, the CG

allowed start date, taking into account the required number of days of the pre-treatment

compared to 1.5 days in the CG scheduling approach, which corresponds to a 81% decrease

11.3 to 1.6 days), and for priority C-patients the average waiting time decreased 86% (from
13.3 to 1.8 days). Furthermore, the maximum waiting times decreased from 35 to 7 days for
priority A-patients, from 35 to 8 for priority B and from 47 to 34 for patients in priority group
C.

7 Discussion
It has been shown that the CG approach for the RT scheduling problem can produce clinically
acceptable schedules. The quality of the generated schedules is better than the historical
clinical schedules based on the quality metrics that have been developed within the clinical
collaboration. The waiting times are shown to be decreased by the automatic algorithm, the
time between ﬁrst consultation and end of treatment is reduced by more than 80% while the
length of the pre-treatment phase remains the same.
Importantly, this work represents a signiﬁcant advancement in RT scheduling research by
providing clinical validation with real operational data. Unlike most theoretical scheduling
models that rely on generated or simpliﬁed data, our approach uses a complete year of actual
patient data from a large multi-site cancer center, enabling direct comparison with manually
constructed clinical schedules. This validation demonstrates that the complex constraints and
objectives integrated into the CG model-including planned machine unavailability, special-
ized treatment protocols, and multi-site coordination–sare not only mathematically sound
but also clinically meaningful and practically implementable. Three main reasons why the
scheduling algorithm produces schedules that are superior to the clinical schedules have
been identiﬁed. First, the rolling-horizon algorithm performs daily batch scheduling instead
of scheduling the patients one-by-one. This offers more degrees of freedom since the algo-
rithm can leverage patient information accumulated throughout the day, and it has also been
shown in previous studies that this leads to better schedules (Pham et al., 2022). Secondly,
the handling of uncertainty in future urgent patients is improved in the CG algorithm; it uses
a dynamic time reservation method that allows for a tradeoff between actual patients and the
patients that are expected to arrive in the future, which was shown in Section 6.2.3 to give
shorter waiting times than the static time reservation method that is used clinically. Third, the
CG algorithm uses mathematical optimization, and is designed to maximize the performance
in the schedule quality metrics described in Section 3.2. In Section 6.2.5, it is shown that if
the problem is modeled correctly, the resulting schedule will be close to optimal using the
proposed mathematical approach. Another potential reason why the CG generated schedule
has shorter waiting times than the clinical schedule lies in the result of objective v; the auto-
matic algorithm sometimes schedules a patient with planned switches between hospitals if it
results in shorter waiting times.
As previously stated, approximately 170 outlier patients with unusually long waiting
times were excluded from the quality measure plot in Figure 12. However, it is possible that
additional waiting times are long in the clinical schedule not as a result of the scheduling
strategy, but as a result of shortage of staff or other resources, due to personal requests, or
inﬂuenced by the COVID pandemic. If excluding the top 15% waiting times in the clinical
schedule, the CG generated schedule still decreases the average waiting times by 75% for
priority A (6.2 to 1.5 days), 82% for priority B (8.8 to 1.6 days), and 81% for priority C (9.5
to 1.8 days). Furthermore, due to the shortage of staff in one of the hospital sites, the two
machines on that site together with the patients scheduled on them in the clinical schedule
were not included when creating the CG schedules or in the quality comparison. If including
these patients and machines with full capacity, the results are very similar. Thus, this does
not seem to be a large source of error.
For each treatment course, the creation date in the OIS is assumed to mimic the arrival
to the hospital. This will be correct in the majority of the cases since the patient is entered
into the OIS at arrival. However, there can be cases when it is not necessarily decided to
treat with RT already at arrival. There is no way to know this from the available data, but the
123

2908
Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2869–2915
Iridium staff assure that these cases are very uncommon. Another source of error is that in
the CG scheduling algorithm, it is assumed that the daily interruptions are known already in
the morning, even though it is likely that the failure is not known until it actually happens.
However, for the rescheduling of the affected patients this does not make a difference, since
we limit the rescheduled fractions to be after the failure occurs.
Since the hospital site preferences were not recorded, they were instead estimated from
the patient zip codes. This is a source of error when evaluating the algorithm for this quality
metric, but should not be a problem if used in practice when the actual preferences are
known. Moreover, different cancer centers can have different scheduling objectives. In some
countries, there are nationally adopted waiting time targets stating the maximum waiting
time between referral and start of treatment. In a previous paper, we showed that there is a
close correlation between minimizing the waiting time and minimizing the violations of the
waiting time targets (Frimodig et al., 2023). This objective is straightforward to include in
the mathematical model.
At Iridium Netwerk, approximately one full time job is spent every week on scheduling
patients on the linacs. For each daily batch scheduling, the automatic algorithm is allowed
to run for maximum one hour, and the average computation time is 20 minutes. Since the
scheduling is done once daily, the CG algorithm will spend at most ﬁve hours per week
constructing schedules, a process that can be done in the background. Although the model
is designed to capture all the major medical and technical constraints present in the clinical
scheduling, some patients may have patient-speciﬁc needs that are not implemented in the
model. Therefore, the generated schedule may need to be manually adjusted. Furthermore,
the staff may also need to spend time validating the schedules. Even with time spent on
these actitivies, there is a potential to save many hours of administrative work every week by
automatically generating schedules.
The medical staff at Iridium Netwerk expresses signiﬁcant interest in applying the auto-
matic scheduling algorithm clinically. Two main beneﬁts of applying the model are identiﬁed,
where the ﬁrst is that the clinical schedules are optimized, and the second is that the algorithm
can be used to simulate department changes, such as buying a new linac, which can help make
more informed decisions. However, several challenges exist for actual clinical implementa-
tion. The most important is that a user interface is needed, either developed within the OIS,
or with communication to it. When accounting for uncertainty in future arrivals, it is possible
that the dynamic time reservation method could be improved by not using a static number
of placeholder patients to add every week in the time horizon based on average arrivals, but
instead using machine learning to predict the future arrivals based on historical arrival rates
and the current occupancy in the schedule. To improve the quality of the schedules, it is
possible that grouping similar treatments to be scheduled after each other could decrease the
times needed for machine setup.
8 Conclusions
To manually schedule patients for RT is complex and labor-intensive. As the number of cancer
cases in the world are rapidly increasing, automated scheduling algorithms can optimize the
resources to achieve short waiting times. The main contribution in this paper is an automatic
scheduling algorithm for the RTSP based on column generation. The model includes all
constraints and objectives necessary for it to work in practice at Iridium Netwerk, a large
cancer center with ten linacs in Antwerp, Belgium. To the best of our knowledge, this is
123

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2869–2915
2909
the ﬁrst model to consider planned machine unavailability and the constraints and objectives
related to the resulting gaps in the schedules. The model is also the ﬁrst to include specialized
treatments, such as consecutive treatments, and non-conventional treatments. The solution
times are accelerated by the use of dynamic programming to solve the subproblems. To
account for uncertainty in the future arrivals of urgent patients, we present a method for
dynamic time reservation, and compare it to the static time reservation method that most
clinics use today. The results show that the dynamic time reservation method outperforms the
static time reservation method. A sensitivity analysis show that the dynamic time reservation
method is robust to ﬂuctuations in arrival rates, which strengthens the conclusion that this
method works very well. By evaluating the performance of the CG approach compared to
an exact method, it can be seen that the CG approach generates schedules that are close to
optimal in a reasonable time frame.
The CG generated schedules are clinically validated and compared to the historical sched-
ules from Iridium Netwerk for a time period of one year. The results show that when
compared to the clinical schedule, the automatically generated schedule decreases the average
patient waiting time by approximately 80%, increases the time consistency between treatment
appointments by 80%, increases the number of fractions scheduled on preferred machines
by 90%, and also improves the majority of the other schedule quality metrics substantially.
