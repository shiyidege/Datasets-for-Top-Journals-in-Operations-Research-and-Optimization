Abstract
Following the development of e-commerce, the manufacturer can sell products via direct or
livestream modes. To meet market demand, the manufacturer will strategically adjust prod-
uct prices, and consumers weigh whether to make early- or later-stage purchases based on
their needs. In the current market, we assume two types of consumers including myopic and
strategic consumers. Meanwhile, the tolerance level of each consumer regarding whether to
delay purchase is heterogeneous. In our work, we built a two-stage game model to analyze
the impact of consumers’ strategic behavior and their patience to delay purchases on man-
ufacturer’s choice of the stage to adopt the livestream selling strategy under three scenarios
including the manufacturer chooses a direct format to sell products in both stages (NN),
the manufacturer chooses a direct format in the ﬁrst stage and a livestream format to sell
products in the second stage (NL), the manufacturer chooses a livestream format in the ﬁrst
stage and a direct format to sell products in the second stage (LN). Our main ﬁndings show
that when strategic consumers dominate the market and consumers are more willing to post-
pone their purchasing, the manufacturer should adopt a penetration pricing strategy under
NL scenario; otherwise, a skimming pricing strategy can be adopted. Under LN scenario,
the manufacturer should directly employ a skimming pricing strategy. In addition, when the
discount factor and the proportion of myopic consumers are small, and the commission rate
is high, the manufacturer is more inclined toward the NL scenario; otherwise, it prefers to
the LN scenario. In the extended models, we discuss the inﬂuence of different parameters,
including the changed popularities of live-streamer, commission rates, and pit fees for the
proﬁt of the manufacturer. Moreover, we explore how the manufacturer selects livestream
selling in both stages, and compare it with three main scenarios by using numerical studies.
The result shows that the LN scenario is optimal in most cases.

1 Introduction
Livestream selling is a popular and promising promotional strategy in platform economics
(Lu et al., 2025; Peng et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024a). As of December 2024, livestream users
in China reached 833 million, marking a year-on-year increase of 17.37 million, accounting
for 75.2% of the netizens. Online retail sales in China reached 1.55225 trillion RMB last year,
marking a year-on-year increase of 7.2%1%. The livestream market in the US is expected to
reach a projected revenue of $ 65,135.9 million by 2030, and a compound annual growth rate
of 21% from 2024 to 20302. In addition, Singapore’s live commerce sales are expected to hit
$1.3 billion in 2025 and surpass $6.6 billion by 2030 3. In recent years, many manufacturers
have taking cooperated with live-streamers to attract more consumers, thereby increasing
product sales. For example, a famous household appliance enterprise, Gree Electric, con-
ducted a live stream promotion on an e-commerce platform, and made sales of Gree over 5
billion RMB in the ﬁrst half of the year 4.
Livestream selling can signiﬁcantly boost sales performance (Huang et al., 2024; Li et al.,
2023; Gu et al., 2024a). However, sellers can choose to implement livestream promotion
strategies during different sales periods. On the one hand, sellers can adopt livestream pro-
motions in the ﬁrst stage of the sales season, increasing the product’s popularity after its
launch (Wang et al., 2024a). For example, an automobile enterprise, WEY, chose to partner
with CCTV News New Media and invited two popular live-streamers to promote the new
VV6 model, attracting signiﬁcant audience attention and market appeal. On the other hand,
sellers collaborate with live-streamers to promote the previous products in the second stage of
the sales season, achieving inventory clearance. For example, according to Nike’s Q2 ﬁnan-
cial report, as of November 30th, 2023, Nike utilized livestream channels to sell overstock
products at discounted prices in live room, helping to reduce inventory by 14% 5. Therefore,
livestream selling can not only promote new products in the ﬁrst stage of the sales season,
but also clear obsolete products in the later stage of the sales season. It is important for the
seller to choose the right selling stage to implement livestream promotion.
Besides stage selection by the seller, different types of the consumers will also signiﬁcantly
inﬂuence the performance of the livestream promotion. On the one hand, myopic consumers
either do not consider buying or only purchase products in the ﬁrst stage, regardless of whether
the enterprise will introduce livestream promotion. Their decisions are usually based on short-
term rather than long-term beneﬁts. Therefore, livestream promotion can effectively stimulate
purchasing desire among myopic consumers in the ﬁrst stage. On the other hand, strategic
consumers are usually more cautious about evaluating the total value in their purchasing deci-
sions and tend to carefully compare and evaluate when implementing livestream promotion
at different stages of the sales season. Speciﬁcally, they evaluate long-term value to decide
whether to make a purchase in the early or later stages, or even not make a purchase at all.
Therefore, sellers should conduct livestream promotion with detailed product information

or a clear demonstration of long-term value. Motivated by the above real-word analysis of
livestream promotion, this study proposes two research questions, including: (1) How do the
proportion of the two types of consumers and the discount factor inﬂuence on the equilibrium
decisions when the manufacturer implements livestream promotion at different stages of the
sales season? (2) Which stage should the manufacturer choose to invite a live-streamer to
conduct or abandon the livestream promotion?
To answer these research questions, we investigate whether a manufacturer sells products
in two stages of the sales season and makes two different pricing decisions. The manufacturer
can invite a live-streamer to introduce and promote products during each stage of the sales
season. However, different proportions of myopic and strategic consumers decide whether to
buy the product during the two stages of the sales season. Therefore, we set up three kinds of
two stages of pricing modeling with three corresponding scenarios from the perspective of
the manufacturer, including (1) the manufacturer choosing the direct-selling format in both
stages (NN scenario), (2) the manufacturer choosing direct-selling and livestream selling in
the ﬁrst and second stages (NL scenario), respectively, and (3) the manufacturer choosing
livestream and direct-selling in the ﬁrst and second stages (LN scenario), respectively. We
ﬁrst derived two demand functions regarding the two stages of the sales season under the three
scenarios. Equilibrium decisions were further derived and compared under different scenarios
based on the proportion of the two types of consumers and the intertemporal discount factor.
Moreover, we discussed different popularities of the live-streamer, commission rates, and pit
fees, and further extend the manufacturer’s choices to adopt livestream format in both stages
(LL scenario) through numerical experiments.
Although the two-stage of livestream selling strategy has been employed in prior literature,
few studies have classiﬁed consumers into two types, i.e., the myopic and strategic, in the
context of livestream pricing decisions. However, myopic and strategic consumers usually
make different purchase decisions when a manufacturer chooses to implement the livestream
selling strategy at different stages of the sales season. Consequently, it will signiﬁcantly
inﬂuence the manufacturer’s sales volume and decision-making regarding stage selection for
implementing livestream promotion. Therefore, this study’s main contribution is to consider
the myopic and strategic consumers in the context of the two stages of livestream selling from
the perspective of the manufacturer. Moreover, we made some interesting ﬁndings includ-
ing: (1) The manufacturer should implement different pricing strategies based on different
situations; (2) When the proportion of myopic consumers is small and the commission rate is
high under NL scenario, an interesting phenomenon can be observed: the second-stage price
will be reduce with respect to the discount factor within a small range; (3) When the discount
factor is high and commission rate is medium under LN scenario, the the ﬁrst-stage price
will gradually decrease with respect to the proportion of myopic consumers within a large
range; (4) By using numerical experiments, we can know that when the discount factor and
the proportion of myopic consumers are small, the manufacturer is more inclined toward the
LL scenario; otherwise, it prefers the LN scenario in most cases. There may be two reasons
that can explain the phenomenon of LL scenario: one is that even if the the proportion of
myopic consumers is low, livestream selling can attract more strategic consumers to shop in
the ﬁrst stage; other is that with the help of a live-streamer, if the price is relatively small,
remaining consumers are shop in the second stage; (5) The enhanced popularity of the live-
streamer increase the total manufacturer proﬁt; (6) Following the increase in commission
rate, the manufacturer prefers the LN scenario. However, an increase in the pit fee inclines
the manufacturer toward the NN scenario.

2 Literature review
This section compiles two streams of our study including two-stage pricing and livestream
selling to ascertain differences between existing literature and this study.
2.1 Two-stage pricing
Faced with different customer demands, sellers regularly adjust prices based on the actual
situation. Therefore, two-stage pricing has received considerable attention in the industry
and academia. This study is closely related to two-stage pricing in operations management.
For example, Chen et al. (2020) developed a two-stage pricing model to analyze the seller’s
proﬁts when serving strategic customers based on the reference price effect, and further
demonstrated that when consumer strategic behavior is low (high), the seller’s proﬁt decreases
as consumer strategic behavior decreases, and increases (decreases) as the reference price
effect increases (decreases). Considering dynamic competition between two companies, Liu
and Zhang (2013) designed a two-stage game-theoretic model to analyze the proﬁtability
of companies that offer vertically differentiated products. Zhao et al. (2019) presented a
two-period mathematical model with a reference price effect with strategic consumers. The
conclusion shows that when the discount factor is within a threshold value, the value of price
matching improves following strategic purchasing behavior. Zheng et al. (2023) examined
the optimal pricing strategy for strategic consumers, and found that when the strength of
the network externality effect is below the threshold value and affects the matrix symmetry,
it is best for the seller to raise prices; otherwise, the seller should reduce prices. Based on
prospect theory, Chaab and Zaccour (2024) discussed pricing decisions for new products
when the seller serves different consumers, and pointed out that consumer strategic behavior
can reinforce or weaken the penetration pricing strategy. They elaborated that, as customers
become more strategic, the proﬁts of both companies decline, and low-quality companies
suffer greater losses than their counterparts.
However, these studies only considered the impact of two-stage pricing on sellers in the
traditional supply chain, while ignoring its inﬂuence in a livestream selling environment.
In this study, we constructed a corresponding model by adopting two-stage game theory
to analyze the impact of equilibrium solutions for the manufacturer that adopt livestream
selling.
2.2 Livestream selling
Recently, livestream selling has become and have attracted the attention of relevant experts
and scholars. Most existing research draw on empirical studies to explore livestream selling
business modes (Gu et al., 2024b; Feng et al., 2024), the introduction of livestream selling
123

Annals of Operations Research
(Du et al., 2023a; Wang and Zhang 2023; Liu et al., 2024) or the selection of livestream selling
modes (Xu et al., 2024; Du et al., 2023b; Niu et al., 2025), etc. Few studies have discussed
whether the live-streamer and seller should cooperate short- or long-term. Speciﬁcally, Ji
et al. (2022) consider two scenarios in a livestream selling environment including quality
commitment and discrimination. The results indicate that under the commitment (discrimi-
nation) quality scheme, the ﬁrst-stage price is always higher (lower) than the second-stage
price, whereas under the quality discrimination scheme, the quality of the inﬂuencer channel
is always worse than that of the traditional online channel. Additionally, although the quality
discrimination scheme proﬁts enterprises more, the quality commitment scheme increases
consumer surplus. Ji et al. (2023) also incorporated promised pricing scheme and dynamic
pricing scheme into models to investigate the impact of introducing a livestream channel in
the ﬁrst-stage. They found that under a dynamic pricing scheme, the decision maker is more
likely to introduce a livestream selling channel, which may increase the price in the existing
channel. Based on the differences in product quality, Wang et al. (2024b) analyzed whether
the manufacturer should adopt livestream selling in the short or long term by constructing a
two-stage game model. The results indicate that short-term livestream selling cause a decline
in product quality, whereas long-term livestream selling helps improve product quality. Mao
et al. (2022) divided the impact of live stream selling into two dimensions– namely emotional
support and information support, and examined how they affect the types of cooperation and
revenue distribution between the retailer and multi-channel network (MCN) by constructing
a two-stage model. The main contribution is that when consumer uncertainty is low, the
MCN prompts the retailer to choose the cooperation type based on the emotional support
level, whereas under high consumer uncertainty, the MCN makes decisions based on the
information support level.
Additionally, some studies explore livestream selling from the perspective of consumer
behavior. For example, Jiang and Cai (2021) examined the impact of impulsive consumption
on the supply chain when consumers engage in livestream selling. The results show that rea-
sonable pricing decisions and high-quality product guarantees are key to customer impulses
and improve business proﬁtability. Therefore, the decision-maker should improve consumer
satisfaction rather than blindly encourage impulsive consumption, which may cause prod-
uct returns. Similarly, Duan and Song (2024) also discussed how impulsive consumption
affects decision-makers under livestream selling environment. They found that if the retailer
considers the return rate, they will lower prices in the livestream channel and improve price
in the traditional channel. By examining the price comparison behavior among consumers,
Lin et al. (2024) found that brand owners should consider the operational characteristics of
livestream selling and the reputation of the live-streamer when making optimal pricing deci-
sions. Despite the live-streamer has a large number of fans, fake low-priced advertising may
negatively affect the reputation and economic interests of the live-streamer and the brand
owner.
Most studies mainly examine the cooperation strategy between the seller and the live
streamer in a single-stage environment. Few studies have considered the impact of livestream
selling strategies at different stages on the proﬁts yielded throughout the selling season. How-
ever, the existing literature neglects to explore whether the seller is suitable for integrating
early (later) stage cooperation with live-streamer with consumer strategic behavior. In this
study, we considered myopic consumers and strategic consumers in a livestream selling mar-
ket. Then, we further analyzed how different types of consumers inﬂuence the manufacturer’s
choices of livestream selling strategies at different stages to ﬁnd more meaningful manage-
ment insights. The detailed differences between this study and the previous studies on supply
chain management are displayed in Table 1.

3 Problem descriptions
In this section, we consider a manufacturer (it) that sells a product in two stages (i ∈{1, 2}),
and sets different prices based on the ﬂuctuating selling season. When a product enters the
market at an early stage, consumers are unaware of its real situation before purchasing it.
Moreover, the consumers’ product valuation is heterogeneous. Then, we assume that the
perceived valuation of the new product for the consumer is v, which is uniformly distributed
between 0 and 1, and that consumers can only purchase the product once at most in the two
stages. Without loss of generality, the market size is standardized to 1 (Schmalensee, 1982;
Kremer et al., 2017). Referring to previous research (Su, 2007; Cachon and Swinney, 2009),
the utility functions in the ﬁrst stage (i  1) and second stage (i  2) are: U1  v −p1
and U2  δv −p2, where U1 and U2 denote the utility of the ﬁrst and second stages,
respectively; p1 and p2 indicate the selling price of the ﬁrst and second stages, respectively;
δ(0 < δ < 1) represents the discount factor, implying the valuation discount generated
by consumer waiting. In other words, it can also be expressed as the degree of consumers
tolerance for delaying purchasing. Therefore, the later the purchase, the smaller the consumer
valuation (Besanko and Winston, 1990; Zhao et al., 2019).
In addition, we assume that there are two types of consumers in the current market: the
myopic consumer (he) and the strategic consumer (she) (Liu and Van Ryzin, 2008). Therefore,
we assume that the proportion of myopic consumers is θ and the proportion of strategic
consumers is 1-θ. For the myopic consumer, if his utility is positive in the ﬁrst stage, he
immediately make a purchasing decision; otherwise, he exits the market (myopic consumers
only purchase products in the ﬁrst stage because of his short-sightedness behavior). For the
strategic consumer, she strategically considers her utility between the two stages and makes
a rational choice between direct and delayed purchasing. If the utility is negative, she will
leave the market; If the utility in the ﬁrst stage is positive and is greater than that in the second
stage, she make the purchase in the ﬁrst stage; otherwise, she will purchase in the second
stage.
The manufacturer can sell products directly to consumers or collaborate with the live-
streamer in two stages. When the manufacturer adopts livestream selling, the consumer’s

manufacturer chooses livestream selling format in the ﬁrst stage and direct-selling format to

6.3 Impact of different commission rates and pit fees
In the original model, we assume that the commission rate (i.e., α) range from 0 to 30%.
However, the manufacturer may pay a higher commission rate to invite the top live-streamer.
In other words, α may be larger than 30%. Apart from the commission rate, some top live-
streamer can charge extra slotting fee, commonly referred to as pit fee, regardless of selling
quantities. Thus, we introduce a pit fee F into the main models and develop two additional
scenarios, referred to as NLF and LNF. The proﬁt functions and equilibrium solutions of
NLF and LNF scenarios were similar to those of the main models, which omit it. Then, we
will conduct a numerical experiment to discuss the variation trends by exploring different
commission rates (α  0.3, 0.4, 0.5) and pit fees (F  0, 0.1, 0.2). For the convenience of
comparison, we assume F  0 and α  0.3 in Figs. 7 and 8, respectively.

7 Managerial implications
Based on the above discussion, this study provides management insights for the operational
decision-making of enterprises. Firstly, if the manufacturer employs the NL scenario, a pen-
etration pricing strategy should be employed when there are few myopic consumers and the
discount factor is high. Conversely, when myopic consumers have a strong market advantage
and the discount factor is high, a skimming pricing strategy should be adopted. If the manu-
facturer prefers to be under LN scenario, it should directly adopt skimming pricing strategy.
Therefore, the manufacturer should adopt different pricing strategies based on the preferred
scenarios.
Secondly, it was found that under different conditions, LN and LL scenarios can yield
more manufacturer proﬁt through the comparison of four scenarios. Therefore, the manu-
facturer should ascertain a comprehensive investigation of real needs for consumers before
selling products, thereby selecting an appropriate livestream selling strategy for different
stages based on an understanding of consumer behavior. Facing with increasingly intelligent
consumers, the manufacturer should often be inclined toward the LN scenario, that is, employ
the livestream and direct-selling in the ﬁrst and second stages, respectively. This is because
launching and promoting new products in the live room can not only increase the exposure of
123

Annals of Operations Research
the initial product, but also help to improve the selling quantities in future, which is beneﬁcial
for both stages. Therefore, we encourage enterprises to adopt livestream selling as early as
possible during the initial release of new products, and make decisions in the later stages
based on the actual situation in the ﬁrst stage.
Lastly, enterprises should choose suitable the live-streamer based on the characteristics of
their products, rather than blindly pursue cooperation with top streamers. Before selecting
a live-streamer, enterprises should comprehensively consider the actual situation, such as
the popularity of the live-streamer, commission rate, pit fee, purchasing conversion rate, etc.
According to the ﬁndings of extension, we can note that collaborating with a top live-streamer
may not always be the most proﬁtable, thus careful selection is necessary.
8 Concluding remarks
In this study, we consider two types of consumers (myopic and strategic consumers) and
their patience levels for delaying purchases. Then, three scenarios are investigated, including
NN scenario, NL scenario and LN scenario, and constructed two-stage pricing models to
analyze the suitable stage for the manufacturer to adopt the livestream cooperative strategy.
Additionally, we employed numerical analysis to explore the scenario where livestream
selling was adopted in both stages (i.e., LL scenario) and discussed the impact of different
parameters variations on the scenario selection, providing scientiﬁc theoretical guidance for
the manufacturer.
8.1 Conclusion
Through comparative analysis of three scenarios, the main conclusions of this study are as
follows: (1) When strategic consumers occupy more market share and the discount factor
is high, the manufacturer should use a penetration pricing strategy under NL scenario; in
contrast, when myopic consumers dominate the market and the discount factor is high, a
skimming pricing strategy is adopted. Moreover, the manufacturer should directly employ
a skimming pricing strategy under LN scenario. In other words, the manufacturer should
adopt different pricing strategies based on different situations; (2) When the proportion of
myopic consumers is small and the commission rate is high under NL scenario, an interesting
phenomenon can be observed: the second-stage price will be reduce with respect to the
discount factor within a small range. The main reason is that the lower θ means potential
consumers are few in the ﬁrst stage, while most of consumers will switch to shop in the second
stage. If the manufacturer still set a high second-stage price, strategic consumers may exist
the market; (3) Under LN scenario, when the discount factor is large and the commission rate
is moderate, an interesting result can be observed: as the proportion of myopic consumers
increases within a high range, the ﬁrst-stage price will decrease. This can also explicate why
the manufacturer often attracts more consumers to purchase products at low prices in the
livestream room when the product is ﬁrst launched, especially the live-streamer with higher
attention, where the price is lower. This is because the manufacturer sets a lower ﬁrst-stage
price to increase product exposure and prepare for product promotion in the second stage;
(4) When the discount factor is large and the proportion of myopic consumers is small, there
exists an abnormal conclusion that the manufacturer is inclined toward LN scenario. The main
reason is that, on the one hand, although there are fewer myopic consumers who may lead to
a decrease in potential market demand in the ﬁrst stage, the fact that the manufacturer invites
123

Annals of Operations Research
the live-streamer to promote products (i.e., LN scenario) attracts more strategic consumers to
directly make purchases, resulting in an increase in manufacturer proﬁt in the ﬁrst stage; On
the other hand, a larger discount factor implies greater potential market demand in the second
stage, and coupled with “small proﬁts but quick turnover” strategy from the manufacturer,
it is conducive to increasing the proﬁt margin in the second stage, thereby boosting overall
proﬁts; (5) Our work supplements the study of Wang et al. (2024b), who pointed out that the
manufacturer prefers to abandon livestream selling or select livestream selling in the early
stage within a certain situation. However, we have different ﬁndings. Speciﬁcally, when the
discount factor and the proportion of myopic consumers are both small and the commission
rate is large, the manufacturer tends to select the NL scenario; otherwise, the manufacturer
prefers to LN scenario in most cases. This is why many manufacturers choose to launch
new products in a livestream room; (6) In the extended model, we ﬁnd that the manufacturer
choose either LN scenario or LL scenario based on the inﬂuence of consumer purchasing
behavior and discount factor. Then, if the popularity of live-streamer is high, it is beneﬁcial
for the proﬁt of the manufacturer. In addition, the increased of commission rate renders the
manufacturer willing to implement LN scenario. Lastly, following increasing pit fee, the
manufacturer prefers to abandon livestream selling. The speciﬁc decision-making situation
still needs to be determined based on real backgrounds.
8.2 Limitations and future work
This study has some limitations that should be solved in the future. For example, we only
consider that one manufacturer sells a single product to consumers, exploring how multi-
manufacturer can sell different products using a two-stage pricing strategy under livestream
selling environment is a new research direction in the future. In addition, we used a theoretical
model to explore pricing strategy in livestream selling with myopic and strategic consumers,
but neglect empirical analyses. In future, we plan to integrate game models and case studies
to obtain more interesting results.
