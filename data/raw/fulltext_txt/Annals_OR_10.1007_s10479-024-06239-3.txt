Abstract
Multinomial trials having probabilities pi =
vi

j=1 v j , i = 1, . . . , n are observed until one
of the outcomes, called the winning outcome, has occurred at least k more times than each
of the others. We give an efﬁcient simulation approach for estimating the probability that
each outcome is the winner as well as the mean number of trials needed. We also show that
the probability that outcome i wins is an increasing function of vi, and is greater than the
probability that outcome j wins when vi > v j.

1 Introduction
A sequence of independent games are played, with each game being won by player i with
probability pi =
vi
n
j=1 v j , i = 1, . . . , n, where v1, . . . , vn are speciﬁed positive values.
The competition ends when there is a player who has amassed at least k more wins than
any other player, with that player being the winner of the competition. Let N denote the
number of games until the competition winner is decided, and let Pi = Pi(v1, . . . , vn) be
the probability that player i wins the competition.
In Sect.2 we consider using simulation to estimate P1, . . . , Pn and E[N], and present
simulation estimators that use a conditional expectation as a control variable. In Sect.3
we show that Pi(v1, . . . , vn) is an increasing function of vi and that Pi(v1, . . . , vn) >
Pj(v1, . . . , vn) if vi > v j.
The type of tournament being considered was introduced in Ross (2024) as a possible rule
for determining which of the players has the largest value when v1, . . . , vn are assumed to
be the unknown values of independent random variables having a given density function.

• Letting the control variable be E[Y|H] results in a larger variance reduction than would

3 Qualitative properties of Pi(v1, . . . , vn)
Let v = (v1, . . . , vn).
Proposition (a)
Pi(v) is an increasing function of vi.
(b)
If v j > vi, then Pj(v) > Pi(v).

Remark
1. Our proof of Proposition 1a used the Poissonization trick of supposing that games are
played at times distributed as a Poisson process, along with a coupling argument, with
the proof following from the fact that if an outcome sequence results in a given player
winning the tournament, then that player will still be the winner if the outcome sequence
is modiﬁed by interspersing additional game wins of that player.
Poissonization was used because an immediate coupling argument, that couples the game
results when v′ is the value vector with the game results when v is the value vector
in such a way that, for every r, the winner of game r when v′ is the value vector is
either i or the winner of game r when v is the value vector, would not prove the result.
For suppose k = 3. Then player 1 would win the tournament if the game results are
2, 3, 2, 2, 3, 2, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1. However, if the game 5 result was changed to a win by
player 1, then player 2 would win the tournament. (That is, player 2 wins if the ﬁrst six
game results are 2, 3, 2, 2, 1, 2.)
2. One might expect that Pi(v) is an decreasing function of v j for j ̸= i, but we haven’t
been able to either prove this or construct a counterexample. However, as is indicated by
Example 1, this is not true when there is a minimal number of games.
