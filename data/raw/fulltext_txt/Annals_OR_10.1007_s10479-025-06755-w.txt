Abstract
In this article, closed form exact formulae are provided for two-asset vulnerable options 
with continuous default risk and partial recovery, stochastic default boundary, stochastic 
interest rate and jump-diffusion dynamics of the underlying assets. The single asset case 
is also covered. Moreover, default risk does not necessarily extend over the entire op­
tion’s lifetime and can be defined on any time interval before expiry. Numerical results 
are provided and show that option prices obtained in this framework significantly differ 
from prices obtained in a standard model.
Keywords  Vulnerable option · Default risk · Structural model · Stochastic barrier · 
Option valuation · Jump-diffusion · Window · Multi-asset option · Partial recovery
1  Introduction
What defines an option as “vulnerable” is the fact that counterparty default risk is taken 
into consideration when calculating its value, i.e. the option holder’s claim may lose part 
or all of its value as a result of the option writer (seller) finding themselves unable to honor 
their obligations. All the options traded in the OTC (Over the Counter) markets are vulner­
able to a greater or lesser extent, depending on the financial resilience of the companies 
that have issued them. OTC options are customized, bilateral contracts typically negotiated 
between financial institutions and large clients (e.g., hedge funds, corporates, sovereigns). 
These include exotic options, structured products, and long-dated options not available 
on exchanges of standardized contracts. The total notional outstanding in OTC markets is 
much larger than in organized markets. As of 2023, based on BIS data, OTC derivatives 
had over $65 trillion notional outstanding across interest rate, FX, and equity OTC options, 
compared with only a few trillion in exchange-traded options.

Yet, counterparty default risk is ignored by standard option pricing theory, thus leading 
to overestimated theoretical values. Examples abound where failure to account properly for 
counterparty default risk led to massive financial losses, or even systemic risk. The textbook 
case of counterparty risk mispricing is the 2008 financial crisis. AIG Financial Products sold 
a large number of credit default swaps and complex structured products with embedded 
options. Buyers of these products assumed AIG was a rock-solid counterparty, so that no 
proper pricing for AIG’s own default risk was performed. When the subprime crisis hit, AIG 
could not meet collateral calls. At the same time, Lehman Brothers, a major counterparty 
in OTC options, defaulted suddenly. All counterparties holding in-the-money options or 
swaps with Lehman found themselves unhedged and with no recovery or delayed recov­
ery. A $182 billion U.S. government bailout was necessary to prevent global contagion. 
More recently, banks holding options written by Archegos Capital incurred $10 + billion in 
combined losses when the latter defaulted in 2021—with Credit Suisse losing $5.5 billion 
alone. Following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, Western firms holding ruble-denominated 
FX options experienced counterparty defaults in 2022, driven by capital controls and pay­
ment prohibitions. This episode underscored the geopolitical dimension of counterparty 
risk, particularly in options involving currency deliverables.
From the late 90s onward, academics and practitioners have attempted to integrate coun­
terparty risk into option pricing. A possible approach, that will not be pursued in this article, 
consists in adjusting the default-free value of a security by a premium, called a ‘credit value 
adjustment’ (CVA), in order to compensate for credit counterparty risk (Antonelli et al., 
2021). However, most major published results on the valuation of vulnerable options rely 
on structural models for default, in which default is an endogenous variable determined by 
the first passage time of the issuer’s assets to a default barrier. The following survey does not 
provide an exhaustive list of all published papers on vulnerable options, but rather focuses 
on what can be considered as the main contributions in terms of citations and significance 
of content.
A first series of contributions is based on the restrictive assumption that default occurs 
only at expiry, which we will refer to as fixed default time. Most of them model the option’s 
underlying and the issuer’s assets as GBMs (Geometric Brownian Motions).
Johnson and Stulz (1987), who are the first to refer to options subject to the default risk of 
their sellers as “vulnerable options”, design a model whereby default happens if the seller’s 
assets are worth less than the option value at expiry. Assuming constant interest rate and 
correlated GBMs, they come up with an analytical formula involving unsolved integrals that 
have to be numerically approximated.
An often cited paper is that of Klein (1996), in which a closed form solution is found 
under assumptions of constant default barrier and interest rate, correlated GBMs, and a 
recovery rate explicitly related to the value of the counterparty’s assets. Klein and Inglis 
(2001) generalize the default barrier by rendering it contingent on changes in the option’s 
value itself, but at the cost of making the model analytically intractable. Using the Klein 
(1996) model of fixed default time and partial recovery as a function of the residual value 
of the firm, Jeon et al. (2016, 2017) obtain semi-analytical solutions for path-dependent 
options such as barrier, lookback and geometric average options. Finally, while preserving 
the assumption of fixed default time, some articles explore a few extensions to the Klein 
(1996) model; e.g., Klein and Inglis (1999) obtain a closed form solution when the interest 
rate follows a Vasicek process (i.e., gaussian, mean-reverting and one-factor), while Tian

et al. (2014) and Wang (2016) derive closed form solutions when asset prices are driven by 
correlated jump-diffusions instead of GBMs.
A second series of papers relies on the more realistic assumption that default can occur at 
any time during the option’s life, which we will denote as continuous default risk.
Based on assumptions of constant default barrier and interest rate, Hull and White (1995) 
obtain upper and lower bounds for the prices of vulnerable options when the two GBMs 
driving the option’s underlying and the selling firm’s assets are correlated, and a closed 
form result when the latter are assumed to be independent. Upon default, a percentage of the 
nominal amount of the claim is paid out, but it is not a function of the value of the counter­
party’s assets. Jarrow and Turnbull (1995) manage to derive a closed form expression for 
the value of a vulnerable option under a one-factor stochastic yield curve. However, their 
reduced-form approach is limited by the arbitrary assumption that the time of bankruptcy 
is exogenous, being modeled as an independent, exponentially distributed random variable. 
The payout ratio is also exogenous, as in Hull and White (1995). Under the assumption 
that the default barrier is constant, Liao and Huang (2005) manage to obtain a closed form 
formula for a vulnerable option with continuous default risk, when the interest rate is Gauss­
ian and all the correlations between the purely diffusive stochastic factors are explicitly 
considered. Moreover, their model incorporates the relevant property of partial recovery 
as a function of the residual value of the firm, as in Klein (1996). Almost all papers subse­
quent to Liao and Huang (2005) that tackle continuous default risk and achieve analytical 
solutions rely on more restrictive assumptions and thus cannot be considered as improve­
ments in terms of results, although they may be interesting insofar as they apply alternative 
methods of calculation. In particular, Jeon and Kim (2019) derive a closed form formula by 
solving a relevant PDE (partial differential equation) via a Mellin transform, but under the 
assumption of constant interest rate, which is more restrictive than the framework of Liao 
and Huang (2005). Hui et al. (2007) model the value of the option writer’s liabilities as a 
GBM correlated with the option writer’s assets and the option’s underlying, which has the 
advantage of enabling them to define a stochastic default barrier. However, they only obtain 
an approximate analytical formula. Besides, their model is based on two flawed assump­
tions: the yield curve is flat, and the option holder receives an exogenously specified fraction 
of the default-free option value when default occurs, as in Hull and White (1995) and Jarrow 
and Turnbull (1995).
The rest of the more recent literature tackling continuous default risk is dedicated to 
exploring alternative stochastic models through numerical approximation methods, e.g. 
GARCH diffusion models through Fourier transform (Ma et al., 2020), mean-reverting sto­
chastic volatility through multi-scale asymptotic methods for PDEs (He et al., 2022; Yang et 
al., 2014), uncertain Cox-Ingersoll-Ross model (Lv et al., 2023).
While the assumption of a fixed default time is convenient for analytical tractability, it 
is clearly overly restrictive; in practice, the option writer may default at any time prior to 
the option’s expiry. Accordingly, this article adopts a framework with continuous default 
risk. In order to reproduce market conditions as closely as possible, it is also assumed that a 
proportion of the claim’s value can be recovered at expiry according to the residual selling 
firm’s value. Within this setting, the objective of this article is to extend exact analytical 
valuation of vulnerable options in several directions.
The first contribution is to provide closed form exact formulae for multi-asset options, 
i.e. options whose payoff is a function of several underlying assets. More specifically, a

vulnerable best-of call on two assets is valued. A vulnerable vanilla call is retrieved as a sim­
pler particular case. It is easy to accommodate the given result and methodology to obtain 
formulae for other popular two-asset options, such as exchange options and spread options 
(with zero strike), thanks to the similarities between the payoffs of these various claims.
The second contribution is to allow continuous default risk to be ‘partial-time’ only, 
instead of extending over the entire option’s lifetime—what we call ‘full-time’ continuous 
default risk. A ‘window’ of vulnerability is defined, i.e. a time interval within the option’s 
life inside which default risk is active; outside this window, there is no default risk. Of 
course, the window of vulnerability can be equal to the total option’s lifetime, i.e. the full-
time case can be retrieved from the partial-time one.
The third contribution is to use a stochastic model that overcomes a number of limitations 
associated with the Black-Scholes model traditionally used to obtain exact formulae in a 
continuous default risk framework. More specifically, the following features are combined:
 –
Instead of being assumed constant or, more generally, a deterministic function of time, 
the default barrier that may be crossed by the GBM modeling the assets of the selling 
firm is defined as a function of the value of the firm’s liabilities which, in turn, evolve 
as another GBM.
 –
Instead of being assumed non-random, the interest rate is defined as a two-factor mean-
reverting Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process allowing calibration to the observed yield curve.
 –
Instead of being assumed purely continuous with respect to time, the underlying assets’ 
paths are defined as jump-diffusion processes mixing a time-dependent parameter GBM 
and a compound Poisson process.
All correlations between all stochastic factors, whether continuous or discontinuous, are 
taken into account. The novelty of our model is to combine all the previously mentioned 
features simultaneously in a continuous default risk framework. For instance, using a more 
restrictive assumption of fixed default time, Wang (2016) handles jump-diffusion processes 
but not stochastic interest rate; in a continuous default risk setting, Liao and Huang (2005) 
handle stochastic interest rate, but not jumps in the underlying assets. The paper is organized 
as follows: Sect. 2 presents the model and three formulae called Proposition 1, Proposi­
tion 2 and Proposition 3; Sect. 3 provides the core of the proof of the formulae; Sect. 4 
reports numerical results; finally, several appendices contain parts of the proof that are not 
expanded on in Sect. 3, as well as a list of definitions of all terms and symbols used in the 
formulae and in the proof.

Classical approaches of structural credit risk modeling, beginning with the seminal Black–
Cox (1976) model, overlook the stochastic nature of liabilities in modern financial environ­
ments, where firms’ obligations often consist of market-linked instruments, variable debt 
issuance schedules, and contingent claims. Modeling the default barrier as a function of 
the firm’s liabilities evolving as a GBM aligns more closely with the economic reality in 
which both sides of the balance sheet are dynamic and subject to market forces. This for­
mulation enhances internal consistency by coupling default risk with both asset and liability 
dynamics, allowing for richer feedback effects between funding structures and solvency. 
Empirically, firms rarely default at fixed thresholds; rather, default occurs when the asset-to-
liability ratio deteriorates, due to correlated market shocks affecting both components. Mod­
eling both assets and liabilities as correlated GBMs permits capturing such co-movements, 
while allowing the default region to reflect realistic balance sheet vulnerabilities.
Moreover, a framework in which counterparty default risk may arise only over a freely 
specified subinterval of the option life provides greater realism and flexibility than mod­
els that assume default risk is permanently present from inception to expiry. In real-world 
markets, the exposure of a derivative contract to counterparty risk often varies over time, 
depending on institutional arrangements, credit support annexes, margining schedules, and 
dynamic credit assessments. For instance, many OTC contracts include delayed settlement, 
deferred payment clauses, or collateralization provisions that mitigate counterparty expo­
sure during parts of the option life. It is also common in practice for options to initially be 
collateralized or guaranteed by a clearing party or a third-party custodian, with credit expo­
sure emerging only once a certain trigger event occurs, such as the removal of collateral, 
downgrade of the counterparty, or entry into a specified forward risk window.
Empirically, the relevance of default risk is also highly concentrated around certain time 
horizons, particularly when the option moves into the money or the counterparty’s finan­
cial condition deteriorates. For example, during the 2008 financial crisis, institutions like 
Lehman Brothers were perceived as safe counterparties until shortly before default, which 
created short-term windows of acute vulnerability rather than persistent risk throughout 
the contract life. Models assuming permanent counterparty risk fail to reflect this temporal 
asymmetry and may overestimate credit risk premiums, especially in the early stages of an 
option life when neither the market exposure nor the default likelihood is significant. Lastly, 
a flexible window-type modeling framework also facilitates scenario-specific analysis, such 
as stress-testing the impact of short-term credit risk within a known horizon (e.g., a debt 
maturity cliff, rating downgrade window, or regulatory intervention period).

Since the seminal empirical papers by Bates (1996) and Pan (2002), market data has 
repeatedly shown that asset returns exhibit excess kurtosis and fat tails inconsistent with 
pure diffusion models. Omitting jumps leads to significant mispricing of out-of-the-money 
options and fails to account for observed implied volatility skews, as downward jumps 
are both frequent and material. This is especially true in periods of elevated volatility and 
market crashes, such as the 2008 financial crisis or the March 2020 COVID-19 selloff, 
during which asset prices experienced abrupt and substantial declines—often exceeding 
5–10% in a single day—reflecting the very dynamics that jump components are designed 
to model. Empirical analyses of options data during these episodes, such as Christoffersen 
et al. (2012), show that jump-diffusion models provide significantly better fit to observed 
market prices and volatility skews than pure diffusion models. Capturing downside jumps 
is particularly critical in the context of vulnerable options, as the former can simultaneously 
reduce the option’s intrinsic value and increase the likelihood of counterparty default via 
sudden shifts in implied volatility surfaces if the issuing firm’s financial health is correlated 
with the underlying, thus creating a non-linear and compounding risk exposure based on the 
interaction between market and credit risk.

also create additional difficulties in terms of model calibration. Moreover, the drawbacks of 
assuming non-random diffusive equity volatility are at least partially offset by the presence 
of random discontinuous volatility.

4  Numerical results
This Section presents a few numerical results obtained by implementing Proposition 1 and 
briefly comments on them. We choose to implement Proposition 1 rather than the other ana­
lytical results because it is the most challenging formula in this paper, involving triple sums 
of quadrivariate normal cumulative distribution functions. If it can be easily dealt with from 
a numerical perspective, then even more so can the other analytical results (Proposition 2 
and Proposition 3), as they are simpler to evaluate.
Our first objective in this Section is purely numerical, i.e. check that the results obtained 
by implementing the formulae provided in this paper are in line with numerical approxima­
tions derived by implementing a Monte Carlo simulation. The latter resorts to an algorithm 
that begins by drawing the possible jumps of the processes N1 (t), N2 (t), N3 (t) over the 
option’s lifetime and sorting them according to their arrival time. Then, if we want our 
simulation to converge to the exact value, there does not seem to be a more efficient way 
than discretizing the SDEs of the processes r (t), A (t), L (t), S1 (t) and S2 (t) between 
each jump. Such a procedure is extremely time-consuming, as it requires a small timestep to 
accurately track the extreme values of the process A (t) /L (t)—theoretically monitored in 
continuous time—in order to determine whether the issuing firm has defaulted.
However, if we assume the interest rate to be constant, then the following faster and more 
precise algorithm can be applied:
(i) Draw the values of the processes A (t), L (t), S1 (t) and S2 (t) at time τ1, which is the 
arrival time of the first jump, whether it be a jump of N1 (t), N2 (t) or N3 (t).
(ii) Compute the conditional minimum of the process A (t) /L (t) over the time interval 
[0, τ1], knowing A (τ1) and L (τ1). This quantity admits a well-known exact formula 
since the processes A (t) and L (t)are standard geometric Brownian motions when the 
interest rate is constant. Notice that the exact formula for the conditional minimum of 
a geometric Brownian motion over a finite time interval requires the drift coefficient of 
the process to be constant, not just non-random, so that the algorithm does not allow the 
interest rate to be modelled as a non-random function of time.
(iii) Draw the values of S1
(

This second Monte Carlo method obviates the need to draw the values of the processes 
A (t), L (t), S1 (t) and S2 (t) at every instant between two consecutive jumps. Even if the 
interest rate is non-constant, this algorithm can still be useful as a semi-analytical bench­
mark to control the accuracy of the result obtained by the general procedure involving the 
discretization of all SDEs over the entire option’s lifetime.
Our second objective in this Section is to give a basic illustration of the effects of the 
additional features of our model on option prices, compared with more standard models. 
More precisely, we focus on the impact of introducing:
 –
 Correlated jumps vs. purely diffusive models.
 –
Stochastic interest rate vs. models in which the yield curve is non-random.
 –
Partial-time vulnerability vs. models in which vulnerability is full-time only.
We begin by specifying the diffusive components of the dynamics of the processes involved, 
following the order in which Brownian motions are numbered. We thus start with the inter­
est rate process. The observed rate at t0 is r0 = 1.5%, while the equilibrium (or steady state) 
value b is 2.5%. The mean-reverting speed is a = 40%. The sensitivities to the short and 
long ends of the yield curve are defined, respectively, as the following cubic polynomials:
 
σr1 (t) = c0 + c1t + c2t2 + c3t3
(85)
 
σr2 (t) = c4 + c5t + c6t2 + c7t3

Two interest rate volatility regimes are defined: ‘lower’ and ‘higher’. In the ‘lower’ rate vol­
atility regime, c0 = 12% and c4 = 16%. These values are multiplied by 2 in the ‘higher’ rate 
volatility regimes. In both rate volatility regimes, we have c1 = 0.01, c2 = 0.02, c3 = 0.03, 
c5 = 0.02, c6 = 0.01, c7 = 0.03. These numbers reflect the fact that the volatility of the 
interest rate is, in general, relatively subdued, compared with equity volatility.
As regards the assets and the liabilities of the option writer, the initial values are 
A (0) = 1100 and L (0) = 950. It is natural to have A (0) > L (0), since the selling firm 
is not supposed to be bankrupt at time t0. It also makes sense that the positive difference 
between A (0) and L (0) is not too large, otherwise vulnerability ceases to be a significant 
issue. In terms of volatility parameters, we have σA = 32% and σL = 24%. These numbers 
reflect a balance sheet in which there is slightly more uncertainty associated with the future 
value of the assets relative to the liabilities. This is often the case, since a significant propor­
tion of the liabilities usually consists of fixed-rate debt, while assets are more volatile. The 
moving bankruptcy threshold is assumed to start close to the ratio of assets over liabilities 
observed at t0 (δ1 = 95%) and to vary smoothly in time (δ2 = 0.05).

and S2. The starting values are S1 (0) = 100 and S2 (0) = 130. Since we want to compare 
the relative values of the variations in S1 and S2 between t0 and expiry t3, we set ω1 = 1

Such a choice of correlation structure can be briefly analyzed as follows:
 –
The value of ρ1.2 aligns with a yield curve where short-term and long-term interest rates 
tend to move inversely.
 –
The values of ρ1.3, ρ1.4, ρ2.3 and ρ2.4 suggest that the selling firm’s liabilities are more 
sensitive to interest rate variations than its assets—particularly at the short end of the 
yield curve. This is consistent with the fact that liabilities include a substantial amount 
of debt.
 –
The values of ρ1.5, ρ1.6, ρ2.5 and ρ2.6 are consistent with a market in which interest rate 
and equity markets are negatively correlated in the short term and positively correlated 
in the long run.
 –
The value of ρ3.4 tends to offset the favourable effect, on default risk, of a higher value 
of the selling firm’s assets by pushing the value of its liabilities higher too, but it also 
alleviates the detrimental effect of lower assets by pushing liabilities lower, thereby 
exerting an overall balancing effect; a negative, non-negligible value of ρ3.4 would, on 
the contrary, amplify the beneficial effect of higher assets by lower liabilities, as well as 
the adverse effect of lower assets by higher liabilities.
 –
Positive, non-negligible values for the coefficients ρ3.5 and ρ3.6 have both beneficial and 
adverse effects for the holder of a call option. If ρ3.5 and ρ3.6 are negative, a decrease in 
the underlying equity assets S1 and S2​—which reduces the likelihood of a call option 
ending in the money—is partially offset by an increase in the value of the issuing firm’s 
assets, thereby lowering default risk. Conversely, higher values of S1 and S2, which 
favor call option exercise, are partially offset by a simultaneous decrease in asset value, 
increasing default risk. A similar analysis applies to the coefficients ρ4.5 and ρ4.6. To 
achieve a more balanced risk profile, low values are adopted for all four coefficients: 
ρ3.5, ρ3.6, ρ4.5, and ρ4.6.
 –
Finally, ρ5.6 is negative and of substantial magnitude, reflecting an intentional use of the 
multi-asset structure to enhance diversification benefits.

We now turn to the parameters pertaining to the discontinuous components of the model. The 
jump processes may exhibit ‘lower’ intensity, as defined by: λ1 = 0.18, λ2 = 0.24, λ3 = 0.32, 
or ‘higher’ intensity, as defined by: λ1 = 0.36, λ2 = 0.48, λ3 = 0.64. It can be noticed that 
more weight has been assigned to the global shock component, reflecting the importance 
of systemic risk. The expected size of the jumps is naught, i.e. ξ1 = ξ2 = ξ3 = 0. In 
other words, jump sizes are not negatively or positively skewed. It should be pointed out, 
though, that to reproduce the well-documented ‘skew’ of implicit volatility encountered 
in the equity markets, negative values should be assigned to the parameters ξ1, ξ2 and ξ3. 
Two regimes of volatility are defined for the jump sizes: a ‘lower’ one with: ε1 = 20%, 
ε2 = 27%, ε3 = 32%, and a ‘higher’ one with: ε1 = 40%, ε2 = 54%, ε3 = 64%. It can be 
noticed that the volatility of the jump sizes of S2 is higher than the volatility of the jump 
sizes of S1. This, combined with the higher intensity of the jump process N2 (t) relative to 
N1 (t), offsets the higher diffusive volatility of S1 relative to S2, so that the total magnitude 
of volatility (including both diffusive and discontinuous sources) does not differ substan­
tially from one underlying asset to the other.
Finally, three regimes of vulnerability are set:
 –
A ‘shorter’ one, in which the window of vulnerability extends over 1/3 of the option life 
and is located between t1 = t3/3 and t2 = 2t3/3.
 –
A ‘longer’ one, in which the window of vulnerability extends over 2/3 of the option life 
and is located between t1 = t3/6 and t2 = 5t3/6.
 –
A full-time one, in which the window of vulnerability extends over the entire option life, 
i.e. t1 is equal to t0 and t2 is equal to t3.

expiry. To illustrate this, we distinguish between a ‘shorter’ expiry (6 months) and a ‘longer’

Whatever the equity and interest rate diffusive volatility, the expiry and the length of the

and/or the magnitude of the jump size volatility. In the environment defined by a com­

higher equity diffusive volatility’, the option value obtained with our model exceeds the 
one obtained in a non-vulnerable Black-Scholes setting when the window of vulnerability 
extends over 1/3 of the option life and when the option expiry is longer. In other words, 
the positive effects of the introduction of jumps on the option value can exceed the negative 
effects of the consideration of the option writer’s default risk.
Of course, these numerical results are highly dependent on the values chosen for the 
parameters of the compound Poisson process modeling the jumps. One might argue that the 
volatilities of the jump sizes selected in the ‘higher jump size volatility’ setting are quite 
high. But the levels of volatility selected in the ‘higher equity diffusive volatility’ setting are 
also quite high, which has an offsetting effect on a possible bias in the weight assigned to the 
discontinuous component of the total volatility of the underlying assets.
It can be noticed that the average differential, w.r.t. the Black-Scholes price, between 
option values when jump intensity and jump size volatility are high and option values when 
jump intensity and jump size volatility are low, is approximately the same in the shorter 
expiry setting and in the longer expiry setting. It can also be observed that the obtained 
prices in the ‘Lower jump intensity and higher jump size volatility’ setting are slightly 
higher than in the ‘Higher jump intensity and lower jump size volatility’ setting, pointing 
to a moderately stronger effect of increasing the jump size volatility, i.e. the average diver­
gence of a jump size with regard to its expectation, relative to increasing the jump intensity, 
i.e. the average number of jumps per unit of time. However, this phenomenon is closely 
related to the fact that the expected jump size parameters of all three jump processes have 
naught value. In general, given the complexity of the model used, entailed by the number of 
correlated stochastic factors, one should be wary of rushing to premature conclusions based 
on the very modest set of data presented in Tables 1, 2, 3 and 4.

5  Conclusion
This article has shown how to develop a valuation method for two-asset and single-asset 
vulnerable options using a model that combines the following advantages:
 –
Default may occur at any time prior to expiry.
 –
Default risk may be defined over only a fraction of the option life.
 –
Partial recovery of the claim is possible at expiry.
 –
Both assets and liabilities of the option writer follow geometric Brownian motions.
 –
The interest rate is a two-factor, mean-reverting Gaussian process.
 –
The option’s underlying assets follow jump-diffusion dynamics.
 –
All correlations between stochastic factors are taken into consideration.
To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to achieve exact, closed form valuation 
in such a comprehensive framework, yielding accurate results much faster than numerical 
approximation techniques, for actively traded two-asset and single-asset options.
It is quite remarkable that analytical tractability is preserved despite the complexity of 
both the payoff structure and the underlying model. Generalization to payoffs involving a 
greater number of correlated underlying assets, although feasible from a purely technical 
standpoint, would be quite cumbersome and tedious. Also, it is not clear how one could 
use a more sophisticated stochastic model for the variations of the asset and liability pro­
cesses, e.g. one that allows for time-dependent drift and volatility coefficients or one that 
is subjected to the same random discontinuous shocks as the option’s underlying assets, 
without losing the ability to derive exact analytical solutions. The ability of the proposed 
model to capture the early onset of default risk due to downward jumps in the underly­
ing—while maintaining tractable, diffusion-based dynamics for the firm’s own assets and 
liabilities—offers a realistic compromise between empirical validity and computational fea­
sibility. Besides, the asymmetry in the modeling assumptions used for asset and liability 
processes, on the one hand, and the option’s underlying asset price processes, on the other 
hand, reflects the empirical observation that tradable market assets are exposed to sudden 
price jumps due to market shocks, earnings surprises, or systemic events, while firms’ bal­
ance sheet components, typically based on slower-moving economic fundamentals, gener­
ally exhibit smoother trajectories.
In general, numerical approximation techniques remain necessary to tackle either higher 
payoff dimension or more sophisticated models, e.g. models combining jumps and stochas­
tic diffusive volatility.
