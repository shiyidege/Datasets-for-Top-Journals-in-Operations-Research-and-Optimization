Abstract
Cold chains are a major source of carbon emissions because they make use refrigerated trucks
and warehouses for distribution and storage of inventory. Consequently, inventory manage-
ment in cold chains should prioritise both proﬁtability and environmental sustainability. This
paper optimises inventory in a cold chain system consisting of a single warehouse and retail
outlet, responsible for storing and selling cold items, respectively. To maintain product quality
and safety, these items must be transported and stored in temperature-controlled environ-
ments. Two inventory models for the proposed cold chain are formulated and compared under
two distinct carbon emissions regulations, namely, cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset, using
both ﬁnancial and environmental metrics. The proposed models factor in carbon emissions
from refrigerated trucks and warehouses used for transporting and storing cold inventory. This
study examines both a numerical example and a real case study involving a frozen seafood
supply chain. The numerical analysis reveals that cap-and-trade regulation is more effec-
tive in reducing environmental impact, leading to 4.4% lower carbon emissions compared
to carbon cap-and-offset regulation. However, from an economic standpoint, carbon cap-
and-offset regulation performs marginally better, generating a 1% higher proﬁt. The study
identiﬁes three key factors that signiﬁcantly inﬂuence both ﬁnancial and environmental out-
comes in the cold chain: truck capacity, fuel efﬁciency, and freezer performance. As a result,
management can best improve proﬁtability and reduce emissions in cold chain operations by
focusing on optimising these critical elements.

This increased consumption can be attributed to the nutritional value of fresh produce which
has ample health beneﬁts. However, fresh produce looses its freshness and nutritional value
over time and therefore, consumers prefer produce when it is as fresh as possible. The most
common way of maintaining the freshness levels of produce is to preserve it at low temper-
atures. In essence, preservation of produce at low temperatures slows down the biological
activities of spoilage agents such as bacteria, yeast and mould which ensures that the pro-
duce stays fresher for longer because it won’t spoil as quickly as it would have under ambient
temperatures. In industrial settings, this is enabled by cold chain logistics networks (Zhang
et al., 2020; Neusel & Hirzel, 2022).
Cold chains make use of uninterrupted systems of cold temperature-controlled transporta-
tion networks and cold temperature-controlled storage nodes in an effort to maintain the safety
and quality of the products (Ndraha et al., 2018). The transportation networks are used for
moving inventory, such as fresh produce and vaccines, between the storage nodes and the
supply and demand nodes in a cold chain, whereas, the storage nodes are used for holding
inventory at different nodes in a cold chain. A successful cold chain operation often makes use
of specialised equipment such as refrigerated trucks for transportation activities, cold storage
warehouses and freezers for storage activities and sensor technologies for temperature mon-
itoring activities (Konovalenko et al., 2021). Figure 1 depicts simpliﬁed cooling systems for
different refrigeration equipment such as commercial fridges, refrigerated trucks and freezers
in cold storage warehouses. Operating most of these refrigeration equipment, used with the
aim of maintaining the products in question within a speciﬁed cold temperature range, uses
a signiﬁcant amount of electric energy. As a result, the use of refrigeration equipment in cold
chains results in the release of signiﬁcant quantities of carbon emissions because most of the
world’s electricity is sourced from fossil fuel-based sources such as coal. Moreover, a vast
majority of the refrigerated transportation trucks used in cold chains are powered by diesel
or gasoline, both of which are fossil fuel-based sources of energy and therefore, also result
in the release of carbon emissions.
Taking the aforementioned factors into consideration, this paper proposes inventory mod-
els for a cold chain comprised of a single warehouse and a single retailer. The proposed
cold chain makes use of an uninterrupted system of refrigerated transportation trucks and
refrigerated storage units that use a sizeable amount of energy and consequently, results
in the release of carbon emissions. The proposed cold chain is studied under two different
carbon cap emissions regulations, namely, carbon cap-and-trade and carbon cap-and-offset

regulations. These two regulatory approaches aim to decrease atmospheric emissions from
various entities, and both function within systems that have established limits (i.e., caps) on
the amount of emissions. The key distinction lies in their mechanisms: cap-and-trade empha-
sises the exchange of emission allowances within the capped system, whereas cap-and-offset
permits companies to fulﬁl their obligations by funding external emission reduction projects.
Consequently, both the cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset models are formulated under the
assumption that there is a cap on the amount of carbon emissions that the cold chain can
release into the atmosphere. The formulation of the cap-and-trade model also assumed that
there is a carbon emissions trading system available for selling excess carbon emissions cred-
its if the emissions released by the cold chain are less than the carbon cap or buying carbon
emissions credits when in short supply (i.e. when the emissions released are greater than the
carbon cap). On the other hand, the formulation of the cap-and-trade model also assumed that
the cold chain has the option of investing in carbon offset projects aimed at carbon emissions
abatement if the emissions released by the cold chain are greater than the carbon cap.
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows: Section 2 provides a brief review of
closely related models in the literature, with a particular focus on inventory models for cold
items and inventory models that account for carbon emissions under carbon cap regulations.
Section 3 provides a brief description of the proposed cold chain as well as lists of assump-
tions and notations adopted when developing the models. The basic inventory model for the
proposed cold chain is formulated in Section 4 and then this is further extended into two
models in Section 5 by accounting for cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset emissions regula-
tions. Section 6 presents the results from numerical analyses of the two models. The paper
is concluded in Section 7 which also provides suggestions for future research.
2 Literature review
The formulation of the inventory models for the proposed cold chain is based on the theory
from two streams of inventory management research, with the ﬁrst stream being the devel-
opment of inventory models for cold items and the second one being the development of
inventory models under carbon cap emissions regulations. Consequently, the focus of the
literature review is on these two research streams.
2.1 Inventory management for cold items
Through the development of the so-called the ‘cold items cost and emission minimisation’
model, Bozorgi et al. (2014) introduced cold items to inventory management research. In
essence, Bozorgi et al. (2014) extended the classic economic order quantity (EOQ) model, as
developed by Harris (1913), to a scenario where the items in the inventory system have to be
stored and transported in a cold temperature-controlled environment. In their model, Bozorgi
et al. (2014) also considered the carbon emissions that result from holding and transporting
the cold inventory items. The aim of Bozorgi et al. (2014)’s model was to minimise both
the inventory management costs, encompassing the holding and transportation costs, and the
carbon emissions that are released.
Given its potential practical applications, Bozorgi et al. (2014)’s model has been extended
to different scenarios. For instance, Bozorgi (2016) presented an extension that accounts for
multiple cold products in the system whereas Hariga et al. (2017) extended the model to a
scenario with a multi-stage cold supply chain under a carbon tax emissions regulation. Other

notable extensions to Bozorgi et al. (2014)’s model include those that incorporated time-
varying demand rate (Shamayleh et al., 2019); carbon cap regulation (As’ad et al., 2020);
stochastic demand rate and carbon tax regulation (Babagolzadeh et al., 2020); uncertain
demand and supply in a vaccine distribution network (I¸sık & Yildiz, 2023); and stock-

2.2 Inventory management under carbon cap regulations
One of the most earliest and most proliﬁc inventory models that explicitly account for carbon
emissions that are released as a result of inventory management activities (such as inventory
holding and ordering) is the model by Chen et al. (2013). In that particular model, often
referred to as the so-called the carbon-constrained EOQ model owing to the fact that it is
based on Harris (1913)’ classic EOQ model, Chen et al. (2013)’s aim was to minimise both the
inventory-related costs and carbon emissions that result from ordering and holding activities.
Chen et al. (2013)’s model was studied under three different carbon emissions regulations,
namely, carbon tax, cap-and-trade and carbon-cap-and-price regulations.
Chen et al. (2013)’s model has been extended numerous times in an effort to make it as
practical as possible under different situations. Case in point, Konur and Schaefer (2014)
presented an extension that compares the effectiveness of a truck load to that of a less-than
truck load transportation mode under carbon cap, cap-and-trade, cap-and-trade and carbon
tax regulations. Likewise, Toptal et al. (2014) formulated an extension that was aimed at
investigating the impact of investing in emissions reduction technologies under carbon cap
and cap-and-trade regulations. Other notable extensions to Chen et al. (2013)’s model include,
but are not limited to, those that incorporated stochastic demand and a carbon cap regulation
(Ghosh et al., 2017); reciprocal preferences and consumer’s low-carbon awareness under
a cap-and-trade regulation (Xia et al., 2018); multi-period carbon reduction mechanisms
under a cap-and-trade regulation solved via the application of Stackelberg game-theoretic
methods (Du et al., 2020); green technology investments with explicit logistics costs under
carbon tax, strict carbon limit and cap-and-trade regulations (Huang et al., 2020); green tech-
nology investments for the purposes of controlling carbon emissions under carbon tax and
cap-and-trade regulations with backordering of shortages (Mishra et al., 2020); item deteri-
oration under carbon tax and cap-and-trade regulations (Yu et al., 2020); green investment-
and promotion-dependent demand rate under carbon tax, cap-and-trade and strict carbon
limit regulations (Hasan et al., 2021); carbon abatement contracts in a supply chain with
asymmetric information under carbon tax and cap-and-trade regulations (Li & Lai, 2021);
item deterioration, imperfect quality and investments in both preservation technologies and
green technologies under a carbon tax regulation (Sepehri et al., 2021); carbon cap and
cap-and-trade regulations in a comparison between traditional supply chains and vendor-
managed inventory-based supply chains (Turken et al., 2021); consumers’ carbon sensitivity
and investments in carbon reduction technologies in a humanitarian supply chain (Fuli et al.,
2022); item deterioration in a closed-loop supply chain under a consignment stock agree-
ment and cap-and-trade regulation (Zhang et al., 2022); item deterioration, investment in
preservation technologies, partial backordering of shortages and selling price- and advertise-
ment cost-dependent demand under a carbon cap regulation (Kumar et al., 2023); geographic
disparities and synergies in carbon reduction efforts (Di et al., 2023); imperfect quality and
item deterioration in a multi-echelon supply chain under carbon tax, cap, cap-and-offset
and cap-and-trade regulations (Sebatjane, 2024c); demand forecasting via machine learning,

2.3 Analysis: research gap and contribution
Table 1 summarises key contributions from a selection of the reviewed models, highlighting
the research gap identiﬁed in this study. The table reveals a signiﬁcant lack of focus on
developing sustainable inventory models for cold items that consider carbon emissions from
transportation and storage. This presents a valuable opportunity for further research, given
the importance of cold items such as fresh produce for sustenance and vaccines for disease
prevention. Notably, there appears to be no existing study in the literature that explicitly
examines inventory replenishment and shipment decisions in a multi-echelon cold chain,
where carbon emissions stem from transportation and refrigeration (storage) activities, under
both cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset regulations. The current study aims to address this gap
in the literature, utilizing the following novel features to achieve its objectives:
• Jointly optimising inventory replenishment and shipment decisions for a two-echelon
cold chain comprised of a single warehouse and a single retail outlet.
• Incorporating capacities for both the refrigerated transportation trucks and the storage
freezers.
• Modelling carbon emissions that result from the use of the trucks and the freezers (i.e.
emissions from both the transportation and storage activities in the cold chain).
• Incorporating the fuel efﬁciency of the trucks and the energy efﬁciency of the freezers
into the carbon emissions function.
• Comparing the effectiveness (in both ﬁnancial and environmental sustainability terms)
of cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset emissions regulations.
3 Problem description
The supply chain under study is comprised of a single warehouse and a single retail outlet
responsible for the storage and selling, respectively, of a single type of item. The item in
question has to be maintained in a cold temperature environment in order to preserve its
freshness and to ensure it is safe for consumption. Consequently, the operations of the supply
chain make use of refrigerated storage units and refrigerated transportation trucks in an effort
to ensure that the item in question is kept at cold temperatures throughout the entire supply
chain and hence, the warehouse and retail outlet under study operate as a cold chain.

The operations of the proposed cold chain are depicted by Fig. 2. Cold products are
procured and stored at the warehouse echelon of the cold chain. The products are stored in
refrigerated storage (i.e. freezer) units at the warehouse in order to maintain them within
the correct temperature range. The retail echelon is used for meeting consumer demand
for the cold products and the retail echelon receives shipments of the cold products (from
the warehouse echelon) at regular intervals. The retail echelon also stores the products in
freezer units in order to maintain them at the right temperature. The freezer units are energy
intensive appliances and hence, using these freezer units results in the release of sizeable
amounts of carbon emissions. In addition to the emissions released as a result of holding (i.e.
storing) the cold inventory in freezers, the inventory replenishment activities (speciﬁcally, the
transportation activities) at both the warehouse and the retail echelons also release sizeable
amounts of carbon emissions.
The proposed cold chain inventory system, whose proﬁle over time is depicted by Fig.3,
is formulated as proﬁt maximisation problem, with the number of shipments delivered by
the warehouse echelon to the retail echelon and the order quantity for the cold products
as the decision variables. Two variants of the problems are presented, with the ﬁrst model
being developed under the assumption of a cap-and-tradeemissions regulation and the second
model being developed under the assumption of a cap-and-trade emissions regulation.
3.1 Assumptions
The mathematical formulation of the proposed cold chain models is guided by the following
assumptions:
• A single type of cold item has to be maintained in a cold temperature-controlled (i.e.
refrigerated) environment across the entire cold chain.

• Demand for the cold inventory is a deterministic constant.
• The warehouse echelon delivers an integer number of shipments of cold inventory during
the course of each warehouse cycle.
• Both the warehouse and the retail echelons make use of identical refrigerated transporta-
tion trucks (denoted as truck units) for inbound transportation.
• Each truck unit has a limited capacity.

• Both the warehouse and the retail echelons make use of identical refrigerated storage

• Each freezer unit has a limited capacity.
• Each freezer unit’s state (i.e. whether it is turned on or off) does not change throughout
a replenishment cycle.

a retail echelon, and consequently, the relevant models for each of these two echelons are
derived separately.

5 Analysis under diﬀerent carbon cap regulations
Some governments have mandated carbon cap emissions regulations whereby caps are
imposed on the amount of carbon emissions that entities can generate (as a result of their
manufacturing operations) and release into the atmosphere. This is mandated in an effort to
incentive ﬁrms to reduce their carbon footprints. The proposed cold chain is analysed under
two variants of the carbon cap emissions regulation, namely, cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset
emissions regulations.
5.1 Model I: carbon cap-and-trade
For the cap-and-trade-model, the cold chain is subjected to a carbon emissions cap of C
(per unit time) and there is a carbon emission trading scheme in place whereby excess
carbon emissions capacity is sold or additional carbon emissions capacity is purchased.

A real-world case study is examined to demonstrate the practicality of the proposed cold

6.1 Case study
A case study is presented to demonstrate the real-world applications of the proposed inventory
model, as depicted in Fig.6. The study examines a South African frozen seafood supply chain,
which includes a wild capture ﬁshing company, a ﬁsh processing facility, a distribution centre,
and a retail outlet. Part of this supply chain operates as a cold chain, speciﬁcally after the ﬁsh
has been processed and packaged. The wild capture ﬁshing company acts as the ﬁsh supplier,
using a wild capture method to catch live ﬁsh. The next stage in the value chain involves
processing the live ﬁsh into packaged, ready-for-sale frozen ﬁsh products such as hake and
calamari. These frozen ﬁsh products are then stored in a cold storage facility for distribution
to various retail stores. Ultimately, consumers purchase the frozen ﬁsh products from retail
outlets.

6.4 Cold chain collaboration analysis
A centralised supply chain optimisation approach is used to solve the two models considered
in this study. Under a centralised supply chain, it is assumed that both members of the
supply chain collaborate in an effort to maximise proﬁt and minimise emissions for the entire
supply chain. In other words, the two members of the cold chain do not decide on optimal
inventory replenishment decision in order to maximise their own individual proﬁt, but rather
to maximise the joint proﬁt for the entire cold chain. In an effort to show the ﬁnancial and
environmental beneﬁts of a centralised cold chain structure over a decentralised structure
whereby the aim of members of the cold chain operate in isolation in order to optimise their
individual proﬁt and emissions, a comparison is made between the two proposed models
under both centralised and decentralised cold chain settings and the results are presented in
Table 4.
Because the proposed cold chain has two members, namely, a warehouse and a retail
outlet, two versions of the decentralised cold chain structure are considered as per Table 4.
The optimal solution for the ﬁrst version of the decentralised structure, termed the ‘warehouse
led’, is obtained by assuming that the warehouse serves as the leader of the cold chain and in
this instance, the warehouse optimises its own inventory replenishment policy to maximise
its own proﬁt and then passes these policy down to the retail outlet. Conversely, the optimal
solution for the second version of the decentralised structure, termed the ‘retail led’, is
obtained by assuming that the retail outlet serves as the leader of the cold chain and makes
replenishment decision to maximise the individual retail proﬁt.
Based on the results in Table 4, the centralised optimisation approach performs better
that the decentralised approach not only in ﬁnancial terms, but also from a sustainability
point of view. Regardless of the emissions regulation or the cold chain leader, the centralised
approach results in the highest possible cold chain proﬁt and lowest possible carbon emis-
sions. Although the individual proﬁts for each of the supply chain members reach their highest
levels when each member serves as a leader, this comes at the expense of the other cold chain
and consequently, the total proﬁt for the cold chain is also compromised. For instance, con-
sider a scenario under the cap-and-trade emissions regulation where the warehouse is the
leader of the cold chain and in this case, a warehouse proﬁt of 70 562 $/year is obtained.
However, because the warehouse’s aim is to maximise its individual proﬁt, the retail outlet is
affected by the warehouse’s decision and the retail proﬁt under this scenario is 75 292 $/year
and the entire cold chain makes a proﬁt of 142 371 $/year. This does not compare well with a
centralised optimisation approach under the same scenario which leads to a higher cold chain
proﬁt of 155 047$/year. Moreover, the emissions released under the centralised approach are
almost 10% less despite the higher proﬁt and this can be attributed to collaboration between
the cold chain members.

• The optimal number of shipments delivered to the retail outlet (n∗) is by far the most
robust output, responding only to large negative changes and only for the cap-and-trade
model, speciﬁcally only responding to 50% decreases in the truck unit capacity (Tc) and
the fuel efﬁciency of the truck units (M PG0) and in both of these cases, n∗experienced a
60% increase. With regards to Tc, the increase can be explained by the fact that opting to
use trucks with smaller capacities would require more trips (and thus, more shipments) in
order to meet the same demand rate. With regards to M PG0, the use of less fuel efﬁcient
trucks would require smaller lot sizes (in an effort to improve fuel efﬁciency because the
weight of the lots has a direct impact of fuel efﬁciency) and hence, the trucks would need
to make more trips. When it comes to the cap-and-trade model, n∗proved to be even
more robust and it did not respond at all for any of the changes tested.
• The optimal order quantity for the retail echelon (Q∗
r ) is also very robust, albeit not as
robust as n∗. Q∗
r was most sensitive to changes in Tc, with a 50% increase in Tc resulting
in a 50% increase in Q∗
r for the cap-and-trade model, whereas the resulting percentage
increase was slightly lower at 12.5% for the cap-and-trade model. This observation can
be explained by the fact that the trucks operate on a full-truckload (FTL) shipping mode
and therefore, it is more economical to ﬁll the truck units to capacity in an effort to take
advantage of economies of scale.
• Changes to all the parameters tested had an impact on the optimal total cold chain proﬁt
(T P∗
cc),with Tc, Ft (i.e.ﬁxedcostofusingatruck)and M PG0 havingthehighestimpacts.
In general, T P∗
cc decreases with decreasing values of Tc and M PG0 and increasing values
of Ft. With regards to Tc, T P∗
cc decreases with Tc because the use of smaller capacity
trucks would require more trips to be made into to meet the same demand and the
additional ﬁxed costs for transportation and order placement (due to more trips) would
lead to reduced proﬁts, as demonstrated by decreases of 19% and 20% in T P∗
cc, in the case
of the cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset models, respectively, following a 50% decrease
in Tc. Likewise, the use of less fuel efﬁcient truck units would require the lot sizes to be
smaller and consequently, the proﬁt made would be reduced because more trips would
need to be made and hence, T P∗
cc experienced decreases of 18% and 29%, in the case of
the cap-and-trade and cap-and-offset models, respectively, following a 50% increase in
M PG0. The decreases in T P∗
cc following increases in Ft can be explained by the fact that
the ﬁxed transportation cost is one of the two components that make up the replenishment
cost and hence, there is a linearly decreasing relationship between the replenishment cost
and the proﬁt.
• The optimal amount of carbon emissions released across the cold chain (CO∗
2cc) is sensi-
tive to changes in Tc, b (i.e. freezer unit capacity), M PG0, C EGF (i.e. amount emissions
released from burning a gallon of fuel) and T EC F (i.e. energy consumption of a freezer
unit), with CO∗
2cc being more sensitive to changes in M PG0 and C EGF. For instance,
50% decreases in M PG0 and C EGF result in the release of 262% higher and 46%
lower, respectively, carbon emissions in the case of the cap-and-trade model, whereas
in the case of the cap-and-trade model, 264% higher and 40% lower carbon emissions
are released following 50% decreases in M PG0 and C EGF, respectively. Both of these
factors (i.e. M PG0 and C EGF) are related to the use of fossil fuels for powering the
transportation tucks. With regards to M PG0, the use of less fuel efﬁcient trucks would
lead to more gallons of fuel being required to transport a given quantity of cold inventory
and consequently, higher quantities carbon emissions are released. C EGF is thus akin
to the efﬁciency of the fuel source being used to power the trucks and in general terms,
if the fuel source is more efﬁcient (as characterised by lower C EGF values) then less
emissions are released.

6.6 Managerial insights
Based on the results of the sensitivity analysis, operations and supply chain management
practitioners in cold chains should take the following measures in order to simultaneously
maximise proﬁts and reduce carbon emissions:
• Based on the observation that an increase in Tc leads to an increase in T Pcc and a decrease
in CO2cc, operations ans supply chain managers should make use of transportation trucks
with larger capacities. By so doing, fewer trips will have to be made and consequently, the
amount of carbon emissions released will be lower. Moreover, owing to the fact that the
shipping mode used by the trucks is FTL transportation, ﬁlling up the trucks to capacity
will lower the shipping costs due to economies of scale and consequently, the proﬁt
generated across the cold chain will be increased. However, the use of larger-capacity
trucks comes with its own set of caveats, for instance, larger trucks are often powered by
relatively larger-capacity engines which result in the release of more emissions. When
deciding on truck capacity, managers should weigh this trade-off carefully. Additionally,
they need to take into account the quantity of emissions produced for each unit of cold
products transported.
• Similarly,managersshouldmakeuseoffreezerunits(forstoragepurposes)withrelatively
larger capacities. This recommendation is based on the observation that an increase in b
leads to an increase in T Pcc and a decrease in CO2cc. Economies of scale will not only
lower the costs (and thus, increase proﬁt) but they will also ensure that the emissions are
spread out over a large number of cold inventory items in storage.
• Given that an increase in M PG0 leads to an increase in T Pcc and a decrease in CO2cc,
managers should use trucks that are as fuel efﬁcient as possible. This will ensure that
the trucks’ fuel tanks do not have to be ﬁlled up as often and consequently, the cost of
transportation will decrease and hence, the proﬁt will increase. In addition, the use of
more fuel efﬁcient trucks means that less carbon emissions are released. Mangers should
also consider using hybrid or pure electric vehicles as part of their truck ﬂeet because
these types of vehicles are generally more fuel efﬁcient. The decision to switch from
diesel and petrol trucks to hybrid and electric trucks should not only consider the fuel
efﬁciency but also the initial capital investment because hybrid and electric trucks are
generally more expensive to purchase.Managers should weigh the initial higher expenses
against potential future cost reductions when considering hybrid or electric trucks. While
hybrid vehicles offer reduced fuel expenses over time, electric trucks eliminate fuel costs
entirely, despite their higher upfront cost.
• Owing to the fact that increases in both C EGF and T EC F lead to increased T Pcc
and decreased CO2cc, managers should ensure that both their fuel sources and their
freezers are as efﬁcient as possible. By so doing, the amount of energy required either
to power the trucks or to refrigerate the cold inventory will be lower and consequently,
both the energy costs and the amount of emissions released will be lower. To achieve
this objective, managers can use optimise their fuel sources by incorporating alternative
sources of energy for powering not only the vehicles but also the freezers. For instance,
managers can invest in freezers powered by natural gas which is not only generally more
efﬁcient at generating heat than electricity but is also a renewable energy source, meaning
that it is better for the environment.
• Based on the observation that centralised cold chain structures result in higher proﬁts and
lowercarbonemissionsthandecentralisedcoldchainstructures,supplychainmanagersat
different entities within the cold chain should not make inventory replenishment decisions

carbon emissions. Instead, such decisions should be taken centrally for the beneﬁt of the
entire cold chain.
6.7 Potential implementation barriers and associated solutions
To achieve the intended outcomes, the proposed managerial insights must be implemented
effectively. Managers should also anticipate potential barriers to implementation and devise
strategies to overcome them. The following implementation barriers and their corresponding
solutions should be considered:
• Regarding the recommendation to utilise large-capacity refrigerated trucks, a possible
barrier is the substantial upfront investment required for purchasing these vehicles to
distribute cold inventory. One way to circumvent this issue is to explore leasing options
for these trucks (if available). Alternatively, outsourcing distribution to a third-party
logistics provider could shift the initial purchasing cost to that company.
• Implementing the suggestion to employ full electric vehicle (EV) trucks may be hin-
dered by their comparatively high purchase price versus internal combustion engine
(ICE) vehicles. To address this, managers could opt for a diverse ﬂeet comprising hybrid
electric vehicles (HEV) and plug-in hybrid electric (PHEV) trucks for distribution. HEV
and PHEV trucks typically cost less than their pure EV counterparts because they use
smaller capacity batteries. While HEV and PHEV trucks still produce carbon emissions,
the amounts are lower than their ICE-powered equivalents, making them a worthwhile
investment given their modest price difference.
• The recommendation to use alternative energy sources for powering freezers and other
refrigeration equipment may also face the barrier of high capital investment. Govern-
ment incentives for renewable energy adoption, such as rebates or tax write-offs, can
serve as potential solutions to overcome this barrier. These incentives can help offset the
substantial initial investment.
7 Conclusion
This paper proposed two models for optimising inventory replenishment and shipment
strategies in a two-echelon cold chain, with a single warehouse and a single retail outlet.
Refrigeration plays a vital role in the cold chain, maintaining the safety, quality and fresh-
ness of the cold inventory items along the entire chain. Owing to its energy-intensive nature,
refrigeration results in the release of signiﬁcant amounts of carbon emissions. In addition to
refrigeration, transportation also plays an important role in the cold chain ensuring that the
cold inventory items are transported (in refrigerated trucks) between the echelons of the cold
chain at correct temperature. Similarly, transportation also results in the release of signiﬁcant
amounts of carbon emissions. Both models are formulated as proﬁt-maximisation problems,
with the retail echelon’s order quantity and the number of shipments of the cold items between
the two echelons being the explicit decision variables. The major difference between the two
models is the type of carbon emissions regulation under consideration, with the ﬁrst model
considering a cap-and-trade regulation, while the second one considers a cap-and-trade reg-
ulation (and by extension, the second model has the amount of carbon emissions that can be
abated by investing in carbon offset projects as an additional explicit decision variable).

Through a numerical examples, the results showed that the cap-and-trade regulation per-
forms slightly better than the cap-and-trade regulation in ﬁnancial terms, with a 0.98% higher
total proﬁt. Conversely, the cap-and-trade regulation performs slightly better than the cap-
and-traderegulationintermsofenvironmentalsustainabilitycriteria,resultingin4.38%lower
amounts of carbon emissions. In addition factors such as truck capacity, freezer capacity, fuel
economy of the trucks, efﬁciency of the fuel source and efﬁciency of the freezer units have
the greatest impact on both the proﬁt and carbon emissions generated across the cold chain.
Consequently, operations and supply chain managers can exploit any of these factors in an
effort to improve the cold chain’s performance in terms of both ﬁnancial and sustainability
criteria.
The proposed inventory models for a two-echelon cold chain can be extended along
different directions to create new models. These future research directions include, but are
not limited to, incorporating different carbon emissions regulations such as carbon tax and
strict carbon limit regulations (Huang et al., 2020; Hasan et al., 2021); different shipping
modes such as less-than-truckload (LTL) in addition to FTL (Konur & Schaefer, 2014);
multiple cold items in the system (Bozorgi, 2016); price-, stock- and freshness-dependent
demand (Feng et al., 2017); consignment stock agreement (Braglia & Zavanella, 2003); and
co-located supply chain members (Turken et al., 2021).
