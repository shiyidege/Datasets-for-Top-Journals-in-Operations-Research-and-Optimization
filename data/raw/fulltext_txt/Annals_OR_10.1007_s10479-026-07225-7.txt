Abstract
The determination of the most important states in Markov Decision Processes has recently
gained interest due to its applicability in increasing both the performance and the explain-
ability of Reinforcement Learning algorithms. In this paper, we extend the concept of
Reconstructed Transition Graphs from simple unweighted graphs to advanced notions of
weighted Multigraphs and Multiplexes to better represent Markov Decision Processes and
identify the most important states. To this end, we adapt the classical centrality measures
(betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector) to our proposed frameworks and delineate the the-
oretical assumptions required to ensure their existence and uniqueness. Compared to other
graph-based measures available in the literature, this novel approach can take into account the
different rewards and transform them into weights speciﬁc to the task. Computational exper-
iments indicate the superior capacity of these measures in the detection of dangerous states
and high-reward situations for non-sparse reward environments compared to state-of-the-art
methodologies. Moreover, a statistical analysis carried out allowed to discover signiﬁcant
interconnections between the newly deﬁned centrality measure structures and Q-value-based
measures that are helpful for the recognition of critical states.

1 Introduction
Inrecentyears,anoutstandinginterestinReinforcementLearning(RL)algorithmsforsolving
sequential decision problems modelled as Markov Decision Processes (MDP) is observed
owing to the exceptional results achieved (Deng & Lin, 2023; Mnih et al., 2013; Silver et al.,
2017). RL methodologies are capable of learning complex tasks by iterating trial-and-error
interactions between an agent and environment (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Thus, at the end
of the training phase, the RL agent is able to perform optimally in any particular situation
according to the learned policy. However, reasoning for a particular selection of actions is
not always straightforward or simple to interpret. This phenomenon is also worsened by the
existence of delayed rewards, meaning that important actions are not always associated with
positive rewards (Sequeira & Gervasio, 2020). Therefore, given their multiple applications in
critical ﬁelds, such as medicine and transportation (Milani et al., 2023; Tjoa & Guan, 2021),
the need for explanations of these black box models has gained importance.
For this reason, the focus of research has moved to Explainable Reinforcement Learning
(XRL), which aims to generate explanations for the agent’s underlying mechanisms in solv-
ing a particular task (Sequeira & Gervasio, 2020). Speciﬁcally, it is fundamental to detect
the elements that could be considered in explanations, among others, subgoals and distal
information (Madumal et al., 2020; Menache et al., 2002; Milani et al., 2023). Among all the
different information, recognising when a particular situation is dangerous could also help
human users be aware of the risk, hence, to pay more attention to the choices suggested by
the agent and correct them if not considered optimal (Davoodi & Komeili, 2021). In fact, the
identiﬁcation of precarious states that could determine completely different outcomes has
major relevance not only in the context of understanding why the agent selected a particular
action, but also for the improvement of the learning phase (Mendonca et al., 2019).
Therefore, the identiﬁcation of speciﬁcally important states can help the agent learn faster
the correct policy through skill acquisition processes and balance also the exploration -
exploitation trade off during the training (Mendonca et al., 2019; Rad et al., 2010). Thus,
the states that have been considered important in the literature refer to the bottlenecks, or
subgoal states, associated to the transitions of the states in the MDPs. For these reasons, a
wide range of measures has been developed for the recognition of these bottleneck states.
In this work, we adopt a broader, task-driven deﬁnition: an important state is any state
that is structurally critical in the MDP’s transition topology and signiﬁcantly changes the
earned return. To this end, particularly interesting is a speciﬁc group of measures that relies
on the analysis of the topological information contained in the Reconstructed Transition
Graphs, i.e., graphs where the nodes represent the states of an MDP, and connections are
included if and only if there exists a transition between the two adjacent nodes (Mendonca
et al., 2019; Moradi et al., 2010; Rad et al., 2010). However, these studies have limited their
methodologies to the adoption of simple unweighted graphs, restricting their application to
sparse-reward MDPs.
In this paper, we extend the state-of-the-art methods that were previously considered only
for simple unweighted Reconstructed Transition Graphs. Contrary to previous modelling
approaches, we deﬁne the weighted Reconstructed Transition Multigraph and Multiplex by
adapting the aforementioned concepts to these advanced network structures. In these set-
tings, we studied the existence and uniqueness conditions for classical centrality measures
(betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector) to determine the main assumptions required to
ensure the well-posedness of the proposed methodologies. In addition to providing the the-
oretical analysis of these novel frameworks, we compare these measures with those in the

2 Background
In this section, we present the theoretical background necessary for the comprehension of the
proposed methodology. Speciﬁcally, we ﬁrst introduce the deﬁnitions relative to the MDP
scenario and, subsequently, the basic characterisation of networks through graph theory and
the corresponding centrality measures that can be computed.
2.1 Markov decision processes
In the sequel, we describe the concept of MDPs and the relevant notations. In particular, we
restrict our description to ﬁnite- and discrete-time MDPs. For an introduction, it is possible to
refer to the classical iterative approach consisting of the interaction between an agent and an
environment that can be associated with the usual Reinforcement Learning context (Sutton
& Barto, 2018).
At each time step t = 0, 1, . . . , T the agent measures the actual state st ∈S from
the environment, and then takes an action at ∈A depending on it, where S and A are
the state and action spaces, respectively. Consequently, this choice will generate an update
of the state presented by the environment st+1 ∈S and a numerical reward rt+1 ∈R
depending on this transition. In detail, the reward is produced by considering a speciﬁc
function r : S × A × S →R that associates to each state, action performed and next state a
compensation, i.e., r(st, at, st+1) = rt+1. In this manner, we can also consider the possible
effects of probabilistic dependencies related to state transitions (Feinberg & Shwartz, 2012).
Therefore, it is fundamental to introduce a formal transition function for passing from each
state to the others: T : S × A →S. Finally, we must deﬁne the discount factor γ ∈[0, 1],
which is useful for identifying the objective function of the general framework. In fact, the
agent’s goal is to maximize the revenue obtained during the episodes, which is represented
by the return Gt = T −t−1

3 Literature review
The relevance of detecting important states in RL has increased given its possible application
in both explaining the choices of trained agents and summarising salient moments in a
trajectory (Amir & Amir, 2018; Amitai et al., 2024; Sequeira & Gervasio, 2020). Moreover,
the number of possible choices for the identiﬁcation of fundamental states has also increased,
leading to an explosion of different adopted measures (Milani et al., 2024). In particular, we
can classify importance measures according to their input. We can divide these into Q-value-
based when they take into account the Q-values of the RL agents and graph-based when
they only consider the topology of the reconstructed graph obtained during training. For the
former class, the focus is on the values of performing an action in a speciﬁc state; hence, on
the speciﬁc knowledge that the agent was able to learn, while in the latter class, the attention
is strictly related to the explored structure of the MDPs, relying only on the connections
between the states through the chosen actions.
In the ﬁrst group of measures, we always depend on the Q-value function obtained during
the training. However, this comes with the limitation that it can only be applied to value-
based RL approaches. Furthermore, these measures are agent-dependent, i.e., different agents
trained using the same algorithm can generate a diverse set of important states.
The Q-value-based class includes a set of simple measures consisting of the difference
between the highest Q-value and its minimum (Amir et al., 2016; Torrey & Taylor, 2013),
or second-highest value (Bellemare et al., 2016; Huber et al., 2021), or the mean value
(Huang et al., 2018) to evaluate the quality of the best action or the difference between the
second-largest Q-value and the minimum for identifying the criticality of the state (Milani et
al., 2024). Nonetheless, these measures consider only the static values of each state; hence,
they do not include the number of visits or time at which they are selected. Therefore, it is
possible to use them in the iterated approach proposed by Milani et al. (2024) to combine
the aforementioned aspects with their initial characteristics.
From these measures, more advanced versions can be derived by combining multiple
innovative methodologies. For example, a “lazy” measure can be deﬁned as the difference
between the highest Q-value and the expected Q-value following a default (lazy) policy (Jacq
et al., 2022), or a state-mask can be generated by recognizing the states that will entail higher
changes in the ﬁnal accumulated reward if a non-optimal action is performed there (Cheng
et al., 2023). A similar approach involves identifying the importance of a state depending on
how much the difference in the Q-value is when one or more of its features are perturbed
(Wang et al., 2023).
Graph-based measures are principally applied to the sub-ﬁeld of automatic skill acqui-
sition, where relevant actions need to be identiﬁed (Mendonca et al., 2019). The core idea
of these methodologies is to reconstruct a graph from the trajectories stored during training,
where the states correspond to the nodes and actions to the edges.

In the ﬁrst pioneering work produced in this context, Digney, 1998 proposed detecting the
salient states according to the number of visits and the gradient of the reinforcement signals
received. Though not clearly stated, this concept resembles the node centrality deﬁnitions for
complex network problems (Bollobás, 1998; Freeman, 1977). Similarly, ¸Sim¸sek & Barto,
2004 considered the identiﬁcation of a novelty measure strictly related to the number of visits
to the state.
Later, the same authors focused on the application of betweenness centrality measures in
Reconstructed Transition Graphs (directed and unweighted) (¸Sim¸sek & Barto, 2007, 2008;
Simsek, 2008). However, multiple options for identifying important states through classical
centrality measures are available for the same type of graphs (Mendonca et al., 2019). Speciﬁc
measures were implemented in addition to these standard formulations. Examples are the
connection graph stability proposed by Rad et al. (2010), where the authors deﬁne centrality
depending on both the length of the shortest path passing on a particular state for each
starting and ending node and the total number of shortest paths linking the same two vertices.
Similarly, Moradi et al., 2010 extended the previous idea by including the importance of
neighbouring nodes to consider not only the local but also the global information.
However, previous attempts to discover relevant states in MDPs involved the adoption
of an unweighted graph, thereby losing information from the reward function. An approach
that attempts to go in the direction of weighted graph analysis is Shoeleh & Asadpour, 2017,
where a combination of the usual transition graph and distance graph is studied. The relevant
advantage of this methodology is its ability to address continuous RL problems (Shoeleh &
Asadpour, 2017, 2019).
Instead of considering the different shortest paths or distances, it is possible to evaluate
the connectivity importance of the Reconstructed Transition Graph by applying speciﬁc clus-
tering algorithms (Evans & ¸Sim¸sek, 2023; Kazemitabar & Beigy, 2009; Mannor et al., 2004;
Shen et al., 2006; ¸Sim¸sek et al., 2005) or by evaluating eigenvector centrality (Taghizadeh &
Beigy, 2013). A completely different approach is to build a Max Flow/Min Cut problem from
the weighted graph to detect the bottleneck states, as proposed by Menache et al. (2002).
After reporting the different methodologies presented in the literature, we describe our
proposed techniques to extend the concept of Reconstructed Transition Graphs to their rep-
resentation as multigraphs and multiplex graphs.
4 Methodology
In the following, we introduce a representation of the Reconstructed Transition Graphs using
multigraphs and multiplexes. In detail, we specify the conditions required for the existence
and uniqueness of the associated topological measures and the general rules of thumb that
will be practically applied in the experimentation phase, as reported in the subsequent section.
4.1 Multigraph approach for detecting important states in MDPs
State-of-the-art methodologies for the identiﬁcation of the important states in MDPs dealing
with the application of centrality measures in Reconstructed Transition Graphs focus, as
previously mentioned in Section 3, on using simple unweighted graphs. Speciﬁcally, they
avoid overlapping between edges and do not allow the presence of weights. Hence, a large
part of the information is lost during the translation phase from the usual MDP exploration
and the creation of the graph. We extend the classical formulation for this problem in order

4.2 The reconstructed transition multiplex and its centrality measures
In this section, we propose a different structure for the Reconstructed Transition Graphs using
Multilayer Graphs. We decompose each state by representing its projection in a speciﬁc layer,
which creates an avenue for analyzing in detail the importance of that particular component
of the state. Moreover, it provides a different set of centrality measures that are derived from
the application of the classical measures in these speciﬁc graphs.
ThemainideaofthisapproachconsistsofconstructingtheReconstructedTransitionGraph
as a particular kind of multilayer graph, i.e., the multiplex, to produce the Reconstructed
Transition Multiplex. Thereby, the only connections between diverse layers link the nodes
indicating the same information at different levels. Each of the aforementioned layers is
determined by a speciﬁc action that can be selected by the agent. Therefore, the total number
of layers in the Reconstructed Transition Multiplex is equal to the cardinality of the action
set, |A|. Maintaining the usual deﬁnition that associates the states of the MDP to the nodes
in the graph, it is clear that in the multiplex formulation, the vertices determine a couple of
123

Annals of Operations Research
states and actions. In fact, the selected node represents an instantiation of the state variables
and its layer indicates the action attached to it. Thereby, while studying this type of graph,
the vertices assume the connotation of state-action pairs.
Additionally, in a deterministic environment, there is at most only one outgoing edge each
state, since the arc is relative to the action layer where it is lying. Indeed, during the construc-
tion process of the Reconstructed Transition Multiplex, every time a new state is discovered,
all the layers are updated by adding in each of them its decomposed representations, while,
if an action has been performed for the ﬁrst time in this freshly encountered state, then it
is generated an edge, connecting the initial state and the subsequent one, only on the layer
corresponding to the chosen action. A complete overview of the creation of the Reconstructed
Transition Multiplex is later discussed in detail.
From this initial identiﬁcation of the design of the proposed graph, we provide the fol-
lowing mathematical deﬁnition of the Reconstructed Transition Multiplex.

The relationship between multigraphs and multiplexes is very strict. In particular, in the
context of the Reconstructed Transition Graph, it can be noted that the majority of the
information required for building both networks is the same. Hence, it is possible to straight-
forwardly construct the multiplex from the multigraph version and vice versa. Additionally,
the Reconstructed Transition Multiplex is the decomposed representation of the Recon-
structed Transition Multigraph: if the graphs at each layer for the former network are uniﬁed,
then the multigraph version is obtained. However, this ﬂattening is possible but at the cost of
losing information.
A major aspect that has not been addressed so far concerns the inter-layer connections that
link together the different decomposed states. With the aim of no loss in the data connected
to the inter-layer edges, it is necessary to set their weights equal to zero. Thereby, it can be
operated equivalently on the collapsed multiplex or the multigraph (Berlingerio et al., 2011;
Coscia et al., 2013).
Despite the two aforementioned structures modelling the same problem, the adoption of
the Reconstructed Transition Multiplex can provide deeper insights into the importance of
each state. This facet is conveyed by the association of the state with a speciﬁc action, lead-
ing in this way to the evaluation of this couple of information. Hereby, it can be remarked a
parallelism between the V- and Q-value functions in the MDPs and the centrality measures
computed in the Reconstructed Transition Multigraphs and Multiplexes. While the V-function
computes the value of the states, i.e., the maximum of the Q-values, thus, considering the
best-case scenario for the speciﬁc state, the Q-function analyzes the values linked to the
different combinations of state and actions, leading to collecting broader information. In the
same manner, the multiplex structure provides a comprehensive knowledge of the vertexes at
different levels, whereas the multigraph representation only summarizes the general under-
standing of the state. Therefore, calculating the centrality measures in the multiplexes will
123

Annals of Operations Research
produce a value that can be, theoretically, linked to a decomposition of the same measures
computed in the relative multigraph.
For computing the centrality measures in the multiplexes, the fact that they can be regarded
as multigraphs can be exploited. Hence, it is possible to take advantage of the equivalence
between the two graphs for the deﬁnition of the weights for the speciﬁc centrality measures.
In this way, the existence conditions found in the previous section can be applied in these
settings directly. In the following, is reported a detailed analysis of the different requirements
needed for each measure.
Starting with the betweenness centrality, it is helpful to recall that the function applied for
the calculation of the weights for the multigraphs is: w(r) = −r. In this situation, this weight
function can be used without any other conditions due to the formulation of the multiplex
as a multigraph. In fact, the weights of the inter-layer connections are set by default to 0,
meaning that the shortest path in both types of graphs is identical. Thus, Theorem 3 holds
also for the Reconstructed Transition Multiplexes.
A similar procedure can be used for the closeness centrality. Nevertheless, it is crucial to
bear in mind that the distances of the networks have to be strictly positive. Hence, the inter-
layer weights have to be modiﬁed to allow the calculation of the length of the shortest path.
Therefore, the inter-layer weights have been ﬁxed at the small constant value ϵI > 0 such
that it is sufﬁciently large to not massively inﬂuence the calculation of the overall closeness
measure.
Furthermore, the betweenness and closeness measures can be applied directly to each
layer graph. In this way, the problem of the manipulation of the inter-layer values is avoided.
On the other hand, the analysis of each decomposed graph can only provide limited details
on the overall structure since these projected graphs are sparse (as already explained at the
beginning of the section). So, there is a high number of isolated nodes. For this reason, we
decided to deﬁne the inter-layer weights as ϵI to maintain as much data collected in the MDPs
as possible.
However, the particularity of the structure highlighted helps ensure the existence of the
closeness centrality in the multiplex version of the Reconstructed Transition Graphs. Indeed,
from Theorem 4 and Corollary 1, it can be noted how the most stringent hypothesis concerns
the existence (and, for the latter one, uniqueness) of edges with an associated nonnegative
reward. These speciﬁc conditions are trivially satisﬁed thanks to the sparsity of the generated
multiplex. Thus, the well-posedness of the closeness centrality is posed by calculating the
intra-layer weights through the weight function wC.
The situation gets more complicated in the study of the eigenvector centralities applied
in the multiplexes. As reported in Section 2.2, a wide range of methodologies that compute
eigenvector measures can be considered. In this work, we analyze three different approaches:
the uniform eigenvector centrality (Solá et al., 2013), the versatility (fourth-order) (De
Domenico et al., 2015, 2013), and the f −eigenvector centrality (third-order) (Tudisco et
al., 2018). As settled for the previous centrality measures, also in this case the usual weight

5 Computational experiments
We test the centrality measures proposed for the Reconstructed Transition Multigraph and
Multiplex and compare them with those used in the simple (unweighted) Reconstructed
Transition Graphs. A brief summary of the information about the measures introduced in the
paper is shown in Table 1.
For the evaluation of the quality of the measures, we considered a customized version of the
Taxi problem (Dietterich, 2000). The general problem is derived from a real-world scenario,
where a taxi has to pick up a passenger in one of the four possible positions (designated
with a speciﬁc colour on the map) and bring them to the destination, in one of the remaining
coloured positions. Therefore, the actions of the agent are possible movements (going South,
North, East, and West) and picking up and dropping off the passenger. The agent has full
knowledge of the scenario; hence, the state representation includes its position, deﬁned as row
and column, passenger location, and destination. At each time step, the reward gained by the
taxi is −1. Only in particular other situations, it will be punished or rewarded: respectively,
it earns −10 for each pick up or drop off in the wrong situation and 100 for bringing the
passenger to the destination.
Based on this standard formulation, we introduced minor modiﬁcations to extend the
problem formulation. In detail, the changes concern the possibility of modifying the map
according to the user input and the introduction of speciﬁc high-trafﬁc locations that, if
visited, will delay the taxi. This phenomenon was formalised by inducing a negative reward
(−50) for actions leading to these positions. Therefore, the task of the agent is more complex
and closer to simulating real-world situations.

5.2 Multiplex results
From the same training trajectories used for the generation of the Reconstructed Transition
Multigraphs, we create the multiplex versions of the corresponding graphs. In particular,
for these networks, the weights of the inter-layer connections were ﬁxed to small constants:
ϵI = 0.01. This choice is expected to have only a limited effect on the proposed measures.
123

Annals of Operations Research
For betweenness and closeness centrality, ϵI affects shortest-path computations only when
inter-layer transitions are involved. For the versatility measure, it determines the relative
weight of the inter-layer couplings in the supra-adjacency matrix, and, thus, the strength of
the connection between the decomposed states across different layers. On the other hand,
the f −eigenvector centrality does not depend explicitly on inter-layer weights in its original
formulation. Indeed, its dependence is only indirect, through the self-loops added to terminal
nodes with the same weighting in order to ensure its existence and uniqueness. Therefore,
the effect of this choice on this measure is expected to be negligible.
In this case, the computation of the centrality measures is performed in the overall mul-
tilayer graph and not in the singular layers. This particular choice is derived by the general
structure of the Reconstructed Transition Multiplex: as stated in Section 4.2, for each decom-
posed state we will have at most one edge in each layer. Therefore, we limited the study
to calculate the different measures in the general multiplex. Then, we also evaluated the
agglomerated importance for each complete state, i.e., we sum the corresponding values for
each layer of the same state and rescale between 0 and 1.
As previously done for the multigraphs, we split the description of the results into before
and after picking up the passenger. In detail, we present the outcomes of the computation of
the centrality measures before letting the client in the taxi and after that in Fig. 9 and Fig. 11,
respectively.

closeness maps is in the position where the passenger is waiting. However, given the division

choice. This is due to the general structure of the multiplex that we have generated. In detail,
since both those measures rely on shortest paths, it can be easily deduced that the values

This pipeline can provide insights into the most useful measures that can be applied to each
particular problem. Moreover, it allows us to link together the different concepts of central-

In order to determine the most useful measures between all the proposed ones and the ones

which measures are summarizing similar information. Hence, we can select just a subset of all
the available methodologies to recognize the required fundamental states. Furthermore, we

groups and for gaining practical insights on the graph-based ones. While for the Q-value-

meaning of the attributed relevance for the graph-based is not always clear. In fact, in the latter
case, we are principally relying on measuring the topological structure of the Reconstructed
Transition Graphs, which do not have any direct translation in the MDP context. Therefore,
we included the action gap, advising, mean, difference measures and their corresponding
iterated formulations in this statistical study.
The correlation matrix is reported in Fig. 15. The ﬁrst interesting ﬁnding is the existence
of strong positive correlations between the betweenness centrality in simple graphs and in the
multiplex. The same results are found with the closeness centrality. This fact highlights how
by calculating the graph measures in the multiplex can lead to a dispersion of the information
to the extent that it is on the same level of not including any data relative to the rewards
earned.
Itisalsointerestingtopointoutthattheeigenvectormeasuresare,amongall,lesscorrelated
with the other ones. The only exception is the f −eigenvector centrality, which, instead,
presents interesting relationships. In particular, other than the expected high correlation with
the closeness measures in the multigraphs and multiplex, it shows a medium level (> 0.50)
of interaction with Q-value-based measures. As we outlined in the previous subsection, the
correlation coefﬁcients with the advising and the difference measures conﬁrm the ability
of the f −eigenvector measure to discriminate the important states in terms of (sub-)goal
completion and avoidance of dangerous states. Thus, we can just evaluate this measure in
order to have a wider comprehension of the relevant information for the tackled MDP.
Another remark concerns the scarcity of correlation between the graph-based and Q-
value-based measures. Only the aforementioned f −eigenvector measure and the closeness
centrality report signiﬁcant values. For all the other ones, we notice a lack of interaction
between the two groups. However, this particular result can be also justiﬁed by the focus
of each class. In fact, while the Q-value-based are strictly related to the trained agent, the
graph-based are only in part altered by the choice of the algorithm used for the solution of
the problem and the training phase. For the latter one, the only relevant information is how
large the exploration of the agent in the MDP is.
From the correlation analysis, we can conﬁrm the necessity of constructing a speciﬁc
measure that could couple together the information relative to both the MDP and the trained
agent. Speciﬁcally, it could be beneﬁcial to include in the weight formulation of the proposed
graphs the Q-values learned. In this way, it would be possible to give insights into the
knowledge stored by the agent and not only linked to the environment.
The main goal of the paper is to contribute in deﬁning new measures based on weighted
multigraphs and multiplexes, identifying the theoretical guarantees for their existence and
uniqueness and only testing in a speciﬁc problem their advantages compared to the measures
present in the literature. However, it is fundamental to mention that we tested these measures
on a single problem. This is a relevant limitation and it will be important to generalize these
results to other MDPs, in order to validate the subset of the most useful measures.

6 Conclusion
In this paper, we introduce a new method for representing MDPs using weighted multigraphs
and multiplexes as Reconstructed Transition Graphs. The purpose of this representation is
to improve the detection of the most relevant states in MDPs by adapting classical centrality
measures such as betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector measures. The theoretical assump-
tions required to ensure their well-posedness (existence and uniqueness) were delineated in
this context.
The proposed methodologies were tested in a benchmark environment. The outcomes
of this comparison conﬁrm the superiority of the novel weighted measures over the classi-
cal measures obtained from simple unweighted graphs. Speciﬁcally, the betweenness and
closeness centralities for the multigraph formulation and the f −eigenvector centrality for
the multiplex model provide the most promising achievements. These results allow us to
diversify the choice of the measures that must be considered based on the MDPs selected. In
fact, while simple graph-based measures supply helpful observations in an environment with
sparse rewards, these novel measures can handle situations where penalties and rewards are
more homogeneous.
Additionally, the measurements obtained are compared through statistical analysis with
baselines from different groups of measures (Q-value-based), highlighting the evidence of
high-quality performance of the f −eigenvector measure in recognising, at the same time,
dangerous states and (sub-)goal states.
However, the lack of a measure that can summarise the information gathered from the
trained agent and MDPs’ structure remains a major issue. Furthermore, it is crucial to test all
available measures for an extensive set of MDPs to evaluate the optimal choices for different
settings relative to the state and action spaces and the associated reward functions. Thus, we
can reliably identify the most useful measures in speciﬁc contexts.
