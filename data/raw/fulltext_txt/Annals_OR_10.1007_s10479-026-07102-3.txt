Abstract
This paper proposes an innovative directional Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) model for setting
benchmarking targets on the frontier of the Production Possibility Set (PPS) in alignment
with strategic goals deﬁned a priori by experts or decision makers. The proposed model itera-
tively adjusts the directional vectors assigned to each Decision Making Unit (DMU) ensuring
that once speciﬁc goals are achieved, further improvement efforts are directed towards indi-
cators with remaining performance gaps. This mechanism enables a dynamic prioritization
of improvement consistent with strategic objectives. Additionally, we deﬁne a Composite
Indicator (CI) that measures the overall effectiveness of each DMU relative to a strategy-
based reference. The CI can be decomposed into a technical score - reﬂecting proximity to
the PPS frontier - and a strategical score - capturing the extent to which goals remain unmet
upon reaching the frontier. The framework proposed is illustrated and validated through an
empirical assessment of 27 European countries using 2022 data from the ‘Education and
Training 2030’ indicators.
Keywords Data Envelopment Analysis · Directional Distance Function · Target setting ·
Composite indicators · Goals

1 Introduction
Education is the foundation for personal fulﬁlment, employability, and responsible citizen-
ship, helping individuals to reach their full potential and contributing to the development of
society. The United Nations (1948) ﬁrst recognised Education as a fundamental human right
and, some years later, as a child right (Unicef, 1989). More recently, quality education was set
as the Sustainable Development Goal 4, promoting inclusive and equitable quality education
and lifelong learning opportunities for all (United Nations, 2015). The concepts of qual-
ity and equity have been widely debated in the education literature. Quality has traditionally
been linked to students’ cognitive performance, measured by large-scale international assess-
ments (De Witte & López-Torres, 2017). Nevertheless, international organisations emphasise
that quality also encompasses broader competencies, including critical thinking, social and
emotional skills (Chernyshenko et al., 2018; OECD, 2015), as well as health and well-
being (UNESCO, 2022), which are essential for lifelong learning and social participation.
A comprehensive notion of quality therefore combines academic achievement with wider
knowledge, skills, and attitudes that learners need to thrive in.
Equity, in turn, is often understood as equality of opportunity: educational outcomes
should reﬂect students’ effort and ability rather than their socioeconomic background or other
circumstances beyond their control (OECD, 2007, 2012). Empirical studies tend to measure
equity by examining how performance is associated with family background (Enchikova
et al., 2025), but complementary perspectives also focus on the distribution of opportunities
within education systems, considering school segregation (D’Inverno et al., 2025), equality of
outcomes (Parker et al., 2018), or the social composition of schools (Tan, 2022). In this sense,
equity goes beyond access, extending to how fairly learning opportunities and outcomes are
shared across student populations.
Quality and equity serve as guiding principles for the European Union (EU) key pri-
orities for Education and Training. For instance, reducing early school leaving (European
Commission, 2015) is closely tied to equity, as dropout rates are disproportionately higher
among disadvantaged groups. Expanding participation in early childhood education (Euro-
pean Union, 2019) promotes both quality (through early cognitive and socio-emotional
development) and equity (by narrowing gaps linked to family background). Similarly, ter-
tiaryattainment(EuropeanUnion,2017)reﬂectsexcellenceandinclusioninhighereducation,
while adult learning (European Union, 2016) relates to lifelong opportunities to update skills.
Finally, the investment in basic and digital skills (European Union, 2018) extends the notion
of quality beyond test-based achievement to competencies essential for democratic partici-
pation and labour market adaptability.
To monitor progress on these key priorities, the European Union (2021) established the
‘Education and Training 2030’ (ET2030), the latest strategic framework setting out seven
benchmarking indicators and their respective EU-level goals. These correspond to common
reference levels to be attained by the EU countries for Europe to converge as a whole, which
is the declared objective of the EU and the mechanism to achieve European cohesion. The
alignment of national policies with the strategic framework for cooperation in Education
and Training helps each Member State to improve its educational performance from early
childhood and care to lifelong learning.
Note that performance refers to a broader concept encompassing both efﬁciency and effec-
tiveness. While efﬁciency refers to the ability of education systems to transform resources
(inputs) into educational outcomes (outputs), effectiveness focuses on the level of outcomes
achieved, regardless of the resources employed. For instance, Camanho et al. (2023) and

Stumbrien˙e et al. (2020) evaluated the effectiveness of European countries following a
Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) approach to build a Composite Indicator (CI) - a single mea-
sure of performance synthesising the multidimensionality of ET2020, a previous framework.
These studies have, however, identiﬁed a misalignment between BoD benchmarking targets
and the ET2020 goals. This mismatch stems from the fact that countries may be incentivised
to pursue further progress in dimensions where goals have already been met by peers, rather
than prioritising those still lagging behind the EU-level aspirations.
Stumbrien˙e et al. (2024) proposed a BoD model that incorporates the ET2030 goals to
suggest common and country-speciﬁc benchmarking targets to set more realistic improve-
ment strategies. However, the study did not address the misalignment between BoD targets
and EU-level goals reported by Camanho et al. (2023) and Stumbrien˙e et al. (2020), and
presents two main limitations. First, country-speciﬁc targets could be less demanding than
actual levels observed for some indicators. Second, common and country-speciﬁc targets
could be more ambitious than the EU-level goals for some indicators still lagging behind. It
would be reasonable for countries to be guided by more ambitious targets than the EU-level
goals, but only if they have already met such levels.
Moreover, although the benchmarking exercises proposed by these studies reﬂect each
country’s position relative to an empirical ‘best practice’ frontier (BPF), they do not fully cap-
ture alignment with the strategic framework deﬁned by the EU-level goals. This is because the
existing gap between observed indicator values and the corresponding goals is not explicitly
accounted for. In this context, this paper aims to introduce a comprehensive BoD framework
for both target-setting and performance measurement aligned with goals, offering two main
methodological contributions.
First, from a target-setting perspective, we introduce an innovative model that iteratively
adjusts the directional vectors assigned to each Decision Making Unit (DMU) for setting
benchmarking targets at the frontier of the Production Possibility Set (PPS), in alignment
with strategic goals deﬁned a priori by experts or decision makers - the ET2030 goals in
the context of this paper. To this end, the directional BoD model proposed by Zanella et al.
(2015) is extended to incorporate, within its formulation, the goal value associated with each
ET2030 indicator considered in the analysis. This allows us to specify an ex-ante direction for
improvement that each country should follow, consisting of the values of ET2030 indicators
whose goals have not yet been met. The main novelty we introduce is that this improvement
direction may be readjusted multiple times throughout the iterative process to ensure that
countries do not pursue further progress on indicators for which goals have been attained, but
instead concentrate efforts on those indicators for which goals remain unmet and therefore
represent a priority of improvement.
Second, from a performance measurement perspective, we propose the estimation of
composite indicator scores denoting the overall effectiveness of countries against a strategy-
basedreference(SR).Thisnewreferenceisrelevantbecauseitallowstocomparetheobserved
values with the strategic goals when less ambitious targets are obtained at the BPF. The SR
extends beyond the PPS frontier (e.g., the BPF) and accounts for the real distance between
countries and the strategic goals. The CI score can then be further decomposed into a technical
score, reﬂecting proximity to the PPS frontier, and a strategical score, capturing the extent
to which goals remain unmet upon reaching the frontier. The measurement of effectiveness
againsttheSRcanbeusedtodiscriminatetheeffectivenessofcountrieslocatedonthefrontier,
based on their distance to the strategic goals located beyond the PPS. This procedure can
be seen as an alternative to the traditional approach of imposing weight restrictions in Data
Envelopment Analysis methods, which also enable enhancing the discriminatory power of
frontier methods via the extension of the frontier through value judgments.

2 Literature review
This section reviews cross-country studies on performance measurement in the education
sector. First, we cover efﬁciency assessments, with a particular focus on the educational
stage assessed, the data source, and the methods employed. Then, we move to cross-country
effectivenessassessmentsusingtheBoDtechniquetoassesseducationalachievements,where
we devote particular attention to the studies addressing the speciﬁc context of the Education
and Training strategic framework. In this context, we identify the knowledge gap and the key
contributions of this paper.
2.1 Cross-country efficiency assessments in education
Efﬁciency assessments in education range from evaluations at the student, classroom or
school level, to analysis at city, district or country level, but the literature on cross-country
comparisons tends to be less extensive, as it is difﬁcult to obtain comparable data at the
national level, and countries are subject to different regulatory conditions (De Witte & López-
Torres, 2017).
Nevertheless, large-scale international assessments enable meaningful comparisons of
educational system performance across countries. Numerous nations currently participate
in initiatives such as the Progress in International Reading Literacy Study (PIRLS), the
Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA), and the Trends in International
Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS), which have generated a substantial body of cross-
country research. In addition, organisations like the OECD and Eurostat - the statistical ofﬁce
of the European Union - regularly collect harmonised, country-level data that further support
comparative analyses.
Most cross-country studies focusing on educational efﬁciency rely on frontier techniques,
in particular Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA), to measure the efﬁcient conversion of
resources into educational outcomes. This is because the measurement relative to a ‘best
practice’ frontier provides a clear picture of how well a unit under assessment maximises
outputs with the minimum possible use of inputs, reﬂecting the concept of efﬁciency (De
Witte & López-Torres, 2017). The DEA technique (Charnes et al., 1978) provides bench-
marking peers from which inefﬁcient units should learn, as well as targets corresponding to
reference levels for these units to become efﬁcient.
Some authors explore DEA-related formulations for efﬁciency assessments, such as Free
Disposal Hull (FDH) (Giménez et al., 2017; Agasisti, 2011; Afonso & Aubyn, 2005), in which
the best-practice frontier is not deﬁned under the assumption of convexity among the units
deﬁning the frontier, or Directional Distance Functions (DDF) (Nepomuceno et al., 2024;

Giménez et al., 2019; Thieme et al., 2012), which allows for the simultaneous increase of
outputs and decrease of inputs. Thieme et al. (2012) were the ﬁrst to incorporate undesirable
outputs (inequality of educational achievements) along with desirable outputs (PISA scores)
using a DDF model. Note that lower values of undesirable outputs represent more equitable
education systems. For a critical review of the treatment of undesirable outputs, see Halkos
and Petrou (2019).
Most efﬁciency studies focused exclusively on secondary education (Capasso et al., 2024;
Mergoni et al., 2023; Ucar & Karsak, 2023; Aparicio et al., 2022, 2018; Cordero et al.,
2022, 2018; Giménez et al., 2019, 2017, 2007; Agasisti, 2014; Aristovnik & Obadi´c, 2014;
Afonso&Aubyn,2006, 2005)ortertiaryeducation(Nepomucenoetal.,2024;Agasisti,2011;
Kocher et al., 2006), but some combined educational stages, providing a more comprehensive
comparison of education systems at country level. Agasisti et al. (2019), Miningou (2019),
and Sutherland et al. (2010), covered both primary and secondary education; Ahec Šonje et al.
(2018) explored secondary and tertiary education; and Bogetoft et al. (2015) and Aristovnik
(2013) covered the three stages.
Regarding the data source, most of the studies reviewed so far rely on large-scale interna-
tional assessments. Note that PISA evaluates students aged between 15 years and 3 months,
and 16 years and 2 months, who have completed at least 6 years of formal schooling (OECD,
2023a). According to the International Standard Classiﬁcation of Education (ISCED), these
students belong to ISCED level 3, which represents upper secondary education. On the
other hand, PIRLS assess fourth-grade students, who belong to ISCED level 1, representing
primary education. Lastly, TIMSS not only evaluates fourth-grade, but also eighth-grade
students, who belong to ISCED level 2, representing lower secondary education. Therefore,
while PIRLS and PISA cover primary and secondary educational stages, respectively, TIMSS
comprises both stages. For this reason, when primary education is considered, secondary edu-
cation tends to be included as well. Cordero et al. (2017) seems to be the only one focusing
exclusively on primary education using PIRLS data.
Some authors combined data from PISA or TIMSS with data from other international
databases, such as Eurostat (Agasisti et al., 2019), OECD (Afonso & Aubyn, 2005, 2006;
Agasisti, 2014; Agasisti et al., 2019; Aristovnik, 2013), UNESCO (Aristovnik & Obadi´c,
2014; Ahec Šonje et al., 2018) and World Bank (Aristovnik, 2013; Aristovnik & Obadi´c,
2014; Ahec Šonje et al., 2018).
From the remaining studies that did not use data from large-scale international assess-
ments, Kocher et al. (2006) relied on data from journals and their rankings, Agasisti (2011)
used data from the OECD database, and Nepomuceno et al. (2024) gathered data from the
World Bank, UNESCO, and journals as well. These three studies concentrate exclusively on
tertiary education. Conversely, Miningou (2019) focused on the assessment of both primary
and secondary education, using OECD, World Bank, and World Development Indicators
databases, and Bogetoft et al. (2015) evaluated all educational stages using OECD and Euro-
stat databases. Table 9 in Appendix A summarises all studies mentioned for a comprehensive
overview.
2.2 Cross-country effectiveness assessments using the BoD technique
The studies reviewed so far focused on education efﬁciency, based on the conversion of
educational resources (inputs) into educational achievements (outputs). This is the most
popular approach in the education benchmarking literature for performance measurement.
123

Annals of Operations Research
Nevertheless, alternative approaches have been considered to evaluate the performance
of education systems in terms of effectiveness, that is, by disregarding resource constraints.
In particular, the ‘Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt’ (BoD) approach (Cherchye et al., 2007), has been
gaining traction for constructing Composite Indicators (CIs) that aggregate individual output
metrics to provide a simpliﬁed yet informative representation of education system effective-
ness. Given the scope of this paper, we now highlight cross-country applications of the BoD
technique in evaluating educational effectiveness across European systems. The details of
these studies can be found in Table 10.
The BoD approach was ﬁrst adopted for the evaluation of educational systems at the
country level in Rogge (2019). The author developed a geometric BoD composite indicator
to assess the progress of EU Member States toward the Europe 2020 strategy (EU2020),
including ﬁve key policy areas: employment, research and development, climate change and
energy sustainability, education, and social inclusion and poverty reduction. In evaluating
the education domain, the author relied on two ET2020 indicators: ‘Early school leavers’
and ‘Tertiary educational attainment’.
Among the studies focused on the strategic framework for Education and Training, Stum-
brien˙e et al. (2020) evaluated the effectiveness of 29 European countries based on 6 of the 8
ET2020 indicators, and two additional ones (‘PISA top achievers’ and ‘Upper secondary or
tertiary educational attainment’). Countries that excelled in a small subset of indicators were
more sensitive to the different weighting schemes. As the ﬂexibility for countries to assign
their weights, and thus prioritise the indicators in which they perform better, decreases, these
countries may not be able to completely disregard the indicators in which they do not perform
as well, which, in turn, leads to lower CI scores. The authors suggested that an assessment
with some degree of ﬂexibility could be a balanced compromise between ﬁxed and fully
ﬂexible weighting schemes. Besides the misalignment between BoD targets and the ET2020
goals, as highlighted in the introduction, another limitation of the study was not to monitor
effectiveness trends over time.
Camanho et al. (2023) addressed this limitation with the assessment of effectiveness
trends and convergence across 31 European countries from 2009 to 2018 by adapting the
Global Malmquist Index proposed by Pastor and Lovell (2005) to a BoD setting. Using the
same six ET2020 benchmarking indicators included by Stumbrien˙e et al. (2020), the authors
also identiﬁed a mismatch between targets and the ET2020 goals, suggesting that some of
these EU-level goals would be particularly ambitious to be reached by the worst-performing
countries and could be revised as ‘the progress of educational systems seems to occur in
different leagues across EU countries’.
Acknowledging this misalignment, Stumbrien˙e et al. (2024) proposed a BoD model that
incorporated the ET2030 goals to identify common and country-speciﬁc targets for 29 Euro-
pean countries, using data from 2022 based on ﬁve ET2030 indicators, as well as on three
PISA indicators to account for the underachievement in mathematics, reading and science,
separately, as in Stumbrien˙e et al. (2020). The model proposed solves a bi-objective problem
that imposes two objectives of closeness: one between observed levels and targets, and the
other between targets and EU-level goals. Consequently, the BoD targets proposed could be
either closer to EU-level goals or closer to observed levels, allowing each country to gradually
improve in the direction of the goals.
However, as outlined in the introduction, a main limitation of the study was the possibility
of obtaining targets less demanding than actual levels, or even more ambitious than the EU-
level goals for some indicators still lagging behind. For instance, the study identiﬁed common
targets of less than 10.4% and 13.7% of low achievers in science and reading, respectively,

while Estonia was the only country attaining the EU-level goal of less than 15% in each
subject.
Following a similar rationale, Stumbrien˙e et al. (2025) proposed region-speciﬁc targets
towards gender equality in the education systems of 93 European NUTS1 level regions, using
data on the ET2030 indicators, and the ‘Young people neither in employment nor in education
and training’. Instead of seeking to close the gap between targets and EU-level goals as in
Stumbrien˙e et al. (2024), the authors sought to close the gender gap between target levels.
They followed a BoD model where the ﬁrst objective of closeness was still between observed
indicator values and targets, but the second was between the targets for male indicators and
the targets for female indicators.
Stumbrien˙e et al. (2022a) evaluated the performance of 26 European countries both in
terms of effectiveness and efﬁciency using indicators on educational inclusion, fairness and
inequality (which included two ET2030 indicators: ‘Early childhood and care participation’
and ‘Tertiary educational attainment’). Stumbrien˙e et al. (2022b) also assessed performance
both in terms of effectiveness and efﬁciency, focusing on the primary education systems
in post-socialist and old democracy EU member states. In contrast, Segovia-González and
Contreras (2023) and Dominguez-Gil et al. (2022), focused only on the effectiveness of
education systems in OECD countries, employing both composite indicators following the
BoD principle.
A common limitation of all these studies was the absence of a robustness assessment
to overcome the deterministic nature of the BoD technique. Pereira et al. (2025) used a
robust conditional BoD model to assess the effectiveness of 33 European countries and
the 3 Belgian communities in creating a positive environment supporting learner mobility.
The authors employed the ‘Deck of Cards’ method to construct utility scales for the six
thematic indicators of the European Commission’s Mobility Scoreboard, allowing them to
incorporate experts’ value judgements. To monitor the evolution of effectiveness over time
the authors used the Global Malmquist Index proposed by Camanho et al. (2023). D’Inverno
et al. (2025) employed a robust conditional DDF-based BoD model to construct a CI and
benchmark OECD education systems in terms of equity.
Following a comprehensive review of cross-country effectiveness assessments using the
BoD technique, including those exclusively focused on the strategic framework for Education
and Training (Camanho et al., 2023; Stumbrien˙e et al., 2024, 2020) or using related indicators
(Stumbrien˙e et al., 2025, 2022b) two main research gaps that this paper aims to cover have
been identiﬁed.
First, to the best of our knowledge, there is no study that effectively aligns BoD benchmark-
ing targets with strategic/theoretical goals set a priori by experts or decision makers. In the
context of the strategic framework for Education and Training, this is relevant to ensure that
countries concentrate efforts on improving those indicators for which goals remain unmet,
rather than pursuing further progress on indicators for which goals have already been attained.
Note that the mismatch between country-speciﬁc BoD targets and EU-level goals identiﬁed
by Camanho et al. (2023) and Stumbrien˙e et al. (2020), remained unaddressed in Stumbrien˙e
et al. (2024).
Second, as far as we know, there are no studies providing a measure of effectiveness
that explicitly accounts for the possible existing gap between observed indicator values
and strategic/theoretical goals. This is relevant for the CI to reﬂect the true positioning of
education systems relative to policy aspirations. While Stumbrien˙e et al. (2024) incorporated
the ET2030 goals into their target-setting efforts, the authors did not provide any effectiveness
measure aligned with EU-level goals.

This paper builds upon these two gaps by proposing an innovative BoD framework for
target-setting and performance measurement aligned with strategic/theoretical goals set a
priori by experts or decision-makers. It extends previous research on the evaluation of Euro-
pean education systems in the context of the strategic framework for Education and Training,
offering a reﬁned BoD-based methodology tailored to this policy context. As outlined in the
introduction, from a target-setting perspective, we propose an iterative process based on a
new directional BoD model to set targets at the BPF aligned with the ET2030 goals; and from
a performance measurement perspective, we introduce an alternative ‘strategy’ reference to
estimate CI scores to discriminate the effectiveness of European education systems relative
to the ET2030 strategy.
3 Methodology
This section brieﬂy describes the directional BoD model proposed by Zanella et al. (2015)
to further discuss the BoD model we propose for target setting aligned with strategic goals.
The estimation of CI scores to rank countries based on their effectiveness is also introduced.
3.1 The BoD setting
The DEA technique can be applied to construct composite indicators, which aggregate the
indicators (or outputs) of a DMU into a single ﬁgure of effectiveness. Cook and Kress (1990)
were the ﬁrst to disregard the conversion of inputs into outputs in the efﬁciency evaluation,
focusing only on the outputs. Some years later, Cherchye et al. (2007) adapted the original
input-oriented model proposed by Charnes et al. (1978), introducing the ‘Beneﬁt-of-the-
Doubt’ (BoD) approach to construct a CI, where a unitary level of input is set for all DMUs.
This ‘dummy input’ equal to one can be interpreted as a ‘helmsman’ attempting to steer the
DMUs toward enhanced effectiveness.
The BoD technique allows each DMU under assessment to assign different weights to the
indicators. As it is rooted in DEA, the weights assigned are not predetermined but speciﬁed
endogenously (Cooper et al., 2004), allowing ﬂexibility as each DMU is seen in its best
possible light. DMUs are given the ‘beneﬁt of the doubt’ of being evaluated with an individual
weighting scheme that favours those indicators on which they perform better. Although this
ﬂexibility may lead some DMUs to ignore indicators on which they perform poorly, the
BoD technique identiﬁes the DMUs that, even under their most favourable weighting, are
outperformed by other units, suggesting their effectiveness can still be improved. Zanella
et al. (2015) proposed the construction of CI scores in the presence of undesirable output
indicators by adapting the DDF formulation proposed by Chung et al. (1997) for efﬁciency
assessments to a BoD setting. In Chung’s formulation, inputs and desirable outputs are treated
as strongly disposable, while undesirable outputs are assumed to be weakly disposable. Note
that under weak disposability, undesirable outputs are considered by-products of desirable
outputsandcannotbereducedfreely;abatementisonlyfeasibleifaccompaniedbyareduction
in desirable outputs or an increase in inputs (Liu et al., 2010).
However, when adapting the DDF model proposed by Chung et al. (1997) to a BoD
setting, weak disposability introduces a key limitation: it allows for negative marginal rates
of substitution between desirable and undesirable outputs, resulting in downward-sloping
segments of the frontier. Such segments imply that a DMU located on the frontier might
simultaneously increase the production of the desirable output and reduce the production of

3.3 Composite indicators for effectiveness assessment aligned with goals
In a context where targets could not be represented by a single β factor, Silva et al. (2020)
proposed the estimation of partial effectiveness scores for each indicator. These scores cor-
respond to the ratio between the observed and target levels for each desirable indicator. As
a ﬁnal measure to evaluate each DMU against the BPF, the authors proposed a technical
effectiveness score corresponding to the arithmetic mean of their partial scores, where all
indicators were given the same importance. This same rationale will be used to evaluate each
DMU against the BPF following the model (3) we propose, but it will be extended to include
undesirable indicators.

Finally, we propose the metric ‘goal-spread’ (g-spread) to estimate the average effec-
tiveness of the sample of j DMUs in relation to each strategic goal. This metric is deﬁned
for each output dimension, corresponding to the geometric mean of its partial score for all
DMUs when evaluated against the SR, as shown in (10). The use of the geometric mean in
this context reﬂects the intention to capture proportional shortfalls across DMUs. Unlike the
arithmetic mean, it penalises uneven effectiveness more strongly, making it a suitable choice
for identifying dimensions where strategic goals are consistently underachieved. In contrast,
the use of the arithmetic mean for the calculation of technical scores preserves additive
interpretability and avoids over-penalizing low scores, which was considered desirable when
aggregating the partial scores. The highest possible g-spread value for a given dimension is

This section contextualizes the empirical assessment of the effectiveness of 27 European

an overview of the main descriptive statistics of the data used in the study. The seven ET2030
benchmarks, their indicators and strategic goals are described as follows:
1. Low achieving 15-year-olds in basic skills: The share of low-achieving 15-year-olds in
reading, mathematics and science should be less than 15%, by 2030;
2. Low achieving eight-graders in digital skills: The share of low-achieving eight-graders
in computer and information literacy should be less than 15%, by 2030;
3. Participation in early childhood education and care: At least 96% of children between
3 years old and the starting age for compulsory primary education should participate in
early childhood education and care, by 2030;
4. Early leavers from education and training: The share of early leavers from education
and training should be less than 9%, by 2030;
5. Tertiary level attainment: The share of 25-34 year-olds with tertiary educational attain-
ment should be at least 45%, by 2030;
6. Exposure of VET graduates to work-based learning: The share of recent graduates from
VET beneﬁting from exposure to work-based learning during their vocational education
and training should be at least 60%, by 2025;
7. Participation of adults in learning: At least 60% of adults aged 25-64 should have par-
ticipated in learning during the last 12 months, by 2030;

The indicator referring to the benchmark Low achieving eight-graders in digital skills, is
given by the share of eighth-graders who fail to reach level 2 on the International Computer
and Information Literacy Study (ICILS). The latest results of ICILS refer to the limited
evaluation of 12 countries in 2013, and 7 countries in 2018. Therefore, this ET2030 indicator
was excluded from the analysis.
The remaining six benchmarks included in the analysis are represented by 8 indicators,
because the benchmark on Low achieving 15-year-olds in basic skills encompasses three
indicators, reﬂecting the share of 15-year-old students who fail to reach level 2 on each
of the PISA competencies - reading, mathematics, and science. However, including many
indicators would increase dimensionality and potentially reduce the model’s discriminatory
power when evaluating countries.
To give equal importance to each benchmark, and, simultaneously, reduce the number
of indicators, we considered that the benchmark on Low achieving 15-year-olds in basic
skills should be represented by a single indicator. As the three PISA indicators tend to be
highly correlated, one ﬁrst option was to represent their benchmark by just one of them,
excluding the other two. However, choosing one indicator over the others would favour some
countries and penalise others. Therefore, it seemed reasonable that all three indicators should
be combined.
Following Camanho et al. (2023), we use the average of the three subject-speciﬁc indi-
cators. This choice avoids privileging any single subject, and remains aligned with the scale
of the established ET2030 goal of having fewer than 15% of low achievers separately in
each PISA domain. While no EU-level benchmark exists for such an aggregated indicator,
the average directly combines the subject-speciﬁc indicators to which ET2030 explicitly
refers and offers an interpretable proxy of how evenly countries perform across the three
core competencies.
Besides assessing the underachievement in each subject separately, OECD (2023b) also
reports the share of students who score below PISA level 2 in any subject, as well as in all
three subjects simultaneously, which could have been used to combine the three competen-
cies into a single measure. However, as illustrated in Appendix C, neither of these measures1
can be meaningfully assessed against the 15% ET2030 goal. As expected, the ﬁrst yields
substantially higher values, while the second produces values below the single-domain mea-
sures, thereby disconnecting both indicators from the established policy threshold. Using
such measures would therefore require redeﬁning the policy goal. Importantly, the purpose
of the empirical application is not to adapt policy goals, but to illustrate how the proposed
methodology can be applied in the context of the existing ET2030 goals. Accordingly, the
15% goal value of low achievers is preserved.
We acknowledge that averaging across domains may allow weaker performance in one
subject to be offset by better performance in another, which may mask non-compliance
with the ET2030 goal of having fewer than 15% of low achievers in each PISA subject.
Therefore, we carefully look at the countries reporting shares of low achievers in single
subjects below the 15% goal that could lead to such an issue. These include Estonia and
Ireland. Estonia proved to be a well-balanced country with at most 15% of low achievers in
each of the three subjects. Displaying an average share of Low Achievers of 13%, Estonia
is the only country in the sample studied to be regarded as having reached the 15% goal by
2022. Conversely, Ireland reported the second-best average value (15.3%), nearly reaching

5.2 ET2030 and a way foward
Although each EU-level goal is attained by at least one country in the sample (see Figure 6),
this does not imply that the goals correspond to a point inside the Production Possibility Set
(PPS). As illustrated in Figure 2, the goals correspond to a point inside the PPS only under
speciﬁc conditions, such as the presence of a Class A DMU that meets all goals (regardless of
whether it lies on the frontier), or when the targets assigned to a Class C DMU also satisfy all
goals. In the latter case, the fact that the goals are reached before hitting the BPF (see Figure
3) implies that the set of goals lies within the PPS. In our empirical application, however, no
country qualiﬁes as Class A, and the targets of Italy and Romania, both classiﬁed as Class C,
still fall short of some goals (see Table 13 in Appendix F). Therefore, the scenario depicted
in Figure 8 applies: while each goal is attained by at least one country, the set of goals does
not correspond to a point within the PPS.
In this context, the methodology proposed was relevant to address the mismatch between
country-speciﬁctargetsandEU-levelgoalsreportedbyStumbrien˙eetal.(2020)andCamanho
et al. (2023), which remained unaddressed by Stumbrien˙e et al. (2024). First, countries only
sought improvements in the direction of the indicators that had yet to reach their EU-level
goals. As such, the indicators already exceeding their EU-level goals remained unchanged,
as they were not considered a priority to be improved. Second, every time a country reached
the EU-level goal for a given indicator in the movement towards the BPF, but the frontier
had not yet been met, the directional vector was readjusted to disregard that indicator from
that moment onwards. As a result, we did not obtain targets that were more ambitious than
the goals for the indicators being improved.

6 Conclusion
Previous research evaluating European education systems in the context of the EU strategy
for Education and Training under a BoD setting (Camanho et al., 2023; Stumbrien˙e et al.,
2020, 2024) presented two main limitations. First, benchmarking targets were found to be
misaligned with EU-level goals, since countries were incentivised to continue improving
indicators for which goals were already met, instead of concentrating efforts on those dimen-
sions where progress was still needed. Second, the evaluations relative to the BPF did not
account for the existing gap between observed indicator values and the corresponding goals.
In response to these limitations, this paper introduces two methodological contributions to
align BoD-based evaluations with strategic goals.
The ﬁrst methodological contribution refers to target-setting. We proposed a new direc-
tional BoD model to set benchmarking targets in alignment with strategic goals set a priori
by experts or decision makers. The novelty lies in the iterative adjustment of improvement
directions, which in empirical applications ensures that countries stop pursuing progress on
indicators where goals have already been attained, and instead focus on those still unmet. In
this paper we conﬁrmed that it is possible to set targets aligned with goals, addressing the
mismatch identiﬁed in previous studies.
The second methodological contribution concerns performance measurement. We pro-
posed the estimation of CI scores denoting overall effectiveness relative to a strategy-based
reference (SR). It extends beyond the BPF and accounts for the existing gap between observed
indicator values and strategic goals. Moreover, the new SR can be seen as an alternative to the
use of weight restrictions proposed by Zanella et al. (2015) to discriminate the units located
on the BPF, since the CI score can be further decomposed into a technical score, considering
the measurement of effectiveness against the BPF, and a strategical score, indicating whether
units still have goals to attain beyond the BPF, and how distant they are in relation to such
goals. In the ET2030 empirical application, this was particularly evident for Croatia, Cyprus,
and Poland, which were all located on the BPF or particularly close to it, but reported the
lowest strategical scores, revealing substantial gaps to the EU-level goals. In addition, more
than half of the countries studied would not be incentivised to improve further if evaluated
only against the BPF, even though they still had EU-level goals to attain. The use of the SR
allowed us to quantify the existing gaps between observed values and goals (overall score),
and between targets and goals (strategical score).
Although the methodology proposed was developed to evaluate education systems under
the EU strategic goals, its structure is adaptable to other policy domains or regulated sectors
where progress is measured against predeﬁned goals. For example, in the EU waste manage-
ment strategy, countries are expected to meet ambitious goals such as increasing recycling
rates and reducing landﬁll use. Similarly, in the water sector, utilities are expected to be com-
pliant with objectives on improving water quality, preventing failures or water losses. In both
123

Annals of Operations Research
cases, our directional BoD model and strategy-based reference could be used to set bench-
marking targets and measure effectiveness in line with strategical expectations, illustrating
the broader applicability of the approach we propose.
Nevertheless, this paper also presents some limitations, which could be addressed in future
research. First, the analysis relied on a single year of data, but future research could examine
the evolution of effectiveness over time, as in Camanho et al. (2023). Secondly, this paper
focused on education systems at national level, building on Stumbrien˙e et al. (2024). We
prioritised a comprehensive deﬁnition of ET2030 by including PISA data, which is rare
at regional level. Future work could also focus on evaluating European regions, even with
fewer indicators. Finally, the framework could be reﬁned by incorporating expert judgments,
either in the weighting of indicators when building the CI or in the speciﬁcation of the initial
directional vectors.
