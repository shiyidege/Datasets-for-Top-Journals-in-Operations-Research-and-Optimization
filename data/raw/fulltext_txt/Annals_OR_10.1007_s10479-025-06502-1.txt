Abstract
The Fuzzy K-Means algorithm extends the well-known classical K-Means algorithm by
replacing the standard allocation matrix with the membership degree one. Consistently with
the fuzzy approach to clustering, this allows for obtaining a soft assignment of the units to
the clusters. The units are assigned to the clusters with membership degree taking values in
the unit interval. In practice, what we get from Fuzzy K-Means is a fuzzy partition where
even the units clearly belonging to only one cluster generally present non-zero membership
degrees to all the clusters. In order to overcome this drawback, a generalization of Fuzzy
K-Means is proposed where an L0 regularization term for the membership degree matrix is
introduced. This makes it possible to obtain a sparse membership degree matrix, where the
units that clearly belong to one cluster have membership degrees strictly equal to one to the
cluster involved and zero to the other clusters, without compromising the soft membership
degrees of the units with unclear assignments. The adequacy of the proposal is evaluated by
means of simulation and real-case studies.
Keywords Clustering · Fuzzy K-Means · Sparsity · L0 regularization
1 Introduction
The fuzzy approach to clustering represents a ﬂexible strategy to discover a partition of units.
In contrast with the standard (non-fuzzy) approach where the units either belong or do not
to a cluster, fuzziness allows for assigning the units to the clusters with membership degrees
ranging from 0 (complete non-membership) to 1 (complete membership). This is fruitful
especially in all those situations where units present intermediate characteristics with respect
to the clusters (prototypes) and, therefore, cannot be strictly assigned to a certain cluster.
The most prominent fuzzy clustering algorithm is the Fuzzy K-Means (FKM) (Bezdek,
1981) that extends the classical (non-fuzzy) K-Means (KM) algorithm (MacQueen, 1967).

To tune the fuzziness of the obtained partition, FKM makes use of the so-called fuzziness
parameter taking values strictly greater than 1. In fact, when the fuzziness parameter is close
to 1, the FKM membership degrees tend to be equal to either 1 or 0, as in classical KM.
In other words, the FKM membership degree matrix reduces to the KM allocation matrix.
Conversely, large values of the parameter lead to an increase in the level of fuzziness of
the partition. This key parameter allows for assigning units with intermediate characteristics
to the clusters with membership degrees far from 1, to highlight their unclear assignments.
However, it also implies that a certain amount of fuzziness occurs even for the assignments of
the units clearly belonging to one of the clusters. As it is well-known, this can be explained
by the fact that FKM gives membership degrees exactly equal to 1 only to the units that
coincide with one of the prototypes. This appears to be counterintuitive because it hides the
clear assignments of such units.
In this work, we aim at ﬁxing such an issue of FKM. In detail, we propose an extension
of FKM able to give membership degrees strictly equal to 1 to all those units for which the
cluster assignments are clear, maintaining the ﬂexibility of the fuzzy approach because units
with intermediate characteristics are assigned to the clusters with membership degrees in the
interval [0, 1]. In other words, our proposal produces a “soft” defuzziﬁcation stimulating the
membership degrees of clearly assigned units to take values in the set {0, 1}, thus mitigating
the complexity of data. This implies sparsity in the membership degrees because, obviously,
for a unit, if one membership degree is equal to 1, then the remaining ones must be equal to
0. To achieve this goal, a regularization, based on the L0 norm, is added to the FKM loss
function.
The paper is organized as follows. In the next Section the FKM algorithm is recalled
and a motivating example is given in order to motivate the need for our proposal. The novel
clustering algorithm, which we will call Fuzzy K-Means with L0 regularization (FKM-L0),
is presented in Sect.3. The results of a simulation study are given in Sect.4, while Sect.5
addresses some applications. Conclusions will be provided in Sect.6.

the assignments of (some) units to clusters are clear. The following example helps to better
explain this point.

parameter m affects all the membership degrees indiscriminately. In particular, by reducing
m, we stimulate the algorithm to give membership degrees very close or equal to 1, but this
occurs for all the units, including those with unclear assignments.
The previous results highlight that the FKM algorithm, regardless the choice of the param-
eter m, does not allow to tune the fuzziness of the partition in such a way to distinguish units
with clear and unclear assignments. For this purpose, in the next section we are going to
propose an extension of FKM involving the L0 regularization term in the loss function. The
new proposal offers a “soft” defuzziﬁcation of the membership degree matrix where only the
clearly assigned units have membership degrees to the proper cluster strictly equal to 1.
3 Fuzzy K-means with L0 regularization
Regularization terms have been originally introduced in the linear regression framework
(see, e.g. Hoerl & Kennard 1970; Tibshirani 1996). Just recently, the use of regularization in
clustering has received an increasing attention. This is not only related to the fuzzy approach
to clustering, but encompasses several approaches such as the possibilistic or model-based
ones. Examples can be found in Hamasuna & Endo (2013); Pan & Shen (2007); Namkoong
et al. (2010); Witten & Tibshirani (2010); Qiu et al. (2015); Arias-Castro & Pu (2017); Guo
et al. (2020); Yang & Ali (2020); Yang & Benjamin (2020); Benjamin & Yang (2022); Wang
et al. (2022); Zhao et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Yang & Benjamin (2023); Wang et al.
(2023); Wang & Zhu (2008).
Generally speaking, regularization terms may refer to the variables or to the units. Regu-
larization terms on the variables are used for variable selection. This goal is often achieved by

often introduced to force the partition to take into account some preliminary information
and/or to have some desirable properties.

assigned to clusters with degrees taking values in the interval [0, 1].

Non-Exhaustive Search
The Non-Exhaustive Search behaves by increasing the number of constrained zero elements
of ui step by step and, at each step, evaluates whether to proceed or not. In details it works
as follows. The starting point is the unconstrained update of ui by using (9). Such a solution,
ui[curr], leads to the loss function value denoted by JFKM−L0[curr]. Obviously, the search
leads to the update of ui corresponding to the minimum of JFKM−L0. Nevertheless, for
simplicity and to highlight the role of λ, the reasoning is expressed in terms of the FKM loss
function. Thus, let JFKM[curr] be the value of the FKM loss function using ui[curr].
The next step consists in constraining one element of ui to 0. In order to select such
an element, the strategy consists in choosing the smallest non-zero element of the current
estimate ui[curr]. The unconstrained elements of ui can then be found by means of (12).

4 Simulation study
A simulation study has been carried out in order to analyze the performance of the FKM-L0
algorithm. In particular, the research questions are:
1. how does FKM-L0 recover the prototypes, also in comparison with standard FKM?
2. how does FKM-L0 detect the partition of the units, also in comparison with standard
FKM?
3. is the selection of λ based on the X B index a good strategy?
4. does the Non-Exhaustive Search outperform the Exhaustive Search?
5. what is the risk of hitting local optima in FKM-L0?
We observe that the third research question on the choice of λ was implicitly addressed by
answering the ﬁrst two questions because we compared the performances of FKM-L0, with
λ chosen in such a way to minimize the X B index, and of the standard FKM algorithm.
4.1 Set-up
The simulation study was conducted across a wide range of scenarios in order to cover a broad
spectrum of realistic situations and provide a comprehensive assessment of the performances.
Various conditions were examined by varying the number of units, the number of variables,
the number of clusters, the presence of noise, and the separation among clusters. Different
balance conditions among clusters, interpreted as varying group sizes, were also considered.
In particular, we randomly generated datasets with N = 60, 120, 180, 240 units and
S = 10, 20 variables. The units were grouped in K = 2, 4 clusters. The clusters were built
in a balanced or unbalanced manner. In the balanced case, the cluster sizes were N

all clusters. In the unbalanced one, one half of the clusters had size 2N

20%. Furthermore, we considered the possible presence of units with unclear assignment by
adding to N a percentage of outlying units. Such a percentage was set equal to 0% in case
of no units with unclear assignment or 20%. Two levels of separation between clusters were
used. This was done by means of the parameter ω taking values equal to 1 (partially separated

from the standard normal distribution. This holds for all the units (with unclear assignment
or not). Finally, with respect to the units with unclear assignment, two different generation
processes were adopted for the scores pertaining to the informative variables. One half of
the units had scores randomly generated from the standard normal distribution in order to
have units with unclear assignment far from the clusters but between them. The other half
had scores generated from the normal distribution with variance equal to 1 and mean values
equal to 2ω (for the ﬁrst half of the informative variables) and −2ω (for the second half).

4.2 Performance measures
In order to compare the Exhaustive Search and the Non-Exhaustive Search, we considered the
differencesincomputationtimeandlossfunctionvaluesuponconvergence.Morespeciﬁcally,
suchtwomeasuresallowedustoassesswhetherandtowhatextenttheNon-ExhaustiveSearch
was more efﬁcient than the Exhaustive Search and whether the two strategies led to the same
minimum. With respect to the latter point, for each search, we focused our attention on the
global optimum. In particular, we assumed that the minimum loss function value out of 10
random starting points was such a global optimum (even if it could be a local optimum
itself). We compared the global optima of FKM-L0 with the Exhaustive Search and those
of FKM-L0 with the Non-Exhaustive Search. We assumed that the two global optima were
essentially the same if one global optimum was not more 0.01% higher than the other one.
The risk of local optima was assessed by assuming that a local optimum occurred if the loss
function value was at least 0.01% higher than that of the global optimum.

4.3 Results
The results are distinguished into three parts. The ﬁrst two parts refer to the comparative
assessment of FKM-L0 with the Exhaustive Search and the Non-Exhaustive Search in terms
of computational aspects, namely, loss function optima (Sect. 4.3.1) and computation time
(Sect. 4.3.2). The latter part concerns the recovery performance of FKM-L0 with respect to
FKM (Sect. 4.3.3).
4.3.1 Loss function optima
With regard to the loss function optima, we studied the FKM-L0 global optima obtained by
using the Exhaustive Search and the Non-Exhaustive Search. We found that, in the simulation
study, FKM-L0 with the Exhaustive Search and the Non-Exhaustive Search virtually led to
the same loss function values upon convergence. For all the datasets, the same global optima
were found by means of the two searches.
Furthermore, we inspected the risk of hitting local optima for FKM-L0. The results were
very good. In fact, the average percentage of attaining the global optimum was 98.20% for
FKM-L0 with both the Exhaustive Search and the Non-Exhaustive Search. Note that this
value was slightly lower than that for the standard FKM algorithm (99.64%).

6 Concluding remarks
In this paper we have proposed an extension of the Fuzzy K-Means algorithm by introducing
an L0 regularization in order to possibly obtain sparsity in the membership degree matrix.
This has led to overcoming a drawback of Fuzzy K-Means which usually assigns all the
units to the clusters with non-zero membership degrees even when the units clearly belong to
one of the clusters. To get the optimal solution of the proposal, called Fuzzy K-Means with
L0 regularization (FKM-L0), two alternative strategies, the Exhaustive Search and the Non-
Exhaustive Search, have been developed. Through an extensive simulation study, we showed
that both work well in terms of attaining the global optimum, but the computational time
of the Non-Exhaustive Search is undoubtedly reduced compared to the Exhaustive Search.
Furthermore, the choice of the parameter that tunes the importance of the L0 regularization
term is discussed. The performance of the proposed method was compared to that of Fuzzy
K-Means through a simulation study and was found to be better in terms of both prototype
and membership degree recovery. Finally, some applications were addressed to evaluate
the adequacy of the proposal in different real life examples. The results were absolutely
satisfactory from a clustering point of view.
