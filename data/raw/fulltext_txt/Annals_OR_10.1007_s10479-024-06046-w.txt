Abstract
In a general high-dimensional process, a large number of process parameters or quality
characteristics is found to be featured through their dependencies and relevance. The features
that have similar characteristics or behaviors in the process operation can be categorized into
multiple groups. Thus, when a few quality characteristics in the process change, it is highly
probable that the process shift would have occurred in a few relevant groups. Recently, several
advanced statistical process control techniques are developed to monitor the changes in high-
dimensional processes under sparsity. However, monitoring schemes that utilize the grouped
pattern of the quality characteristics are sparse. This paper proposes a new method to monitor
the high-dimensional process when the grouped structure of the process data is observed. The
proposed method identiﬁes the potentially changed groups and individual variables within
the groups based on a modiﬁed sparse group LASSO (MSGL) model. Then, a monitoring
statistic is obtained using MSGL-based likelihood function to test abnormality of the process.
Extensive numerical studies are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness and efﬁciency of
the proposed method. In addition, a real-life application of a liqueﬁed natural gas process is
presented to illustrate the proposed method.

2011). In addition, simultaneous monitoring methods have difﬁculties in monitoring that
processes when only a few variables are responsible for process change. It is particularly
reasonable that a few variables are responsible for process change in the high-dimensional

Under sparsity, it is intuitive to reduce the dimension and develop a compressed model
which is more sensitive to minor changes. Hawkins (1991, 1993) and Runger (1996) demon-
strate the effectiveness of process monitoring by focusing on the speciﬁc assignable causes.
Beyond the traditional multivariate statistical process control (MSPC) such as Hotelling’s T 2,
multivariateexponentiallyweightedmovingaverage(MEWMA)andmultivariatecumulative
sum (MCUSUM), several advanced MSPC techniques have been developed for monitoring
high-dimensional processes under sparsity (Kim et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2014; Zou & Qiu,
2009). See Lim & Lee (2017), Maboudou-Tchao (2019), Jalilibal et al. (2019) for the general
discussion in high-dimensional process monitoring.
One of the main streams of the high-dimensional process monitoring methods is to incor-
porate variable selection (VS) techniques. Speciﬁcally, these methods consist of two main
steps: identiﬁcation of the potentially changed variables via VS and monitoring the process
change. For the process mean testing, Wang and Jiang (2009) propose a method by applying a
forward variable selection technique to identify the potentially changed variables. Jiang et al.
(2012) extend Wang and Jiang’s model by applying EWMA and suggest the likelihood type
for the monitoring statistic. Zou and Qiu (2009) develop a control chart using least absolute
shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO, Hastie et al., 2008; Zou, 2006) and provide an
aggregated type of the monitoring statistic. For the process variability testing, Li et al. (2013)
propose an L1 norm penalized likelihood based method to estimate sparse covariance matrix,
and this lead to several monitoring procedures such as Cai and Liu (2011), Abdella et al.
(2019) and Abdella et al. (2020). See more articles that develop the methods to monitor the
variability in Maboudou-Tchao et al. (2013), Maboudou-Tchao et al. (2013), and Yeh et al.
(2012). Capizzi and Masarotto (2011) develop a combination of the least angle regression
(LAR) (Efron et al., 2004) with MEWMA for monitoring the mean and the variability simul-
taneously, and Wang et al. (2014) propose a simultaneous monitoring method for process
mean and covariance matrix via penalized likelihood estimation.
The VS-based methods have potential extensions by considering different fault identi-
ﬁcation procedures such as various step-down procedures (Hawkins, 1991; Li et al., 2008;
Mason et al., 1995, 1997; Sullivan et al., 2007; Zhu & Jiang, 2009). More variants of VS-based
methodology and relevant discussions are found in Peres and Fogliatto (2018), Abdella et al.
(2017), Weese et al. (2016), Capizzi (2015), Woodall and Montgomery (2014), Mehmood
et al. (2012).

theoretical progress and an improvement in computation by deriving the distributions of the
likelihood-based monitoring statistic under H0 and H1, it does not provide information on
the fault in the process because the L2 norm regularization shrinks the coefﬁcients towards
zero but not exactly zero as in the L1 norm regularization.
These methods may not function well under speciﬁc structures of the process. In general
high-dimensional processes, a large number of process parameters or quality characteristics
arefoundtobefeaturedthroughtheirdependenciesandrelevance.Thus,thehigh-dimensional
process variables can be categorized based on the similarity of the characteristics or the
relevance of the behavior of the potential process change. When few quality characteristics
are changed in the process under sparsity setting, it is highly probable that the process shift
would occur in a few relevant groups. The multistage process is one of the typical examples
where each stage can be considered as a group and a few variables in a speciﬁc group may
shift together. In this case, exploiting the patterns would provide more advantages in VS than
choosing the variables individually just as the traditional VS-based methods do. Therefore,
in this paper, we propose a new method to monitor the high-dimensional process when the
grouped structure’s behavior of the process is observed. The main idea of the proposed
method is to identify the potentially shifted group ﬁrst and select individual variables under
sparsity. Hence, it has the potential reduction of the computational complexity compared
to the traditional VS-based methods that can be seen as ‘individual VS’. In addition, the
proposed method provides engineers with the practical information of the fault in the process
while detecting the process shifts.
This paper is organized as follows. In Sect. 2 we propose a sparse group LASSO based
control chart. We modify the procedure appropriately to implement sparsity within a group
and between groups. In Sect. 3 we demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method in
process diagnostics and shift detection throughout extensive simulation studies compared to
the traditional control charts and individual VS-based charts. An illustrative example of the
proposed method is provided in Sect. 4, followed by conclusions and future work in Sect. 5.

every selected group is responsible for the process change. Thus, the strategy for sparsity
of the proposed method is to select a few potential faulty groups ﬁrst and then select a few
responsible variables within those selected groups. Consequently, we determine the values

sufﬁcient information regarding the shifts within the SPC context. We discuss this issue in
a subsequent section and demonstrate the sensitivity of our proposed method through the
numerical simulation studies under various shift scenarios.
2.4 Fault diagnosis
When the process triggers the alarm and it is determined as a fault (i.e., it is not a false alarm),
it is signiﬁcantly of interest which variables are responsible for the out-of-control. While a
large body of literature suggest various methods to identify the faulty variables, one of the
advantages of the VS-based method is that the suspicious variables can be already diagnosed
in VS step before alarming. Thus, practitioners can assume that the selected variables when
alarmed are the suspicious variables or faulty variables without additional methods to identify
them (Jiang et al., 2012; Wang & Jiang, 2009). That is, once we obtain ˆμ from Eq. (6), the
nonzero elements can be seen as faulty variables.

5 Conclusion
In this paper, we propose a sparse group variable selection based SPC chart for monitoring
highdimensionalprocessesespeciallywhentheprocessdatahavegroupedstructureandwhen
the behavior of the process shift follows a grouped pattern. Under sparsity of both between
groups and within-a-group patterns, we apply the concept of sparse group LASSO and modify
it appropriately. Speciﬁcally, we determine the sparsity parameters as ratios between 0 and 1
which is beneﬁcial to practitioners. Furthermore, the proposed method selects the variables
adaptively according to the shift size and the number of shifted variables. Thus, the proposed
chart is more ﬂexible than the existing VS-based methodologies such as VSMEWMA that
chooses a ﬁxed number of variables.
We demonstrate the performance of the proposed chart through extensive simulation
studies and show that the proposed method outperforms other charts when the process shift
tends to occur in sparse groups where a certain structure of correlation between groups and

In addition, this paper highlights the potential for extending the penalized likelihood
approach to other special data structures, such as spatial structure and grouped spatial struc-
ture. The exploration of mixed regularizations, such as fused LASSO and smoothly clipped
absolute deviation (SCAD), holds great interest in this regard (Fan & Li, 2006; Wang & Leng,
2007; Wang et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2019; Zou & Li, 2008). For instance, Zhang et al. (2019)
proposed a chart based on the fused LASSO penalty in the likelihood. Their chart includes
two penalty terms, similar to Eq. (3): one penalizes the individual component μi, while the
other penalizes the difference between adjacent variables, μi+1 −μi. This design makes the
chart well-suited for detecting shifts in a clustered manner, when the process data exhibits
spatial structure and strong correlation between adjacent variables. Considering the possibil-
ity of grouped patterns and spatial correlation within each group, our proposed approach can
be extended by incorporating an additional penalty term for μi+1 −μi. This would enable
the chart to effectively capture shifts in grouped data with spatial correlation. Therefore,
future research could explore the integration of different penalty types and investigate their
performance in various data structures, and this will contribute to the advancement of process
monitoring techniques for diverse data scenarios with different penalized methods.
