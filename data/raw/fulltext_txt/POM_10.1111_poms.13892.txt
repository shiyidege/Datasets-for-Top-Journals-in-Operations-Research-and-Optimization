New products are highly valued by manufacturers and retailers due to their vital role in revenue generation.
Product life cycle curves often vary by their shapes and are complicated by promotional activities that induce
spiky and irregular behaviors. We collaborate with JD.com to develop a flexible product life cycle curve
forecasting framework based on Bayesian functional regression that accounts for useful covariate information,
including product attributes and promotion. The functional model treats product life cycle curves as target
variables and includes both scalar and functional predictors, capturing time-varying promotional activities.
Harnessing the power of basis function transformation, the developed model can effectively characterize the
local features and temporal evolution of sales curves. Our Bayesian framework can generate initial curve
forecasts before the product launch and update the forecasts dynamically as new sales data is collected. We
validate the superior performance of our method through extensive numerical experiments using three real-
world data sets. Our forecasting framework reduces the forecasting error by 5.35%-30.76% over JD.com’s
current model and outperforms alternative models significantly. Furthermore, the estimated promotion effect
function provides useful insights into how promotional activities interact with sales curves.
Key words : product life cycle, sales forecasting, Bayesian model, functional regression

Introduction
New products are frequently introduced into the market as innovative technologies evolve and
customer tastes change over time. According to Noble (2011), 30,000 new consumer products are
launched each year, and 95% of them fail, which underlines the highly volatile sales profiles of new
products. For both manufacturers and retailers, accurate sales forecasting is essential to making

inventory and sales planning decisions. Before a new product launch, manufacturers have to sched-
ule production and arrange shipments according to sales forecasts. Meanwhile, retailers need to
negotiate order quantities and decide the optimal inventory allocation to satisfy customer orders
in a timely fashion. New products can attract enormous attention from customers for online retail-
ers, especially new customers who have not placed orders before. Online platforms have developed
data-driven product development and marketing tools for merchants to incubate and promote new
products (Dawson 2020). However, if customer demand exceeds the planned inventory level for the
new product, the resulting stock-outs will hurt short-term sales and cause customer churns. After
a new product launch, manufacturers and retailers will collect initial sales data and update the
initial forecasts to account for the new information. An adequate adjustment is particularly critical
to new product sales forecasting because the initial forecasts are often biased.
In this paper, we study the new product life cycle (PLC) curve modeling and forecasting problem
based on our collaboration with JD.com, the largest online retailer in China. JD.com offers millions
of new products every year (including tailor-made items), and the new product sales have increased
by 50% in the first three quarters of 2021 (Wang 2021). Our objective is to provide an accurate
product life cycle curve forecast before product launch as well as updated curve forecasts after
product launch using newly collected sales data. Unlike a point estimate, a PLC curve forecast
encodes the sales trend across different stages of a new product diffusion (e.g., introduction and
growth) and maintains desirable curve properties such as smoothness.
At JD.com, the typical new product sales forecasting process can be divided into three stages,
as depicted in Figure 1. Specifically, the developed new product life cycle curve model in this
paper will mainly provide forecasts during the first two stages. In the first stage (before launch),
because no sales data is available for the new product, our model will leverage historical sales
data from other products by exploiting the relationship between sales curves and relevant covariate
information. In the second stage (4 weeks after launch), the collected initial sales data will be used
to update the curve forecasts. After the product is on sale for three months, JD.com will switch
to advanced forecasting approaches based on time series and machine learning as the new product
enters its maturity stage and sufficient sales data is collected. Notably, product sales are often in
high volume and subject to a great extent of volatility in the first two stages, which is why JD.com
desires a different approach to generating forecasts.
New product life cycle curve forecasting is challenging from both theoretical and practical per-
spectives. First, the target variable is a curve that features varying shapes and geometries for
different products. Traditional parametric curve models may struggle to establish a good relation-
ship between curve parameters with product attributes. It remains unclear how to link product
attributes with diffusion and piecewise linear curve models in a tractable regression framework (for

the promotion itself can be viewed as a function of time. Modeling both time-invariant and time-
varying features for PLC curves calls for new statistical tools that go beyond traditional diffusion
and parametric curve models.

1.1.
Contributions
To tackle the above challenges, we propose a new Bayesian functional regression framework to
model and forecast new product PLC curves. Built on the basis transformation of PLC curves, our
framework enables a flexible characterization of diverse and potentially irregular sales curves of
new products in the early stages of product diffusion. We leverage both B-splines and functional
principal components for the basis function construction.
Through the functional regression model, we capture both product attributes and promotional
activities as important covariate information for PLC curve modeling. Because promotional activ-
ities are time-dependent, our framework treats promotion as a functional curve predictor and
provides a general model that incorporates both functional response and functional predictor vari-
ables. As such, we can separate the role of promotion effect from product attributes in PLC curve
modeling, which is critical to sales forecasting of new products. The functional modeling of pro-
motion stands for a significant contribution to the PLC curve literature.
We develop the functional regression model in a Bayesian framework, so we are able to update
sales curve forecasts dynamically in response to newly collected sales data. The ability to update
curve forecasts accurately is essential to correct the biases of initial forecasts. We further leverage
clustering and classification techniques to improve the out-of-sample forecasting performance.
We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework through comprehensive numerical studies on
three real-world PLC curve data sets (from Dell, JD.com, and Intel). We first validate the superior
curve fitting performance of functional models on a computer PLC data set of Dell. We show that
functional models outperform existing parametric curve models including Bass, polynomials, and
piecewise linear models using various goodness-of-fit metrics. Based on a case study with JD.com

that comprises more than 300 SKUs from multiple brands, we evaluate the out-of-sample forecasting

benchmarks. The improvements are further confirmed using the microprocessor sales data from

Our numerical results also shed light on the interplay between product attributes, promotion,
and PLC curves. We examine the value of modeling product attributes and promotion effects in

mating the promotion effect as a bivariate function, we show how promotion affects sales in periods
before and after the promotion time. These estimates can help retailers coordinate new product
launches and promotional events. We also discuss common features of failed products launched on
the online marketplace.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 1.2 reviews related literature. Section
2 develops the Bayesian functional regression framework that includes product attributes and
promotional activities as predictors for PLC curves. Section 3 evaluates the fitting performance of
functional models on Dell’s data. Section 4 presents the case study with JD.com that focuses on
PLC curve forecasts for new products. Section 5 tests the generalizability of our framework on a
data set from Intel. Section 6 concludes the paper.
1.2.
Related Literature
Our paper contributes to the literature on new product sales forecasting, PLC curve modeling, and
Bayesian functional regression models in marketing and operations management.
New product sales forecasting. Sales forecasting, and particularly, sales forecasting for new
products, plays an essential role in supply chain planning and marketing. In the classical newsven-
dor problem, finding the optimal order quantity requires the distributional forecasts of demand
as input. The value of sales forecasting has been analyzed in different operational settings. For
example, Steinker et al. (2017) demonstrate an improved sales forecasting model with the help
of weather information can reduce the labor cost at the order fulfillment center by 11.6%. Ban
et al. (2019) study the dynamic procurement problem of new products and show that failure to
use the covariate information can result in 16-18% more total costs based on a case study with
Zara. Other valuable covariate information such as leading-indicator products, distribution chan-
nel, advance purchases, social media, and Google trend has also been exploited in sales forecasting
(Neelamegham and Chintagunta 1999, Moe and Fader 2002, Aytac and Wu 2013, Cui et al. 2018,
Boone et al. 2018). In addition, clustering techniques and machine learning models have been lever-
aged to boost forecasting performance of new product sales (Ferreira et al. 2016, Baardman et al.

2017, Cohen et al. 2022b) (the readers may find a comprehensive and recent review of forecasting
techniques in Cohen et al. (2022a)). Unlike the majority of the sales forecasting literature that
focuses on scalar target variables, our work presents a functional regression framework to predict
sales curves as a whole, which captures the temporal sales pattern within a PLC explicitly.
PLC curve modeling. PLC curve modeling is a fundamental topic in marketing and revenue
management. The classical PLC curve theory often draws on the Bass diffusion model and its
different variants (Bass 1969, Kumar and Swaminathan 2003, Niu 2006). For example, Chung et al.
(2012) propose a diffusion model with three sales components from different groups of customers,
which is designed for short-life-cycle products such as movies and games. Yelland and Dong (2014)
develop a hierarchical Bayesian forecasting approach for fashion product demand based on the
Weibull curve. Other types of parametric curve models have been tested empirically, including
polynomials and piecewise linear models (Headen 1966, Cox 1967). However, these methods are
not designed specifically for new products. Hu et al. (2019) develop a data-driven curve modeling
framework designed for new products. Their method combines clustering and parametric curve
models, which is shown to deliver promising forecasting performance on a computer data set from
Dell. More recently, Guo et al. (2022) propose a new diffusion model based on the tilted-Gompertz
distribution, which can better capture the skewness of the practical product diffusion. They also
develop a exponential smoothing scheme so the forecasting can be adjusted in response to the
environmental changes. We note that this body of literature mostly discards the use of covariate
information such as product attributes and promotional activities in fitting PLC curve models
(e.g., Hu et al. (2019) only use product attributes to form product clusters). In contrast, our PLC
curve models enable a parsimonious characterization of the relationship between PLC curves and
covariates.
Bayesian functional regression. Because of its modeling power and ability to generate
dynamic forecasts, Bayesian regression model has been widely applied to forecasting tasks in mar-
keting and operations management, including movie viewership (Neelamegham and Chintagunta
1999), recorded music sales (Lee et al. 2003), Facebook app installations (Trusov et al. 2013), and
air cargo transport risk (Shang et al. 2017). On a parallel track, functional regression tools have
been developed to model longitudinal data that can be naturally viewed as functions or curves (Yao
et al. 2005). Successful applications of functional regression have been demonstrated in biology,
medicine, and signal processing (Goldsmith et al. 2012, Morris 2015, Kadri et al. 2016). To the best
of our knowledge, the only paper that leverages functional regression to analyze marketing or oper-
ational data is Sood et al. (2009). Specifically, Sood et al. (2009) introduce a functional regression
model that uses past market penetration curves as predictors to forecast several scalar variables
of interest. Their model transforms penetration curves into principal components, which are then

regression framework that enables dynamic updates of curve forecasts using newly collected sales
information. To that end, we are the first to develop Bayesian function regression models to tackle
a general sales forecasting problem for PLCs.
2.
Method
This section presents our forecasting framework that involves two main components: clustering (and
classification) and Bayesian functional regression. In the first step, our model applies clustering to
historical training products such that each cluster represents a set of products that share similar
PLC patterns. Second, for each cluster, we fit a Bayesian functional regression model to learn the
relationship between PLC curves and covariate information. Then, when a new product ABC is
about to launch, our model would classify ABC (using the available covariate information) with
respect to the defined clusters and obtain classification probabilities. In the meantime, the trained
Bayesian functional regression models generate initial PLC curve forecasts for ABC, which are
aggregated together using the classification probabilities to obtain the prelaunch forecast of ABC.
After product launch, the new sales data observed for ABC is used to update the prelaunch forecast
in a Bayesian manner.
Following Hu et al. (2019), we work with normalized PLC curve data in both clustering and
Bayesian functional model fitting. After normalization, the total sales volume in the considered
time span of the PLC equals 1. This normalization allows us to better model the shape effect of
covariates, even when the products vary by their sales volumes significantly. To generate forecasts
on the actual volume, we scale the normalized forecasts by the projected sales volume provided by
the company.
The key contribution and building block of our framework is the Bayesian functional regression
model that flexibly captures both static and time-varying covariates. We first introduce how func-
tional data analysis can help capture relevant product attributes (Section 2.1) and promotional
activities (Section 2.2) in PLC curve modeling. We then describe the proposed Bayesian framework
(Section 2.3) and the corresponding estimation and prediction strategies (Section 2.4). Lastly, we
present the clustering and classification scheme and the overall forecasting roadmap (section 2.5).

Functional data analysis (FDA) is a set of statistical tools developed for the analysis of data for
which the ideal units are functions or curves (Morris and Carroll 2006). The goal of FDA is to
extract information from the observations of curves while exploiting the smoothness (regularity)

sion and function-on-function regression), in which the response variable is the PLC curve and the
predictors include product attributes and other relevant features. Specifically, we consider PLC
curves of N products (items), and each product has P attributes (features), such as brand and

2.2.
Promotion Modeling
In the practical PLC curves observed by online retailers, the product sales are subject to a variety
of promotional activities scheduled by both retailers and merchants. These promotions create
significant shocks to PLC curves, inducing spikes and irregularities at different scales. Ignoring
the promotion effect can lead to biased covariate coefficient estimates and inaccurate PLC curve
models. To retailers, separating the effect of promotion from product attributes also sheds light on
the value of promotional activities at different stages of a product life cycle, which can help plan
and schedule future promotions of new products. We present how to model the temporal effect of
promotional activities in a functional regression model below.
Because merchant promotions are often on a smaller scale and less predictable, we focus on
the platform-wise promotional events set by retailers (e.g., Prime Day/Cyber Monday of Amazon
and 618/Singles’ Day of Alibaba and JD.com). Typically, those promotional events are planned
in advance and have fixed timetables, so they can be incorporated as an additional predictor in
the functional regression model. However, unlike product attributes that are fixed throughout the
whole life cycle, promotions interact with PLC curves at different times. Therefore, we model
the promotion effect via a parametric functional curve over a fixed time window. Depending on
the nature of the promotional event, the promotion effect can be one-shot (only induces sales to
jump during the promotion time) or accumulative (the sales may climb up gradually before the
promotion time). For example, in the one-shot form, a promotion curve can be introduced as

it enjoys lower model complexity with fewer parameters that need to be estimated.
2.3.
Bayesian Functional Regression
In this section, we develop a Bayesian functional regression framework for PLC curve forecasting
based on the proposed basis space models. We choose the Bayesian forecasting model because it
allows us to (a) generate forecasts with different information sets; (b) dynamically update the PLC
curve forecasts as new sales data comes in; and (c) provide distributional forecasts in addition to
point estimate, which can help retailers make operational decisions such as inventory placement
and allocation. In particular, the ability to update forecasts in response to the new information
(e.g., new sales data) is highly valuable.

2.4.
Estimation and Prediction
For the proposed Bayesian functional regression model, estimating the posterior distribution of
parameters is intractable due to the convolution of distributions at different stages. Therefore,
we adopt the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method to draw samples from the posterior
distributions with Gibbs sampling (Neelamegham and Chintagunta 1999, Morris 2015). Specifically,
MCMC constructs a Markov chain that can approximate the target posterior distribution with its
stationary distribution. In this study, we apply Gibbs sampling to draw sequential samples from
conditional distributions specified by the hierarchical structure of the model. To ensure convergence,
we used a burn-in period of 2,000 iterations and subsequently performed a large number of iterations
to generate the target distributions.

2.5.
Clustering and Classification
The estimated coefficients Bp(t), Γ, and C(s,t) in functional regression models quantify the associa-
tion between covariates and PLC curves. In practice, the associated effect can vary among products,
i.e., products can exhibit different PLC curves even when they belong to the same subcategory
or share similar product attributes. In particular, we observe certain products are more likely to
accumulate high sales at the early stage of their PLC than others, and this difference can not be
fully explained by a single set of coefficients. As a result, fitting a single model that works well for
all products can be challenging. To alleviate this issue, we apply a hierarchical clustering method
to cluster PLC curves based on the similarity of curve geometries. Specifically, we use the Pearson
correlation coefficient to measure the degree of dissimilarity between normalized sales curves and
adopt the Ward’s linkage distance in clustering. The hierarchical clustering method is implemented

using the hclust function of R package stats. After clustering, each product is assigned a cluster
containing products that share similar sales patterns. We then fit the Bayesian functional regression
model to each cluster separately, following the estimation procedure described in Section 2.4.
For a new product like ABC, its attributes are known before launch, but the cluster label is
unknown and must be predicted (the company does not know which cluster a new product belongs
to). To fulfill this task, we train a random forest classification model to find a mapping from the
product attributes to the cluster label, i.e., a probabilistic prediction about the cluster label. This
classification model shares a similar rationale to that of Baardman et al. (2017), except our clusters
are defined by curves. The final forecast is obtained by aggregating cluster forecasts based on the
cluster probabilities (e.g., taking expectation with respect to the cluster label).
Remark 1. Our numerical result indicates that clustering can improve the out-of-sample perfor-
mance significantly. However, it should be noted that the proposed Bayesian functional regression
model can work without clustering, and clustering only plays a complementary role to our fore-
casting framework. We only choose a simple and parsimonious clustering method to illustrate its
benefit, and one can tailor the clustering method to different applications for further improvement.
In summary, our forecasting framework follows a three-step procedure described below.

1. Clustering and model training:

b. Generate PLC curve forecasts of the new product with the functional regression model

c. Aggregate the cluster PLC curve forecasts according to the cluster probabilities.

Clustering
Because the main goal of clustering is to discover the underlying similarity between PLC curves,
we first smoothed out the promotion effect by using the average sales before and after the pro-
motional event to impute the promotion sales. Note that the smoothing procedure is only applied
for clustering, and the Bayesian functional model is fitted to the original sales data. To determine
the ideal number of clusters, we compute the Silhouette width as a measure of clustering quality
(the closer the Silhouette width is to one, the better the clustering quality). The resulting optimal
number of clusters is 2, of which the curves are displayed in Figure 5. The two clusters differ in
the occurrence of the peak period: many products in cluster 1 accumulate high sales early in the
life cycle, while a majority of products in cluster 2 follow a more traditional diffusion pattern.
We also examine the two clusters by their common attributes and have two observations. First,
the percentage of products using Intel’s CPU is 86% in the second cluster, which is significantly
higher than 68% in the first cluster. Second, more than 60% of products in the second cluster have

4.5.
New Product Forecasting Results and Discussion
We test the forecasting performance of PLC curves for new products in two scenarios: (i) the
prelaunch forecast that is provided before any sales data is collected and (ii) the updated forecast
with the sales data from the first 4 weeks after product launch (4-week updated forecast). The
prelaunch forecast covers the first 12 weeks, whereas the 4-week updated forecast concerns the
next 8 weeks (i.e., weeks 5-12). These two scenarios represent the two different business contexts
faced by the online retailer: the prelaunch forecasts mainly support the preorder decision and
initial inventory placement decision, whereas the 4-week updated forecasts help the retailer plan
promotional activities and make replenishment decisions.
We compare the forecasting results generated by FB and FPCA against poly4, JD.com’s forecast,
and two methods in the literature: the functional model from Sood et al. (2009) and the tilted-
Gompertz model with exponential smoothing (TiGo-ETS) from Guo et al. (2022). The poly4 model
is estimated in a Bayesian framework, so updated forecasts can be obtained with new data (we
do not adopt the trapezoid model because it is hard to update the estimation of turning points
in a Bayesian fashion). The model from Sood et al. (2009) requires observed sales data from the
same product as inputs, so we only apply it to forecasting after launch. In contrast, TiGo-ETS can
generate prelaunch forecasts using historical complete PLC curves from other products. JD.com’s
forecasting method is adapted from a variant of the Bass model, which can be updated according
to new data.

Here we examine the posterior distribution of C(s,t) following FB. Figure 7 depicts the estimated

Specifically, the top two figures present C(s = 4,t) and C(s = 8,t) as functions of t, which evaluate
the effect of a promotional event scheduled at s = 4,8 on the sales curve. As expected, a promotional
event will boost sales during the promotional week. Moreover, prior to the promotional event, sales
may edge lower as customers hold off their purchases in expectation of future savings (referred to
as the prior effect of promotion). For example, the promotion in week 8 has a negative impact on
sales in week 6, as shown in Figure 7(b). According to the estimated C(s = 4,t) and C(s = 8,t),
such effect may only stretch to 2 weeks before the promotion. Similarly, sales in weeks posterior
to the promotion can experience a drop (referred to as the posterior effect of promotion). Figure
7(c) depicts how promotion scheduled in different weeks affects the sales in week 4. It turns out
that promotion in week 2 can reduce sales in week 4 significantly. However, both the prior effect
and posterior effect of promotion are heterogeneous and depend on the time of the promotion and
its relative position in PLCs, which underscores the complicated interactions between promotion
and PLCs. Nevertheless, we observe that the estimated promotion effect function mostly has a
single peak at the week of promotion and is relatively flat outside the promotional period. This
may partially support the superior performance of the time-invariant model that better trades off
model complexity and curve fitness.
4.5.2.
The Value of Product Attributes and Promotion Effect Modeling. To examine
the contribution of product attributes and promotion effects to new product PLC curve forecasting,
we investigate three variants of FB with different use of covariate information: FB that only
considers product attributes, FB that only considers (time-invariant) promotion effect, and FB
that considers neither of them. The overall RMSE and relative reduction in RMSE over JD.com’s
forecasts are reported in Table 7. The results illustrate that the inclusion of promotion effect and
product attributes can reduce overall RMSE by 10.5% and 6.9%, respectively. Combining them
results in a 14.8% improvement of RMSE, which stresses the value of modeling both features in PLC
curve forecasting. Interestingly, the promotion effect plays a bigger role in PLC curve forecasting
than product attributes. It highlights the importance of calibrating the promotion effect for new
products launched on online marketplaces.

Concluding Remarks
In this paper, we study how to model and forecast new product life cycle curves in a dynamic
retail environment with promotions. To overcome the limitations of existing works in connecting
covariate information with sales curves, we develop a novel PLC curve forecasting framework for
new products, harnessing the power of functional regression. Our framework models the relationship
between PLC curves and covariates explicitly through the basis function decomposition of curves.
Notably, our framework enables a flexible characterization of the effect from product attributes
and promotional events, which are critical to capturing the variability of PLC curves observed by
retailers. The resulting functional model represents a general function-on-function regression with
both scalar and functional predictors. We then design a Bayesian forecasting procedure that allows

We have run extensive numerical experiments to validate the proposed framework on large real-
world PLC data sets. When there is no covariate information (on the Dell data set), we illustrate
that functional models provide superior curve fitting performance over existing curve models.
Using JD.com’s data set that contains detailed product attributes and promotional information
for more than 300 SKUs, we show that the proposed Bayesian functional regression framework
improves forecasting accuracy against the state-of-the-art benchmarks, including JD.com’s current
forecasting algorithm. The improvement stems from the way we model product attributes and
promotions. Our results also uncover the relationship between promotional events and PLC curves
and distill useful insights for retailers.
Due to the success of Consumer-to-Manufacturer products, JD.com will launch millions more
new products to reach a broader customer base. The developed PLC curve forecasting framework
will help JD.com better match supply and demand to achieve higher operational efficiency and
customer satisfaction.
Our framework can be extended to address other relevant issues in PLC curve forecasting. First,
one can incorporate other related predictors such as social media trends and prelaunch events on
different platforms. Second, while we model PLC curves of different products independently, it is
possible to impose a correlation structure among multiple PLC curves. However, the benefit of
modeling correlation needs to be weighed against the increased model complexity and estimation
difficulty. Third, there are a large number of products that have very short life cycles and low sales
volumes. Specifically, their sales can reach a small peak (in terms of volume) within a few weeks
and diminish quickly afterward (two such examples are given in Appendix C). We may consider
them as failed products. The most common feature of those failed products is that they often

come from small brands, i.e., the companies that are less known and of smaller scale. These small
companies have relatively limited marketing resources, and as a result, it is very hard for them
to build a reputation and promote new products on a large online marketplace. Additionally, for
many failed products, there is a significant number of competing products that share the same
basic product attributes (e..g, CPU, GPU, and SSD). It is expected that the products facing a
more competitive market are more likely to fail, raising questions about their positioning strategy.
It would be valuable to develop a classification model to identify and predict the failure of new
products at the early stage of PLCs. One can incorporate our PLC curve forecasting model to
fulfill such a task. Moreover, another interesting decision-making problem is how to integrate the
functional forecasting framework into promotion schedule optimization and pricing optimization,
which can be challenging when there are multiple products launched at different times. We leave
these as future research directions.

Appendix B:
Detailed Information about PLC Length
We first compute the proper product life cycle length for each product following the method of Hu et al.
(2019): the PLC ends if the cumulative sales of an item have been less than 4% of the total sales for four
consecutive weeks. The distribution of PLC lengths in the JD.com data set is presented in Table 9. It shows
that more than 80% of products have life cycles shorter than 30 periods. Because many products have a
relatively long tail towards the end of their life cycle, the distribution implies that most products start to
enter the “mature” stage within 12 weeks. We also provide several example SKUs in Figure 12, where the
first 12-week sales are highlighted. Moreover, we calculate the ratio of product sales in the first 12 weeks to
the total sales in the entire PLC, of which the distribution is shown in Figure 11. We observe that the sales
from the first 12 weeks often account for more than 40% of the total sales.
