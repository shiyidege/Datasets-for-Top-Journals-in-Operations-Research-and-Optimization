Abstract
The increasing global demand for raw materials has led to excessive waste production
and environmental degradation, necessitating the adoption of Closed-Loop supply chains
(CLSCs). However, current models often lack a comprehensive approach that integrates con-
sumer preferences, carbon emission reduction strategies, and strategic collaborations among
supply chain members. This study develops an optimal decision-making strategy for max-
imizing proﬁtability and reducing carbon emissions in a two-period CLSC involving four
key players, including manufacturer, retailer, collector, and recycler. Using a Stackelberg
game-theoretic approach, we analyze seven alliance models and a baseline scenario (non-
alliance) to assess their impact on economic and environmental outcomes. The results reveal
that the seventh alliance, which includes all four players, achieves the highest total proﬁt
of 1830.81 units, marking a 71.16% improvement over the baseline model. Moreover, this
alliance facilitates a 44.84-unit reduction in carbon emissions, demonstrating the potential
for achieving both ﬁnancial and sustainability beneﬁts. The analysis also shows that strategic
alliances improve cost efﬁciency, with a 15.3% reduction in marginal costs, and enhance
supply chain resilience, reducing demand ﬂuctuations by 12.7%. Furthermore, sensitivity
analysis indicates that an increase in consumer sensitivity to carbon reduction from 0.3 to
0.9 leads to a 19.2% rise in demand for green products and a 14.8% increase in overall proﬁt.
These ﬁndings provide actionable insights for business leaders and policymakers. Companies
can enhance CLSC performance through strategic alliances, dynamic pricing models, and
sustainability-focused marketing campaigns. Meanwhile, policymakers can design carbon
pricing policies, tax incentives, and regulatory frameworks that promote environmentally
responsible business practices.

1 Introduction
The rapid increase in global population and industrialization has signiﬁcantly increased
the use of natural resources, leading to environmental concerns such as excessive waste
production and rising greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (Bing et al., 2016). The extraction
and utilization of raw materials have intensiﬁed environmental degradation, necessitating
sustainable approaches to mitigate these effects. According to recent studies, approximately
1.3 billion tons of waste are generated annually, and this ﬁgure is expected to double by 2050,
exacerbating the challenges associated with resource scarcity and environmental pollution
(Hoornweg & Bhada-Tata, 2012).
CLSCs have emerged as an effective solution to address these issues by integrating reverse
logistics into traditional supply chain systems. CLSCs facilitate the collection, recycling,
remanufacturing, and resale of products, thereby reducing the dependency on virgin raw
materials and minimizing waste disposal (Corbett & Savaskan, 2003; Fleischmann et al.,
2000; Guide Jr & Wassenhove, 2009). By utilizing recycled materials, CLSCs enhance
economic efﬁciency, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and promote environmental respon-
sibility (Mukhopadhyay & Ma, 2009). This approach aligns with the growing consumer
preference for environmentally friendly products, often called "green consumers," who are
willing to pay a premium for sustainable options (Atkin et al., 2012; Xu & Wang, 2018).
Governments are increasingly pressuring manufacturers to reduce carbon emissions, and
investing in sustainable practices is seen as an effective strategy to meet these demands
(Ramani & Giovanni, 2017; Taleizadeh et al., 2018; Xu & Wang, 2018). Utilizing second-
hand materials not only supports these efforts but also offers economic beneﬁts (Zhou et al.,
2019). Addressing sustainability challenges in supply chain management requires a strategic
approach that balances economic efﬁciency with environmental responsibility. While CLSCs
have been extensively studied, there is still a need for more comprehensive models that con-
sider dynamic consumer preferences, strategic alliances, and multi-period decision-making.
Many existing studies focus primarily on pricing strategies or carbon footprint reduction,
often overlooking how consumer behavior and supply chain collaboration can collectively
enhance proﬁtability and sustainability. Additionally, prior research utilizes single-period
models, which fail to capture the evolving nature of market conditions and decision-making
over time.
This study aims to develop an optimized decision-making framework for a two-period
CLSC, incorporating the roles of manufacturers, retailers, collectors, and recyclers. By inte-
grating these key players, we provide a more holistic perspective on how consumer behavior,
carbon emission reduction strategies, and cooperative alliances inﬂuence overall supply chain
performance. The study utilizes the Stackelberg game-theoretic model to evaluate different
alliance structures, determining which collaborative strategies yield the highest ﬁnancial
and environmental beneﬁts. The objective is to guide supply chain stakeholders in mak-
ing informed decisions that simultaneously maximize proﬁts and contribute to long-term
sustainability. This study aims to address the following questions:
• What is the optimal decision-making strategy for maximizing proﬁt while reducing carbon
emissions in a two-period CLSC?

• How do consumer preferences for environmentally friendly products inﬂuence demand,
pricing, and investment decisions in CLSCs?
• What are the ﬁnancial and environmental trade-offs associated with remanufacturing, recy-

• How do different alliance structures impact proﬁtability, supply chain stability, and overall

This study makes several key contributions. Unlike traditional models that focus solely
on pricing or environmental policies, this research integrates pricing decisions, carbon emis-
sion reduction strategies, and consumer behavior into a uniﬁed framework. It expands on
prior research by introducing a dynamic two-period model, allowing for a more realistic
representation of supply chain interactions over time. Most existing studies overlook the
combined impact of these factors, which are crucial for a comprehensive understanding of
supply chain efﬁciency and sustainability. By analyzing seven different alliance structures
among manufacturers, retailers, collectors, and recyclers, this research provides insights into
the most effective collaboration strategies for maximizing proﬁtability and sustainability.
This work also uniquely examines how various levels of cooperation affect pricing and envi-
ronmental goals, offering a practical perspective that can help businesses balance economic
and ecological objectives. Furthermore, utilizing the Stackelberg model, this study offers
a robust methodological foundation to optimize decision-making, align incentives among
supply chain participants, and develop more adaptive supply chain strategies in response to
market and regulatory changes.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Sect. 2 presents a review of the rele-
vant literature, identifying gaps and positioning this study within the broader CLSC research
landscape. Section 3 outlines the problem formulation, including the key assumptions and
mathematical modeling approach. Section 4 provides numerical analysis and sensitivity anal-
ysis, illustrating the impact of various parameters on supply chain performance. Section 5
discusses managerial insights derived from the ﬁndings, while Sect. 6 concludes with key
ﬁndings and directions for future research.
2 Literature review
In this section, we review the existing literature on CLSC, consumer preferences, two-period
modeling, and the collaboration of supply chain players to form alliances, speciﬁcally focus-
ing on the Stackelberg model. Our review aims to identify the research gap and situate our
study within the broader context of CLSC literature.
2.1 Consumer’s preferences and recycling
Consumer preferences play a signiﬁcant role in shaping ﬁrm policies. As consumers become
more environmentally conscious, their choices between new and remanufactured products
strongly inﬂuence producers (Guide et al., 2010). Today’s consumers are increasingly sup-
portive of green products, and both governments and ﬁrms are becoming more sensitive to
the environmental implications of product selection (Guide Jr & Wassenhove, 2009). For
instance, studies such as the one on Dell laptops show clear price differences between new
and remanufactured products, showing how consumer preferences cause price differences in
the market (Ovchinnikov, 2011). Several studies have explored different pricing strategies to
address these preferences (Ferguson & Toktay, 2006; Ovchinnikov, 2011; Xu et al., 2017).

Consumers tend to value products with minimal environmental impact, and research indi-
cates that they are willing to pay a premium for green products (Xu & Wang, 2018). For
example, the optimal pricing for new and remanufactured products has been studied to under-
stand its inﬂuence on consumer decisions (Abbey et al., 2015; Taleizadeh et al., 2023a). The
relationship between remanufactured product availability and new product demand requires
careful pricing strategies (Agrawal et al., 2015). Previous research designed different sustain-
able CLSCs, including recycling and reuse (Tirkolaee et al., 2022). Recent studies emphasize
that sustainable retail supply chains require new strategies to manage waste and improve
operational efﬁciency, as traditional retail models contribute signiﬁcantly to environmental
problems such as food waste and plastic disposal (Hübner et al., 2024). Scholars evaluated a
two-period CLSC model incorporating consumer preferences, focusing on reducing carbon
emissions and recycling rates (Xu & Wang, 2018). Another study extended this model by
including a third-party collector to enhance the system’s efﬁciency (Wang et al., 2019a).
One study also examined the impact of consumers’ low-carbon preferences and government
intervention on a three-echelon supply chain (Zhou et al., 2023). They explored how con-
sumer preferences and regulatory frameworks interact, inﬂuencing supply chain strategies
and decisions.
Our study advances this literature by distinguishing between re-manufactured and recycled
products, emphasizing how consumer preferences impact demand for both types of products.
We incorporate these preferences into a two-period CLSC model with four key players,
examining pricing strategies and carbon emission reductions that previous literature has not
comprehensively addressed.
2.2 Social responsibility and stable production
Industrial regulations and governmental policies to reduce carbon emissions have promoted
the production of eco-friendly products, fostering sustainable supply chains (Wang, 2020;
Wang et al., 2019a). Achieving environmental, social, and economic objectives helps meet
current needs while ensuring that future resources remain available. This balance is essential
for driving sustainable development (Butler, 2001; Kaboli Chalmardi & Camacho-Vallejo,
2019).
The dual role of carbon emission reduction and remanufacturing in resource conservation
and pollution mitigation is well documented (Mukhopadhyay & Ma, 2009). The stability of
supply chains is increasingly evaluated through their environmental features (Kaboli Chal-
mardi & Camacho-Vallejo, 2019; Yu & Solvang, 2016). Consumers are becoming more
attentive to ﬁrms’ social responsibilities, driving demand for environmentally friendly prod-
ucts, a concept known as "green consumerism." Investments by ﬁrms in carbon emission
reduction often led to customer attraction and increased proﬁtability (Glock et al., 2012). The
relationship between remanufacturing in CLSCs and stable production has been explored to
highlight economic and environmental beneﬁts (Taleizadeh et al., 2019; Xu & Wang, 2018).
Recentstudiesexplorerobustandrisk-awaresustainableclosed-loopsupplychainmodelsthat
integrate carbon reduction, cost efﬁciency, and employment considerations to enhance system
resilience and environmental sustainability (Lotﬁet al., 2024). Additionally, some research
suggests that a Nash-based cooperative approach can incentivize agricultural supply chains
to adopt low-carbon strategies through policy support and collaborative decision-making
(Hamido˘glu & Weber, 2024).
Recent studies have examined both remanufacturing and recycling within CLSCs, empha-

2017; Taleizadeh et al., 2017; Xu & Wang, 2018). A CLSC framework emphasizing recy-

2018). Previous research proposed a sustainable closed-loop supply chain network for per-
ishable products, aiming to minimize costs, reduce CO2 emissions, and maximize social
responsibility and consumer satisfaction (Goli et al., 2020). Researchers also designed a
sustainable closed-loop supply chain network in the aluminum industry by optimizing eco-
nomic, social, and environmental objectives. It provides a framework for social responsibility
and environmental impact estimation using life cycle assessment and novel meta-heuristic
algorithms to enhance the supply chain’s sustainability (Pahlevan et al., 2021). These studies
illustrate the growing importance of developing innovative methods and models that address
environmental and social responsibilities within CLSCs.
The role of CLSCs in promoting social responsibility, reducing greenhouse gas emis-
sions, decreasing energy usage, creating jobs, and addressing consumer sensitivities is
well-documented. Additionally, by reducing costs, adding value through recycled products,
and promoting consumer purchasing, ﬁrms can achieve stable production and proﬁtability.
Our paper explores recycling and remanufacturing processes, carbon emission reductions,
and product pricing strategies to maximize proﬁtability within the CLSC framework.
2.3 Pricing, alliance, and inventory
Some studies employed the Stackelberg model to improve supply chain participants’ condi-
tions, enhance their independence, and inﬂuence decision-making processes (Dastidar, 2004;
Parvasi & Taleizadeh, 2021; Parvasi et al., 2024a; Wang & Wang, 2010). In this model, the
manufacturer is typically viewed as the leader, with the retailer as the follower (Yang & Zhou,
2006). The strategic pricing of recycled products is increasingly recognized as a crucial ele-
ment (Ferrer & Swaminathan, 2010). The Stackelberg model has facilitated decision-making
and coordination in the supply chain by balancing cooperation and competition (Huang
& Wang, 2017). Consumer willingness to pay for new and remanufactured products has
been the subject of numerous studies focusing on pricing strategies (Abbey et al., 2015).
Recent advancements in optimization techniques, particularly metaheuristic algorithms, have

improving efﬁciency and sustainability (Weber et al., 2023). Similarly, circular economy
models have been successfully implemented in agricultural supply chains to reduce waste
and improve resource utilization, showing promising economic and environmental beneﬁts
(Shahsavani et al., 2024). A study examined a single-stage green CLSC, focusing on pricing
and cooperation between the green manufacturer, retailer, and collector to improve envi-
ronmental responsibility (Asghari et al., 2022). Two-period models have also been explored
to understand decision-making and competition among supply chain members (Taleizadeh
et al., 2023b; Webster & Mitra, 2007). For example, research that includes remanufactur-

and their impact on proﬁtability in a periodic CLSC (Xu & Wang, 2018). Another study
aimed to establish a differential game model for a dual-channel closed-loop supply chain
system, focusing on deriving recycling and pricing strategies from a dynamic perspective
(Yu et al., 2024). The inclusion of third-party collectors to increase stock product collection
has been examined in a two-period supply chain context (Yi et al., 2016). Additionally, a
two-period CLSC has been assessed by incorporating a third-party collector (Wang et al.,
2019a). Studies suggest that third-party collectors are a more cost-effective option compared
to manufacturers or retailers managing product collection themselves (Dominguez, 2020).
The competitive dynamics between new and stock products in a CLSC with three players,
including a recycler, have been analyzed, with ﬁndings suggesting that investing in recycling

et al., 2019a). The role of alliances in supply chains has been widely examined, particularly
in terms of their impact on efﬁciency, cost savings, and sustainability. For example, alliances
have been shown to improve proﬁtability, sales, product collection, recycling efﬁciency, and
overall efﬁciency (Taleizadeh et al., 2017). In a CLSC context, research has suggested that the
formation of alliances can lead to substantial proﬁts and efﬁciencies. Government interven-
tions, such as carbon tax policies and subsidy programs, along with strategic alliances among
manufacturers, retailers, and recyclers, have been studied to assess their effects on income

practices, particularly through multi-objective optimization frameworks (Tirkolaee, 2024).
Moreover, the integration of blockchain technology in supply chain networks has enhanced
transparency by providing secure and immutable transaction records. This technology also
supports environmental sustainability by optimizing resource usage and minimizing waste
generation (Babaei et al., 2024). Furthermore, recent studies emphasize the role of ﬁnancial
strategies in supply chain competition, demonstrating how pricing and ﬁnancing decisions
shape market positioning and proﬁtability (Parvasi et al., 2024b). The inﬂuence of alliances
on decision-making has been documented, with evidence suggesting that alliances increase

3 Modelling
In this study, we model a two-period sales marketing strategy for a product involving four key
players in the supply chain: a manufacturer, a retailer, a collector, and a recycler. Our goal is
to analyze how these players interact and collaborate in a CLSC to enhance proﬁtability and
sustainability.
In the ﬁrst period, the manufacturer produces new products at a cost of cm per unit. These
products are then sold to the retailer at a wholesale price of w1. The retailer, in turn, offers
these products to consumers at a retail price of p1.
In the second period, we deal with two types of stock products: remanufacturable products
and recyclable products. Remanufactured products are those that can be repaired or have
parts replaced to function like new ones. Due to their previous usage, they are sold at a lower
price than new products. On the other hand, recyclable products cannot be repaired; they
can be broken down into parts for recycling. Recycling these products helps fulﬁll social
responsibility goals by reducing the need for new raw materials and lowering greenhouse
gas emissions. We refer to these as “green products.”
The collector purchases used products from consumers, paying u1 for products that can
be remanufactured and u2 for those meant for recycling. The retailer buys remanufacturable
products from the collector at a price of v1. After refurbishing these products, the retailer
sells them to consumers at a price of p2r. The recycler acquires recyclable products from
the collector at a cost of θu1, incurs an additional recycling cost of cp per unit, and sells the
recycled materials to the manufacturer at a price of v2. The manufacturer invests a cost of c1
to produce new products and faces an additional cost c2 when raw materials are scarce. After
acquiring recycled materials from the recycler, the manufacturer produces green products and
sells them to the retailer at a wholesale price of w2g. In the second period, the market consists
of two main products: remade products sold at a retail price of p2r and green products sold
at a retail price of p2g.
Figure 1 shows the relationships among the players (manufacturer, retailer, collector,
recycler, and consumer) in the CLSC. These relationships are modeled using the Stackelberg
model, where the manufacturer acts as the leader. This model helps deﬁne the optimized
pricing strategy and the level of investment required to reduce carbon emissions.
We explore seven strategic alliances to increase proﬁtability and collaboration in collecting
products and reducing carbon emissions. These alliances are evaluated against a base model,
which does not involve any collaboration among players. The alliances are illustrated in Fig. 2
and summarized as follows:
• The ﬁrst alliance involves collaboration between the manufacturer and recycler to invest
in reducing carbon emissions, recycling materials, and producing green products.
• The second alliance sees the retailer and collector working together to gather and recycle
materials.

practices by reducing waste and carbon emissions.
Our ﬁndings indicate that applying recycling and remanufacturing strategies not only
paves the way for achieving environmental and social goals but also results in proﬁtability
and fulﬁlling economic objectives. By utilizing CLSC, managers can balance product price
and investment by reducing greenhouse gases, boosting customer satisfaction and demand.
Stakeholders, including investors and regulatory bodies, can beneﬁt from insights in this
study to evaluate the long-term sustainability of companies. By investing in businesses prior-
itizing reverse logistics and sustainable practices, stakeholders can ensure stable returns and
support corporate social responsibility.

this study, by balancing economic growth and environmental preservation. Implementing the
proposed model and forming alliances enables companies to reduce costs, optimize resource
allocation, and improve product lifecycle management. This leads to higher customer sat-
isfaction as companies can offer better pricing and quality, fostering loyalty and long-term
relationships. Moreover, the alliance strategy among supply chain members satisﬁes social

8 Conclusion
This study developed an optimal decision-making strategy for maximizing total proﬁt in a
two-period CLSC, integrating carbon emission reduction, consumer preferences, competitive
pricing, and strategic alliances among supply chain members. By employing a Stackelberg
game-theoretic model, the study systematically analyzed seven alliance models that involved
varying degrees of cooperation among manufacturers, retailers, collectors, and recyclers. The
results illustrate the profound impact of collaborative strategies on ﬁnancial and environmen-
tal outcomes, offering a structured approach for sustainable supply chain decision-making.
The ﬁndings revealed that the Seventh Alliance, which includes full collaboration among
all supply chain members, achieved the highest total proﬁt of 1830.81 units—71.16% higher
than the base model. This increase was attributed to the combined beneﬁts of optimized
inventory management, cost-sharing in carbon reduction initiatives, and improved recycling
processes. The study also conﬁrmed that higher levels of investment in emission reduction
led to greater consumer demand and proﬁtability, particularly when consumer sensitivity to
sustainability concerns is strong.
Consumer behavior emerged as a pivotal factor in shaping supply chain performance.
The sensitivity analysis demonstrated that as consumer awareness of carbon emission
reduction increased from 0.3 to 0.9, demand for green and remanufactured products grew,
signiﬁcantly improving overall supply chain proﬁtability. The responsiveness of demand to
carbon-conscious investments underscore the importance of aligning business strategies with
evolving sustainability preferences. Additionally, optimizing λ and δ played a crucial role in
determining market outcomes. The study found that when λ is between 0.5 and 1, and δ is
between 0 and 0.5, ﬁrms can maximize product demand and proﬁtability.

Strategic pricing also played a crucial role in enhancing proﬁtability. The Seventh Alliance
achieved a green product price (p2g) of 92.31 units, compared to 96.53 units in the base
model, making green products more attractive while maintaining proﬁtability. Competition
between green and remanufactured products was strongest when consumer sensitivity was
within an optimal range, allowing companies to optimize product differentiation strategies
and enhance market competitiveness. The sensitivity analysis conﬁrmed that companies that
proactively adapt their pricing strategies to consumer preferences for sustainable products
gain a signiﬁcant competitive advantage.
From an environmental perspective, the study validated that greater collaboration in the
supply chain results in signiﬁcantly lower carbon emissions. The Seventh Alliance achieved
the highest carbon emission reduction rate (e2) of 44.84 units, highlighting the role of strate-
gic partnerships in fostering sustainability. The ﬁndings reinforce that ﬁnancial success and
environmental responsibility are not competing priorities but complementary goals that busi-
nesses can achieve through well-structured CLSC strategies.
Furthermore, sensitivity analyses revealed that market demand in the second period (a2)
and production costs (c1 and cp) signiﬁcantly impact the supply chain’s proﬁtability. An
increase in a2 led to higher proﬁts across all models, with the Seventh Alliance beneﬁting
the most. Conversely, increases in c1 and cp resulted in decreased proﬁtability, underscoring
the need for efﬁcient cost management within the supply chain.
The real-world applicability of this study extends to industries such as automotive, elec-
tronics, and fashion, where closed-loop supply chains are increasingly becoming a priority.
Automobile manufacturers like Tesla and Toyota have successfully implemented battery
recycling programs, reducing reliance on virgin raw materials while enhancing proﬁtability
through remanufactured components. Similarly, companies in the electronics sector, such as
Apple, have introduced trade-in programs that align with the study’s ﬁndings on consumer
preferences for sustainable products. The fashion industry has also leveraged closed-loop
models, with brands like Patagonia and H&M offering take-back and recycling programs
that increase customer loyalty and revenue.
The results suggest that ﬁrms seeking to optimize their CLSC operations should invest
in dynamic pricing models that reﬂect consumer sensitivity to sustainability, implement
incentive-driven recycling programs, and adopt technology-driven transparency solutions,
such as blockchain-based carbon tracking and AI-powered demand forecasting. These
approaches not only improve ﬁnancial performance but also strengthen brand positioning
in sustainability-conscious markets.
Policymakers can apply the insights from this study to design regulations and incentives
that encourage sustainable practices. Carbon pricing mechanisms, tax credits for recycling
initiatives, and subsidies for green product development could further accelerate the adoption
of CLSC models across industries. Companies should therefore focus on implementing veriﬁ-
able carbon reduction strategies, such as blockchain-based emission tracking and third-party
sustainability certiﬁcations, to maintain consumer conﬁdence and regulatory compliance.
This study opens multiple exciting avenues for future research and practical applications.
Integrating policy-driven incentives, such as government regulations, carbon pricing mecha-
nisms, and subsidy programs, could further enhance sustainability outcomes. Incorporating
these factors into CLSC models would help businesses and policymakers develop more
reﬁned strategies for fostering sustainable practices.
The model’s two-period approach provides a solid foundation for decision-making but can
be expanded to multi-period or stochastic environments, better reﬂecting the dynamic nature
of real-world supply chains. By extending the model, future studies can uncover long-term
sustainability trends and optimize supply chain resilience over multiple time horizons.
