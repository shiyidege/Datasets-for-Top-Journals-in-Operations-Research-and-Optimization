Abstract
In this paper we review several optimization problems under uncertainty that can be repreВӯ
sented as the problem of finding the optimal rearrangement of a matrix. By rearrangement 
we mean a permutation of the order of the elements in each column of the matrix, such 
that each column turns out to be oppositely ordered to the sum of the others. A simple heuВӯ
ristic iterative procedure called the Rearrangement Algorithm has been designed to find 
rearranged matrices so that the vector of their rowsums is minimal in the so-called Schur 
order, a notion connected to convex stochastic dominance, and to provide approximations 
to the solutions of various challenging problems with uncertainty scenarios. By changing 
the initial design of the matrix, or by introducing a family of matrices to be rearranged 
simultaneously, or by rearranging the columns in a prescribed way or in blocks, the ReВӯ
arrangement Algorithm can be adapted to deal with several applications in a variety of 
fields including: the assembly line crew scheduling problem with uncertain labour times, 
the multi-way partitioning problem, the computation of dependence uncertainty bounds in 
finance, the fair allocation of indivisible items, and the estimation of a joint distribution 
subject to statistical uncertainty.

sible scenarios the production times of the different production lines are as synchronized as 
possible. In this case the function to be minimized is the sum of the variances of the rowВӯ
sums of all k scenario matrices.
A first algorithm able to reduce the total variance has been named the Block Swapping 
Algorithm (BSA), after the fact that it uses very simple rearrangements in which only two 
elements are swapped, but in a specific block of columns. The use of blocks of columns, 
instead of single ones, is pivotal to an improvement of the algorithm; see Sect.В 2 below.
A further customization of this algorithm (Cust BSA) prescribes, priori to the application 
of BSA, a preprocessing to find an initial assignment with an efficiently reduced variance. 
Cornilly et al. (2022) provides very detailed figures for the application of the two algorithms 
and high quality solutions also in high-dimensional set-ups.

2вҖӮ Rearranging by blocks and the partitioning problem
The quality of the approximations obtained by the RA can be improved by rearranging 
blocks of columns instead of single columns. With a proof completely analogous to that 
of PropositionВ 1.2 (just replace j and (вҲ’j) by two disjoint blocks of columns B and B s.t. 
B вҲӘB = {1, . . . , d}), one can show that for a symmetric, strictly convex function f, an optiВӯ

to the sum of the other block of columns. Under this more general perspective, the standard 
RA described in Sect.В 1 can be seen as a particular case which just considers the rearrangeВӯ
ment of each single-column block against the sum of the remaining columns.
An illustration is given by the matrix Z in Fig. 2. It is easy to check that Z is a rearranged 
matrix (has all single columns arranged oppositely to the sum of the others) but it is not optiВӯ

matrix with constant rowsums. Of course, it is possible to find non-optimal matrices where 
rearranging by blocks does not help to decrease the objective function, a trivial example is 
given again by the matrix ЛңY in RemarkВ 1.2, (ii).
The rearrangement of all possible blocks of columns is a more stringent necessary condiВӯ

idea of using rearrangements for blocks of columns was first mentioned inВ Bernard and 
McLeish (2016), and then applied inВ Boudt et al. (2018) to the classical problem of number 
partitioning.

d numbers into n sets with sums that are as equal as possible; see for instanceВ Schreiber et 
al. (2018). A brilliant introduction to the two-way (n = 2) partitioning problem is given 
inВ Hayes (2002), where the author also illustrates the connection to other fields like for 
instance the problem of fair allocation treated in Sect.В 4 below. Note that even in the case 
n = 2 the problem remains NP-hard.

problem it is more effective to use the methodology described inВ Boudt et al. (2018), based 
on variance equalization. The rationale behind the so-called BRAVE (Block Rearrangement 
Algorithm with Variance Equalization) algorithm is the fact that it is efficient to choose two 
blocks of columns so that the variance of the corresponding rowsums is as similar as posВӯ
sible. Since the selected blocks are two (the selected columns and the remaining ones), this 
turns out to be equivalent to a 2-partitioning problem, for which one can use two classical 
algorithms: the greedy algorithm (from the largest to the smallest, assign each number to the 
1вҖҜ3

one subset having the smaller sum) or the KK differencing algorithm (at each stage choose

remains in the list which represents the discrepancy of the partition).

guarantee that the necessary rearrangement conditions for optimality are satisfied.
Moreover, the BRAVE can be applied to the more general n-partitioning problem just 
by adding rows to the matrix X. A detailed study of the numerical performance of BRAVE 
is given inВ Boudt et al. (2018), the final picture being that, at a cost of little extra (but still 
polynomial) time, BRAVE is a good competitor to the various heuristic methods to solve 
the n-partitioning problem and typically outperforms both the greedy and KK algorithms. 
As further improvements, the BRAVE can also be mixed with the standard RA after a numВӯ

sampled from a Beta distribution with two parameters that can evolve over the different 
steps; seeВ Bernard et al. (2025).
3вҖӮ Dependence uncertainty bounds in finance and insurance
In the last decade the RA became extremely popular in finance and insurance because of its 
successful application to model uncertainty. Looking back in retrospect, the reason of this 
numerical success does not lie in the algorithm itself, which was already known fromВ Hsu 
(1984), CoffmanВ Jr and Yannakakis (1984), andВ RГјschendorf (1983) in the current form, but 
in the design of the starting matrix X (actually, two matrices X and X).
The story goes back to the father of modern probability theory, A.N.В Kolmogorov, who 
gave to one of his student, G. D. Makarov, the problem of finding the lower and upper 
best-possible bounds on the distribution function of a sum of d random variables with 
given marginal distributions. The problem can be equivalently stated as finding the miniВӯ
mum and maximum possible quantile of a sum of d random variables with given marginal 
distributions.
Nowadays, this problem has a relevant application to the current regulatory framework 
for banking and insurance supervision, under which banks and insurance companies have to 
compute a typically high quantile of the distribution of a sum of risks with given marginal 
distributions but no information on their interdependence. Upper and lower bounds on a 
quantile can be interpreted as a measure of model uncertainty induced by possible depenВӯ
dence scenarios. A detailed framework is described inВ Embrechts et al. (2013).
Solving the original exercise, Makarov (1981) provided the best-possible bounds for 
d = 2 random variables. Independently from MakarovвҖҷs approach,В  RГјschendorf (1982) 
gave a more elegant proof of the same result using duality. FigureВ 3 illustrates the support 
of the bivariate distribution maximizing the Оұ-quantile for the sum of two random variables 
uniformly distributed on the unit interval [0,В 1].
The solution provided by Makarov and RГјschendorf for d = 2 could not be extended to 
an arbitrary number of random variables, so the problem for general dimensions remained

Analogously to the ALCS problem with different scenarios (here each matrix V k repВӯ

applied so that each j-th column of the matrices V k is simultaneously rearranged oppositely

columns are iteratively sorted derives from the computations of covariances between the 
values received by all pairs of agents. From the design of the initial family of matrices, each 
iteration of this slightly sophisticated version of the RA is equivalent to an efficient local 
swap of one item between two agents.

procedure reduces the sum of the variance of the rowsums, approximating the ideal situation 
in which each agent believes that all agents have received the same utility. If feasible, this 
zero variance allocation is called social equality (also called equitability inВ Freeman et al., 
2019). But there might be other goals to be pursued.

satisfies an a-priori fixed optimality property, which is referred to as a fairness criterion. 
In the specialized literature, there exists a broad range of fairness notions; among the most 
popular ones are maximum social welfare and the no-envy rule, but other notions do exist as 
the already mentioned equitability rule; seeВ Thomson (2011), Lang and Rothe (2016), and 
Bouveret and LemaГ®tre (2016) for surveys.
Maximization of social welfare is a rather intuitive goal: one maximizes the sum of the 
utilities received by each of the agents involved. However, this rule might produce disapВӯ
pointing individual allocations, leaving some agents with the belief to have received less 
than the others (and hence envying them). One would rather prefer an allocation that makes 
everyone happy, that is under which each agent believes to have received the best allocaВӯ
tion. This is called an envy-free scenario. Even if envy-freeness is not always achievable in 
practice (suppose to have two agents and only one diamond), one can always try to miniВӯ
mize the maximum envy between any pair of agents. Finding a minimum envy allocation 
is another challenging, NP-hard multidimensional bottleneck problem; seeВ Bouveret and 
Lematre (2016).
Though the MinCov algorithm (like the RA) is in principle only able to reduce the variВӯ
ance of rowsums (and hence approximate social equality), it is possible to slightly change 
the design of the initial family of matrices to allow for the treatment of different fairness 
rules, including social welfare and envy. This is the very simple rationale that lies behind 
the MinCovTarget Algorithm. For each matrix one adds an extra column representing a 
negative item (a malus), which is given to each agent. This is called the target value. If

the matrices (produced by the MinCov algorithm) will output an allocation which gives to 
everybody more or less the same and, hence, by considering the malus value, each agent 
will believe to have received slightly more of the others, producing a no-envy (or minimum 
envy) allocation.
The introduction of the target value is a very clever way to allow for a trade off between 
different fairness criteria. With a null target the MinCovTarget algorithm pursues social 
equality, but by increasing the value of the target the algorithm will start to prioritize indiВӯ
vidual wealth and produce no-envy allocations. By further increasing the target value to 
infinity (actually, one can take the largest value of +(V )), a MinCovTarget allocation will 
approximate maximum social welfare.

The link between the RA and the notion of entropy stems from the problem of inferВӯ

scenarios. A first study of this connection in finance has been given in Bernard et al. (2019), 
Bernard and McLeish (2016) andВ Bernard et al. (2018), with a real application illustrated 
inВ Bondarenko and Bernard (2024).
In a financial market, suppose that traded options are available on a set of two assets 
as well as on an index which is a function of these assets. Using the price information it is 
possible to infer the risk-neutral distribution of future stock returns X,В Y ,В  as well as the 
distribution of their sum S = X + Y .
However, the multivariate dependence of the vector (X, Y ) or (X, Y, S) is not observВӯ
able directly. Similarly to what illustrated in Sect.В 3, the RA can be applied to a three-
column matrix X where each column is a quantile discretization of the distributions of 
X,В Y ,В  and вҲ’S. The iterations of the RA will then try to achieve X + Y вҲ’S = 0, that is a 
dependence structure which is consistent with the constraint that X + Y = S. Since there 
are infinitely many dependence structures that are compatible with this requirement and also 
with the marginal distributions of the assets, one could expect that each run of the RA would 
terminate with a quite different joint distribution.
The heuristic results published inВ Bernard et al. (2018) go in an opposite and promising 
direction: among all the possible joint models that are consistent with the initial data, the 
RA (the BRA version is actually used in the study) tends to output the one with maximal 
entropy; and this independently from the initial (randomized) matrix X. Roughly speakВӯ
ing, the RA spontaneously tends to find a final joint distribution of maximal entropy. Even 
though this behavior seems reasonable and consistent with the typical evolution of systems, 
providing a formal proof remains an open problem. The problem of inferring a joint distriВӯ
bution function without direct observations has been further developed and generalized to 
obtain the marginal distributions of linear combinations; seeВ Bernard et al. (2021). As more 
than a mathematical curiosity, in Bernard and McLeish (2016) the authors find the depenВӯ
dence between two uniformly distributed variables that makes the distribution of their sum 
statistically indistinguishable from a normal distribution.
A similar and possibly relevant hydrological application concerns the multivariate stoВӯ
chastic modeling of a water flow S downstream the confluence of two rivers X and Y , i.e. of 
the random behavior of the variable S = X + Y ; see Fig. 6. In practice, one can statistically 
estimate the separate distributions of X,В Y , and S via the time series of water flows meaВӯ
sured at the corresponding stations. By using a quantile discretization of the distributions 
of X, Y , and вҲ’S, one could infer the multivariate dependence of the vector (X, Y, S) by 
running the RA on the corresponding three-column matrix X, obtaining a joint distribution 
that, among all the possible ones, maximizes the entropy of the system. This would offer a 
novel, non-parametric way to estimate the multivariate dependence of a geophysical system 
under statistical uncertainty.

6вҖӮ Dependence uncertainty and other applications
Many optimization problems in a variety of fields like operations research, finance, insurВӯ
ance, and economics, can be represented in the form of finding the optimal rearrangement of 
the elements within the columns of a given matrix. Very often, such representation offers a 
more effective and computationally efficient way to attack the problem at hand, and allows 
to treat uncertainty scenarios.
For a broad variety of cost functions, a necessary condition for optimality is that each 
column of the matrix is oppositely arranged to the sum of the others, leading to a very simВӯ
ple and intuitive Rearrangement Algorithm which rearranges an initial (possibly randomВӯ
ized) matrix until finding a rearranged one. The Rearrangement algorithm aims at finding 
matrices with minimal rowsums vector with respect to the Schur order.
By changing the initial design of the matrix, or by introducing a family of matrices to be 
rearranged simultaneously, or by rearranging the columns in a prescribed way or in blocks, 
the Rearranging Algorithm can be adapted to treat a variety of problems. In this paper we 
review all the existing extensions of the algorithm and the corresponding efficiently treated 
applications.
Though remaining an heuristic procedure for a computationally hard problem (for which 
exact solutions probably cannot be found in polynomial time), and despite the existence of 
counterexamples (some of which can be mitigated by randomly breaking the ties), the RearВӯ
rangement Algorithm (or one of its more sophisticated version) proved to be able to find 
excellent approximations to the solution of several relevant problems; and this in a polyВӯ
nomial runtime. Most notably, the algorithm can work out large dimensional frameworks 
where literally no other algorithm is feasible. It can be roughly stated that the larger the 
problem, the better the Rearrangement Algorithm works. It is also worth mentioning how 
the fulfillment of a (only) necessary mathematical condition provides outputs very close to 
optimality.

inВ RГјschendorf (1983), only in the last decade it gathered a lot of popularity especially 
in the field of finance and insurance (Sect.В 3). The algorithm was given a new life in the 
paperВ Puccetti & RГјschendorf (2012), where an intelligent design of the starting matrices
