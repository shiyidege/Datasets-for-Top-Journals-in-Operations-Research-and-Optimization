Abstract
Considering grocery delivery optimization for the supermarket chain, this paper proposes
a coordinative grocery delivery service (CGDS) strategy which is implementable by the
supermarket-chain enterprise. By coordinating courier resources, the CGDS mode enhances
the operational efÔ¨Āciency of grocery delivery for the supermarket chain adopting the
individually-operated grocery delivery service (IOGDS) mode, where all stores separately
run their delivery service. For the deterministic supermarket-chain CGDS problem, this study
presents a mathematical programming model using the arc-Ô¨āow and set-partitioning formu-
lations, accounting for unique properties such as multiple depots and shared heterogeneous
couriers. A branch-and-price-and-cut method incorporating two categories of valid inequal-
ities is developed, along with an implicit enumeration algorithm for the pricing subproblem
to meet the pickup-and-delivery requirements. The computational experiments on both the
benchmark and newly-generated instance sets demonstrate the effectiveness and superiority
of the proposed solution methods. The experimental results also highlight the clear advan-
tage of the CGDS mode over the IOGDS mode, offering relevant strategic insights for the
supermarket-chain operators.

In most existing supermarket operations, the last-mile delivery is executed in a decentral-

providing a strong rationale for investigating relatively integrated operational strategies.
One such strategy, from the perspective of collaborative logistics, is courier sharing, which
is named as the coordinative grocery delivery service (CGDS) mode in this paper. In the
CGDS mode, a centrally managed planner can consolidate the delivery routes across stores
by allowing any courier, who is employed by the supermarket chain and remains afÔ¨Āliated
with a speciÔ¨Āc home store, to serve order(s) originating from any store in the chain. The CGDS
mode seeks to pool delivery resources across the network while respecting the operational
constraints such as depot-speciÔ¨Āc tour origins and courier shift requirements. The differences
in grocery delivery efÔ¨Āciency between the IOGDS and CGDS modes can be demonstrated
through the following instance. Consider a supermarket chain retaining two stores, either of
which receives two grocery orders and has only one courier available. Assume that the loading
capacity of either courier is 10 units, and the grocery loading quantity of either grocery order
is 4 units. The superiority of the CGDS mode in both courier utilization and transportation
cost is illustrated by Fig. 1. The IOGDS mode activates two couriers and requires a total
travel distance of 41 units for grocery delivery, whereas one courier (from the store receiving
grocery orders 3 and 4) is activated to complete all four grocery deliveries with a total travel
distance of 35 units in the CGDS mode.
This study is aimed at investigating the CGDS operations optimization in the deterministic
setting wherein all orders for the upcoming horizon are known prior to dispatch. Besides,
couriers begin and end their tours at Ô¨Āxed home stores, and the routing decisions must satisfy
capacity limits, service time windows, as well as contractual work-hour constraints. The
justiÔ¨Ācation for the deterministic consideration is provided from two aspects. Firstly, the
deterministic (e.g. day-ahead) planning regime, which differs fundamentally from the real-

This paper thus introduces the supermarket-chain CGDS problem (SCCGDSP), which
integrates the multi-depot courier home bases, pickup-delivery pairings, vehicle capacity
constraints, and customer time windows within a uniÔ¨Āed optimization model. Each order
is associated with a designated pickup store, and each courier must begin and end a route
at her/his home store. The coordination is achieved by allowing couriers to serve orders
originating from non-home stores, subject to all feasibility constraints. The SCCGDSP,
which generalizes the classical pickup-and-delivery problem (PDP) with time windows
(PDPTW) (Savelsbergh & Sol, 1995) to a multi-depot, cross-servicing context, gives rise
to a challenging combinatorial optimization problem. Its strong NP-hardness can be simply
established by demonstrating that the special case having only one store and one courier
with an unlimited capacity is exactly the traveling salesman problem (TSP) with time win-
dows (Lawler et al., 1991). Despite its computational intractability, this study attempts an
exact solution to the SCCGDSP, which would turn into accurate information and thus a
crucial basis for assessing the CGDS strategy. This paper develops an exact branch-and-
price-and-cut (BPC) algorithm underpinned by a specialized pulse-based pricing procedure.
Unlike the heuristic approaches commonly adopted for providing quality solutions in a non-
deterministic setting, the proposed framework is designed to yield a provably optimal solution
for the supermarket-chain coordinated in-house delivery system of moderate scale, thereby
providing rigorous performance benchmarks and methodological insights which are currently
absent from the literature. More speciÔ¨Ācally, the presented exact solution method would serve
as a mechanism for ofÔ¨āine assessment of the CGDS strategy, rather than being ready for the
practical day-to-day dispatching planning in a massive supermarket chain.
We note that this study is a sequel to Hwang et al. (2025) (i.e. our Ô¨Ārst research work on
the supermarket-chain grocery delivery optimization) in which the crowdshipping mecha-
nism instead is addressed. Contrasting sharply with the SCCGDSP, the crowdshipping-based
model studied in Hwang et al. (2025) considers the crowdsourced couriers as well as open-
ended delivery routes and is solved using a metaheuristic approach tailored to the large-scale
problem-solving environment. Together, these two studies investigate distinct but com-
plementary operational paradigms in the supermarket-chain online grocery delivery. The
contributions of this paper are threefold:
(i) Problem formulation: We deÔ¨Āne the SCCGDSP, a coordinated multi-depot PDP
(MDPDP) that captures the key operational features of the supermarket-chain in-house
grocery delivery system.
(ii) Methodological innovation: We develop an exact BPC algorithm with a pulse-based
pricing routine, representing the Ô¨Ārst exact solution approach to a multi-depot, cross-
servicing PDP in the context of coordinated in-house grocery logistics.
(iii) Managerial insights: Through computational experiments, we quantify the operational
beneÔ¨Āts of the coordinated delivery over decentralized store-level planning, i.e. the cost-
saving potential of courier coordination, which would justify the investment in relevant
complex IT systems for the CGDS mode implementation and provide the decision-
support insights for the supermarket-chain enterprises considering the network-level
coordination.

2 Literature Review
This study contributes to the literature of the PDPTW and its variants, as well as corre-
sponding exact solution approaches. Readers are referred to Parragh et al. (2008a,b) for a
comprehensive survey of the PDPs. The basic PDP is derived from combining the TSP with
the precedence constraint which mandates that the pickup must be completed prior to the
corresponding delivery by the sole courier. The branch-and-bound (BnB) method was Ô¨Ārst
applied by Kalantari et al. (1985) and then Fischetti and Toth (1989) in solving such single-
vehicle PDP (SVPDP) with up to 15 service requests. Subsequently, Ruland and Rodin (1997)
introduced the polyhedral structure of the SVPDP and proposed four classes of valid inequal-
ities which were involved in their branch-and-cut (BnC) algorithm. Considering the SVPDP
with time windows (SVPDPTW), Desrosiers et al. (1986) developed the forward dynamic
programming method which utilizes the hard time windows to reduce the solution space
by eliminating sizable states and can solve the SVPDPTW with up to 40 service requests.
The general PDPTW, which considers multiple vehicles and is relatively practical in road
freight transportation, has been extensively studied. Dumas et al. (1991) Ô¨Ārst developed a
column generation solution procedure to solve the PDPTW with up to 55 service requests.
Lu and Dessouky (2004) later designed a BnC algorithm which is capable of coping with the
PDP with/without time window constraints. The PDPTW incorporating the customer time
windows and the maximum ride time constraints of the operating vehicles for carrying pas-
sengers is known as the dial-a-ride problem (DARP). Considering the DARP, Cordeau (2006)
presented a mixed integer program containing seven classes of valid inequalities based on a
two-index formulation and developed a BnC algorithm which can solve the DARP with up to
48 service requests. Then Ropke et al. (2007) improved Cordeau‚Äôs two-index formulation for
the PDPTW by introducing new classes of inequalities and produced a BnC algorithm which
can solve the instances with up to 75 service requests. Ropke and Cordeau (2009) presented
a BPC algorithm for the PDPTW and utilized two distinct pricing subproblems separately
for the elementary and non-elementary shortest path computations. Baldacci et al. (2011)
proposed an exact algorithm for the PDPTW based on a set-partitioning-like formulation
and designed a bounding procedure which utilizes two dual ascent heuristics coupled with a
cut-and-column-generation scheme to derive the lower bounds for the linear programming
(LP) relaxation. By combining those lower bounds with the established upper bounds, they
signiÔ¨Ācantly decreased the number of variables in the formulation and effectively solved
moderate-sized instances using a commercial integer programming solver. It is worth noting

it to the hub, while ‚Äúdelivery" is deÔ¨Āned as loading goods at the hub and delivering them to

centers between cities rather than the urban logistics domain considered in our study, thus
presenting a fundamental difference in the basic setup. Bettinelli et al. (2014) considered
a MDPDP with heterogeneous Ô¨āeets and soft time windows for both pickup and delivery,
where violations incur penalty costs. Although multiple depots and heterogeneous Ô¨āeets were

restricted to serving requests originating from their home depots. Therefore, the system-wide
courier coordination with cross-store service that the SCCGDSP addresses was not captured.
Overall, the existing PDPTW and MDPDP exact-solution studies either focused on the
single-depot settings or considered multiple depots under the isolated operational structures.
None explicitly modeled the coordinated courier routing with cross-store service considering
strict pickup-delivery coupling and heterogeneous couriers. Table 1 summarizes existing

Note that the MDPDPs (cf. the last three rows of Table 1) are different from the many-to-
many vehicle routing problems (VRPs) (ňôIbi¬łs Bozyel et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Nikolopoulou
et al., 2019). Firstly, under general circumstances, the VRP does not allow for changes in
cargo-related actions during the route (Braekers et al., 2016). In the delivery-type VRPs,
for instance, the vehicle, which loads the cargo at the depot, can only unload it at the cus-
tomer‚Äôs location and reloading during the route is prohibited. Similarly, for the collection-type
VRPs (Kim et al., 2006), intermediate unloading is prohibited and the vehicle must return
to the depot for unloading after completing all collections. In contrast, the PDPs allow for
Ô¨āexible loading and unloading during the route, e.g. couriers in the SCCGDSP can pick
up new grocery orders from other stores along their way for delivery. Secondly, unlike the
SCCGDSP in which ‚Äúmulti-depot‚ÄĚ refers to multiple stores serving as the starting points
for their respective courier Ô¨āeets and as the pickup nodes, the many-to-many VRP generally
requires cross-docking actions to fulÔ¨Āllthe demand of differentsuppliers for a single customer
(or one supplier for multiple customers). Such actions can often be simpliÔ¨Āed in the PDPs as
demand split (Wang et al., 2021), i.e. fulÔ¨Ālling customer demand in relatively small/frequent
deliveries. In the SCCGDSP, where each grocery order is placed to an individual store in
the chain for fulÔ¨Āllment as explained above, there is no scenario in which multiple stores
work together to meet a single grocery order, thus differentiating it fundamentally from the
demand split or many-to-many settings.

3 Problem Description
The proposed SCCGDSP is elucidated by the following comprehensive deÔ¨Ānitions. Let n
represent the total quantity of grocery orders requiring fulÔ¨Āllment. We establish the concept
of a directed graph G = (Z, A), wherein the node set Z is deÔ¨Āned as Z = {1, . . . , n, n +
1, . . . , 2n, 2n + 1, . . . , 2n + m}, and the set of arcs is denoted as A. Considering the nature
that each order is fulÔ¨Ālled by exactly one store (i.e. there is no situation where multiple stores
prepare a single grocery order), we utilize two distinct subsets, namely P = {1, . . . , n}
and D = {n + 1, . . . , 2n}, which respectively characterize the pickup and delivery nodes.
Additionally, we introduce the set H = {2n + 1, . . . , 2n + m} to represent m stores which
receive and prepare grocery orders and deÔ¨Āne N = P ‚ą™D, i.e. Z = N ‚ą™H. These stores
also function as the depots for the associated courier Ô¨āeets. Consequently, each order i ‚ąą
{1, . . . , n} is linked to a designated pickup node i, along with a corresponding delivery
node n + i. Notably, it is essential to observe that although we establish a distinct node
categorization, distinct nodes may correspond to the same physical location, such as in the
instances where multiple orders originate from a single store, resulting in identical pickup
locations for these orders.

5 k-pricing Problems
The k-pricing problems have been deÔ¨Āned in the previous section for solving the RLMP
iteratively. Due to the similar construction, we only describe the solution approach for an
arbitrarily given k-pricing problem (kPP). The kPP in our SCCGDSP is a variant of the
classic elementary shortest path problem with resource constraints (ESPPRC) (Irnich &
Desaulniers, 2005), after considering the precedence relation (i.e., each request has a pickup
and a delivery node, and the delivery node has to be visited before the pickup node), and
the pairing relation (i.e., the pickup node has to be visited if the delivery node is visited).
Considering the N P-hardness intrinsic to the elementary shortest path problem, as conÔ¨Ārmed
by Dror (1994), it becomes crucial to address the pivotal aspect which profoundly impacts
the overall problem-solving efÔ¨Āciency. Prior studies, e.g. Ropke and Cordeau (2009) and
Cherkesly et al. (2015), had proposed to solve such ESPPRC with pickup and delivery
(ESPPRCPD) optimally through the dynamic-programming-based labeling method.
To expedite the solving process of the ESPPRCPD, we present an implicit enumeration-
inspired exact approach called adapted pulse algorithm (APA), which expands the core pulse
idea proposed by Lozano and Medaglia (2013) for the constrained shortest path problem.
The APA functions through systematically exploring potential paths from the source to the
destination node. This process can be likened to propagating a ‚Äėpulse‚Äô through the network.
Each pulse represents a potential path and carries information about the accumulated cost and
resource consumption. As the pulse advances through the network, it checks the feasibility
of the path by comparing the consumed resources against the predeÔ¨Āned constraints. If a
pulse encounters a path that exceeds any resource constraint, it is terminated early, thereby
pruning infeasible solutions from further consideration. Additionally, the algorithm employs

6 Branch-and-price-and-cut Algorithm
As an improved variant of the classic BnB algorithm, the BPC algorithm externally calls the
main framework of BnB and uses the CG approach on each node of the BnB tree to solve the
LP-relaxed set-partitioning formulated master problem for obtaining the upper bound value
of the node.
According to the classic BnB, we Ô¨Ārst construct an initial node for the subsequent BnB
tree and add such node to the node searching queue with priorities to trigger the main solution
procedure. When the node queue is not empty, the algorithm selects the node with the highest
priority to start solving. For each selected node, the algorithm constructs an RLMP of the
corresponding LMP and solves it iteratively through the CG approach. In each iteration,
the CG algorithm Ô¨Ārst solves the RLMP to obtain the matched LP solution and then Ô¨Ānds
new decision variables (columns) with the probability to improve the current LP solution.
These variables are added to the existing RLMP after resolving the pricing subproblem. The
resulting new RLMP will continue the iteration solving procedure until no new column that
satisÔ¨Āes the Ô¨Ālter condition can be discovered. After the CG algorithm receives an incumbent
LP solution, the LP relaxation can be tightened (i.e. reduce the solution space) by some
valid inequalities (i.e. cuts). Effective separation algorithms must be designed to identify
cuts violated by the incumbent LP solution. Subsequently, these cuts are incorporated into
the RLMP as constraints, and the CG algorithm is invoked to solve it once again until the
separation algorithms determine that the incumbent LP solution generated by the CG does
not violate any valid cuts. Then the loop of the cutting part ends, and the incumbent LP
optimal solution performs as an upper bound of the node of the BnB tree. The algorithm
needs to judge whether the obtained solution is a legal integer solution as follows:
‚ÄĘ If the LP solution proves to be a valid integer solution, it is also considered a feasible
solution for the original integer programming problem. Then the corresponding value
needs to be updated as a lower bound for the solution within the BnB framework.
‚ÄĘ If the LP solution does not qualify as a valid integer solution, the algorithm must then
assess the potential for obtaining an improved integer solution by pursuing exploration
into newly generated nodes resulting from branching. In any case where the upper bound
of a node remains lower than or equal to the lower bound of the framework, the pursuit of a
123

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2829‚Äď2868
2851
superior solution compared to the existing optimal integer solution becomes unattainable.
Consequently, such nodes are pruned from consideration. Conversely, if the upper bound
surpasses the lower bound, the algorithm branches the node, leading to the creation of
two new search nodes. These nodes are then placed into the search queue to await further
exploration.
Once the search queue becomes empty, signifying the completion of exploration through
all valid nodes, the algorithm concludes its process. Then the lower bound value within the
framework represents an optimal solution for the original maximization problem.

6.2 Separation Algorithms
For the RCs, we apply the separation algorithms proposed by Ropke and Cordeau (2009)
to extract the RC1 and RC2, as well as the separation algorithms introduced in Ropke et
al. (2007) to search for the RC3. These algorithms involve solving iteratively a series of
subproblems to identify and separate the violated constraints efÔ¨Āciently.
‚ÄĘ RC1 and RC2 separation: The separation algorithms for the RC1 and RC2 work by
identifying the subtours within the current solution that violate the speciÔ¨Āc constraints.
The steps involved are:
1. Identify all subtours within the current solution.
2. For each subtour, check if it violates the RC1 or RC2 constraint.
3. If a violation is found, then isolate the subtour and formulate a subproblem.
4. Solve the subproblem to correct the violation by Ô¨Ānding an alternative feasible sub-
tour.
‚ÄĘ RC3 separation: The separation algorithm for the RC3 involves a relatively complex
search for constraint violations across the entire solution:
1. Generate potential paths that may violate the RC3.
2. Evaluate each path to determine if it meets the required robustness criteria.
3. If a violation is detected, then isolate the path and solve a subproblem to Ô¨Ānd a feasible
correction.
As for the NRCs, speciÔ¨Ācally the SRI, the separation is done by pure enumeration.
‚ÄĘ SRI separation: Given a limited size for the subsets, pure enumeration would be effective
and practicable. The steps involved are:
1. Generate all possible subsets of the delivery nodes with a size of 3.
2. For each subset, check for any violation of the NRC constraints.
3. If a violation is found, then address it by adjusting the routes accordingly.

7.2 Performance of the Proposed BPC Algorithm
With the goal of evaluating the efÔ¨Āciency and effectiveness of the proposed BPC algorithm via
performance comparisons with the benchmark methods in existing literature, the objective
function considered in the SCCGDSP was set as the minimization of delivery cost instead
of the customer satisfaction maximization. Please refer to Appendix A for the details about
relevant adjustments made to the model for Ô¨Ātting the instance set RCL07. We followed the
cost calculation framework adopted in Ropke and Cordeau (2009) and Bettinelli et al. (2014),
where the term ‚Äúunit" represents an abstract cost metric rather than an actual monetary value.
In particular, these studies prioritized minimizing the number of activated couriers as the
primary objective, by imposing a Ô¨Āxed penalty of 10,000 units per courier. This modeling
approach may differ from the real-world cost structure but was retained in our study to ensure
the consistency and comparability with the referenced benchmark settings.
Owing to the prevailing use of the labeling-based methods for solving the subproblems
in the column generation framework for the PDPTW, we selected two classical labeling
approaches for performance comparisons in our experiments. This would highlight the supe-
riority of the APA, which has not been applied to the PDPTW problem yet in the existing
literature. The solutions obtained by the BPC framework in Ropke and Cordeau (2009), with
seven valid inequalities and classical forward labeling algorithm for the ESPPRC-structured
pricing subproblem, and those generated through the BnP framework in Bettinelli et al.
(2014), with the bidirectional labeling algorithm for the similar subproblem, are called RC09
and BCR14, respectively. We also refer to the solutions yielded through our proposed BPC
framework with the robust/non-robust valid inequalities (cf. Section 4) and the APA for solv-
ing the ESPPRC pricing subproblem as BPCHH24. The performance comparison results
based on the RCL07 instances for the objective function of delivery cost minimization, after
involving all originally appeared acceleration strategies, are demonstrated in Table 6. The
column zUB represents the best objective-value upper bound available in the literature. If the
instance was solved to optimality, the optimal objective value was reported in the correspond-
ing column z‚ąófor each algorithm. Otherwise, the best lower bound obtained from the LP
relaxation was provided instead.
Note that any result in bold italic in Table 6 implies that an optimal solution was not found.
It is clearly evident that, within the predetermined time limit, the BPC algorithm proposed
by this study was capable to Ô¨Ānd an optimal solution for more instances than those two
reference methods. This provides an initial demonstration of the commendable performance
of our proposed algorithm in terms of solving efÔ¨Ācacy. We also observed that, in the general
context, the computational demands of BPCHH24 exhibited a trend akin to that of RC09 (e.g.
across both instance classes AA and BB). This phenomenon can be attributed to their shared
BPC solving structure, where a similar inclination is maintained despite differences in the cuts
and subproblem resolution algorithms employed. The relatively simple solution structure and
advanced bidirectional searching scheme applied in BCR14 exhibited its superiority in certain
speciÔ¨Āc instances, e.g. AA70, BB50, BB65, and CC50. However, the lack of the integration
of valid inequalities in the algorithm led to inferior efÔ¨Āciency in solving the root node for
the most complex instance class DD and the worst-case scenario of four instances failing
to Ô¨Ānd an optimal solution. It can be concluded by the experiment results that BPCHH24
achieved satisfactory results with regard to the solution quality, computational efÔ¨Āciency, and
robustness on the RCL07 instances, which contain only one depot.

7.3 Impacts of Valid Inequalities
Recall the results presented by Ropke and Cordeau (2009) and Li et al. (2019). The RCs
which only impact the arc-Ô¨āow model (i.e. the pricing subproblem) can achieve a positive

section, we Ô¨Ārst bundled these RCs together and evaluated their effects on the solution quality
of the root node on the RCL07 instances under four distinct scenarios, to validate the claims
made in the existing literature and verify the inÔ¨āuence of the SRI on the solution. Afterward,
we conducted a further examination of the impact of each individual cut on the algorithm
performance on the HH24 instances, which involve multiple depots.
Table 7 presents the root node outcomes derived from BPCHH24 on the RCL07 instances,
for which the objective function of delivery cost minimization was considered. Drawing par-
allels with the Ô¨Āndings in Ropke and Cordeau (2009), the column labeled ‚ÄėNo_cuts‚Äô displays
the proportion of the objective-value lower bound (denoted by z, which was computed without
the inclusion of any cut) to the objective-value upper bound (designated by z, which represents
either the optimal objective value or best-known one when the optimum is not identiÔ¨Āed).
Subsequent columns elucidate the gap reduction achieved through various inequality fami-
lies, including RCs, NRCs (i.e. SRI), and Full (with all cuts). The derived values were based
on the formula: 100(z‚Ä≤ ‚ąíz)/(z ‚ąíz), where z‚Ä≤ denotes the lower bound determined with
the respective cuts. Note that occasionally a value from the ‚ÄėFull‚Äô column was lower than
that from ‚ÄėRCs‚Äô or ‚ÄėNRCs‚Äô, e.g. AA60, CC35, and CC40. Such instances arose due to the
heuristic-based separation of inequalities.
Table 7 reveals that the utilization of both forms of effective inequalities generally lifted the
quality of the root node results in the majority of cases, excluding BB55 and CC60, although

exclusively utilizing NRCs was comparable to that achieved through the implementation of
all RCs. This convergence may demonstrate that considering NRCs exclusively in a multitude
of existing BPC applications could be deemed a rational approach, as it offers a relatively
simple and efÔ¨Ācient separation method, resulting in a greater relative advantage than utilizing
heuristics to separate multiple RCs.

opposing objective. It is observed that results that align with intuition are easily recognizable.
When striving to maximize customer satisfaction, it is crucial to aim for the quickest delivery
or to complete it within a certain time frame (e.g., within [ei, fi] for any i ‚ąąD), as this
reduces the customer‚Äôs time window to some extent, which will reasonably increase the
number of activated couriers and thus raise the cost. On the other hand, when the focus is
on reducing the delivery cost, customer satisfaction will also lessen to a lower level. This
may be due to potential idle time during delivery to lower the cost of activating new couriers,
which will inevitably result in delayed order deliveries and diminished satisfaction levels.
In this regard, it is advisable for stakeholders to thoroughly assess the trade-off between
cost and customer satisfaction before Ô¨Ānalizing their distribution plan. By prioritizing and

8 Concluding Remarks
Aiming to help the supermarket-chain enterprises gain an advantage in the increasingly com-
petitive grocery delivery industry, this paper has proposed a CGDS strategy as a competitive
alternative to the traditional IOGDS mode. The distinct complexities of the CGDS-derived
SCCGDSP have been abstracted from practical implementation requirements, including mul-
tiple depots, heterogeneous shared courier Ô¨āeets, one/two-sided hard time windows. We have
formulated the SCCGDSP using both the arc-Ô¨āow and set-partitioning models, which can
accommodate different objective functions such as customer satisfaction maximization and
delivery cost minimization. Then the column-generation-based BPC framework has been
developed for the SCCGDSP solving, providing the provably optimal benchmark solutions
for the supermarket-chain stakeholders.
In developing the solution framework, two classes of valid inequalities, namely RCs and
NRCs, have been incorporated into the BPC algorithm to tighten the LP relaxation and
improve the lower bounds. To address the computational challenges of the pricing subprob-
lem, which can be classiÔ¨Āed as the ESPPRCPD, we have adapted an implicit enumeration
algorithm originally developed for the VRPTW and modiÔ¨Āed it to accommodate the spe-
ciÔ¨Āc requirements of the pickup-and-delivery constraints. The effectiveness of the proposed
framework has been demonstrated through computational experiments on the benchmark
instances with up to 75 orders (i.e. 150 pickup and delivery nodes), as well as on newly
generated multi-depot instances. The results have indicated that the NRCs in particular can
substantially strengthen the root-node bounds for both the PDP and SCCGDSP. Furthermore,
the comparisons between the CGDS and IOGDS have conÔ¨Ārmed the operational advantages
of coordinated delivery, with the optimal solutions yielded from the SCCGDSP serving as
the meaningful performance benchmark.
It is crucial to note that the SCCGDSP, which has been studied under a deterministic, day-
ahead planning setting, represents a deliberate modeling choice to enable the development
of an exact optimization framework. While such a setting is commonly adopted in super-
market operations for next-day order fulÔ¨Āllment, it inevitably abstracts away the dynamic
and stochastic elements such as real-time order arrivals, travel time uncertainty, and online
re-optimization. Extending the proposed BPC framework to dynamic or stochastic environ-
ments would introduce substantial challenges, including the need for frequent re-solving,
rapidly changing state information, and signiÔ¨Ācantly increased computational burden, which
would compromise the model tractability. Addressing these challenges would likely require
the heuristic, hybrid, or rolling-horizon solution approaches rather than purely exact meth-
ods. Exploring such extensions, as well as investigating the trade-off between customer
satisfaction and delivery cost under uncertainty, constitutes an important direction for future
research.
