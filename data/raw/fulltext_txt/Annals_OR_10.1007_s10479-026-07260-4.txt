Abstract
In post-disaster situations, swiftly delivering humanitarian assistance to victims amid chaos
and uncertainty poses a signiﬁcant challenge in practice. Furthermore, efﬁcient distribution
of restricted resources, effective inventory control, and optimal resource allocation remain
imperative priorities for humanitarian organizations that strive to meet urgent needs under
adverse conditions. This study proposes a two-echelon Covering Inventory Routing Problem
(CIRP) that integrates the Inventory Routing Problem (IRP) and the Covering Tour Problem
(CTP) to support decision-making in the distribution of medical kits in post-disaster human-
itarian logistics. A scenario-based probabilistic Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP)
model is introduced to decrease costs while adequately addressing unpredictable demand.
The applicability of the model was assessed through scenario analysis and a case study. In
addition, a three-phase matheuristic algorithm is proposed to solve the CIRP. The results
demonstrate that integrating IRP and CTP in a two-echelon structure improves both cost efﬁ-
ciency and the reach of aid delivery under uncertainty. The use of a static-dynamic inventory
approach, together with coordinated routing, effectively minimizes emergency shipments
and adapts to ﬂuctuating demand, providing valuable support for decision-making in real-
time humanitarian contexts. The three-phase matheuristic achieved cost reductions of over
70% relative to the model’s incumbent solution within the ﬁrst hour on large-scale instances,
highlighting its practical use in accelerating decision-making amid post-disaster uncertainty.

1 Introduction
Humanity has long been vulnerable to natural disasters, which continue to cause signiﬁcant
disruption despite major advances in science and technology. In recent years, catastrophic
events such as tsunamis, ﬂoods, and earthquakes have caused extensive human and economic
losses. Disaster preparedness and timely response are vital components of crisis management.
Among these components, logistics is often regarded as one of the most critical aspects
in humanitarian relief efforts (Burkart et al., 2017). Humanitarian logistics focuses on the
evacuation of affected populations and the management of the ﬂow and storage of essential
aid items such as food, shelter, medical supplies, and hygiene products (Ghaebi Panah et al.,
2025; Temiz et al., 2025). Due to severe infrastructure damage and the growing demand after
disasters, access to basic supplies is often limited. A well-organized distribution system can
reduce human suffering and prevent further loss (Oruc & Kara, 2018). In this context, efﬁcient
and sustainable management of limited logistics resources becomes essential. Humanitarian
organizations prioritize an effective allocation of resources to meet urgent needs in uncertain
and challenging conditions (Sabouhi et al., 2021). As a result, humanitarian logistics has
become a signiﬁcant ﬁeld of study in which both rapid response and resource optimization
are central themes.
One of the key challenges in this ﬁeld is the coordination of inventory management with
distribution planning. Effective inventory control is as critical as transportation to ensure
timely and cost-effective delivery of aid. The simultaneous consideration of vehicle routing
and inventory decisions, formulated as the Inventory Routing Problem (IRP), has received
increasing attention in the literature due to its potential to improve the performance of human-
itarian operations (Coelho et al., 2014; Köseli et al., 2023; Soysal, 2016).
To improve the efﬁciency of relief operations under time and cost constraints, it is crucial
to ensure that all affected areas are either visited directly or within a predeﬁned coverage
radius of a visited route (Current & Schilling, 1989). The Covering Tour Problem (CTP)
addresses this by minimizing the total route length, cost, or operating time while maximizing
coverage (Flores-Garza et al., 2017). A clustering approach within the CTP framework helps
prioritize locations based on urgency, proximity, and speciﬁc needs (Gendreau et al., 1997).
By grouping areas by geographical proximity, demand intensity, or nature of their needs,
clustering reduces complexity and provides structure to the chaotic post-disaster environment
(Kovács & Spens, 2007). This approach enables efﬁcient routing and effective allocation of
scarce resources (Jahre & Jensen, 2010), while also helping to navigate the uncertain supply
and demand dynamics common in emergencies (Özdamar et al., 2004).
From a complementary point of view, integrating the Covering Tour Problem (CTP) into
the Inventory Routing Problem (IRP) framework allows a more holistic approach to human-
itarian logistics. While the IRP focuses on cost-efﬁcient inventory management and routing
decisions, the CTP ensures that critical demand points are adequately covered, even if not
all locations are directly visited. This integration provides a robust decision support mecha-
nism that not only enhances inventory and distribution efﬁciency, but also ensures coverage
of high-priority areas. Although the application of CTP in humanitarian logistics remains
relatively underexplored in the literature, its incorporation into the IRP framework has the

potential to signiﬁcantly improve the effectiveness of relief operations by ensuring that scarce
resources are allocated and utilized in the most impactful manner.
This study addresses a tactical-level problem in post-disaster humanitarian logistics,
focusing speciﬁcally on distribution and inventory planning for medical kits. It introduces a
two-echelon Covering Inventory Routing Problem (CIRP), which integrates three key deci-
sion components: (i) selecting optimal locations for health centers to enable efﬁcient supply
distribution to demand points, (ii) determining appropriate inventory levels at these cen-
ters based on a speciﬁed inventory control policy, while accounting for possible emergency
replenishments, and (iii) planning vehicle routes to effectively deliver supplies and meet the
needs of the affected areas.
The model developed in this study is formulated as a scenario-based stochastic Mixed
Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model, aiming to minimize total costs by optimizing
the distribution plan. It adopts a two-echelon structure: in the ﬁrst echelon, supplies are trans-
ported from a central depot to designated health centers, which act as intermediate facilities;
in the second echelon, these centers distribute the medical kits to demand points. This hierar-
chical structure improves material ﬂow efﬁciency and enables coordinated decision-making
for both inventory management and vehicle routing across the network. The demand in health
centers is driven by the needs of the demand points, which are uncertain and modeled using
scenario-based usage rates for each period. To manage this uncertainty, inventory control in
health centers follows a static-dynamic policy over a ﬁnite planning horizon. Although the
order timing is ﬁxed in advance, the quantities are dynamically adjusted at the beginning of
each period based on the real demand conditions (Bookbinder & Tan, 1988). This approach
balances planning stability with operational ﬂexibility.
Additionally, this study proposes a three-phase matheuristic algorithm, built upon the
mathematical formulation, to efﬁciently solve the complex two-echelon CIRP. The heuristic
simpliﬁes the solution process by decomposing the problem into three sequential phases:
clustering, route generation, and inventory-routing optimization. In the ﬁrst phase, demand
points and potential health center locations are grouped using a set partitioning model to
form operational clusters. The second phase employs a distance-based randomized route
generation heuristic to construct feasible delivery routes within these clusters. In the ﬁnal
phase, a scenario-based stochastic MILP is used to assign routes and make inventory decisions
under demand uncertainty. To evaluate the practicality of the proposed approach, both a base
case and multiple scenarios were developed and analyzed. These analyses aim to generate
insights that improve understanding of the real-world applicability of the model and support
informed decision-making for humanitarian organizations. Furthermore, the performance of
the proposed heuristic was evaluated in larger problems to demonstrate its scalability and
computational efﬁciency. To the best of our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst study to introduce
a decision support system for the two-echelon CIRP in post-disaster humanitarian logistics
that incorporates a static-dynamic inventory control policy under demand uncertainty.
The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents a comprehensive
review of the relevant literature. Section 3 deﬁnes the two-echelon CIRP in the context of
humanitarian logistics, introduces the mathematical formulation, and outlines the proposed
solution approach. Section 4 reports the ﬁndings of the numerical analyses conducted through
a case study. Section 5 discusses key managerial insights, emphasizing the practical impli-
cations of the model and its outcomes for decision-makers in humanitarian logistics. Finally,
Section 6 concludes the study and suggests potential avenues for future research.

and Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm. Some studies in the literature address the

can access drinking water distribution points within a deﬁned time frame, minimizing travel

the selected distribution plan based on their proximity to the nearest distribution center. The
aim of Naji-Azimi et al. (2012) is to ensure that all demand points are covered within a speci-
ﬁed distance while optimizing the locations of potential satellite distribution centers, vehicle
routes, and quantities to be delivered to each center. In their study, Shaﬁee and Berglund
(2016) aim to maximize the number of protected consumers by optimizing vehicle routes to
cover areas affected by contamination events.

Various inventory control approaches have been observed in the literature, employing
static, dynamic, or hybrid (static-dynamic) policies, with differences in the way uncertainties
in the distribution of humanitarian relief are managed. Static policies dominate studies such
as Eskandari-Khanghahi et al. (2018), Sakiani et al. (2020), Chang et al. (2022), Wang and
Nie (2023), Chang et al. (2024), and Wang et al. (2024), focusing on ﬁxed planning over
time. Dynamic policies emphasizing adaptability to real-time changes are less common,
but appear in studies such as Ekici and Özener (2020) and Çankaya et al. (2019). Hybrid
static-dynamic policies that combine ﬁxed and ﬂexible strategies for increased robustness
are presented in Masoumi et al. (2022) and Zheng and Zhou (2023). Yang and Feng (2013)
employ a static policy to maximize inventory slack in the presence of uncertain travel times
and distribution rates. Tavana et al. (2018) introduced a hybrid static-dynamic approach to
optimize storage and replenishment based on the expiration dates of the product. Espejo-Díaz
and Guerrero (2019) use static policies to explore the frequency of inventory decisions in
dynamic post-disaster scenarios.
The literature review reveals that the integration of the CIRP into a static-dynamic inven-
tory control policy has signiﬁcant potential to advance the ﬁeld. This paper contributes by
developing an optimization model and a solution approach for a two-stage CIRP under
demand uncertainty. The following section outlines the problem and presents the mathemati-
cal model designed to address it. The subsequent sections examine the practical implications
of the proposed model, providing in-depth analyses that highlight its effectiveness and real-
world applicability.
3 Problem description, mathematical formulation, and solution
approach
In this section, we provide a formal deﬁnition of the problem and present its scenario-based
probabilistic MILP formulation. Following the problem description and mathematical model,
a solution strategy is introduced to efﬁciently address larger problem instances.
3.1 Problem description
The current study addresses the CIRP, which is described on a graph G = {V , A}, where
V = Vh ∪Vd ∪{0} represents the set of nodes comprising potential health centers (Vh),
demand points (Vd), the depot {0} and A = {(i, j) : i, j ∈Vh ∪Vd, i ̸= j} deﬁne the set
of arcs. The problem involves making three simultaneous decisions: selecting the location
of the health center, managing the inventory and emergency shipments for the health center,
and planning the vehicle routes between the health centers and the demand points. The ﬁrst
decision involves selecting optimal locations for health centers to function as distribution
centers, ensuring strategic placement to efﬁciently supply demand points. This decision is
associated with a ﬁxed opening cost zn for each selected health center. The second decision
pertains to inventory management, and involves a static-dynamic inventory policy in which
order timing is predetermined at the outset of the planning horizon, while order quantities are
dynamically adjusted at the beginning of each period. This methodology ensures adequate
inventory levels at health centers to accommodate ﬂuctuating demand while maintaining
coordination with routing decisions. In this structure, replenishment decisions are determined
123

by the current inventory levels observed at each health center, allowing the system to respond
to realized demand over time. Inventory levels evolve based on the quantities delivered to
demand points and, when necessary, on emergency shipments from third-party suppliers.
Emergency replenishment results in a unit cost of ϕn per unit, while unit inventory holding
in each health center incurs a cost hn for the remaining stock. The third decision concerns
the routing and distribution of supplies from health centers to demand points.
Within this two-echelon distribution network, each selected health center sources products
from a central depot using a homogeneous ﬂeet of trucks with ﬁxed capacity ω. Transportation
from the depot to health centers incurs a distribution cost θn, which reﬂects the ﬁrst-echelon
round-trip delivery expenses. The uncertainty of demand is modeled using a scenario-based
approach, where each scenario s ∈S = 1, 2..., |S| occurs with probability ps. Demand at
each demand point is modeled as scenario-dependent and is represented through a usage rate
parameter deﬁned for each demand point, period, and scenario. Accordingly, demand varies
across scenarios, capturing different possible realizations over time. This representation is
particularly suitable for humanitarian logistics settings, where demand is highly uncertain
and can change rapidly due to evolving disaster conditions. Health centers operate within a
ﬁnite planning horizon T = {1, 2, ..., |T |}, ensuring that inventory levels are synchronized
with immediate and future requirements, thus allowing for responsive stock management
under dynamic conditions. In the second echelon, the distribution of goods from health
centers to demand points uses a heterogeneous ﬂeet of vehicles Kn = {1, 2..., |Kn|}, each
vehicle k having a distinct capacity ρn,k. Transportation capacity is incorporated through
vehicle capacities in both echelons, where shipment quantities are constrained by the capac-
ity of the vehicles used for distribution. Demand points are organized into predeﬁned clusters
based on geographic proximity, facilitating effective resource allocation within each clus-
ter. Geographical proximity is adopted as the primary clustering criterion, as the proposed
coverage-based service structure relies on the assumption that nearby demand points can be
effectively served through a common representative location. In this context, spatial closeness
ensures that indirect service within a cluster remains operationally feasible and practically
meaningful. Therefore, clustering based on geographic proximity provides a simple yet real-
istic representation of service regions in humanitarian logistics settings. A primary demand
point within each cluster is designated as the main recipient of supplies, while other demand
points are served indirectly through this central node, thus simplifying routing within the
network. Only the main demand points are physically visited by vehicles and represent the
demand of their associated clusters. The set of available assignments between demand points
within the same predeﬁned cluster is denoted by B = {(i, j) : i, j ∈Vd}, where each pair
(i, j) must belong to the same cluster to be considered a valid connection within the clus-
ter. Furthermore, the cluster structure is represented by Cl, where each Cl denotes the set of
demand points assigned to the clusterl, and the total number of clusters is given by β. Routing
costs πi, j,k between nodes and unit handling costs δ at demand points are also incorporated.
This approach simpliﬁes distribution and enhances coverage, making the model suitable for
medium- to large-scale instances commonly encountered in disaster logistics. Figure 1 shows
an illustrative example of CIRP in humanitarian logistics.
The objective of this problem is to minimize total costs, which include opening, trans-
portation, inventory, and related expenses. This is achieved through the optimization of health
center locations, the implementation of a static-dynamic inventory management strategy, and
the efﬁcient planning of vehicle routes between health centers and demand points, while
accounting for ﬂuctuations in supply and demand.

3.3 A three-phase matheuristic solution approach
The two-echelon CIRP is highly complex due to its NP-hard components, such as facility
location, inventory control, and vehicle routing. The unpredictability of demand exacer-
bates this complexity. Exact methods become impractical for large instances, especially in
time-sensitive contexts such as health logistics. A matheuristic approach, which combines
optimization and heuristics, offers an effective solution by balancing accuracy and efﬁciency.
The proposed matheuristic comprises three phases. In the ﬁrst phase, a set partitioning
model is used to group predeﬁned clusters and potential health centers based on their spa-
tial relationships and operational feasibility. The second phase employs a distance-based
randomized vehicle-route generation heuristic to construct feasible delivery routes for each

3.3.1 Phase 1: grouping via set partitioning
In the ﬁrst phase, a set partitioning model is developed to group predeﬁned clusters with
potential health centers. The objective is to form meaningful, operationally feasible groups of
potential health centers and demand points within predeﬁned clusters, considering geograph-
ical proximity and demand intensity. In the proposed model, one demand point is selected in
each group as the group center, and the remaining predeﬁned clusters and potential health
centers are assigned to this center. Group centers are not predeﬁned but are determined
within the grouping process. Demand points that enable more cost-efﬁcient assignment of
surrounding nodes, taking transportation and distribution costs into account, are selected as
group centers. Vehicles located in potential health centers are assigned to the respective group
centers, laying the logistical foundation for the group setup. The objective of the model is to

positive usage rate indicates that demand exists at that location, and such points are treated as
active demand points. Therefore, the grouping procedure explicitly focuses on these active

in instances where demand points are part of a predeﬁned cluster, an additional constraint
applies: if one demand point within that cluster is linked to a group center in a particular
period, all other demand points in that cluster must also be linked to the same group center.

A distance-based randomized vehicle route-generation algorithm that prioritizes random-
ness and distance is applied to develop viable, varied routes between health centers and
demand points. This algorithm uses two data structures: predeﬁned clusters and groups
derived from the set-partitioning model. The ﬁrst structure, called the “predeﬁned_clusters,”
represents a geographical group of predeﬁned demand points. The second structure, referred
to as “set partitioning groups,” is derived from the output of a previous set partitioning model
and maps each group name to a set of nodes–including both health centers and demand
points–based on feasible assignments under decision variable Si, j,t.
The algorithm commences by creating a reverse mapping from each demand point to
its associated cluster in the “predeﬁned_clusters” dictionary. This reverse mapping is used
to enforce structural constraints during route generation: no two demand points in the same
predeﬁned cluster may appear on the same route. Singleton clusters, which consist of a single
demand point, are recognized to ensure that they are included in the generated routes. To
fairly allocate computational resources across different cluster sizes, the total number of
routes is proportionally distributed among the set-partitioning groups, based on the number
of demand points each group has. A minimum route allocation is ensured to provide equitable
representation for all groups.
The vehicle route generation process is performed independently for each set-partitioning
group. In each group, demand points and health centers are pinpointed. Multiple candidate
routes are generated for every health center within the group. Each route forms a closed
loop that begins and ends at the same health center. Demand points are incorporated into
the route using a probabilistic sampling strategy guided by the distance matrix, favoring
points closer to the current position. A random multiplier is applied to the distance weights
to enhance diversity. This is achieved by adding slight random noise to each distance value
before computing the inverse weight, introducing controlled randomness into the selection
process.
To avoid redundancy, the system keeps track of previously generated routes and ﬁlters out
duplicates. This iterative procedure continues until the current group’s route limit is reached
or a stagnation threshold is met, indicating that no new valid routes are being identiﬁed. The
ﬁnal routes are organized in a structured dictionary that links each set-partitioning group to
its corresponding list of valid routes.
3.3.3 Phase 3: vehicle route assignment and inventory planning
The concluding phase of the proposed three-stage matheuristic algorithm addresses the
problem of fulﬁlling demand through pre-generated, feasible delivery routes and planning
inventory ﬂows under uncertain demand conditions. During this step, a scenario-based prob-
abilistic MILP model is formulated to determine the optimal selection and operation of these
routes across various time frames and demand scenarios. These routes, together with their
associated health centers and demand points, are generated in Phase 2. The objective is to
satisfy demand at each demand point while minimizing total cost, including ﬁxed opening
costs, distribution and transportation costs, inventory holding costs, emergency shipment
costs, and handling costs. The model integrates inventory dynamics and emergency logis-
tics while respecting route capacities, delivery-assignment feasibility, and facility-operation
constraints.

4 Numerical analyses
This section presents a detailed analysis using the proposed mathematical model and the
heuristic algorithm to evaluate their performance and beneﬁts across varying instance sizes.
We offer a comprehensive review of the results obtained from applying the model, with a
primary focus on its performance under representative problem settings. Additionally, we
evaluate the practical beneﬁts of incorporating speciﬁc features into the decision-making
process, including a static inventory control strategy and the omission of routing. The per-
formance of the model is measured against a set of predeﬁned key performance indicators
(KPIs), which include: (i) direct distribution cost, (ii) opening cost, (iii) inventory cost, (iv)
emergency cost, (v) routing cost, and (vi) handling costs. Lastly, we evaluate the effectiveness
of the heuristic algorithm in addressing larger problem instances. For consistency, a relative
MIP gap tolerance of 0.02% was applied for the base case and the scenario analyses.

4.1 Base case description
This section outlines the application of the proposed model for the distribution and inventory
planning of health kits to be delivered to disaster victims in a potential post-earthquake
scenario in Istanbul, Türkiye. Türkiye has a history of signiﬁcant seismic activity due to
numerousfaultlinesthatintersectintheregion,leadingtofrequentandimpactfulearthquakes.
Istanbul, Türkiye’s most populous city, has experienced severe earthquakes in the past and
remains vulnerable to seismic disasters (Kılcı et al., 2015). Given its high risk of signiﬁcant
damage in a major earthquake, the investigation was focused on Istanbul’s Kartal district to
evaluate its disaster preparedness. Based on the approaches described in Temiz et al. (2025)
and Mutlu et al. (2026), possible location and demand points of the health center are selected.
The study includes one depot (D0), three potential health centers (HCs), and 12 demand points
(DPs). The locations of the nodes on Google Maps are presented in Figure 3.
The planning horizon spans four days. Vehicle capacities are tailored to the kits’ speciﬁc
dimensions and volume requirements. The model assumes a homogeneous ﬂeet for direct
transportation from the depot to health centers, with each vehicle capable of carrying up to
2,933 units. Direct transportation costs are based on a fuel consumption rate of 50 liters per
100 km. In the second echelon, a homogeneous ﬂeet of vehicles with a capacity of 1,566 units
is used to move supplies from health centers to demand points. Each health center has access
to two vehicles. The routing cost in this stage is calculated based on the distance traveled (see
Table 11), using a fuel consumption rate of 45 liters per 100 km. In both transportation stages,
a fuel price of e1.13 per liter is applied ((Webﬂeet, 2024); (GlobalPetrolPrices, 2024)). For
the second stage, this distance-based cost is combined with a ﬁxed operational cost of e75
per health center, ensuring consistency in total transportation expenses. The detailed routing
costs included in the second stage are presented in Table 12. This approach captures both
variable and ﬁxed cost components, providing a comprehensive representation of the routing
cost structure while considering vehicle capacity and ﬂeet availability.

4.5 Analyses on optimization model performance
The performance analysis presented in this section is motivated by the observation that
the MILP model does not reach optimality even after extended computation. Speciﬁcally,
the model continues to exhibit an optimality gap despite up to 24 hours of solution time.
This persistent gap suggests that the problem structure, with its complex constraints and
uncertain scenarios, poses considerable computational challenges. As the solver progresses,
improvements in the objective value and lower bound become marginal and convergence
slows considerably. Therefore, evaluating how the model behaves over extended time hori-
zons provides valuable insights into its tractability and solution quality. This analysis also
justiﬁes the need for alternative solution methods, such as heuristics, to generate high-quality
solutions within reasonable computational limits.
Figure 4 illustrates the performance of the base case on increasing computation times,
ranging from 3 to 24 hours. Despite the extended computation time, the model does not
reach optimality. This is indicated by the persistent optimality gap (MILP gap), which
remains above 1.7%. Although both the feasible solution cost and the lower bound show
slight improvements, the MILP gap stabilizes after 18 hours, indicating that further compu-
tation yields limited beneﬁts. In particular, even at the 24-hour mark, the base case remains
unsolved to optimality. This computational intractability motivated the development of a
dedicated heuristic approach to obtain high-quality solutions within a practical time frame.
4.6 Analyses on larger-sized instances
This section evaluates the performance of the proposed heuristic in terms of both compu-
tational efﬁciency and solution quality. A total of 12 problem instances, each with varying
numbers of health centers and demand points, were used to test the approach. These instances
were designed to reﬂect various operational scenarios and demand distributions, enabling a
robust assessment of the heuristic’s adaptability and scalability.
123

Annals of Operations Research
Fig. 4 Base case performance by computation time
To evaluate the performance of the proposed heuristic, instance data were constructed
speciﬁcally for the problem setting. Distance data between health centers and demand points
were derived from selected instances of the Traveling Salesman Problem Library (TSPLIB)
repository (Reinelt, 1991). The following TSPLIB instances were used in this study: ftv33,
ftv35, ftv38, ftv44, ftv47, ftv55, ftv64, ftv70, kroA100, kroC100, kroD100, and kroE100. These
benchmark problems, named and provided in the TSPLIB collection, were selected to repre-
sent a variety of network sizes and spatial layouts. Based on their spatial data, clusters were
formed to group demand points and health centers. The number and size of clusters vary
according to the node count of each instance. To ensure realistic distance scaling, the values
of the ftv instances were divided by 10, while those of the kro instances were divided by 100.
To construct the data set required for the set partitioning model, only one additional
parameter (li,t) was introduced beyond those already deﬁned in the base case. This binary
parameter takes the value of 1 if the demand point i ∈Vd has a positive usage rate in any
scenario during period t ∈T , and 0 otherwise. It ensures that only demand points with
actual demand activity are considered for group assignment. Apart from this parameter, all
other data values required for the set partitioning model were adopted directly from the base
case. For each test instance, the set partitioning model was executed for a ﬁxed time limit of
600 seconds, and the resulting solution was used in the subsequent phases of the heuristic
framework.
In the second phase, the parameter total_route_limit speciﬁes the maximum num-
ber of routes to generate across all groups. This parameter is adjusted based on the total
number of demand points and groups in the instance. In the test cases used in this study, the
total route limit ranges from 450 to 1500, depending on the instance size. To promote route
diversity, controlled randomness is introduced during route generation by multiplying each
distance by a random factor between 0.8 and 1.2 before computing selection probabilities
proportional to the inverse of the perturbed distance.
In the third phase of the proposed three-phase matheuristic algorithm, several additional
sets and parameters are introduced beyond those deﬁned in the base case. The set 
n rep-
resents the set of feasible delivery routes associated with each health center. These routes
are the output of the second phase of the heuristic approach and serve as input for the ﬁnal

Annals of Operations Research
optimization model. For each route r in this set, the subset ξn,r contains the demand points
visited along the route. The parameter ψ denotes the route capacity and is set equal to the
vehicle capacity used in the base case’s second-tier distribution, ensuring consistency across
the modeling framework. The parameter ui, j,t is a binary input that replaces a decision vari-
able used in the original MILP model. It takes the value 1 if demand point i is assigned to
demand point j in period t, and 0 otherwise. The grouping results from the set partitioning
model determine the values of this parameter. If a demand point is identiﬁed as the center of
its respective group, it is assigned to itself (i.e., ui,i,t = 1). If the point is not the group center,
it is assigned to the geographically closest demand point that is designated as the group center
within the same group. This assignment structure ensures that each demand point is associ-
ated with a representative delivery node, facilitating route selection and inventory planning
in the framework’s ﬁnal phase.
All remaining parameters across the three phases of the solution framework were adopted
from the base case, ensuring consistency throughout the modeling process.
The performance of the proposed heuristic was ﬁrst evaluated in the base case to assess
its efﬁciency relative to the MILP model. As a reference point, the base case was solved in
17 minutes, yielding a solution cost of 6679.4 with an optimality gap of 2%. In contrast, the
proposed heuristic produced a feasible and optimal solution for the same instance in just 38
seconds, with a slightly higher cost of 6887.6. Following this initial evaluation, the heuristic
was further tested on larger, more complex instances to assess its scalability and robustness.
Table 10 shows the results of the comparison of the MILP model with the proposed
three-phase matheuristic algorithm for larger instances.
The performance of the proposed three-phase matheuristic was evaluated in a range of
benchmark instances with varying numbers of demand points and health centers. For each
instance, the heuristic was executed within time limits of 1, 2, and 3 hours, and its performance
was evaluated based on the feasibility of the solution, the cost efﬁciency and the convergence
behavior as the runtime increased.
The results indicate that the heuristic consistently produced feasible solutions in all
instances tested, including those where the MILP model failed to provide any output due to
time or memory limitations (e.g., instances ftv64, kroA100, kroC100, kroD100, and kroE100).
This conﬁrms the scalability and robustness of the matheuristic approach to handle large-scale
CIRP instances.
For smaller instances, such as ftv33 and ftv35, the heuristic exhibited rapid convergence.
Within an hour, it already outperformed the MILP model in terms of cost (e.g., 29.8% and
17.9% reductions, respectively). However, some marginal degradation occurred in subse-
quent runs due to the heuristic’s nondeterministic nature. Overall, the heuristic consistently
produced feasible solutions in all tested instances, with signiﬁcant improvements observed
as the time limit increased. In particular, the average gap between the heuristic and model
solutions decreased from 1-hour to 3-hour runs, indicating effective solution reﬁnement over
time. In particular, for instances such as ftv44 and ftv47, the heuristic achieved more than
70% cost reduction compared to the initial model output within the ﬁrst hour, with marginal
additional improvements thereafter. This suggests that the heuristic can generate high-quality
solutions even within a limited computational time.
These ﬁndings reinforce the effectiveness of the matheuristic approach in solving medium-
and large-scale CIRP instances. Particularly under strict time and memory constraints, the
proposed method demonstrates superior practical applicability, offering reliable and cost-
effective solutions where exact optimization becomes computationally infeasible.

5 Managerial insights
The results of this study provide important managerial insights for decision-makers designing
and managing humanitarian logistics systems under uncertainty. First, the results under-
score the critical role of inventory management ﬂexibility in ensuring service reliability.
The static-dynamic inventory policy applied in the base case scenario allows health centers
to dynamically adjust their order quantities over different time periods, facilitating a more
effective response to demand ﬂuctuations. This adaptability markedly reduces the depen-
dency on costly emergency shipments, albeit with a marginal increase in inventory costs.
In contrast, static policy, despite its simplicity in implementation, incurs higher total costs
due to its inability to accommodate actual demand variations, highlighting the importance of
adopting inventory strategies that improve operational responsiveness in uncertain environ-
ments.
Another signiﬁcant insight is the substantial efﬁciency gains from routing-based distribu-
tion strategies, particularly milk run deliveries in the second echelon. Compared to a direct
delivery approach, the base case solution achieves a 55.7% reduction in total cost, primar-
ily due to optimized vehicle utilization and route consolidation. This ﬁnding suggests that
enabling a vehicle to serve multiple demand points within a single trip, particularly when
these points are geographically close, yields considerable beneﬁts for transportation and facil-
ity utilization. Consequently, in medium- to large-scale humanitarian operations, adopting
consolidated routing approaches can signiﬁcantly enhance cost-effectiveness and logistical
performance.
In addition, the analysis reveals that strategies that appear administratively straightfor-
ward, such as ﬁxed inventory levels or one-to-one delivery routes, can incur hidden costs
when they fail to respond to dynamic demand patterns. Although these strategies may be
appropriate in highly predictable or low-variability contexts, their inﬂexibility can lead to
service failures and increased reliance on reactive measures, such as emergency shipments, in
more volatile environments. Therefore, decision-makers must carefully assess the simplicity
of these policies against the operational risks they present.
Furthermore, cluster-based delivery planning, in which demand points are aggregated and
a primary delivery location is selected within each cluster, effectively streamlines distri-
bution operations. This approach simpliﬁes routing while ensuring comprehensive demand
coverage, thereby supporting both cost efﬁciency and operational scalability. Humanitarian
logistics decision-makers can beneﬁt from incorporating such spatial structuring techniques
to manage large networks with limited resources.
In general, the study emphasizes that achieving a balance between cost efﬁciency and ser-
vice quality requires an integrated approach to facility location, inventory management, and
vehicle routing. Making these decisions independently could compromise the overall effec-
tiveness of the system. Instead, models that consider these components in an integrated way
can provide practical, ﬂexible, and cost-effective solutions given the constraints and needs
of humanitarian operations. In post-disaster environments, where rapid action and careful
resource allocation are essential, this integrated perspective proves especially valuable.
Furthermore, the computational ﬁndings validate the practical utility of the proposed three-
phase matheuristic algorithm, speciﬁcally developed to address the integrated challenges
posed by the CIRP model. From a managerial perspective, this heuristic offers a viable
alternative to exact optimization methods, particularly in time-sensitive, resource-constrained
contexts in humanitarian logistics. Its ability to generate feasible, high-quality solutions in
a fraction of the time required by the MILP model, especially for large-scale instances,
123

Annals of Operations Research
demonstrates its applicability in real-world situations where quick and reliable decision-
making is imperative. For example, while the MILP model encountered difﬁculties or failed to
yield results within reasonable time frames for several benchmark instances, the matheuristic
consistently delivered robust solutions and, in some cases, even surpassed the model in terms
of cost within short computational periods. This suggests that logistics planners can use such
scalable heuristics to enable rapid, effective decision-making, thereby enhancing the overall
agility and responsiveness of humanitarian operations.
6 Conclusion
This study proposes a scenario-based stochastic MILP model to address the distribution
and inventory planning of health kits for disaster victims in the context of post-disaster
humanitarian logistics. The model integrates IRP with CTP in a two-echelon structure to
ensure efﬁcient and timely delivery of aid. The objective of the model is to reduce overall
expenses, encompassing the ﬁxed costs of establishing health centers, the distribution costs
from the depot to these centers, the transportation expenses between health centers and
demand points, the costs associated with inventory storage at health centers, and the costs
of emergency shipments received by these centers. Cost minimization is crucial in the post-
disaster context, where efﬁcient use of limited resources ensures the sustainability of relief
operations and helps reach more affected individuals. In this regard, by integrating the IRP
and CTP approaches, the proposed CIRP provides a comprehensive framework that supports
effective service delivery through optimal health center location selection, inventory level
management, emergency shipment planning, and strategic vehicle routing.
Themodelisbuiltonatwo-echelondistributionstructure,wheresuppliesareﬁrstsentfrom
a depot to potential health centers and then delivered to demand points. Inventory decisions
follow a static-dynamic policy that allows stock levels to be managed across multiple periods.
A stochastic MILP model based on scenarios is formulated to account for the unpredictable
nature of demand.
To evaluate the practical relevance of the proposed model, a real-world case study was
conducted for the post-earthquake distribution of health kits in Istanbul, Türkiye. The model
was tested over a four-day planning horizon. Compared to a purely static inventory strategy,
the proposed static-dynamic policy resulted in a 1.21% reduction in total cost, primarily due
to lower emergency shipment needs, although it incurred higher inventory-related expenses.
This trade-off highlights the beneﬁt of ﬂexible planning in uncertain environments. Addition-
ally, integrating a coverage-based routing structure signiﬁcantly improved system efﬁciency
by enhancing access to remote or difﬁcult-to-reach demand points. The model also achieved
a good balance between vehicle capacity utilization and inventory requirements.
A comparison between milk-run routing and direct-delivery approaches revealed that
eliminating route optimization led to a notable increase in total logistics costs. Speciﬁcally,
the absence of coordinated routing led to higher transportation expenses and facility setup
costs, underscoring the strategic value of route consolidation in humanitarian supply chains.
The base case scenario, which incorporates routing-based distribution, particularly milk run
delivery in the second echelon, demonstrates a 55.7% reduction in total cost relative to the
direct delivery approach, further highlighting the efﬁciency gains enabled by coordinated
routing.
To address the computational challenges of solving large-scale instances with exact meth-
ods, a customized three-phase matheuristic algorithm was introduced. Overall, the proposed
123

Annals of Operations Research
three-phase matheuristic appears to perform particularly well with larger instances. Although
it showed improvements over the MILP model in just one hour in smaller cases, its real advan-
tage became more evident in more complex instances. For some of these larger cases, the
heuristic reduced the cost by more than 70% in the ﬁrst hour alone and continued to improve
slightly in subsequent runs. In contrast, the MILP model sometimes failed to produce any
results in a reasonable time frame for these larger problems. This underlines the practical
strength of our heuristic. In real-world conditions, where time is limited and resources are
often scarce, such an approach could provide decision-makers in humanitarian logistics with
a faster, yet reliable alternative.
The study emphasizes the importance of combining two-level distribution strategies with
adaptive inventory control to deliver efﬁcient aid under uncertainty. The heuristic supports
decision-making under computational resource constraints, providing a robust tool for emer-
gency logistics planning. It integrated inventory and coverage decisions in a two-echelon
post-disaster setting, a combination that remains relatively uncommon in the literature. Future
research could enhance the model by incorporating real-time updates, accounting for uncer-
tain travel times, integrating drones and alternative transportation, implementing intelligent
routing, and adapting it to various disasters, such as ﬂoods and wildﬁres.

The total number of clusters, denoted by β, is determined based on the spatial distribution
of demand points and potential health center locations. Clusters are deﬁned to ensure that
demand points within each cluster are geographically close, resulting in compact service
regions. At the same time, the number of clusters is selected to maintain operational feasibility
in routing and coverage decisions, avoiding excessively large or overly fragmented cluster
structures.
Vehicle capacities are determined based on the volume of health kits and the loading
capacities of the vehicles. According to the referenced study (Rawls & Turnquist, 2010), the
storage volume of a single health kit is reported as 1.16 f t3, corresponding to approximately
0.033 m3 in the metric system. For ﬁrst-echelon transportation (depot to health centers), a
standard semi-trailer with an approximate loading capacity of 96 m3 is considered. Based
on this approximation, the vehicle capacity is estimated to be approximately 2933 units. For
second-echelon distribution (health centers to demand points), a medium-sized rigid truck
with an approximate loading capacity of 51 m3 is used. Using the same approach, the vehicle
capacity is estimated to be approximately 1566 units. Accordingly, vehicle capacities are
determined by considering the relationship between the physical volume of health kits and
vehicle loading capacities.
Fuel consumption values are speciﬁed based on the vehicle types used in each echelon. For
ﬁrst-echelon transportation, semi-trailer trucks are assumed to consume 50 liters per 100 km,
while for second-echelon distribution, medium-sized rigid trucks are assumed to consume
45 liters per 100 km. These values fall within commonly reported ranges in the literature and
industry reports and are used to represent long-distance and urban distribution operations in
humanitarian logistics settings.

The routing cost structure in the second echelon consists of both variable (distance-based)
and ﬁxed components. The variable routing cost associated with traveling between nodes i
and j using vehicle k ∈Kn is deﬁned as:
πi, j,k = di, j × 0.45 × 1.13
where di, j denotes the distance between nodes i and j, 0.45 represents the fuel consumption
rate (liters per km), and 1.13 denotes the fuel price (e/liter). In addition to the distance-based
cost, a ﬁxed operational cost of e75 per health center is included to cover handling and
dispatching activities.
The distribution cost from the depot to health center n ∈Vh, denoted by θn, is calculated
as:
θn = 2 × d0,n × 0.5 × 1.13 + 100
where d0,n represents the distance between the depot and health center n, 0.5 denotes the fuel
consumption rate (liters per km), and 1.13 is the fuel price (e/liter). The factor of 2 accounts
for round-trip transportation, while the ﬁxed cost of e100 represents loading, unloading, and
dispatching operations associated with ﬁrst-echelon deliveries.
