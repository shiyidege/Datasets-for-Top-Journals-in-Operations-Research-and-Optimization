Abstract
When dealing with binary regression problems, it is common practice to address first 
variable selection and model fitting on a training set, and then to assess its prediction 
performance on a test set. In this setting, the paper casts a feature selection algorithm for 
logistic regression that jointly optimizes explicative and predictive abilities of the avail­
able information set. To this aim, a forward search is implemented within the covariate 
space that iteratively selects the predictor whose inclusion in the model yields the highest 
significant increase in the Area Under the ROC curve (AUC) with respect to the previous 
step. The resulting procedure adheres to a parsimony principle and returns the relative 
contribution of each regressor in the prediction accuracy of the final model. The proposal 
is show-cased with a study on financial literacy and pension planning, on the wake of the 
survey on Household Income and Wealth run by the Bank of Italy in 2020. Indeed, recent 
literature in behavioral economic and finance highlight that boosting financial literacy 
among the population is a key strategy to sustain efficacy of both public policies and 
individual well-being face to the ageing of the population and times of economic crises.
Keywords  Variable Selection · Logistic Regression · AUC · Financial Literacy · Pension 
planning
1  Introduction
Many algorithms and methods are currently available within the statistical literature to 
perform variable selection in logistic regression. Starting from the classical ones pertain­
ing to Generalized Linear Models (forward, backward, stepwise, best-subset search), to 
methods tackling high-dimensionality, like screening Fan and Lv (2008), regularization and 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
penalization afforded by LASSO and its variants (see Hastie et al. 2020 for a recent over­
view on their relative performance), it is worth to notice that the assessment of prediction 
performance is usually a subsequent stage of the variable selection phase, with no direct 
evaluation of the contribution of each predictor towards the final prediction accuracy. One 
notable exception is provided by Pepe et al. (2006), in which model estimation is performed 
with an optimization of the AUC indicator, parameterized in terms of regression coeffi­
cients. To fill in this gap, the paper contributes to the state of the art with a forward variable 
selection for logistic regression that chooses, at each step, the (significant) predictor which 
yields the highest significant increase in prediction performance with respect to the previous 
model. We resort to the Area Under the Curve (AUC) indicator based on the construction of 
Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves (Fawcett, 2006) to evaluate the prediction 
abilities of the estimated model. As a matter of fact, this measure is widely accepted, easy to 
interpret, and—most importantly—the literature provides sound statistical inference tools 
to compare correlated ROC curves. As a general principle, the algorithm can be adapted to 
integrate other performance measure in the selection phase.
The proposed method has a broad applicative extent. Hereafter, it will be tested with 
a two-stage analysis, framed within the research on behavioral finance, financial literacy 
and pension planning. As a matter of fact, the ageing of the population witnessed by sev­
eral industrialized countries along with the occurrence of economic crises, act as a lever­
age to the urge of promoting financial literacy of the population and, in general, financial 
behaviors towards a sustainable longevity risk management. On the wake of the financial 
literacy module integrated in the 2020 edition of the Survey of Household Income and 
Wealth (SHIW) carried out by the Bank of Italy, we show-case the proposed variable selec­
tion algorithm by first quantifying the extent by which subjective characteristics contribute 
jointly to explain and predict financial literacy of the individuals, and then, by measuring 
the effect of financial literacy in explaining and predicting pension planning, among other 
individual characteristics.
The paper is organized as follow: Sect. 2 sets the methodological framework and intro­
duces the proposed variable selection method. Then, Sect. 3 provides the background on 
financial literacy and pension planning, with the presentation of the data under examine 
delivered in Sect. 3.1. The case study is illustrated and discussed in Sect. 4, in a comparative 
perspective with other variable selection methods. Concluding remarks are addressed in 
Sect. 5. Devoted appendixes at the end of the paper supplement the discussion with explor­
atory data analysis and further applications on the case study.
2  A feature selection algorithm optimizing explicative and predictive 
performance of logistic regression

is a well-acknowledged and popular method to solve binary classification problems and to 
identify the main drivers xi of the probability of observing a binary outcome Yi ∈{0, 1}, 
with Yi = 1 corresponding to the event of interest.

Important methodological and applicative issues concern the selection of the most meaning­

An Overview of Variable Selection for Logistic Regression The following list is meant 
to provide a non-exhaustive summary of the variable selection strategies available in the 
statistical literature that mostly fit with the proposed framework for analogies in either 
goals and methods. Given this circumstance, Bayesian variable selection are not discussed: 
see Tian et al. (2019) for more details.
 
●
Regularization methods, like Lasso, Grouped Lasso—Fused Lasso and Elastic Net, are 
suitable to perform variable selection in high dimensional settings (Tibshirani, 1996; 
Friedman et al., 2010; Tay et al., 2023). The shrinkage procedure forces selected regres­
sion parameters to collapse towards zero, performing a penalized estimation and thus 
calibrating the effect of predictors on the response: the optimal value for the regulariza­
tion parameter is determined with cross-validation;
 
●
As for linear regression, variable selection for GLM can be performed with forward, 
backward, stepwise, best-subset search on the basis of information criteria; see Bertsi­
mas et al. (2016) for an extensive study suggesting that the gained feasibility of best-
subset practices goes hand in hand with better predictive performance. On this wake, 
a further investigation carried out in Hastie et al. (2020) concluded that “neither best-
subset nor the LASSO uniformly dominate the other, with best-subset generally per­
forming better in very high signal-to-noise (SNR) ratio regimes, and the LASSO better 
in low SNR regimes”.
 
●
Sure Independence Screening (SIS), introduced in Fan and Lv (2008) for linear re­
gression and in Giordano et al. (2020) for Generalized Extreme Value regression for 
imbalanced binary data, is a sort of soft regularization: regression parameters are not 
collapsed to zero, but they are ranked according to their importance. Then, only the most 
relevant ones are retained. Empirically, the procedure can be implemented by repeating 
the ranking scheme on random sub-samples of the original data set, and then retaining 
only the ones occurring more frequently across replications;
 
●
Knockoffs filtering (Candès et al., 2018; Yuan et al., 2024) is a procedure consisting 
in creating copies of the predictor variables, which are independent among themselves 
but share the same association with the response as the original regression, allowing to 
control the false discovery rate when performing variable selection.1

For binary classification problems, a popular indicator of the prediction performance 
of a given method is the ROC curve2 (Fawcett, 2006): this curve is obtained by plotting 
values of specificity against those of sensitivity for the classification outcome. Specifically, 
the resulting Area Under the Curve (AUC), ranging in [ 1

indicating performances similar to that of a random allocation (no discrimination ability of 
the binary classifier), and prediction improving with values approaching the upper bound 
(Bamber, 1975). As heuristic decision rule, AUC ∈[0.7, 0.8) are considered acceptable, 
AUC ∈[0.8, 0.9) are considered excellent, and AUC ≥0.9 are considered outstanding 
(Mandrekar, 2010): see de Hond et al. (2022) for a survey of the different interpretation 
of AUC values to assess the poor–fair–good–very good or excellent classification perfor­
mance. For the purpose of the forthcoming application, from inspection of the literature 
it can be concluded heuristically that values over 0.7 can be considered at least fair or 
sufficiently satisfactory, especially for traits difficult to predict, like financial literacy and 
propensity to pension planning.
A consistent bulk of applied research has been carried out on the topics to assess predic­
tion performance of different methods with a comparative perspective and given a specific 
domain, and very often the AUC value is considered as the target outcome to be maximized. 
Other possibilities exist, for instance the Brier Score (Brier, 1959), but ROC analysis is usu­
ally preferred since it allows for simple visualization of results as well.
In this setting, the AUC indicator has seldom been exploited within model specification 
and estimation to improve prediction performance: for instance, in Zhou et al. (2012), the 
Authors resort to a method proposed by Pepe et al. (2006) based on parameterization of 
the AUC curves for logistic regression and its optimization as an objective function: given 
model specification, parameter estimates are those maximizing the AUC.
The proposal casted by the paper, instead, is tailored to a forward variable selection pro­
cedure that adjusts the classical search algorithm to construct the best model specification 
in terms of both fitting and prediction performance. The steps of the proposal are detailed 
in the following section.

To explain or to predict? Although prediction of the outcome is a data-depending prob­

3  Background on financial literacy and pension planning
In recent decades industrialized countries have witnessed two phenomena: the increase in 
life expectancy and the decrease in fertility rates. The progressive demographic aging of the 
population is causing important consequences in many sectors: from the pension system 
to the demand for long-term social and health-care services that the elderly would need in 
the eventual loss, total or partial, of their autonomy. Given these premises, it is of foremost 
importance to boost financial well-being of the individuals since several world-wide evi­
dence indicate that financial literacy implies a stronger well-being and a reduced individual 
longevity risk, managed with subscription of private pensions, life and health insurance, 
for instance. As pointed out in Lusardi (2019), financial literacy is an essential indicator of 
people’s ability to make financial decisions. Many studies have shown that basic financial 
education helps improve individuals’ well-being throughout the life cycle (see Fornero and 
Lo Prete 2023): from youth—when people make financial choices that typically have long-
lasting effects (such as taking out a mortgage)—to old age—when they have to front the 
risk of outliving. Several recent studies support the hypothesis that financial literacy pro­
motes pension planning but also the acceptance of reforms. For instance, Bucher-Koenen 
and Lusardi (2011) studied financial literacy in Germany based on SAVE survey. Besides 
gender and regional differences, they found that financial knowledge has a positive impact 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
on pension planning. A survey study discussed in Sekita (2011) provided evidence that the 
Japanese have a low level of financial literacy, especially women, young people and those 
with low education or income. The study also suggested that those with more financial 
knowledge are more likely to plan for retirement. Fornero and Lo Prete (2023) present a 
review of the existing literature on pension literacy, on financial literacy as a tool to reduce 
the gender gap before and after retirement, as well as on the link between financial literacy 
and public decisions that influence individuals’ well-being. For instance, with reference to 
Italy and using data obtained from the inclusion of an ad-hoc module in the SHARE survey, 
findings from Oggero et al. (2023) show that individuals with higher financial literacy and 
greater knowledge of the pension system are more likely to support reforms. From a cross-
country perspective, Fornero and Lo Prete (2019) find that the electoral cost of pension 
reforms is significantly lower in countries with higher levels of financial literacy, even after 
controlling for education and macro-political conditions. Overall, financial literacy oper­
ates at the micro level, by enabling the understanding and use of pension rules, a necessary 
condition for informed planning, and at the macro level, by promoting the democratic legiti­
macy of reforms that make individual planning more effective and predictable. As a matter 
of fact, individuals are required to have a minimum level of financial knowledge to take 
informed decisions throughout the entire life cycle, such as retirement planning, savings 
management or underwriting health or life insurance policies in order to adequately cope 
with the needs they will have in older ages also due to longevity risk. Policy-makers should 
then promote initiatives tailored to raise people awareness on the topics and to improve their 
financial behaviour with respect to own present and future needs and actions.
It is worth to remark that financial literacy does not only concern retirement planning, but 
encompasses various aspects of personal finance and enhances overall financial well-being 
(Kaiser et al., 2020). The reference Cole et al. (2011), for instance, studies how financial 
knowledge drives demand for financial services and how it determines financial behavior in 
emerging market countries, referring to evidence from India and Indonesia.
It is for this reason that, at public level, several financial institutions have been program­
ming official survey studies to measure financial well-being and financial literacy, as the 
Consumer Financial Protection Bureau’s (CFPB) National Financial Well-Being Survey 
(Fisher & Ryan, 2018). Given the circumstance that both the Italian longevity experience 
and the level of financial literacy deserve attention (Di Salvatore et al., 2018; D’Alessio 
et al., 2020), the devoted module integrated within the Survey on Household Income and 
Wealth (SHIW, Faiella & Gambacorta 2007) issued by the Bank of Italy once every 2 years 
will be analysed in the sequel, with reference to the 2020 edition.
Specifically, we will consider the “Big Three” financial literacy questions introduced in 
Lusardi and Mitchell (2011), which are used nowadays by more than 20 countries to mea­
sure the level of financial literacy within the population.
3.1  The financial literacy module within the SHIW survey 2020: data pre-processing
According to the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), 
financial literacy is “a combination of awareness, knowledge, skill, attitude, and behavior 
necessary to make sound financial decisions and ultimately achieve individual financial 
well-being”. This definition emphasizes the practical application of financial knowledge 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
to make informed and effective financial decisions, with the ultimate goal of improving an 
individual’s financial well-being.
As pointed out in Lusardi and Mitchell (2011), the cost of incorrect financial decisions 
caused by illiteracy can have heavy repercussions on a country’s finances, since it increases 
dependence of the population on the welfare network provided by governments, which 
could results for example in an increase in taxes. From this investigation, it emerged that 
financial illiteracy is worryingly prevalent in the US, particularly among women, young 
people and the lower classes. Shifting the study to the level of racial ethnic differences they 
showed that African–Americans and Hispanics are those with less financial knowledge. 
The lack of such knowledge causes serious consequences on household finances, as these 
individuals are less likely to plan for retirement, and they are also very vulnerable especially 
in times of crisis not being able to make decisions on issues such as savings, investments 
and debts. Atkinson and Messy (2012) present a comparative analysis across the OECD 
countries, to identify patterns, differences, or similarities in financial literacy outcomes as 
well as common challenges, and assess the effectiveness of financial education initiatives.

(Faiella & Gambacorta, 2007) was integrated with a module investigating financial literacy, 
on the wake of the US Health and Retirement Study (HRS, Fisher & Ryan, 2018). The Ital­

4  A case study on financial literacy and pension planning
Given quality data released at official level, adequate supervised learning methods should be 
applied to answer the research question: “What are the main factors explaining and predict­
ing financial literacy and pension planning of individuals?”. A recent review of the bulk of 
scientific literature addressing these goals can be found in Goyal and Kumar (2021).
This section is devoted to illustrate the applicative extent of the feature selection algo­
rithm proposed in Sect. 2.1 with a case study on the SHIW 2020 survey, tailored to address 
the following research questions: 
R1: 
identify the socio-demographic characteristics that are able to both explain and 
predict financial literacy in a substantive way, by assessing their relative contribution to the 
accuracy of the final binary classifier;
R2: 
identify the socio-demographic characteristics that are able to both explain and 
predict pension planning in a substantive way, by assessing also the alledged contribution of 
financial literacy.

A final work of remark is due on the moderate values of AUC attained by the selected 
model, as well as by the best-subset and full model specification. Evidence of the circum­

of correct answers provided by respondents to a series of 8 questions, partitioning respon­

edge: Logistic regression: AUC = 0.73, Decision Trees: AUC = 0.63, Random Forests:

case, prediction and AUC evaluations differ in a very moderate manner, and they range 
around AUC = 0.70. As a matter of fact, beyond numeracy and digital skills, recent litera­
ture indicate that general cognitive abilities are associated with enhanced financial literacy 
and skills (Gignac and Stevens, 2024).
4.2  Explaining and predicting pension planning, controlling for financial literacy
Many contributions studied the relationship between financial literacy and pension plan­
ning concluding that a well-informed and financially literate population is better positioned 
to make strategic and informed decisions, ultimately leading to more secure and fulfilling 
retirements (see for example, Bucher-Koenen and Lusardi 2011; Sekita 2011; Crossan et 
al. 2011; Alessie et al. 2002; Von Rooij et al. 2012). With reference to the Italian Popula­
tion, evidence that financial literacy is a strong driver of pension plan participation has 
been found on the basis of the 2006 SHIW edition using a linear probability model with 
OLS (Fornero and Monticone, 2011). Further, as a result of the investigation carried out in 
Lusardi and Mitchell (2011), it emerged that those with higher level of financial knowledge 
are more likely to plan for retirement arguing that “financial literacy influences retirement 
planning, and not the other way around”.
Thus, in conclusion of our investigation, we briefly discuss the implementation of our 
proposal to the search of the most predictive explanatory factors of a binary indicator of 
pension planning (Pens_ins), so that Yi = 1 if the i-th respondent has subscribed a private 
pension contract, and Yi = 0 otherwise. Figure 3 displays the distribution of the Lusardi–
Mitchell score conditional to pension planning behavior, showing a neat association between 
the two traits.
For the sake of the analysis, we will focus on the sub-sample of n = 2954 individu­
als either employed or self-employed (see Table 12), of whom nW = 850 are women and 
nM = 2904 are men: the corresponding frequency distributions are reported in Table 13.

5  Concluding remarks and research agenda
From the methodological point of view, the paper contributes to the state of the art with 
a forward variable selection for logistic regression that iteratively includes the significant 
predictor maximizing the increase in the AUC index with respect to the previous model, 
assumed as measure of predictive performance of the implied binary classification. In this 
way, parsimony of the model is also preserved since classical significance of effects is 
sometimes redundant and may not carry relevant improvement for the discrimination ability 
of the binary classifier (Demidenko, 2016). The proposed method has a broad applicative 
extent, as it is suitable in all the circumstances for which it is of interest to understand which 
factors mostly drive a given response outcome, a common task in behavioral and social sci­
ences. It is worth to remark that the proposal can be adapted to fit asymmetric link functions 
in the setting of GEV regression (La Rocca et al., 2023, 2024) in case of imbalanced binary 
outcomes.
The narrative thread of the discussion has been a case study on financial literacy and 
pension planning, assumed as an individual measure to tackle longevity risk management. 
Several scientific research has already highlighted some of the drivers of financial literacy

Appendix
Appendix 1: Supplementary exploratory data analysis
The procedure considered in input a total of 18 covariates, including both socio-demo­
graphic factors and indicators of financial and economic behaviour, including:
 
●
0–1 Dummy variables flagging belonging to a given age generation: pre-boomer, boom­
er, Generation X, Millenials (benchmark category);
 
●
Employment status: 0–1 dummies for being employed, self-employed or not-employed 
(benchmark category);
 
●
Status of the home of the household: 0–1 dummies for owning the house, living in a 
house for rent, other option (benchmark category);
 
●
Size of the household in terms of number of components;
 
●
Gender (dummy: 0 for men, 1 for women);
 
●
Marital status, with levels coded with the following binary variables:
 –
Married (dummy: 0 for unmarried, 1 for married);
 –
Single (dummy: 0 for people in a relationship, 1 for single);
 –
Divorced (0–1 dummy: 1 for divorced people);
 –
Widower (0–1 dummy: 1 for widower) (assumed as benchmark category).
 
●
University eduction, with the following dummies flagging the status:
 –
Bachelor degree (0–1 dummy: 1 if highest education is bachelor degree);
 –
Master degree (0–1 dummy: 1 if highest education is master degree);
 –
No diploma (0–1 dummy: 1 if the individual has no diploma: benchmark category)
 
●
Macro-area of Geographical residence: with dummies flagging North, Center, South 
and Islands (assumed as benchmark category).
 
●
Single-salary (0–1 dummy: 1 if the household has single-salary).Recalling that 
the Lusardi–Mitchell score is the observed outcome of a count variable ranging in 
{0, 1, 2, 3}, reporting the number of correct answers to the ‘Big Three’ questions, Fig. 6 
display the spineplot of the distribution of the Lusardi–Mitchell Score (LM) conditional 
to gender and geographic area of residence of the head of the household, in charge of 
answering the questionnaire.
Table 21 reports the double frequency (in percentage) of the financial literacy indicator 
(Y = 1 if LM = 3, Y = 0 otherwise) and the geographic area of residence, separately for 
male and female individuals. Regardless of gender, the majority of literate individuals reside 
in the North of Italy, whereas the majority of female is not literate and reside in the South.
Table 22 reports the cross-classification of the indicator of financial literacy (Y) and 
of Age generation, showing a clear lack of independence between the two variables 
(X2 = 45.137, p-value ∼1e −10). Indeed, the majority of literate individuals belongs to 
Boomer and GenX generations (Tables 23 and 24).

Appendix 2: Omitting non-responses for the variable selection algorithm
The case studies discussed in Sections and were tailored to identify the main drivers of the 
ability of providing a correct answer to all the three financial literacy questions integrated 
with the SHIW 2020 survey. In that case, Yi = 0 in case the i-th respondent was not able to 
achieve a full Lusardi–Mitchell score, including both non-responses and wrong answers. In 
the following, we replicate the analysis by omitting non-responses. Given the lower sample 
sizes, within the variable selection algorithm K = 5 is set for cross validation within the 
variable selection algorithm and significance of effects are evaluated at α = 0.3. The test set 
for final assessment is composed of 20% cases.
