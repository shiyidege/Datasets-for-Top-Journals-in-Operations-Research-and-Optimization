How to operate ship fleets under uncertainty 
1 
Abstract: Ships operated by a liner company are scattered around the world to transport goods. A liner 
2 
company needs to adjust its shipping network every few months by repositioning its ships to respond 
3 
to uncertain container shipping demand. Few studies investigate a liner company’s multi-period 
4 
heterogeneous fleet deployment problem under uncertainty, considering fleet repositioning, ship 
5 
chartering, demand fulfillment, cargo allocation, and adaptive fleet sizes. To this end, this study 
6 
formulates a mixed-integer linear programming model that captures all of these elements. This study 
7 
also designs a Benders-based branch-and-cut algorithm for this NP-hard problem. Two types of 
8 
acceleration strategies, including approximate upper bound tightening inequalities and Pareto-optimal 
9 
cuts, are applied to improve the performance of the algorithm. Extensive numerical experiments show 
10 
that the proposed algorithm significantly outperforms CPLEX and CPLEX’s Benders decomposition 
11 
framework in solving the model. We conduct an intensive analysis and find that multistage stochastic 
12 
programming can lead to better solutions than two-stage stochastic programming. We also find that 10% 
13 
of the benefit provided by the multistage model over the two-stage model is due to better fleet 
14 
deployment decisions and that 90% of the benefit is due to better demand fulfillment and allocation 
15 
decisions. By exploring three practical questions regarding driver analysis of liner company 
16 
profitability, benefits analysis of adaptive fleet sizes, and the influence of the COVID-19 pandemic on 
17 
liner shipping, we show how liner companies can benefit from the managerial insights obtained in this 
18 
study. 
19 
Keywords: Multistage fleet deployment optimization; liner shipping operations management; fleet 
20 
repositioning; cargo allocation; heterogeneous ship fleets; adaptive fleet sizes; Benders decomposition.

1 Introduction 
22 
The shipping industry plays a vital role in international trade and the global economy (Fransoo and 
23 
Lee, 2013; Roy et al., 2020). Supported by the recent global economic recovery, approximately 11 
24 
billion tons of goods were transported by ship in 2021 (UNCTAD, 2022). In particular, global 
25 
containerized trade, which decreased by 1.3% in 2020, rebounded in 2021, reaching 165 million 20-
26 
foot equivalent units (TEUs) (UNCTAD, 2022), which is evidence that the shipping industry is 
27 
currently thriving. However, fluctuations in world trade and unexpected incidents, including the 
28 
COVID-19 pandemic, have brought great uncertainty to the shipping market. Hence, how liner 
29 
companies operate ship fleets under uncertainty is extremely important. 
30 
For a liner company, the operation of ship fleets involves many intertwined decisions, such as the 
31 
number of heterogeneous fleets (categorized by their load capacities) deployed on each route, which is 
32 

 
2 
related to the specific sailing sequence of these ships on each route and to the shipping demand. Liner 
33 
company managers must determine whether to charter ships in or out when there is a deficit or surplus 
34 
in a particular ship type, how to reposition ship fleets between different ship routes, whether to fulfill 
35 
the transportation demand in the current shipment period or to postpone fulfillment to the next period, 
36 
and how to adjust adaptive fleet sizes to best respond to uncertain demand. The operation of ship 
37 
fleets under uncertainty is already an intractable problem (Christiansen et al., 2013); the above-
38 
intertwined decisions further complicate it. 
39 
Uncertainty in the shipping market mainly stems from the changing trends in the world economy 
40 
caused by changes in seasonal demand and unexpected incidents. Hence, the demand structure may 
41 
change greatly over a long period of time. For example, affected by the abrupt outbreak of COVID-19, 
42 
container throughput at the Port of Shanghai fell by 8.4% from January to April 2020 (CWTN, 2021), 
43 
which inevitably caused changes in the shipping network. 
44 
Thus, to remain competitive, liner companies must adjust their ship fleets every few months in 
45 
response to uncertainty in shipping demand. The uncertain future may have many possible scenarios, 
46 
and intertwined decisions regarding demand fulfillment and allocation must be made for every 
47 
possible scenario in each time period of the planning horizon. A key long-term decision for a liner 
48 
company is how to deploy a heterogeneous ship fleet in a shipping network in the first stage with 
49 
uncertain demand. In addition to these deployed ships, this study allows for the possibility that a liner 
50 
company may charter in additional ships for point-to-point transportation if an origin-destination (O-
51 
D) pair is in particularly high demand. When optimizing the operation plan of ship fleets, liner 
52 
companies need careful evaluation and decision support based on scientific methodologies, such as 
53 
multistage decision models, to comprehensively plan the deployment of ship fleets and their 
54 
repositioning operations under uncertainty to compete in the growing market. However, throughout 
55 
the liner shipping industry, the planning of networks, including the construction of routes and fleet 
56 
movements, is still primarily performed manually, and the fleet repositioning cost is rarely factored 
57 
into liner shipping models (Wang, 2013). Hence, this study proposes a multistage fleet operation 
58 
optimization model that involves the first-stage decisions of determining the number of deployed 
59 
regular ships of different types on ship routes in the network of the liner company, the sailing 
60 
sequence of these deployed regular ships, and the numbers of chartered-in and chartered-out regular 
61 
ships when there is a deficit or surplus in some ship types, as well as the decisions in the following 
62 
stages of determining the numbers of additional point-to-point ships, and the numbers of accepted, 
63 
delayed, and shipped containers for all O-D pairs in each time period (one time period corresponds to 
64 
one stage). The objective of this decision model is to maximize the expected total profit earned by the 
65 

 
3 
liner company during the planning horizon. 
66 
This study is motivated by the above-mentioned real-world challenge and contributes to the 
67 
literature on liner operations management by proposing a mixed integer linear programming (MILP) 
68 
model and a Benders-based branch-and-cut (BBC) algorithm with two acceleration strategies. This 
69 
study provides liner companies with scientific methods to integrate fleet repositioning, ship chartering, 
70 
demand fulfillment, cargo allocation, and adaptive fleet sizes into their fleet deployment optimization 
71 
to balance the cost-profit trade-off. Since this problem is NP-hard, we propose an exact and efficient 
72 
algorithm to solve it on a practical scale. 
73 
We leverage real-world shipping routes and conduct computational experiments at different 
74 
problem scales to evaluate the model without adaptive fleet sizes and the performance of the proposed 
75 
BBC algorithm and the acceleration strategies. Moreover, we investigate the effect of uncertainty on 
76 
the operations management of liner companies, and we show that multistage stochastic programming 
77 
can lead to higher profit than two-stage stochastic programming or deterministic programming. We 
78 
also conduct an intensive analysis of how multistage stochastic programming can lead to better 
79 
solutions. Lastly, three practical questions regarding driver analysis of liner company profitability, 
80 
benefits analysis of adaptive fleet sizes, and the influence of the COVID-19 pandemic on liner 
81 
shipping are addressed. Managerial insights are obtained to guide the operations of ship fleets under 
82 
uncertainty for liner companies based on the results of the computational experiments. We believe that 
83 
the novel model and the algorithm, especially the quantitative decision methodology, may enable liner 
84 
companies to improve their decision efficiency in an uncertain maritime transportation market.

2 Literature review and discussion 
86 
This study focuses on the multistage stochastic fleet deployment problem (FDP) and designs an 
87 
exact algorithm to solve it. Thus, this section reviews the streams of related literature from three 
88 
perspectives: the FDP, the design of exact algorithms for maritime-related problems, and the 
89 
multistage stochastic programming framework. 
90 
The first research stream is related to the FDP. Readers interested in overviews of the FDP can refer 
91 
to Christiansen et al. (2013), Meng et al. (2014), Lee and Song (2017), and Christiansen et al. (2020). 
92 
As an essential planning problem for liner companies, the FDP assigns available fleets to 
93 
predetermined voyages to maximize the total profit or to minimize the total expense. When liner 
94 
companies assign fleets to provide predetermined services, several aspects must be considered. One of 
95 
the most important aspects is ship chartering. Wang and Meng (2012) propose an MILP model for an 
96 
FDP that allows a liner company to deploy its own and chartered-in ships. Another important aspect 
97 
lies in the different types of ships in a shipping network, which is a heterogeneous FDP (Wang et al., 
98 

 
4 
2013; Ng, 2014). Tierney et al. (2015) note that the fleet repositioning problem (FRP) has received 
99 
little attention in the literature related to the FDP and study the FRP with the consideration of cargo 
100 
flows. Xia et al. (2015) jointly plan fleet deployment, speed optimization, and cargo allocation to 
101 
maximize the total profit of a liner company. Ng (2017) develops an MILP model for an FDP with the 
102 
aim of minimizing the total cost, containing the operating cost and the ship chartering cost. Wetzel 
103 
and Tierney (2020) integrate the FRP into the FDP to determine fleet deployment and ship 
104 
repositioning. It should be noted that most of the literature on the FDP (e.g., Kepaptsoglou et al., 2015; 
105 
Tierney et al., 2015; Zhen et al., 2019) is based on homogeneous fleets, as this assumption simplifies 
106 
the model and analysis. However, this study considers heterogeneous fleets and allows different types 
107 
of ships to be deployed on the same route, making our problem more realistic because liner companies 
108 
often operate different types of ships and deploy these ships according to their transport needs. 
109 
Table 1 mainly reviews papers that consider heterogeneous fleets. The majority of the reviewed 
110 
works only consider deterministic issues. Although a few papers consider uncertainty, they mainly use 
111 
two-stage stochastic programming to deal with it. However, two-stage stochastic programming 
112 
assumes that all information about uncertainty is realized after decisions on the first-stage problem are 
113 
made, which may not reflect some complex and realistic environments. In our problem, liner company 
114 
managers only know the exact transport demand for one time period and the probability distributions 
115 
of demand for time periods immediately beyond the decision time rather than knowing the exact 
116 
transport demand for all future time periods. Hence, to address the sequential realization of 
117 
uncertainties, we use multistage stochastic programming in which uncertainty for a given stage is 
118 
realized only after the decisions of the previous stage are made. 
119 
Regarding decision variables, the majority of reviewed works optimize cargo allocation, and a few 
120 
consider other realistic issues, such as fleet repositioning and ship chartering. Hence, to help liner 
121 
companies best respond to uncertain demand, this study examines an integrated problem of fleet 
122 
deployment, fleet repositioning, ship chartering, demand fulfillment and allocation that allows for 
123 
container delay, and adaptive fleet sizes. Finally, only a few works offer exact algorithms for the 
124 
proposed problem, but this study does. In addition, although some papers do not emphasize 
125 
algorithmic design, they offer mathematical formulations such as novel MILP models by Wang and 
126 
Meng (2012), Ng (2014), and Ng (2017), and approximate solution approaches such as sample 
127 
average approximation (SAA) by Wang et al. (2013). 
128

129 
problems. Many papers on maritime shipping, such as Brouer et al. (2014), Tierney et al. (2015), Xia 
130 
et al. (2015), and Wetzel and Tierney (2020), use heuristics to solve the problems investigated. Howe-

The last research stream is about multistage stochastic programming. Uncertainty is a key factor in 
172 
many decision problems, such as the production routing problem (Adulyasak et al., 2015), the 
173 
container routing problem (Dong et al., 2015), and the location selection problem (Bayram and Yaman, 
174 
2018). As is usual in stochastic programming formulations, uncertainty is represented by a finite set of 
175 
scenarios, each of which is composed of collective random outcomes (Dong et al., 2015). Most of the 
176 
stochastic programming methods are based on the two-stage model (Zhen et al., 2019), which is a 
177 
special case of a more general method called the multistage model. The main difference between two-
178 
stage and multistage programming is that the former assumes that all information about uncertainty is 
179 
known after decisions regarding the first-stage problem have been made, whereas the latter assumes 
180 

 
7 
that uncertainty for a given time period (stage) is only realized after the decisions for the previous 
181 
time period (stage) have been made. Readers who are interested in a more detailed description of 
182 
multistage stochastic programming can refer to Birge and Louveaux (2011). 
183 
In summary, the prevailing trend in the literature on the FDP is to consider homogeneous ships 
184 
since this assumption simplifies the model and the analysis. Besides, the majority of studies on the 
185 
heterogeneous FDP do not consider uncertainty issues. To the best of our knowledge, our study is the 
186 
first to formulate the problem as a multistage stochastic programming model. Our study also 
187 
incorporates into the FDP some operations decisions that are largely ignored in the literature, such as 
188 
those concerning fleet repositioning, ship chartering, demand fulfillment, cargo allocation, cargo delay, 
189 
and adaptive fleet sizes, because fleet repositioning and ship chartering problems are medium- to 
190 
long-term decisions that have a direct impact on liner companies’ operations. Moreover, an exact 
191 
algorithm based on BD and branch-and-cut algorithms is developed to solve the proposed FDP. Two 
192 
types of acceleration strategies, i.e., approximate upper bound tightening inequalities and Pareto-
193 
optimal cuts, are applied to improve the performance of the proposed algorithm.

3 Problem background 
195 
This study focuses on liner operations management under uncertainty. From an academic 
196 
perspective, a rigorous and complete statement of the core problem in this study is a liner company’s 
197 
multi-period heterogeneous FDP under uncertainty, considering fleet repositioning, ship chartering, 
198 
demand fulfillment, cargo allocation, and adaptive fleet sizes. Before we present a mathematical 
199 
formulation of the problem, the following sections elaborate on five elements: (1) the shipping 
200 
network with uncertainty, (2) the model’s multistage feature for considering multi-period planning and 
201 
multistage decision-making, (3) the heterogeneous fleet with different ship types, (4) the liner’s fleet 
202 
repositioning decision, (5) the ship chartering when there is a deficit or surplus of some ship types, 
203 
which allows for the adaptive fleet sizes. 
204 
3.1 Shipping network with uncertainty 
205 
Weekly service for a ship route, one of the most common modes in container transport services, 
206 
means that the headway between two adjacent ships serving the same route is seven days. Suppose 
207 
that a liner company operates a set 𝑅 of ship routes (services) that visit ports on a weekly service 
208 
frequency to transport containers. Figure 1 depicts an illustrative shipping network with three routes 
209 
denoted by 𝑅= {1, 2, 3}. Each ship route has fixed port rotations, and the itinerary of each route 
210 
forms a loop. To maintain the weekly service frequency, a fleet of ships rather than a single ship is 
211 
generally deployed on each route in the service network. For example, it takes a ship three weeks to 
212 
finish a round trip of ship route 2 in Figure 1 (a round trip is an itinerary of a ship route that forms a 
213 

 
8 
loop in practice), and thus a fleet of three ships is required to be deployed on route 2 to maintain a 
214 
weekly service frequency. Next, denoting the number of ships deployed on route 𝑟 by 𝑛,, it is easy to 
215 
understand that the total time for a ship to complete a round trip of the route is 7𝑛, days. Since 
216 
container ships visit ports of call on a weekly base, the minimum decision-making time unit for liner 
217 
companies is one week. Here, the “weekly schedule for ships” is not an assumption, but a practical 
218 
reality. However, travel times are not affected by the weekly service frequency, and we only need to 
219 
know in which week each ship visits each port. Second, the “time period” considered in this study is 
220 
used to indicate the decision-making time points. That is, liner companies make decisions at the 
221 
beginning of time periods 0, 1, and others, but not at other time points.

The liner company operating the above network earns revenue by transporting containers between 
225

228 
accept more containers in a period if it anticipates low demand in subsequent periods. As a result, 
229 
some container shipments will be delayed, and the company will incur a penalty cost. 
230 
Over a long planning horizon, container shipping demand fluctuates constantly. For example, 
231 
demand for face masks surged following the sudden outbreak of COVID-19. Hence, considering a 
232 
shipping network under container shipping demand uncertainty brings our problem closer to reality. 
233 
To deal with this uncertainty, we use stochastic programming to formulate the problem.

3.2 Multi-period planning and multistage decision-making 
235 
As a strategic decision, multi-period heterogeneous fleet deployment is often determined for a long 
236 
planning horizon. Therefore, the external and internal environments inevitably change under 
237 

 
9 
uncertainty, and liner company managers tend to divide the planning horizon into shorter time periods 
238 
to reflect the uncertainty in these different periods. Because the decisions between adjacent time 
239 
periods are also correlated, some postponed transport demand in one time period must be fulfilled in a 
240 
future time period. After the transport demand for one time period is realized, the liner company must 
241 
determine the number of accepted containers to maximize its profit. However, some accepted 
242 
containers may be delayed due to capacity constraints, resulting in a corresponding penalty cost. This 
243 
study allows containers to be delayed for multiple periods until the end of the planning horizon, which 
244 
means that these delayed containers must be shipped during or before the last time period of the 
245 
planning horizon. Hence, this study considers how to determine the numbers of accepted, shipped, 
246 
and delayed containers in each period to maximize the total profit. 
247 
In addition to the fleet deployment of regular ships, this study also allows for the possibility that a 
248 
liner company may charter in additional ships for point-to-point transportation if an O-D pair is in 
249 
particularly high demand. Specifically, let 𝑈 represent a subset of O-D pairs (𝑜, 𝑑) of ports that can 
250 
provide additional point-to-point ships in the shipping network, and assume that each origin in 𝑈 has 
251 
an infinite number of point-to-point ships ready to be chartered in by the liner company. If the 
252 
transportation demand for an O-D pair suddenly increases in a certain period and the ships initially 
253 
deployed by the liner company cannot satisfy the surge in transportation volume, the liner company 
254 
may charter in additional ships to complete the point-to-point transportation of the cargo. Therefore, 
255 
frequently adjusting point-to-point ships enables liner companies to best respond to uncertain demand. 
256 
Two-stage stochastic programming based on discrete probability distributions is widely used to 
257 
address uncertain shipping demand and the rental prices of chartered-in point-to-point ships. However, 
258 
multistage stochastic programming may more accurately reflect the uncertain environment in this 
259 
problem. The main difference between two-stage and multistage programming is demonstrated in 
260 
Figure 2. Two-stage stochastic programming assumes that all information about uncertainty is known 
261 
after making the first-stage decisions, which is not in line with our problem. In our problem, managers 
262 
of liner companies only know the exact transport demand and rental price for one time period, and the 
263 
probability distributions of demand and price in the time periods immediately following the moment 
264 
of their decision. They do not know the exact transport demand or rental price for all future time 
265 
periods. Hence, we adopt multistage stochastic programming in which uncertainty for a given stage is 
266 
realized only after the decision for the previous stage has been made. Here, stage 1 corresponds to 
267 
period 0, and each of periods 1, 2, ⋯ corresponds to one week. Note that stage 1 (i.e., period 0) is 
268 
regarded as a few weeks before stage 2 (i.e., period 1) because liner companies usually design 
269 
shipping services and advertise them for booking several months in advance (Maersk, 2022). Hence, 
270 

 
10 
fleet deployment of regular ships (including the available ships of different types on all ship routes in 
271 
the network, the sailing sequence of these deployed ships, and the numbers of chartered-in and 
272 
chartered-out ships) is determined only in the first stage (which is also period 0). At the end of period 
273 
0, all deployed regular ships, including repositioned ships, should be in place. In the following stages 
274 
(i.e., from period 1), when the container shipping demand and the rental prices of chartering in point-
275 
to-point ships become realized, the model determines, for each time period under each scenario, the 
276 
number of chartered-in point-to-point ships, the number of accepted containers for each O-D pair, the 
277 
number of delayed containers for each O-D pair, and the numbers of shipped containers for each O-D 
278 
pair by the deployed regular and point-to-point ships.

294

3.5 Ship chartering 
307 
Ship chartering is one of the most commonly used methods of maritime cargo transportation. To 
308 
take part in seaborne trade, liner companies must make significant investments in ships. However, not 
309 
all liner companies have enough ships to meet the adjustment needs of their shipping networks 
310 
because the amount of investment capital required for container ships is extremely large. For this 
311 
reason, liner companies may prefer to charter ships from other liner companies rather than to purchase 
312 
new ships to handle increased container volume. In addition, when they have a surplus of a certain 
313 
ship type, liner companies may seek to charter out idle ships to other companies. It is therefore 
314 
necessary to consider the FRP that allows for ship chartering. 
315 
To more clearly explain the heterogeneous FRP with chartering, we use the example in Section 3.3 
316 
to characterize the repositioning of regular ships. Let 𝐻 represent the set of all regular ship groups 
317 
owned by the liner company and other liner companies, where ships in the same group are of the same 
318 
ship type. Let 𝐻G and 𝐻H represent the subsets of regular ship groups owned by the liner company and 
319 
other liner companies, respectively. Hence, 𝐻G has three groups, i.e., 𝐻G = {ℎG, ℎH, ℎJ}, which are the 
320 
4,000-TEU regular ships on route 1, 8,000-TEU regular ships on route 2, and 4,000-TEU regular 
321 
ships on route 3. 𝐻H has only one group, 𝐻H = {ℎK}, which is the 4,000-TEU regular ship idle at the 
322 
Port of Singapore. Let 𝑓M, denote the repositioning cost for a regular ship in group ℎ to move to route 
323 
𝑟. Obviously, a reasonable fleet repositioning plan can greatly reduce the rescheduling cost. 
324 
In addition to the chartering of regular ships, this study allows for the possibility that a liner 
325 
company may charter in additional ships for point-to-point transportation if an O-D pair is in 
326 
particularly high demand.

3.6 Summary of the problem 
328 
One point needs to be noted before formulating the mathematical model of this problem. Both the 
329 
number of containers to be transported and the number of ships to sail on a route to achieve a weekly 
330 

 
12 
frequency are defined in weekly terms, which follows industry practice (most liner services have a 
331 
weekly frequency) and related studies (Xia et al., 2015). 
332 
In sum, a shipping network operated by a global liner company should be designed in response to 
333 
global economic trends and changes in cargo volumes. Liner companies, therefore, have to regularly 
334 
adjust their service networks to remain competitive, thereby requiring liner companies to reoperate 
335 
ship fleets during the planning horizon. To deal with these complex and intertwined decisions, this 
336 
study investigates a liner company’s multi-period heterogeneous FDP under uncertainty, considering 
337 
fleet repositioning, ship chartering, demand fulfillment, cargo allocation, and adaptive fleet sizes. 
338 
Thus, this study incorporates the first-stage decisions of determining the number of deployed regular 
339 
ships of different types on ship routes in the network of the liner company, the sailing sequence of 
340 
these deployed regular ships, and the numbers of chartered-in and chartered-out regular ships when 
341 
there is a deficit or surplus in some ship types, and the decisions in the following stages of 
342 
determining, for each time period under each scenario, the number of chartered-in point-to-point ships, 
343 
the number of accepted containers for each O-D pair, the number of delayed containers for each O-D 
344 
pair, and the numbers of shipped containers for each O-D pair by the deployed regular ships and 
345 
point-to-point ships. The objective of the problem is to maximize the expected total profit earned by 
346 
the liner company during the planning horizon, which consists of seven terms: the container shipping 
347 
revenue from regular and point-to-point ships, the penalty cost of delayed container delivery, the 
348 
rental cost of chartering in point-to-point ships, the repositioning cost of regular ships, the operating 
349 
costs of all deployed regular ships, the rental cost of chartered-in regular ships, and the total profit 
350 
from chartered-out ships.

4.1 Multistage stochastic programming with nonanticipativity constraints 
353 
Multistage stochastic programming considers that uncertainty for a given time period (stage) is only 
354 
realized after the decisions for the previous time period (stage) have been made. It should be noted 
355 
that stage 1 corresponds to period 0, in which fleet deployment of regular ships is determined; the 
356 
decisions in the planning horizon from period 1 to the last period concern operational-level plans for 
357 
point-to-point ships as well as transport demand fulfillment and allocation. 
358 
A scenario tree, an intuitive representation of the branching process induced by the gradual 
359 
observation of the stochastic progress, is usually used to construct scenarios in multistage problems. 
360 
The left part of Figure 4 depicts an example with four periods and 10 scenarios to illustrate the 
361 
scenario tree. One artificial root node, i.e., node 0, is in period 0, in which fleet deployment of regular 
362 
ships is determined. In period 1, three different realizations of uncertain demand and rental price of 
363 

 
13 
chartering in point-to-point ships, represented by nodes 1–3, become known. In period 2, four 
364 
different realizations, represented by nodes 4–7, become known. The above branching process goes 
365 
on until the last period, i.e., period 3, where the outcome related to each leaf node in the last period in 
366 
a unique path from the root to a leaf corresponds to a scenario, which results in a total of 10 scenarios. 
367 
Hence, a scenario in a multistage stochastic programming model is a path from the root node to a leaf 
368 
node. For multistage problems, nonanticipativity constraints must be added to ensure that the 
369 
decisions in a certain period depend only on the data revealed up to that period and not on the 
370 
information that will be realized in the future (Adulyasak et al., 2015). In other words, the decisions in 
371 
a period in two different scenarios should be identical if the scenarios share the same parent node in 
372 
the scenario tree during the period. Here, let 𝒚O,P represent the decision variables that need to be 
373 
decided in period 𝑡 under scenario 𝑠. For the example shown in the left part of Figure 4, the 
374 
nonanticipativity constraints for periods 1–3 are summarized in the right part of Figure 4.

497 
5 Benders-based branch-and-cut (BBC) algorithm 
498 
The NP-hardness of our problem means that it is highly unlikely that a polynomial-time algorithm 
499 
can be designed for this problem. We need an efficient and exact algorithm to solve the problem of 
500 
practical scale with realistic data. Due to a large number of decision variables and constraints in the 
501 
problem, solving the MILP model [M1] is difficult. BD is a partitioning algorithm for large-scale 
502 
MILP models and that is shown to be efficient in solving many stochastic programming problems (Rei 
503 
et al., 2009; Adulyasak et al., 2015). However, the presence of integrality constraints in master 
504 
problems (MPs) in the basic BD algorithm substantially increases the solution time. Hence, we 
505 
introduce a BBC algorithm to solve model [M1] that solves the linear programming (LP) relaxation of 
506 
the MP at each iteration. Moreover, a tailored acceleration strategy and a standard acceleration 
507 
technique from stochastic programming (Pareto-optimal cuts) are applied to improve the convergence 
508 
of the BBC algorithm. The setting without adaptive fleet sizes is adopted to introduce the algorithm 
509 
design for brevity. 
510 
5.1 Overview of the solution approach 
511 
In brief, a BBC algorithm is a branch-and-bound algorithm in which BD is used to compute upper 
512 
bounds by solving linear relaxations of MPs (for maximization problems), and Benders cuts may be 
513 
added to strengthen the linear relaxations of MPs. Specifically, in a basic BD algorithm, the presence 
514 
of integrality constraints in the MP makes its solution time much longer than that of dual subproblems. 
515 
The computational complexity of the MP results in slow convergence of the BD algorithms. To deal 
516 
with this issue, Geoffrion et al. (1974) indicate that obtaining the optimal solution to the MP at each 
517 
iteration is not necessary, which means that obtaining a near-optimal solution efficiently may be 
518 
beneficial. Embedding Benders cuts within a branch-and-cut framework for solving the MP is an 
519 
efficient way to deal with the above issue (Pearce and Forbes, 2018). An outline of our BBC 
520 
algorithm is summarized in Algorithm 1.

664

the proposed BBC algorithm for small computational instances is verified. For the medium-scale 
667

23

793

794

795

6.4.2 Benefits analysis of adaptive fleet sizes 
797 
When the planning horizon consists of multiple periods, the liner company can react more 
798 
effectively to uncertain demand by adjusting fleet capacities in view of realized demand. We consider 
799 
a setting in which for certain O-D pairs, ships can be deployed to provide point-to-point shipping 
800 
services on an ad hoc manner. Our detailed numerical experiments are reported in Appendix 8. 
801 
Specifically, we consider a scenario with charter prices independent of demand, and a scenario with 
802 
charter prices related to demand (i.e., the charter price is higher when demand is higher). We find that 
803 
adaptive fleet sizes can increase the profit of the liner company, but the average relative gaps in 
804 
company profits with charter prices independent of demand and related to demand are only 1.09% and 
805 
1.76%, respectively, even when the coefficient of variation of demand is as high as 2. When adaptive 
806 
fleet sizes are available (either the scenario in which charter prices are independent of demand or the 
807 
scenario in which charter prices are related to demand), in the face of huge fluctuations in demand, the 
808 
liner company prefers to deploy ships with smaller capacity in the first stage and then to charter in 
809 
more point-to-point ships to respond to increased demand more flexibly. When the coefficient of 
810 
variation of demand is reduced to 1, 0.5, or 0.25, the relative gaps in company profits between the 
811 
company with adaptive fleet sizes and the company with fixed fleet sizes are at most 0.63%, 0.20%, 
812 
and 0.05%, respectively. Therefore, in practice, when demand fluctuation is moderate, liner 
813 
companies, especially those without operations research based decision-making support, can ignore 
814 
adaptive fleet sizes because the benefits brought by this factor are too small, and companies have to 
815 
make many decisions manually. Moreover, it is unclear whether the liner company should charter in 
816 

 
28 
more point-to-point ships and whether it will earn more profits in the scenario of demand-related 
817 
charter prices than in the scenario of demand-independent charter prices. 
818 
6.4.3 Influence of the COVID-19 pandemic on liner shipping 
819 
Finally, we investigate the influence of the COVID-19 pandemic on liner shipping. Pandemics 
820 
prolong the preparation time for repositioning, thus increasing the total repositioning time. Our 
821 
computational results (elaborated in Appendix 9) show that the expected profit decreases as the 
822 
number of days for preparation increases. Moreover, when the number of preparation days is less than 
823 
10, the expected profit is very sensitive to changes in the number of preparation days. Hence, 
824 
governments need to think carefully about how to set the number of quarantine days for foreign ships 
825 
to balance the trade-off between containing diseases and harming the profits of liner companies. 
826 
7 Conclusions 
827 
This study shows how to operate ship fleets under uncertainty to deal with a liner company’s multi-
828 
period heterogeneous FDP in a shipping network, considering fleet repositioning, ship chartering, 
829 
demand fulfillment, cargo allocation, and adaptive fleet sizes. Although this issue is crucial for liner 
830 
company operations, it is rarely studied in the literature. To fill this research gap, we first introduced a 
831 
multistage optimization problem and develop a mixed-integer linear programming model for the 
832 
problem. Since this problem is NP-hard, we designed a BBC approach to solve the model. Two types 
833 
of acceleration strategies were applied to improve the performance of the proposed algorithm. 
834 
Contributions of this paper are summarized from the following three aspects. 
835 
From the perspective of problem modeling, we formulate the problem as a multistage stochastic 
836 
programming model to allow for adaptive fleet sizes, allowing liner companies to rent additional 
837 
point-to-point ships for transportation in addition to deployed ships from the decisions in the first 
838 
stage. In the first stage, the model determines the number of deployed regular ships of different types 
839 
on ship routes in the network of the liner company, the sailing sequence of these deployed regular 
840 
ships, and the numbers of chartered-in and chartered-out regular ships when there is a deficit or 
841 
surplus in some ship types; in the following stages, when the container shipping demand and rental 
842 
prices of chartering in point-to-point ships become realized, the model determines, for each time 
843 
period under each scenario, the number of chartered-in point-to-point ships, the number of accepted 
844 
containers for each O-D pair, the number of delayed containers for each O-D pair, and the numbers of 
845 
shipped containers for each O-D pair by the deployed regular ships and point-to-point ships. 
846 
From the perspective of algorithm design, the NP-hardness of the problem does not stop us from 
847 
looking for an exact and efficient algorithm to solve the problem of practical scale with realistic data. 
848 
We design a BBC algorithm to solve the formulated model. To tackle the challenge of solving the 
849 

 
29 
multistage optimization problem, we derive two types of acceleration strategies, including 
850 
approximate upper bound tightening inequalities and Pareto-optimal cuts, to improve the performance 
851 
of the proposed algorithm. The accuracy and efficiency of the proposed BBC algorithm for 
852 
computational instances are verified. 
853 
From the perspective of managerial insights, we first investigate the impact of uncertainty on the 
854 
operations management of liner companies and find that the use of multistage stochastic programming 
855 
can lead to higher profit than the use of two-stage stochastic programming or deterministic 
856 
programming. We next conduct an intensive analysis of how multistage stochastic programming can 
857 
lead to better solutions and find that the benefits brought by multistage are divided into two categories: 
858 
one is the improved deployment decisions, and the other is demand fulfillment and allocation 
859 
decisions with full utilization of demand information. The latter accounts for 90% of the total benefits. 
860 
Finally, we discuss three practical questions regarding driver analysis of liner company profitability, 
861 
benefits analysis of adaptive fleet sizes, and the influence of the COVID-19 pandemic on liner 
862 
shipping. Using our solution method, the decision-makers of liner companies can quickly obtain a 
863 
plan that includes fleet deployment, fleet repositioning, ship chartering, demand fulfillment, and cargo 
864 
allocation scheduling to deal with uncertainties in the shipping market. 
865 
As further research, we intend to explore several extensions. First, although the uncertainty of 
866 
container shipping demand is considered, the uncertainty of weather in ports can also be incorporated 
867 
because weather conditions directly influence the availability of ships mooring there. However, when 
868 
weather disturbances are also considered, the formulations of the multistage optimization problem 
869 
must include more decision variables and constraints, necessitating the development of more effective 
870 
algorithms. Furthermore, in addition to fleet repositioning, ship chartering, demand fulfillment, and 
871 
cargo allocation, several other operations management elements, such as empty container 
872 
repositioning (Crainic et al., 1993), ship refueling (Besbes and Savin, 2009), and on-time shipment 
873 
delivery (Choi et al., 2012), can be incorporated. Jointly optimizing decisions for the above integrated 
874 
problem would be beneficial for liner companies, but this is more complicated to solve and merits 
875 
future research.
