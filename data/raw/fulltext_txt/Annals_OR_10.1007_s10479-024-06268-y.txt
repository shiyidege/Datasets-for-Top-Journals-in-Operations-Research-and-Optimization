Abstract
Motivated by the recent emergence and rapid growth of online healthcare platforms, this
paper investigates its impacts on the healthcare ecosystem by taking into consideration of
heterogeneous patients, homogeneous service providers and proп¬Ғt-driven hospitals. A game-
theoretical model with a queueing framework is developed, and two healthcare systems are
considered, including a monopolistic system with either an ofп¬Ӯine, traditional hospital, or an
online, Internet hospital, and a duopolistic system with both kinds of hospitals. Our study
п¬Ғnds that in both systems a large population of patients always increases the healthcare
supply and demand and the hospital utilization, which leads to an improvement not only in
the hospital proп¬Ғtability, but also in the welfare of both patients and service providers. When
considering a monopolistic system only, our study suggests that the monopolistic Internet
hospital outperforms its counterpart in hospital utilization only when the wait cost is not high
(i.e., patients are less sensitive to wait time), especially when the wait cost is medium and the
patient-provider ratio is small. When considering a duopolistic system, this study concludes
that the decisions of patients, service providers, and hospital managers of both hospitals are
interrelated, and that a large population of service providers increases its hospitalвҖҷs supply,
demand, and wage rate, while decreases its wait times. When comparing the two monopolistic
with the duopolistic system, the latter outperforms and improves every entityвҖҷs utility (i.e.,
patients, hospital, and society) when there is abundance of service providers with more
being allocated to the Internet hospital, whereas the monopolistic system dominates when
the patient population is large, and more providers are allocated to the traditional hospital.

1 Introduction
The rapid growth in the Internet and mobile technologies fuels the emergence of various
вҖңInternet hospitalвҖқ platforms, which connect patients (regardless of their illness severity)
with doctors (Wang et al., 2021). In contrast to the traditional hospitals that provide ofп¬Ӯine
healthcareservices,theInternethospitalsovercomethegeographicalburdentoprovidetimely
service, and have been burgeoning in many countries, especially since the start of the COVID-
19 pandemic (Nguyen et al., 2021). Telemedicine services in some developed countries,
such as the UK and the United States, have been growing rapidly over recent years (Rajan
et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021), and the Chinese government also recently advocated the
establishment of Internet hospitals, with more than 1600 Internet hospitals being built from
2018 to June 2021 (Xinhua News Agency, 2021).
In the context of telemedical and traditional services, and to better understand the interplay
between the Internet and traditional hospitals, we consider two scenarios of the healthcare
market: (a) the monopoly, and (b) the duopoly. A monopolistic healthcare market describes a
market with only one type of hospital, either a traditional hospital or an Internet hospital. One
example of a monopolistic Internet hospital is the Yinchuan Smart Hospital established by
the Chinese government in 2016 in collaboration with the mobile medical platform Haodaifu
(PeopleвҖҷs Daily, 2016), which has recruited 130,000 specialists from 6,800 national hospitals
and provides services of diagnosis, treatment, referral, and monitoring. A duopolistic health-
care market describes a market where there coexist both types of hospitals (i.e., both the
traditional and Internet hospitals), which create cross-channel (i.e., online and ofп¬Ӯine) com-
petition for patients given limited resources and a п¬Ғxed market. Such a duopolistic example
is commonly observed in the practice of Internet hospitals. For instance, Xuhui Cloud Hos-
pital established Guanzhong Internet Hospital in 2020, in parallel with its ofп¬Ӯine competitor,
Xuhui-Zhongshan Hospital, an afп¬Ғliation of Fudan University.1 Throughout the paper, we
refer to the monopolistic healthcare provider as a monopolistic Internet hospital or a monop-
olistic traditional hospital, and the duopolistic system as a system with an Internet and a
traditional hospital.
Although both monopolistic and duopolistic systems in healthcare markets have been
widely seen in real life, this has not drawn comparable attention from academia. In this
paper, we focus on the potential impacts of introducing an Internet hospital to the healthcare
system, which consists of managers of both online and ofп¬Ӯine channels, service providers
(i.e., doctors), and patients. This paper develops a game-theoretic model with a queueing
framework to jointly determine the optimal decisions on service pricing and wage setting for
the hospital manager, the supply rate for the service providers, and the patient demand rate.
Furthermore, our research conducts numerical analyses to compare the performances of a
monopolistic (e.g., Yinchuan Smart Internet Hospital) and a duopolistic healthcare system
(e.g., Guanzhong Internet Hospital and Xuhui-Zhongshan Hospital) under various popula-
tion sizes of patients and service providers. Ultimately, we intend to examine the following
research questions: (1) What are the dynamics of supply and demand in a monopolistic
and a duopolistic healthcare system? (2) How is a healthcare system affected by different
population sizes of patients and service providers? (3) Which healthcare system is better?
Monopolistic or duopolistic?
To answer these questions, a model with a game-theoretic queueing framework is
employed to examine the two types of hospitals. Both online and ofп¬Ӯine healthcare channels
managed by hospital managers are dependent on the service supply of independent providers

so as to maximize the hospital proп¬Ғt. (2) Given the pay rate, the service providers determine
their availability to the hospital by maximizing their utility functions. (3) Patients are assumed
to be heterogeneous, deciding for themselves whether or not to seek services, and from which

assumed to be price- and wait-time-sensitive, and the wait time is assumed to be decreasing
with the service supply, while increasing with the patient demand. Under this framework, we
analyze three settings: two вҖңmonopolisticвҖқ settings, where a monopolistic traditional hospi-
tal or a monopolistic Internet hospital serves the entire market; and a вҖңduopolisticвҖқ setting,
where both the traditional and the Internet hospital coexist and compete for the market share
and the limited resources.
From the model, we derive the optimal service price, provider wage, and the rates of
supply and demand; we further conduct sensitivity analyses by varying the size of the patient
population and service capacity to garner more managerial insights. Under the monopolistic
setting, we п¬Ғnd that a larger number of potential patients induces greater supply and demand,
and ultimately leads to an improved hospital utilization rate, a larger operation proп¬Ғt, and a
higher welfare for the patients and service providers. It is worth noting that our monopolistic
model setting is similar to that of Benjaafar et al. (2022), with an extension of the trans-
portation cost. Our results conп¬Ғrm their п¬Ғndings on the population of service providers that a
larger provider population increases the service supply, reduces the wait times, and improves
the hospital proп¬Ғtability, while a smaller population increases the hospital utilization rate
and the provider welfare. Complementing the Benjaafar et al.вҖҷs (2022) work, we extend the
coverage of the healthcare system to further consider a duopolistic structure, where the Inter-
net hospital and the traditional hospital both compete for the same market share and limited
resources and compare its performance with its monopolistic counterpart. We observe that
there then exists a vicious cycle triggered by service capacity. If a hospital (either the Internet
or the traditional hospital) fails to recruit enough service providers, its market will be eroded
by its competitor, thus forcing it to slash its service price to keep its patients, causing it to pay
less to its service providers, which in turn restricts its service capacity. Several measurement
indices are adopted for performance evaluation, including the rate of supply and demand,
the hospital operating proп¬Ғt, and the welfare of patients, service providers, and society. Our
results show that the performance of a healthcare system depends mainly on the develop-
ment trend of the healthcare market. If the healthcare system is expecting an oversupply,
where there is excessive supply (i.e., a large population of service providers) beyond what is
demanded (i.e., population of patients), the duopolistic system with more resources allocated
to the Internet hospital will be better (in terms of all performance indices). In contrast, if
it is not guaranteed that a decent number of resources can be allocated to the duopolistic
Internet hospital, the monopolistic system with only the Internet hospital is more favorable.
If the healthcare demand is relatively strong, replacing the monopolistic system dominated
by the traditional hospital with the Internet hospital will beneп¬Ғt all the system participants,
including patients, providers, and hospitals.
Our work makes two major contributions to the healthcare operations and management
literature. First, this paper introduces a game-theoretical queueing model capturing the inter-
actions between the online, Internet hospitals, and the ofп¬Ӯine, traditional hospitals, in which
the optimal price and wage are derived through the dynamics of supply and demand. In
addition, the mechanism under duopolistic competition is characterized, and the exogenous
factors of patient and provider populations are considered to examine their impacts on the
equilibrium results. We model a healthcare system composed of both Internet and traditional

2 Literature review
This study is most closely related to three streams of the service operations management
literature that employs a combination of the game-theoretic framework and the queueing
theory to analyze the operational implications of pricing strategies, online healthcare service,
and channel competition (Betcheva et al. 2021; Keskinocak and Savva 2019; Dai & Tayur,
2021; Fainman & Kucukyazici, 2020).Query In the п¬Ғrst research stream, numerous works
have been examined in terms of service pay rate and price rate in the on-demand service2
and two-tier healthcare markets. For example, based on an on-demand service platform,
Taylor (2018) and Bai et al. (2019) characterize the optimal price that the platform charges
its customers and the optimal wage the platform pays its independent providers to coordinate
endogenous demand with endogenous supply; Benjaafar et al. (2022) examine the potential
effects of price and wage on labor welfare with different sizes of the labor pool. In a two-
tier healthcare system that is composed of public hospitals and private hospitals, the pricing
policies of private hospitals have been extensively studied in the literature. Andritsos and
Aп¬Ӯaki (2015), Hua et al. (2016), and Teymourifar et al. (2021) study a healthcare system
where potential patients are delay-sensitive and the population size is п¬Ғxed. Zhou et al.
(2017) consider a healthcare system that consists of high-level (such as tertiary) and low-
level hospitals, and derive the optimal service pricing decisions of the two proп¬Ғt-maximizing
hospitals from a sequential game model. Savva et al. (2019) explore the optimal service price
while investigating the effects of yardstick competition in the healthcare service system. In

The results presented in Proposition 3 characterize the changes to wage and price in terms
of the population size of service providers, the market size, and the wait sensitivity of patients.
Part (i) of Proposition 3 reп¬Ӯects that given a п¬Ғxed market size (), a larger population of
service providers (N) always causes a decreasing wage rate, yet leads to a п¬Ғrst increasing, then
decreasing service price. The former part of Part (i) is intuitive, in that the larger the provider
population, the more the healthcare supply, which leads to a lower wage rate. However, the
latter part of Part (i) about the variation of service price in the provider population stems
from the system wait time. When the healthcare supply is relatively limited (i.e., a small
provider population), the expected wait time for the healthcare service is relatively long, and
decreasing with the growing provider population. The reducing wait time improves patient
satisfaction and boosts healthcare demand by bringing back patients who previously left the
system. However, when the supply is sufп¬Ғciently large and the effect of reducing wait time is
negligible or not signiп¬Ғcant enough to attract more demand, the service price is then declining
with the growing supply. Part (ii) of Proposition 3 suggests that a growing healthcare market
increases both the wage and price. In order to satisfy a growing demand, then the hospital
manager must expand service capacity by raising the wage rate to service providers; in
return, the growing market allows the manager to raise the service price to improve hospital
proп¬Ғtability.
Part (iii) of Proposition 3 reveals that the optimal wage increases in patient waiting cost.
When the waiting cost is high, patients are reluctant to wait for services and more likely to
leave the healthcare system; in an attempt to keep patients, the hospital manager must reduce
wait time by increasing service capacity and raising the wage rate. However, it is surprising
that when patients are more sensitive to wait time, a lower price is not always optimal for
a proп¬Ғt-maximizing hospital. As previously discussed, a high wait cost reduces healthcare
demand. Intuitively, in addition to reducing wait time, hospital managers can also lower
service prices to keep patients. However, this is only true, when: 1) the population of service
providers is relatively small (Proposition 3(iii)(a)(1), (b)(1), (d)(1)), as shown in Figure 2
(a), or 2) the constant b is medium, 2 < ОІ < 1 +

optimal service price may increase with the wait cost; the more sensitive patients are to
the wait time, the higher the optimal service price. To better understand this, we need to
know the dynamics of provider population ( N ), waiting cost ( hk ), wage rate (wM

service price (pM

increasing wage rate drives up costs with a growing provider population; 2) on the other

there exists a threshold such that, when the wait cost is sufп¬Ғciently large, the optimal price
eventually rises along with the wait cost. This also implies that the wait cost becomes so large
that patients are more sensitive to the wait cost than to the service price; therefore, raising
the price does not stop many patients from visiting a hospital, but it does help to improve a

Proposition 6 shows that, in contrast to the monopolistic system, the equilibrium results
(i.e., utilization rate, price, wage, wait times, supply rate, and demand rate) in the duopolistic
system are interrelated in terms of the number of service providers. In a duopolistic sys-
tem, hospitals are competing for not only the market, but also the resources. Given limited
resources (the total number of service providers in the duopolistic system N), an increasing
number of service providers at one hospital (denoted as Hk) leads to its competitorвҖҷs (denoted
as HkвҲ’) service supply to decline. The growing supply of Hk reduces its wait time and boosts
up its demand, while at the same time eroding HkвҲ’вҖҷs market share. It is intuitive to think that
HkвҲ’, with its eroded supply capacity, would have extended wait times. However, the effect
of HkвҲ’вҖҷs shrinking demand exceeds that of its shrinking supply, which ultimately shortens
its wait times. As for the utilization rate (ПҒ DвҲ—
k
), Hk exhibits an upward then downward pat-
tern, which indicates that the hospital utilization rate is improved by the growing demand but
eventually falls once the persistently growing supply fails to create more demand. In contrast,
the utilization rate of HkвҲ’consistently declines with the eroded supply and demand, which
further implies that the erosive effect of demand surpasses that of supply. Similarly, HkвҖҷs
price also shows an inverted-U shape, which implies that demand increases at a faster rate
than supply to allow a growing optimal price, and eventually levels off with the consistently
growing supply, triggering the price to drop. HkвҲ’вҖҷs price, on the other hand, always drops
with its decreasing supply capacity. Lastly, the optimal wage of both Hk and HkвҲ’exhibit a
downward pattern. It is intuitive that HkвҖҷs wage declines with the growing supply; however,
the drop of HkвҲ’вҖҷs optimal wage again reп¬Ӯects that the demand declines at a faster rate than
the falling supply. From these observations of a duopolistic system, we may see that even
though the service supply (i.e., the number of service providers) is exogenous, it triggers a
vicious cycle. Thus, the tools available to the hospital manager, including price and wage
rate, to adjust healthcare demand and supply become futile if the service supply is limited.

7 Concluding remarks
Several managerial insights can be drawn from the analytical results. In a monopolistic
system, raising the wage of the service provider to expand supply capacity and reduce patient
wait time is always effective in stimulating demand, especially when the patient wait cost is
high. On the contrary, when patients are extremely sensitive to the wait time, even more so
than to the service price, it eventually renders the price-cutting strategy futile. In other words,
a hospital manager must be cautious about the patient wait cost when formulating a strategy. In
a duopolistic system, with two hospitals competing for market share and healthcare resources,
a hospitalвҖҷs equilibrium results, including its utilization rate, wage and price rates, wait time,
and supply and demand rate, are interrelated with those of its counterpart. The success of a
hospital mainly depends on whether it can recruit sufп¬Ғcient service providers, as a failure to
secure healthcare supply would draw it into a vicious cycle, leading to lower supply, demand,
utilization rate, price, and wage.
When making a strategic decision over the selection of a healthcare system (i.e., a duopoly
or two monopolistic hospitals), the government must have a clear understanding of the market
situation and development trend. When the healthcare supply is relatively abundant and the
demand is relatively weak, the Internet hospital is preferred. On the contrary, as the aging
population grows and the healthcare supply becomes relatively scarce, the traditional hospital
is more favorable. The introduction of an Internet hospital to an existing monopoly system
inevitably decreases the traditional hospitalвҖҷs welfare, and the entire healthcare system is
only better off if sufп¬Ғcient capacity can be retained in the traditional hospital.
Given the assumptions made throughout this paper, we admit that there are certain lim-
itations to this research, which can be relaxed and extended for future research. First, we
assume that the service value perceived by patients is higher for a traditional hospital than
for an Internet hospital, and this is also reп¬Ӯected in the wait cost (i.e., the wait cost of Internet
hospitals is lower). A future study may want to consider relaxing the assumption to examine
123

Annals of Operations Research
its impacts on equilibrium results. Second, when considering a duopolistic healthcare system
with both Internet and traditional hospitals, we simplify and describe them as parallel in
the model, which implies that all patients can be equally treated by the traditional and the
Internet hospitals. In the real world, however, an Internet hospital is constrained with the
scope of service; some complex cases might require in-person consultation and specialized
equipment. Therefore, the future study may extend the model to a hierarchical system to con-
sider the healthcare referral mechanism. Third, we assume that patients are fully responsible
for the service fee. In reality, the medical expenses usually can be partially (or even fully)
covered by insurance. The future research might extend the model to consider the expense
coverage. Fourth, we assume the patient arrival rate to be equal to all systems, including two
monopolistic systems and the duopoly system. However, this constraint can also be relaxed
to examine how the equilibrium results are affected by different system arrive rates. Finally,
we assume that all the providers have homogeneous service capacity per unit time, but this
assumption could be released for future study to consider heterogeneous service capacity
that better aligns with reality.
