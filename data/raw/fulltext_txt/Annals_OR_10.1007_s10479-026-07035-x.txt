Abstract
This paper investigates a stochastic Returnable Transport Items (RTI) distribution network 
design problem for an RTI service provider responsible for managing RTI flows under un­
certain demand and returns. The objective is to determine the optimal number and location 
of intermediate facilities to accommodate RTI flows while making repositioning decisions 
across the network. A two-stage stochastic programming model is developed to minimise 
costs associated with network design, RTI storage, the choice of direct or indirect ship­
ping, and the allocation of multiple transportation modes in both forward and reverse 
flow channels. To address this complex problem, a robust sample average approximation 
method is proposed, with its efficiency evaluated through statistical validation. Addition­
ally, the stochastic model’s effectiveness is assessed by computing the expected value 
of perfect information and the value of stochastic solutions. Comprehensive numerical 
experiments, including 64 instances with varying cost and robustness coefficients, provide 
managerial insights. The results demonstrate that efficient RTI network design signifi­
cantly impacts multi-period operational decisions and highlights the critical role of RTI 
depots in mitigating demand uncertainty. Furthermore, the robust stochastic optimisation 
approach employed delivers high-quality solutions with an optimality gap of less than 1% 
within reasonable computational times. This study highlights the importance of integrating 
robust decision-making in RTI network design, offering practical insights for managing 
uncertainty and optimising cost efficiency across complex RTI networks.

1  Introduction
1.1  Business context
This paper examines the design of a distribution network for returnable transport items (RTI) 
used to transport manufacturing products across different stages of a supply chain. Many 
industrial companies have recognised that RTIs, including pallets, trays, boxes, crates, and 
refillable liquid containers, offer significant economic and environmental advantages, mak­
ing them a vital corporate asset in modern logistics (Glock, 2017). According to Research 
and Markets (2021), the RTI market was valued at approximately $7,800 million in 2020 
and is projected to exceed $11,000 million by 2026, driven by increasing demand for export 
and import of goods. Several of the world’s largest manufacturing sectors, including chemi­
cals, pharmaceuticals, food and beverages, and automotive, have successfully implemented 
RTI systems, reporting that they are both economically viable and commercially advanta­
geous (Hellström & Johansson, 2010).
In this business context, an RTI service provider (agency) is tasked with managing the 
flow of returnable transport items within a distribution network comprising the following 
entities: points of import/export (I/E), RTI depots, and manufacturers. In this network, RTIs 
can be transported directly between points of I/E and manufacturers (direct shipping) or 
routed through depots (indirect shipping). RTIs either contain raw materials for manufac­
turing plants or finished goods for delivery to points of I/E. Multiple transportation modes 
facilitate the inbound and outbound flows. Upon arrival at a manufacturer’s plant, inbound 
RTIs are emptied. The emptied RTIs can either be stored at the manufacturer’s site for future 
reuse, incurring overhead storage costs, returned directly to points of I/E at a long-haul 
transportation cost, or sent to RTI depots via the backward flow channel, incurring both 
backward transportation and depot storage costs. Efficient repositioning of empty RTIs is 
critical to meeting network demand. The problem addressed in this paper incorporates the 
uncertain nature of demand over a planning horizon. It seeks to balance these cost factors, 
i.e., storage, transportation, and repositioning, while considering demand uncertainty. The 
proposed model aims to provide robust and practical solutions for managing RTI flows in 
such a dynamic and complex environment (Lee & Song, 2017).
The RTIs supply chain is generally classified into “open-loop supply chain” and “closed-
loop supply chain” (Glock, 2017). In an open-loop supply chain, the ownership of the RTIs 
is transferred to the recipient upon the arrival of the products, hence the return of the RTIs 
is not required. In contrast, in a closed-loop supply chain, RTIs must be returned to the 
sender (Bilbao et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2015), which is the case in this paper. While RTI 
management inherently relies on both strategic decisions (e.g., the number and location of 
depots) and operational decisions (e.g., flows and inventory of empty and loaded RTIs), 
the existing literature largely evaluates RTI distribution decisions assuming that the strate­
gic network design is already fixed. Consequently, the joint design of RTI depot locations 
and operational policies remains under-explored, particularly in business contexts where 
an intermediary plays a central coordination role. To address this gap, we formulate two 
research questions.
RQ1. Where should RTI depots be located, how many are required, and how should they 
be allocated to manufacturers and I/E points in order to ensure an efficient RTI distribution 
network?

In a closed-loop setting, these strategic locational decisions directly influence RTI opera­
tions. However, the literature shows that operational inventory-flow decisions are typically 
modelled separately from strategic facility location decisions (Tornese et al., 2021). This 
motivates the second research question:
RQ2: Given the chosen depot locations, what are the optimal flow and inventory policies 
for empty and loaded RTIs over time that minimise the total supply chain cost?
Together, these questions highlight the strong interdependence between strategic network 
design and operational decision-making in RTI systems. This interrelationship complicates 
decision-making for policymakers and practitioners and has received limited analytical 
attention to date.
1.2  Motivation
According to (Buehlmann et al., 2009), North Carolina, the ninth fastest-growing state in the 
U.S. and the sixth largest in population growth over the past decade,1 actively follows RTI 
distribution and recycling strategies under legislation designed to optimise RTI resource 
utilisation. This highlights the critical importance of designing an optimal RTI distribution 
network, a priority for policymakers in the region. Motivated by this need, we developed a 
mathematical model to address the RTI distribution problem within North Carolina, aiming 
to minimise total costs while adhering to operational constraints inherent to the industry. 
The proposed model is applied to a hypothetical case study centred on the Wilmington 
seaport as the point of I/E. As noted by the North Carolina State Ports Authority,2 the Port 
of Wilmington is one of the few southern ports offering extensive storage areas, berths, 
terminals, and warehouse facilities, thereby supporting the international trade needs of the 
fast-growing Southeast U.S. market. Given that all RTI suppliers and manufacturers in this 
study are situated within North Carolina, the state provides an ideal benchmark for local 
authorities seeking to implement effective RTI distribution strategies.
While the logistics of RTIs inherently involve circular flows and uncertainty in demand 
and return patterns, the full benefits of a closed-loop RTI supply chain can only be realised 
through a robust and adaptive network design. Glock (2017) also emphasised the impor­
tance of determining the optimal number and location of RTI depots as critical decision 
variables to generate practical and managerial insights. As a key contribution of this paper, 
we show that the design of an efficient distribution network for both empty and loaded RTIs 
(i.e., determining the optimal number and location of RTI depots) has a critical influence on 
operational decisions in a multi-period setting.
Our key finding shows that incorporating model robustness into the design significantly 
reduces variability in RTI flows and lowers the need for excess storage capacity at depots, 
but this comes at the expense of higher expected total cost. This robustness–cost trade-off is 
particularly pronounced when holding costs for empty RTIs are low. In such cases, a more 
risk-averse or conservative decision-maker decentralises the network by opening more 
depots and using multiple sourcing to hedge against uncertainty. These insights offer clear 
guidelines for balancing cost efficiency and operational reliability in practice. To operation­
alise this, we formulate a two-stage stochastic optimisation model that captures both the

1.3  Theoretical development
Closed-loop RTI systems involve the continuous circulation of empty and loaded RTIs 
between manufacturers, depots, and I/E points. Theoretical perspectives on closed-loop 
logistics emphasise that such systems require coordinated management of distribution, col­
lection, and storage activities, and that the physical configuration of the network strongly 
influences the feasibility and cost of operational decisions (Glock, 2017). In RTI-intensive 
industries, particularly those involving an intermediary, the interplay between RTI avail­
ability, transportation flows, and storage capacity leads to an inherent hierarchical decision 
structure. Within this hierarchy, strategic decisions, such as determining the number and 
location of RTI depots and allocating manufacturers and I/E points, shape the long-term 
structure of the network. Once these decisions are made, tactical and operational decisions 
related to transportation planning, empty RTI repositioning, and inventory replenishment 
must be executed recurrently over multiple periods. This temporal hierarchy is well recog­
nised in logistics and supply chain planning, where strategic network design provides the 
framework for short-term operational flexibility (Klibi et al., 2010; Amiri-Aref et al., 2018). 
The strong interdependence between these decision layers motivates the need for integrated 
modelling approaches that evaluate both strategic depot placement and operational flow–
inventory policies in a unified framework.
In addition to this hierarchical structure, RTI distribution networks are characterised by 
high levels of demand and return uncertainty, arising from variable production schedules, 
limited synchronisation among partners, and fluctuating transportation conditions. Theo­
retical studies in supply chain planning show that failure to account for such uncertainty 
may lead to inefficient or unstable network configurations, particularly in systems where 
assets must be returned and reused (Lee & Song, 2017; Sarmadi et al., 2020). To model 
such uncertainty, stochastic representations using multiple plausible scenarios provide a 
theoretically grounded approach for capturing a wide range of potential system outcomes. 
However, in contexts where probability distributions are unstable or difficult to estimate, 
solutions derived from purely stochastic models may exhibit poor robustness across sce­
narios. To ensure both performance and stability, we draw on the theoretical foundations of 
the robust mean–risk framework (Mulvey et al., 1995), which balances expected cost with 
solution variability to enhance robustness under uncertainty. Integrating this perspective 
into the hierarchical structure allows us to identify strategic and operational decisions that 
remain feasible and cost-effective across diverse realisations of RTI demand.
In the next section, we review the existing literature taking into consideration the design 
of RTI logistic systems and the implications of operations management in the closed-loop 
supply chain. In Sect. 3, we present our proposed two-stage mathematical model. Sect. 4 
presents the solution procedure including the SAA method, and its robust counterpart to 
address the two-stage stochastic programming computational challenges. In Sect. 5, we 
conduct various computational experiments to validate the application of the developed

2  Literature review
2.1  RTI closed-loop supply chain decisions
From a broader perspective, our study addresses a specific case of closed-loop supply chains, 
focusing on the management of RTIs and their reverse flow through the network (Govindan 
et al., 2015). Following the classification in Glock (2017), RTI systems can be grouped into 
three types based on the structure and coordination of their supply chain interactions. Type 
1 involves a single-sender, single-recipient system, where either party manages both loaded 
and empty RTI flows. Type 2 extends this to a single-sender, multi-recipient configura­
tion, adding complexity due to distribution across multiple endpoints. Type 3, which is the 
focus of our work, introduces a central service provider who manages RTI flows between 
senders and recipients. This setting introduces a reverse logistics component that requires 
simultaneous optimisation of forward and reverse flows (Kroon & Vrijens, 1995; Guide & 
Wassenhove, 2006). Our research builds upon this Type 3 structure and contributes a formal 
optimisation model that captures the complexities of coordinating forward and reverse RTI 
flows under uncertainty.
The foundational RTI depot location model incorporating flow costs was introduced by 
Kroon and Vrijens (1995). Since then, the literature has expanded to encompass a broader 
range of operational decisions in RTI systems. For example, Buchanan and Abad (1998), 
Chew et al. (2002), and Tsiliyannis (2005) highlighted the importance of integrating trans­
portation and inventory costs to evaluate system performance more effectively. Building 
on this, other studies such as Mollenkopf et al. (2005), Breen (2006), Grimes-Casey et al. 
(2007), and Thoroe et al. (2009) emphasised the critical role of timely RTI returns and the 
need for coordinated management of loaded and empty units in both forward and reverse 
flows. Furthermore, recent contributions have explored greater network flexibility through 
RTI rental or leasing options (Carrano et al., 2015; Hariga et al., 2016), and multi-period 
circular network designs to reduce inefficiencies such as empty running (Han et al., 2022). 
Most recently, Chen et al. (2022) highlighted the ongoing challenge of coordinating trans­
portation, inventory, and flow management decisions in RTI networks.
2.2  RTI distribution network modelling
Managing RTI networks presents significant complexity, requiring integrated frameworks 
that can jointly address key operational activities such as transportation, inventory man­
agement, and facility location. Several studies have tackled this challenge by developing 
mathematical or simulation-based models focused on specific dimensions of RTI manage­
ment. For instance, Glock and Kim (2014) analysed RTI order quantities and shipment 
frequencies under defined delivery conditions, while Zhang et al. (2015) demonstrated the 
cost-effectiveness of jointly optimising transportation and inventory decisions. A simula­
tion-based, multi-objective model was proposed by Bottani et al. (2015) to evaluate hold­
ing and transport costs of empty RTIs. In the context of inventory control, Cobb (2016)

developed a model incorporating RTI inspection and repair cycles. Soysal (2016) formu­
lated an RTI inventory routing problem over a fixed network, which was later extended 
by Iassinovskaia et al. (2017) to include time windows. Sarkar et al. (2017) incorporated 
both RTI holding and transport costs in a fixed network, while SteadieSeifi et al. (2017) 
developed a decision-making model for RTI flows using predefined hub locations. From 
a facility planning perspective, Tornese et al. (2018) examined the location of RTI repair 
centres, integrating cross-docking and operational policies through simulation. More recent 
contributions–including Zhang et al. (2019), Zhang et al. (2022), and Li et al. (2022)–have 
stressed the need for multi-period and multimodal coordination in closed-loop RTI systems.
Most of the aforementioned studies are built on models that assume static and deter­
ministic demand conditions. However, in real-world applications, both the demand for and 
the return of RTIs are subject to dynamic and uncertain variations, which can substantially 
affect the relevance and robustness of such models. Recognising this, several researchers–
including Atamer et al. (2013), Kim et al. (2014), Kim and Glock (2014), Glock and Kim 
(2016), and Garg et al. (2024)–have highlighted the importance of incorporating uncertainty 
into RTI systems. Their work demonstrates the impact of stochastic demand parameters and 
periodic fluctuations on operational outcomes. Nevertheless, these contributions are largely 
limited to systems classified as Type 1 or Type 2 under the taxonomy proposed by Glock 
(2017), where either the sender or recipient manages the RTI flows. The role of an inter­
mediate service provider–central to Type 3 systems–is not considered in these models. This 
leaves a significant gap in the literature, as Type 3 systems introduce additional coordina­
tion challenges and require more sophisticated modelling approaches. Our work addresses 
this gap by developing a robust optimisation model for RTI networks with an intermediary 
service provider under demand and return uncertainty, capturing the complexities inherent 
in Type 3 systems.
In this paper, we address Type 3 RTI systems, where a service provider is responsible 
for managing the storage and distribution of empty RTIs through intermediate facilities 
such as depots, ensuring availability for both suppliers and customers. The location of these 
facilities represents a critical strategic decision in RTI supply chain design. To fill the gap 
identified in the literature, we develop a decision-support model specifically tailored for RTI 
service providers operating under stochastic demand and return conditions. We formulate 
the problem as a two-stage stochastic programming model. The first stage supports strategic 
decisions, including determining the number and locations of intermediate facilities and 
assigning them to network nodes under uncertainty. The second stage captures tactical-level 
decisions on transportation and inventory flows once uncertainty is realised, considering 
both forward and reverse RTI flows across multiple transportation modes via direct and 
indirect shipping.
To the best of our knowledge, no prior study has jointly addressed these strategic and 
tactical decisions within a unified, uncertainty-aware modelling framework for Type 3 RTI 
systems. Our model provides a comprehensive and practical tool for service providers man­
aging complex RTI networks under real-world conditions.

3  Problem description and modelling approach
3.1  Problem description
In the following, we outline the sequence of events associated with the flow of RTIs in the 
distribution network, with a graphical representation provided in Fig. 1. This figure illus­
trates both the flow of information (orders) and the physical flow of empty and loaded RTIs 
relevant to the problem studied in this paper.
When a manufacturer requires empty RTIs, it submits a request to the RTI agency speci­
fying the quantity needed, often triggered by the readiness of finished goods for shipping 
through the backward flow channel. The agency then coordinates with RTI depots to sup­
ply the requested empty RTIs to the manufacturer. Simultaneously, the agency checks the 
availability of empty RTIs at the point of I/E to explore the possibility of empty RTIs direct-
shipping to the manufacturer. Considering that large companies frequently rent or lease 
RTIs in practice, our study includes this option for cases where the network’s available RTI 
storage cannot fulfil the manufacturer’s request. Once the RTIs are loaded with finished 
goods at the manufacturer, the agency manages their transportation in the backward flow 
channel using available transportation modes. This may involve direct-shipping or indirect-
shipping routes. Effective coordination among the key actors (i.e. points of I/E, RTI depots, 
and manufacturers) is critical for achieving a reliable and cost-efficient RTI distribution 
network. Such coordination is essential for optimising the network’s performance while 
addressing the complexities of RTI flows (Chen et al., 2022).

Since the manufacturer’s demand for empty RTIs is uncertain, managing the storage of 
empty RTIs at depots naturally becomes an inventory control problem. Most existing loca­
tion-inventory studies have adopted a continuous review (r, Q) control policy, often assum­
ing a Normal demand distribution. However, this approach results in a stationary demand 
process, reducing the problem to a single-period planning horizon (i.e., a static setting) and 
failing to establish a hierarchy between location and inventory decisions. Such assump­
tions limit the applicability of this policy to the RTI distribution industry, which typically 
involves a stochastic demand process in a non-stationary setting. To overcome these limita­
tions, we employ a periodic order-up-to level (R, S) inventory policy, which is well-suited 
for inventory systems with non-stationary demand (Askin, 1981; Bollapragada & Morton, 
1999; Graves, 1999). This policy offers greater flexibility, as it accounts for variations in 
demand over multiple periods. Notably, the (R, S) policy is more general and encompasses 
other standard inventory policies as special cases (Babai et al., 2011; Lagodimos et al., 
2012). This makes it particularly appropriate for the dynamic and uncertain nature of RTI 
distribution networks.
The model presented in this paper addresses a hierarchical decision-making framework 
encompassing location-allocation, transportation planning, and inventory control for both 
empty and loaded RTIs. The first stage focuses on the strategic design of the network, deter­
mining the optimal number, location, and allocation of RTI depots. These decisions are 
made as here-and-now variables, prior to the realisation of uncertain demand. Once the 
network configuration is established, the second-stage model addresses several operational 
decisions. This includes multimodal transportation planning via forward and backward flow 
channels. Additionally, it encompasses inventory control and policy decisions for RTIs. 
These operational decisions, referred to as wait-and-see variables, are made periodically 
throughout the planning horizon, enabling the system to adapt dynamically to realised 
demand conditions.
The following assumptions have been made for the model formulation:
 
●
The potential nodes have adequate infrastructure for establishing depots.
 
●
The replacement and repair of RTIs are not required.
 
●
The RTI back-order is allowed if the demand cannot be met in a given period. However, 
this has to be met in the following periods.
 
●
Each I/E point, depot, and manufacturer is constrained by storage capacity.
 
●
Manufacturers have no production capacity.
 
●
The finished goods produced by manufacturers should be sent to I/E points.
Below, we introduce the modelling approach used in this paper in detail.
3.2  Robust mean-risk modelling approach
In this section, we first present a generic robust mean-risk stochastic optimisation frame­
work, based on the approaches proposed by Mulvey et al. (1995) and Ruszczyński and 
Shapiro (2009b). We then tailor this framework to formulate a mathematical model for the 
RTI distribution network design problem described earlier. In this approach, uncertainty in 
demand and RTI returns is captured through a finite set of scenarios representing possible 
realisations.

incorporating risk measures into the optimisation model.

3.3  Two-stage stochastic programming model
The first stage of the RTI distribution network design problem focuses exclusively on opti­

4  Solution approach
Solving the proposed model for designing an RTI distribution network presents significant 
challenges, primarily due to the computation of the expected value in the objective function. 
Calculating this expected value for discrete distributions requires solving numerous linear 
programming models, one for each demand realisation (i.e., scenario). To address this, we 
employ the Sample Average Approximation (SAA) method, as proposed in Santoso et al. 
(2005), which ensures that the solutions converge to the optimal values as the number of 
scenarios increases (Shapiro et al., 2021). However, the applicability of the SAA approach 
relies on the existence of a feasible solution and a finite objective value (Shapiro, 2003). 
In the context of the RTI supply chain, this assumption can be problematic. Manufacturers’ 
uncertain demand distributions may vary, or the parameters of these distributions might 
not be known. Consequently, the model is prone to generating infeasible solutions due to 
constraint violations in one or more scenarios. To address this issue, we propose a robust 
optimisation modelling approach to formulate a robust counterpart of the SAA problem over 
scenarios generated using the Monte Carlo procedure. The robustness ensures that the solu­
tions remain feasible under all considered scenarios. To validate the quality of the solutions 
obtained from the robust SAA model, we calculate the optimality gap between the solutions

relationship represents a critical design tension for decision-makers navigating uncertain 
environments.

and reduced slack at the expense of higher baseline costs. In this context, the cost of robust­
ness quantifies the price paid to hedge against variability and ensure system-wide reliability.
5.2.2  Risk attitude and network design
Cost structure and decision-maker risk attitude (ρ) interact strongly in shaping the RTI net­
work design. As seen in Fig. 5, under cost scenarios with low holding costs (structures b 
and d), more conservative decision-makers (higher ρ) tend to open more depots. This decen­
tralisation provides flexibility and allows the system to buffer against uncertainty in demand 
and return volumes. In contrast, under high holding cost structures (a and c), centralised 
storage becomes more economical–even for risk-averse planners–highlighting the decisive 
influence of cost parameters.

These results underscore the critical role of cost structure in shaping the decision space. 
When costs permit, conservative planners hedge against uncertainty by decentralising the

preferences highlights the importance of aligning financial incentives with risk-manage­

5.2.3  Sourcing strategy under robust and risk-averse planning
In addition to depot configuration, the sourcing strategy–particularly the use of multiple 
sourcing–adapts in response to both model robustness and risk aversion. Figures 6 and 6-2 
show that the percentage of manufacturers allocated to multiple depots increases with both 
model robustness (ν) and risk aversion (ρ). As uncertainty grows or risk tolerance decreases, 
multi-sourcing becomes an essential strategy to maintain service levels and hedge against 
disruptions. This diversification aligns with the increased depot count under similar condi­
tions, suggesting that risk mitigation drives both sourcing complexity and spatial expansion 
of the network.
To illustrate this in practice, Fig. 7 presents the network solution for an instance with cost

depots, with 33% of manufacturers being allocated to more than one depot. This example 
demonstrates how sourcing and facility decisions interact to shape a network configuration 
that balances cost efficiency, robustness, and service coverage. Detailed network configura­
tions across all instances are included in Appendix C, Tables C1–C4.

5.3.1  Key findings analysis
The numerical experiments reveal several important patterns regarding the behaviour of 
RTI distribution networks under varying levels of robustness, risk aversion, and cost struc­
tures. These findings contribute to the literature on robust optimisation in closed-loop logis­
tics and provide new insights into the interplay between risk preferences and network design 
decisions.
A central finding concerns the trade-off between robustness and cost efficiency. Increas­
ing the robustness parameter strengthens the stability of operational decisions, as reflected 
in the low coefficients of variation across scenarios, but this comes at the expense of higher 
expected total costs. This result confirms the classical challenge between solution stabil­
ity and cost minimisation documented in uncertain environments (Ben-Tal & Nemirovski, 
1998; Bertsimas et al., 2011), and extends it to the domain of returnable transport item 
networks. Importantly, our analysis quantifies this trade-off in a closed-loop context, where 
both forward and reverse flows must be coordinated. The observed reduction in flow vari­
ability and storage requirements under higher robustness levels demonstrates that conser­
vative planning approaches propagate throughout the entire network structure, not just 
affecting individual decision variables. This systematic effect has received limited attention 
in prior RTI research, which has predominantly focused on deterministic models or simple 
stochastic approaches (Glock & Kim, 2016).
Our second key contribution relates to the interaction between cost parameters and risk 
attitude in shaping network structure. We find that under favourable cost structures (i.e., low 
empty-RTI holding costs), risk-averse decision-makers adopt more decentralised networks 
with a greater number of depots, while high holding costs drive centralisation regardless of 
risk preferences. This demonstrates that economic drivers can either amplify or suppress the 
behavioural effects associated with risk aversion. The role of risk aversion in facility loca­
tion is well-established in supply chain design literature (e.g., Baron et al. 2011; Gabrel et 
al. 2014), where conservative planners tend to diversify and decentralise capacity to buffer 
uncertainties. However, our findings reveal an important boundary condition: when eco­
nomic parameters create strong centralisation incentives, even highly risk-averse decision-
makers converge toward concentrated network structures. This suggests that cost structure 
acts as a moderating variable that can override behavioural preferences, a phenomenon that 
has not been previously documented in the RTI context. Our results thus extend existing 
literature by identifying conditions under which economic rationality dominates risk-based 
decision solutions in closed-loop systems.
The emergence of multi-sourcing as a natural risk-mitigation mechanism represents 
a third important finding. We observe that allocating manufacturers to multiple depots 
becomes increasingly prevalent as either robustness or risk aversion increases, enhancing 
service reliability and flexibility under volatile demand conditions. This aligns with estab­
lished results in supply chain risk management (e.g., Shin et al. 2000; Kleindorfer and Saad 
2005), which emphasise diversification as a hedge against uncertainty. However, the RTI 
context introduces unique considerations: multi-sourcing must balance not only supply risk 
but also the coordination of empty RTI returns and the potential fragmentation of reusable 
asset pools. Our findings suggest that the benefits of diversification outweigh these coor­
dination challenges, particularly under high uncertainty. The observed alignment between 
decentralisation, multi-sourcing, and risk-aversion reveals the structural adaptations that 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
closed-loop networks employ to hedge against uncertainty, contributing to a more nuanced 
understanding of how reusable asset systems differ from traditional forward supply chains.
5.3.2  Practical and managerial implications
The findings of this study provide actionable guidance for managers and practitioners 
responsible for designing and operating RTI distribution networks. We organise implica­
tions around three key decision areas: robustness calibration, network configuration strat­
egy, and sourcing policy design.
Robustness calibration and the stability-cost trade-off. Managers face a fundamental 
decision regarding how much robustness to build into their RTI networks. Our results 
demonstrate that while enhanced robustness reduces operational variability in empty and 
loaded RTI flows, this stability comes at a measurable cost premium. Practitioners should 
therefore adopt a context-specific approach. Robustness should be prioritised in industries 
with strict delivery windows (e.g., automotive just-in-time manufacturing, pharmaceutical 
cold chains), where service failures incur penalties, or when managing high-value products 
where stockouts have severe consequences. In these contexts, the cost of disruption typi­
cally exceeds the incremental expense of robust planning. Conversely, firms operating under 
tight cost constraints with limited pricing power, or managing commodity products where 
alternative supply arrangements can be quickly established, may accept lower robustness 
while optimising for expected cost. Implementation requires scenario-based cost-benefit 
analyses that compare the additional cost of robust solutions against estimated disruption 
costs.
Network configuration and the cost-risk nexus. The interaction between cost structure 
and risk attitude has direct implications for strategic network design. When empty-RTI 
holding costs are low, risk-averse organisations should consider decentralised depot config­
urations, as spreading capacity across multiple sites creates redundancy and buffers uncer­
tainty in RTI demand and returns. This strategy proves particularly valuable when return 
flows are unpredictable or manufacturers exhibit heterogeneous usage patterns, such as in 
beverage distribution where empty container storage is relatively inexpensive. However, 
when holding costs are high, even strongly risk-averse managers should favour centralised 
networks, as economic parameters dominate behavioural preferences. In such contexts, risk 
mitigation should be achieved through operational mechanisms such as safety stock poli­
cies or flexible transportation rather than facility proliferation. Managers should begin by 
assessing their cost structure, particularly empty-RTI holding costs relative to transportation 
and facility costs, to determine whether decentralisation is economically viable as a risk-
mitigation strategy.
Multi-sourcing as a resilience strategy. Our results indicate that allocating manufactur­
ers to multiple depots becomes increasingly attractive as uncertainty and risk aversion rise. 
When planning RTI networks in industries characterised by fluctuating production sched­
ules, seasonal demand variation, or irregular return flows, managers should build in deliber­
ate redundancy by establishing secondary sourcing relationships. This provides operational 
flexibility and reduces dependence on any single depot-manufacturer link. However, multi-
sourcing introduces coordination complexity, particularly in managing empty RTI returns 
across multiple depots. Organisations should therefore invest in information systems and 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
tracking technologies that facilitate visibility of RTI locations and movements, while pool­
ing arrangements and standardised RTI designs can reduce coordination burden.
In synthesis, practitioners should adopt a structured approach to RTI network design 
that assesses organisational risk tolerance and service criticality, evaluates cost structures to 
determine the economic feasibility of decentralisation, calibrates robustness levels through 
scenario analysis, applies multi-sourcing selectively to high-variability relationships, and 
invests in enabling technologies for coordination.
6  Conclusions
This study developed an integrated two-stage stochastic optimisation framework for design­
ing RTI distribution networks under demand and return uncertainty. By jointly modelling 
strategic depot location, transportation flows through forward and reverse channels, direct 
and indirect shipping options, and periodic inventory replenishment, the proposed approach 
captures the interdependence between long-term network design and short-term operational 
decisions. The introduction of a robust mean–risk formulation further enables the model to 
balance expected cost with operational stability, providing solutions that perform reliably 
across a wide range of uncertain conditions.
The results demonstrate how robustness, risk attitude, and cost structure collectively 
influence depot configuration, sourcing flexibility, and system performance. In doing so, 
the study contributes to a more nuanced understanding of RTI logistics, particularly in con­
texts involving an intermediary coordination role and multi-period uncertainty. The insights 
derived from the model support practitioners in selecting RTI network designs that reflect 
their operational priorities, financial constraints, and tolerance for variability.
One of the limitations of the paper is that the key features of the design of the SAA 
procedure can influence the optimality gap of our study. Therefore, our future research will 
investigate novel optimisation approaches (e.g. a hybrid approach of data mining and net­
work design theory (Van Nguyen et al., 2020) and the integration of reinforcement learning 
to solve dynamic problems (Lee et al., 2024), (Tomljenovic et al., 2024)) to explore different 
control strategies for returning RTIs based on an optimised network under various scenar­
ios. Another limitation we faced was related to the data collection. As RTI network design 
is a relatively new concept in the industrial context, data collection and access to further 
detailed information were shortcomings. We believe that the inclusion of the uncertainty on 
other input parameters (e.g. transportation capacity expansion (Chen et al., 2022) and lead-
time (Archetti et al., 2021)) will be worth investigating in a reverse logistics network design 
problem. Moreover, future research could benefit from the integration of other robustness 
frameworks such as those developed in Bertsimas and Sim (2004) and Lotfi and Zenios 
(2018), which offer alternative approaches to handling uncertainty. The former introduces a 
tractable budgeted uncertainty set that balances conservatism and performance degradation, 
while the latter incorporates coherent risk measures like robust VaR and CVaR to address 
tail-risk and distributional ambiguity.
