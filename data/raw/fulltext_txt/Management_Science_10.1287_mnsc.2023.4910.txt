Abstract. It is well documented that people have misperceptions about energy costs. 
However, there is mixed empirical evidence about the effectiveness of information proviВӯ
sion and its persistence over time. Understanding the interplay between information 
regarding the private and social benefits of low-carbon technologies and identifying mechВӯ
anisms through which information treatments affect peopleвҖҷs long-term behaviors can help 
reconcile the mixed findings in the literature. In this paper, we measured the long-term 
effects of information provision through online experiments involving more than 3,000 
households in China. We provided people with information on low-carbon light bulbsвҖҷ 
monetary and climate benefits and observed their purchasing decisions over 10 months. 
We find that information on private or social benefits leads to a temporary effect on most 
households and a persistent one on the uninformed. This evidence suggests that informaВӯ
tion provision works through two different mechanisms: increasing saliency and filling the 
information gap.

1. Introduction
Imperfect information about product characteristics and 
benefits can reduce the demand for high-quality proВӯ
ducts. This may result in suboptimal purchasing deciВӯ
sions, yielding private and social losses. This is 
particularly problematic from consumersвҖҷ underpurchВӯ
asing of low-carbon products, which generates externaliВӯ
ties that exacerbate global climate change (Weber 2017). 
Because greenhouse emissions are long lived, intervenВӯ
tions to reduce them should work in the long term, not 
only temporarily.
Several ways to motivate consumers to be more enviВӯ
ronmentally conscious have been proposed (see AbraВӯ
hamse et al. 2005, Gillingham and Palmer 2014, and 
White et al. 2019 for reviews). One primary means is to 
convey information regarding the consequences of their 
behaviors. As society has become more environmentally 
conscious, consumers demand more comprehensive 
information about products, and firms are subjected to 
increasingly stringent environmental regulations. This 
study contributes to the growing empirical evidence on 
information provision and energy-efficient adoptions. 
We inform consumers and draw their attention to energy 
costs or the environmental consequences of their adoptВӯ
ing behavior, involving more than 3,000 households in 
China over 10months. Our research provides new inВӯ
sights into this strand of literature from several aspects.
First, it has been established that information may inВӯ
fluence people through two mechanismsвҖ”a cue-driven 
mechanism that increases the saliency of a productвҖҷs 
benefits and activates affect (Shiv and Fedorikhin 1999) 
and an information provision mechanism that fills the

Particularly, if the information only works through a 
cue-driven mechanism, we will not be able to observe a 
long-term effect from infrequent information intervenВӯ

once the cue is removed, consumersвҖҷ behaviors return to 
the uncued level (Laibson 2001, Bernheim and Rangel

for the mixed findings on the long-term effects of inforВӯ

incorrect inferences about householdsвҖҷ responses to 
information or an insufficient account for household 
heterogeneity can lead to suboptimal policies, such as 
under- or overprovision of information. Our results sugВӯ
gest that we could improve the cost-effectiveness of 
information intervention and achieve persistent effects 
by targeting households with insufficient knowledge 
about energy efficiency and its climate consequences.
Second, in a standard model of consumer behavior, 
consumers should only trade off their private costs and 
benefits (i.e., monetary). Then, why do we expect any 
behavioral change after providing information about the 
negative externalities of private actions (e.g., Toledo 
2016)? One plausible reason is that lower environmental 
impacts from adoptions are linked to using less energy, a

mach et al. 2017). In our context, reducing one kilowatt- 
hour of energy use can save ВҘ0.54 as a private benefit and 
reduce 0.624kg of carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions as a 
public benefit.1 Suppose that consumers are aware of the 
connection between these two types of benefits and that 
they exhibit a response to environmental information. 
Then, we should be cautious before drawing conclusions 
based on the assumption that consumers care intrinsically 
about the externality of their actions. This is because they 
may have responded to the private benefits implied by 
the environmental information. In this case, providing 
both types of information might be inefficient considering 
consumersвҖҷ limited attention and cognitive bandwidth. If 
contrary to the standard model, environmental informaВӯ
tion provision induces consumers to internalize at least 
part of the negative externality of their energy consumpВӯ
tion, then providing multiple attributes of the same mesВӯ
sage may yield a more significant combined effect. Our 
research quantifies how much consumers are willing to 
internalize the externality of energy consumption because 
of information using the reveal-preference approach, 
which has both theoretical and practical appeals.2
Third, despite answering general questions about 
energy-efficient adoptions, our developing country conВӯ
text provides additional insights into the challenges in 
low- and middle-income countries. Those insights are 
valuable because credit constraints, imperfect informaВӯ
tion, bounded rationality of consumers, and environmenВӯ
tal preferences play essential roles in energy-efficient 
adoptions (Davis et al. 2014, Gerarden et al. 2015, Levine 
et al. 2018). By conducting our experiments in China (the 
largest emitter; however, it has set ambitious climate 
objectives to reduce emissions in the coming years), we 
show that imperfect information and environmental preВӯ
ferences are the main obstacles to adopting climate- 
friendly products in this context. Additionally, our data 
allow us to examine if the cognitive cost of poverty (see 
Haushofer and Fehr 2014 for an overview) impedes 
energy-efficient adoptions. If poverty imposes a cognitive 
tax on consumers, then poor consumers may not be able 
to fully utilize information in decision making. By 
showing how well consumers at divergent income levels 
process information and how closely updated beliefs corВӯ
relate with adoption behavior, our research helps us 
understand how different consumers receive and process 
information about energy efficiency given the interplay 
between poverty and cognition.
Additionally, spanning almost one year, our experiment 
provides insights into the persistence of information treatВӯ
ments. This is needed to impute the cost-effectiveness of 
behavioral interventions and is especially relevant for long- 
lasting environmental issues, such as climate change.
This paper presents two longitudinal experiments 
motivated by the mentioned gaps in the extant knowlВӯ
edge. We chose to focus on lighting given that it uses 
considerable energy and that electricity is the most 
important contributor to greenhouse gas (GHG) emisВӯ
sions, thus leading to global climate change and other 
environmental externalities. In the first experiment, in line 
with the approach adopted by Allcott and Taubinsky 
(2015), we informed consumers about the benefits of low- 
carbon products. We measured their relative willingness- 
to-pay (WTP). We further investigated the interplay 
between monetary and environmental information, and

2. Related Works
2.1. Information Interventions
A large body of the information intervention literature 
focuses on conveying information on the consequences 
of oneвҖҷs behavior. Providing private consequences of 
oneвҖҷs behavior is based on neoclassical economics asВӯ
sumptions that people are self-interested and utilize all 
available information to make rational choices. Such 
interventions have been conducted in diverse contexts, 
including labor supply (Chetty and Saez 2013), obtaining 
a college education (Bleemer and Zafar 2018), healthy 
eating (Wake 2018), vaccination take-ups (Kimura et al. 
2007), and tax compliance (Bott et al. 2020). Alternatively, 
interventions that provide social consequences of oneвҖҷs 
behavior rely on the assumption that people have prosoВӯ
cial preferences and are motivated by a need to benefit 
others. A typical context for such interventions is proВӯ
moting proenvironmental decisions (Toledo 2016).
Another line of influential literature concerns informaВӯ
tion regarding an individualвҖҷs performance relative 
to others. Such reference-based interventions may evoke 
a feeling of competition, conformity, or social pressure, 
and therefore, they are conceptually distinct from 
consequence-based information interventions. For examВӯ
ple, instead of assuming that utilities depend on the absoВӯ
lute value of consumption, they are based on the premise 
that ordinal rank in the distribution of consumption also 
affects utilities (Frank 1985, Robson 1992). The effectiveВӯ
ness of reference-based interventions is evaluated in conВӯ
texts such as voting (Gerber and Rogers 2009), online 
public goods provision (Chen et al. 2010), retirement savВӯ
ings (Beshears et al. 2015), and reducing traffic violations 
(Chen et al. 2017).
The effectiveness of information interventions varies 
greatly depending on the implementation context, 
whether they were conducted by academics or governВӯ
ments, their behavioral channel, and the outcome of 
interest. For example, Hummel and Maedche (2019) 
documented that interventions applied in the context of 
health have larger effect sizes than interventions aimed 
at reducing energy consumption (average effect sizes of 
44% versus 32% over the control). In addition, providing 
social references is usually more effective than disclosing 
consequences (average effect sizes of 29% versus 20% 
over the control). DellaVigna and Linos (2022) indicated 
that the effect sizes of trials conducted by academic 
researchers and governmental agencies differ signifiВӯ
cantly (average effect sizes of 33% versus 8% over the 
control). They documented that statistical power and 
selective publication account for most of this difference. 
Focusing on healthy eating, Cadario and Chandon 
(2020) indicated that cognitively oriented interventions 
that change what people know yield about half of the 
effect sizes compared with affectively oriented interВӯ
ventions that change what people feel. Furthermore, 
Haaland et al. (2023) pointed out that effect sizes on 
behavioral measures are typically much smaller than 
effect sizes on belief updating in response to information 
treatment.
Some information treatment studies evaluated the lonВӯ
gevity of the treatment and discussed the possible chanВӯ
nels at work. For example, Bott et al. (2020) found that 
moral suasion had no long-term effects in the tax compliВӯ
ance context, whereas a detection letter caused belief 
updating of taxpayers and reduced tax evasion even one 
year after the intervention. Hong et al. (2022) found that 
providing organizational cost information reduced staВӯ
tionery consumption in a university only in the short 
term. They concluded that the cue-driven channel is 
more plausible than the information provision channel. 
Differently, Roth et al. (2022) reported a four-week 
treatment effect of providing debt-to-Gross Domestic 
Product ratio on survey participantsвҖҷ support for govВӯ
ernment spending, working through belief updating of

2.2. Applications to the Environmental and 
Climate Domain
In the environmental and climate domain, most 
consequence-based information intervention studies 
focus on the short-term effect, and the findings are 
mixed. Some studies found positive effects. For example, 
providing information about running costs or emissions 
effectively raised stated WTP for efficient washing 
machines (Bull 2012) and increased stated choice for effiВӯ
cient water heaters (Newell and SiikamВЁaki 2014). Allcott 
and Taubinsky (2015) also found that providing cost- 
saving information increased the efficient light bulb marВӯ
ket share in an incentivized online experiment. However, 
they reported another finding; when information was 
provided to in-store customers, it did not increase efficient 
light bulb purchases. Similarly, Allcott and Sweeney 
(2016) found that information alone did not affect demand 
for energy-efficient durables. Sometimes, information 
may lead to an adverse effect. By conducting a randomВӯ
ized, controlled trial with a major Italian online retailerвҖҷs 
website, dвҖҷAdda et al. (2022) reported that adding cost 
information to energy labels increased purchasing of less 
efficient refrigerators.
Understanding the roles of belief updating and saliency 
in the environmental context has theoretical and practical 
importance. Allcott and Taubinsky (2015) provided sugВӯ
gestive evidence that information treatment may act parВӯ
tially through belief updating. By measuring the temporal 
dynamics of peopleвҖҷs beliefs and behavior, our experiВӯ
ments identified that belief updating and saliency are both 
at work in such contexts. In addition, our experimental 
design allowed us to solve the вҖңtranslated equivalenceвҖқ 
problem using incentivized experiments, contributing to 
the understanding of the interplay between private and 
social information in the environmental domain.
A widely implemented behavioral program that sends 
household reports periodically has attracted many reВӯ
searchersвҖҷ attention, and its long-term effectiveness has 
been investigated. It involves multiple interventions, 
including social comparisons, energy or water conservaВӯ
tion information, and using feedback. The combined 
effect of consequence- and reference-based interventions 
on conservation behavior is usually long lasting. For 
example, combining environmental messages and social 
comparison information reduced household water use 
(Ferraro et al. 2011, Ferraro and Price 2013) and electricity 
use (Asensio and Delmas 2015). In addition, households 
receiving those reports had persistent changes in energy 
usage, even when the reports were discontinued after 
two years (Allcott and Rogers 2014). Costa and Kahn 
(2013) focused on the role of ideology. They found that 
such reports were two to four times more effective with 
political liberals than conservatives in reducing energy 
consumption.
Compared with these studies, our goal is not to evaluВӯ
ate the effectiveness of such integrated programs. 
Instead, we focus on providing information about the 
private or social benefits of adoption behaviors without 
an augmentation of social comparisons. Furthermore, 
one unique feature of our experiment is to provide the 
information treatment just once, which allows us to 
cleanly observe the effect of belief updating without 
repeatedly activating cues. This design is essential for 
distinguishing between the two mechanisms.
3. The Conceptual Framework
The experiments serve two goals: identifying the mechaВӯ
nisms through which information treatments affect peoВӯ
pleвҖҷs proenvironmental behaviors and measuring the 
externality internalization to understand the interplay 
between information concerning private and social 
benefits.
3.1. The Two Mechanisms
In general, information can affect the adoption of 
climate-friendly durable goods through two mechaВӯ
nisms (Kahneman 2011). The first is a standard informaВӯ
tion provision mechanism that has normative appeals. It 
is guided by the premise that information provision fills 
the information gap for individuals who have biased 
beliefs about the benefits of the behavior (adopting low- 
carbon products in this case), updating their information 
sets and reoptimizing their consumption. This deliberaВӯ
tive process involves thinking, reasoning, and consciousВӯ
ness, which should only influence consumers who are 
not perfectly informed. Thus, as this mechanism primarВӯ
ily relies on information provision, its long-term effects 
will be determined by the amount of information reВӯ
tained in memory.
Alternatively, a cue-driven behavioral mechanism asВӯ
sumes that information treatment makes the benefits of 
an action salient by providing a вҖңcueвҖқ that is temporarily 
higher than the marginal utility of an action. Hence, once 
the cue is removed, peopleвҖҷs actions return to their preВӯ
treatment level. The cue-driven mechanism assumes 
that our brains struggle to give equal attention to multiВӯ
ple foci at once and will, therefore, register them to a subВӯ
conscious hierarchical system, whereby salient elements 
are given priority (Guido 2001). Inattention could be

Households, therefore, are making short- versus long-

Information about monetary savings can debias all 
imperfectly informed households about the short- and

choices. Independently, the salience effect from making 
long-term savings stand out relative to its price can temВӯ

light bulbs. However, as noted previously, people will 
start valuing future savings less once the cue is removed.

mechanisms, we measured participantsвҖҷ baseline beliefs 
regarding the benefits of energy-efficient appliances to 
identify informed consumers (Haaland et al. 2023). If fillВӯ

participants who lack access to perfect information (i.e., 
those holding low baseline beliefs) will increase their 
WTP immediately after information exposure. However, 
participants who are perfectly informed about the beneВӯ

high baseline beliefs) are not expected to increase their 
WTP because they have no information gap to fill.

aim is to increase the temporary saliency of the informaВӯ

effect will diminish after the вҖңcueвҖқ is removed.3 Finally, 
if the information treatment works through both mechaВӯ
nisms simultaneously, it would yield a combination of 
the impacts, which differs from either set of effects in isoВӯ
lation. These predictions should hold for both types of

any behavioral change observed after providing climate

the private savings from it. In this case, we expect that 
providing information on climate consequences on top

providing monetary benefits alone.

mation dissemination.
4.1.2. Procedure. The experiment was articulated in 
three waves. In wave 1, we provided pertinent (treated) 
or irrelevant (control) information to all recruited subВӯ
jects and measured their changes in WTP. The short- 
term effect of the information treatment (as explained in 
Table 1, column (1)) and the internalization of public benВӯ
efits (as illustrated in Figure 1) was measured in wave 1. 
We then conducted waves 2 and 3; they were 3 and 
10 months later. In the two follow-up waves, we did not 
provide any information about the benefits of the light 
bulbs or reminders so that we could measure the effect of 
the information supplied in wave 1 on WTP in later 
waves (as explained in Table 1, column (2)).
Wave 1 involved three rounds of incentive-compatible 
choices between a light-emitting diode (LED) light bulb 
and a compact fluorescent light bulb (CFL).6 Each round 
included multiple choice tasks, allowing us to infer conВӯ
sumersвҖҷ relative WTP for an LED compared with a CFL.
Participants were presented with different informaВӯ
tion screens between each of the two consecutive choice 
rounds. Wave 1 ended with a postexperiment survey 
that included questions on baseline beliefs about the benВӯ
efits of LEDs, demographics, habits, and attitudes 
toward climate change. This design is both within subВӯ
ject (we have captured pre- and postinformation choices) 
and between subject (consumers received different inforВӯ
mation screens).
Waves 2 and 3 each involved only one round of light 
bulb choices identical to that implemented in wave 1, but 
information screens were omitted. The experimental 
instructions and questionnaires are available in Online 
Appendix A.1.
4.2. Measures and Manipulation
4.2.1. Information. In wave 1, each participant was ranВӯ
domly assigned to a treatment or a control condition, 
which differed in the content and the order of the pieces 
of information provided. All subjects assigned to the 
treatment conditions received the same information on 
the monetary cost of LEDs and CFLs along with one 
version of the environmental impact information. The 
monetary information focused on demonstrating the 
superiority of LED light bulbs relative to CFLs in terms 
of significant energy savings (50%) and durability (twice 
as long), which would generate substantial financial benВӯ
efits. Specifically, the average life spans of an LED and a 
CFL are 10 and 5years, respectively, assuming that each 
light bulb is on for four hours per day. With an electricity 
cost of 0.53 yuan/kWh and the price of a CFL fixed at 8 
yuan, replacing a CFL with an LED would save an 
energy cost of 54.1 yuan or a total cost of 62.1 yuan in 
10 years (without discounting).
On the other hand, the information on environmental 
impact highlighted the relationship between electricity 
consumption and harmful emissions. We varied the perВӯ
ceived proximity of energy use consequences by describВӯ
ing the emissions either in terms of local air pollutants or

2015). Subjects assigned to the control conditions reВӯ

4.2.2. Relative WTP. Choices provided to the particiВӯ
pants were incentive compatible. Consumers were given 
a CNY 30 (about USD 4.5) вҖңshopping budgetвҖқ that they 
could use to purchase an LED or a CFL at varying prices. 
Each consumer made a series of pairwise choices betВӯ
ween an LED and a CFL of the same luminosity and 
brand following a standard вҖңbisection procedureвҖқ (disВӯ
cussed further in Section 6).
Specifically, across all pairwise choices, the CFL price 
was fixed at eight, its median price in the market. In the 
first task, the LED price was set at 16 and subsequently 
varied between 8 and 28 (representing the lowest and 
highest prices in the market) adaptively.7 For example, if 
the participant chose the LED over the CFL (chose the 
CFL over the LED) in the first task, in the next task we 
would raise (reduce) the price of the LED so that it is less 
(more) attractive than in the previous round. In all tasks, 
we provided participants with the images and the sale 
prices of the two light bulbs. The positions of the two 
light bulb options were randomized.
The bisection procedure allowed us to observe LED 
choices at absolute prices of 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, and 28, repreВӯ
senting costs of 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, and 20 relative to the equivВӯ
alent CFL. We assumed that consumers who switched 
choices between any two relative prices would have relaВӯ
tive WTP equal to the mean of those prices. For example, 
consumers who chose LEDs at relative price 8 (i.e., when 
the LED costs 16 and the CFL costs 8) but chose CFL at 
relative price 12 (i.e., when the LED costs 20 and the CFL 
costs 8) were assumed to have relative WTP of 10.
Some subjects had censored WTPs, as they preferred 
either the CFL or the LED at all relative prices. Thus, we 
imputed absolute WTPs of 6 and 30 (i.e., relative WTPs 
of 2 and 22 for bottom-coded and top-coded consuВӯ
mers, respectively). We will later demonstrate the sensiВӯ
tivity of the results to these assumed values.
In each wave, one of the choices was randomly selected 
as the вҖңofficial purchase.вҖқ We shipped consumers the 
light bulb they had chosen in that official purchase, 
charged the light bulb price, and added the remaining 
amount (CNY 30 minus the chosen light bulb price) to the 
participantвҖҷs account on the survey platform. Participants 
could deposit all funds earned on the survey platform 
into their bank account.
4.2.3. Baseline Beliefs. In wave 1, after the information 
intervention and the WTP elicitation, all consumers 
answered two questions probing their baseline beliefs 
regarding LEDsвҖҷ monetary and environmental benefits.8
To evaluate the belief related to cost savings, we asked 
participants to respond to the following question: 
вҖңBefore participating in the experiment, did you know 
that for the same lighting effect, an LED only costs half as 
much as a CFL?вҖқ Participants can choose from (A) I did 
not know at all; (B) I knew a bit, but the saving is larger 
than I thought; (C) I knew a bit, but the saving is smaller 
than I thought; and (D) I knew how much an LED saves 
me. Subjects who chose A or B (C or D) were categorized 
as having high (low) beliefs about LEDsвҖҷ specific beneВӯ
fits. The question about the environmental benefits 
was similar. We further discuss the belief elicitation in 
Section 6.
4.2.4. Attitudes Toward Climate Change. In the postexВӯ
periment survey of wave 1, subjects were asked вҖңhow conВӯ
vinced are you that global warming is happening.вҖқ They 
then chose from one to four, indicating вҖңcompletely conВӯ
vinced,вҖқ вҖңmostly convinced,вҖқ вҖңnot so convinced,вҖқ and 
вҖңnot at all convinced,вҖқ respectively. We categorized subВӯ
jects who chose three or four as вҖңclimate change skeptics.вҖқ

4.4. Results
4.4.1. Baseline Relative WTP. The average relative 
baseline WTP for an LED light bulb compared with a 
CFL was 11.49 (standard deviation (SD) ВҲ 9.45), which is 
significantly greater than 0 (t(1,267) ВҲ 43.31, p < 0.001). 
About 20% of participants had a negative relative WTP 
for LEDs (i.e., they have a lower maximum WTP for the 
more efficient light bulb). Having higher income, being 
male, owning LEDs, and having high beliefs about the 
monetary savings of LEDs were positively correlated 
with high baseline WTP (see the regression table in 
Online Appendix E.1). These findings concur with our 
intuition and provide further evidence that the differВӯ
ences in WTP are meaningful.
4.4.2. The Short-Term Treatment Effect on WTP. On 
average, monetary information and environmental 
information increased relative WTP for an LED by CNY 
2.29 (SD ВҲ 7.28) and 2.66 (SD ВҲ 8.01), respectively, and 
both amounts are significantly greater than 0 (t(1,057) ВҲ
10.243, p < 0.001 and t(1,057) ВҲ 10.81, p < 0.001, respecВӯ
tively). Their combined effect (i.e., CNY 4.95 (SD ВҲ
9.38)) corresponds to about 25% of the baseline WTP for 
an LED (ВҘ19.49) or about 43% of the baseline WTP for an 
LED relative to a CFL (ВҘ11.49). On the other hand, 
WTPs in the two control conditions remained constant 
(t(417.99) ВҲ 0.12, p ВҲ 0.90 and t(417.96) ВҲ 0.24, p ВҲ 0.81 
for each round, respectively). Figure 2 presents WTPs 
across all treatments.
The demand curves for LED light bulbs shown in 
Figure 3 indicate that each piece of information shifts the 
treatment groupsвҖҷ LED light bulb demand upward. 
After two rounds of information provision, more than 
60% of the treated subjects indicated a WTP of at least 
CNY 30, in contrast to only 30% at the baseline.11 It is 
worth noting that about 10% of the treated subjects still 
expressed lower WTP for LEDs than for CFLs after two 
rounds of information provision. On the contrary, the 
control information does not shift the demand curves.
To determine if the perceived proximity of environВӯ
mental consequences impacted the participantsвҖҷ WTP

We summarize the experimental results. Experiment 1

Although the two types of information are translated

An increase in WTP because of pure monetary concern

(2021) that the rich are more likely to exhibit prosocial 
behaviors. It does not necessarily mean that the poor 
attach little value to the public benefits. As the poor face 
budget constraints frequently, their marginal utility of 
money might be higher than the rich.

5. Experiment 2
Experiment 2 involved two waves conducted one month 
apart. Its design complements that of Experiment 1 in 
four crucial aspects. First, the WTP elicitation in ExperiВӯ
ment 2 was hypothetical. In Experiment 1, we asked 
respondents for their postal addresses so that the choice 
of light bulbs would be incentive compatible. This pracВӯ
tice naturally increases attrition among participants who 
care more about their privacy (as shown in Online 
Appendix C). To decrease attrition rates, we gave up 
incentive compatibility in Experiment 2, relying on 
stated preference, and we experimented with a new surВӯ
vey platform. In this way, we achieved a low attrition 
rate of about 10% for follow-up waves. Additionally, 
receiving a light bulb sent by the experimenter in the preВӯ
vious wave may affect WTP in follow-up waves, possiВӯ
bly because of a preference for consistency (Cialdini et al. 
1995, Guadagno and Cialdini 2010). Therefore, using 
hypothetical choices in Experiment 2 has additional 
advantages because it avoids the potential confounding 
effect of receiving a light bulb.
Second, in Experiment 1, we provided the same 
respondent with information about private and social 
benefits to explore their interaction. This within-subject 
information provision, however, makes it impossible to 
assess the long-term effects of each type of information 
intervention. We, therefore, provided only one type of 
information to each subject in Experiment 2, either monВӯ
etary or environmental, to assess the persistent effect of 
providing each type of LED benefit separately.
The third difference in Experiment 2 is that instead 
of measuring baseline beliefs about the benefits of 
LEDs after information treatments, we measured them 
before the information treatments. We use these two 
approaches because respondents may be subject to conВӯ
sistency bias in their responses (Falk and Zimmermann 
2011), resulting in a biased reported belief or a biased 
WTP. Fortunately, both approaches produced comparaВӯ
ble results, alleviating our concerns regarding the consisВӯ
tency preference of respondents.
Fourth, instead of choosing between one LED and one 
CFL as was done in Experiment 1, we asked subjects to 
choose between a pack of four LEDs and a pack of four 
CFLs, and we decreased the measurement scales from ВҘ 
4 to ВҘ 1 per light bulb, providing robust results for our 
elicitation. We believe that the complementing design of 
Experiments 1 and 2 can strengthen the reliability of our 
conclusions.

Participants were recruited through the cell phone appliВӯ

social application market in 2021. Recruiting through

essentially eliminating attrition concerns. The new recruitВӯ

qualified participants (>18years old and not a student) 
who began the survey, 1,358 completed it. The sample 
covers 32 of the 34 provincial-level divisions in China.
Similar to Experiment 1, the median age of the particiВӯ

recruited the sample with a median household income

6. General Discussions
6.1. Elicitation of Relative WTP
Several incentive-compatible WTP elicitation methods 
are widely used, including the bisection (or ping-pong) 
method, the BeckerвҖ“DegrootвҖ“Marchak method, aucВӯ
tions, and multiple price lists. We chose the bisection 
method because pairwise choices are easy to understand 
for participants, as they replicate the purchasing deciВӯ
sions that are made in real life.
Theoretically, a decision maker with well-defined preВӯ
ferences is supposed to make the same decision in each 
elicitation method (procedure invariance). However, the 
empirical validity of procedure invariance is mixed 
(Voelckner 2006, Bateman et al. 2009, Roosen et al. 2010, 
Brebner and Sonnemans 2018). Specifically, although 
available evidence indicates that the absolute WTP is 
affected by the elicitation method and choice context, the 
relative WTP is mostly consistent. The phenomenon 
whereby consumersвҖҷ absolute valuation of goods is arbiВӯ
trary, whereas relative valuations appear orderly, is 
called вҖңcoherent arbitrarinessвҖқ (Ariely et al. 2003). To 
obtain coherent valuations, we chose to elicit relative 
rather than absolute WTPs. Notably, our conclusionsвҖҷ 
reliability relies on comparing relative WTP across difВӯ
ferent treatment groups, not an accurate estimation of 
absolute WTP. Moreover, we varied the number of light 
bulbs (one versus four) and the measurement scales (ВҘ4 
versus ВҘ1 per light bulb) in Experiments 1 and 2 to ensure 
that the effect of the information treatments was robust.
Another potential concern is the incentive compatibilВӯ
ity of the bisection method because of its adaptive nature 
(as previous choices determine the later ones). SpecifiВӯ
cally, respondents may learn during the experiment that 
LED price would decline if they keep choosing the CFL. 
In this way, they can decrease the expected WTP for the 
LED. However, an ample body of evidence suggests that 
this compatibility issue is immaterial in practice (AbdelВӯ
laoui 2000, Bleichrodt et al. 2010, Aydogan and Gao 
2020). As argued by Bardsley et al. (2009), whereas theoВӯ
retical incentive compatibility might be an issue, behavВӯ
ioral incentive compatibility is not. Moreover, suppose 
such a learning effect really exists. In that case, we should 
expect decreases in WTP for LEDs across elicitation 
rounds, which should drive changes in WTP downward, 
suggesting that the true effect should be higher than our 
observed effect.
6.2. Measuring Beliefs
In Experiment 1, we measured beliefs introspectively by 
asking participants if the provided information was 
novel to them after the information provision. In ExperiВӯ
ment 2, we measured prior and posterior beliefs by askВӯ
ing participants to choose one correct statement from 
three statements about the relative benefit of LEDs comВӯ
pared with CFLs. By comparing prior and posterior 
beliefs, we also measured belief updating. The belief 
measures in the two experiments yielded consistent 
results.
Another common way to measure subjective beliefs is 
to ask for numeric quantities. For example, in our case, 
we can ask participants to give the energy cost of LEDs 
and the carbon emission of LEDs in kilograms per year. 
Although respondents should be relatively good at asВӯ
sessing energy cost, they are unfamiliar with kilograms 
of carbon emission. If they are unsure about the response 
scale, there will be high levels of measurement error 
(Haaland et al. 2023). Because of this concern, we meaВӯ
sured beliefs using multiple choice questions to reduce 
the dispersion of beliefs.
Our belief measures were not incentivized. The eviВӯ
dence of incentivesвҖҷ impact on belief elicitation is mixed. 
Charness et al. (2021) find that introspection seems to do 
as well as relatively complex incentivized methods. 
Trautmann and van de Kuilen (2015), Allcott et al. (2020), 
Hoffman and Burks (2020), and Roth and Wohlfart 
(2020) also draw similar conclusions. In addition, HaaВӯ
land et al. (2023) pointed out that incentives have similar 
effects as a prompt to google the statistic of interest. It 
might be a problem for our study as our information proВӯ
vision treatments require tight control over the informaВӯ
tion environment. Weighing the pros and cons, we 
decided to implement nonincentivized elicitation.
6.3. Inattention
RespondentsвҖҷ inattention threatens the internal validity 
of experiments in social sciences research. It occurs when 
respondents carelessly read instructions or items and 
provide thoughtless answers, which is especially a conВӯ
cern for online surveys because of respondentsвҖҷ low 
intrinsic interest, long measures, no social exchange, and 
little control over the environment (Vecchio et al. 2020). 
Moreover, it is more of a concern with experiments relyВӯ
ing on stated preference data than those relying on 
revealed preference data (Murphy et al. 2005).
Multiple data patterns can result from inattentive or 
careless responses. For example, some respondents may

6.4. Demand Effect
Demand effect is a concern in nonnatural experiments, 
as participants might deliberately choose to either comВӯ
ply with or defy the perceived intent of the study, as 
inferred from the detected cues (Zizzo 2010). Therefore, 
the demand effect, if present, should be more substantial 
immediately after the information treatment than in the 
follow-up waves. To assess the magnitude of the potenВӯ
tial demand effect right after information provision, we 
conducted an additional one-wave study and applied 
the method proposed by De Quidt et al. (2018).
The basic idea behind this approach is to create two 
phrasings for demand treatments to signal a desire for 
high or low actions. Each of our respondents completed 
one of the nine experimental conditions provided in this 
design. We included the three original conditions apВӯ
plied in Experiment 2 (the control, monetary, and enviВӯ
ronmental treatments denoted as вҖңneutralвҖқ phrasings). 
We then added a вҖңpositiveвҖқ phrasing to each of the three 
original conditions. Specifically, on the information 
screen, participants were told, вҖңWe expect that particiВӯ
pants who are shown this information will increase their 
WTP for LEDs.вҖқ We also constructed a вҖңnegativeвҖқ phrasВӯ
ing for each of the three original conditions by telling 
our participants, вҖңWe expect that participants who are 
shown this information will decrease their WTP for 
LEDs,вҖқ resulting in nine experimental conditions.
We aim to assess if the behavior under the neutral 
phrasing differs significantly from the unobserved 
demand-free behavior. We assume that any underlying 
demand effect is less extreme than the positive and negaВӯ
tive phrasings. This is because the magnitude of the 
demand effect would increase with the proportion of 
respondents who detected the researchersвҖҷ intent. This 
effect would be further pronounced in treatments where 
the expected results are conveyed to the participants. By 
deliberately inducing experimenter demand using posiВӯ
tive and negative phrasings, we can find upper and 
lower bounds on the demand effect induced by our neuВӯ
tral phrasing.

conclude that the demand effect is not likely to be the priВӯ
mary driver of our results.
7. Conclusions and Implications
Focusing on China as the largest CO2 emitter and the 
largest consumer market for electronic consumer goods 
globally, we addressed a policy-relevant issue, evaluatВӯ
ing the role of filling the information gap versus increasВӯ
ing saliency on peopleвҖҷs demand for climate-friendly 
goods. Our results indicate that an information intervenВӯ
tionвҖҷs persistent effect depends on householdsвҖҷ baseline 
beliefs. Thus, by identifying the households that conВӯ
tinue to be impacted by information for long periods, 
policy makers can better evaluate the cost-effectiveness 
of promoting the adoption of low-carbon appliances in 
different populations.
Identifying the cognitive route also contributes to disВӯ
ambiguating the mixed empirical evidence reported in 
the extant literature about the effectiveness of informaВӯ
tion campaigns. Because a major effect of information is 
achieved through belief updating of low-belief consuВӯ
mers, the effect size of an information intervention 
would be small in environments where consumers canВӯ
not process the information immediately or correctly. In 
addition, without belief updating, the effect size would 
be essentially zero when information saliency is weak. 
This is likely the case in physical stores where hundreds 
of products and other stimuli compete for attention.
Consistent with these inferences, the positive effect of 
an information intervention is usually reported in studВӯ
ies with deliberately simple and controlled choice envirВӯ
onments (e.g., Newell and SiikamВЁaki 2014, Allcott and 
Taubinsky 2015), whereas null results are mostly found 
by studies conducted in physical stores (e.g., Allcott and 
Taubinsky 2015, Allcott and Sweeney 2016). An exВӯ
ception is the finding of an randomized controlled trial 
(RCT) conducted in an online store, where the authors 
found that adding energy cost information alongside 
energy labels increases purchasing of less efficient refrigВӯ
erators (dвҖҷAdda et al. 2022). In addition, the authors 
reported that treated consumers spent more time browsВӯ
ing and searching for products. Although the RCT did 
not test for consumersвҖҷ baseline beliefs, this adverse 
effect of information can be explained by consumersвҖҷ 
overestimating savings from purchasing efficient appliВӯ
ances. By correcting for this biased belief, information 
treatment increased, rather than decreased, inefficient 
purchases.
Our findings show that information on monetary savВӯ
ings and information on environmental benefits are not 
perfect substitutes. They can affect the adoption behavior 
of low-carbon appliances through information filling 
and cue-driven channels. On the one hand, policies that 
provide information can enjoy a short-term effect on 
WTP because of the cue-driven channel, regardless of 
the consumersвҖҷ baseline beliefs. At the same time, for the 
uninformed, providing information can influence their 
WTP through the cognitive channel. Furthermore, it 
may persistently affect uninformed consumers because 
of belief updating. However, the long-term componentвҖҷs 
marginal benefit should decrease with repeated interВӯ
vention, as more consumers would become informed of 
the message in each successive round. Thus, these short- 
and long-term effects should be incorporated into calcuВӯ
lating the cost-effectiveness of information programs.
Moreover, our results suggest that information proВӯ
gramsвҖҷ cost-effectiveness is highly sensitive to the targeted 
groupвҖҷs attitudes toward climate change. For example, 
climate change skeptics, who constituted 5% of our samВӯ
ple, did not respond to the environmental information. 
Hence, we speculate that when such an information interВӯ
vention is applied to a population with a higher proporВӯ
tion of climate change skeptics, the aggregate effect of 
information provision would be lower. Similarly, inforВӯ
mation campaigns should intensify when climate change 
impacts are particularly salient because of extreme cliВӯ
matic events or specific policy decisions. Thus, informaВӯ
tion campaign designers can substantially improve their 
efficacy by targeting the most receptive audience.
Our research is of value to marketers, companies, 
and nonprofit organizations seeking to encourage low- 
carbon consumption and promote sustainability. ChiВӯ
nese (and other world) companies face ambitious climate 
objectives. For example, China has committed to becomВӯ
ing carbon neutral by 2060. This requires an unpreceВӯ
dented transformation in production and businesses. 
Adopting low-carbon and energy-efficient technologies 
is a key pillar of this transformation. In addition, Chinese 
citizens are concerned about the risks associated with 
climate change (DechezleprЛҶetre et al. 2022), and they 
demand more comprehensive product information. Our
