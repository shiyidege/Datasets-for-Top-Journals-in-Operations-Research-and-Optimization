Abstract
Despite the growing importance of climate change adaptation, current studies offer limited
methodologies for selecting optimal supply network strategies under uncertain and dynamic
environmental conditions. This study aims to ﬁll this gap by identifying and prioritizing
the most suitable supply network strategies considering sustainability and climate change
impacts. A novel hybrid multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) approach is developed,
integrating Fermatean Fuzzy Analytical Hierarchy Process (FF-AHP) and Fermatean Fuzzy
Weighted Integrated Sum Product (FF-WISP) methods to address uncertainty and complexity
in the decision-making process. First, a comprehensive hierarchical evaluation framework,
consisting of three levels of criteria, including environmental, resilience, and socioeconomic
dimensions, was developed through literature review and expert input. FF-AHP was applied
to calculate the relative importance of these criteria, while FF-WISP ranked four strategic
alternatives. The results highlight Hybrid (Global + Local) with Flexible Logistics as the
most effective strategy, followed by Regional Sourcing with High-Resilience Investment.
Sensitivity analysis with randomized expert weights conﬁrmed the robustness of the proposed
model. This study contributes a ﬂexible and practical decision-support tool for policymakers
and supply chain managers facing climate uncertainty. Future research may extend this model
by integrating smart logistics technologies and renewable energy-based criteria for broader
sustainability applications.

1 Introduction
Climate change is a rapidly increasing major crisis in the world. Extreme weather conditions,
such as heavy rainfall, heat waves, and drought, cause major damage to infrastructure and
power lines, as well as adverse health effects (Bag et al., 2023). Additionally, such extreme
weather events and climate change increasingly destabilize global transportation systems,
creating signiﬁcant uncertainty and operational troubles (Guitouni et al., 2025). Also, mit-
igating climate change is one of the greatest problems facing societies, which is closely
connected to the topic of the future energy system (Potrˇc et al., 2021).
Since these problems caused by climate change can easily impact the supply chain net-
work, the production and transactions of industrial products are also affected (Li et al., 2025).
Unpredictable extreme events and disruptions due to climate change are considered an essen-
tial part of the supply chain network (Zavala et al., 2019). For instance, the extreme heat wave
in China caused reduced food production and even food shortages and price increases in both
local and global food supply chains (Ali et al., 2023).
As climate change affects both social and commercial activities, many countries face many
challenges, such as uneven development across different regions, resource scarcity, and difﬁ-
culty in adaptation (Li et al., 2025). Adopting climate change conditions of supply networks
has many advantages, such as less environmental pollution, combating industrial waste,
adaptation to extreme conditions, and sustainability. Thus, designing a supply chain network
under climate change conditions may support sustainability and environmental protection.
Sensitivity of the industrial sector to climate change may help them recognize variations in
exposure to climate change across regions and respond to climate change in their regions
(Li et al., 2025). Given the uncertainty of future climate conditions, sound planning and
decision-making processes are critical (Guitouni et al., 2025). This study presents a novel
framework for supply networks in the face of climate change.
In this context, the selection of appropriate supply network strategies becomes a critical
factor in building climate-resilient and sustainable operations. As supply chains are increas-
ingly exposed to climate-related disruptions, decision-makers must not only anticipate risks
but also proactively choose strategies that can withstand or adapt to varying environmen-
tal conditions. Strategic choices, such as sourcing models, inventory policies, and logistics
ﬂexibility, can signiﬁcantly inﬂuence a supply network’s ability to maintain functionality
during extreme weather events. Therefore, identifying and implementing the most suitable
strategy under climate uncertainty is essential for minimizing disruptions, preserving supply
continuity, and supporting long-term sustainability goals. A well-structured strategy enables
ﬁrms to optimize operational efﬁciency while reducing environmental impacts, enhancing
adaptability, and ensuring economic resilience. This study addresses this pressing need by
proposing a systematic decision-making model to evaluate and prioritize supply network
strategies in response to climate change challenges.
This study aims to identify and prioritize supply network strategies in response to the
growing challenges posed by climate change. The primary objective is to determine the most
effective and appropriate strategy among several alternatives, based on a comprehensive set of
evaluation criteria. These criteria were established through an extensive literature review and
validated by expert input, ensuring a robust and context-speciﬁc assessment framework that
incorporates both sustainability and climate-related considerations. To achieve this goal, the
study introduces a novel hybrid multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) approach to address
the uncertainty and complexity inherent in strategic supply network decision-making under
climate uncertainty.

3 Proposed methodology
This study proposes an innovative hybrid decision-making methodology to identify the most
effective strategy for managing risks in supply networks under climate change. The method-
ology, termed FF-WISP, introduces a novel integration of Fermatean fuzzy logic with the
traditional WISP method. Furthermore, this approach presents the ﬁrst known combination of
the AHP and WISP within a Fermatean fuzzy environment, thus offering a robust framework
for addressing complex MCDM problems characterized by uncertainty.
The process begins with the application of the FF-AHP to determine the relative impor-
tance of evaluation criteria across hierarchical levels. Pairwise comparison matrices (PCMs)
are used to capture expert judgments, which are then aggregated and tested for consistency.

3.1 Preliminaries
In complex decision-making problems, particularly those involving conﬂicting criteria under
uncertainty, traditional fuzzy approaches such as Intuitionistic and Pythagorean fuzzy sets
may fall short. This limitation stems from the structural constraints imposed on the member-
ship and non-membership degrees—speciﬁcally, their sum (in Intuitionistic) or squared sum
(in Pythagorean) must not exceed 1. These constraints narrow the representation space and
may restrict the ability to model expert hesitations or ambiguity effectively.
In contrast, FFSs, introduced by Senapati and Yager (Senapati & Yager, 2019), overcome
this restriction by allowing the sum of the cubes of membership (μ) and non-membership
(ν) degrees to be at most 1. This expanded ﬂexibility enables a richer expression of uncer-
tainty and greater tolerance for ambiguity in expert evaluations. FFSs have thus emerged

particularly in MCDM under uncertain or imprecise environments (Garg et al., 2020).

3.2 Fermatean fuzzy AHP
FF-AHPbuildsupontheclassicalAHPdevelopedbySaaty(Saaty,1980),awidelyrecognized
method for solving decision problems involving quantitative and qualitative criteria. AHP
provides a structured and transparent approach for decision-making by organizing complex
problemsintohierarchicallevelsandallowingdecision-makerstomakepairwisecomparisons
between criteria. One of its key strengths is its ability to incorporate subjective judgments,
making it highly applicable in both individual and group decision contexts (Ertugrul Ayyildiz
& Taskin Gumus, 2020). Fuzzy extensions of AHP have been developed to better manage
ambiguity and vagueness in expert evaluations. These models integrate fuzzy logic and fuzzy
set theory, enabling more ﬂexible representations of linguistic preferences (Ertugrul Ayyildiz,

allow for a broader and more expressive modeling of uncertainty compared to intuitionistic
and Pythagorean fuzzy sets. FF-AHP enhances the decision-making process by capturing
hesitancy, inconsistency, and ambiguity more effectively (Ayvaz et al., 2024). In this study,
the FF-AHP method is applied to determine the relative importance of evaluation criteria in

3.3 Fermatean fuzzy WISP
Traditional MCDM methods are generally classiﬁed into two main categories: utility-based
approaches, which are user-friendly and widely applicable (Hafezalkotob et al., 2019), and
outranking methods, which, while more analytical, tend to become computationally burden-
some when dealing with a large number of alternatives (Rani et al., 2023). Among these, the
WISP method has gained attention for its simplicity, computational efﬁciency, and ability to
evaluate beneﬁt and cost-type criteria simultaneously.
The WISP approach, originally proposed by Stanujkic et al. (2023), merges the strengths
of the Weighted Sum Model (WSM) and the Weighted Product Model (WPM). By combining
these techniques, WISP simpliﬁes the normalization process and employs four distinct utility
measures to calculate the comprehensive utility score of each alternative, thereby facilitating
more straightforward and transparent rankings (Stanujkic et al., 2022). Recent studies suggest
that WISP achieves more robust and reliable results than many conventional MCDM methods
by utilizing multiple utility perspectives (Görçün et al., 2024).
WISP is used in this study instead of classical benchmarks such as TOPSIS, VIKOR, or
PROMETHEE because WISP integrates additive (WSM) and multiplicative (WPM) aggre-
gation into a single scheme and evaluates alternatives through multiple utility measures,
yielding balanced compensation between beneﬁt and cost criteria and reducing ranking sen-
sitivity to any single normalization or operator. Prior comparative studies show that WISP’s
rankings are highly correlated with prominent MCDM methods while retaining transparent
computations suitable for extensive sensitivity analysis (Stanujki´c et al., 2021). Methodolog-
ical analyses further report the robustness of WISP under different normalization procedures
and its alignment with results from other methods, supporting reliability in practice (Zavad-
skas et al., 2022a, 2022b). In climate-oriented supply network evaluation, this WSM + WPM
balance is crucial to avoid the anomalies of purely additive or purely multiplicative mod-
els, considering the trade-offs across environmental, resilience, and economic dimensions
are non-linear and mixed-type (beneﬁt/cost). Moreover, recent fuzzy extensions of WISP
demonstrate its compatibility with uncertainty modeling, reinforcing its suitability for our
Fermatean fuzzy framework (Zavadskas et al., 2022a, 2022b).
While several extensions of the WISP method have been proposed using various fuzzy
environments and real-world applications (Rani et al., 2024), this study presents the ﬁrst
adaptation of WISP within a Fermatean fuzzy framework. This research introduces the FF-
WISP model to address the limitations of earlier fuzzy approaches (e.g., intuitionistic and
Pythagorean sets), which impose restrictive conditions on membership values. This enhanced

4.1 Calculation of criteria weights with the FF-AHP method
In this study, expert input played a critical role across several stages of the methodology.
First, experts contributed to identifying the key criteria that inﬂuence the resilience of sup-
ply networks under climate uncertainty. Next, their evaluations guided the development of a
hierarchical structure, helping to determine the relative importance of these criteria. Finally,
expert feedback was used to interpret and validate the results within the context of sus-
tainable supply chain management. A rigorous selection process was applied to ensure the
validity and reliability of the expert contributions. Experts were selected based on criteria
including a minimum of ﬁve years of relevant professional or academic experience, demon-
strated expertise in supply chain management or related ﬁelds, active involvement in research
or practice, and a strong publication record. Therefore, a panel of ten anonymous experts
(E1–E10) was formed. Throughout the study, these experts participated in structured evalua-
tions using linguistic terms, contributed to pairwise comparisons using the FF-AHP approach,
supported the consistency and soundness of the decision framework, and constructed an alter-
native evaluation matrix. Their diverse backgrounds ensured that the resulting model was not
only theoretically rigorous but also grounded in practical relevance. The involvement of this
panel signiﬁcantly enhanced the robustness, transparency, and applicability of the proposed
methodology for resilient and sustainable supply network design.
The panel comprises ten anonymous experts (academics, senior industry practitioners,
and policy/risk consultants), selected using minimum experience (≥5 years), domain exper-
tise, and active scholarly/industrial engagement criteria. We apply the FFW operator at the
123

Annals of Operations Research
judgment level to aggregate judgments, which preserves inter-expert heterogeneity without
forcing consensus. Conﬂicts in opinions are thus resolved statistically via aggregation rather
than ad-hoc override; additionally, we monitor CI/CR per expert and request clariﬁcation
only when CR exceeds the 0.10 threshold (none did). While we adopt equal expert weighting
ex-ante to avoid subjective privileging, we also conduct robustness checks with non-uniform
weights. Speciﬁcally, Sect. 4.2’s sensitivity analysis includes randomized expert-weight sce-
narios (Table 9).
To enable a robust comparison of the main evaluation criteria, input was obtained from
a panel of domain experts using a predeﬁned set of linguistic terms (as shown in Table 2).
Based on these assessments, PCMs were constructed for each expert, and the complete set
of PCMs is presented in Table 5.

lows: The diagonal elements are all marked as E (Equal importance), since each criterion
is compared with itself. According to Expert 1, C1 (Environmental Sustainability) is con-

C3 (Socioeconomic Factors), reﬂected by the VH (Very High Importance) rating. Simi-
larly, Expert 1 judges C2 as very high (VH) in importance compared to C3, assigning it a VH
value. These comparisons indicate that Expert 1 prioritizes environmental and climate-related
dimensions over socioeconomic aspects.
Next, the consistency ratios for each expert’s PCM were calculated to verify the reliability
of their judgments, with the results summarized in Table 6. Once conﬁrmed that all matrices
met the acceptable consistency thresholds, the AHP procedure was executed to derive the
ﬁnal weight values for the main criteria.
FF-AHP was utilized to determine the relative importance of the main criteria for selecting
the most suitable supply network strategy under climate change. The methodology was
rigorously applied following all procedural steps, assigning equal weighting to each expert’s
input. Expert evaluations were aggregated by leveraging the FF-AHP approach to derive
consistent and structured priority values. These values were then used to calculate the ﬁnal
weights of the main criteria, forming the basis for a comprehensive multi-criteria assessment
of strategic alternatives. The main criteria weights are obtained as 0.362, 0.342, and 0.297 for
Environmental Sustainability, Climate Resilience, and Socioeconomic Factors, respectively.

4.2 Ranking alternatives with the FF-WISP method
Each expert evaluates the set of alternative strategies (see Fig. 2) using the predeﬁned criteria
listed in Table 12 (see Appendix). To operationalize the ranking stage, we carry forward
the FF-AHP weights obtained for all Level-3 criteria (Table 8) and apply the FF-WISP
procedure to the expert evaluations reported in Appendix Table 12. The resulting FFN-based
decision matrices are then aggregated using the FFW operator, assuming each expert has
equal weight, ensuring a consistent and uniﬁed representation of expert opinions. Speciﬁcally,
for each criterion j, expert FFN ratings are aggregated via the FFW operator (Eq. 10) and
defuzziﬁed using the score function (Eq. 8) to obtain crisp performances. These are combined
with the FF-AHP weights to compute WSM/WPM values by criterion type (beneﬁt or cost;
Eqs. 15–18). Subsequently, both WSM and WPM values are computed for each alternative.
These values serve as the basis for calculating the utility scores associated with each option.
The remaining steps of the proposed methodology are then executed. We then derive the four
utility measures (Eqs. 19–22), normalize them (Eqs. 23–26), and compute the overall utility
for each alternative (Eq. 29), as illustrated in Fig. 3.
Figure 3 presents the evaluated supply network strategies’ ﬁnal utility scores, high-
lighting each alternative’s performance under the proposed FF–AHP–FF–WISP framework.
Among the four strategies, Hybrid (Global + Local) with Flexible Logistics emerges as the
most effective option with a normalized score of 1.000, indicating its superior adaptability
and robustness in managing climate-related supply chain challenges. Closely following is
Regional Sourcing & High-Resilience Investment with a score of 0.993, reﬂecting strong
performance, particularly in resilience and environmental adaptability. The Multiple Global
Suppliers & Moderate Safety Stock strategy scored 0.939, suggesting moderate ﬂexibility
and risk mitigation beneﬁts. Lastly, Single Global Sourcing & Lean Inventory received the
lowest score (0.922), reﬂecting its limited responsiveness and higher vulnerability due to
centralized sourcing and minimal inventory buffers. These results underscore the strategic

Theresultspresentedinthetablereﬂecttheimpactofvaryingexpertweightsetsontheﬁnal
utility scores of the four strategic alternatives using the FF-WISP method. Across all ten sim-
ulated scenarios, Hybrid (Global + Local) with Flexible Logistics (S4) consistently achieves
the highest score, either maintaining a perfect score of 1.000 or only slightly decreasing (as
seen in Set-6 and Set-9), reinforcing its position as the most robust and favorable strategy
under climate-related uncertainties. Similarly, Regional Sourcing & High-Resilience Invest-
ment (S3) also demonstrates strong performance, with scores ranging between 0.988 and
1.000, suggesting its stability as a top-tier strategy across a variety of weighting conditions.
In contrast, Single Global Sourcing & Lean Inventory (S1) and Multiple Global Suppliers
& Moderate Safety Stock (S2) exhibit greater sensitivity to changes in expert weights, with
S1’s scores ranging from 0.897 to 0.960 and S2 from 0.927 to 0.971. While both still per-
form well, these ﬂuctuations indicate a relatively higher dependence on speciﬁc weighting
schemes. Overall, the sensitivity analysis conﬁrms the robustness of the proposed ranking
framework, particularly the dominance of hybrid and regionally resilient strategies in enhanc-
ing supply chain sustainability and adaptability in the face of climate change.

5 Discussion
This study proposes scientiﬁc and robust results for ranking supply networks in the face of
climate change. For this purpose, with the help of literature review and expert views, this
paper offers 3-Level criteria sets. Level 1 represents Environmental Sustainability, Climate
Resilience, and Socioeconomic Factors; the second level identiﬁes Level-2 criteria, and the
Level-3 identiﬁes sub-criteria based on Level 2. Thus, alternative supply networks are eval-
uated under very comprehensive criteria using a novel hybrid MCDM methodology in this
decision-making process.
The results show that the most important main criterion for this decision-making pro-
cess is Environmental Sustainability. Among the nine sub-criteria in Level 2, the two most
important criteria were determined as Environmental Impact and Resource Efﬁciency with
0.1311 and 0.1256 weights. The environmental impact criteria are divided into environmen-
tally focused sub-criteria such as use of hazardous materials, pollution and waste production,
and biodiversity and habitat preservation. Among the Level 3 sub-criteria, the ﬁrst two most
important sub-criteria were determined as Hazardous Material Usage and Pollution & Waste
Generation. These criteria represent the negative impacts that may occur on the environment
123

Annals of Operations Research
as a result of supply network activities. Companies can improve their environmental perfor-
mance by determining their supply chain strategies in a way that will reduce the negative
impacts on the environment (Sabouhi et al., 2021). Furthermore, Climate Vulnerability and
Climate Adaptation Investment were determined as the third most important criteria with
equal weight values. The Infrastructure Upgrades criterion, which is linked to the Climate
Adaptation Investment criterion, was determined as the fourth most important sub-criterion.
This criterion refers to investment in physical improvements that protect the supply chain
from climate risks. Among the 27 sub-criteria in Level 3, the least important one was deter-
mined to be Stakeholder Engagement & Acceptance, which is related to the Community
Impact criterion. While each identiﬁed criterion is important for the supply network, this cri-
terion was rated as less important than those with particularly high environmental impacts.
Experts rated social impacts as less signiﬁcant for this sector than criteria based on envi-
ronmental and economic factors. These results emphasize that the activities and strategies
of supply networks should be evaluated in the light of environmental and climatic criteria
rather than social criteria. Mahmoud et al. (Mahmoud et al., 2024) emphasize the increasing
interest in cleaner production technologies and environmental impact mitigation in supply
chain management.
According to the ranking results of the alternatives, the supply networks are ranked from
most important to least important as follows: S4-S3-S2-S1. According to this ranking, the
best supply network strategy in light of the current criteria is determined as Hybrid (Global
+ Local) with Flexible Logistics Combining. All over the world, transportation infrastruc-
ture and logistics are becoming increasingly important as they become key factors affecting
international trade (Shibasaki et al., 2021). Combining both global and local suppliers with
adaptable logistics capabilities, this strategy offers operational ﬂexibility. It responds quickly
to disruptions by dynamically changing resources and routes, but it causes medium to high
complexity and costs. Since the criteria are placed in order of importance, focusing on envi-
ronmental and climate change adaptation rather than cost, this efﬁcient strategy that provides
operational ﬂexibility is selected as the best alternative. Given the priority placed on environ-
mental impact criteria, this alternative strategy stood out among others, particularly due to
its prominence in these aspects, according to the numerous expert panels. Flexible logistics
not only play an important role in achieving economic development, improving logistics efﬁ-
ciency, and increasing customer satisfaction, but also in ensuring efﬁcient and ﬂexible supply
chain management (Tian et al., 2025). Therefore, while the costs may be high, the long-term
beneﬁts will be greater. In this respect, it promotes economic development. Experts have
evaluated these alternative features as good in terms of efﬁcient use of resources. The second
important strategy is determined as Regional Sourcing & High-Resilience Investment. This
approach uses suppliers closer to the target markets, accompanied by signiﬁcant investment in
climate-resilient infrastructure and operations. However, it provides high resilience against
climate disruptions but requires substantial upfront investments. Li et al., to highlight the
importance of this issue, established an assessment framework for the vulnerability of indus-
trial sectors to climate change by considering a large number of industrial ﬁrms in China and
proposed approaches to reduce this vulnerability (Li et al., 2025). The Multiple Global Sup-
pliers & Moderate Safety Stock strategy ranked third in this evaluation process. This strategy
relies on multiple suppliers across different global regions, supported by moderate inventory
buffers. Balancing cost efﬁciency and resilience reduces risk through supplier diversiﬁca-
tion but increases coordination complexity. As can be seen, these strategies present different
advantages and disadvantages for the supply chain. Moreover, the least suitable strategy was
determined to be Single Global Sourcing & Lean Inventory. This strategy involves sourcing
primarily from one global supplier with minimal inventory to reduce costs. However, it is
123

Annals of Operations Research
highly vulnerable to climate disruptions due to dependency on a single location and limited
safety stock. This strategy, which has less ﬂexibility, has been evaluated by experts as more
advanced and less compatible than hybrid options.
Oursensitivityexercises,includingrandomizedandstructurednon-uniformexpertweight-
ings, show that the leading alternatives remain unchanged, indicating that the results are not
artifacts of equal weighting and are robust to reasonable expert-aggregation schemes.
The results propose that while all of these supply networks contribute to sustainable
development,decision-makersandmanagersshouldconsiderenvironmentalimpact,resource
efﬁciency, climate vulnerability, and especially hazardous material usage, energy efﬁciency,
pollution, and waste generation based on these criteria when evaluating and determining
supply network strategy to conﬁrm alignment with global sustainability goals and under
climate mitigation conditions.
6 Conclusion
This study emphasizes the critical factors of evaluating the sustainable supply networks in
the face of climate change. For this aim, it stresses that the optimization process of sup-
ply networks is important for more sustainable supply chain management, with a technique
reinforced by organized and holistic evaluation frameworks. The innovative and new integra-
tion of FF-AHP and FF-WISP methods provides a robust framework for evaluating supply
networks according to a comprehensive 3-Level criteria set.
The results highlight that the most vital factor in this decision-making process is Environ-
mental Sustainability, and the most important sub-criteria are Hazardous Material Usage and
Pollution & Waste Generation, which are based on this main criterion. The ﬁndings emphasize
that in evaluating and ranking the supply network process under climate change conditions,
governments and managers are guided through the assessment process using a wide range
of criteria sets, mainly reducing negative environmental impact. Additionally, the proposed
novel methodology suggests a systematic procedure that can be improved in various contexts.
Moreover, this study signiﬁcantly contributes to the literature on this progression by provid-
ing a comprehensive, systematic, and detailed analysis reﬂecting the connection between
environmental sustainability and climate change conditions with supply network optimiza-
tion. Thus, it ﬁlls the gap in this important area of the literature with a robust approach and
provides a guide for both future studies and managers, governments, and policymakers.
Further work can expand the supply network optimization process by including smart
logistics technology integration and renewable energy integration. This situation will produce
a more comprehensive framework for decision-making and the evaluation process. Addition-
ally, the proposed hybrid MCDM approach can be extended to other issues, such as logistic
system management or smart cities activities, and the criteria can be differentiated according
to these ﬁelds. These structures will further the pertinence and strength of the approach and
contribute to universal efforts to expand the scope of sustainability developments.
