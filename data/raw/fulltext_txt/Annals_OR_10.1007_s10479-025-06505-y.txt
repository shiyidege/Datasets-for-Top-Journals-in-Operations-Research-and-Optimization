Abstract
The Internet of Things (IoT) is a global platform of Internet-connected smart devices that
allows manufacturers to sensorize marketed products. Such products can provide manufac-
turers with feedback about relevant information, such as their status, use and location, which
contributes to signiﬁcantly reduce uncertainty in the time, quantity and quality of the prod-
ucts recovered during the reverse logistics (RL) process. This sensorization is especially
simple in connected appliances given the ease of installing sensors inside. This work shows
the convenience of using the IoT in the RL process of the household appliances sector by
analyzing the impact on recovery management. To do so, it proposes a simulation model
based on system dynamics. The main ﬁndings indicate that the IoT permits more reverse
supply chains’ inventory by signiﬁcantly increasing collected products’ average value. This,
in turn, allows for more backorder demand to be met and improves the client service level.
In light of all this, we conclude that the average RL process proﬁt made with an IoT model is
signiﬁcantly better than the RL process without such a model. With this simulation model, it
should also be noted that interoperability and communication improve throughout the supply
chain, which allows better decision making.
Keywords Household appliances · Internet of Things · Reverse logistics · Supply chain
management · System dynamics · Smart homes
1 Introduction
The European continent has faced a signiﬁcant increase in the generation of Waste Electrical
and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) in the last decade, which has gone from 8.5 million
tons (Mt) in 2010 to 10.8 Mt in 2020. Of this ﬁgure, 50% (5.4 Mt) correspond to the waste
generated by household appliances, 23.1% (2.5 Mt) to large home appliances, 13.9% (1.5 Mt)
to small home appliances and 13% (1.4 Mt) to cooling and freezing (APPLiA, 2023). At the
same time, the volume of appropriately collected and recycled WEEE has increased thanks

to the investments made by industry and the implementation of European regulations on the

of WEEE generated in the household appliance sector in 2020, only 54% (2.9 Mt) were

Of the remaining accounts for the 1 Mt mixed with metal scraps, 0.3 Mt ended up in

in Europe in 2022. All of this must be seen as an important challenge so that our society
manages in the best possible way the process of recovering and treating the waste that all these
products will generate at the end of their useful life to reduce the environmental impact as
much as possible. This process, called the reverse logistics process (RL) process (Guide Jr &
Van Wassenhove, 2002), is key to guarantee the adequate treatment of recovered components,
materials and waste (Krikke, 2011), and signiﬁcantly contributes to the transition toward more
circular and sustainable business models (Alarcón et al., 2020) by reducing the environmental
impact and promoting circular economy in WEEE management (Guarnieri et al., 2020).
RL has become a key competence of modern supply chains (Guarnieri et al., 2020), mainly
for its importance in recovering the value of products and managing their disposal. The
supply chain with RL, or reverse supply chain, reverse traditional or forward logistics ﬂows
by making them bidirectional (Guide Jr & Van Wassenhove, 2002) to include activities like
direct reuse, repair, refurbishment, remanufacturing, cannibalization, recycling, incineration
or landﬁlling (Alarcón et al., 2020). However, additional RL activities increase supply chain
management complexity because of the high degree of uncertainty in the time, quality and
quantity of returned or recovered products (Dekker et al., 2004).
One way of reducing this growing complexity in reverse supply chain is to apply new
technologies based on the Internet of Things (IoT). The yields obtained by adopting IoT
solutions are: more transparent and accurate information; the real-time visibility of materials
ﬂow along forward and reverse supply chains (Reaidy et al., 2015); more accurate product
tracking and traceability (Costa et al., 2013; Raman et al., 2018). The use of the elements
and technologies that the IoT provides can help to, among other issues, drive automation,
capture data and optimize the management of key processes, such as production planning
(Alarcón et al., 2016), or others that are more speciﬁc to reverse supply chain like recovery,
classiﬁcation, repair or remanufacturing. Perhaps, the real advantage that this new concept
offers reverse supply chain is the possibility to sensorize each product and to acquire data in
real time about, for example, its behavior, status and location. Properly processing the data
received from each product can help reverse supply chain to reduce the uncertainty about the
end of its life cycle and to, therefore, optimally plan its recovery and treatment.
In the last few years, employing the IoT has proved crucial to precisely manage reverse
supply chains, and it is widely accepted that it has become a very promising tool to make
huge rapid advances in circular economy development and, more especially, in those sectors
where its implementation is relatively easy. Besides, household appliances is an ideal sector
for IoT implementation due not only to the vast amounts of dangerous and polluting waste
produced but also to: (a) the size and characteristics of household appliances (refrigerators,
washing machines, dishwashers, televisions, monitors, ovens, microwaves, air-conditioning
appliances), which facilitate employing sensors that, in turn, may not be seen by clients
without altering their functionality or design; (b) the operational conditions of the majority
of products, which ensures electric supply to sensors and their Internet connection; and (c)
the low impact of implementing sensors on products’ ﬁnal price.

It is also worth considering European consumers’ growing interest in using connected
appliances in smart homes, which has been accounted for by the 400% increase in the number
of these smart homes in the last 7 years (from 18 million in 2017 to 72.1 million in 2024).
Certainly, the application of new IoT-based technologies will continue to grow in forthcoming
years, which will provide users with more convenience and new opportunities to improve the
transmission and collection of data to manufacturers in the sector.
Despite the suitability of the household appliances sector for implementing the IoT in the
RL process, very little has been published on real implementations (Ingemarsdotter et al.,
2019; Zhou et al., 2017) and its use is very nascent. For this reason, researchers, managers and
users still have serious doubts about the speciﬁc advantages of applying the IoT in this sector
and its impact on key performance indicators, such as inventory level, backorder demand,
client service level and, ﬁnally, reverse supply chain’s proﬁt.
A good way to help understand the advantages of using the IoT in the RL process of the
home appliance sector, and therefore enhance its use, is to compare scenarios, i.e., the non
IoT versus the IoT. The construction of different models that could be compared to certain
key variables would not only allow us to understand the possibilities for improvement, but
could also even quantify and provide their order of magnitude. So, it is worth highlighting
the usefulness of simulation methods as tools for comparing different models, processes
or scenarios in the supply chain ﬁeld (Terzi & Cavalieri, 2004), speciﬁcally the system
dynamics (SD) method as an emerging powerful tool to simulate a supply chain and to
demonstrate the impact of different policies on its performance (Rebs et al., 2019). SD
can be a helpful tool when used in the RL ﬁeld (Georgiadis & Vlachos, 2004a). It is a
methodology and computer simulation modeling technique used to understand the dynamic
behavior of complex systems to analyze and solve complex problems (Poles, 2013). It is,
therefore, considered an appropriate tool for the reverse supply chain context that is herein
contemplated.
For all these reasons, our main motivation is to improve the sustainability of the companies
and supply chains that belong to the house appliances sector by demonstrating the positive
impact of using the IoT on the key performance indicators of reverse supply chains. For
this purpose, an SD model is proposed to simulate the operation of several scenarios by
providing a strategic decision-support system. In this case, the simulation model compares
the performance of key indicators, such as inventory, service level and proﬁts, between a
reverse supply chain with and without the IoT.
Hence, on the one hand, the practical signiﬁcance of this research is manifold:
• Promote environmental care by facilitating the recovery and treatment of waste by a more
efﬁcient RL process due to IoT use.
• Design a strategic decision-support tool to facilitate managers’ decisions about incorpo-
rating the IoT into a product.
• Demonstrate that IoT use in the RL process of the house appliances sector improves the
performance indicators of supply chain so that company managers can better understand
the advantages of its implementation.
• Promote IoT use in the RL process as a way to make it more efﬁcient by improving the
key indicators and sustainability of supply chains.
• Develop a new model using SD that includes relevant characteristics of the household
appliances sector (not previously modeled) to be applied with the appropriate modiﬁcations
to quantify the impact of using the IoT in other similar scenarios.
On the other hand, theoretical signiﬁcances are achieved by:

2.1 Using the IoT in the reverse logistics field for household appliances
Nowadays, investment in technological innovations is essential to improve the performance
and efﬁciency of business processes. Such investments allow organizations to adopt new
tools and methodologies, which optimize their operations, reduce costs and improve client
satisfaction(Mashudetal.,2021,2022;Pervinetal.,2020).Oneofthetoolswiththestrongtest
impacts in recent years has been the IoT (Pérez-Perales et al., 2018). It represents a crucial
innovation by transforming common objects into smart, connected devices characterized by
their ability to collect and transmit real-time data on the location, status and performance of
products and resources (Bressanelli et al., 2018).
In the supply chain management context, IoT features provide unprecedented visibility for
the ﬂow of materials and products (Ingemarsdotter et al., 2020) by enabling remote monitor-
ing, improved traceability and process automation (Rejeb et al., 2022). This, in turn, facilitates
data-driven decision making (Pérez-Perales et al., 2018), helps to reduce uncertainty, min-
imizes errors, reduces the risk of loss and facilitates a rapid response to any problems that
may arise in supply chains (Geerts & Leary, 2014) by offering a complete, up-to-date picture
of the ﬂow of materials and products. All this allows companies to better anticipate inventory
needs, optimize production planning and improve client service levels (Guide et al., 2000;
Williams & Waller, 2011).
One of the supply chain management processes in which the IoT is truly transformative
is the RL process. The integration and use of IoT-based devices in this process provides real-
time data on the status and location of returned products, which improves the efﬁciency and
accuracy of recovery and treatment operations (Parry et al., 2016). This not only facilitates the
planning and execution of activities, such as recycling, remanufacturing and ﬁnal disposal,
but also optimizes the traceability of products throughout their life cycle. The improved
visibility and responsiveness offered by the IoT help to minimize the costs associated with
RL and to maximize the value recovery of returned products to, thus, contribute to a more
sustainable and efﬁcient supply chain (Bressanelli et al., 2018).
The advantages of the IoT in the RL process are particularly evident in the household
appliances sector, which is characterized by the large quantity and wide variety of generated
electronic waste (Bressanelli et al., 2020). For example, in 2019 around 53.6 Mt of electric
123

and electronic waste were generated worldwide (Forti et al., 2020), of which around 50%
were generated from the household appliances sector (APPLiA, 2023), and only 17% were
appropriately collected and recycled. These ﬁgures justify that improving waste management
should be made a priority need worldwide.
This need for improvement in the management of the waste from household appliances
could be addressed with technologies like the IoT (Gu et al., 2017; Nižeti´c et al., 2020),
mainly because the implementation of IoT devices into household appliances allows the
detailed, real-time monitoring of each unit from its sale to the end of its useful life. All this
greatly facilitates the recovery and recycling of valuable components, as well as the correct
disposal of hazardous materials (Ingemarsdotter et al., 2020). Furthermore, the data collected
by means of the IoT can be used to optimize collection routes, to improve disassembly and
recycling processes, and to increase transparency and accountability in waste management
(Parajuly & Wenzel, 2017).
However, despite the potential beneﬁts of the IoT in the RL ﬁeld of household appliances,
the reviewed literature mainly focuses on theoretical applications or limited case studies,
with only a few works based on real implementations.
In one of these scarce works, Ingemarsdotter et al., (2019) examine the application of
the IoT to washing machines to monitor and control washing cycles by means of a mobile
application that allows remote management.
Another of the works that focus on IoT use in the RL ﬁeld of household appliances is that
by Bressanelli et al., (2017). Their work proposes and discusses the actions needed to reshape
the washing machine industry to move toward a circula economy scenario. It suggests actions
to develop a circular economy business model for washing machines built on four pillars:
(i) circular product redesign; (ii) establishing a new business model; (iii) a reverse cycle due
to supply chain redesign; (iv) technology as an enabler: the IoT, cloud support, big data and
analytics. In the last block, these authors indicate that the IoT offers several opportunities,
such as the generation of diagnostic data, remote control and service, single user access,
measurement, payment and ﬁrmware updating. Eleven smart connected washing machines
operate, and a large data volume is generated on machine type, ownership and location, uti-
lization and functioning status, diagnostic data, measurement of main consumables (energy,
water, detergents), login access and usage by single users and their respective bills, connected
repairers or service providers. These data should be stored in an appropriate data repository
in the cloud from where they can be retrieved to support the development of predictive analyt-
ics to, for instance, forecast the future statuses of washing machines and, consequently, their
future maintenance and repair needs, as well as spare parts. The results of this work show
that households can proﬁt from an average yearly saving of almost 30% of today’s washing
costs, a country’s total electricity generation and water use can be respectively reduced by
about 0.6% and 1%, and the amount of generated waste would also lower.
In summary, the main opportunities that the IoT offers for products like household appli-
ances consist of improving the tracking and maintenance of records of products in use and
their post-use, condition monitoring, predictive maintenance, an accurate estimation of the
useful life of devices and products, and decision-making support (Ingemarsdotter et al.,
2020), to improve efﬁciency in the RL process, reduce the number of WEEE and increase
the percentage of properly controlled and waste managed. All these potential advantages and
the existing gap in the literature reveal the need to conduct more practical research and real
applications on IoT use in the household appliances sector to improve RL.

2.2 Using system dynamics in the RL field for household appliances
System dynamics is a computational simulation modeling methodology and technique used
to understand complex systems’ dynamic behavior. Introduced by Jay W. Forrester (Forrester,
1961), SD was presented as a modeling and simulation methodology for long-term decision
making in dynamic industrial management problems. Since then, SD has been a widely
used tool by academics, companies and government organizations in complex and dynamic
contexts to improve decision making (Ghisolﬁet al., 2017), such as developing strategies
development and designing policies (Alamerew & Brissaud, 2020). In the same vein, some
authors also consider SD to be a powerful, efﬁcient methodology to study in-depth problems
of dynamic complexity (Das & Dutta, 2013), while others highlight its ability to model a
large number of entities and to facilitate understanding of the system. (Moubed et al., 2021;
Oliveira et al., 2016).
In the supply chain management context, SD has been used to model and optimize material
and information ﬂows, to evaluate inventory and production policies, and to improve global
reverse supply chain resilience and sustainability.
Hence SD is presented as an essential tool in academic research into supply chain man-
agement, especially in the RL ﬁeld. This simulation tool, which is based on feedback control
theory and non linear dynamics, allows complex interactions and feedback within reverse
supplychainstobemodeled(Franco,2019;Sterman,2002).SDsimulationsstandoutfortheir
ability to incorporate feedback loops, which are essential for understanding and improving
reverse supply chain models (Rebs et al., 2019).
The application of SD has proven extremely effective in various RL aspects, ranging from
the management of closed supply chain (Georgiadis & Besiou, 2008; Georgiadis & Vlachos,
2004b; Golroudbary & Zahraee, 2015; Quariguasi Frota Neto et al., 2009), and from the
evaluation of treatment strategies (Alamerew & Brissaud, 2020; Das & Dutta, 2013; Wang
et al., 2014) to the analysis of sustainable business models (Georgiadis, 2013; Rebs et al.,
2019). These studies highlight the critical importance of SD as a robust tool that not only
improves processes in RL, but also facilitates informed and sustainable decision making in
supply chains.
In the RL context, it is important to remark that the SD methodology has been speciﬁcally
applied to the household appliance sector to addres various issues related to its management.
For example, Georgiadis and Besiou (2008) developed an SD model for the recycling of
electrical appliances in Greece by identifying key factors, such as legislation, reuse of non-
renewable materials and waste reduction. They went on to extend this model to include
economic dimensions by evaluating the impact of various internal and external variables on
RL (Georgiadis & Besiou, 2010). In another work, Georgiadis (2013) applied SD to study
capacity planning policies in recycling networks by recommending simultaneous investments
in production capacity and collection centers to improve long-term proﬁtability.
Additionally, Poles (2013) explored how remanufacturing and inventory management
processes can be optimized using SD and concluded that increasing product returns and
reducingdeliverytimesimprovee-wasteremanufacturingcapacity.Furthermore,Besiouetal.
(2012) investigated the interaction between formal recovery systems and informal collectors
by discovering that the integration of the latter into formal systems promotes sustainability.
Studies like those by Wang et al. (2014) and Wang and Huo (2023) have used SD to evaluate
government support policies and their effect on the recycling industry. They indicate that
combined incentive and sanction strategies are more effective than unilateral measures.

Finally, Ghisolﬁet al. (2017) used an SD model to analyze how legal incentives and
bargaining power inﬂuence the formalization of contracts with recycling companies by high-
lighting the importance of legal incentives, even with a small volume of collected waste.
As demonstrated in the cited works, SD is a suitable tool for modeling the RL of the
household appliances sector when applied to a wide range of issues and problems. Yet despite
not only the enormous potential of IoT use in reverse supply chains, and more speciﬁcally
in the household appliance sector, but also growing interest in modeling RL systems with
SD, the literature contains only one model that addresses RL simulation by means of SD
with technology related to the IoT, namely radio frequency identiﬁcation. This model has
been applied by several works, such as Gu and Gao (2011b, 2011c) and Gu et al. (2014).
These authors present an SD model applied to reverse supply chain by analyzing the results
of simulation both with and without radio frequency identiﬁcation (Gu & Gao, 2011c). In
this way, the change in inventories, service levels, and also the proﬁts of the collector, the
dismantling center and the remanufacturer, are shown by comparing both situations: with
and without radio frequency identiﬁcation. The results conclude that the service levels and
proﬁts of the collector, the disassembly center and the remanufacturer will increase, even as
their inventories grow when radio frequency identiﬁcation is used in the RL process. The
same authors (Gu & Gao, 2011b) have also compared the proﬁts made by applying radio
frequency identiﬁcation in an RL process at the supply and demand level by employing the
same model as in their last work. This study shows that radio frequency identiﬁcation use
helps to reduce the uncertainty of the quantity of collected used products in the RL process
and increases proﬁts by more than 10% on average when demand exceeds supply. In a later
work, Gu et al. (2014) also applied SD to simulate employing radio frequency identiﬁcation
in the same process model, and to compare the impact of distinct transport interruptions on
different RL process members’ proﬁts. This work concludes that the highest degree of impact
occurs at the beginning of the RL process due to transport interruptions between the used
products market and the recovery facility. With this model, a RL process with three levels
(collector, disassembly and remanufacturer) is represented, and variation in the inventory
levels, service level and proﬁts of each level is shown. Indeed, with the model, service levels
and proﬁts increase when radio frequency identiﬁcation technology is used in the RL.
Nevertheless, this model does not refer to any speciﬁc product and it proposes only one
alternative treatment for recovered products. In our paper, this model is taken as a reference
after adapting it to the household appliances sector and increasing the number of disposition
alternatives to the three principal alternatives (reuse, repair and recycling). Table 1 offers a
comparison of the most representative works from the literature review. Many of them ana-
lyze IoT use in RL within the household appliances or e-waste scope, while others apply SD
simulation, however studies into IoT use are lacking. To encompass the four main character-
istics of this work (RL, the IoT, Household and SD), we bridge this gap in the literature by
proposing and testing a simulation model with notable contributions. Therefore, this paper
offers multiple novelties to the literature, which are: (i) the use of SD to support decisions
and simulate different IoT implementation scenarios in home appliances; (ii) the simulation
of RL process management in household appliances, from collection to ﬁnal treatment, by
contemplating reuse, repair and recycling; (iii) the demonstration that IoT use reduces the
backordering cost and improves client service by increasing the proﬁtability of the RL pro-
cess; and (iv) the promotion of Circular Economy and improved waste management in the
home appliance sector through the use of IoT.

3 Problem description and assumptions
This paper models a conventional reverse supply chain in household appliances by consider-
ing three basic RL operations of this sector: product collection, inspection and sorting, and
treatment or disposal. Based on these three operations, a three-stage reverse supply chain is
deﬁned that consists of (see Fig. 2):
• Stage 1: a product recovery plant for the collection of RL products
• Stage 2: a plant for the inspection and sorting of RL products
• Stage 3: three plants for the most frequent appliance treatments of RL products in a circular
eonomy context: reuse, repair and recycling (Alcayaga et al., 2019).
The SD model proposed by Gu and Gao (2011b, 2011c) and Gu et al., (2014) is used as
a starting point for the present work given its structural similarity with the targeted reverse
supply chain. This model has been employed by the original authors to analyze different
parameters in several contexts. Although the reverse supply chain structure presented in all
the aforecited papers has always been the same (collector, disassembly center and remanufac-
turer), the SD model in this work was readapted by considering three treatment alternatives
in Stage 3 (reuse, repair and recycling) instead of one. In this way, our model reﬂects the
reality of the RL process of the household appliances sector in a more precise and updated
way.
In this way, the reverse supply chain would consist of a ﬁrst product collection plant
(Recovery plant) that recovers products from clients (it is the product entry to the reverse
supply chain) and sends them to the second Inspection and Sorting plant, where products are
inspected to determine their treatment.
Three different routes exist from this second plant depending on the treatment of each
recovered product:
• Route 1, products can be derived to reuse (Reuse plant) to introduce them back onto the
secondary market without treatment.
• Route 2, products can be sent to a Repair plant, where product treatment is required before
placing them back on the market. From this Repair plant, if the product has been restored
to its original state (e.g. by replacing a part or component that was damaged in transit or
by ﬁxing an assembly defect), it is shipped to the primary market. Otherwise, it is diverted
to the secondary market.

• Route 3, products can be sent to a recycling plant, where they are disassembled to put their
components to good use.
These described product ﬂows are represented in Fig. 2 by a solid blue line. The demand
of recovered products comes from the primary market (recovered products that do not require
any treatment for reuse) and the secondary market (recovered products that require treatment),
while the components demand comes from manufacturing facilities. Consequently, demand
is allocated in the plants of Stage 3 and moves toward the plants of Stages 2 and 1, which
originates a ﬂow in the opposite direction to the product ﬂow, as depicted in Fig. 2 by a
ﬂashing orange line.
It should be noted that all the plants maintain their own inventory to meet demand, which
is one of the main variables used in the simulation model. The Recovery plant inventory
feeds on the used products collected from clients. These products are employed to fulﬁll the
Inspection and Sorting orders. With the Inspection and Sorting plant inventory, the orders
from plants for reuse, repair and recycling are, in turn, covered. These plants receive the
product, carry out the corresponding treatment and include it in their own inventories from
which the demands for the primary and secondary markets or factories are met as and when
appropriate.
Brief descriptions of the main assumptions of the problem are (in this order):
1. A used product is recovered in a three-echelon reverse supply chain
2. Last year’s historical rate of recovered products is deﬁned as normal distribution, with a
mean and standard deviation for each scenario
3. The shipment time between plants is 1 week. The delivery time of reused, repaired or
recycled products is 4 weeks
4. The model’s costs consist of a transporting cost between plants, a recovery cost when
a product is collected from the end client, a holding cost and a back ordering cost in
each plant, and a unit monitoring cost when the IoT is used, as reported by a household
appliance recovered company
5. Backorders incur a cost for the plant until they are delivered
6. All the recovered products are subjected to one of three possible treatments: reuse, repair
or recycling.
4 Work methodology
The present work applies an SD approach to simulate an reverse supply chain from the
electrical appliances sector and to analyze its performance by studying three key parameters:
inventory, orders backlog and proﬁt. These parameters are compared over a 100-week period
in two different scenarios: Scenario 1, which does not use the IoT in electrical appliances;
Scenario 2, characterized by using the IoT in electrical appliances.
In the area of simulation by SD, authors like Aracil and Gordillo (1997) justify that the
development and simulation process includes three main phases: conceptualization, where
information is provided about the problem that is to be addressed and its principal aspects
are deﬁned, as is the whole problem in its complete context; formulation, where the problem
is formulated based on formal language by establishing the stock ﬂow diagram and the
equations model that are established from the diagram and evaluation, where the hypotheses
proposed about the model are tested and the obtained results are evaluated.
An additional validation phase is added to this modeling systems’ phases following the
recommendations in the work of Law (2019) about how to build valid and credible simulation
123

Annals of Operations Research
Fig. 3 Main steps of the work methodology
models. This additional phase can be understood as the process of determining whether the
created simulation model is an accurate representation of the system.
As a result, a work methodology is presented that encompasses all the four previously
mentioned phases, like that used in some works such as Mula et al. (2013), Haghighi Rad
and Rowzan (2018) and Moubed et al. (2021) (Fig. 3):
• Step 1 describes the problem by deﬁning the reverse supply chain structure and products
ﬂow
• Step 2 presents the simulation model by deﬁning the parameters used to measure reverse
supply chain performance, showing the stock ﬂow diagram. This step also deﬁnes the
equations employed to simulate the model and explains the values of both scenarios
• Step 3 validates the simulation model with external data batteries, which demonstrate that
the model performs as expected
• Step 4 simulates the model. Graphs showing how the parameters evolve for each scenario
are obtained. Models’ performances are compared and the obtained results are discussed.
5 Simulation model description
According to the reverse supply chain scheme in Fig. 2, the objective of the simulation model
is to analyze the performance of the supply chain described with different scenarios, based on
a study for speciﬁc parameters. The impact of the IoT on an reverse supply chain is analyzed
by comparing a chain without the IoT (Scenario 1) and the same chain with the IoT (Scenario
2). From a practical point of view, the incorporation of the IoT into Scenario 2 was carried
out thanks to the smart connection of appliances with the client’s Wi-Fi and by automatically
sending data to the company.
Three parameters (related to the Recovery plant) are deﬁned to measure reverse supply
chain performance:
123

Annals of Operations Research
1. the inventory level, which shows the amount of product stored in the Recovery plant.
2. the service level, given by the number of the Recovery plant backorders concerning the
Inspection and Sorting plant.
3. proﬁt, given by the difference between the Recovery plant sales income from the Inspec-
tion and Sorting plant and the costs associated with product recovery and handling,
inventory and backordering.
The applied hypothesis is that Scenario 2 (with IoT) represents improvements in relation
to Scenario 1 (without IoT). The simulation using SD allows this hypothesis to be veriﬁed.
It also provides an order of magnitude of the improvements in the selected parameters that
can be achieved by using the IoT in a reverse supply chain.
The objective of the simulation is to demonstrate the validity of the hypothesis and to
promote IoT use by manufacturers of connected household appliances so that they can know
the behavior, status and location of products by anticipating their disposition decisions. The
stock ﬂow diagram of the studied reverse supply chain is illustrated in Fig. 4, which also
shows the relations between the different chain elements considered in the simulation model.
As observed, three large stages that correspond to the main actions of the RL exist, and they
follow an open loop reverse supply chain from left to right: a ﬁrst stage in which products are
collected (Recovery plant); a second stage in which they are inspected and sorted (Inspection
and Sorting plant); and a third one in which disposition alternatives take place (Reuse, Repair
and Recycling plants). The main variables from each stage are highlighted in a different color:
green for Stage 1, brown for Stage 2 and violet for Stage 3. Inventories are depicted with a
solid line, while orders or demand backlog are reﬂected by a ﬂashing line.

The economic values of costs and income were determined by a data analysis based on our

The recovery rate represents the number of recovered products during a certain time period
(1 week) in the recovery plant. This parameter is deﬁned as a variable random normal type,
determined by the following information: maximum, minimum, mean value, standard devi-
ation and parameter of random number calculations. In Scenario 1, this variable is simulated
with normal distribution [N (μ1, σ1)] because the amount of collected product to be used in
the reverse supply chain is generally uncertain and its behavior resembles normal distribu-
tion. In Scenario 2, it is also simulated with normal distribution [N (μ2, σ2)], where μ2 is the
new mean value and σ2 represents the new standard deviation of the scenario with the IoT.
To quantify the mean and standard deviation values in the two scenarios, a survey was
sent to a representative sample of 200 clients in the study target company to ask about two
aspects:
• Question 1 (without the IoT): Have you thought of contacting the company to deliver your
electrical appliance to it when you no longer use it?

6 Simulation model validation
Simulation was carried out using the Vensim© DSS for the Windows simulation program,
version 6.2, on a computer with an Intel Core i3 microprocessor and a 64-bit operating system.
Before performing simulation, the model’s validity was evaluated by different tests. These
tests consisted in simulating four scenarios with four different data batteries that combined
the mean and standard deviation values of the product recovery rate. These data batteries
allowed model performance to be known and to, therefore, foresee the order of magnitude
of the simulation results.
The four used data batteries (DB1, 2, 3 and 4) were as follows:
• DB1: both Scenarios 1 and 2 had the same mean product collection rate and a very similar
standard deviation (μ1 = μ2 = 75, σ1 = 4 σ2 = 4.5).
• DB2: both Scenarios 1 and 2 had the same mean and a very different standard deviation
(μ1 = μ2 = 75, σ1 = 25 σ2 = 1).
• DB3: both Scenarios 1 and 2 had very different means and the same standard deviation
(μ1 = 45 μ2 = 65, σ1 = σ2 = 5).
• DB4: both Scenarios 1 and 2 had very different means and very distinct standard deviations
(μ1 = 20 μ2 = 50, σ1 = 5 σ2 = 45).

After analyzing the obtained results, as major ﬁndings, the study revealed that the imple-
mentation of IoT technologies in the reverse supply chain of the home appliance sector would
signiﬁcantly improve the efﬁciency of the process by facilitating more efﬁcient management
of collecting and treating appliances at the end of their useful life. In addition, IoT use would
offer greater transparency and traceability by allowing the precise tracking of the ﬂow of
materials, which reduces uncertainties associated with the quality and quantity of recovered
products. Finally, the development of an SD-based model proved the positive impact of the
IoT on key reverse supply chain performance indicators, such as inventory, service and proﬁt
levels, by providing a solid strategic decision-making support system in this sector.
Given the nature of these recovered products, whose storage cost is relatively low, it was
proven that holding high average levels of inventory to meet demand would be worthwhile
because the total inventory cost would be compensated by the increase in the client service
level. Precisely, IoT use would allow and facilitate the increase in the “order quantity” (aver-
age value of the collected products) that would, in turn, signiﬁcantly increase that reverse
supply chain’s inventory. The comparison to the non IoT case was made by assuming a spe-
ciﬁc ordering frequency (1 week) with a lead time of zero (time to obtain recovered products
from clients) and a higher ordering cost (unit monitoring cost) in the IoT case.
These results could not be extrapolated to other cases in which perishable products or
with certain defectibility are managed (Pervin et al., 2018; Roy et al., 2020), where inventory
costs could be signiﬁcantly higher or change over time and, thus, the trade-off between the
inventory cost and the service level could change.
In view of the obtained results, several managerial implications can be highlighted. Inte-
grating the IoT into reverse supply chain can transform the way in which companies manage
their RL operations by providing greater control and efﬁciency. One of the main advantages
is an improvement of the provided data about each recovered product, which would imply
an improvement in inventory management and process efﬁciency. This improvement would,
in turn, allow higher satisfaction of demand for pending orders and would, thus, improve
the client service level. Additionally, the greater accuracy and real-time tracking capabilities
offered by the IoT could provide not only better decision making, but also greater coordina-
tion and collaboration in reverse supply chains. Moreover, both operational efﬁciency and
proﬁtability of operations improved so that managers could leverage these beneﬁts to main-
tain a competitive advantage by responding more effectively to changing market dynamics
and client expectations.
8 Conclusions
IoT technologies have proven key for providing the necessary data to reduce the uncertainty
inherent to products to be recovered in a reverse supply chain. One of the sectors with the
highest IoT implementation potentials is household appliances because of its relatively easy
application and the increasing need to reduce waste in one of the world’s most polluting
sectors. However, only a few works were found that address using IoT for reverse supply
chain in this sector.

Annals of Operations Research
To bridge this gap in the literature, this work proposes a model to improve reverse supply
chain performance by analyzing the impact of IoT use on an reverse supply chain of household
appliances with an SD-based simulation model. The SD-based supply chain simulation is a
tool that models real system elements, and simulates and achieves similar performance to
real ones by establishing parameters and variables to analyze the effects on the system under
study.
The modiﬁcation of speciﬁc parameters in the study allowed us to simulate the perfor-
mance of the reverse supply chain in a hypothetical scenario with the IoT versus the same
scenario without the IoT, and to observe the possible differences that could be obtained
between both. The results showed that applying the IoT in the reverse supply chain increased
both inventory and client service levels, and led to more proﬁts for the Recovery plant.
Therefore, the main contributions of this paper were as follows: on the one hand, to
demonstrate the interest of incorporating the IoT into the home appliance sector for better
recovery management; on the other hand, to propose an SD model applied to a household
appliances collector as a real-world test case. Moreover, as dynamic simulated RL behavior
can be used to evaluate supply chain performance, the SD model provides a strategic dynamic
decision-support system for conducting several scenario analyses. In this way, the practical
implications of this work include facilitating a quantitative evaluation of the impact of IoT
on reverse supply chains, offering a comparative analysis of performance with and without
IoT integration, using simulation as a decision-making aid by analyzing different possible
future scenarios, evaluating various inventory management policies for recovered products
and assessing reverse supply chains’ robustness when changes in input data or inventory
management policies occur.
Future research lines can focus on either extending the analysis of applying the IoT to other
household appliances reverse supply chain stages or improving the quality of the proposed
models by more profoundly characterizing this sector’s behavior.

Explanation of the Vensim equations:
• RANDOM NORMAL ({min},{max},{mean},{stdev},{seed})—Provides normal distri-
bution with a minimum value (min), a maximum value (max), the average value (mean),
standard deviation (stdev) and random number calculation parameter (seed).
• INTEG (rate, initial value)—Returns the integral of the rate. The rate is numerically inte-
grated. The initial value is the value of the variable on the left-hand side of the equation at
the start of simulation.
• MAX (A,B)—Returns the bigger of A and B.
• MIN (A,B)—Returns the smaller of A and B.
• DELAY FIXED (input, delay time, initial value)—Returns the value of the input delayed
by the delay time. The initial value is the value of the variable on the left-hand side of the
equation at the start of simulation.
