Abstract
The stochastic ﬂexible job-shop scheduling problem with random processing times is con-
sidered in this paper, where the makespan service level is maximized, i.e., the probability that
a schedule is completed before a given deadline. In practice, this deadline corresponds to the
scheduling horizon (e.g., a shift, a day or a week) during which the set of jobs to schedule
must be completed. Compared to previous research, the uncertainty is related to the machine
and the processing times of all operations processed on a machine are similarly impacted by
the machine status. This helps to propose a new way of generating scenarios and deal with
uncertainty. New bounds on the makespan service level are also proposed, and included in a
tabu search approach. Computational experiments are conducted, that illustrate the relevance
of the proposed approach. Several perspectives are proposed in the conclusions of the paper.
Keywords Scheduling · Flexible job-shop scheduling · Makespan service level · Stochastic
processing times · Machine-related uncertainty
1 Introduction
Found in many practical contexts where multiple resources can perform the same opera-
tion, the Flexible Job-shop Scheduling Problem (FJSP) is currently one of the most studied
scheduling problems (see the recent review of Dauzère-Pérès et al. (2024)). This problem
presents itself when identical resources of a given type are available or when resources are
ﬂexible and can process different types of operations. The additional complexity associated
with the assignment of operations to resources makes the FJSP signiﬁcantly more difﬁcult
to solve than the classical Job-shop Scheduling Problem (JSP).
In this article, we consider the stochastic version of the FJSP (SFJSP) with machine-
related uncertainty, since we believe that a crucial factor to be considered in many industrial

strategies to further guide the exploration of the neighborhood. Computational experiments
are conducted to validate our approach.
The paper is organized as follows. After a short literature review in Section 2, the problem
is described in Section 3, and in particular the notion of makespan service level. Then, Section
4 discusses key takeaways following (Flores-Gómez et al., 2023), focusing on the difference
between minimizing the mathematical expectation of the makespan and maximizing the

2 Literature review
The FJSP was originally stated by Brucker and Schlie (1990). Ever since, many papers have
been published proposing different solution approaches for this problem. Brandimarte (1993)
decomposes the problem into an assignment problem and a scheduling problem, and proposes
a hierarchical algorithm. Paulli (1995) proposes a very similar approach for the Flexible
Manufacturing System Scheduling problem, which is basically the FJSP with a constraint
limiting the maximal number of jobs that can be processed at the same time. In Hurink et al.
(1994), a more integrated approach using tabu search is suggested. Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli
(1997) were the ﬁrst to propose an integrated approach for the FJSP, called the multiprocessor
job-shop scheduling problem, using tabu search. The approach relies on a disjunctive graph
representation of each instance, a connected neighborhood structure representation of the
search space and feasibility and evaluation conditions. In García-León et al. (2015), a tabu
search using the neighborhood structure from Dauzère-Péres (1994) in the disjunctive graph
and novel feasibility and evaluation conditions are presented to optimize any regular criterion.
This work is extended to multi-objective optimization by García-León et al. (2019). In Shen
et al. (2018), the conditions in Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997) are revisited and improved
for the FJSP with sequence-dependent setup times and makespan minimization.
Several other techniques than tabu search have been used to solve different versions of
the FJSP such as Rossi and Dini (2007), where Ant Colony Optimization is used to solve the
fully ﬂexible FJSP with sequence-dependent setup and transportation times, and operation
lag times. Xing et al. (2009) propose a simulation model for a multi-objective version of
the FJSP to minimize the makespan, the workload of all the machines, and the workload
of critical machines. Yazdani et al. (2010) propose a heuristic based on the exploration of a

parallel variable neighborhood to increase the diversiﬁcation during the exploration of the
search space based on shake and local search procedures. Pezzella et al. (2008) develop a
GA-based approach to solve a resource-constrained operation-machine assignment problem
and the FJSP while optimizing the makespan, and Gao et al. (2008) propose a hybrid GA to
minimize the makespan, the maximal machine workload, and the total workload. A recent
survey on the FJSP and its extensions can be found in Dauzère-Pérès et al. (2024), which
shows that the research is both very diverse and active.
In the literature, the stochastic version of the classical job-shop scheduling problem has
been signiﬁcantly less addressed than its deterministic counterpart, even if real-life problems
may include stochastic parameters. The vast majority of research in scheduling considers the
continuous availability of resources (most often machines) on the scheduling horizon. This
is not necessarily the case in real industrial settings since a machine may not be productive
during short periods of time for different reasons (e.g., machine stop, start-up delay) or
for longer periods of time (e.g., machine breakdown, preventive maintenance). A literature
review on scheduling with deterministic machine availability constraints is proposed in Ma
et al. (2010). A survey of meta-heuristics for solving job-shop problems speciﬁcally with
machine availability constraints can be found in Tamssaouet et al. (2018).
When studied, the common practice in regards to the SFJSP is to minimize the mathemati-
cal expectation of a scheduling criterion. Very few papers consider the notion of service level
in scheduling problems. To the best of our knowledge, Golenko-Ginzburg et al. (1995) were
the ﬁrst to deﬁne the delivery performance as the probability of a given job to be completed
on time (i.e., to respect its due date). Daniels and Carrillo (1997) deﬁne, for a single-machine
scheduling problem, the β-robust scheduling problem (β-RSP) as the problem of identifying
the schedule with the maximum likelihood of achieving a ﬂow time not larger than a given
target level. The notion of β-robustness was extended by Beck and Wilson (2007) to deal
with the probabilistic JSP, while exploring the notion of α-makespan. An important differ-
ence between our work and the one of Beck and Wilson (2007) lies in the way the problem is
posed. Beck and Wilson (2007) aim to determine “as small a value of D as possible”, where
D is the time threshold (maximum value of the makespan, denoted T in our work), such that
there exists a sequence S whose probability that its makespan is smaller than or equal to D is
equal to a given probability α. In our work, we do not focus on minimizing the time thresh-
old, as it is considered as an input of the problem, but on maximizing the probability α (see
Section 3.2). Wu et al. (2009) approach the β-RSP with a constraint programming model,
explicitly representing the uncertainty and robustness as input parameters and objectives, by
developing explicit representations of the task uncertainty and enabling the uncertainty to
propagate. Although all the aforementioned papers deal with the optimization of the “service
level”, the term itself, used in inventory management (see for instance (Silver et al., 2016)),
was ﬁrst coined in scheduling in Dauzère-Pérès et al. (2005) and Dauzère-Pérès et al. (2013).
3 Problem description
As an extension of the JSP, the FJSP is thus NP-hard in the strong sense (Garey et al., 1976).
The random nature of processing times adds another level of complexity to the deterministic
FJSP, making the Stochastic FJSP (SFJSP) at least as hard to solve.

3.1 Stochastic Flexible Job-shop Scheduling Problem with machine-related
uncertainty
The FJSP aims at scheduling a set of operations O partitioned into a set of jobs J on a
set of machines M. The operations of each job j are grouped in set O j (
j∈J O j = O),
and have to be processed in a speciﬁc order (routing). A machine can only perform one
operation at a time, and cannot be interrupted once the operation starts, i.e., preemption is
not allowed. Unlike the JSP, each operation i can be processed on any machine in a given
subset of machines Mi in M in the FJSP. The processing time pim of operation i may vary
depending on the machine m to which i is assigned (i.e., processing times are machine-
dependent). Solving the FJSP requires determining both an assignment of the operations to
the machines and an order of the operations on the machines (i.e., a sequence of operations
on each machine, which implicitly includes the assignment of operations to machines). The
objective is to optimize a scheduling criterion, the most common one being the makespan or
Cmax, which is the maximum completion time of all operations.
Contrary to the FJSP, the SFJSP includes random processing times described via known
probability distributions. Let each random variable ξim modeling the processing time of
operation i on machine m be discrete and deﬁned on a ﬁnite support [cim, dim]. In practical
settings, an operation i takes a minimum time (cim) to be completed on a machine m and does
not take more than a maximum time (dim) under normal operating conditions (Jacobs et al.,
2003). By normal operating conditions, we mean that only short stoppages of the machine are
considered, but not major disturbances (e.g., machine breakdown, unexpected job arrivals,
shortage of raw materials). The latter usually entails a rescheduling problem (Ghaleb et al.,
2020). We want the proposed assignment and sequence of operations to be able to withstand
small variations of processing times without requiring these assignment and sequence to be
updated. It is important to differentiate a sequence, which corresponds to the order in which
the operations are processed on the machines, from a schedule, that also includes the starting
times of operations. A schedule can only be deﬁned when the processing times of operations
are deterministic, as starting times are not exactly known in stochastic scheduling problems
such as ours.
In most research, including our previous research on the problem (Flores-Gómez et al.,
2023), processing times are assumed to be independent random variables. In this paper, as the
uncertainty is machine related, we consider that the status of a machine impacts the processing
times of all operations processed on the machine equivalently, i.e., that the deviation from the
mean processing time is the same for all operations. Our assumption is that, if a machine is not
performing as expected, then it should perform in the same way for all operations. Actually,
we believe that the opposite could be seen as odd, i.e., that the machine performs better
than expected for one operation and worse than expected for another operation. This helps
to signiﬁcantly reduce the number of scenarios required to model the stochastic processing
times in the Monte Carlo simulation used in our solution approach.
3.2 Notion of makespan service level
In Flores-Gómez et al. (2021, 2023), we extend the study on the relevance of the notion of
makespan service level as deﬁned in Dauzère-Pérès et al. (2005, 2013). To better understand
the practicality of the service level in scheduling, it is important to highlight that this notion is
frequently employed in inventory management theory. In scheduling applications, a service
level represents the probability that a criterion is lesser (or greater) than or equal to a given
123

Annals of Operations Research
value. If the used performance measure is the makespan, i.e., the date of completion of
all jobs in the system, then the makespan service level is the probability of ﬁnishing the
execution of all jobs before a given point in time. It is thus possible to consider the service
level of any criterion in deterministic scheduling. Optimizing a service level on a scheduling
criterion leads to sequences of good quality with a high probability and, therefore, optimizes
the robustness of the solution. In the case of the makespan, it makes more sense to maximize
the chance of ﬁnishing all jobs before a given deadline than to minimize the mean completion
time of all jobs, especially from an industrial standpoint when it is important to know how
many hours the operator of a given machine would be needed.
As the processing times are random variables, the makespan of a sequence S is also a
random variable. To study the service level α(S, T ) on the makespan, translates as computing
the probability that the makespan of sequence S is lower than or equal to a given threshold,
denoted by T . The makespan service level is formally deﬁned below:
α(S, T ) = P(Cmax(S, ξ) ≤T ),
where ξ is a multivariate random variable of dimension n (n = |O|).

3.5 Generation of new instances
In our previous work, the case where the variability of the processing times of the operations
are related to the job they belong to, is taken into account (i.e., all operations in the routing
of a job are considered stochastic). However, the uncertainty in the processing times can
be related to the machine the operations are assigned to, and vary from one machine to
another. The level of ﬂexibility has more impact since the machines that are more prone to
variability should typically not be used for the operations with the largest processing times.
To illustrate this case, we generated instances where the processing times of all operations
that can be processed by one machine are considered stochastic. We consider that the random
processing times follow the beta distribution (Marshall & Olkin, 2007), which depends on
four parameters: c and d (limits of the deﬁnition domain), a > 0 (shape) and b > 0 (scale).
The Probability Distribution Function (PDF) of the beta distribution is recalled in Appendix
C.1 in the supplementary material.
As in most of literature, we consider processing times from different operations to be
random independent variables, which is not very realistic, in particular in our case where the
uncertainty is machine related. In reality, if an operation takes more time to be executed in one
machine, there is a high probability that this phenomenon will also happen for all operations
executed in the same machine. Instead of random generation, we envision a scenario as the
representation of an event, which is in our opinion a more realistic representation of reality.
A new scenario generation approach is proposed accordingly in Section 5.3.
The set of scenarios  is generated for every machine m ∈M. This leads to scenarios
associated with operation i such that m ∈Mi. The benchmark instances from Hurink et
al. (1994); Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997) extended in our previous work are described in
Tables 11 and 12 in the supplementary material. These instances, consider different levels of
machine ﬂexibility associated with the average number of machines in the subsets Mi (i.e.,
the average number of machines that can execute operation i). Such instances were extended
by setting the mean values μ = ¯pim, ∀i ∈O j, ∀m ∈Mi, where ¯pim is the original processing
time in the benchmark instances. Unless indicated otherwise, σ = 0.15μ, c = μ −0.2μ and
d = μ + 0.8μ. More details can be found in Section 5.3.
4 Key takeaways on minimizing mathematical expectation
A very common practice when considering stochasticity in scheduling problems is to min-
imize the mathematical expectation of a deterministic criterion, mainly because of its
interesting properties, but also because of the relatively easy adaptation of deterministic
methods to solve the associated stochastic problem. Section 4.1 discusses numerical experi-
mentsshowingthelimitationofminimizingthemathematicalexpectationwhenthemakespan
service level should be maximized. Section 4.2 illustrates how schedules with a minimum
expected makespan can have a very poor makespan service level.

from a mathematical expectation standpoint (mt06-edata machine 3, la15-rdata machine
3, la30-rdata machine 3, la04-vdata machine 4 and la37-vdata machine 12), but also in
all ﬁve occurrences α(Sinit) > 0 for those particular machines.
Optimizing the mathematical expectation of the makespan (which is very common in
stochastic optimization) might lead to a poor job sequence when decision makers are inter-
ested in completing jobs before a given deadline. Hence, it is more relevant to explicitly
consider the makespan service level as a criterion.

5 Solution approach
As already stated, the deterministic FJSP is NP-hard in the strong sense, and exact approaches,
mainly mathematical models solved by a standard solver, can only handle small instances.
As the stochastic FJSP is more difﬁcult, a heuristic approach, based on the tabu search in
Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997) for the deterministic FJSP (recalled in Section 5.1), was
extended for the SFJSP in Flores-Gómez et al. (2021) and improved in Flores-Gómez et
al. (2023) (recalled in Section 5.2). In this paper, we propose a new scenario generation
procedure in Section 5.3 and new bounds on the makespan service level in Sections 5.4
and 5.5 to enhance the tabu search approach, which is summarized in Section 5.6.
5.1 Solving the deterministic flexible job-shop scheduling problem
For the sake of completeness, the main guidelines on the original tabu search from Dauzère-
Pérès and Paulli (1997) are given in this section. This tabu search relies on an extension,
introduced in Dauzère-Péres (1994) and illustrated in Figure 5, of the disjunctive graph
model for the JSP (see Roy and Sussmann (1964)). In a disjunctive graph G = (N, A, E),
N is the set of nodes associated with operations O and two virtual nodes 0 and ∗(∀m ∈
M, p0m = p∗m = 0 and M0 = M∗= ∅), A is the set of conjunctive arcs modeling the
routing of operations, and E is the set of disjunctive arcs modeling the potential assignment
and sequencing of the operations on the machines.
In the disjunctive graph, the weight of an arc is the processing time of the operation from
which the arc starts. The processing times are machine dependent, thus the lengths of two arcs
between two operations might be different if the two operations can be both assigned to two
common machines. In the example illustrated in Figure 5, there are 3 jobs to be processed on
3 machines. Operations 1 and 2 belong to the ﬁrst job, operations 3, 4, and 5 to the second job,
and operations 6, 7, and 8 to the third job. Operations 2, 3, 4, 7, and 8 can only be processed on
a single machine (M3 = M8 = {1}, M4 = {2} and M2 = M7 = {3}), whereas operations
1, 5 and 6 may be processed on two machines (M1 = {2, 3}, M5 = {1, 3} and M6 = {1, 2}).
Let us denote by S the selection of conjunctive arcs (directed arc) , where the disjunctive

Using this disjunctive graph representation, a neighborhood structure is introduced and
exploited to ﬁnd the best solution possible. A neighbor sequence corresponds to moving an
operation i sequenced between operations s and t on the machine assigned to i in the con-
junctive graph of the current solution, between operations s′ and t′. This move is performed
by deleting the conjunctive arcs (s, i) and (i, t), adding the conjunctive arc (s, t), deleting
the conjunctive arc (s′, t′) and adding the conjunctive arcs (s′, i) and (i, t′). Operation i is
reassigned if operations s′ and t′ are assigned to another machine than operations s and t,
otherwise i is “only” re-sequenced on the same machine. To ensure that a cycle is not cre-
ated in the resulting graph before moving an operation, the sufﬁcient feasibility conditions
proposed in Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997) are used in this paper. A lower bound and an
upper bound are also introduced by Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997) to evaluate the quality
of a move.
The length of a path in the conjunctive graph G′ is the sum of the weights of the arcs on
the path. Let L(i, i′) be the longest path between operations i and i′ (= 0 if there is no path),
ri = L(0, i) denotes the release date (head) of i and qi = L(i, ∗) −pia(i) its delivery time
(tail), where operation i has been assigned to machine a(i) ∈Mi. A longest path between
virtual nodes 0 and ∗is called a critical path, and its length r∗= L(0, ∗) is the value of the
makespan of the sequence associated with G′.
5.2 Solving the stochastic flexible job-shop scheduling problem
Solving the SFJSP, in our case, means ﬁnding a feasible sequence with the maximum service
level. Relying on Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997), a tabu search approach to explore a large
number of feasible sequences is presented in Flores-Gómez et al. (2023). An overview of the
proposed approach can be found in Algorithm 2 in the supplementary material.
An initial sequence Sinit referred in Step 1 is obtained by applying a fast constructive
priority-rule-based heuristic and the tabu search of Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli (1997) minimiz-
ing the makespan based on ¯p (processing times from the original instances, see Section 3.5).
An upper bound and a lower bound on the makespan service level are proposed to guide the
tabu search. The sequence in the neighborhood with the largest estimated service level is
selected (Step 9 of Algorithm 2).
To evaluate the service level of a sequence, a set of scenarios  is generated, and an
algorithm based on Monte Carlo sampling-based approximation is implemented in Flores-

service level is denoted α(S, T , ), where  is the generated set of scenarios for computation.
The proposed solution approach is based on the tabu search of Dauzère-Pérès and Paulli
(1997) and includes a Monte Carlo sampling-based procedure to represent and deal with
uncertainties.
5.3 A new scenario generation procedure
In our previous research, the scenarios were generated randomly, and extensive numerical
experiments were conducted to study the impact of critical hyper-parameters such as the
number of scenarios in set  used to estimate the makespan service level, or the deﬁnition of
the beta probability distribution function through parameter σ (see Section 3.5), while ana-

this is considered common practice in stochastic optimization, in reality, a scenario ω ∈
that improves the applicability in a real case scenario should represent a machine state. We
consider that the status of a machine impacts the processing times of all operations processed

it should perform in the same way for all operations. Actually, we believe that the opposite
could be seen as odd, i.e., that the machine performs better than expected for one operation

6 Computational experiments
In the computational experiments, a single machine has stochastic processing times in each
instance. It may seem trivial to just avoid the use of that machine but, by doing so, completing
all operations may take much more time. Thus, the goal is rather to assign to the “stochastic”
machine the operations with a small impact on the makespan service level (i.e., not in the
critical path). In practice, the processing times of operations may vary from one machine to
another due to the different characteristics for similar machines. Therefore, monitoring the
health of the machines is crucial to ensure consistent processing times and to improve the
overall efﬁciency of the manufacturing process.

7 Conclusions and perspectives
In this paper, the makespan service level is maximized in the stochastic ﬂexible job-shop
scheduling problem when the uncertainty is machine related, which makes sense in numerous
real-life applications. This enhances the relevance of the makespan service level, in particular
when there is ﬂexibility in the choice of machines on which operations are processed as in
the FJSP. To solve the optimization problem, a tabu search approach is combined with an
innovativescenariogenerationapproachwhendealingwithmachine-relateduncertainty.New
randomly generated instances are proposed for the stochastic ﬂexible job-shop scheduling
problem. Moreover, new bounds on the makespan service level are proposed and validated
to enhance the tabu search, as well as two different strategies to further guide the exploration
of the neighborhood. Computational experiments are presented and analyzed to validate our
approach.
We see various relevant perspectives to this work. The notion of critical machines as well
as how to identify them in the stochastic FJSP are interesting to investigate, as it could help
to design more efﬁcient methods and, from a practical standpoint, could help to determine
more robust schedules. Also, for a given scheduling horizon T , our problem can be used in
an order acceptance approach, where jobs are only added in the set of jobs to schedule if the
makespan service level is high enough.
Other optimization problems than the one studied in this paper are also being targeted, for
example minimizing threshold T given a minimum makespan service level, as in Beck and
