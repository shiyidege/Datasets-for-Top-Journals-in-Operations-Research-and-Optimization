Abstract
This paper compares the performance of groups of units by composing indicators of corporate
social responsibility (CSR) from an efﬁciency and productivity perspective, applicable across
various industries. From a methodological perspective, our work extends the traditional input-
oriented Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) model in the multiplier form, by ﬁrst adapting it to
accommodate the non-convexities of the production set, and second, by innovatively applying
it to compare indicators across groups of ﬁrms. This adaptation, pioneered in our study,
leverages the framework previously established in the literature to address more complex
scenarios. From an empirical perspective, we contribute by comparing the efﬁciency and
productivity in CSR activities of food and beverage companies across regions of Europe, the
United States and Canada, and Asia–Paciﬁc over the period 2009–2018. The paper reveals
that USA-Canadian ﬁrms tend to perform best with respect to CSR, followed by European
ﬁrms, and that Asian-Paciﬁc ﬁrms achieve the worst efﬁciency and productivity results. The
study also shows that regional catching up in CSR productivity occurred over the analyzed
period.

1 Introduction
Corporate social responsibility (CSR) can be deﬁned as an integration of social, environmen-
tal, ethical, human rights, and consumer concerns into ﬁrms’ business operations and strategy
(European Commission, 2011). The incorporation of CSR allows the managing of the ﬁrm
in the interests of different stakeholders in order to optimize value (Chandler, 2016). Six key
terms underpin the CSR concept: economic, social, ethical, stakeholders, sustainability, and

voluntary (Sarkar and Searcy, 2016). In the particular case of the food and beverage manu-
facturing industry, CSR engagement of ﬁrms is increasing which is related to factors such as
consumer concerns about the food safety, the associations between food and health and the
responsible consumption as well as the excessive generation of food waste and sustainable
food security concerns (European Commission, 2016; Kabadurmus et al., 2022; Wu and Lin,

CSR includes a lot of different activities within its three main dimensions: social, envi-
ronmental and governance CSR. Social CSR is a corporate response to the needs of primary
stakeholders and includes labor relations (such as good workplace conditions or employee
proﬁt sharing), employment diversity (such as employment of minorities or disabled per-
sons), business practice (such as product safety or quality), and community involvement
(such as charitable donations or education programs). Environmental CSR reﬂects ﬁrms’
environmental awareness: involvement in recycling, waste reduction, renewable energies or
biotechnology. Governance dimension of CSR encompasses exceptional practices with board
independence, auditor independence, or shareholders rights. One of the major questions in the
research on CSR is how to aggregate these CSR dimensions into overall indicators of CSR.
This aggregation is undertaken in the literature using different methods such as summing
up the scores on different CSR dimensions, subjectively weighting different dimensions, or
introducing objective weighting through data envelopment analysis (DEA). DEA (Banker
et al., 1984; Charnes et al., 1978) is a non-parametric methodology for the measurement of
efﬁciency of decision making units (DMUs) with multiple inputs and multiple outputs. DEA
measures efﬁciency by determining the distance of each DMU to the frontier enveloping
the data. The DEA-based approach to aggregate indicators is called the beneﬁt-of-the-doubt
(BoD) DEA model (Cherchye et al., 2004, 2007a, 2007b), which measures efﬁciency with
inclusion of sub-indicators as outputs and a single unitary input with the aim of creating a
so-called composite indicator for each analyzed DMU. It presents a certain advantage in that
the weights to aggregate CSR metrics are derived directly from the data. Because of this
characteristic, this study builds upon BoD model to derive composite indicators of CSR.
From an empirical point of view, Bendheim et al. (1998) initiated the use of BoD model
in the aggregation of CSR metrics, assessing the efﬁciency of CSR using data on transporta-
tion, heavy industry, service, primary industry, consumer products and light manufacturing
in the USA. Chen and Delmas (2011) aggregated CSR metrics for sectors of agriculture,
forestry, ﬁshing, mining, manufacturing, ﬁnance, insurance, real estate, services, retail trade,
transportation, wholesale trade, construction and public administration in the USA, while
Belu and Manescu (2013) focused on listed companies from the sectors of basic resources,
industrial goods, consumer search goods, durable experience goods, nondurable experience
goods, industrial services and experience services. Staessens et al. (2019) used BoD for
CSR of Flemish sheltered workshops. Later attempts at aggregation of CSR metrics with the
usage of BoD include the studies by Aparicio and Kapelko (2019) that analyzed CSR data
of US companies in manufacturing, mining, construction, ﬁnance, retail trade, wholesale
trade, services, and transportation sectors, Oliveira et al. (2019) that focused on CSR data
of large mining ﬁrms and Lahouel et al., (2021, 2022) that relied on CSR data of interna-
tional airline companies. When the studies restricted to the food and beverage manufacturing
industry are of concern, the BoD model was used by Engida et al. (2018) and Aparicio et al.
(2020) to analyze CSR of this sector in Europe.1 All of the aforementioned papers are in
general restricted to ﬁrms in the speciﬁc geographical regions and, as far as we are aware,

From a methodological perspective, many different enhancements of the original BoD
model have been introduced in the literature. Examples include the model that accounts for
slacks (Sahoo and Acharya, 2012); the model that takes into account the preference structure
among indicators based on the directional distance function (Fusco, 2015); the directional
model with undesirable indicators (Zanella et al., 2015); the robust conditional model that
takes into account external factors (De Witte and Rogge, 2010, 2011); the robust and non-
compensatory composite indicator within the directional distance function (Vidoli et al.,
2015); the spatial robust BoD model (Fusco et al., 2018); an intra- and inter-group BoD
models (Karagiannis and Karagiannis, 2018); an inter-group BoD model based on Global
Frontier Difference index (Van Puyenbroeck and Rogge, 2020); a translation-invariant direc-
tional distance function model (Aparicio and Kapelko, 2019); a model based on directional
distance function and goal programming (Oliveira et al., 2019); a model aiming to achieve
the least distance to the frontier (Aparicio et al., 2020); a method integrating the spatial
dependence into the robust directional model in the case of undesirable outputs (Fusco et al.,
2020); a model based on the notion of multiple attribute utility theory (Lahouel et al., 2021); a
model based on directional distance function and the Malmquist index (Lahouel et al., 2022);
and a model that combines BoD with multi-directional efﬁciency analysis (Fusco, 2023). A
related line of research focuses on how to aggregate DMU-level BoD-scores to group-level
DMU-scores with contributions by Karagiannis (2017) and Rogge (2018a, 2018b).3 All of
the aforementioned BoD developments assumed convexity of the production set. Convexity
implies that points on the frontier used to evaluate observations are constructed not with actual
observations themselves, but using linear combinations of these actual observations. Specif-
ically, convexity of a production set signiﬁes that if two input vectors x1 and x2 can produce
two output vectors y1 and y2, respectively, then any positive linear combination of these input
vectors αx1 +(1−α)x2 with α ∈⟨0, 1⟩, can produce the output vector αy1 +(1−α)y2. Nev-
ertheless, recent literature has underscored the signiﬁcance of reevaluating the assumption
of convexity in production technology within DEA frameworks (see, for example, Kerstens
et al., 2019, Esteve et al., 2020; Kerstens & Van de Woestyne, 2021; Wilson, 2021; Kerstens
et al., 2022). While assuming convexity has been a longstanding axiom in traditional DEA
models, its implications on efﬁciency scores have been increasingly scrutinized. This assump-
tion can impose a rigid structure that may not accurately capture the complexities inherent in
real-world production processes, potentially leading to biased efﬁciency assessments. In this
paper, we intentionally depart from this conventional assumption and embrace a more ﬂexible

approach that acknowledges the inherent non-convexity of production sets. This departure
from strict convexity assumption not only aligns with the evolving discourse in the research
ﬁeld but also enhances the robustness and applicability of our composite indicator construc-
tion. In particular, we believe that this issue holds relevance in the construction of composite
indicators using frontier techniques, as they often rely on typical microeconomic assumptions
such as convexity. However, within our framework, particularly under the utilization of the
BoD model, the assumption of using inputs to generate outputs is not mandated. Indeed, there
are not explicit inputs, necessitating a cautious approach when assuming certain traditional
production theory postulates since they are related to the standard context with inputs and
outputs. In this sense, we advocate for a conservative stance, prioritizing methodological
robustness over the strict adherence to a multitude of axioms, including convexity which
we relax in our approach. The direct empirical implication of our assumption is the non-
convexifying free disposal hull (FDH) estimator introduced by Deprins et al. (1984) as the
approach to data envelopment. Unfortunately, a construction of the non-convex approach
when implementing the BoD model is a problem that has attracted a limited attention in
the literature so far. The only study on the subject is by García-Romero et al. (2016) that
introduced the FDH output-oriented model without inputs, which however is not exactly the
model that we propose in this paper. In particular, García-Romero et al. (2016) proposal is
an envelopment form of a non-convex output-oriented model and our proposal is a multiplier
form of a non-convex input-oriented model. In fact, the original BoD model is a convex
model but input-oriented.
Moreover, it is often important to assess differences in efﬁciencies between groups of
ﬁrms that could be associated, for example, with regional location of ﬁrms, as in the case of
our empirical analysis. It is well known that efﬁciency measures cannot be directly compared
between these groups due to the existence of different technologies; speciﬁc methodologies
should be used in such contexts, such as for example, the so-called Camanho-Dyson index
(Camanho and Dyson, 2006). Camanho and Dyson (2006) introduced a Malmquist-type
index that derives a relative performance of two or more groups of DMUs, allowing for
direct efﬁciency comparisons of groups. Later, Aparicio et al. (2017) extended the original
Camanho-Dyson index to the context of the panel data or the pseudo-panel data with the
objective of assessing the changes in the performance gap over time. Aparicio and Santín
(2018) introduced the improvement on Aparicio et al. (2017) by providing the solutions to
interpretational and non-circularity problems. The original paper of Camanho and Dyson
(2006), as well its further developments in the technical efﬁciency context (Aparicio and
Santín, 2018; Aparicio et al., 2017, 2021),4 assumed Constant Returns to Scale (CRS) or
Variable Returns to Scale (VRS). However, the Aparicio and Santín (2018) extension of
Camanho-Dyson index has never been used in the context of the non-convex BoD approach.
The present study aims to ﬁll in the aforementioned gaps in the literature. In particular,
the objective of the study is to develop a BoD model with a non-convex structure within the
Aparicio and Santín (2018) index and apply it in the context of CSR. The present study is the
ﬁrst to formulate a BoD model under the non-convexity assumption, based on the original
multiplier formulation of input-oriented BoD model, and use it in the evaluation of groups
of DMUs. The empirical focus is a sample of food and beverage manufacturing ﬁrms in
different world regions over the period 2009–2018. The study’s contributions are twofold.
First, from an empirical point of view, this paper compares efﬁciency and productivity in CSR
activities by food and beverage companies in regions of Europe, the United States and Canada,

this formulation facilitates the identiﬁcation of key variables (indicators) critical for each

each variable directly linked to the value of its corresponding (optimal) weight. Additionally,
our model can accommodate supplementary information from experts about the interrelations
among the dimensions considered in the study, enhancing its applicability and depth (see,
for example, Gulati et al., 2023), when such expert insights are available.

Assuming the non-convex approach, the inefﬁcient Observation D is projected onto Point D’
located on the orthant spanned by B, which consists of the dominating observations. Unit
B functions as a role model for the inefﬁcient Unit D. In DEA, inefﬁcient observations are
projected onto a linear combination of efﬁcient observations. For example, Observation D is
projected to Point D", which is a linear combination of Observations A and C, which in DEA
functions as a role model. Hence, the non-convex approach identiﬁes one role model, which
is an actual observation in contrast to a ﬁctitious convex combination of efﬁcient producers
in DEA. In the non-convex approach, the efﬁciency measure for Unit D is deﬁned by 0D/0D’,
while in DEA it is measured by 0D/0D”. Moreover, in the non-convex approach, Observa-
tions A, B, and C are efﬁcient because they are not dominated by any other observation.
D is inefﬁcient because it produces fewer of both outputs than B, so it is dominated by B.
Observations A and C do not dominate any other observations. Observation B is efﬁcient
and not dominated under the non-convex approach, but imposing convexity it is dominated
by some convex combination of A and C.

3 Dataset
This paper uses CSR data from Sustainalytics, which has been widely used in recent research
(e.g., in Auer, 2016; Kim et al., 2014; Engida et al., 2018). Unlike most other CSR datasets,
Sustainalytics is not limited to a speciﬁc geographic region. Therefore, it suits well one of the
purposesofthisstudyrelatedwiththecomparisonofﬁrmsindifferentregions.5 Sustainalytics
data is compiled from multiple sources such as annual reports, CSR websites, CSR reports and
media sources (Auer, 2016). Companies in the food and beverage manufacturing industry
were chosen based on the sectoral classiﬁcation of Sustainalytics (4-digit level), which is
drawn upon the Global Industry Classiﬁcation Standard (GICS).
The Sustainalytics dataset contains scores on numerous indicators for three dimensions of
CSR: environmental (e.g., water management and renewable energies programs, and sustain-
ability policy), social (e.g., philanthropic activities and diversity programs), and governance
(e.g., board independence, and bribery and corruption policy). The dataset itself provides the
information on two types of scores: raw scores and weighted scores. The raw scores range
from 0 to 100, where 0 is the lowest rating. The weighted scores indicate the importance of
an indicator in a speciﬁc industry and because of this feature we use them in our analysis.
For example, the indicator of fair-trade products is assigned in Sustainalytics higher weights
in the industry under study than in other sectors. The ﬁnal dataset (after removing missing
observations) covers the 2009–2018 period for food and beverage companies from Europe,
the United States and Canada, and the Asia–Paciﬁc region.6 Such classiﬁcation in regions
is consistent with Sustainalytics and previous research (e.g., Auer, 2016). Unfortunately, it
proved impossible to conduct analysis by country (or by a more detailed region), because
there were too few observations for the analyzed industry in some countries to obtain a reli-
able (discriminant) efﬁciency scores. Nevertheless, the analysis by such regions is consistent
with previous research that applied the same dataset (e.g., Auer, 2016; Engida et al., 2018).

4 Results and discussion
In the ﬁrst step of our analysis, we tested whether our sample is suitable for the analysis
under the non-convex approach by applying a test of convexity of Simar and Wilson (2020).
This test consists of an improvement of the initial test proposed by Kneip et al. (2016); the
modiﬁcation is in the form of the application of bootstrap and multiple sample splits, which
remove vagueness regarding the choice of a single split that was assumed in the approach by
Kneip et al. (2016). In this test the null hypothesis of convexity (that is, that both non-convex
and convex estimators are consistent) is checked against an alternative one (that is, that only
non-convex estimator is consistent). Table 2 summarizes the results of this test for our data
per year (with the application of 10 sample splits and 1000 bootstrap replications).7
The results in Table 2 show that our data do not reject convexity in 2010, 2011, 2012 and
2018, but they do reject convexity in 2009, 2013, 2014, 2015, 2016 and 2017. Moreover,
taking into account that not rejecting null hypothesis does not signify that the null hypothesis
is true and that the non-convex estimator is consistent (Wilson, 2021), we believe that our data
provide an evidence of substantial non-convexity. Therefore, we can conclude that the dataset
suits quite well the purpose of the paper to estimate a non-convex model to compute CSR
composite indicators. The results of the test serve also as an indirect comparison between
the standard convex model and the non-convex which this paper concerns, pointing out that
there are differences between both models.

5 Conclusions
Our paper advances the measurement of composite indicators by extending the traditional
input-oriented Beneﬁt-of-the-Doubt (BoD) model to address non-convexities and group
evaluation in the context of production. This methodological enhancement is pivotal for
a more accurate and robust analysis across Decision Making Units (DMUs). Speciﬁcally,
our approach utilizes a multiplier formulation of the BoD model, which is implemented via
linear programming, rather than the more conventional mixed-integer linear programming.
This not only simpliﬁes computational demands but also ensures a closer alignment with the
original BoD model principles. Building on these methodological advancements, our study
then shifts focus to the applied context, examining the efﬁciency and productivity of food
and beverage manufacturing ﬁrms in their CSR activities, which encompass environmental,
social, and governance dimensions. We conduct a global analysis of CSR engagement across
ﬁrms in Europe, the United States and Canada, and the Asia–Paciﬁc over the period from 2009
to 2018. By modifying the original BoD model to include non-convexities and employing
the group evaluation approach, we developed indices to compare CSR performance between
regions and over time. Our results highlight signiﬁcant regional differences in CSR efﬁciency
and productivity, with ﬁrms in the USA and Canada typically achieving better outcomes than
those in Europe and the Asia–Paciﬁc. Additionally, the study observes a ‘catching up’ trend
in CSR productivity among these regions during certain years of the analysis.
The results of this study could be of interest to food and beverage ﬁrms’ managers,
CSR analysts, and policy makers in terms of ﬁrms’ efﬁciency and productivity with regard
to CSR, how these indicators change over time, and how they compare between ﬁrms in
different regions. Because environmental, social and governance dimensions were taken
into account in CSR composite measures, the ﬁndings of the study could serve also as the
indication of possible sustainability improvements. The results could provide ﬁrms with a
better understanding of CSR efﬁciency and productivity, which could encourage ﬁrms to
develop their business operations towards more responsible and sustainable practices.

Future research could apply ranking methods of ﬁrms’ CSR efﬁciency and productivity.
It could also further extend the BoD model developed in the paper considering the imprecise
nature of CSR indicators or the estimation of efﬁciency and productivity indicators for each
dimension of CSR separately. Furthermore, the non-convex BoD model in the multiplier
form developed in this paper is ready to incorporate weight restrictions such as experts’
opinions on the importance of indicator dimensions. Hence, future research study could
focus on extending the BoD model presented in this paper to include decision-making actors’
perspectives on the importance of speciﬁc indicators, provided that such information can be
objectively obtained. Future research directions could also critically address the robustness
of research outcomes, particularly focusing on the inﬂuence of varying sample sizes across
groups. One promising approach involves the use of the order-m model (Daraio and Simar,
2007) within the framework of Free Disposal Hull and Data Envelopment Analysis. This
model adjusts efﬁciency scores to minimize the impact of outliers and heterogeneous sample
sizes, thereby providing more stable and comparable results across different groups or units.
Another innovative direction would be the incorporation of machine learning techniques
such as Random Forest. Known for its robustness against overﬁtting and its ability to handle
large datasets with numerous predictor variables, Random Forest could be utilized to verify
the stability of results obtained from more traditional statistical methods. By employing
this technique, researchers could explore the importance of different predictors and their
interactions in a more nuanced way, potentially uncovering patterns and inﬂuences that
simpler models might miss. Additionally, comparing the outputs of Random Forest with
those from conventional models could provide valuable insights into the consistency and
reliability of ﬁndings across different methodological approaches.
