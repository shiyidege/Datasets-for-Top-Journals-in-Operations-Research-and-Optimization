Abstract
Pandemics, geopolitical strife, and regional tensions in recent years have intensified the 
disruption risks faced by global supply chains. To address the growing need for resilience 
evaluation, a quantitative indicator is proposed for measuring supply chain susceptibility 
to disruptions. A supply chain network is represented as a multistate resilience supply 
chain network (MRSCN), where nodes denote suppliers, assemblers, and markets, and 
arcs represent logistics connections. Due to shared resources, production and transporta­
tion capacities may be limited. System reliability is defined as the probability that the 
MRSCN successfully delivers goods to markets. In contrast to previous studies, an al­
gorithm is proposed that considers uncertainties in both transportation and production 
capacities. A quantitative metric, based on system reliability, is computed to evaluate the 
impact of newly added suppliers during disruption scenarios. By comparing reliability 
before and after supplier integration, the contribution of new suppliers to overall supply 
chain resilience is quantified. The algorithm further enables the identification and analysis 
of supply chain vulnerabilities, offering valuable insights for decision-makers.
Keywords  Supply chain · Resilience · System reliability · Multistate · Disruption
1  Introduction
The globalization of supply chains has emerged as a defining feature of modern business, 
allowing companies to expand their operations across international markets. This devel­
opment enhances efficiency, lowers costs, and increases access to a wider customer base. 
Nonetheless, the growing complexity and geographical dispersion of global supply net­

works have increased their vulnerability to a wide range of disruptions, including natu­
ral disasters, geopolitical tensions, and public health crises. Notable examples include the 
blockage of the Suez Canal, the Great East Japan Earthquake, the U.S.–China trade war, and 
the COVID-19 pandemic. (Bruckler et al., 2024; Meier & Pinto, 2024). For instance, the 
COVID-19 pandemic illustrated how local production shutdowns can trigger widespread 
shortages that propagate through highly specialized, globally connected supply chains, ulti­
mately disrupting entire systems and networks (Xu et al., 2020). The aforementioned sce­
narios have had a profound impact on global supply chains, leading to extensive disruptions, 
logistical bottlenecks, and significant economic repercussions worldwide (Raj et al., 2022). 
The abrupt and widespread nature of such disruptions underscores the inherent vulnerability 
of supply chains and reinforces the urgent need for resilience in responding to unforeseen 
events. In light of increasing uncertainty and the growing frequency of disruptive incidents, 
there is a rising imperative for research aimed at redesigning supply chain networks to be 
both sustainable and resilient (Choi, 2021; Raj et al., 2022).
In the context of supply chain management, resilience refers to the capability of a sup­
ply chain to rapidly adapt to and recover from unexpected events or disruptions, thereby 
restoring normal operations with minimal delay (Bruckler et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2021). 
Supply chain resilience has become a key concept to mitigate disruptions and ensure conti­
nuity of operations in the face of challenges such as pandemics and environmental disasters 
(Bruckler et al., 2024). Given that resilience encompasses multiple stages (before, during, 
and after a disruption), a resilient supply chain must exhibit dynamic capabilities such as 
managing supplier production capacity and logistics transportation capacity across key 
phases, including preparation, adjustment, response, and recovery (Chowdhury & Quad­
dus, 2017; Song et al., 2024). The supplier production capacity is subject to uncertainty 
arising from factors such as material shortages, labor disruptions, and equipment failures. 
Similarly, logistics companies responsible for transportation operate under limited container 
capacity, which can be affected by external factors such as shared usage with other clients 
or the rapid spread of infectious diseases. Due to the inherent uncertainty in both produc­
tion and transportation, these capacities are modeled as random variables within the pro­
posed framework. To enhance the supply chain resilience, Wei et al. (2024) emphasized 
that supplier integration has a significant influence on enhancing supply chain resilience. 
Butt (2025) further investigated the impact of competitive relationships among suppliers 
on supply chain performance. In addition, several researchers have evaluated supply chain 
resilience using approaches such as mixed-integer programming (Abushaega et al., 2024; 
Kaur et al., 2024), multi-objective decision-making (Kumar et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2025), 
and stochastic capacity planning (Tafakkori et al., 2023). A key challenge lies in evaluating 
the supply chain’s ability to fulfill market demand under uncertainties in supplier production 
and transportation capacities, as well as in determining whether resilience can be sustained 
through the integration of additional suppliers. To address this issue, this study proposes a 
quantitative indicator to assess the impact of supplier integration under disruption scenarios. 
This indicator enables the evaluation of the supply chain’s capacity to maintain and restore 
service levels, thereby mitigating the effects of disruptions and enhancing overall resilience.
Under the uncertainty in both production and transportation capacities, network analy­
sis is a practical and widely used approach for evaluating the performance of real-world 
systems (Huang, 2022, 2025; Niu et al., 2023). In the context of supply chains, the entire 
transportation process can be modeled as a network to support decision-makers in under­
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
standing and managing the overall system. In this paper, the activity-on-arrow diagram is 
adopted to represent the supply chain network, where each node corresponds to a supplier, 
assembler, or market, and each arc is represented as a logistics connection. Then, the supply 
chain network is modeled as a multistate resilience supply chain network (MRSCN) to more 
accurately capture the uncertain characteristics of both suppliers and logistics companies.
Typically, system reliability is the probability that the demand can be successfully trans­
ferred to the terminal. From a supply chain management perspective, the probability, called 
system reliability, can be considered a performance index. Previous research has also con­
sidered the damage rate of goods (Lin et al., 2019), budget (Lin et al., 2016; Niu et al., 2022; 
Xu et al., 2021; Yeh et al., 2014), sustainable level (Lin & Lin, 2024b), carbon emission 
(Huang, 2025; Niu et al., 2023), or inventory (Huang, 2019, 2022). To evaluate system 
reliability, several researchers (Huang et al., 2022; Lin & Lin, 2024a; Xu et al., 2021) have 
adopted the concept of a minimal path (MP), defined as an ordered sequence of arcs from the 
source node to the sink node without cycles. Recognizing the critical role of system reliabil­
ity, a system reliability-based quantitative indicator is introduced to evaluate supply chain 
resilience. System reliability is defined as the probability that the MRSCN can successfully 
deliver sufficient goods while meeting demand. This study contributes by quantifying sup­
plier disruptions, which are modeled as probabilities of sustaining normal supply capacity, 
and by analyzing system reliability before and after the integration of new suppliers. This 
allows for a systematic assessment of supply chain resilience. In addition, a quantitative 
indicator is proposed to evaluate the effects of different supplier integration strategies under 
anticipated disruption scenarios, thereby providing a basis for informed decision-making.
The. The proposed MRSCN model is presented in Sect. 2. The system reliability and 
resilience assessment framework, along with the corresponding algorithm, is introduced in 
Sect. 3. A real case example based on the supply chain of a leading electric vehicle manu­

Finally, the conclusions are summarized in Sect. 5.

2.1  Multistate resilience supply chain network
An MRSCN is structured with m arcs and various nodes, including z suppliers, several 
assemblers, and v markets. At the start of each node, goods are provided by the supplier, 
and transportation along the arcs is handled by a contracted logistics company. Let C = {ci| 
1 ≤ i ≤ m + z} be a set of m + z components (suppliers and arcs). For each component ci, where 
i = 1, 2,…, m + 1, m + 2,…, m + z, the available production or transportation capacity can be 
classified into θi discrete states. These states are denoted as 0 = γi1 < γi2 < ··· < γiθi where γiq 
represents the qth possible capacity state, with q ∈ {1, 2,…, θi}. The notation sj is denoted 
as the jth supplier, j = 1, 2,…, z, and bu is denoted as the uth market, u = 1, 2,…, v. For a pair 
of supplier sj and market bu, there exists kj,u MPs connecting them, where j ∈ {1, 2,…, z} 
and u ∈ {1, 2,…, v}. Let ωj,u,p be the pth MP linking sj and bu for j = 1, 2,…, z, u = 1, 2,…, v, 
p = 1, 2,…, kj,u. To evaluate the system reliability of MRSCN, this paper adheres further to 
the following assumptions:
1. The flow in the MRSCN must satisfy the flow-conservation law (Ford & Fulkerson, 
2015).
2. The flow in the MRSCN is an integer value.
3. The transportation (production) capacities of different logistics companies (suppliers) 
in the MRSCN both before and during disruptions are statistically independent.
4. All existing and newly added suppliers in the MRSCN are assumed to be immediately 
available for participation in supply chain operations.
Vectors are computed according to the following rules:
1. H ≤ G (h1, h2,…, hm) ≤ (g1, g2,…, gm): hi ≤ gi for each i.
2. H < G (h1, h2,…, hm) < (g1, g2,…, gm): H ≤ G and hi < gi at least one i.

2.3  Disruption modeling
To quantify the extent of the disruption, let the value β represent the probability of sustain 
the same production capacity. The corresponding values β may vary due to differences in 
suppliers’ geographical locations or the extent to which they are affected by unforeseen 
events. In an MRSCN, according to Assumption 3, the production capacity during a disrup­
tion is calculated by multiplying the historical production capacity probability by β, the 
probability of being able to operate normally under disruption. Let PrB{xi} and PrD{xi} 
denote the probability of the production capacity xi for i = m + 1, 2,…, m + z, before and

4  Illustration through a real case
In this section, a real case of electric vehicle manufacturer is studied to apply the proposed 
model and algorithm to demonstrate its practicality. Faced with disruptions stemming from 
the COVID-19 pandemic, geopolitical instability, and export limitations, which together 
caused critical supply chain bottlenecks, the manufacturer opted to evaluate supply chain 
resilience and estimate the probability of meeting market demand.
4.1  Case description
In 2022 and 2023, a leading electric vehicle manufacturer encountered serious difficulties 
in securing lithium—a key material for EV batteries—due to reliance on international sup­
pliers in South America and Australia. Global logistics disruptions, geopolitical tensions, 
and export restrictions created severe supply chain bottlenecks. In response, the company 
turned to U.S.-based suppliers to reduce international dependency. However, domestic sup­
pliers faced their own challenges, such as limited capacity and upstream material shortages, 
resulting in further delays, increased costs, and difficulty meeting rising market demand.
The estimated monthly demand for EV battery materials is 200 tons in Europe and 300 
tons in North America. Defining one unit as 100 tons, this corresponds to a demand vector 
D = (2, 3). The critical EV battery material is shipped using Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit 
(TEU) containers, each with a capacity of 20 tons. Therefore, transporting one unit (100 
tons) requires around 5 TEUs. In addition, each supplier and contracted logistics company 
is associated with multiple capacity levels, with corresponding probability distributions 
derived from long-term observations. An overview of the planned supply chain is provided 
in Fig. 2. The expected new supplier and their associated transport paths are illustrated using 
dotted lines. The data on production and transportation capacities is presented in Tables 1.

4.3  Discussions
We further evaluate the impact of probability β representing the likelihood of sustaining 
production capacity on the network reliability. The impact of varying β values from 0.1 to 1 
in increments of 0.1 on system reliability and resilience is presented in Table 4.
It is shown that the addition of a stable new supplier leads to significant improvements 
in both reliability and resilience, particularly when β is low. This demonstrates the value of 
proactive sourcing strategies under high disruption risk. As β increases, marginal improve­
ments diminish, indicating that excessive redundancy may reduce efficiency when the sup­
ply base is already stable. Resilience is measured as the difference in system reliability 
before and after the addition of a new supplier, and is used as a quantitative indicator for 
assessing supply chain strategies. Even under high β, system reliability is improved with the 
presence of a backup supplier, highlighting its strategic role in risk mitigation and supplier 
selection.
Although the newly added supplier is affected by the disruption in this case, in real-world 
scenarios, if the supplier is located outside the disruption zone, has diversified sources of 
supply, or operates in a stable economic and regulatory environment, it may still maintain 
baseline supply stability and therefore be considered unaffected by the disruption. Under

5  Conclusions
Recent occurrences of pandemics, geopolitical tensions, and regional conflicts have consid­
erably heightened the risk of disruptions within global supply chains. The resilience of the 
supply chain in the presence of disruptions has been assessed based on system reliability. 
Through the representation of both production and transportation capacities as multistate 
variables, the supply chain network was analyzed using a multistate resilience supply chain 
network incorporating minimal component capacity vectors and the recursive sum of dis­
joint product algorithm. To measure the improvement in performance following disruption 
mitigation strategies, a resilience assessment was developed based on changes in system 
reliability before and after the addition of new suppliers. In the real case, the system reliabil­
ity is calculated as Roriginal
(2,3)
= 0.5677 without the inclusion of new supplier, and increases 
to Rnew
(2,3) = 0.7233 with the addition of newly added supplier. The newly added supplier 
has contributed to a 15% improvement in system reliability. By examining the observed 
improvement, the role of the new supplier in enhancing and maintaining the resilience of the 
supply chain can be evaluated. Insights into supply chain vulnerabilities and improvement 
strategies are made available to decision-makers through the application of the proposed 
model and algorithm. The contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
I. 
An algorithm is proposed to assess resilience performance of supply chain which is 
involved the uncertainty of transportation capacity and production capacity.
II. A quantitative indicator, referred to as system reliability, is calculated using the RSDP 
method based on all MCCVs, allowing decision-makers to evaluate supply chain per­
formance under uncertainties in transportation and production capacities.
III. Based on the system reliability and the difference in the probability β of sustaining 
production capacity during disruption, the resilience performance Ω is offered to deci­
sion-makers for evaluating the impact of additional suppliers and facilitating supplier 
integration.
The proposed algorithm is developed based on the fundamental concept of minimal paths. 
Although the minimal cut concept is commonly applied in network analysis and provides a 
potential alternative, its application in supply chain settings, especially for evaluating sup­
plier impact and considering budget constraints, has not been sufficiently examined in the 
existing literature. Future research is encouraged to explore this direction to fill the gap in 
system reliability assessment.
