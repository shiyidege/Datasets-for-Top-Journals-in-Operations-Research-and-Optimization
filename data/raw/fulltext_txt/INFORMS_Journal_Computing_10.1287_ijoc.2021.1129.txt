Abstract
Fluence map optimization for intensity-modulated radiation therapy planning can be formulated as
a large-scale inverse problem with competing objectives and constraints associated with the tumors and
organs-at-risk. Unfortunately, clinically relevant doseвАУvolume constraints are nonconvex, so standard
algorithms for convex problems cannot be directly applied. While prior work focused on convex approxi-
mations for these constraints, we propose a novel relaxation approach to handle nonconvex doseвАУvolume
constraints. We develop eпђГcient, provably convergent algorithms based on partial minimization, and
show how to adapt them to handle maximum-dose constraints and infeasible problems.
We demon-
strate our approach using the CORT dataset, and show that it is easily adaptable to radiation treatment
planning with doseвАУvolume constraints for multiple tumors and organs-at-risk.

Introduction
Cancer is one of the leading causes of death worldwide, with approximately 38.4% of the population
expected to develop cancer at some point in their lives [1]. Many cancer therapies target the weakened
DNA damage response of cancer cells; in particular, radiotherapy uses high-energy ionizing radiation to
damage cancer cell DNA [2, 3]. Unfortunately, this radiation also damages the healthy cells in its path,
so the main goal of radiation treatment planning is to maximize the diпђАerential between damage to the
tumor and to nearby healthy tissue. The most common form of radiotherapy is external beam radiation
therapy (EBRT), where photon beams generated by a linear accelerator are directed at a patient from
a rotating gantry [4]. By surrounding a tumor with beams from multiple angles, physicians can design
treatment plans that target tumors with a variety of geometries while avoiding a large dose to critical
structures.
One way to limit damage to healthy tissue is to require that treatment plans satisfy various constraints
on the amount of radiation delivered to organs-at-risk (OARs). For example, it is often clinically relevant
to impose maximum-dose constraints on a fraction of an organ, known as a doseвАУvolume constraint. Un-
fortunately, these constraints are nonconvex, so convex formulations and algorithms cannot be directly
applied to the treatment planning problem. We propose a novel approach to handle doseвАУvolume con-
straints for intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) while preserving their nonconvex structure, as
opposed to previous eпђАorts that focused on convex approximations. The proposed method is amenable
to eпђГcient algorithms based on partial minimization and naturally adapts to handle maximum-dose con-
straints and cases of infeasiblity. We demonstrate our approach using the CORT dataset [5, 6], and we
show that it easily adapts to radiation treatment planning with doseвАУvolume constraints for multiple
tumors and OARs. To start oпђА, we give a brief overview of EBRT, including IMRT, and the speciпђБc
challenge posed by doseвАУvolume constraints.

1.1
External beam radiation therapy
Conventional EBRT began soon after the discovery of X-rays in 1895 and was signiпђБcantly impacted by
technological innovations such as advanced imaging and multileaf collimators (MLC) [7]. SpeciпђБcally,
the invention of computed tomography (CT) in the 1970s enabled accurate three-dimensional mapping
of a patientвАЩs internal anatomy, while the introduction of the MLC to radiotherapy in the 1990s provided
more control over beam shape and пђВuence. For example, three-dimensional conformal radiation therapy
(3D-CRT) uses the sliding leaves of the MLC to shape beam outlines to match tumor geometries, reducing
radiation exposure to healthy tissue, while IMRT uses MLCs to not only shape beam outlines, but also
modulate beam пђВuence, giving additional control over the patient dose distribution [8].
In the early history of radiotherapy, treatment plans were created using a trial-and-error approach
called forward planning, where physicians would calculate dose distributions for diпђАerent sets of beam
parameters until an acceptable treatment plan was found. However, modulating beam пђВuence for IMRT
involves discretizing each beam into subunits, called beamlets or bixels, which dramatically increases the
number of variables under consideration, rendering forward planning infeasible [9, 10]. In the 1980s, the
idea of inverse planning for IMRT was introduced by Brahme [11, 12], where mathematical optimization
was used to calculate beam parameters to deliver a speciпђБed dose distribution.
Within radiotherapy inverse planning, there are a variety of diпђАerent paradigms regarding dose ob-
jectives, mathematical models, and optimization methods. For instance, while our proposed model and
many others seek to optimize dose distributions deпђБned in terms of physical criteria such as dose and
volume [9, 13], it is also possible to consider biological criteria such as tumor control probability (TCP)
and normal-tissue complication probability (NTCP) [14], equivalent uniform dose (EUD) [15, 16, 17],
and biologically eпђАective dose (BED) [18, 19]. Additionally, many early approaches to inverse planning
used analytical direct methods inspired by the similarities between IMRT planning and CT image recon-
struction [20, 21, 22], but the trend has since moved towards approximate iterative methods [23, 24, 25].
Amongst iterative methods, the presence of nonconvexity within many IMRT models has fueled
the debate between stochastic methods such as simulated annealing [26, 27, 28] and genetic algorithms
[29, 30], which can escape local minima but tend to have higher computational costs, versus gradient-based
methods using techniques such as NewtonвАЩs method [21, 31], L-BFGS [32, 33], and conjugate gradient
methods [34, 35], which tend to be faster but can get trapped in local minima. Our method uses a
projected gradient approach to handle issues related to nonconvexity, which we discuss in Section 1.3.
For a more detailed history of radiotherapy and IMRT, see [7, 8, 9, 10, 13, 36].
1.2
Intensity-modulated radiation therapy
In current practice, the patient undergoes imaging (e.g., CT) before IMRT treatment, producing a three-
dimensional model of their internal anatomy. This model is discretized into volume units (voxels) and
labeled by structure. Next, the physician speciпђБes the prescription, often in terms of a uniform target
to the tumor with various constraints on critical structures. The goal of the treatment planner is then
to determine the number of beams, beam angles, and beamlet intensities that will deliver the prescribed
dose of radiation to the tumor while keeping doses to healthy tissue low. Inverse planning for IMRT
typically involves three steps [10]:
1. Beam angles: Determine the number of beams and their orientations.
2. Fluence map: Calculate beamlet intensities to deliver the desired dose distribution to the patient.
3. Segmentation: Design MLC sequences that achieve the optimized пђВuence map.
These steps require solving optimization problems that can be approached separately or in combination
[37, 38, 39, 40]. In this paper, we focus on the пђВuence map optimization (FMO) problem of assigning
beamlet intensities for a given set of beam angles.
The FMO problem has been approached using a number of diпђАerent mathematical frameworks in-
cluding linear programming [41, 42, 43], piecewise linearвАУquadratic models [44, 45, 46], quadratic pro-
gramming [31, 34, 47], and other nonlinear programming models [14, 15, 25, 48]. Using multi-objective
optimization, many studies have generated solutions along the Pareto frontier to explore the trade-oпђА
between competing tumor and healthy tissue objectives and constraints [17, 32, 49]. Other approaches
include formulating the FMO problem as a dynamical system [50] or eigenvalue problem [51], using
a database of reference doseвАУvolume histograms to guide optimization [52], and clustering beams and
voxels to handle large-scale problems [53]. We develop a new approach that is well adapted to handle
nonconvex doseвАУvolume constraints, in addition to convex maximum-dose and mean-dose constraints.
We пђБrst explain all three constraint types.

Radiation dose is measured in gray (Gy), where one Gy is deпђБned as the absorption of one joule of
energy per kilogram of matter. Although lower doses are always better for healthy tissue, tolerable doses
for many tissues are known empirically and depend on the risk level that a patient and physician are
willing to accept. In practice, tolerable doses for diпђАerent tissues are formulated using the following three
types of constraints:
вАҐ Maximum dose: No voxel in the organ receives more than dmax Gy.
вАҐ Mean dose: Average radiation per voxel does not exceed dmean Gy.
вАҐ DoseвАУvolume: At most p% of the organ volume receives more than ddv Gy.
Serial structures such as the spinal cord and brainstem lose functionality if any of their subvolumes are
damaged, so maximum-dose constraints are a good indicator of tissue damage. Maximum-dose and mean-
dose constraints deпђБne a convex feasible region for the decision variables. The associated optimization
problems can be solved at scale; for example, inverse planning with linear inequality constraints can be
formulated as a convex quadratic program, with state-of-the-art approaches and commercial implemen-
tations readily available (see e.g., [54]). On the other hand, parallel structures such as the liver and lungs
are composed of semi-independent units. In this case, a fraction of the parallel organ can be damaged
without loss of functionality, so doseвАУvolume constraints are appropriate [34]. Assigning maximum-dose
constraints on partial volumes to achieve the desired doseвАУvolume criteria was пђБrst introduced by Langer
and Leong in 1987 [41] and brought to the attention of the mathematical community by Shepard et al.
in 1999 [36].
1.3
Challenge of doseвАУvolume constraints
In contrast to maximum-dose and mean-dose constraints, doseвАУvolume constraints are combinatorial,
since the choice of the p% of organ voxels that may receive more than ddv Gy is left to the planner.
In early IMRT research, using only a small number of beamlets and voxels, it was possible to solve the
treatment planning problem for all possible voxel combinations satisfying a doseвАУvolume constraint and
then choose the best resulting plan [41]. This approach becomes infeasible as the problem size increases
to clinically relevant situations: for an organ with n voxels and a doseвАУvolume constraint imposed on m
of these voxels, there are n!/(n вИТm)!m! possible voxel combinations that would satisfy the constraint [34].

Fortunately, many investigations into the properties of local minima induced by nonconvex doseвАУvolume
constraints have concluded that, given a good initialization, it is possible to пђБnd local minima whose
dose distributions are close to the global minimum using fast gradient methods [9, 55, 83], so the use of
stochastic methods may not be warranted [13, 14, 84]. In this case, the emphasis in IMRT is often on
пђБnding a good local minimum that satisпђБes clinical objectives rather than пђБnding the global minimum
[13, 85].
1.4
Contributions
In this paper, we develop a novel approach to handle doseвАУvolume constraints in the radiation treatment
planning problem. In contrast to prior eпђАorts, which focused on convex approximations, our formulation
is nonconvex, yet can be eпђГciently solved using continuous optimization techniques. We start with the
idealized problem, formulated using objectives for the tumor and constraints for the OARs, and extend it
to a пђВexible formulation that allows re-weighting strategies to balance infeasible solutions (with respect
to the OARs) against delivering a suпђГcient amount of radiation to the tumor. To solve this formulation,
we develop a customized algorithm that combines quadratic programming with fast nonlinear operators,
and show that it is guaranteed to converge to a stationary point of the proposed model. The resulting
approach is scalable, handles multiple objectives on tumors and OARs, and returns actionable treatment
plans on patient-scale datasets.
The paper proceeds as follows. In Section 2 we formulate radiation treatment planning as a nonconvex
optimization problem over the пђВuence map. In Section 3 we develop a custom optimization algorithm
and provide a convergence analysis. In Section 4 we present multiple radiation treatment use cases with
data from an anonymized cancer patient dataset, demonstrating the feasibility of our approach. Finally,
in Section 5 we summarize the results and discuss future avenues for research.

Numerical Results
Our examples are performed on data from an anonymized prostate cancer patient included in the CORT
dataset [5], available in the GigaScience repository, GigaDB [6]. The dataset includes beamlet-to-voxel
maps for beam angles ranging from 0вЧ¶to 358вЧ¶in increments of 2вЧ¶, PTVs in the prostate and lymph
nodes, and OARs including the rectum, bladder, femoral heads, and unspeciпђБed tissue (Figure 4). In the
CORT dataset, beamlet intensities are measured in monitor units (MU), where 100 MU delivers a dose
of 1 Gy in 10 cm depth in water in the center of a 10 cm √Ч 10 cm radiation пђБeld [5].
For a пђБxed set of beams, we consider the problem of treating tumors with doseвАУvolume constraints of
increasing diпђГculty on various combinations of critical structures. In Section 4.1, we look at an example
with one PTV and one OAR, and show how the proposed approach works for a single doseвАУvolume
constraint on the OAR. In Section 4.2, we increase the complexity of the previous example by imposing
multiple doseвАУvolume constraints on the same PTV and OAR. In Section 4.3, we show how the approach
handles multiple PTVs and OARs. Finally, in Section 4.4, we compare our approach to other published
methods. For all of our examples, we use seven equally spaced beams with angles ranging from 0вЧ¶to
312вЧ¶in increments of 52вЧ¶. We set our regularization parameter at ќї = 10вИТ8, our stopping tolerance
at ѕµ = 10вИТ3, and for simplicity we let ќ±i = ќ±j = 1 for all i вИИI and all j вИИJ dv, unless otherwise
stated. Whenever there is overlap between PTVs and OARs, the voxels in common are assigned to the
PTV. However, this choice is not required for our model, and a single voxel may be assigned to multiple
structures if desired.
All examples were run on a computer with a 2.9 GHz dual-core Intel Core i5
processor with 8 GB RAM.
We use the cumulative doseвАУvolume histogram as a way of evaluating the quality of the resulting
treatment plans. In a doseвАУvolume histogram, each point represents the percent of the organ volume
that receives at least a particular dose. For example, we aim to deliver a uniform dose of 81 Gy to the
prostate tumor, corresponding to the doseвАУvolume histogram on the left panel of Figure 5. In this case,
100% of the organ volume receives exactly 81 Gy. As we cannot deliver a perfectly uniform dose, our
goal is to пђБnd a treatment plan that is as close as possible to this idealized dose proпђБle. For instance,
in the right panel of Figure 5 we see the doseвАУvolume histogram of the solution to (27) for one prostate
tumor with regularization parameter ќї = 10вИТ8. We use the beamlet intensities from this solution as our
initialization for our examples in Sections 4.1, 4.2, and 4.4.1.
