Abstract
This study introduces a novel Two-Stage Total Partial Productivity Data Envelopment Analy-
sis (2S-TPP-DEA) model to evaluate the efﬁciency of the Chinese banking sector. Traditional
DEA and network DEA models, while useful for analyzing multi-stage processes, often
oversimplify the relationships between inputs, intermediate outputs, and ﬁnal outputs by
aggregating data and assuming ﬁxed functional relationships. Our proposed 2S-TPP-DEA
model addresses these limitations by evaluating how effectively each input contributes to
outputs at different stages of banking operations, rather than simply aggregating them. This
approach offers a clearer, more realistic picture of efﬁciency—especially across banks that
differ in size, structure, and market conditions. In addition, the model incorporates key con-
textual factors such as market structure, input prices, and ﬁnancial distress, offering a more
comprehensive and realistic understanding of efﬁciency in diverse banking environments.
The ﬁndings highlight signiﬁcant differences in efﬁciency across ownership structures, with
state-owned banks beneﬁting from government support, while joint-stock banks show higher
responsiveness to market forces. These insights provide valuable guidance for policymak-
ers and banking managers in optimizing strategies to improve efﬁciency and sustain growth
in the face of economic pressures. By ﬁlling a gap in existing methodologies, this research
contributes to the ongoing discourse on efﬁciency measurement in complex ﬁnancial systems.

1 Introduction
The quest for operational efﬁciency in the banking sector is driven by an ever-evolving market
landscape characterized by stringent regulatory demands, ﬁerce competition, and economic
ﬂuctuations (Fungáˇcová et al., 2013; Luo et al., 2024). This is particularly evident in the
Chinese banking industry, which plays a critical role within the global ﬁnancial system (Lee
& Chih, 2013; Luo et al., 2011; Tan et al., 2021a). Traditional models for assessing bank-
ing efﬁciency, such as the production and intermediation approaches, have provided valuable
insights but are often inadequate when applied to the complexities of modern ﬁnancial ecosys-
tems, especially in the context of multi-stage processes and varying environmental conditions
(Fujii et al., 2014; Quaranta et al., 2018).
Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) and its extensions, including network DEA, have
become popular tools for measuring the efﬁciency of decision-making units (DMUs), such
as banks (Staub et al., 2010; Tan & Floros, 2019). These models have evolved to account for
multi-stage processes, recognizing that banking operations consist of both production (input
to intermediary outputs) and intermediation (intermediary outputs to ﬁnal outputs) stages.
Network DEA models, in particular, have improved upon traditional DEA by breaking down
complexsystemsintostagesandallowingformoredetailedanalysisofintermediateprocesses
(Tone and Tsutsui, 2009).
Despite these advances, several key limitations remain. First, most existing models assume
that the relationships between inputs, intermediates, and outputs are linear or follow prede-
ﬁned patterns, which may not reﬂect the true complexity of banking processes. Second,
traditional and network DEA models typically rely on summing inputs and outputs in each
stage, potentially oversimplifying how resources are utilized and transformed within the
system. This can obscure important insights into how speciﬁc inputs affect outputs across
various stages of the banking process. Furthermore, many of these models fail to adequately
account for external contextual factors such as market structure, input prices, and ﬁnancial
distress, which can signiﬁcantly inﬂuence banking operations and efﬁciency (Antunes et al.,
2022; Tan & Floros, 2019).
To address these gaps, this study proposes a novel 2S-TPP-DEA model. This model com-
plements and extends existing DEA methodologies by addressing several limitations in the
current literature. First, the 2S-TPP-DEA model reduces the bias introduced by extreme input
or output values, which can distort productivity assessments in traditional models. By focus-
ing on partial productivity at each stage, this approach enables a more precise understanding
of how individual inputs contribute to both intermediate and ﬁnal outputs. Second, the model
introduces ﬂexibility in the analysis of multi-stage processes, moving beyond the restrictive
functional assumptions commonly found in network DEA models. Third, it integrates con-
textual factors that are often overlooked but are crucial for understanding the complexities of
modern banking, particularly in heterogeneous sectors like Chinese banking. By ﬁlling these
gaps in the literature, the 2S-TPP-DEA model offers a more robust framework for analyzing
banking efﬁciency, making it particularly suited to the diverse and complex operations of
Chinese banks.

Rather than replacing DEA, 2S-TPP reﬁnes efﬁciency measurement by (i) changing
the aggregation operator from a ratio-of-weighted-sums to a sum-of-normalized ratios, (ii)
decomposing efﬁciency into production (inputs →intermediates) and intermediation (inter-
mediates →outputs) partial productivities with explicit weights, and (iii) adding a feasibility
guard that stabilizes scores in heterogeneous samples. This yields stage-level diagnostics
that standard one-shot DEA does not provide without network structures. We therefore posi-
tion 2S-TPP as a pragmatic, stage-aware complement to DEA. Hence, this study not only
enhances theoretical understanding but also provides practical insights for policymakers and
banking managers who seek to improve operational strategies in the face of evolving eco-
nomic pressures.
Moreover, this study pioneers a comprehensive analysis of how contextual factors such
as market structure (Bhatia et al., 2018; O’Connell, 2023), price of inputs (Hassanasab &
Margaritis, 2017), and ﬁnancial distress (Nanda & Nicholas, 2014) impact on banking partial
productivities through both production and intermediation lenses. Such an approach is crucial
for understanding the dual impact of operational practices and market dynamics on bank
performance in China, which is particularly relevant in the distinct ownership arrangements
(state-owned, foreign, and joint-stock) that currently exists (Tan, 2020).
More speciﬁcally, China’s banking sector is not a homogenous entity. It boasts a diverse
mix of banks, each with its own competitive advantages and disadvantages. State-owned
banks (SOBs) hold a dominant position, wielding a signiﬁcant market share and beneﬁting
from implicit government guarantees. However, their focus on policy objectives can some-
times overshadow purely commercial interests, potentially impacting efﬁciency. Joint-Stock
Banks, commercially oriented, are increasingly challenging the SOBs. They are generally
more ﬂexible and responsive to market forces but might face funding constraints compared
to their state-owned counterparts. Foreign banks bring international expertise and best prac-
tices, contributing to competition and innovation. However, their limited branch networks
and focus on high-end clientele can restrict their overall impact on the market. City Com-
mercial Banks (CCBs) cater to local businesses and households, offering valuable services
to speciﬁc communities. While their geographic limitations can hinder their efﬁciency com-
pared to nationwide players, they provide crucial services within their regions. Rural banks,
focused on serving the agricultural sector, often face challenges with limited resources and
infrastructure. This diversity in market structure creates a complex competitive landscape.
While competition can drive efﬁciency gains, it can also lead to price wars and riskier lending
practices, potentially impacting ﬁnancial stability.
Some Chinese banks are troubled by worries of ﬁnancial difﬁculty. During times of quick
credit growth, banks may face ﬁnancial pressure due to non-performing loans affecting their
capital and proﬁtability. This may appear in various forms: (1) Reduced Use of Resources:
Banks experiencing ﬁnancial difﬁculties may need to limit lending and cut operating costs.
Although it may enhance short-term cost management, it may restrict growth chances too;
(2) Concentrate on Avoiding Risk: A struggling bank may become excessively risk-averse,
choosing loan security over potentially lucrative endeavors. The cautious method can result
in missed chances and decreased efﬁciency; (3) Ineffective distribution of resources: During
crises, banks may focus on immediate survival rather than long-term investments in infras-
tructure and technology. This could hinder improvements in efﬁciency in the long term. It is
important to comprehend the impact of ﬁnancial distress on resource allocation and utilization
in order to assess bank performance effectively.
The cost of key inputs, such as labor, funds, and capital, signiﬁcantly impacts a bank’s
efﬁciency. In China, we see some unique scenarios: (1) Labor Costs: Salaries for skilled
ﬁnancial professionals are rising as China’s economy matures. This can put pressure on

banks to optimize their workforce and utilize technology effectively to improve efﬁciency;
(2) Cost of Capital: Access to capital can vary depending on a bank’s ownership structure
and risk proﬁle. State-owned banks might enjoy lower funding costs compared to private
banks, potentially inﬂuencing their operational strategies; (3) The price of funds, particularly
deposits, plays a crucial role in determining a bank’s efﬁciency. Higher costs of deposits can
squeeze proﬁt margins, compelling banks to optimize their operations and reduce inefﬁcien-
cies. Conversely, lower deposit costs can enhance a bank’s ability to allocate resources more
effectively, potentially improving overall efﬁciency and competitiveness. This cost-efﬁciency
trade-off requires careful consideration when evaluating bank performance. The interplay
between these three factors—market structure, ﬁnancial distress, and input prices—creates a
dynamic and complex landscape for Chinese banks. Traditional efﬁciency analysis methods
might struggle to capture the nuances of this environment. The ﬁndings show that while most
banks exhibit moderate productivity, there is a signiﬁcant variation. SOBs tend to have higher
productivity due to government support and larger capital bases. Investment in labor force
and market concentration are also positively associated with productivity. Financial distress,
however, has a negative impact on productivity, especially for joint-stock banks.

2 Literature review
The efﬁciency of the Chinese banking sector, a crucial component of the global ﬁnancial sys-
tem, has been the focus of extensive research. Various methodologies have been employed to
assess bank performance and determine the factors that inﬂuence efﬁciency. Among these,
Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) has emerged as a leading approach for efﬁciency mea-
surement, offering ﬂexibility in dealing with multiple inputs and outputs in decision-making
units (DMUs).
Recent years have witnessed signiﬁcant methodological advancements in Data Envelop-
ment Analysis (DEA) applied to the banking sector, particularly in capturing multi-stage,
dynamic, and behavioural dimensions of bank performance. Fukuyama and Tan (2020)
introduced a three-stage network DEA framework that decomposes overall efﬁciency into
input, stability, and output components while explicitly incorporating market power and loan
loss provisions as indicators of ﬁnancial stability. One similar study was also conducted by
Fukuyama and Tan (2024) to propose a network DEA model to investigate the dual role
of loan loss reserves in the banking production process. Building on this line of research,
Fukuyama and Tan (2021) and Fukuyama and Tan (2022a, 2022b) extended DEA to assess the
interaction between bank efﬁciency, innovation, and corporate social responsibility (CSR),
introducing novel constructs such as strategic disposability to address undesirable inputs and
outputs in network systems. Further, Fukuyama et al. (2023) proposed a dynamic network
DEA with a sequential and behavioural-causal structure, advancing the temporal and causal
interpretation of efﬁciency in the Chinese banking industry. The incorporation of causal
modeling in DEA and its application in the Chinese banking industry were also provided by
Fukuyama et al. (2024).
The contrubutions from the literature on bank efﬁciency analysis are also reﬂected by the
proposal of innovative hybrid DEA models as well as the inclusion of novel second-stage
123

Annals of Operations Research
analysis. For instance, Antunes et al., (2022, 2024a) combine DEA with robust endogenous
neural networks and multilayer perceptron-stochastic structural relationship programming
to explore the determinants of efﬁciency in Chinese banks. Antunes et al. (2024b) further
propose a Trigonometric Envelopment Analysis for Ideal Solutions (TEA-IS), which merges
the strengths of DEA and Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) in estimating Chinese
bank performance. Tan et al. (2021b) introduce a network DEA framework, combining it
with integrated Multi-Layer Perceptron/Hidden Markov model to evaluate the relationship
between competition and efﬁciency in Chinese banks.
While these applications of DEA offer valuable insights into bank efﬁciency, several
limitations have been noted in the literature. One signiﬁcant limitation is DEA’s sensitivity
to the selection of inputs and outputs. The efﬁciency scores produced by DEA can vary
considerably depending on which variables are chosen, leading to potentially biased results
(Piranetal.,2021).Moreover,DEAassumesthatallinputsandoutputsarecrisp,deterministic
values, which may not reﬂect the uncertainties inherent in real-world scenarios, particularly
in complex environments like the banking sector (Mirhedayatian et al., 2014). Another issue
is the computational burden that DEA faces with high-dimensional data. As the number of
inputs and outputs increases, solving multiple linear programming problems for each DMU
becomes more resource-intensive and time-consuming (Lau, 2013), limiting the practicality
of DEA for large datasets.
Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) methods, often used alongside DEA, also
present their own set of challenges. While MCDM is useful for evaluating complex decisions
with multiple criteria, it typically requires substantial amounts of data and computational
resources, making it resource-intensive (Dohale et al., 2021). Another drawback is the sub-
jectivity involved in weighting criteria. The importance assigned to different factors can
vary signiﬁcantly among decision-makers, leading to biases that affect the results (Barak
& Javanmard, 2020). Additionally, MCDM methods must balance conﬂicting criteria, often
settling for compromise solutions that may not fully satisfy any single criterion, resulting in
suboptimal outcomes (Choudhary et al., 2022).
The limitations of both DEA and MCDM, as outlined above, underscore the need for new
models that can better capture the complexities of banking operations. This study addresses
these gaps by proposing the 2S-TPP-DEA model, which offers several advantages over tra-
ditional DEA and MCDM approaches. The 2S-TPP-DEA model conceptualizes total partial
productivity as a weighted sum of ratios, instead of relying on the simple ratio of sums com-
monly used in traditional DEA models. This methodological shift allows for a more granular
analysis of each stage in the banking process, providing a deeper understanding of how inputs
are converted to intermediate outputs and ultimately to ﬁnal outputs. By normalizing input
values through the use of averages, the 2S-TPP-DEA model mitigates the impact of outliers
and extreme values, which can distort productivity assessments in traditional DEA models.
This approach is particularly beneﬁcial in the Chinese banking context, where banks dif-
fer signiﬁcantly in size, ownership structures, and market focus. For instance, state-owned
banks tend to have larger capital bases and receive more government support compared to
joint-stock or foreign banks. The 2S-TPP-DEA model’s ability to provide more representa-
tive productivity measures across these diverse bank types is a signiﬁcant improvement over
previous models.
Moreover, the stage-speciﬁc analysis enabled by the 2S-TPP-DEA model allows for the
identiﬁcation of productivity drivers at different points in the banking process. While tra-
ditional DEA models often aggregate inputs and outputs, potentially obscuring the distinct
contributions of each input, the 2S-TPP-DEA model separates the production stage (inputs
to intermediate outputs) from the intermediation stage (intermediate outputs to ﬁnal outputs).

This separation enables a more precise assessment of how efﬁciently resources are utilized

In response to the challenges posed by high-dimensional data in DEA, the 2S-TPP-DEA
model reduces the computational burden by focusing on partial productivity ratios, which

ﬁnancial distress, providing a more comprehensive view of banking efﬁciency. Traditional
DEA models often overlook these external factors, yet they play a crucial role in inﬂuencing

3 Methodology
The 2S-TPP-DEA (Two-Stage Total Partial Productivity Data Envelopment Analysis) model
introduces a novel way of calculating partial productivities (and ultimately, efﬁciencies) by
using ratios where outputs at each stage are divided by a simple average of inputs, as opposed
to traditional DEA (Data Envelopment Analysis) models that typically compute efﬁciency
by dividing a sum of weighted outputs by a sum of weighted inputs.

depending on the speciﬁc context and objectives of the analysis. First, the 2S-TPP-DEA

environments where input quantities vary signiﬁcantly across DMUs and can distort pro-
ductivity assessments. Second, by examining the ratio of each output to the average input,
this approach provides insights into the marginal productivity of inputs. It helps to highlight
how effectively, on average, resources are converted into outputs, offering a micro-level per-

this novel method allows for a more detailed analysis of the production process, especially
in complex operations where multiple stages of production are involved. Each stage can be
analyzed separately to identify the strengths and weakness of productivity sources, which
is valuable for process optimization and for understanding which stages need improvement.

The DGP described above introduces a stochastic modeling approach to simulate the
interdependencies and variabilities inherent in production processes across different stages
of production within DMUs. This DGP blends deterministic components with stochastic
modulation to reﬂect real-world uncertainties and operational dynamics. In other words,
the DGP integrates deterministic and stochastic elements to capture both predictable and
unpredictable variations in production. In terms of the deterministic component, each stage
of production (inputs to intermediates and intermediates to outputs) is modeled using linear
relationships. These relationships are deﬁned by averaging processes where outputs at each
stage depend linearly on the inputs from the preceding stage. This part of the model reﬂects
the systematic portion of the production process that can be anticipated based on historical
data or theoretical understanding. On the other hand, the stochastic modulation by means of
random variables α and β is introduced to modify the inﬂuence of each stage’s deterministic
outputs. These variables can be sampled from uniform or beta distributions, representing
the inherent randomness or unexplained variability in how inputs are converted to outputs.

The case study on Chinese banks and their underlying 2S-TPP DEA model comprises a
total of 20 variables distributed among matrices X, Y, and Z, here one must consider the roles
these variables play in the bank’s operations and their relevance to the stages of ﬁnancial
production or services. Inputs typically encompass resources consumed to produce outputs,
typically costs, expenses, or other forms of ﬁnancial outlay that are necessary for generating
revenue. On the other hand, intermediate variables can be seen as outputs of the ﬁrst stage
and inputs to the second stage, often representing transitional or process-enabling factors
in ﬁnancial production. Finally, outputs englobe results of business operations, generally
revenue, proﬁts, or other indicators of ﬁnancial success. The dataset was collected from
BankFocus and encompasses a period from 2014 to 2023.
In short, one may see from appendix A, within the banking industry, inputs include costs
and expenses because they reﬂect the consumption of resources (Isik & Hassan, 2002).
Intermediate outputs/inputs are often items that serve dual roles, contributing to both costs
and revenue generation, such as deposits which are both liabilities and sources for loanable
funds (Holod & Lewis, 2011). And, outputs focus on ﬁnancial achievements and proﬁtability
metrics, directly reﬂecting the success and efﬁciency of the bank’s operations (Giokas, 2008).
3.2 Z/X ratio derived dataset: the production approach in banking (Stage 1)
Now, with respect to the Z/X and Y/Z variables, derived from the previous rationale, the Z/X
ratios signify how effectively a bank utilizes its operational inputs to generate and manage key
ﬁnancial assets and capabilities (Wang et al., 2014). This ﬁrst stage is usually referred to as
the production approach in banking (Kwon & Lee, 2015). Speciﬁcally, these ratios measure
the marginal productivity with which consumed resources (averaged costs and expenses) are
transformed ﬁnancial resources like loans, deposits, and earnings assets, which are central
to its revenue generation capacity. Formally, the Z/X ratio for each intermediate variable is
calculated via eqs. (8) and (9).
3.3 Y/Z ratio derived dataset: the intermediation approach in banking (Stage 2)
On the other hand, the Y/Z ratios evaluate the effectiveness with which intermediate assets and
capabilities are utilized to generate ﬁnal outputs, reﬂecting ﬁnancial stability and proﬁtability
(Zha et al., 2016). An analogous interpretation can be given for this stage, usually known as
intermediation approach in banking (Henriques et al., 2020). Hence, the Y/Z ratios indicate
that a bank is highly effective at leveraging its ﬁnancial resources and capital to produce
outputs that directly contribute to its proﬁtability, such as net income, interest income, and

3.4 Input prices
In ﬁnance and production theory, prices of inputs are crucial for understanding the cost
structure and, hence, the operational efﬁciency of a ﬁrm (Huang & Fu, 2013). In fact, prices
of inputs represent the cost of using the inputs. In this research, three distinct input prices
are computed based on the original dataset, as described next: (1) price of labor (personnel
expenses per business volume), measuring the cost of labor per unit of business volume; (2)
price of capital (interest expenses per ﬁxed assets), reﬂecting the cost of using capital; and
(3) price of funds (interest expenses per deposits), accounting for the cost of funds per unit
of deposits.

3.6 Financial distress
The Altman z-score is a well-known measure of ﬁnancial distress and bankruptcy risk (Alt-
man, 1968). It combines various ﬁnancial ratios to assess the likelihood of a ﬁrm going
bankrupt, aiming at integrating proﬁtability, leverage, and operating efﬁciency to predict

probability of bankruptcy (gray zone). Consequently, if z-score < 1.81, there is a high prob-
ability of bankruptcy (distress zone) (Khaddaﬁet al., 2017). Based on the collected dataset

China is diverse, comprising several types of institutions that cater to different segments of
the economy, each with distinct roles, regulatory frameworks, and strategic focuses (Dong
et al., 2016). For example, state-owned banks are the largest and most inﬂuential in China’s
banking landscape (Berger et al., 2009). These banks are majority-owned by the central gov-
ernment and include major entities like the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China, China
Construction Bank, Bank of China, and Agricultural Bank of China. They traditionally serve
as the main conduits for monetary policy and ﬁnancial regulation enforcement, providing
signiﬁcant support to governmental economic objectives, including ﬁnancing infrastructural
mega-projects and supporting other state-owned enterprises (Cull & Xu, 2003). Due to their
size and political backing, they have considerable inﬂuence over China’s economic stability
and growth (Laurenceson & Chai, 2001). On the other hand, joint-stock commercial banks
are shareholder-owned entities that operate similarly to commercial banks in other global
markets (Fu & Heffernan, 2009). These banks are publically traded (Wang et al., 2018) and
include well-known names such as China Merchants Bank, Shanghai Pudong Development
Bank, and China Minsheng Bank. They were established in the late twentieth century as part
of China’s banking reforms to introduce competition and efﬁciency in the banking sector.
Joint-stock banks tend to be more commercially oriented than state-owned banks, catering
to both corporate and retail banking sectors, and are signiﬁcant players in China’s urban
centers (Chen & Shen, 2024). In their turn, foreign banks in China are international banks
that have been allowed to establish branches or wholly foreign-owned entities within the
country (Leung, 1997). Examples include HSBC, Standard Chartered, and Citibank. These
banks bring international banking practices to China and are particularly active in serving
multinational corporations, offering foreign exchange and trade ﬁnance services (He & Fu,
2008). They also cater to high-net-worth individuals and afﬂuent Chinese who seek sophisti-
cated banking services. The presence of foreign banks has helped to increase competitiveness
and innovation in the Chinese banking sector (Hope et al., 2008). Lastly, city commercial

banks primarily operate within city jurisdictions and cater to local businesses, consumers, and
regional governments (Shih et al., 2007). These banks were transformed from urban credit

small and medium-sized enterprises and meet the speciﬁc ﬁnancial needs of local communi-
ties. Over time, some city banks have expanded operations beyond their initial geographies

and poverty alleviation programs. They help facilitate rural economic development through
loans, deposits, and other ﬁnancial products tailored to the needs of the rural population
(Peng et al., 2021).

prior two-stage DEA studies of the banking sector (Anh, 2025; Hafsal et al., 2020), which

4.1 Robustness Checks
In order to provide additional robustness checks, we (1) winsorize all contextual ratios at the
1st/99th percentiles and (2) apply log1p to heavy-tailed positives (ROA, Lerner proxy, Pk,
HHI). The results are reported in Tables 7, 8, and 9.
Compared to OLS on raw data, the winsorized OLS stabilizes the main effects and shifts
most interaction terms toward economically coherent signs, often with higher statistical sig-
niﬁcance. Since interaction terms are highly leverage-sensitive, trimming tails reduces the
inﬂuence of a few extreme observations and recovers the average heterogeneous effect instead
of a tail-driven contrast. However, after winsorization, it is worth mentioning that the main
effect of liquidity becomes positive for TPP and for both stages. This indicates the earlier neg-
ative association was driven by outliers (e.g., distressed years or very small banks) rather than
the typical bank. Besides, ownership–price interactions continue to show clear size/ownership
heterogeneity even after trimming. Foreign banks are more cost-sensitive—both to labor and
to capital/funding—whereas state-owned banks appear partly insulated from capital-price
pressures in intermediation. Despite regulatory and governmental actions, state-owed banks
still do not translate better CAMELS/TOPSIS proﬁles into Stage-2 efﬁciency as effectively
as other banks after trimming.
Readers should acknowledge, however, that the sign reversal of liquidity between raw
and winsorized OLS is economically coherent once we recognize that liquidity ratios are
heavy-tailed and often regime-dependent in banking panels. In stress years or distressed
institutions, liquidity can jump sharply due to precautionary hoarding or forced balance-sheet
contraction, precisely when intermediation activity and productivity fall; a small number of
such high-leverage observations can dominate OLS and generate a negative average slope.
After winsorization, the estimated liquidity effect becomes positive and signiﬁcant for TPP
and both stages, consistent with the ‘buffer’ channel whereby moderate liquidity improves
funding stability, reduces disruption costs, and supports smoother credit transformation.
Hence, the raw OLS coefﬁcient should be interpreted as being driven by tail stress regimes,
whereas the winsorized estimates better capture the typical marginal effect among the bulk

5 Conclusions
This study contributes to the literature by shifting the focus from traditional DEA-based efﬁ-
ciency measures to a more differentiated analysis of total partial productivities through the
2S-TPP-DEA framework. The model provides a clearer understanding of how efﬁciency is
generated and lost within the two operational stages of banking—production and intermedi-
ation—and offers a diagnostic tool that banks can use to improve internal resource allocation
and resilience under changing economic conditions. Applied to the Chinese banking indus-
try, the approach captures heterogeneity among bank types and reveals the joint inﬂuence of
market structure, input prices, and ﬁnancial distress on productivity. These results underscore
the importance of stage-speciﬁc management and policy actions to enhance operational and
ﬁnancial performance.
5.1 Implications for financial stability and regulation
In addition to its methodological and managerial relevance, the ﬁndings of this study carry
important implications for banking regulation and ﬁnancial stability. The two-stage decom-
position of total partial productivity provides regulators with a diagnostic view of where
inefﬁciencies originate—whether from production-related resource use or from intermedi-
ation and credit allocation processes. This insight can help supervisory authorities identify
vulnerable institutions early and design targeted interventions before inefﬁciencies translate
into systemic risk.
The evidence that ﬁnancial distress and market structure signiﬁcantly inﬂuence productiv-
ity suggests that regulatory frameworks should adopt more differentiated approaches. Instead
of applying uniform prudential standards, regulators could use stage-speciﬁc efﬁciency indi-
cators derived from the 2S-TPP-DEA model to guide tailored actions. For example, banks
exhibiting low intermediation-stage productivity could be encouraged to enhance credit risk
assessment and capital adequacy practices, while those with production-stage inefﬁciencies
might beneﬁt from policies supporting technological investment or operational restructuring.
Moreover, the inclusion of contextual variables such as input prices and market concen-
tration offers a foundation for macroprudential stress testing. The 2S-TPP-DEA model could
serve as a complementary tool to simulate how external shocks—such as rising funding costs
or changing competitive conditions—affect efﬁciency and resilience across different owner-
ship types. Policymakers could employ these insights to distinguish between inefﬁciencies
arising from adverse market environments and those driven by internal mismanagement,
enabling proportionate and evidence-based support for distressed banks. By integrating efﬁ-
ciency analysis into the broader regulatory toolkit, the model contributes to the development
of a more adaptive and stability-oriented supervisory framework.
This study also lays the groundwork for future research. The longitudinal application of
the 2S-TPP-DEA model could provide a more comprehensive picture of efﬁciency trends
123

Annals of Operations Research
over time. Additionally, incorporating external environmental factors, such as technological
advancements and global economic ﬂuctuations, could offer a richer understanding of bank
performance in a constantly evolving landscape. Although this study focuses on the Chi-
nese banking industry, the 2S-TPP-DEA framework is broadly applicable to other emerging
economies with comparable banking structures. Many developing ﬁnancial systems—such
as those in India, Brazil, Indonesia, and South Africa—exhibit similar features of market
heterogeneity, regulatory transition, and exposure to ﬁnancial distress. The model’s ability to
decompose efﬁciency into production and intermediation stages while accounting for con-
textual variables like market structure and input prices makes it well suited for analyzing such
environments. Applying this approach cross-nationally could yield valuable insights into how
institutional and macroeconomic differences inﬂuence bank productivity and resilience. By
building upon this foundation, future research can contribute to a more efﬁcient and stable
banking sector both in China and across the wider emerging-market context.

Appendix B Distributional Comparison between Beta and Uniform
assumptions
The baseline results in the main text are computed from the deterministic 2S-TPP model. The
stochastic DGP described in Sect. 3 is explored here as a robustness extension. Readers should
notethattheefﬁciencyscoresreportedinthemaintextcorrespondtothedeterministicsolution
of the 2S-TPP model, obtained by fully solving the underlying optimization problem for each
bank. This deterministic solution deﬁnes the productivity frontier, endogenous weights, and
stage-level decompositions. However, the stochastic component introduced now does not
re-estimate the frontier or weights. Instead, it perturbs the deterministic efﬁciency scores
using random modulation terms to assess uncertainty and robustness as described by the
pseudo-code depicted in Table 10.
Hence, to assess the sensitivity of efﬁciency estimates to the stochastic speciﬁcation, we
compare three data-generating processes for the multiplicative perturbation terms applied to
stage-level productivity: (i) independent Uniform(0,1); (ii) independent Beta(a, b); and (iii)
Beta(a, b) with dependence induced via a Gaussian copula (ρ = 0.3). The Beta distributions
used in the stochastic sensitivity analysis are not estimated from the data but selected as cal-
ibrated robustness scenarios. We consider symmetric Beta(2,2) and Beta(5,5) distributions,
both centered at 0.5, to represent increasing degrees of concentration around the mean while
preserving the same support as the Uniform(0,1) base scenario. Beta(2,2) reﬂects moder-
ate dispersion, whereas Beta(5,5) represents a low-noise environment with shocks tightly
clustered around the central value. This nested speciﬁcation allows us to assess how progres-
sively stronger distributional assumptions affect uncertainty and inference without altering
the deterministic efﬁciency frontier.
While point efﬁciency estimates and rankings remain remarkably stable across speciﬁca-
tions (Spearman correlations between 0.989 and 0.998) (cf. Table 11), the implied uncertainty
differs substantially (cf. Table 12). Precisely, Table 12 reports Monte Carlo sensitivity sum-
maries obtained by perturbing the deterministic 2S-TPP scores with random modulation
terms; it does not re-estimate the 2S-TPP frontier under each distribution.
Results displayed in Fig. 6 indicate that point estimates and rankings remain numerically
stable across stochastic speciﬁcations, while conﬁdence intervals and dispersion measures
vary according to the assumed distribution. For instance, the Uniform draws generate the
widest conﬁdence intervals, whereas Beta distributions yield progressively tighter bands,
especially under Beta(5,5). Introducing dependence slightly enlarges conﬁdence intervals
but does not affect rank stability. Therefore, these ﬁndings indicate that the main empirical
conclusions are driven by the deterministic productivity structure, while the stochastic layer
primarily governs the degree of epistemic uncertainty rather than relative performance.
