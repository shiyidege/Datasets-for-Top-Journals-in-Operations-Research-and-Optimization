Abstract
In today’s fast-paced world, driven by technological developments, workers face increasing
workloads, leading to fatigue, stress, and disruptions in their circadian cycles. These biologi-
cal, physical, and behavioral effects negatively impact worker health, safety, and productivity.
To address this challenge, this study proposes an optimization of workforce scheduling con-
sidering fatigue management and productivity balance through a Mixed Integer Programming
(MIP)approach.Twoobjectivefunctionsareconsidered:Theﬁrstobjectivefocusesonfatigue
control by implementing a rest-rotation system. To enforce compliance, two additional penal-
ties are introduced: (1) a preference based penalty for workers assigned to preferred shifts
outside their planned rotation, and (2) an out of rotation penalty for assignments that vio-
late both rotation schedules and worker preferences. The second objective aims to maximize
productivity by minimizing the workload imbalance among workers. The model was tested
in 40 small and medium instances, demonstrating its ability to optimize the distribution of
the workforce while balancing fatigue management and productivity. The results show that
integrating wellness considerations into personnel scheduling improves productivity while

1 Introduction
In 2017, Jeffrey C. Hall, Michael Rosbash, and Michael W. Young received the Nobel Prize
(Ledford & Callaway, 2017), for their genetic discoveries related to behavioral rhythms
(Bargiello & Young, 1984), the construction and maintenance of the biological clock
(Bargiello et al., 1984), and the restoration of rhythmicity in circadian locomotor behavior,
initially studied in Drosophila melanogaster (Zehring et al., 1984); laying the groundwork for
further studies that revealed the existence of an endogenous circadian clock. Consequently,
the way how humans particularly manage their sleep habits can have an impact on their
own biological rhythm, usually known as circadian cycle or circadian rhythms; they are the
physical, mental, and behavioral changes that follow a roughly 24-hour cycle and respond to
light and darkness.
Technological advances, evolving trends, and the increasing demand for goods and ser-
vices have disrupted human biological synchronization, leading many workers to face the
demands of rotating shifts, night work, and irregular sleep–rest patterns. These high levels
of shift work are associated with fatigue, stress, and disruptions in circadian cycles, which
are essential for physiological functions Prize (2017); such disturbances can lead to a range
of medium– and long-term health problems (Garde et al., 2020). Consequently, circadian
disruption is recognized as a signiﬁcant public health problem, with night work representing
a major occupational risk factor with potential long-term health impacts (Nabe-Nielsen et
al., 2025).
Considering that the workload of workers is the result of a shift work scheduling made by
companies, the way schedule is made plays a crucial role in operational efﬁciency in various
sectors, such as healthcare, transportation, and manufacturing. It determines how workers
can meet performance objectives while ensuring their well-being and safety. Traditional shift
scheduling models have focused on maximizing performance, minimizing total costs, and, in
recent years, reducing worker fatigue. To address these challenges, two main approaches are
used: exact methods and approximated methods (Ernst et al., 2004), and recently, artiﬁcial
intelligence and machine learning approaches have been used to evaluate fatigue conditions
(Kakhi et al., 2024).
Consequently, despite the fact that numerous studies have included the minimization of
fatigue or its considerations as a constraint in models to solve the problem of shift work
scheduling (Xu & Hall, 2021), the literature is still scarce that not only determines fatigue as
a decreasing condition derived from work time, but also considers it as the result of circadian
rhythm disturbances. This added to the fact that the intensiﬁcation of population migration,
technological improvements, and economic demands have led to an increase in shift-based
workload (Poormoosa et al., 2024),(Lee & Jeong, 2021); so, recognition and inclusion of
circadian cycle disruptions are mandatory as part of shift scheduling models and also their
real application.

Factors such as the direction of shift rotation (forward vs. backward), the length of shift
cycles, and individual chronotypes (morning vs. evening) can inﬂuence the extent of circadian
disruption experienced by shift workers. Similarly, strategies such as strategic light exposure,
melatonin supplementation, and carefully designed shift schedules can help mitigate the
negative effects of circadian misalignment and promote better health and well-being among
shift workers (Boivin et al., 2022). In addition, the integration of circadian considerations
into shift scheduling optimization models represents a signiﬁcant advancement in the ﬁeld.
Such models aim to minimize circadian disturbance by incorporating factors such as shift
rotation patterns, rest periods, and individual chronotypes (Xu & Hall, 2021). Implementing
guidelines that limit consecutive night shifts, promote forward rotating shifts, and ensure
adequate time off after night shifts can signiﬁcantly reduce fatigue and improve sleep quality
among shift workers (Grøtting & Øvergård, 2023).
Thus, this research describes a bi-objective integer programming model that is focused on
fatigue control generated by disturbances on circadian cycle of workers, through the imple-
mentation of a rest-rotation system, and maximizes productivity while workload imbalance
is minimized. The reduction in fatigue is represented as a weighted function of excessive
consecutive nights, days of non-compliance with rest, and excessive consecutive working
days. The conditions that are inspired by circadian cycle perturbations are represented by the
maximum and minimum number of days in a shift, as well as resting days and the forward
rotation. All of these conditions were deﬁned according to the recommendations given by
Garde et al. (2020) to increase the performance, safety, and health of the workers.
The initial results produced schedules that complied with governmental requirements
while incorporating recommended circadian rhythm principles. However, in some cases, the
resulting schedules were not entirely fair, as they disproportionately assigned higher work-
loads to certain workers due to predeﬁned conditions. This highlighted a potential conﬂict
between generating a schedule that minimizes fatigue and one that ensures workload balance.
To address this, a bi-objective approach was adopted. The model was executed using Python
and Gurobi, and it was solved using a ϵ-constraint approach, and it was initially checked with
different instances sizes according to the workers, the planning horizon, consecutive nights,
shift days, and excessive consecutive days.
The main contributions of this research are mainly: (i) the consideration of conditions
associated with circadian cycle disruptions through constraints of the rotation system, (ii)
the validation of the opposite nature of the analyzed objectives which represents a novelty
approach not considered before in this way, (iii) the corresponding trade-off between fatigue
and imbalance in workload minimization, and the discrepancy between single-objective and
bi-objective extreme solutions according to possible behaviors in schedule deﬁnition.
The reminder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes an overview of
the literature on the shift scheduling problem, the main applications and case studies, and
circadian conditions related to this problem. Section 3 describes in detail the mathematical
model approach and the constraints associated with circadian conditions. Section 4 shows
the results obtained as well as the discussion of the results found. Finally, the conclusions
and future work are shown in Section 5.

2 Literature review
This section provides an overview of the latest research on shift scheduling, main deﬁnitions,
applications, and the relationship with circadian rhythms that the literature has developed so
far, highlighting methodologies and key ﬁndings.
2.1 Personnel scheduling: shift work scheduling
There are many classiﬁcations used to describe scheduling problems; one of the most widely
used is the classiﬁcation proposed by Brucker et al. (2011); permanent centered planning
(police, healthcare), wavy centered planning (call center, warehouse center schedules), mobil-
ity centered planning (transport) and project-based planning (Personnel scheduling: Models
and complexity), in the latter, the shift scheduling problem (SSP) can be found. Shift schedul-
ing is characterized by the inclusion of rotating workloads (day, evening, night) (Silva, 2023).
It also considers the assignment of personnel to speciﬁc work periods according to produc-
tion requirements, regulatory conditions, and workers’ needs and preferences (Herawati et
al., 2018) in order to improve worker performance, and ensure their health and safety at work
(Borgonjon & Maenhout, 2022).
The design of shift schedules should also consider the workload and intensity of work
during different shifts, as these factors can exacerbate the effects of sleep deprivation and
fatigue (Alsharari et al., 2021). To achieve this, the primary goal is to align work schedules
with the natural rhythms of the human body, reducing stress, and improving productivity. The
impact of shift work extends beyond the individual, affecting family life, social interactions,
and overall quality of life (Silva, 2023). Thus, considering that employees’ needs can differ
from workers’ preferences according to their own life, shift work scheduling becomes a
complex problem to solve; therefore, the development and implementation of sophisticated
mathematical models is the main approach for researchers for these types of problem (Knauth
& Hornberger, 2003).
Numerous research on SPP can be found in the literature. For example, Özder et al. (2020)
presents a systematic review of the literature on personnel scheduling problems, classifying
studies based on various criteria such as application area, solution method, and objective
function. It highlights the diversity of approaches used to tackle these problems, ranging
from exact methods like linear programming to heuristics and metaheuristics. Likewise,
authors focus their research on personnel scheduling considering the need and preferences
of employees at the same time ensuring use of resources to achieve balanced workload dis-
tribution. According to Özder et al. (2020), the most common objective function in the study
is to minimize personnel costs, followed by balanced assignment and demand satisfaction.
Similarly, the main areas of study include nursing, production, and other healthcare services.
To solve scheduling problems oriented to personnel or workers, different researches
have used exact and approximated methods as well as a single-objective or multi-objective
approaches. On the one hand, on exact methods, Nobil et al. (2022) presents a mixed-integer
linear programming for a case study in Iran to minimize the total cost of the workforce;
this approach allowed a cost reduction compared to manual hospital scheduling. More-
over, Kletzander and Musliu (2020) solves the general employee scheduling problem (GES),
under deterministic scenarios; focuses on creating automated solutions to meet varying work
demands while satisfying constraints such as legal requirements and employee satisfaction.
They also state the problem as NP-Hard, as well as offer a set of benchmark instances to
evaluate the problem with other approaches.

proactive strategies can be used to prevent fatigue before it manifests itself, while reactive
approaches address fatigue after it has already occurred; also, work-induced fatigue on shifts
is a major concern and affects worker health, safety, and productivity.
2.2 Applications
The shift work scheduling is extensively studied in a much broader range of industries,
including transportation (airlines, railways, public transport), emergency services (police,
ﬁre, ambulance), call centers, hospitality, retail, and manufacturing (Ernst et al., 2004). In
healthcare, optimizing shift schedules for nurses and other medical staff is crucial to ensuring
patient safety and minimizing medical errors. The importance of shift work in modern society
is emphasized by its necessity in various industries (Boivin et al., 2022).
In concordance, the rotation of medical personnel, such as nurses, doctors, and assistant
crews, to guarantee the complete attention of the patient (at all levels of medical services) is
the most studied approach (Ferri et al., 2016). According to Abdalkareem et al. (2021), the
operating room scheduling is mainly studied in healthcare scheduling followed by the nurse
rostering problem and the patient admission scheduling.
For example, Grøtting and Øvergård (2023) research on the association between shift work
schedule and absence from work in nurses in a Norwegian hospital. They used a multivariate
linear regression analysis to evaluate the relationship among variables determined in a cross-
sectional study. As a result, the author found that the individual adjustment in the shift work
schedule could be associated with lower rates of sickness/absence. In addition, Epstein et al.
(2023) develops a qualitative interview study with nurses and assistant nurses; from the results
analysis, they highlights the importance of involving workers in the process of scheduling
work shifts, including putting aside time in their working day for this task. Similarly, Lee
and Jeong (2021) propose a study in which the compliance with a set of recommendations
for the shift work of nurses in Korea, including night shifts, is analyzed.
In other application areas, (Kervezee et al., 2021) addressed a study on police ofﬁcers to
identify the relationship between chronotype and sleep behavior during rotating shift work.
The authors used mixed linear modeling to assess the effect of shift type, chronotype, and
their interaction. They concluded that the chronotype is associated with the duration of sleep
depending on the shift type (morning, evening, night), which is a starting point to consider
chronobiological principles in work schedules. In transport, Chabal et al. (2024) analyzes
the sleep, fatigue, and lifestyle behaviors of sailors, demonstrating that not only the fact that
task scheduling inﬂuences sleep disturbance, but also the place where tasks are performed
has a signiﬁcant impact. Similarly, Wen et al. (2021) addresses topics such as a review of
models and algorithms around airline crew scheduling; the authors focus on the need to keep

2.3 Circadian rhythm and shift work scheduling
When shift work requires working at night and sleeping during the day, it disrupts the align-
ment between these internal rhythms and the external environment, leading to circadian
desynchronization (Boivin et al., 2022). Disruptions in circadian rhythm among shift work-
ers can have signiﬁcant consequences, affecting not only their sleep/wake cycle but also
their chrononutrition and psychosocial well-being, potentially leading to metabolic disor-
ders such as hypertension, dyslipidemia, dysglycemia, and abdominal obesity (Mohd Azmi
et al., 2020). Likewise, disturbances contribute to cardiovascular disease, obesity, and sleep
disorders (Kecklund & Axelsson, 2016) (Bostock, 2024), as well as circadian misalignment
alters risk-taking preferences (Hisler et al., 2023). Similarly, diet management has been
shown to be an effective method for addressing circadian misalignment among shift workers
(Phoi & Keogh, 2019). In addition, there is an increase in the risk of accidents for shift work-
ers, especially during their commute home from a night shift (Gupta et al., 2019); accident
rates are signiﬁcantly higher during night shifts due to decreased alertness (Ganesan et al.,
2019)(Härmä et al., 2020); it has a relationship with the impact of exposure to artiﬁcial light
during the night shift of workers (Festus et al., 2024).
Consequently, the American Academy of Sleep Medicine and the Sleep Research Society
provide guiding principles to determine the duration of the work shift and to address the
effects of the duration of the work shift on performance, safety, and health (Gurubhagavat-
ula et al., 2021). These principles emphasize the importance of understanding the factors
that contribute to fatigue and associated risks and of implementing evidence-based coun-
termeasures (Gurubhagavatula et al., 2021). These countermeasures include educating and
training workers in effective fatigue management practices and highlighting the importance
of sleep (Knauth & Hornberger, 2003). It is worth also mentioning that women constitute
approximately half of the shift workforce and are more prone to experience work-life con-
ﬂicts, highlighting the importance of considering gender-speciﬁc factors in shift scheduling
practices (Boivin et al., 2022).
Thus, some research has focused on incorporating the conditions given above to avoid or
minimize their negative effects on workers. For example, Mohamed et al. (2023) describes
a simulation-based framework considering multi-individual circadian entertainment. The
authors highlight key elements of light exposure in the synchronization of circadian cycles
(with different intrinsic circadian cycles). Rahman and St. Hilaire, M.A., Grant, L.K., Barger,
L.K., Brainard, G.C., Czeisler, C.A. Lockley, S.W. (2022) propose dynamic lighting sched-
ules to support circadian adaptation. Through a controlled experiment, they suggested that
optimizing lighting levels and types in shift work can help regulate the circadian cycle. Garde
et al. (2020) developed a comprehensive study with different aspects of the participants’ sleep
quality, identifying a signiﬁcant relationship between the number of consecutive night shifts
and the decrease in sleep quality (avoiding 2, 4 or 7 consecutive nights of night shifts).
In conclusion, while recent decades of research have focused on worker well-being along
with improving company productivity, studies on shift work scheduling that incorporate
circadian cycle conditions for workload stabilization remain scarce. Therefore, understanding
the relationship between shift work and human circadian rhythms is crucial to developing
effective strategies to mitigate the short-, mid-, and long-term adverse effects of rotating
shifts on workers. Consequently, this work addresses the recommendation given by Garde et

3 Mathematical model
The mathematical model describes the shift scheduling for a set of workers with equal skills
that will be assigned to three possible shifts per day (morning-1, evening-2, night-3), accord-
ing to a planning horizon. Each worker must be assigned to only one shift per day, and
each shift must have a minimum and maximum number of workers. Each worker follows
a forward-rotation shift schedule, assigned in accordance with governmental requirements
and recommended circadian rhythm principles. In this schedule, workers rotate sequentially
through a full block of morning, evening and night shifts, repeating the pattern; this system
is referred to as forward rotation. In addition, each worker may indicate personal preferences
regarding assigned shifts and days; however, assignments may be made outside the standard
rotation or individual preferences if necessary.
The problem involves a bi-objective approach that considers two conﬂicting goals. The
ﬁrst objective minimizes fatigue, considering consecutive nights with excessive rest, non-
compliance with rest, and consecutive working days with excessive duration. In addition,
it incorporates a penalty system that considers assignments out of the expected forward
rotation schedule. This system includes penalties based on workers personal preferences but
out of rotation, and penalties for assignments out of preferences and expected rotation. This
structure prioritizes circadian-aligned scheduling, but allows ﬂexibility when operationally
needed or convenient. The second objective minimizes workload imbalance, looking for a fair
assignment of shifts for all workers. The imbalance is measured as the total deviation from
the expected balanced workload throughout the planning horizon. This objective addresses
operational and fairness concerns, preventing unfair or unbalanced assignment of shifts to
speciﬁc workers.
These objectives represent the trade-off between a fatigue-oriented scheduling and a bal-
ance of operational assignment in scheduling. Speciﬁcally, this trade-off arises from the
distinct scheduling behaviors promoted by each objective. The fatigue objective encourages
assignment patterns that preserve circadian alignment, limit sequences of night work, and
provide sufﬁcient rest opportunities. These requirements may lead to reducing the number of
assignments for speciﬁc workers, or to avoiding particular shift rotations to prevent fatigue
accumulation. In contrast, the workload imbalance objective prioritizes deﬁning a uniform
distribution of assignments across all workers, which may require the reallocation of shifts to
the same workers whose assignments, according to the fatigue objective, would be limited.
As a result, improving one objective tends to deteriorate the other; then, it is expected that
the solutions that minimize fatigue may present a higher workload imbalance, while more
balanced schedules may include more fatigue-related penalties.
In addition, the objective function for fatigue minimization (z1) constitutes a represen-
tation of short-term physiological adverse effects–circadian disruption, inadequate sleep
duration, and fatigue–which, according to scientiﬁc literature, are the underlying mechanisms
leading to acute safety risks and possibly long-term health risks, such as injuries, cancer, and
cardiometabolic diseases (Garde et al., 2020). Speciﬁcally, the design of z1’s components
and associated constraints is supported in the evidence-based recommendations provided by
(Garde et al., 2020), developed to mitigate occupational risks associated with night shift-
work. The model elements quantify speciﬁc physiological stressors: (i)Penalizing Excessive

3.1 Problem description
3.1.1 Sets
The mathematical model employs the following sets:
• I: Set of available workers, i ∈I, where |I| = A
• J: Set of shifts {1-morning, 2-evening, 3-night}, j ∈J, where |J| = S
• K: Set of planning days, k ∈K, where |K| = P
3.1.2 Parameters
The model parameters are deﬁned as follows:
• A: Number of workers available
• S: Total number of available shifts per day (S = 3)
• P: Total number of days according to the planning horizon
• P H: Planning horizon (P H = P)
• D: Block of days that pass in a shift
• Nmax: Maximum number of night shifts allowed for the worker
• Wmax: Maximum number of consecutive workdays for the worker
• t: Time index for consecutive period calculations (t = 0, 1, 2, ..., Nmax for night shifts; t
= 0, 1, 2, ..., Wmax for workdays)
• m j: Minimum number of workers required for shift j ∈J per day
• n j: Maximum number of workers allowed for shift j ∈J per day
• M1: Large positive constant used for constraint linearization
• minshi f ts: Minimum number of shifts expected for a balanced workload per worker
• pari, j,k:Binary parameter indicating if worker i can be assigned to shift j on day k
according to forward rotation schedule and circadian rhythm recommendations
• pprefi, j,k: Binary parameter indicating if worker i prefers to be assigned to shift j on
day k
3.1.3 Decision variables
The decision variables are classiﬁed into assignment, penalty control, fatigue measurement,
and imbalance measurement variables:
Assignment variables:
• xi, j,k: Binary variable that is 1 if a worker i is assigned to shift j on day k, 0 otherwise
Penalty control variables:
• P E N ALi, j,k: Binary variable that allows activation as a penalty if a worker i is assigned
to shift j which is preferred but outside of the rotation of the shift forward on day k

• P RE Fi, j,k: Binary variable that is 1 if there is an effective penalty when a worker i
is assigned to shift j that is preferred and out of the shift forward rotation on day k, 0
otherwise
• ROT P E N ALi, j,k: Binary variable that allows activation as a penalty if a worker i is
assigned to shift j that is not preferred and out of the shift forward rotation on day k
• OUT ROTi, j,k: Binary variable that is 1 if there is an effective penalty when a worker i
is assigned to shift j that is not preferred and out of the shift forward rotation on day k,
0 otherwise
The penalty system uses two pairs of variables to distinguish between different types of
rotation deviations, implementing a hierarchical penalty structure. The P E N AL/P RE F pair
manages assignments where workers prefer shifts outside their planned rotation, applying
moderate penalties. The ROT P E N AL/OUT ROT pair manages assignments that violate
both rotation and worker preferences, applying higher penalties.
Fatigue measurement variables:
• T NCi,k: Total excessive consecutive night shifts of worker i on day k
• DIi,k: Days of resting non-compliance of worker i on day k
• DCTi,k: Excessive consecutive days of worker i on day k
Imbalance measurement variables:
• B ALi: Workload imbalance for worker i, representing the total additional days in the
workload for worker i, out of the expected balance according to the planning horizon

The ﬁrst objective function (1) represents the fatigue calculation, including the penalty
system. The ﬁrst term includes the total excessive consecutive nights, total non-compliance
days of resting, and total excessive consecutive days. The second term represents the total
penalties obtained by activating the preferences. The ﬁnal term corresponds to the total
penalties due to an activation out of the expected rotation and out of preferences.
The objective function coefﬁcients implement a hierarchical penalty structure based on the
severity of circadian rhythm violations. The fatigue components (T NC, DI, DCT ) enter the
objective function with coefﬁcient 1, representing a direct impact measured in days, serving
as the foundation of our penalty structure. This ensures equivalent treatment of excessive
consecutive night shifts, rest non-compliance days, and excessive consecutive working days,
enabling aggregation since all components share the same dimensional units.
The penalty coefﬁcients 2 and 3 implement a hierarchical structure, for operational con-
venience and decision-making guidance. The coefﬁcient value of 2 for P RE F penalties
represents moderate circadian violations, where workers receive preferred shifts out of their
expected optimal rotation. This deviation is twice as costly as a base fatigue disruption. In the
case of coefﬁcient value of 3 for OUT ROT penalties represents severe violations affecting
both rotation and preferences. This progression (1:2:3) maintains dimensional consistency in
day units while establishing a clear penalty order, assignments following circadian rhythm are
least penalized, preferred shifts outside rotation receive moderate penalties, and assignments
violating both rotation and preferences receive the highest penalties. This hierarchical struc-
ture is consistent with evidence that circadian disruption becomes greater when schedules
deviate further from the forward-rotating pattern, and that schedule tolerability is reduced
when assignments also conﬂict with individual shift preferences. Therefore, assignments that
preserve rotation and preferences represent the lowest disruption, assignments that break rota-
tion but maintain preferences represent a moderate disruption, and assignments that violate
both rotation and preferences represent the highest disruption (Boivin et al., 2022; Garde et
al., 2020; Knauth & Hornberger, 2003).
The second objective function (2) represents the imbalance as the total additional days in
the work load for all workers, out of the expected balance according to the planning horizon.
Constraint (3) represents the calculation of excessive consecutive nights according to
expected Nmax. Constraint (4) represent the calculation of non-compliance days of resting
according to expected Wmax. Constraint (5) represent the calculation of excessive consecu-
tive days to 1 expected day. Constraint (6) represent the possible activation of assignment
according to par, P E N AL, and ROT P E N AL. Constraint (7) represents that only shift

Constraints (10)-(11) represent the activation for P E N AL according to preferences and

4 Computational experiment
In this section, the computational experiment was executed to validate the corresponding
model. The experimental design addresses three main goals. The ﬁrst goal is to analyze
the trade-offs generated between the fatigue and the imbalance in optimal Pareto fronts,
considering small instances and medium instances, with and without random preferences.
The second goal is to describe the speciﬁc results for speciﬁc instances to analyze the optimal
solutions in a single-objective approach, and the bi-objective perspective to analyze the
potential shift schedules. The third goal is to compare the model behavior against an existing
literature-based benchmark to validate the functionality.
4.1 General parameters
The general parameter settings for the experiments are presented in Table 1. The correspond-
ing values were deﬁned for dual purposes. First, to align with international recommendations
based on circadian rhythm for fatigue management. Second, explore operational scenarios
relevant to small and medium enterprises, while considering Colombian labor regulatory
frameworks related to working hours, rest periods, and night work conditions (Resolution
2646 of 2008 and Decree 1072 of 2015).
For workers, planning horizon, days per shift block, and maximum consecutive workdays,
Uniform Distributions - UD were selected to ensure unbiased exploration of the parameter
space. Considering the theoretical nature of this study, uniform distributions provide the
same probability to all values within speciﬁed ranges. This enables exploration through
diverse organizational scenarios without introducing preferences toward particular conﬁgu-
rations. The ranges of workers represent small teams to medium-sized organizational units.
The planning horizons correspond to monthly through quarterly scheduling cycles. Days per
shift block represent typical duration periods in the same speciﬁc shift before expected rota-
tion, balancing continuity with circadian rhythm considerations. The maximum consecutive
workdays serve as fatigue prevention limits based on occupational health recommendations.

4.3 Generation of instances
The instances used to check the proposed model are based on a basic deﬁnition according
to the ﬁle name, considering the number of workers, the planning horizon, the block of days
to pass in a shift, the number of consecutive workdays, the number of night shifts allowed,
and the indication if random preferences are allowed. A random instance can be generated
according to these elements and following the criteria in the Table 1.
For example the instance “12workers_PH42_D5_W6_N3_RPREF_YES” indicates the
assignment of 12 workers, in a planning horizon of 42 days, with 5 days in each shift

block, a max of 6 consecutive workdays, a max of 3-night shifts, with existing preferences,
and the instance “5workers_PH60_D7_W5_N2_RPREF_NO” indicates the assignment of 5
workers, in a planning horizon of 60 days, with 7 days in each shift block, a max of 5 number
of consecutive workdays, a max of 2-night shifts, with no existing preferences.
Speciﬁcally, the RPREF parameter controls the activation of worker preferences in the
mathematical model through the pprefi, j,k binary parameters deﬁned in Section 3. If
RPREF_YES, random preference values are generated for each combination of worker-shift-
day, where the values pprefi, j,k are randomly set to 1, to indicate that the worker i prefers
shift j on day k. When RPREF_NO, all preference parameters are set to zero (pprefi, j,k = 0
for all i, j, k), deactivating the preference-based penalty components in the objective func-
tion. This allows for systematic evaluation of preference consideration impact on scheduling
solutions, considering that the same base instance can be solved with and without worker
preferences, to isolate their effect on the trade-off between fatigue and imbalance objectives.
For each instance, the rotation schedule parameters pari, j,k are generated by randomly
assigning initial shift types to the workers, and applying the forward rotation pattern with
D blocks. Speciﬁcally, each worker is assigned a starting shift and is expected to remain in
that shift for D consecutive days before rotating to the next shift in the sequence (morning
(1) evening (2) night (3) morning (1)). For example, if a worker starts in shift 1 (morning)
with D = 5, the preferred schedule would expect him to work for 5 consecutive morning
shifts, then rotate to shift 2 (evening) for 5 days, then to shift 3 (night) for 5 days, and the
cycle repeats. This rotation pattern represents the expected schedule, according to circadian
rhythm recommendations, with possible deviations according to the penalty system deﬁned
in Section 3.
The OUTROT indication (associated with OUT ROTi, j,k variables) is an experimental
control parameter, that determines the ﬂexibility of shift assignments according to expected
rotation pattern. When OUTROT_YES, constraint (20) from Section 3 is deactivated. This
allows the model to assign workers to shifts out of their expected forward rotation when
operationally beneﬁcial. When OUTROT_NO, constraint (20) is activated, enforcing strict
compliance with the forward rotation pattern and deactivating any out-of-rotation assign-
ments.

but varies the activation and deactivation of RPREF, to systematically analyze the impact of
worker preferences on the scheduling solutions, according to trade-off between fatigue and
imbalance objectives.

4.7 Benchmark comparison
The model proposed in this study was compared against that of (Herawati et al., 2018), applied
to an Indonesian hotel. Their model primarily aims to minimize the number of unfulﬁlled
worker shift preferences. Its constraints stipulate that each employee must work only one
shift per day or have a day off; that no worker assigned to an evening or night shift can
be scheduled for the morning shift the following day; a maximum of two consecutive night
shifts; a minimum and maximum number of workers per shift; and the balancing of physical
workload, measured using Borg’s Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE) scale.
The evaluation of the optimal solution from the Herawati model–corresponding to their
reported case of scheduling 4 security workers in a non-star hotel in Yogyakarta across 3 shifts
over 30 days–revealed numerous violations according to the components of the objective
functions deﬁned in this study. These include: 11 instances of missing rest periods after a night
shift, 10 violations of the maximum allowable consecutive workdays, 17 granted preferences
that broke the established rotation, 19 interrupted rotations, and 5 cases of imbalance. To
complement the comparison, the proposed bi-objective model was decomposed into two
single-objectivescenarios.Thesolutionobtainedbyoptimizingfor z1,whenevaluatedagainst
the objective function from Herawati et al. (2018), yields a value 1.8 times higher, coupled
with a 8 percent reduction in physical workload. On the other hand, the solution obtained
by optimizing for z2 results in a value twice as large as theirs, while achieving a workload
reduction of 9 percent.

5 Conclusions
This work presents a bi-objective shift scheduling problem that is focused on fatigue and
workloadimbalanceminimization.Thisproblemconsideredagroupofworkerswiththesame
skills that will be assigned to three possible shifts per day according to a planning horizon.
Each worker had an expected shift forward rotation schedule to be assigned according to the
required governmental requirements and the recommended features of the circadian rhythm.
Also, each worker can indicate a personal preference about the shifts and days to be assigned,
and the assignment could be executed out of shift forward rotation and preferences if needed.
The goals were to analyze the trade-off between fatigue and imbalance, to consider the impact
of allowing assignment outside of expected rotation according to circadian rhythm, and to
generate the corresponding insights.
Based on computational experiments, the bi-objective analysis suggests that a favorable
trade-off exists between fatigue reduction and imbalance control when assignments deviate
from the expected forward shift rotation aligned with the circadian rhythm while also consid-
ering workers’ personal preferences. In contrast, a strictly restrictive approach that adheres
only to the expected shift rotation limits scheduling ﬂexibility. Allowing assignments out-
side the expected rotation while incorporating worker preferences signiﬁcantly increases the
range of viable solutions, as evidenced by the solutions identiﬁed on the Pareto front.
In terms of solutions, a notable gap was identiﬁed between single objective optimal solu-
tions – focused on independent objectives (fatigue and imbalance) - and extreme solutions
on the Pareto front from a bi-objective perspective. For the minimum imbalance solution,
various schedule combinations can achieve the lowest imbalance in days, though they may
negatively impact fatigue levels. Similarly, for the minimum fatigue solution, there is a sig-
niﬁcant gap in the imbalance value between the single-objective optimum and the extreme
Pareto front solution. This ﬁnding highlights an important insight: prioritizing a single objec-
tive can have a substantial impact on scheduling conditions for workers, reinforcing the need
for a bi-objective approach.
Future research will focus on developing an efﬁcient solution method to handle large
instances and reduce the computational time required for medium-sized instances. Addi-
tionally, future work will explore incorporating diverse worker skill sets and extending
the multiobjective perspective by adding an extra dimension to shift scheduling conditions.
Another promising approach involves introducing stochastic elements to account for work-
ers’ real availability and resilience to schedule changes, as well as ﬂuctuating demand for
each shift. Finally, the study will be extended with a speciﬁc case analysis.
