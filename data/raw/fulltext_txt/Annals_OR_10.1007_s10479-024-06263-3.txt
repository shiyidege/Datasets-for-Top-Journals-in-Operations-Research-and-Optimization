Abstract
A generalized n-site Asymmetric Simple Inclusion Process (ASIP) network is studied, where
gate-opening instants are determined by a renewal process and arrivals occur to all sites.
Various types of batch particle movements between sites are analyzed: (i) unidirectional
probabilistic forward movements; (ii) probabilistic forward movements combined with feed-
back to the ﬁrst site; and (iii) general probabilistic multidirectional movements. In contrast to
the tedious successive substitution method used in previous ASIP studies, an efﬁcient matrix
approach is applied to derive the multidimensional probability generating function (PGF)
of site occupancies right after gate opening instants. The complexity of the ASIP processes
allows us to obtain explicit PGF results for small-size networks only, while for larger net-
works, a formula to calculate the mean site occupancies is derived for all types of movements.
In movement case (i) the means are directly and explicitly calculated. For movement case
(ii), where the network is homogeneous with equal probabilities of forward movements from
site i to downstream sites j ≥i, we show that the ratio between the mean occupancies of
consecutive sites approaches a constant when the network becomes large, and calculate this
ratio. Finally, we investigate an n-site network where at gate opening instants all gates open
simultaneously, and particles move in all directions. Numerical examples are presented.

1 Introduction and outline
The recently introduced Asymmetric Simple Inclusion Process (ASIP) (Reuveni et al., 2011,
2012a,b) is a tandem network within the family of Tandem Stochastic Networks (TSNs).
It comprises n sites (queues) Q1, . . . , Qn in series, where each site has an unlimited buffer
capacity and a gate in front of it. A stochastic process controls gate opening instants. When
the gate in front of Q j opens, all particles present in Q j instantaneously move to Q j+1

all particles present in Qn simultaneously leave the system. After a particles transfer, the
corresponding gate immediately closes again.
Two notable TSNs are the celebrated Jackson Tandem Networks (TJN) (Jackson, 1957,
1963, 1956; Chen & Yao, 2001) and the Asymmetric Simple Exclusion Process (ASEP)
(Derrida, 1998; Golinelli & Mallick, 2006; Bundschuh, 2002). In the TJN, the buffer capacity
of each site is unlimited, while the gate capacity (maximum number of particles that can pass
through the gate when it opens) is only a single particle. In the ASEP, both gate capacity
and buffer size are limited to a single particle only, causing blocking in particles movement
and system evolution. In deviation from the latter two TSNs, in the classical ASIP (Reuveni
et al., 2011, 2012a,b) both buffer size and gate capacity are unlimited, implying that the
“service” mechanism is equivalent to an unlimited batch-size service. In all three TSNs
indicated above, particles arrive only to the ﬁrst site according to a Poisson process and
propagate unidirectionally through the system. Gate j opens independently of the other
gates every exponential time with parameter μ j. The classical ASIP model was further
studied and analyzed in Reuveni et al. (2014), where occupancy probabilities and limit laws
were investigated. Lauber Bonomo and Reuveni (2019) calculated occupancy correlations in
the classical ASIP.
Boxma et al. (2016) extended the analysis of the ASIP model and investigated a gener-
alized ASIP, where the time intervals between successive gate openings are controlled by a
Markovian renewal process, and particles arrive to all sites during the time interval between
two successive gate openings. At a gate opening instant only a single gate opens, where gate
j opens with probability p j, n
j=1 p j = 1. The emphasis in Boxma et al. (2016) is on
the derivation of the probability generating functions (PGFs) of site occupancies. The same
authors extended the investigation of Boxma et al. (2016) and derived the systems’ workload
distributions Boxma et al. (2021). A further analysis of a generalized ASIP (G-ASIP) with
arrivals to all sites was performed recently in Yeger and Yechiali (2022a), where a novel
and efﬁcient matrix formulation approach to calculate the joint PGF of site-occupancies was
developed, replacing the tedious successive substitution method used in all previous stud-
ies. Performance measures of a G-ASIP were studied in Yeger and Yechiali (2022b), where
explicit expressions for their distributions and means were obtained for various probability
distribution functions of the random intervals between successive gate openings.
As indicated in Reuveni et al. (2011) and in Boxma et al. (2016), the ASIP model is a
genericmodelthatmayrepresentmanydifferentstochasticprocessesinqueueing(e.g.,simul-
taneous movements of ships along a series of sluices, crowd movements through sections
in an amusement park, car peloton movements along a single-line road, etc.), in physics, in
chemistry, in biology, in production lines, and more. In particular, in Yeger et al. (2023) it was
shown that the ASIP model can serve as a mathematical representation of the microbiological
process of vesicular transport and transport of proteins between cells.
In this paper, we relax the ASIP and G-ASIP classical assumption of deterministic uni-
directional movement of particles between consecutive sites at gate opening instants, and
analyze the following three movement variants: (i) Unidirectional probabilistic forward
movements; (ii) Probabilistic forward movements combined with feedback to the ﬁrst site;
and (iii) General probabilistic multidirectional movements.

site j within the network as Q j, ( j = 1, 2, . . . , n). The random variable O represents the
time elapsing between two consecutive gate opening instants, while at each such instant,
gate j opens with probability p j. A j counts the random number of arrivals to site j during
the time interval between two consecutive gate openings. The multidimensional probability

2 Variants of particle movements in the G-ASIP
As indicated in the Introduction, in this paper we extend the research on G-ASIP networks
to system’s evolution cases not studied before. In the classical ASIP, when the gate in front
of site j ( j = 1, 2, . . . , n −1) opens all particles present in site j move instantaneously to
the subsequent site, site j + 1, or exit the system when j = n. In this paper, the following
three variants of particle movements at gate opeining instants are investigated:
1. Particle probabilistic unidirectional movements to all sites. That is, when gate j opens
( j = 1, 2, . . . , n ), all particles there move simultaneously with probability m jk to a
downstream site k, k ≥j, or leave the system with probability m j,n+1. This movement
scenario is depicted in Fig.1a for a 3-site network.
2. Probabilistic forward movements combined with feedback to the ﬁrst site. When gate j
opens ( j = 1, 2, . . . , n −1 ), all particles there move simultaneously either to site j + 1
(with probability m j) or fed back to site 1 (with probability 1 −m j). When the gate of
site n opens, particles either exit the system or fed back to the origin. Figure1b depicts
this movement variant for a 3-site network.
3. General probabilistic multidirectional movements to all sites. That is, when gate j opens
( j = 1, 2, . . . , n ), all particles there move simultaneously either to site k, k = 1, 2, . . . , n
(with probability m jk), or exit the system (with probability m j,n+1). This movement is
depicted in Fig.1c for a 3-site G-ASIP network.
The ﬁrst two types of movement dynamics delineate the operational characteristics of a
manufacturing assembly, wherein subsequent to each phase, the batch undergoes a quality
assessment. The ﬁrst variant describes a manufacturing assembly where, upon completing a
step in the production process, the batch is permitted to progress one or more steps ahead,

3 Unidirectional G-ASIP with probabilistic movements to all sites
Consider an n-site G-ASIP network, where gate openings are governed by a renewal process.
At a gate opening instant only a single gate opens and it is gate j with probability p j > 0,

j=1 p j = 1. The gate inter-opening intervals are i.i.d random variables, all distributed
like O. During a gate inter-opening interval O, arrivals occur at all sites Q1, Q2, . . . , Qn
according to a general arrival process. Let ˆA(z1, z2, . . . , zn) denote the joint PGF of the
number of arrivals to the various sites during an inter-opening interval. The complexity of
the model (see Reuveni et al. (2012a), Boxma et al. (2016)) allows us to derive explicit
solutions for the site occupancies joint PGF of small size networks only. For the general
n-site system, we derive a matrix formulation to calculate the mean site occupancies and
present a direct recursive formula for that calculation. As a numerical example, we consider
a homogeneous G-ASIP with n = 10 sites and calculate explicitly the system’s mean site
occupancies.

7 Conclusion
In this study, we extend the analysis of the n-site Asymmetric Simple Inclusion Process
(ASIP) network (introduced in Reuveni et al. (2011)) to a generalized ASIP (G-ASIP) frame-
work, where particles arrive at all sites, and move in all directions at gate-opening instants,
including feedback, and gate opening intervals are controlled by a renewal process. Unlike
previous approaches relying on tedious successive substitution processes, we apply a more
efﬁcient matrix approach to derive the multi-dimensional probability generating functions
(PGFs) of site occupancies and their means.
Three variants of particle movements are investigated: (i) unidirectional probabilistic
forward movements; (ii) probabilistic forward movements combined with feedback to the ﬁrst
site; and (iii) general probabilistic multidirectional movements. Explicit results for mean site
occupancies are derived for n-site networks, along with PGFs for small-size systems. Notably,
in the case of homogeneous networks with equal probabilities of forward movements, we
show that the ratio between the mean occupancies of consecutive sites converges to a constant
as the number of sites grows large.
Furthermore, we explore an n-site network where all gates open simultaneously at gate
opening instants, allowing particles to move in all directions. We establish that the mean site
occupancies vector satisﬁes E[ ⃗X] = (I −P)−1P · E[ ⃗A], where E[ ⃗X] represents the mean
site occupancy vector, P is the routing probability matrix corresponding to the general case,
and E[ ⃗A] is the vector of mean arrivals to the various sites during a random inter-opening
interval O. Numerical examples are provided to illustrate the theoretical results.
This study offers further insights into the dynamics of generalized ASIP networks. It
presents efﬁcient analytical tools for analyzing complex particle movement phenomena.
The ﬁndings contribute to a deeper understanding of the stochastic processes driving such
network-type systems.
