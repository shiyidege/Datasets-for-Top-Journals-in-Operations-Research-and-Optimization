Abstract
This paper examines the relationship between the ’¼ünancial costs associated with carbon emis-
sions and the intensity of green revenues relative to emissions and ’¼ürm-level default risk.
Contributing to the ongoing debate on emissions measurement approaches, we construct two
novel intensity measures that capture the monetary value of emissions: average carbon cost
(Cost/GHG) and green-revenue intensity (GR/GHG). Using global ’¼ürm-level data from 2008
to 2022, we ’¼ünd that exposure to carbon costs is strongly associated with increased default
risk, while the evidence for green-revenue intensity is more modest and does not translate
into a clear reduction in market-implied default probabilities. On average, a $1,000 increase
in carbon cost per tonne of emissions is associated with a 0.70 percentage point rise in the
probability of default and a reduction in Distance-to-Default, and these relationships remain
robust when absorbing time-varying industry cycles and country-level macro and policy con-
ditions. The association between carbon-cost exposure and default risk is most pronounced
in high transition-risk sectors and is weaker in ’¼ürms with higher managerial ability and oper-
ational ef’¼üciency. Overall, the results highlight the importance of incorporating monetary
valuations of transition exposures when assessing climate-related credit risk.

2 Carbon intensity and default risk
2.1 Capturing transition carbon risk
A central challenge in ’¼ünancial markets is accurately pricing transition risks associated with
the shift toward a low-carbon economy. Firms increasingly face pressures to transparently
disclose their carbon footprints and associated risks, prompting researchers and ’¼ünancial
practitioners to critically evaluate which metrics best capture these risks. The primary debate
has revolved around two metrics: absolute carbon emissions and carbon intensity.
Absolute carbon emissions, direct measures of total greenhouse gases produced (Scopes
1, 2, and 3), are extensively employed in regulatory disclosures and investor assessments
due to their simplicity and alignment with global climate targets like the Paris Agreement
(Bolton & Kacperczyk, 2021; Awijen et al., 2024; Engelmann, 2025). However, using raw
emissions alone neglects critical ’¼ünancial context, such as ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ capacity to absorb the
economic impacts of compliance and transition-related costs (Lioui & Misra, 2023). High-
emitting ’¼ürms with strong ’¼ünancial positions may face less transition risk than smaller ’¼ürms
with lower emissions but weaker ’¼ünancial health.
Alternatively, carbon intensity normalises emissions by revenue or market value, thereby
embedding a ’¼ünancial context. This metric better highlights a ’¼ürmŌĆÖs relative ef’¼üciency and
’¼ünancial capacity to manage regulatory and market-driven costs associated with decarboni-
sation (Bolton & Kacperczyk, 2023).
Lioui and Misra (2023) critically examine the implications of these different metrics,
demonstrating that methodological choices signi’¼ücantly affect interpretations of carbon pre-
miums. They highlight that value-weighted carbon factors often suggest a negative premium,
whereas emissions-weighted factors consistently yield positive premiums due to differences
in leverage and the treatment of ’¼ürm size. These discrepancies underline the complexity
in capturing transition risks accurately and emphasize the importance of selecting metrics
aligned with ’¼ünancial outcomes and risk management purposes.
This paper uses Cost/GHG as a proxy, linking emissions directly to ’¼ünancial impacts.
Unlike emissions or intensity alone, Cost/GHG explicitly quanti’¼ües economic burdens asso-
ciated with regulatory compliance and carbon pricing, providing a direct link to ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ cash
’¼éow and default risks. This ’¼ünancial integration makes Cost/GHG a dynamic and practical
metric for capturing transition risk across diverse regulatory environments and economic
cycles, addressing methodological issues identi’¼üed by previous research (Lioui & Misra,
2023). Figure 1 shows how our metric shows low correlation to the traditional metrics found
in literature. Consistent with this visual evidence, Table 12 reports uniformly low correlations
with total emissions and conventional carbon-intensity metrics, indicating that our measure
captures a distinct dimension of ’¼ürm-level exposure.
Overall, the choice of metric substantially shapes the understanding and measurement
of transition risks. While absolute emissions and carbon intensity provide valuable insights,
integrating ’¼ünancial outcomes through Cost/GHG may offer enhanced robustness and greater
’¼ünancial relevance, particularly in assessing ’¼ürm-level default probabilities during the tran-
sition to a low-carbon economy.

2.3 Marginal abatement cost theory
Theoretically, our approach mirrors the theory of marginal abatement cost curves. The theory
posits that climate mitigation resources should be allocated where the cost of abating the next
tonne of GHG emissions are lowest. This framework has been used by scholars (e.g., Ellerman
and Decaux, 1998; Criqui et al., 1999) who mention that MAC curves can analyze the
economic impacts of emissions trading under the Kyoto Protocol. MAC-based analysis can
reveal gains from emissions trading, showing that broader, less constrained carbon markets
reduce overall mitigation costs.
More recently, Martinsson et al. (2024) mention that the gains from abating an additional
unit of emissions are shaped by carbon costs, but these bene’¼üts must be evaluated relative to
the costs of abatement, which vary across ’¼ürms and industries due to differences in produc-
tion technologies and other sector-speci’¼üc factors. Because direct information on marginal
abatement costs (MAC) at the ’¼ürm is unavailable (Gillingham & Stock, 2018), we rely on
measures of pollution abatement and control expenditures (PACE) as an indirect indicator
similarly with Martinsson et al. (2024). Assuming that abatement cost functions are upward
sloping and convex, higher levels of cost per GHG imply correspondingly higher marginal
abatement costs.
The concept of evaluating a ’¼ürmŌĆÖs cost per unit of emissions is closely aligned with MAC
theory. MAC represents the cost incurred to reduce one additional unit of emissions, providing
a framework to compare mitigation options across economies. Similarly, focusing on a ’¼ürmŌĆÖs
per-unit emission costs emphasizes the heterogeneity in abatement costs driven by differences
in production technologies, processes, and industry-speci’¼üc factors. Both approaches prior-
itize cost-effectiveness, as reductions are most ef’¼üciently achieved where the cost of abating
an additional unit is lowest. Under MAC theory, ’¼ürms with lower marginal costs undertake
greater reductions, while higher-cost ’¼ürms abate less, mirroring the logic of allocating cli-
mate ’¼ünance to ’¼ürms based on their per-unit costs. In our empirical setting, the revenue-side
measure is constructed as green revenues per tonne of emissions (GR/GHG). This is not a
marginal ŌĆ£bene’¼üt of emissionsŌĆØ in the MAC sense. Rather, it captures green-revenue intensity
conditional on the emissions footprint, and therefore provides a complementary indicator of
transition positioning that can be evaluated alongside carbon-cost exposure.
Analogous to the MAC theory, which captures the cost of an additional tonne of emis-
sions, green sales per tonne captures green-revenue intensity per emission. Firms with higher
GR/GHG generate more green revenue for each unit of emissions, which can be interpreted

2.4 The role of regulatory exposure
A central component of our analysis is to test whether the effects of these environmental
variables, Cost/GHG and GR/GHG, are mediated by the regulatory environment in which
a ’¼ürm operates. This follows a growing body of work demonstrating that the materiality of
environmental risk depends heavily on the policy setting. In Dumrose and H├Čck (2023), ’¼ürms
in jurisdictions with stringent climate regulations face signi’¼ücantly wider credit spreads if
they exhibit poor carbon performance, suggesting that the regulatory environment ampli’¼ües
the ’¼ünancial consequences of environmental exposure. Similarly, evidence from South Korea
shows that after the introduction of a carbon trading scheme, default risk rose substantially
for carbon-intensive ’¼ürms, particularly those with weak governance or foreign ownership

of Pastor etal. (2021), who argue thatuncertainty surrounding governmentpolicy can increase
’¼ünancial volatility and systemic risk, especially for ’¼ürms holding carbon-intensive assets. In
line with this, we hypothesise that in regulated industries, Cost/GHG may act as a ’¼ünancial
burden, while in unregulated sectors, it may serve as a proxy for voluntary environmental
action or strategic readiness, leading to differential effects on default risk.
These dynamics are not purely speculative. As shown in Bauer and Hann (2010), ’¼ürms
with proactive environmental policies are perceived as less risky by credit rating agencies,
while ’¼ürms with unresolved environmental liabilities face rating downgrades and higher
spreads. The implication is clear, carbon cost exposures become riskier when external regu-

this context, our analysis tests whether the relationship between Cost/GHG and default risk
depends on regulatory context, offering insights into how transition policy risk is priced into
credit markets.

2.5 Internal capabilities as risk moderators
The second channel we explore relates to ’¼ürm-level capabilities that may condition how envi-

are understood as internal assets that enhance a ’¼ürmŌĆÖs capacity to manage environmental
risk. Drawing from the resource-based view and the natural-resource-based view of the ’¼ürm
(Barney, 1991; Hart, 1995), it is expected that ’¼ürms with superior internal capabilities are
better positioned to navigate regulatory shifts, reduce carbon dependencies, and innovate in
low-carbon markets.
We adopt measures developed by Demerjian et al. (2012), where managerial ability (MA)
is derived as the residual from a ’¼ürm ef’¼üciency score after controlling for ’¼ürm size, com-
plexity, and market share. Operational ’¼ürm ef’¼üciency (FEff), meanwhile, captures how well
a ’¼ürm converts inputs into revenues, and is constructed using Data Envelopment Analysis

Bonsall et al. (2017)), are highly predictive of creditworthiness. More capable managers and
more ef’¼ücient ’¼ürms are likely to anticipate regulatory burdens, allocate capital prudently,
and invest strategically in green initiatives, thus mitigating the impact of Cost/GHG and
enhancing the risk-reducing effects of GR/GHG.
Indeed, ’¼ürms with higher managerial ability tend to exhibit lower borrowing costs in
the presence of carbon risk (Jung et al. (2018)), as they are more likely to disclose envi-
ronmental data and adopt mitigation strategies. Likewise, Bansal and Clelland (2004) show
that ’¼ürms with strong environmental legitimacy, often tied to capable leadership, face lower
unsystematic risk, supporting the notion that managerial quality buffers default risk under
environmental stress. These ’¼ündings motivate our investigation into the interaction between
MA, FEff, and environmental exposures, and whether internal ’¼ürm characteristics condition
the credit relevance of transition risk.
In this framework, we expect that Cost/GHG may increase perceived default risk more
acutely for ’¼ürms with weak capabilities, while capable ’¼ürms may absorb or even leverage
environmental cost pressures strategically. Conversely, the positive effect of GR/GHG in
lowering default risk may be strongest in ’¼ürms that can operationalise green strategies through
effective management or high productivity. Our empirical analysis of these channels allows
us to test whether the observed relationships between environmental exposures and default
risk are inherent to these variables, or if they depend on the ’¼ürmŌĆÖs ability to adapt, absorb,
or capitalise on transition dynamics.

4.3 Quantile regression results
To better understand how carbon-related ’¼ünancial exposures affect different segments of the
’¼ürm-level risk distribution, we estimate quantile regressions for two default risk measures:
Distance-to-Default (D2D) and its implied probability of default (D2D PD). This approach
allows us to investigate whether the effects of Cost/GHG and GR/GHG differ across ’¼ürms
with varying levels of ’¼ünancial vulnerability, ranging from low-risk (higher D2D, lower
D2D PD) to high-risk (lower D2D, higher D2D PD) pro’¼üles. All speci’¼ücations include
the full set of controls and absorb ’¼ürm and year ’¼üxed effects using a two-step (within-
transformation) procedure. All speci’¼ücations include the full set of controls and are estimated
across the 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, and 90th percentiles of each outcome variable. Quantiles
are de’¼üned using the pooled ’¼ürm-year distribution of each dependent variable (D2D or
D2D PD) within the relevant estimation sample, rather than being re-de’¼üned separately
within each calendar year. This implies that years with more ’¼ürm-year observations receive
greater weight in determining the empirical quantile cut points and in the estimation of each
quantile regression. To address the concern that earlier years may be overrepresented when
using the pooled sample, we report an early versus late split for Cost/GHG in Table 22.The
estimation sample for GR/GHG begins in 2016 and runs through 2022, so the quantiles are
constructed over a relatively short and balanced time window, reducing concerns that early
years disproportionately in’¼éuence the quantile estimates.
Results for Cost/GHG are shown in Table 5. Across D2D quantiles, the estimated effects
of Cost/GHG are negative, with the most pronounced and statistically signi’¼ücant estimate at
the median (Q50). At Q50, a one-unit increase in Cost/GHG (in $000 per tonne of CO2) is
associated with a 0.037 reduction in D2D (p<0.01). The coef’¼ücients at Q10, Q25, Q75, and
Q90 are also negative but not statistically signi’¼ücant. These ’¼ündings suggest that carbon-
cost exposure is most clearly re’¼éected in the solvency buffer of ’¼ürms in the middle of the
distribution, while effects are weaker at the tails.
For D2D PD, the effects of Cost/GHG are positive and statistically signi’¼ücant across
all reported quantiles. Coef’¼ücients are tightly clustered around 0.004ŌĆō0.005 (all signi’¼ücant
at the 1% level), indicating a broadly robust association between higher carbon costs and
higher market-implied default probabilities throughout the risk distribution. These results
are consistent with the notion that carbon pricing and regulatory compliance burdens dispro-
portionately affect ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ perceived default risk, even outside the most distressed segment of
’¼ürms.
The results for GR/GHG are more stable but subtler in magnitude. Across the D2D quan-
tiles, we ’¼ünd that GR/GHG is positively associated with D2D and statistically signi’¼ücant
from Q10 through Q90, with coef’¼ücients around 0.002 (signi’¼ücant at conventional levels at
all quantiles). This suggests that green revenue generation per unit of emissions is associated
with improved ’¼ünancial standing across most of the solvency distribution.
In contrast, the effects of GR/GHG on D2D PD are generally weak. Point estimates

We ’¼ünd that ’¼ürm-level environmental exposures, particularly the carbon-related costs and
green revenue opportunities, are associated with default risk during the transition to a low-
carbon economy, in line with MAC theory. Regulatory measures aimed at addressing climate
changemaythereforebere’¼éectedin’¼ürm-levelcreditconditions,withthepotentialforadverse

We interpret these estimates as conditional associations after controlling for ’¼ürm charac-
teristics and ’¼üxed effects. Establishing causal effects would require exogenous variation in
carbon-cost exposure or green-revenue eligibility (e.g., regulatory discontinuities or quasi-
experimental policy changes), which lies beyond the scope of this study.
