Abstract
Project control, as an iterative process crucial for managing project uncertainties, demands
continual adaptation throughout the project’s lifespan. Activity duration unpredictability,
inherents in project networks, poses challenges to meeting deadlines. In response, project
managers collect real-time data and employ corrective actions to ensure timely and budget-
compliant project delivery. Despite extensive research on project control methodologies,
explicit methods for determining the optimal size of corrective actions in stochastic envi-
ronments have received limited attention. This study addresses this gap by integrating
probabilistic optimization models into project control strategies, emphasizing the minimiza-
tion of corrective action costs. The model ensures that a speciﬁed percentile of the project
duration distribution aligns with a predeﬁned deadline, offering a probability of meeting the
promised project duration. Two control strategies are proposed: preventive, involving a one-
time optimization before project initiation, and protective, with continuous re-optimization
at speciﬁc tracking periods during project execution. Computational experiments on artiﬁ-
cial datasets reveal that protective strategies outperform preventive strategies, showing faster
convergence and reduced variability in project duration distribution. Results indicate that, on
average, a minimum of 6 re-optimizations during project execution is required to minimize
both the cumulative cost of corrective actions and the average deviations between project
duration realizations and the promised deadline.

1 Introduction
Project management encounters the inevitable challenge of navigating uncertainty inherent
within project networks. Central to this challenge is the domain of project control, a dynamic
process characterized by ongoing monitoring and adaptation throughout a project’s lifespan.
Throughout the project’s lifecycle, real-time data must be gathered, offering insights into its
actual state. This information empowers project managers to take corrective actions, steering
the project back on course for timely and budget-compliant delivery. Yet, the inherent unpre-
dictability of activity durations can disrupt project schedules and undermine their adherence
to deadlines, demanding innovative approaches for project control and corrective action pro-
cess. In this study, we aim to address the issues that have received relatively less attention
within the realm of project management and control.
Although researchers have extensively explored various methodologies for project control,
particularly focusing on corrective action processes, there has been limited exploration of
explicit methods for determining the size of corrective action to align with project deadlines
in projects with stochastic activity durations. The current research is centered around inte-
grating probabilistic optimization models into project control strategies designed speciﬁcally
to accommodate the unpredictable nature of project networks, which makes it well-suited to
tackle uncertainties and optimize corrective action process. More precisely, it directly inte-
grates time, cost, and uncertainty in optimizing corrective actions, recognizing the interplay
between these elements and offers a dynamic approach with adaptive decision-making. The
optimization aspect, with the speciﬁc goal of achieving cost-efﬁciency while ensuring timely
project delivery sets this research apart in the realm of project control.
The contribution of this study is threefold. First, a probabilistic optimization model is
introduced to optimize the size of the corrective action process with an explicit focus on
minimizing the costs associated with the corrective actions. Simultaneously, it ensures that a
speciﬁed percentile of the project duration distribution adheres to a predeﬁned deadline, guar-
anteeing a certain probability of meeting the promised project duration. Such probabilistic
optimization models must not only be adaptive and agile but also capable of responding effec-
tively to real-world variations and disruptions, acknowledging the unpredictability inherent
in project environments. Second, two distinct project control strategies are proposed. The
preventive control strategy involves a one-time probabilistic optimization executed before
project initiation, while the protective control strategy engages in continuous probabilistic re-
optimization at speciﬁc tracking periods. During these periods, corrective actions are adapted
based on updated project information, thus allowing for alignment with the evolving project
environment. Finally, the study delves into the cost-efﬁciency implications of these strategies,
examining factors such as network topological structure and diverse levels of deadline adher-
ence. Our ultimate objective is to provide insights for practitioners in making data-driven
decisions during project control.
The structure of this paper can be outlined as follows: Section 2 provides an overview
of prior research and key contributions in the relevant body of literature. Section 3 details
the proposed methodology, while Section 4 focuses on the conducted experiments and their
outcomes. Lastly, the research conclusions are summarized in Section 5.

2.1 Stochastic project networks
Inpracticalprojectmanagementscenarios,therealizationthattheactualdurationsofactivities
often deviate from deterministic values in the baseline schedule has prompted the adoption of
stochastic project networks. These networks employ stochastic random variables and prob-
ability distributions to capture the inherent uncertainty associated with activity durations.
Departing from deterministic approaches reliant on ﬁxed values for activity and project
durations, stochastic project networks acknowledge the intrinsic variability and randomness
prevalent in real-world project environments. Within this stochastic framework, the project
duration is conceptualized as a stochastic variable, its distribution determined by the under-
lying distributions of activity durations. One prominent technique used in stochastic project
management, particularly for estimating activity durations, is the Program Evaluation and
Review Technique (PERT, Fazar, 1959), which accounts for uncertainty by representing activ-
ity durations as probability distributions and makes use of three time estimates (optimistic,
most likely and pessimistic) to model the uncertainty associated with activity durations.
Various methodologies, including exact methods, simulations, and analytical approxi-
mations, are employed to determine the project duration distribution. Vaseghi et al. (2024)
provides a comprehensive overview of these methods and clariﬁes that the choice between
an exact or an approximation approach is based on the complexity index 1 (CI) of the network
(Bein et al., 1992). Among the analytical approximation methods are bounding approaches,
which propose either lower or upper bounds on the project duration distribution. In studies
conducted by Shogan (1977); Colajanni et al. (2000) and Ludwig et al. (2001), various com-
parisons between different lower and upper bounds aim to identify the tightest bounds that
exhibit minimal deviations from the actual project duration distribution. Departing from a
singular deterministic value for project duration, determining the project duration distribu-
tion empowers project managers to undertake a statistical and comprehensive risk analysis.
Vaseghi et al. (2024) introduced an analytical procedure that explores how individual activ-
ities inﬂuence the overall project duration distribution proﬁle, integrating project network
structure and activity duration distribution information.
2.2 Project control and corrective action process
Throughout the project execution, where uncertainties in activity durations and potential
deviations from planned timelines are inevitable, the role of project control becomes appar-
ent. Project control encompasses the continuous monitoring of actual project progress and the
collection of real-time information from completed project segments. This practice equips
project managers with crucial insights into both the challenges and opportunities unfolding
within the project and serves as a mechanism to ascertain whether the project adheres to its
schedule and budget constraints. In instances where the project lags behind its schedule, par-

ticularly in high-risk areas, taking corrective actions becomes imperative. The main objective
of project control and the corrective action process is to ensure the timely delivery of the
project within the predeﬁned budgetary limits. This is particularly vital due to the inherent
uncertainties and unforeseeable changes in activity durations, which can lead to deviations
from the baseline schedule and the established deadline. The corrective action process serves
a diverse set of purposes, and its implementation involves various strategies, such as activity
crashing, variability reduction, and fast tracking. Notably, the literature, including studies
by Martens and Vanhoucke (2019); Song et al. (2020) and Vaseghi and Vanhoucke (2023)
extensively provide a comprehensive overview of these types of corrective actions.
Project control mechanisms can be implemented through two distinct strategies: the
project-level and activity-level approaches. The project-level approach involves a comprehen-
sive analysis of overall project performance during each tracking period, initiating corrective
actions if performance falls below a predeﬁned threshold, particularly focusing on critical
activities. This top-down approach, deﬁned by Vanhoucke (2011), is exempliﬁed by tech-
niques such as earned value management (EVM, Fleming and Koppelman, 2010), which
assesses project performance based on established metrics, emphasizing planned versus
actual progress while considering both cost and schedule aspects. However, despite its com-
prehensive view, EVM lacks the capacity to address uncertainties. Studies by Hu et al. (2016);
Martens and Vanhoucke (2019) and Song et al. (2020) have employed the top-down approach
as the project control strategy. The bottom-up or activity-level approach, also deﬁned by Van-
houcke (2011), involves identifying and evaluating activities during each tracking period,
relying on sensitivity metrics and information obtained from schedule risk analysis (SRA,
Hulett, 1996). Corrective actions are taken if the sensitivity metrics surpass a predetermined
threshold. While excelling in mitigating speciﬁc risks and uncertainties within the project,
the bottom-up approach does not directly integrate time and cost in control strategies, limit-
ing comprehensive project oversight. Studies by Vanhoucke (2010); Song et al. (2021) and
Vaseghi and Vanhoucke (2023) have employed the bottom-up approach as the project control
strategy. These contrasting approaches display the trade-offs and considerations involved in
selecting an appropriate control strategy based on the project’s characteristics and objectives.
Analytical ranking procedure and creation of action set An illustrative example of the bottom-
up project control approach is embodied in the study by Vaseghi et al. (2024). In this study,
an analytical risk analysis is introduced, systematically investigating the impact of speciﬁc
action types on the activity duration distribution and, consequently, on the broader project
duration distribution. Subsequently, activities will be ranked based on their impact on project
duration distribution, forming a hierarchical list from the most to least inﬂuential. The top x
activities in this ranking construct the action set, encompassing the most sensitive activities
for potential corrective actions during project execution. During project execution, ongoing
activities are regularly assessed, and if they align with the action set while displaying delays
beyond planned durations, corrective actions are promptly taken. At the conclusion of each
tracking period, a recalibration of the risk analysis is executed to reﬂect updated information,
thereby reﬁning the ranking list and adjusting the action set. This dynamic approach ensures
that the action set evolves, capturing both ongoing activities subject to immediate actions
and yet-to-commence activities marked for future corrective actions. The foresight provided
by a predeﬁned action set enables project managers to anticipate and prepare for forthcom-
ing positive and negative events, concentrating their efforts on the most sensitive activities.
This methodology offers an adaptive approach to project control, aligning with the inherent
uncertainties of project environments and facilitating proactive management strategies.

2.3 Research gap and rationale for the present study
While past investigations into project control implicitly consider the imperative of meeting
project deadlines, a distinct gap emerges in the absence of an explicit procedure to guaran-
tee the realization of these deadlines in stochastic project networks. Existing methodologies
predominantly rely on analytical procedures or simulation techniques to identify the most
sensitive activities for corrective actions, accompanied by predetermined action thresholds
that prompt corrective actions to reduce project duration and/or variability. Furthermore, sev-
eral research endeavors have employed optimization techniques within the realm of project
control, employing methodologies such as stochastic optimization models, dynamic pro-
gramming and optimal control theory. These approaches aim to determine the optimal timing
and frequency of project control points throughout the project life cycle, emphasizing the
strategic importance of timing for effective control (Falco and Macchiaroli, 1998; Hazir and
Schmidt, 2013; Kogan et al., 2002; Partovi and Burton, 1993; Raz and Erel, 2000). The
primary objective of these investigations is to propose strategic frameworks for identifying
the most opportune instances for project control interventions, with the goal of enhancing
project performance. Hazir (2015) offers a comprehensive overview of these studies and their
applications.
This study presents a pioneering methodology that integrates optimization techniques
for determining the optimal size of corrective actions given uncertainties and variability
in stochastic environments. Departing from a purely analytical approach, a probabilistic
optimization model is formulated with the objective of minimizing costs associated with
corrective action processes. Simultaneously, it ensures a predetermined probability of meet-
ing the project deadline. This probability reﬂects that a speciﬁed percentile of the project
duration distribution aligns with the committed project duration. The outcome of the opti-
mization process is an optimal action set, representing candidate activities for corrective
action along with their optimal sizes. Employing optimization in this context by integrat-
ing the time, cost, and uncertainty elements, yielding a pre-established, optimized action
set serves as a comprehensive and proactive strategy for project control and enhances the
precision of decision-making in stochastic project environments. Further, while conventional
methods assume deterministic or random distribution of corrective action impacts on activity
durations, our approach involves adjusting the original activity duration distribution through
the implementation of speciﬁc corrective actions (Vaseghi et al., 2024). The impact of these
actions is then evaluated comprehensively across the entire project duration distribution pro-
ﬁle. This methodology mitigates the oversight of inherent variability and uncertainty linked
to the broader implications of corrective actions on the overall project duration.

3.1 Probabilistic optimization model
In this study, a probabilistic optimization model is formulated tailored to stochastic project
networks that integrates time, cost, and uncertainty facets. The development of such a model
stems from the intrinsic and inescapable uncertainty and variability prevalent in activity
durations within stochastic project environments. The core objective of this optimization
strategy is twofold. First, to ascertain the optimal magnitude of corrective actions for activities
and second, to attain a delicate equilibrium between minimizing the costs associated with
the corrective action process and ensuring that a predetermined z-percentile of the project
duration realizations aligns with the project deadline. The output of this optimization model
is a pre-established and optimized action set, comprising candidate activities for corrective
actions, each assigned an optimal action size. This innovative model plays a pivotal role
in supporting project managers by developing a decision-making framework during project
control and corrective action process. By accounting for uncertainties and variability, it
provides a more informed perspective, aligning with project-speciﬁc objectives related to
cost-efﬁciency and meeting stringent deadlines. Table 1 summarizes the main parameters
employed in this optimization framework as a guideline for the next sections.
The probabilistic optimization model employed in this research employs stochastic project
networks to incorporate uncertainty elements. As outlined in Section 2.1, these networks
characterize activity durations through stochastic variables and distributions rather than deter-
ministic values. Consequently, the project duration is treated as a stochastic variable, not
conﬁned to a singular point estimate, with its distribution shaped by the network’s structure
and the activity duration distributions. In this study, the assumption is made that activity
durations exhibit right-skewed behavior, and their variability is captured by the lognormal
distribution. The choice of the lognormal distribution is rooted in its theoretical robustness
and empirical validation (Trietsch et al., 2012) and successful applications documented in
various project control studies (e.g., Song et al. (2020) and Bie et al. (2012)).

Determination of the project duration distribution The approach for establishing the project
duration distribution relies on the complexity index of project networks. For projects char-
acterized by a CI of 0, known as series-parallel graphs (SP graph), the exact approach is
employed. These networks are streamlined to a single arc through a series of series and
parallel reductions until only one activity remains, represented by a single arc (Bein et al.,
1992). The project completion time distribution for these SP graphs is then determined using
a sequence of convolution operators for series reductions and product operators for paral-
lel reductions. In contrast, for projects with a CI higher than 0, termed irreducible graphs,
analytical bounding methods are utilized to establish the tightest lower and upper bounds
on the actual project duration distribution. The bounding methods involve transforming the
network into a SP-graph, facilitating a more straightforward computation of the makespan
distribution, which results in an approximate makespan distribution. As validated by Vaseghi
et al. (2024), the pairwise disjoint chains method by Spelde (1976) and the duplication pro-
cess by Dodin (1985) are employed for determining the tightest lower and upper bounds,
respectively. The study by Vaseghi et al. (2024) provides a thorough overview of both exact
and approximation approaches for determining project completion time distribution.
Implementation of action
In this study, rather than assuming the impact of actions on activity duration to be deter-
ministic or randomly distributed, we modify the original activity duration distribution by
modeling corrective actions, considering the uncertainty and variability in their impact on
the entire project duration distribution. More precisely, the optimization model determines
the optimal size of actions for each activity by implementing the action on the activity dura-
tion distributions. Following Heyde (1963), it is argued that the geometric mean of a skewed
distribution (e.g., lognormal) provides a more accurate and robust representation of the cen-
tral tendency of data than arithmetic mean as it ﬁlters out the inﬂuence of extremely long
durations. Therefore, a particular type of action, identiﬁed as a form of activity crashing cor-
rective action, has been proposed by Vaseghi et al. (2024). This action is speciﬁcally designed
to explore the impact of decreasing the geometric mean μ of the activity duration distribu-
tion while leaving the geometric standard deviation σ unchanged (Eq. 1). This adjustment is
accomplished through the utilization of an additive model, which involves a leftward shift in
the underlying normal distribution of activity durations. Given that the reduction in geometric
mean μ inﬂuences both the lognormal distribution’s central tendency and shape, the trans-
formed distribution retains a lognormal pattern but with a decreased mean and data spread.

The subsequent sections describe the decision variables, objective function and constraint
of the probabilistic optimization model.
Decision Variables. In this model, decision variables correspond to the size of corrective
actions for individual activities within the project. As illustrated in Eq. 1, the parameter α
governs the extent of activity crashing, inﬂuencing the reduction in the geometric mean μ
of the activity duration distribution. Speciﬁcally, α, representing the size of corrective action
for each activity, forms a decision variable array denoted as α = [α1, α2, ..., αN], where N
is the total number of activities in the project network.
When α approaches 1, the reduction in μ is minimal, resulting in a marginal decrease in the
arithmetic mean m and standard deviation s of the activity duration distribution. Conversely,
when α is close to 0, the reduction in μ is substantial, leading to a signiﬁcant decrease
in both m and s. Consequently, the degree of reduction in activity duration parameters is
inﬂuenced by both the initial characteristics of activity duration and the value of α, giving
rise to varying costs associated with implementing actions on different activities. Note that
the model allows for the reduction of α to its minimal values. However, practical constraints
may limit achieving these minimal values, as complete elimination of all activity variability
may not be feasible.
Objective Function. The objective of our optimization model is to minimize the compre-
hensivecostlinkedwithcorrectiveactionsforeffectiveprojectcontrol.Ourapproachemploys
corrective actions to speciﬁcally reduce activity durations, with a dual aim of diminishing
both the mean and standard deviation of the activity duration distribution. Although the moti-
vation of our model is expressed in terms of reducing the geometric mean of the log-normal
duration distribution, the arithmetic mean–and thus the expected duration–scales propor-
tionally. Therefore, linking the cost to the arithmetic mean is a convenient and consistent
approximation that captures the practical relationship between corrective actions and their
associated costs. Further, the cost of action is modeled as a linear function of the reduc-
tion in the expected activity duration. This formulation follows the conventional time–cost
trade-off and activity-crashing literature, where the marginal cost per unit of time reduction is
assumed constant. In practice, crashing costs are often convex, reﬂecting diminishing returns;
however, the linear speciﬁcation provides a clear and tractable baseline that can easily be
extended to nonlinear cost structures if needed. This assumption allows for a practical and
intuitive connection between the anticipated behavior of activity durations and the ﬁnancial
implications of actions. Figure 2 visually demonstrates this correlation, emphasizing how the
cost of taking action on an activity increases with a decrease in the mean of activity duration
from the initial value of mi.

within the probabilistic optimization model. This optimization model can be interpreted as
a form of joint chance-constrained optimization. In this framework, the durations of project
activities are modeled as random variables following log-normal distributions, leading to a
stochastic representation of the total project duration. The decision variables, represented
by the vector α determine the magnitude of corrective actions applied to each activity and,
consequently, inﬂuence the overall distribution of project completion time. The objective is to
minimize the total cost of these actions while satisfying a probabilistic deadline requirement.
Speciﬁcally, the project must be completed before a speciﬁed due date δ with a conﬁdence
level not less than a target probability z. This constraint jointly depends on the random dura-
tions of all activities, as they collectively determine the distribution of the project completion
time. Therefore, the model naturally falls within the class of joint chance-constrained opti-
mization problems, where the feasibility condition is deﬁned in terms of a joint probability
over multiple sources of uncertainty.
3.2 Project control strategies
In this section, we present two distinctive project control strategies that will be implemented
in the computational experiments detailed in Section 4, aiming to assess the applicabil-
ity of the probabilistic optimization model introduced in Section 3.1. The ﬁrst strategy,
termed the preventive control strategy, entails a singular probabilistic optimization to fulﬁll
a predeﬁned project deadline. Subsequently, corrective actions are implemented based on
the initial optimization outcomes before the commencement of the project. Additionally,
an alternative approach, denoted as the protective control strategy, is introduced. Unlike
the one-time optimization in the preventive strategy, the probabilistic optimization model

value, resulting in diminished project variability and a mean project duration that closely
aligns with the project deadline. These control strategies will be elaborated in the subsequent
sections and visually depicted in Figure 5.
Preventive control strategy Following the construction of the baseline schedule in the schedul-
ing step and the deﬁnition of uncertainty for activity durations in the risk analysis step, it
becomes imperative to specify the objective function and constraint for the probabilistic
optimization model. Subsequently, as illustrated in Figure 5, the preventive control strategy
typically involves a singular optimization to determine the optimal size of corrective actions
for individual activities, establishing a pre-determined and optimized action set. Once the
action set is created, preventive actions are taken on activities before the project initia-
tion, ensuring that the speciﬁed percentile of the project duration distribution adheres to the
promised deadline with minimal associated costs. After implementing actions according to
the action set, this strategy employs the Monte Carlo simulation to simulate project progress,
and the project performance will be examined. The performance measures will be detailed
later in Eqs. 9 to 12 in Section 4.2.
Given that actions are taken before the start of the project and no actions are performed on
activities during project execution, the costs linked to corrective actions are incurred upfront
in this strategy. Furthermore, this strategy may not be adaptive and responsive to unexpected
123

Annals of Operations Research
changes or uncertainties during the project. The primary objective of one-time optimization
and the preventive strategy is the proactive addressing of uncertainties before the project starts
by optimizing corrective actions to establish a predetermined action set and implementing
the actions once. Therefore, for projects with relatively stable environments and well-known
uncertainties, and if uncertainties are relatively static, a one-time optimization might be
sufﬁcient.
Protective control strategy In the dynamic landscape of project control and within a prob-
abilistic project framework, the shift from uncertainty to certainty during project execution
holds crucial signiﬁcance. As the project advances and attains speciﬁc completion milestones,
the interplay between known facts and persistent uncertainties undergoes a transformative
shift. At each tracking period, accomplished and completed segments of the project and activ-
ities shift from probabilistic and variable states to ﬁxed and certain realities. These completed
activities are characterized by durations represented by distributions with a standard devia-
tion of 0 and a mean aligned with the actual duration. This convergence toward deterministic
values effectively brings the project duration closer to the predeﬁned deadline. Concur-
rently, ongoing and yet-to-commence activities remain enveloped in uncertainty, expressed
through probabilistic duration distributions. In response to this dynamic context, we propose
an alternative strategy, termed the protective control strategy, which introduces a dynamic
and adaptive approach by re-conducting the probabilistic optimization model.
After establishing the baseline schedule, deﬁning uncertainty for activity durations, and
specifying the objective function and constraints aligned with the project’s objectives, the
optimization process deviates from a one-time occurrence. Instead, the optimization is recur-
rent at predetermined tracking periods throughout project execution, ranging from 0% to
100% completion in 5% increments. During these intervals, the probabilistic optimization
model is re-performed, and the action set is adjusted, accordingly. The action set comprises
two categories: activities in progress, representing ongoing tasks eligible for immediate cor-
rective action, and not-commenced activities, included to proactively address uncertainties.
This dual-category approach ensures a comprehensive strategy for managing potential project
disruptions. Following each re-optimization, activities in progress are systematically evalu-
ated, and protective actions are exclusively applied to in progress activities within the action
set. At the conclusion of each tracking period, with actions implemented on eligible activ-
ities, the project data and decision variables of the optimization model are updated based
on reﬁned information from completed project segments. Notably, since actions are only
applied to activities in progress within the adjusted action set, costs associated with correc-
tive actions are incurred adaptively based on evolving conditions. The right part of Figure 5
shows the iterative nature of this periodic monitoring process, as the process will be repeated
until project is ﬁnished.
The proposed model can be solved efﬁciently through numerical optimization. The deci-
sion variables modify the parameters of the activity duration distributions, which in turn affect
the overall project duration distribution. The optimization seeks values of α that minimize
the total cost of action while ensuring that a given percentile of the resulting project dura-
tion distribution does not exceed the speciﬁed deadline. Because the mapping from α to the
project duration distribution involves nonlinear operations such as convolution and quantile
evaluation, the constraint is inherently nonlinear. In the preventive strategy, the optimization
is performed once using expected activity durations, whereas in the protective strategy, the
procedure is repeated over multiple stochastic realizations, and the average performance of
the resulting solution is evaluated.

4.3.3 Comparative analysis of preventive and protective strategies
In this section, a comprehensive comparative analysis is conducted to assess the performance
of preventive and protective strategies concerning deviation index and cost efﬁciency. This
assessment is conducted under diverse conditions, encompassing varied percentile choices
and network structures. The primary objective of this experiment is to determine the optimal
number of re-optimizations essential during project execution within the protective strategy,
with a focus on minimizing both the deviation index and cumulative costs linked to corrective
actions. The investigation will consider different percentiles of the project duration distribu-
tion, a crucial element of the probabilistic optimization model’s constraint, aiming to achieve
diverse levels of adherence to deadlines. The z-percentile constraint signiﬁes the proportion
of project durations expected to meet the project deadline, reﬂecting a willingness to tolerate
a certain degree of risk and uncertainty. Speciﬁcally, a higher percentile (e.g., 80% or 90%)
establishes a more stringent criterion, intensifying the challenge of achievement.

study aims to explore the impact of percentile selection, a fundamental factor in the opti-
mization model, on the average cumulative cost and average deviation of project duration
from the speciﬁed deadline across 1,000 realizations. In this experimental setup, we assume
a deadline equal to the mean project duration before taking any actions at project initiation,
with a percentile range spanning from 50% to 100%. Consequently, the probabilistic opti-
mization model is solved for various percentile choices, encompassing both preventive and
protective strategies. In the preventive strategy, actions are implemented before the project
initiation, whereas in the protective strategy, optimization is re-performed at each tracking
period based on the updated project state, and actions are applied only to ongoing activi-
ties that belong to the action set identiﬁed by the optimization model. Therefore, the total
cost of action accumulates over successive tracking periods as new information becomes
available during project execution. This formulation ensures that only relevant and currently
executable activities contribute to the cumulative cost, while allowing the optimization to

In summary, these outcomes demonstrate a clear trade-off between cost and performance
across the two control strategies. At low deadline-adherence levels, the preventive strategy
yields a slightly lower cumulative cost of action because fewer activities are adjusted; how-
ever, it also exhibits substantially higher failure deviations for realizations that exceed the
deadline. In contrast, the protective strategy, which re-optimizes during execution, achieves
markedly lower failure and overreaction indices, reﬂecting improved reliability and more
efﬁcient use of resources. For higher adherence levels, the protective strategy even results
in a lower cumulative cost than the preventive approach, since actions are targeted to ongo-
ing activities with updated information, avoiding unnecessary interventions. These ﬁndings
conﬁrm that periodic re-optimization enables project managers to maintain high schedule
reliability while keeping the total corrective cost minimal.

5 Conclusion
This study introduces a probabilistic optimization model, designed to address the inherent
unpredictability in project networks and integrated to the corrective actions process and
project control. By considering time, cost, and uncertainty factors, the model constructs
an action set, deﬁning both the activities and the optimal size of actions. These models are
customized to minimize corrective action costs while ensuring that a predetermined percentile
oftheprojectdurationdistributionalignswithprojectdeadlines.Twoprojectcontrolstrategies
have been proposed: the preventive strategy, involving a one-time optimization and action
implementation before project initiation, and the protective strategy, incorporating multiple
re-optimizations during project progress for adjusting the action set.
The subsequent experiments had a threefold purpose. First, to assess the relevance of the
probabilistic optimization model by exploring the impact of percentile choice and deadline on
project duration distribution and the cumulative cost of corrective actions. Second, to conduct
a comparative analysis between the proposed strategies, revealing that the protective strategy,
with data gathering and re-optimization during project progress, leads to faster convergence
and less variability after 25% completion of the project. Third, to investigate the impact
of different deadline adherence levels on the cumulative cost of corrective actions and the
averagedeviationbetweenactualprojectdurationsandpromiseddeadlines.Notably,thestudy

revealed that for low percentiles, the preventive strategy resulted in higher average deviations
for projects exceeding the deadline, while for high percentiles, it led to higher deviations for
projects meeting the deadline, termed as failure and overreaction, respectively. To mitigate
these issues, the study concluded that a minimum of 6 re-optimizations is necessary to
minimize both cumulative costs and failure/overreactions.
This study provides valuable insights for both academia and practitioners. Future research
could validate these concepts in practical settings using empirical data, particularly in high-
uncertainty project environments. Additionally, extensions of the model, such as constraints
on the number of implemented actions or alternative action types like variability reduc-
tion, warrant exploration. Furthermore, the study empowers project managers by aligning
strategies with project requirements, enabling adaptability, dynamic response and continuous
improvement through re-optimization. This adaptability is particularly beneﬁcial for projects
navigating dynamic and uncertain environments, allowing project managers to respond effec-
tively to changing circumstances and emerging information during the project’s course.
