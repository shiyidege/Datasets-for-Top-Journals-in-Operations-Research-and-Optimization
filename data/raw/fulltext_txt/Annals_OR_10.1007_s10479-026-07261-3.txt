Abstract
In the considered coupled task problem (CTP) we have to schedule n jobs on a single machine,
each consisting of two tasks with exact time delay between them, while the objective is to
minimize the total completion time of the jobs. We analyze a greedy type algorithm – called
SDF (Shortest Delay First) – from worst case point of view, and we give bounds for the
asymptotic behavior of SDF for the special case where each task has equal length processing
time p. For this case, the best-known upper bound on the asymptotic performance ratio
of algorithm SDF is 5

1 Introduction
The problem of scheduling coupled tasks with exact delays was ﬁrst published by Shapiro
in his pioneering paper (Shapiro, 1980). The practical problem he modeled was scheduling
a radar tracking system: a radar transmits pulses and their reﬂections are received. In the
time interval, while the system transmits pulses, it cannot receive any arriving reﬂected
pulse. These two radar activities are modeled as a pair of coupled tasks with a ﬁxed delay
between them. Several variants of this basic radar system were investigated over the last

Generally, one measure of the efﬁciency of heuristics is the performance ratio deﬁned
as follows. Let Jn be an instance of n items. Let A(Jn) and OPT(Jn) denote the objective
function values obtained by solving the instance Jn with the algorithm A and with an exact
algorithm, respectively. Algorithm A is called a ρ-approximation algorithm for some ρ ≥1
if
A(Jn) ≤ρ OPT(Jn)
for every instance of jobs. The smallest possible value of this ρ is called the worst-case
(performance) ratio of algorithm A, and is denoted by ρA.
For a long time, researchers have focused mainly on classic CTPs where the objective
is to minimize the maximum completion time called makespan. CTPs with other objective
functions were studied very sporadically until the paper by Chen and Zhang. In this paper,
a near-complete complexity overview was given for CTPs whose objective function is to
minimize the total sum of job completion times (see Chen and Zhang (2021)). This paper
redeﬁned the research on these types of problem, and within a few years several papers with
different complexity results were published, new polynomial algorithms were deﬁned, and
their approximation behavior was also studied (see Györgyi (2023); Békési et al. (2023b;
2023)).
Formally, in the CTP there is a list Jn = {1, 2, . . . , n} of n jobs (items) that must be
scheduled on a single machine. Each job consists of two tasks; the execution order of the
tasks is ﬁxed. The tasks of job i are denoted by ai and bi. To simplify the notation, we
will also denote the processing times of the two tasks by ai and bi; the meaning of these
notations will be clear from context. The start and completion times of the two tasks are
denoted by s(ai), s(bi), and c(ai), c(bi), respectively. For job i, the delay time between the
completion time of the ﬁrst task and the start time of the second task must be exactly Li, i.e.
s(bi) = c(ai)+ Li. The machine can execute at most one task at a time and preemption is not
allowed. A job is completed when both of its tasks are ﬁnished. This time is the completion
time of the job i, and is denoted by Ci. Obviously, Ci = c(bi). Let A be an algorithm, then
the makespan is C(A, Jn) = max1≤i≤n Ci and the sum of the completion times of the jobs
is T (A, Jn) = n

Let t1 and t2 be two time points where the machine is busy. The time period between t1
and t2 is an idle time if in the interval (t1, t2) the machine is idle. The length of an idle time
is the length of this time window.
In a given schedule σ, we say that job i starts earlier than job j if s(ai) < s(a j). Similarly,
job i ﬁnishes earlier than job j if c(bi) < c(b j). Job in σ is the j-th starting job if exactly
j −1 jobs start earlier, and it is the j-th ﬁnishing job if exactly j −1 jobs ﬁnish earlier.
We will say that in a schedule i and j are successive if s(bi) < s(a j), the jobs are
nested if s(ai) < s(a j) < s(b j) < s(bi), and the jobs are interleaved if s(ai) < s(a j) <
s(bi) < s(b j). The size of job i is size(i) = ai + Li + bi. To determine different CTPs,
we use the three-ﬁeld representation α|β|γ proposed by Graham et al. (1979) for machine
scheduling problems. α, β, and γ denote the machine environment, the job characteristics
and the objective function, respectively. For example, the problem considered by Shapiro can
be described by the following notation 1|(a j, L j, b j)|Cmax, and the CTP where the objective
is to minimize the total completion time, and the delay time L j is ﬁxed to a constant L for
each job, is denoted by 1|(a j, L, b j)|  C j.
2 Related works
The study of CTPs was initiated by Shapiro’s paper (Shapiro, 1980). He proved that classic
CTP is NP-hard in the general case. The next outstanding result was published by Orman
and Potts (1997). They investigated the problem from a complexity point of view and gave
an exhaustive analysis of the complexity status of classic CTPs with different processing and
delay times. They plotted the changes in complexity status using a directed graph. It turns out
that most problems – including very special cases – are NP-hard, and only a few problems
can be solved in polynomial time (see, e.g. Orman and Potts (1997); Blazewicz et al. (2001);
Tanas and Blazewicz (2007)). The analysis is almost complete; the so-called Identical CTP
– 1|(a, L, b)|Cmax – is the only exception for which the complexity status is not known. For
this problem, only pseudo-polynomial algorithms were published (Ahr et al., 2004; Baptiste,
2010).
For classic CTP, the ﬁrst approximation algorithm was deﬁned in Ageev and Baburin
(2007). They considered the UET problem, where the length of tasks is 1 for each job:
1|(1, L j, 1)|Cmax, and an upper bound of 7/4 on the asymptotic behavior of the algorithm
was proved. Békési et al. later proved in Békésietal. (2009) that the upper bound is sharp.
Another heuristic was deﬁned in Ageev and Kononov (2007) for the solution of the general
problem, and it was shown that their solution yields an approximation algorithm of 3.5. In
the same paper, additional algorithms were deﬁned and analyzed. The algorithm given for
the approximation solution of 1|(a j, L, b j), ai ≤bi|Cmax, was shown to yield an upper
bound of 3, and the heuristic deﬁned for problem 1|(a j, L, a j)|Cmax, was analyzed, and
an upper bound of 2.5 was given for the asymptotic behavior of the algorithm. In Ageev
(2016) further algorithms were analyzed and all provided upper bounds on the asymptotic
behavioroftheheuristicsconsidered.Thecomplexitystatusoftheproblem1|(1, L j, 1), L j ∈
{L1, L2}|Cmax is currently unknown. An algorithm for this problem was deﬁned in Békési
et al. (2022a) and it was proven that the worst-case performance ratio of the algorithm was
in the interval (1.5789, 1.5796).
Non-approximability results have also been obtained for tasks with restricted parameters.
In Ageev and Kononov (2007) it was proven that there is no polynomial time (2 −ε)-
approximation for the problem 1|(ai, Li, bi), ai = bi|Cmax unless P = NP. In Ageev

et al. (2020). They noted that, beyond classical CTP, very few studies have been done on
problems with different objective functions. This wall was broken down by Chen and Zhang,
who in Chen and Zhang (2021) gave a comprehensive complexity analysis for the problem
1|(a j, L j, b j)|  C j.

4 The technique we use in the worst case analysis
In the following, we ﬁrst describe the algorithm whose behavior we will analyze in the rest of
the paper. Then we outline the technique applied to analyze the algorithm. The main objective
of our analysis is to calculate a narrow interval for the asymptotic behavior of algorithm SDF.
The analysis can be divided into two parts.
Step 1. Set an upper bound on the worst-case ratio:
– As a ﬁrst step, we decrease the cardinality of the set of instances that must be investigated
while we calculate the upper bound. These instances are called dominant, and we prove
that it is sufﬁcient to test for the dominant instances to satisfy the upper bound. (Section
4.)
– Knowing the structure of the dominant instances, we examine some upper bounds that
can be given for each subformula required to compute the upper bound. (Subsections
5.1-5.3)
– Using these partial results, we give a closed formula for the upper bound on the asymptotic

8 Conclusions
In this paper, we investigated the CTP 1|(p, L j, p)|  C j. We analyzed a greedy type algo-
rithm which was ﬁrst presented in Györgyi (2023), and we gave asymptotic worst case ratios
of this algorithm for the case p ≥2. It is a natural question how can we improve the bounds
for ρSDF(p), p ≥2? It is also an obvious question how the SDF algorithm works with dif-
ferent parameters ai, Li, and bi. The next open question is also straightforward: what kind
of other approximation algorithms can be constructed for the problem 1|(p, L j, p)|  C j
with better asymptotic behavior?
