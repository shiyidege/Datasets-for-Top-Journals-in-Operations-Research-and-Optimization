Abstract
We investigate robust models for Expected Shortfall (ES) and Omega Ratio (OR) optimi­
zation under joint uncertainty in both the probability distribution and the threshold. We 
apply this approach to actively manage portfolios comprising U.S. industry indices. Our 
empirical analysis shows that the robust ES and OR portfolios significantly outperform 
the benchmark index and active alternative strategies, even after adjusting for risk and 
transaction costs. Additionally, our findings demonstrate that the proposed robust optimi­
zation shifts allocations away from defensive sectors toward high-performing industries, 
capitalizing on upside-only momentum exposure and asset mispricing. Through simula­
tion, we reveal that robust ES portfolios show pronounced advantages under high market 
volatility and cross-asset systematic risk variability, whereas robust OR portfolios benefit 
from low idiosyncratic volatility and notable asset mispricing. These findings underscore 
the effectiveness of robust ES and OR optimization in active portfolio management, high­
lighting their capacity to deliver strong performance and resilience under adverse market 
conditions.

1  Introduction
Modern portfolio theory, pioneered by Markowitz (1952), asserts that a risk-averse inves­
tor seeks to build a portfolio that optimally balances return (mean) and risk (variance). 
Despite its undeniable influence on financial theory, this approach has two notable limita­
tions. First, by assuming a Gaussian (normal) distribution of returns and using variance as 
the sole risk measure, the Markowitz approach may lead to suboptimal choices for assets 
with asymmetric or “heavy-tailed” risk profiles (see Harlow (1991); Cont (2001); Buckley 
et al. (2008); Le Courtois and Xu (2024)). Second, it assumes that expected returns and 
risks are either known or can be estimated with high precision, which is often not the case. 
In practice, portfolios are biased towards estimation errors (see Michaud (1989); DeMiguel 
et al. (2009); Chopra and Ziemba (2013); La Torre et al. (2024)). In this study, we address 
these challenges by exploring portfolio selection methods that account for non-Gaussian 
return distributions and estimation errors in return and risk.
We propose a portfolio optimization method tailored for active fund management. The 
active fund’s objective is to track and outperform (enhance) the benchmark, which may be 
a market or style index. To achieve this, the fund manager constructs a portfolio by over­
weighting some securities and underweighting others relative to the benchmark, aiming to 
capitalize on potential market inefficiencies.
We examine two approaches to fund management. In the first, the fund manager seeks to 
minimize portfolio risk relative to the benchmark index. In the second, the active strategy 
aims to balance outperforming the benchmark’s mean return while minimizing risk.1
In the first case, we minimize the portfolio’s Expected shortfall (ES). ES is a first-degree 
lower partial moment (LPM) that measures downside risk below the target return (thresh­
old). In the second case, we maximize the portfolio’s Omega ratio (OR). Proposed by Keat­
ing and Shadwick (2002) as an alternative to the mean-variance-based Sharpe ratio, OR is 
the ratio of the first-order upper partial moment and first-order LPM calculated at the same 
threshold. In our application, the ES and OR thresholds are set equal to the mean return of 
the benchmark index, ensuring consistency with the fund’s objective of outperforming the 
benchmark (see Guastaroba et al. (2016) and references therein).
ES and OR have important advantages. Unlike the variance and Sharpe ratio, ES and OR 
are model-free and apply to any probability distribution. They are consistent with second-
degree stochastic dominance (SSD) which applies to all risk-averse investors regardless 
of their specific preferences (Ogryczak and Ruszczynski, 2002).2 ES and OR are closely 
related to another popular family of coherent risk measures, called expectiles, which also 
distinguish upside and downside deviations (Taylor, 2022). Both ES and OR optimizations 
can be formulated as linear programming problems, ensuring their computational feasibility.

Since the true probability distribution is unknown, the portfolio is subject not only to 
measurable risk (e.g., volatility or tail risk) but also to unmeasurable risk, also known as 
Knightian uncertainty (Knight, 1921). Knightian uncertainty corresponds to situations in 
which some events do not have an obvious probability assignment. The experimental rel­
evance of the distinction between risk and uncertainty has been formally discussed by Ells­
berg (1961), whose findings have shown that agents are not always able to derive a unique 
probability distribution over possible scenarios.
In the context of portfolio selection, a risk-averse investor will construct minimum-risk 
portfolios whereas an uncertainty-averse investor will seek portfolios that are robust to 
uncertainties. Ignoring uncertainty can lead to suboptimal asset allocation and thus, a reduc­
tion of investors’ utility.3
ES and OR can be highly sensitive to uncertainty and estimation errors, as noted by 
Grootveld and Hallerbach (1999), Varga-Haszonits and Kondor (2008), Caccioli et al. 
(2018). Specifically, the estimation process for partial moments discards more data points 
compared to variance estimation, making the parameters estimated by these measures more 
vulnerable to estimation errors than those defined across the full data range.
One popular approach to address uncertainty is robust optimization (Ben-Tal et al., 2009; 
Bertsimas et al., 2011). First introduced by Mulvey et al. (1995) for large-scale systems, 
robust optimization was extended to multiple areas including portfolio optimization (see 
Georgantas et al. (2024) for a review). Intuitively, a goal of robust portfolio optimization is 
to seek the portfolio weights within the feasible set whose worst-case scenario realization is 
better than for any other portfolio.
Unlike the deterministic risk approach, where inputs are just point-estimate forecasts, 
robust portfolio optimization ensures the optimality of the solution for all elements in the 
prespecified uncertainty set. Uncertainty sets may be formed by the difference of expert 
opinions or alternative estimates of a probability distribution generated via statistical 
techniques. The size of the uncertainty set is typically determined by the level of desired 
robustness.
In our application, both the probability distribution of asset returns and the threshold 
(benchmark mean) are assumed to be unknown and are therefore treated as sources of uncer­
tainty. To address this, we propose a robust portfolio optimization framework that specifies 
a joint uncertainty set encompassing both the probability distribution and the threshold. 
In this study, we use the box uncertainty specification for the joint set that is analogous to 
the confidence interval in statistics. Box uncertainty is commonly used for robust portfolio 
optimization (e.g., Zhu et al. (2009); Kapsos et al. (2014); Kim et al. (2018); Liesiö et al. 
(2020); Sehgal et al. (2023)).
We apply the joint uncertainty approach to an industry rotation strategy, using 49 U.S. 
industry indices as the asset universe and a broad market index as the benchmark. Out-of-
sample performance analysis reveals that the robust ES and OR portfolios, optimized under 
joint uncertainty, substantially outperform the benchmark index, even after accounting for

Portfolio composition analysis shows that, under joint uncertainty, robust portfolios tend

Simulation analysis confirms that the advantages of the joint uncertainty approach are 
resilient across various data-generating processes, model parameters, and estimation errors. 
The simulations also reveal valuable insights into the drivers of robust portfolio perfor­
mance. Specifically, we find that the robust OR portfolio’s performance is sensitive to idio­
syncratic risk, with greater outperformance occurring under conditions of low idiosyncratic 
volatility and the presence of asset mispricing. Conversely, the robust ES portfolio’s perfor­
mance is influenced by the systematic risk of assets, with its advantages becoming particu­
larly evident under high market (benchmark) volatility and significant cross-asset variation 
in market risk.
We contribute to the growing literature on robust counterparts of non-Markowitz port­
folio selection approaches, including lower-partial moments (Zhu et al., 2009), the Omega 
ratio (Kapsos et al., 2014), Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR) 
(Lotfi and Zenios, 2018), and stochastic dominance (Liesiö et al., 2020). Our study extends 
this line of research by exploring an alternative specification of the uncertainty set in which 
both the probability distribution and the threshold are treated as uncertain.
To the best of our knowledge, it is the first to provide a comprehensive out-of-sample 
empirical analysis of the performance of robust ES and OR portfolios, using both real-world 
and simulated datasets.
Most studies investigating the OR optimization problem assume a constant threshold 
level—typically the risk-free rate or zero. For example, Kapsos et al. (2014) set the thresh­
old of their robust OR model to a fixed 3% per annum. Only a few exceptions in the lit­
erature relax this assumption by allowing the threshold to vary. Guastaroba et al. (2016) 
proposed the index-enhancing strategy based on OR where the threshold can be constant or 
stochastic. However, the Guastaroba et al. (2016) approach is not robust and does not take 
the uncertainties into account.
Sharma et al. (2017) redefined the robust OR problem for a loss-averse investor by taking 
the distribution-dependent threshold point as the Conditional Value-at-Risk of the bench­
mark. Sehgal et al. (2023) used the Value-at-Risk as the threshold point for the robust OR 
optimization. In Sharma et al. (2017) and Sehgal et al. (2023), the robustness is introduced 
both in the Omega measure and the threshold (CVaR and VaR, respectively).
According to Sharma et al. (2017) and Sehgal et al. (2023), using VaR and CVaR as 
the threshold in OR optimization is justified by the need to reflect individual investors’ 
risk preferences. This approach, however, has two major drawbacks. First, setting the OR 
threshold to an arbitrarily high percentile can result in a suboptimal portfolio choice that 
does not meet the SSD condition (Balder and Schweizer, 2017). This condition requires that 
the mean return of the optimal portfolio be at least as high as that of the benchmark. Second, 
the interpretation of the threshold in terms of risk appetite may not be valid if the probability 
distribution of portfolio returns is heavy-tailed (Balter et al., 2024).

Our approach to the robust OR model differs from those proposed by Sharma et al. 
(2017) and Sehgal et al. (2023). In our application, the objective is to construct an active 
fund portfolio that all risk-averse investors would prefer over the benchmark, regardless 
of their individual preferences. Afterwards, the individual investor can combine the active 
fund shares with the risk-free asset (i.e., T-bills) to fulfil her specific risk preferences (see 
Bernard et al. (2021) for a review of the “two-fund separation” theory).4 In addition, we 
investigate the effect of the stochastic threshold on the performance of robust ES portfolios. 
This has not been previously studied to the best of our knowledge.
We also contribute to the scarce literature investigating the factor structure of robust 
portfolio returns. Most studies in this area focus on mean-variance portfolios, finding that 
the robust mean-variance portfolio has a higher level of systematic risk (Kim et al., 2013a, 
b, 2014a, b). To our knowledge, our study is the first to investigate the systematic risk profile 
of robust ES and OR portfolios, as well as their factor timing properties using the Carhart 
(1997) four-factor model.
This paper is structured as follows. Section 2 explains the specification of the uncertainty 
set (Sect. 2.1), introduces the robust portfolio optimization problems (Sects. 2.2 and 2.3), 
and explains the data and investment strategies scrutinized (Sect. 2.4). The out-of-sample 
portfolio performances obtained using historical data are presented in Sect. 3. Section 4 
presents and discusses the dependency of the portfolios on common risk factor movements 
as well as the ability of robust portfolios to provide crash risk protection. In Sect. 5, we pres­
ent an analysis of robust portfolios using simulated data. We conclude in Sect. 6.

2.1  Specification of uncertainty sets
In this study, we focus on the data-driven approach, in which investors have no informa­
tion beyond the evolving history of asset return observations. In particular, many models 
are based on using a sample of asset returns to represent the state space of equally likely 
states. This empirical probability distribution-based approach yields statistically consistent 
estimates for the portfolios’ return distributions if the returns are serially independent and 
identically distributed.
In practice, most applications often have to rely on small data samples, at least relative 
to the number of base assets, and hence favourable asymptotic properties do not necessar­
ily imply that the obtained state-space offers a reasonable representation of future returns. 
Moreover, asset returns can exhibit dynamic patterns such as price reversals and volatil­
ity jumps, which violate the assumption that the joint distribution of asset returns remains 
unchanged over time.

2.4  Data and investment strategy
As a base dataset for our empirical analysis, we consider 49 industry portfolios defined by 
SIC code from the Kenneth French data library. By using portfolios we minimize the draw­
backs that would occur with individual stocks—issues of size bias, selection bias, short-
sales restrictions, idiosyncratic risk, higher transaction costs and illiquidity. The data sample 
includes all daily return observations starting from the first trading day in January 1964 to 
the last trading day in December 2019 spanning a time horizon of 56 years. The benchmark 
is the all-share index from the CRSP database, a value-weighted average of common stocks 
listed on the NYSE, NYSE MKT, and NASDAQ stock exchanges. The industry-specific 
daily mean returns vary from 0.026 to 0.064% with an industry-specific standard deviation 
ranging from 0.83 to 2.33%. The benchmark has a daily mean return of 0.044% along with a 
standard deviation of 0.98%. Furthermore, most portfolios have negative skewness, positive 
excess kurtosis, and generally exhibit a non-normal distribution with Jarque-Bera test p-val­
ues close to zero (see Table A1 in Supplementary Appendix A for the descriptive statistics 
of industry indices and CRSP index). These features are a potential issue for mean-variance 
frameworks but not for the ES and OR approaches, which consider the entire distribution 
and not just the first two moments.
We employ a standard active trading strategy with fixed rebalancing frequency and no 
short positions. The short-selling constraints are realistic, as most investment funds are 
restricted from taking short positions. Additionally, these investment restrictions limit the 
effects of estimation error (Jagannathan and Ma, 2003). We use a 12-month estimation 
window and a 6-month holding period. This configuration allows us to capture industry 
momentum (Moskowitz and Grinblatt, 1999), which is based on maximizing long-only 
portfolio return and a 12-month estimation window. Semi-annual rebalancing is a common 
practice for managing momentum-based funds, as seen with ETFs tracking MSCI Momen­
tum Indices (e.g., iShares and Xtrackers). Further supporting this approach, Flint and Ver­
maak (2023) recommend a 6-month rebalancing interval as optimal for strategies that aim 
to reduce risk, which aligns with one of our primary objectives.
The choice of data and strategy is motivated by the application to active funds and 
grounded in prior literature on active mutual fund performance. Carhart (1997) argues that 
a one-year momentum effect explains the “hot hands” phenomenon observed in mutual 
funds. Mulvey and Kim (2008) find that top-performing active funds are strongly correlated 
with long-only, industry-level momentum strategies. Grinblatt et al. (2020) confirm that 
approximately two-thirds of mutual fund managers follow momentum strategies, and their 
investment style tends to be persistent. Additionally, many fund managers offer various 
investment products based on industry momentum or sector rotation.6
To check the robustness of the empirical results and shed light on whether robust optimi­
zation should be applied for portfolio selection, we conduct portfolio performance analysis 
using simulated data in Sect. 5. In particular, we analyze how robust portfolio performance 
depends on the specifications of the data-generating process, model parameter values and 
estimation error.

3  Out-of-sample performance analysis
In this section, we analyze the out-of-sample performance of different specifications of 
uncertainty sets for ES and OR using real data described in Sect. 2.4. The purpose of the 
analysis is to find whether the robust optimization provides any significant performance 
improvement for these two approaches, rather than to find which approach is better. The fund 
manager can choose an appropriate approach based on the objectives and risk preferences.
We use 49 industry portfolios as base assets and the CRSP all-share index as a benchmark. 
We consider two strategies for ES and OR—non-robust and robust portfolios optimized 
using joint uncertainty. The full list of portfolios observed along with their abbreviations 
and details are summarized in Table 1.
The γ parameter controls the level of portfolio robustness to uncertainty. If we are highly 
unsure about the input estimates, we decrease the lower bounds in the uncertainty set (3) 
by reducing γ and, hence, extending these uncertainty sets. This results in a portfolio that is 
more robust to uncertainty, that is the portfolio that yields the best result under the ‘worst’ 
scenario from the joint set PTγ. We provide results for γ = {0.9, 0.8, 0.7, 0.6}, correspond­
ing to different levels of uncertainty (from low to high).7
3.1  Performance measures

evaluate the out-of-sample performance of competing portfolios, we use multiple measures 
which are standard in the analysis of portfolio performance. We compute the mean excess 
return, standard and semi-deviations (std. dev. and semi-dev.) and skewness of the out-of-
sample returns. As the tail risk measure, we use Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR95%) for

tracking error. IR does not account for distribution asymmetry and downside risk. There­

we calculate the OR of active returns, which shows the trade-off between the maximization 
of expected overperformance and the minimization of expected underperformance from the 
benchmark (Dembo and Rosen, 1999). For both SR and OR, we set the threshold at zero 
(i.e., the mean active return of the benchmark), such that the upside potential of the active 
portfolio corresponds to positive excess returns, and the downside risk corresponds to nega­
tive excess returns.

sensitivity to transaction costs as well as a measure of the stability of the weights over time.

the most conservative values for fixed transaction costs used in the literature (see, DeMiguel 
et al. (2009); Platanakis et al. (2021)).

4  Robust portfolios and risk factors
In this section, we investigate whether the robust optimization approach has any unintended 
side effects, which can further explain the behaviour of robust portfolios. Earlier studies 
showed that higher robustness among robust mean-variance portfolios results in a higher 
correlation with the Fama-French risk factors (Kim et al., 2013a, b, 2014a, b). Factor analy­
sis is an important aspect of risk management. It allows monitoring and managing the sys­
tematic risks of actively managed funds. The exposure to systematic risks can be reduced 
using derivative instruments or by constraining the exposure of the portfolio to risk factors 
in optimization problems (Kim et al., 2014b). Therefore, we conduct the factor exposure 
analysis for ES and OR portfolios and their robust counterparts listed in Table 1.
As factors, we take the four common stock-market factors of Carhart (1997): (1) gen­
eral stock market return minus T-bill return (Mkt −Rf), which is a risk premium of our 
benchmark in Sect. 3, (2) small-capitalization-stocks return minus big-capitalization-stocks 
return (SMB), (3) value-stocks return minus growth-stocks return (HML) and (4) winner-
stocks return minus loser-stocks return (MOM). These factors explain a large part of the 
general return variation of the industry portfolios (Moskowitz and Grinblatt, 1999; Post et 
al., 2018). We perform two pieces of factor analysis: (i) factor structure and timing analysis 
in Sect. 4.1; (ii) factor crash risk analysis in Sect. 4.2.11

(1997) model. Here, we try to answer three questions. What is the OOS exposure of robust 
portfolios to four fundamental risk factors? Can robust portfolios yield abnormal returns, 
capitalizing on industry mispricing? Can robust portfolios exploit factor timing?

4.2  Factor crash risk
Christoffersen and Langlois (2013) provide evidence of significant lower-tail dependencies 
among the four Carhart (1997) factors, despite their small or even negative linear correla­
tions. This finding indicates that a normally distributed four-factor model (even with timing 
terms as in Sect. 4.1) may not accurately capture the joint distribution of portfolio returns, 
particularly in the left tail. Recognizing multifactor tail risk yields substantial benefits for 
portfolio allocation, risk assessment, and asset pricing (Christoffersen and Langlois, 2013; 
Chabi-Yo et al., 2022).

estimate a multivariate systematic risk measure (MCRASH), proposed by Chabi-Yo et al.

Table 6 reports the sensitivity of the ES portfolios to crashes in the Carhart (1997) four 
factors. The results demonstrate that robust optimization reduces both univariate and mul­
tivariate systematic crash risks for the ES portfolio. Specifically, RESJU minimizes multi­
variate systematic risk, achieving MCRASH = 0.10 when γ = 0.6. This indicates that if 
at least one factor crashes, the probability of RESJU realizing a return at or below its 1% 
quantile is 10%, whereas the non-robust ES portfolio has a 7% higher likelihood of crashing 
under the same condition.13 Moreover, RESJU exhibits significantly lower exposure to the 
SMB and HML tail events than the non-robust ES portfolio.
Table 7 presents the OOS factor crash risks for the OR portfolios. Similar to the ES 
case, the robust OR portfolios are less sensitive to the tail events of the Carhart factors 
compared to their non-robust counterpart. RORJU achieves the lowest MCRASH value of 
0.06 when γ = 0.7, reducing the multivariate systematic risk of the non-robust OR portfolio 
by 7%. Additionally, RORJU minimizes the individual CRASHSMB, CRASHHML and 
CRASHMOM metrics.
In summary, robust optimization under joint uncertainty effectively protects both ES and 
OR portfolios from systematic crash risks, significantly reducing the likelihood of extreme 
losses for optimized portfolios during severe crashes in fundamental factors.

We assume that the market is composed of 49 risky assets. The excess returns of

DGP for the factor returns or noise.
The model is calibrated from the real data used in the analysis of Sects. 3 and 4. We 
first regress the individual excess industry indices daily returns on the Carhart (1997) four 
factors over the same time period (Jan 1964–Dec 2019). We obtain the estimates of the mis­

we fit two multivariate Gaussian models, N(ˆµb, ˆΣb) and N(0, ˆΣε), to the Carhart factor 
returns and residual estimates, respectively.
The median and range of mispricing coefficients, ˆα, are 0.09 bps and 5.34 bps. The 
median and range of four factor loadings, ˆB, are {0.99, 0.24, 0.14, − 0.01} and {0.93, 1.15, 
1.58, 0.34}, respectively. The four factor means, ˆµb, are {2.57, 0.63, 1.52, 3.01} bps and 
standard deviations, ˆΣb, are {98.42, 52.42, 50.60, 70.55} bps. The median and range of 
residual standard deviations, ˆΣε, are 82.07 bps and 176.56 bps, respectively.

We analysed ten specifications of the simulation approach: 
1. Base model The DGP model is calibrated using daily returns of industry indices and 
Carhart’s four factors, as described earlier. The factors and residuals are assumed to be 
independently and identically distributed. For portfolio optimization, we generate 250 
pseudo-observations from the fitted model to serve as the input sample.
2. High Idiosyncratic Volatility (IDV) The standard deviations of noise in the base model, 
N(0, Σε), are set to values corresponding to the in-sample one-year period with the 
highest average idiosyncratic volatility. DeMiguel et al. (2009) and Platanakis et al. 
(2021) find that optimal portfolio performance relative to passive strategy may depend 
on the level of average idiosyncratic volatility.
3. Low IDV The standard deviations of noise in the base model, N(0, ˆΣε), are set to values 
corresponding to the in-sample one-year period with the lowest average idiosyncratic 
volatility.
4. High Market Volatility (MV) The standard deviations of the market factor returns in the 
base model, RMkt−Rf, are set to values corresponding to the in-sample period with the 
highest market volatility. Given that the market portfolio serves as both a fundamental 
factor and a benchmark in our analysis, evaluating performance under varying market 
volatility conditions may provide deeper insights into robust portfolio optimization.
5. Low MV The standard deviations of RMkt−Rf are set to values corresponding to the 
in-sample period with the lowest market volatility.
6. Zero α The mispricing coefficient vector α in the base model is set to a vector of zeros. 
Platanakis et al. (2021) show that the nonzero α’s are an important condition for the 
optimal portfolio superiority over the passive strategy.
7. βMkt−Rf = 1 The factor loadings for the Mkt −Rf factor in the base model are set 
to ones. This specification tests how the heterogeneity in assets’ exposure to the bench­
mark market influences the out-of-sample performance of the robust portfolios.
8. GMM Factors We relax the Gaussianity assumption of the base model, and fit 
the multivariate GMM model with two states to the Carhart (1997) four factors,

6  Conclusion
This paper explores the application of the robust optimization framework within the context 
of active portfolio management. Specifically, we address two key objectives of active fund 
managers: (1) minimizing risk, as measured by Expected shortfall (ES), and (2) achieving 
an optimal balance between reward and risk, represented by the Omega ratio, relative to a 
benchmark index. To enhance the robustness of optimization, we introduce an alternative 
formulation of the uncertainty set, termed “joint uncertainty”. This approach assumes that 
uncertainty simultaneously affects both the probability distributions of the underlying assets 
and the threshold level, defined as the benchmark’s expected return.

Using the U.S. industry indices as base assets and the broad market index as a bench­
mark, we showed that the joint uncertainty approach significantly improves out-of-sample 
performance compared to the non-robust portfolio. The outperformance is both economi­
cally and statistically significant, even after accounting for risks and transaction costs.
A comprehensive analysis of the relationship between robust portfolios and Carhart fun­
damental risk factors reveals several key findings regarding the joint uncertainty approach: 
(1) it significantly alters the systematic risk profiles of portfolios optimized using the 
Expected shortfall and Omega ratio objective; (2) it effectively exploits asset mispricing 
and upside-only momentum exposure in out-of-sample performance; and (3) it offers pro­
tection against factor crash risks, substantially lowering the probability of extreme losses for 
portfolios during severe crashes in four fundamental factors.
The simulation analysis revealed that the advantages of the joint uncertainty approach 
are robust across a wide range of data-generating processes, model parameters, and degrees 
of estimation error. The superior performance of ES under joint uncertainty is particularly 
evident in scenarios characterized by low idiosyncratic volatility, high volatility of the mar­
ket benchmark, and heterogeneous asset exposures to the benchmark market. Conversely, 
the enhanced performance of OR under joint uncertainty is most pronounced in conditions 
featuring low idiosyncratic volatility, asset mispricing, and homogeneous asset dependence 
on the benchmark market.
We conclude by pointing out two promising areas for further research. First, this study 
employs a box specification for joint uncertainty sets. Future research could explore robust 
portfolio optimization under joint convex or ellipsoidal uncertainty sets (Lotfi and Zenios, 
2018; Sehgal et al., 2023). Second, this paper focuses exclusively on the Carhart four-factor 
model. Extending the analysis to nonlinear latent factor models, such as those based on 
instrumented principal components (Kelly et al., 2019) or autoencoder-based factors (Gu et 
al., 2021), could provide valuable insights about robust portfolio construction.
