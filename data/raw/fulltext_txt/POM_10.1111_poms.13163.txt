Our paper investigates the eﬀects of introducing performance-based incentives in a competitive healthcare
market. We consider a market in which a payer applies a performance-based compensation contract to two
competing hospitals. We use G/G/m queueing dynamics to describe the patient care process and include
information asymmetry between the payer and the hospitals regarding the hospital operating costs.
Our paper is the ﬁrst to study the performance-based contracting problem in a healthcare market in the
presence of competition on both the quality of and the access to care and the cost information asymmetry
between the payer and care providers. We analyze the socially optimal and Nash equilibrium outcomes under
bonus compensation where each of the competing hospitals is rewarded based on patient beneﬁts delivered
at that hospital.
We show that both the stronger competition among hospitals and the introduction of bonus incentives
can enhance patient beneﬁts. Furthermore, we demonstrate that, in the presence of information asymmetry
between the payer and the hospitals regarding hospitals’ operating costs, the social welfare loss generated
by the fee-for-service compensation as well as by the optimal bonus contract can be partially mitigated by
increasing the degree of competition for patients. Such mitigation eﬀect is ampliﬁed when the potential cost
diﬀerential between the hospitals is suﬃciently high and, simultaneously, the correlation between their cost
parameters is not too negative.
Key words : Healthcare competition; information asymmetry; performance-based incentives.

1.
Introduction
Faced with increasing pressure to contain rising costs while maintaining high-quality care, health-
care systems in the U.S. and other developed countries are experimenting with policies that aim to
control the cost of care by incentivizing care providers to compete for patients and tying provider
compensation to the quality of delivered care. In the U.S., the Patient Protection and Aﬀord-
able Care Act (also known as the Aﬀordable Care Act or ACA) introduced in 2010 (111th Cong.
2010) has created a way for tens of millions of new patients to access care while simultaneously
introducing a number of new approaches designed to slow down the rise in the overall cost of care.

One of the key features of the rapidly changing incentives landscape that aﬀects both patients
and providers is the growing number of performance-based incentive programs that condition
payments to providers on the quality of care delivered (see Cashin et al. 2014 for a detailed review of
performance-based incentive programs in OECD countries and Rosenthal et al. 2006 for the study
of performance-based incentives in US Health Management Organizations, or HMOs). Currently,
one of the largest performance-based programs in the U.S. is the hospital value-based purchasing
(VBP) program authorized by the ACA and run by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services
(CMS) since 2012 (CMS 2018).
The notion of service quality is central to any healthcare initiative that aims to align payments
for care with desired performance targets. Healthcare service quality has multiple dimensions and
in the hospital context includes both clinical outcomes and clinical process measures. For example,
the clinical outcomes used by the Medicare VBP program include 30-day mortality rates from acute
myocardial infarction, heart failure, and pneumonia as well as complication and infection rates
from such conditions. On the process side, the quality metrics include adherence to evidence-based
medical procedures as well as metrics characterizing timely access to and delivery of care. Timely
access to care, in particular, plays an important role in shaping care outcomes in both inpatient
and outpatient settings. In the U.S., the adoption of the ACA substantially lowered the barrier for
patients to access care “in principle,” shifting the emphasis to patients’ accessing care “on time.”
Following a substantial increase in the number of patients who are eligible for care, the “timeliness”
component of healthcare delivery is likely to become one of the front-and-center issues in the U.S.,
as it has been for some time in a number of OECD countries (Brekke et al. 2008). In particular,
within the hospital care delivery system, “timeliness” often translates into the necessity to limit
patient wait for non-emergency procedures.

ﬁlled with direct-to-consumer hospital advertising (Schenker et al. 2014). This new competitive
dynamic is based largely on the quality of care and access to care rather than the cost to a patient.
This is not surprising since in many developed countries patients, on the one hand, do not make
signiﬁcant payments to care providers, and, on the other hand, such payments, where they exist,
do not signiﬁcantly vary by the provider, and in the U.S. the pricing of healthcare services remains
complex and frequently not transparent, with the cost of care often not known to a patient until
after the care has been delivered (HFMA 2014). In the health economics literature it is standard
to describe competition among hospitals as being driven by both quality of care and access to care
they provide, with the “access” usually deﬁned in terms of a physical distance that the patient
must travel to get care (see, for example, Brekke et al. 2006, 2008, 2010a,b). In our work, we focus
on a diﬀerent access measure, namely, on the time a patient must wait before care is delivered.
The introduction of performance-based incentives into competitive healthcare markets motivated
us to focus on two major research questions.
The ﬁrst research question we pose addresses a gap in the extant literature and adopts a per-
spective of a payer for healthcare services, such as a government agency, focusing on how a payer
should design performance contracts with care providers that compete for patients. Our analysis
of this question has a distinct practical ﬂavor, since it studies a class of performance contracts
(“bonus” contracts) often chosen by payers, and focuses, in particular, on “uniform” bonus settings
where the same bonus contract is applied to all competing providers. A prominent example of such
contract is provided by the NHS England that applies the same performance-based terms to all
hospitals, calculating the bonus compensation on a monthly basis (Jiang et al. 2012). Since the
asymmetry of information between the payer and the care providers is an often-emphasized feature
of healthcare markets (see, for example, De Fraja 2000), our model assumes that the hospitals are
operating under a full knowledge of their costs while the payer must rely on the distributional
cost information. The key results of our analysis (Propositions 4a and 5a) express the optimal
“uniform” bonus contract in terms of few easy-to-estimate parameters.
The second question relates to the ability of such “uniform” bonus contracts to generate socially
optimal care outcomes. The concerns about perceived fairness compel payers to apply the same
performance-based compensation scheme to all care providers, and we are interested in quantifying
the social cost arising from this requirement. In particular, we connect the value of the welfare gap
arising under the optimal “uniform” bonus contract in the settings with substantial cost diﬀerential
among providers and also calculate the degree to which these gaps are mitigated by the competition
for patients (Propositions 4b,c and 5b,c).
Our modeling is driven by these two research questions and is designed to provide a parsimonious
reﬂection of the most important features of the actual healthcare markets. In particular, we focus

on settings where a payer uses both the quality of delivered care and patient access to it as key
indicators of hospital performance. Following the service operations management literature (i.e.
Allon and Federgruen 2007, 2008), we model the process of patient care at each hospital using
G/G/m queueing dynamics. This modeling approach, while simplistic, still provides an adequate
qualitative description of the uncertain nature of demand for hospital services and of the uncertain
load such demand places on hospital resources. Similar to Allon and Federgruen (2008), we assume
that the patient demand rate for a particular hospital is a function of its own performance as
well as that of its competitors. We also assume that each hospital maximizes its expected proﬁt
when faced with performance-based reimbursements. To provide analytical characterizations of the
Nash equilibrium quality and access outcomes under performance-based incentives, we consider a
duopoly setting. For each hospital, we model the patient demand as linearly increasing in the beneﬁt
patients receive from care at this hospital, and as linearly decreasing in the patient beneﬁt delivered
by the hospital’s competitor. The patient beneﬁt, in turn, is assumed to be linear in hospital’s own
performance indicators. Following Brekke et al. (2017a,b), we model hospital operating cost as a
multiplicative function of the quality of patient service and the hospital service capacity.
Our paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes
the modeling assumptions and the optimization problems facing the social planner, the payer and
the hospitals, and derives “base-case” estimates for the model’s parameters. Section 4 provides
analytical characterization of the socially optimal outcomes and the Nash equilibrium outcomes
under an arbitrary “bonus” contract. Section 5 focuses on the analysis of the optimal bonus contract
and the social welfare outcomes it generates, and Section 6 summarizes the managerial implications
of our ﬁndings.
2.
Literature Review
The healthcare context provides an important example of a public sector setting where a separate
entity, the payer, needs to incentivize competing proﬁt-maximizing care providers to maintain
appropriate standards of care. To the best of our knowledge, our paper is the ﬁrst to study the
performance-based contracting problem in a healthcare market in the presence of competition and
information asymmetry.
There exists a large body of literature that analyzes service competition in general and healthcare
competition in particular along the dimensions of quality, access and price. Pope (1989), Brekke
et al. (2006, 2010a), and Gravelle et al. (2012) investigate quality competition between healthcare
providers. Andritsos and Aﬂaki (2015), Brekke et al. (2008), Png and Reitman (1994), and Savva
et al. (2019) study waiting time competition in healthcare and other services. Joint price and
quality competition has been a popular topic in healthcare (Brekke et al. 2010b, Ma and Burgess

1993) and in general services (Allon and Federgruen 2007, 2008, 2009, Bernstein and Federgruen
2004). However, in the healthcare markets, like the Medicare and the NHS England, hospitals are
reimbursed by the payer for most of the care they provide, and the “price” of service may not be
as salient as the quality of service for patients making a choice of a care provider. Moreover, none
of the aforementioned papers studies performance-based contracts for hospitals competing on both
quality and access.
The operations management literature contains a number of papers that focus on performance-
based incentives in general services (Akan et al. 2011), as well as in call centers (Ren and Zhou
2008, Hasija et al. 2008), and in healthcare settings (De Fraja 2000, So and Tang 2000, Fuloria
and Zenios 2001, Jiang et al. 2012, Lee and Zenios 2012, Andritsos and Aﬂaki 2015, Savva et al.
2019). These papers, with the exception of Andritsos and Aﬂaki (2015) and Savva et al. (2019),
analyze settings with a monopolistic service provider. In Andritsos and Aﬂaki (2015), a comparison
between the monopolistic and two duopolistic settings reveals that the introduction of competition
can hamper a hospital’s ability to achieve economies of scale and increase wait times. Savva et al.
(2019) consider a yardstick compensation scheme for competing hospitals.
In Pope (1989), Jiang et al. (2012), Andritsos and Aﬂaki (2015), and Savva et al. (2019), access
is used as a driver for the performance-based contracts, whereas So and Tang (2000), Fuloria and
Zenios (2001), and Lee and Zenios (2012) consider performance-based contracts based on quality.
However, all of these papers assume that hospitals do not compete for patients. The literature on
performance-based contracting with asymmetric information in healthcare is sparse (see, e.g., De
Fraja 2000, Fuloria and Zenios 2001, and Jiang et al. 2012). However, these papers consider neither
quality and access jointly, nor competition between hospitals.
Our paper focuses on a more complex setting, where competition between providers occurs in the
presence of performance-based incentives imposed by a payer that is uncertain about the providers’
costs. Savva et al. (2019) is closest to our paper in terms of the topic of analysis as it presents
a game-theoretic queueing model of yardstick competition, in which a regulator maximizes social
welfare by determining the service price and the transfer payments to service providers that act as
monopolists in their local markets. Our analysis diﬀers from that in Savva et al. (2019) in three
important ways. First, the care providers in our model compete for patients directly via demand
functions, rather than through the incentive payments. Second, our analysis assumes an exogenous
fee-for-service component of the providers’ compensation and focuses instead on the performance-
based incentives that tie providers’ compensation to the quality and access performance metrics
in a ﬁxed-price competitive setting. Third, we model the cost information asymmetry between the
payer and providers. Our setting is complementary to the one in Savva et al. (2019).

3.
Competition and Incentives: The Model
We consider a healthcare system consisting of a payer (i.e., a government agency) and two hospi-
tals, indexed by i = 1,2, with patients sensitive to both the quality of and the access to a single
condition/procedure such as hip replacement. We assume that the payer can monitor quality and
access provided by each hospital and design contracts based on the achieved levels of these perfor-
mance indicators (Brekke et al. 2010a, Jiang et al. 2012). In the presence of competition between
the hospitals, the payer designs a performance-based reimbursement contract, and the hospitals
select the quality and the access levels. In this system, the payer acts as a Stackelberg leader by
using performance-based incentives to induce hospital investment in patient service quality and
service capacity.
Below we describe the assumptions we make regarding the dynamics of patient care, as well as
the objectives and constraints of each participant.
3.1.
The Dynamics, Beneﬁts and Costs of Patient Care
Following the approach in the extant literature (see, i.e., Allon and Federgruen 2007, Allon and
Federgruen 2008), we use the G/G/m queueing model as an approximation for the dynamics of
healthcare services that patients receive at each hospital. We assume that patients form a single
group with homogeneous treatment needs and durations. The level of simpliﬁcation in our model
reﬂects a “macro” view of patient care that ignores a number of operational details, allowing us
in return to gain analytical insights into the eﬀects of performance-based incentives on the time
patients have to wait for care and on the quality of that care.
3.2.
Patient Beneﬁt and Demand Functions
From the point of view of a social planner, providing timely, high-quality treatments to patients
generates social welfare. The social welfare, or “beneﬁt”, accrued by a patient that receives care
at each hospital is driven by two factors. The ﬁrst factor is the level of access to care, or service
level, represented by wi, the expected wait time at hospital i. Note that the wait times reﬂect
the appointment delay patients experience before receiving care, i.e., the delay between the time a
patient joins a queue (schedules her appointment) and the time the care starts. The second factor
driving patient demand is the quality of care, qi, a non-negative scalar that encompasses all quality
attributes that inﬂuence patient experiences and clinical outcomes. The quality measure we use
reﬂects the process and the outcomes of care, once the patient is in the hospital, rather than the
timeliness of care delivery, which we characterize with a separate access measure. In separating the
access-to-care and the quality-of-care measure, we follow Allon and Federgruen (2007), Allon and
Federgruen (2008) and Allon and Federgruen (2009).

The speciﬁcation of the hospital daily cost function (7) is related to the general functional forms
used in healthcare economics (see, for example, Brekke et al. 2010a, 2017a,b), where the cost
function is assumed to be monotone in quality and patient demand. In particular, the cost function
adopted by Brekke et al. (2017a,b) is equal to the sum of a multiplicative (supermodular) term

demand as well, and can be considered a special case of the general form outlined in aforementioned
models, but with the caveat that we use the service capacity, e.g., the number of staﬀed beds, rather
than demand, as a basis for the cost assessment. Such a supermodular functional form implies that
the marginal improvement in quality becomes more costly as the hospital becomes larger, and,
also, that the cost associated with marginal improvement in hospital capacity increases with the
quality of care the hospital delivers.

level of personnel, equipment and processes that impact hospital operating cost (7), and, on the
other hand, determines patient demand for hospital services (as in (4)). In other words, rather

Our approach to modeling quality as a scalar that determines both patient demand and provider’s

The notion of care quality we use is supported by the growing amount of data of increasing
breadth and granularity on patient experience and clinical outcomes of care that is being col-
lected and made publicly available in many developed healthcare markets. For example, in the
US, the CMS Hospital Compare database provides a number of items of individual-hospital-level
data that facilitate a better-informed patient choice when it comes to hospital care provision. In
particular, a patient, after entering her zip code, can compare side-to-side a number of hospi-

of medical imaging, and payment and “value of care”, with some data separated by procedure/
clinical condition. In England, the NHS (similar to CMS in the US) makes a comprehensive
list of hospital care quality indicators (for a number of popular procedures and services) pub-

number of reported indicators, however, raises the issue of how can they be aggregated into a
meaningful scalar measure. While any particular candidate for such an encompassing measure
has its limitations, two of the publicly available measures may serve as good examples of such a
scalar. In the US, the CMS aggregates the hospital performance across the seven quality areas
mentioned above into a single star rating (the detailed description of the data and the method-
ology is available at https://www.medicare.gov/hospitalcompare/Data/Hospital-overall-ratings-

collected by the independent regulator Care Quality Commission (CQC, https://www.cqc.org.uk/).
These overall ratings, in turn, represent aggregations of ratings over ﬁve characteristics of care
delivery: “safe,” “eﬀective,” “caring,” “responsive,” “well-led.” These CQC provider ratings are
similar to the star-based system used by the CMS in that they are reported in category-based, qual-
itative terms (“outstanding,” “good,” “adequate,” and “requires improvement”) complemented by
color-coding (the CQC website mentioned above provides details of the rating process and the
available data.)

probability distribution of the hospitals’ costs. To complete the description of the informational
structure of our model, we assume that each hospital possesses exact knowledge of both its own cost
type as well as the cost type of its competitor. We make such an assumption mainly for the sake
of analytical tractability as an alternative involving modeling the beliefs of each hospital regarding
the cost of its competitor would require a far more complex Bayesian equilibrium analysis of the
Nash game between the hospitals. While this assumption is limiting, it is not unreasonable since
when two hospitals compete for patients, they are often located in the same geographical region
and would have signiﬁcant amount of knowledge on the operating costs of their local competitors.
Such an assumption is also common in the literature (see, for example, Allon and Federgruen 2007,
Andritsos and Aﬂaki 2015, Bernstein and Federgruen 2004, Brekke et al. 2010a).

conditions, such adjustments are likely to be small compared to potential diﬀerences between the
operating costs of competing hospitals (see, for example, a discussion in Jiang et al. 2012 of the
compensation used by the NHS England). Thus, our assumption of a “uniform” base compensation
rate for competing hospitals is not unreasonable. Fourth, in our model we consider the performance

The “participation” constraint (19) ensures that the hospitals cannot lose money under the imposed
reimbursement structure, no matter what the hospital cost parameters γ is. We note that (18)-(20)
represent a typical structure of the principal’s problem in the principal-agent models (Laﬀont and
Tirole 1993). A key feature of the payer’s optimization problem (18)-(20) is the reliance on a single
performance-based contract applied to all hospitals as opposed to a menu of cost-revealing contracts
typically recommended in a literature as a recipe for dealing with asymmetric cost information
(Bolton and Dewatripont 2005). One notes that a “uniform” contract may create an opportunity
for a further loss of social welfare in comparison with a menu of contracts. At the same time, our
use of a single contract reﬂects the reality of healthcare markets where none of the commonly used
reimbursement contracts is menu-based. This reality is a consequence of the limited market and/or
regulatory power for payers as any attempt to impose such menu on providers would be viewed as
unfair and discriminatory.
In our model, the payer inﬂuences hospitals via compensation functions (9) that incentivize
hospitals to improve the level of beneﬁts delivered to patients. Given hospital’s cost structure, and
depending on what patients in a particular market value more, it may respond by adjusting its
capacity, keeping the quality of care unchanged, or it may adjust quality of care, without changing
its capacity, or it may adjust both. Thus, the hospital optimization problems in the presence of
incentives and competition cannot be re-expressed in a form that depends only on hospital capacity
values µi. It must also include, for example, the quality of care qi it delivers.
The model of healthcare delivery system we introduce aims to provide a qualitative match to
reality. In particular, we model the interaction among three distinct entities: the payer, the hos-
pitals competing for patients on the basis of quality of care and access to care, and the patients
themselves. While our model has characteristics that can also be applied to other service envi-
ronments, most readily to retail services, insurance-based services and warranty-based after-sales
support and maintenance services, there are four features of our approach that, taken together,
narrow its applicability to healthcare delivery systems:
1) Service providers are incentivized to compete for consumers,
2) consumers choose providers based on quality of service and access to it, rather than price,
3) payer acts as an agent of consumers,
4) payer imposes the same contract on all service providers.
The principal-agent framework we use may also be applicable to retail and insurance settings, in
addition to healthcare settings. However, in those retail settings where wholesaler has the power
to play the role of the principal that sets compensation contracts for retailers, a potentially strong
competition among retailers often has a price-based component, even when it is complemented by
quality of products sold as well as by auxiliary services provided, or by the availability of products

16
Jiang, Pang and Savin: Performance Incentives and Competition in Healthcare Markets
(i.e., by ﬁll rate, as in Bernstein and Federgruen 2004). On the other hand, insurance services that
have event-based service components, or warranty-based after-sales support services typically have
a more salient non-price (such as quality and access) characteristics. But, even in settings where
the payer has a power to act as a principal, it often controls the number of certiﬁed providers,
and, thus, weakens the competition among them, or even “routes” customers to a pre-determined
service provider.
The role played by the payer in our model is distinct from that in many other services, including
private insurance and warranty sellers. While being the principal for providers of services, the payer
in our model is acting as an “agent” of consumers in that the revenue part of its objective function
is assumed to be perfectly aligned with consumer utility. Both the market power of the payer and
its focus on consumers’ best interests describe most closely a government agency (such as Centers
for Medicare & Medicaid Services in the US) rather than a private, for-proﬁt insurance company.
Being a government agency, however, the payer must be mindful of the “fairness” concerns, since
its power to impose incentives on providers is not absolute. In our model, we treat the payer as the
principal who does not have the power to discriminate among providers by imposing individualized
contracts. An example of such “uniform” contract is provided by Hospital Readmissions Reduction
Program (CMS 2017) in the US. This contrasts with other businesses (e.g., private insurance or
warranty sellers) where payers, acting as principals in the principal-agent models, can negotiate
individualized contracts with providers. Note that this “uniform” feature of performance-based
compensation has direct implications for the payer’s decision making. In particular, the payer treats
provider costs as a pair of values, and is not inﬂuenced by which cost value corresponds to which
provider.
In summary, while some features of our model can be used to describe other service environments,
in its entirety our model ﬁts well only the realities of healthcare delivery.

Discussion
The U.S. healthcare system is experimenting with a growing number of performance-based incentive
programs that are being tested on providers competing for patients in an environment of increasing
transparency about care quality. The combination of performance incentives and competition is
likely to remain a dominant factor shaping healthcare delivery as payers (government agencies such
as Medicare) expand the use of performance targets while patients, faced with rising insurance
deductibles and the increasing availability of data on provider performance, simultaneously become
increasingly discerning consumers of healthcare services. Our paper examines how incentives set by
payers interact with the competitiveness and information asymmetry present in healthcare systems.
Our analysis is based on an approach that models the dynamics of patient care delivery at each
competing hospital as that of G/G/m queue, with hospitals’ operating costs being multiplicative in
service rate and quality of provided care. To reﬂect the healthcare reality, we assume the presence
of information asymmetry regarding the precise value of hospital operating costs between the payer
and the providers. In describing patient demand, we use a parsimonious model that relies on a
linear approximation to patient utility from receiving care that depends on both the quality of and
the access to care and the MNL model to capture patient choice of care providers in a duopoly.
We derive the expressions for the socially optimal outcomes and use them as a benchmark for
equilibrium outcomes under a popular type of performance-based incentives that rewards each
competitor for the quality and access to care it delivers. The focus of our analysis is two-fold. From
the normative standpoint, we identify the impact that the simultaneous presence of competition for
patients and lack of precise information on hospital operating costs have on the contract parameters
the payers should use in designing the bonus incentives, i.e., on the optimal bonus contracts. Our
results on the joint impact of the competition and the information asymmetry outline a complex
dynamics, with the two factors going hand-in-hand in generating socially optimal outcomes in
some settings, and counteracting in others. The complex nature of this relationship may increase
an appeal of simpler, even if suboptimal, approaches in practical settings. Adopting a practical
perspective, we quantify the eﬀects of payer using a heuristic bonus contract that ignores the
presence of these two factors (see Appendix D). Two most important conclusions from our analysis
are that, from the social welfare perspective, the heuristic performs as well as the optimal contract

30
Jiang, Pang and Savin: Performance Incentives and Competition in Healthcare Markets
in the absence of information asymmetry and that it may result in a social welfare loss in settings
where the range of the hospital cost distribution is signiﬁcant - and that such social welfare loss
can be alleviated by stronger competition.
Overall, our analysis indicates that, under the bonus incentive scheme, the competition between
the providers may have lower impact on payer’s ability to coordinate the system than the lack
of precise information, no matter whether the payer chooses the “normative” or the “practical”
approach to setting bonus incentives. In view of the beneﬁts stemming from the removal of informa-
tion asymmetry, it appears that the payer’s eﬀorts in ascertaining the hospital operating cost value
may produce higher returns than a comparable investment in estimating the demand interaction
parameters between competing providers.
In our paper, we analyze the performance of “bonus” incentive contracts that tie the incentives
of each provider to their own performance. In practice, payers may also include the elements
of compensation that depend on how well the providers perform relative to competition. Such
“yardstick” contracts may have potential of achieving higher degree of system coordination (Shleifer
1985). It is important to note, however, that the “yardstick” approach is also likely to have higher
implementation costs as hospitals may feel that tying their compensation to the factors outside of
their control, such as the performance of competitors, is unfair. Thus, when selecting an appropriate
contract type, the payer must account for its own, often limited, ability to impose a particular
contract structure, and thus, must weigh in the potential disparity in the contract implementation
costs. For example, in the U.S., a private insurance company, even a large one, may ﬁnd it easier
to use the “bonus” compensation, while Medicare, the largest payer for health care services in
the country, is able to use the “yardstick” approach as part of the VBP program (CMS 2018).
The analysis of advantages and costs of more general contract families represents an important
extension to our present work.

Proposition D1 outlines the potential eﬀects of using bonus performance-based compensation that ignores
the presence of competition and information asymmetry. The result of part a) of Proposition D1 points
out to an interesting consequence of applying a heuristic contract in a setting where both providers are
monopolists and where the payer is overly optimistic about his knowledge of hospital operating costs. On
one hand, when the probability of the “low-cost” scenario is limited, the optimal bonus contract perfectly
coordinates the system, while the heuristic contract leads to ineﬃciency caused by underpayment to care
providers. On the other hand, as the “high-cost” scenario becomes more likely, the optimal bonus contract
produces worse coordination than the heuristic one: while the payer always improves its objective with correct
accounting of the competition, the expected social welfare is aﬀected negatively. Thus, a suboptimal version
of the bonus contract may, in addition to having lower informational requirement, also lead to improved
system coordination. The contrast between the eﬀects of information asymmetry and that of competition
is underscored by the results of part b) of Proposition D1: once the information asymmetry is removed,
the heuristic contract coordinates the system as perfectly as the optimal bonus contract. The mechanism of
such coordination becomes clear if one compares the bonus parameters under two contracts as well as the
diﬀerences in payer’s objective value (D7) and payments to hospitals (D8): the heuristic contract provides a
more aggressive compensation, and overpays the hospitals for the same, socially optimal outcomes.
