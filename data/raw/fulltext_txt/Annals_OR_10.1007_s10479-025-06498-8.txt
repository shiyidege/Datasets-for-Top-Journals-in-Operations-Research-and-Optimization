Abstract
Sustainability in supply chains (SC) is a topic that has been widely discussed recently and,
similarly to the quest to develop green SCs, it does not contemplate the integration of eco-
nomic and social dimensions of sustainable SC management (SSCM). This article proposes
a conceptual framework to provide key factors that act as a valuable tool to further develop
quantitative models of location, inventory and transportation (LIT) problems in SSCs. The
objectives of this paper are to: (i) review the literature by focusing on conceptual frameworks
for sustainable SC; (ii) map and construct the conceptual framework blocks by describing
each element and dimension; and (iii) validate the conceptual framework. The conceptual
framework comprises ﬁve main blocks: SC structures, sustainable inputs, quantitative mod-
els, inventory policies and sustainability objectives. These ﬁve blocks are then formed by
different dimensions and elements, which are described in this paper and validated in a min-
ing case study. The results show that no previous studies propose a reference conceptual
framework to model a sustainable LIT problem in a CLSC context, which is the main novelty
of this paper. Finally, the conceptual framework is validated and applied as a diagnostic tool
in two copper mining companies.
Keywords Sustainable supply chain · Quantitative models · Facility location · Inventory
management · Transportation, mining industry
1 Introduction
The long-term success of any organisation must be based not only on proﬁtability, but also on
its contribution to the future of people and the planet. Supply chains (SC) are essential systems
of organisations, the consideration of sustainability at the SC level needs to be considered
(Bubicz et al., 2019). Due to increasing environmental interests and stricter laws on waste
generated during product development and downstream, one of the most complex problems
to consider is the management of this waste at both strategic and tactical levels. This issue has

transporting the products or raw materials become a safety risk along the route (Ouertani et al.,

social impact in the modelling objectives generates changes in inventory and transport costs
(Zhalechian et al., 2016).
A conceptual framework that combines the knowledge-based view theory and the sys-
tems theory by incorporating elements of the SC structure, sustainability inputs, quantitative
models and inventory policies, among others, is proposed. This study puts forward a loca-
tion, inventory and transportation (LIT) conceptual framework to provide key factors to be
used as a valuable tool to further develop quantitative models of LIT problems in SSCs. The
main challenge of the proposed framework lies in an integrated approach of LIT to con-
sider sustainability aspects, i.e., apart from environmental aspects, social (e.g. safety risk in
transportation problems or job creation in locations problems) and economic aspects (e.g.
economic development in location problems or cost reduction in inventory problems), which
should be incorporated. So the main research objectives (ROs) of this paper are to: (RO1)
propose a reference framework as a basis for the subsequent development and implementa-
tion of quantitative models to precisely deﬁne and categorise the LIT problem under study;
(RO2) validate and apply the proposed conceptual framework in different real-world case
studies.
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 outlines the methodology
followed to review the literature and build the conceptual framework. Section 3 presents the
literature review of conceptual frameworks for SSCM. Section 4 describes the conceptual
framework and its elements. Section 5 validates the conceptual framework. Finally, Sect. 6
includes the conclusions and further research.
2 Conceptualisation methodology
A conceptual framework identiﬁes relevant variables, classiﬁes them, describes their inter-
actions, and allows items to be mapped. This type of conceptual system consists of multiple
concepts with many interrelated propositions (Meredith, 1993). This section presents the
methodology used to build a framework that describes the key factors and explains their
relations to face LIT problems in an SSCM context. The methodology used by Ahmad et al.
(2017), Hernández et al. (2008) and Seuring and Müller (2008) is adapted to develop the
proposed conceptual framework shown in Fig. 1.
The conceptualisation process of this framework is based on the one hand, on the system-
atic literature review by Becerra et al. (2021) and, on the other hand, on a new search made out
in databases Web of Science (WOS) and Scopus using the search criteria “sustainable supply

3 Literature review
It is important to previously frame this study within the literature review of 91 papers on
SIM quantitative models in a green SC carried out by Becerra et al. (2021). Its main ﬁndings
conclude that a SIM model in a green SC should be modelled with mathematical program-
ming by incorporating the 3S pillars, and by considering facility location factors, inventory

management aspects and transportation routes as a whole to improve the system’s perfor-
mance. In the literature review of the conceptual frameworks, it is worth mentioning that
the ﬁrst study by Svensson (2007) proposes a conceptual SSCM framework. It claims an
expanded approach to SCM by emphasising the economic, environmental and social aspects
of business practices and theory. Some papers attempt to bridge sustainability and SC risk
management, such as Seuring and Müller (2008), who conceptualise their literature-based
analysis by focusing on a focal company, supplier risk evaluation and sustainability from
the products perspective. Rostamzadeh et al. (2018) develop a framework for sustainable
SC risk management (SSCRM) evaluations by means of an integrated fuzzy multicriteria
decision-making (MCDM) approach based on the technique in order of preference by sim-
ilarity to the ideal solution and criteria importance with intercriteria correlation methods.
Some authors focus on organisational practices in SSCM. For example, Fabbe-Costes et al.
(2014) explore the empirical reality of environmental scanning practices at different levels,
and from individuals and groups of people to the entire societal environment. Ahmad et al.
(2017) conceptualise the key factors for SSCM, speciﬁcally for the oil and gas industry,
by deﬁning organisational aspects as internal factors. Govindan (2018) identiﬁes the indi-
cators, drivers and barriers of sustainable consumption and production by centring on the
food industry based on the stakeholder theory. The identiﬁcation of barriers and drivers is a
topic covered by Narimissa et al. (2020), who focus on implementing and improving SSCM.
From a process perspective, Dubey et al. (2017) adopt system components approach to deﬁne
a conceptual framework for green SCM to contribute to the knowledge-based view theory
and systems theory. Brandenburg et al. (2019) illustrate how SC structures and processes
are integrated into the components of its natural, social, business and political environments.
Najjar et al. (2020) focus on selecting a sustainability strategy across a multitiered SC and
not only on a focal organisation’s immediate suppliers and customers. Other authors focus
on developing assessment frameworks for SSC. For instance, the proposal by Schöggl et al.
(2016) provides the basis for assessing and aggregating sustainability information. Qorri
et al. (2018) provide new insights into sustainability performance measurement approaches,
SC conﬁguration and metrics selection by applying multicriteria decision-making methods.
Finally, Michel-Villarreal et al. (2021) attempt to extend current research by proposing a
conceptual framework that summarises strategies to support SC resilience capabilities in the
short food SCs context by contemplating sustainability aspects.
Table 1 summarises the above-mentioned research and groups them according to the objec-
tive of the conceptual framework. The criteria to classify them are: SC processes where,
in this criterion, the proposed conceptual framework aims to deﬁne the key elements for
processes within SCs that relate them to sustainability concepts; a risk assessment where
proposals focus on developing a risk assessment tool by incorporating sustainability fac-
tors; an organisational approach where frameworks focus on identifying the organisational
contextual factors to incorporate sustainability into SCs; an SC assessment where, in this
category, proposals focus on developing conceptual frameworks to assess SC performance,
sustainability and key indicators; resilience capabilities for which the publications in this
criterion seek to synthesise strategies that support SCs’ resilience capabilities.
Having reviewed the main characteristics and conceptual frameworks in relation to SSCs,
we were unable to ﬁnd any study that provides a conceptual framework oriented to develop
quantitative models for LIT problems in an SSC, which is the main purpose of the herein
proposed conceptual framework.

The proposed conceptual framework is based on ﬁve main blocks: the CLSC structure
block, the sustainability input block, the mathematical model block, the inventory policies
block and the sustainability objective block.
The CLSC structure block is based on the models presented by Akbari-Kasgari et al.
(2020), Al-Haidous et al. (2020), Moslemi et al. (2017) and Jindal et al. (2015). The general
network consists of four-echelon forward logistics and four-echelon reverse logistics. A
forward SC is a structure consisting of suppliers, i, processing plants,j, distributors, k, and
customers, r. The proposed structure contemplates the reverse logistics, a collection and
repair stage, c, a recycling stage, d, a recycled material storage stage, s, and a disposal stage.
Figure 2 describes the above-explained SC structure by incorporating the parameters and
decision variables for the design of the SSC that presents a closed loop structure, as well
as the material ﬂow in forward and reverse ways. The proposed parameters and variables
are classiﬁed according to LIT decisions, and also consider the sustainability approach with
economic, environmental and social dimensions.
For the sustainability input block, speciﬁc parameters to fulﬁl the sustainable objectives
are proposed. The ﬁrst considered aspect is the economic one. It is transversal to any study
that seeks SC sustainability to contemplate the economic factor, of which the main inputs for
the optimisation models are costs, revenues, prices and capital. For the environmental factor,
the most recurrent impact is related to CO2 emissions from energy use (see Saif & Elhedhli,
2016). This is relevant because SC activities are often responsible for more than 75% of
GHG emissions (see Huang et al., 2009). It is important to develop the social dimension
because an unbalanced situation towards sustainability pillars exists and leads to research
gaps appearing in the social sustainability topic (Becerra et al., 2021; Cañas et al., 2020;
Popovic et al., 2017).

The conceptual framework also proposes the need for quantitative models to develop a sus-
tainable LIT model. A block for mathematical modelling by means of an optimisation-based
simulation model is speciﬁcally proposed (see Fig. 3). This block contemplates strategical
and tactical SC problems, such as location, inventory and transportation problems. Here
optimisation mechanisms should be developed to support the decision making of facility and
warehouse location, collection centres, selection of suppliers and distributors, and optimal

transportation of products in forward and reverse logistics, including inventory management
decisions. One of the objectives of the optimisation model is to discover the optimal location
of processing plants, distribution centres, collection and repair centres, recycle centres and
scrap warehouses. This objective can be met by optimising the SC network structure, loca-
tions and scales of facilities (Ivanov, 2018). The proposed parameters are the capital cost of
investment, capacities of facilities, the GHG emission rates of facilities, the number of jobs
required for that size and the external cost associated with the impact on socio-environmental
welfare. Transportation decisions are related to the selection of the best mode for transport-
ing products in the SC. Employing better transportation decisions results in partly cutting
costs and, in turn, results in travelling a shorter total distance. This can also help to reduce
fuel consumption and, hence, GHG emissions. After estimating fuel consumption amounts,
they are estimated in relation to carbon emission levels by means of a fuel conversion factor
(Glock & Kim, 2015; Soysal et al., 2015). The distribution cost includes a ﬁxed cost and
a variable cost. The ﬁxed cost is incurred whenever a vehicle is dispatched on a route. It
may involve any cost that does not depend on the transported lot size, the route, the number
of stops on the route, among others. The variable cost component depends on the travelled
distance (Castellano et al., 2019).
When developing a SIM in the context of a SC different approaches need to be considered
to incorporate inventory decisions into the quantitative model. On the one hand, one can
consider the sustainable EOQ model proposed by Battini et al. (2014) or a discrete-event
simulation model (Saif & Elhedhli, 2016) or through an MRP model (Melnyk et al., 2001),
among others.

assumed. Hence, they are responsible for optimizing the inventory held by processing plants
and recycle centers, respectively.

The optimisation-based simulation model goal is to understand the impact of input param-

Finally, a block with sustainability objectives is presented. The main pursued sustainability
purposes correspond to economic and environmental (Becerra et al., 2021). For the economic
aspect, the commonest criterion is minimisation of costs, e.g., total inventory costs, total
transportation costs and total location costs (Guo et al., 2018). The sum of all these costs
and their minimisation correspond to the model’s economic purpose. It is also possible
to incorporate the environmental objective by means of a cost factor; i.e., the quantity of
emitted gases is multiplied by a penalty price and incorporated into all the aforementioned
cost functions; or to directly deﬁne the objective of minimizing GHG emissions (Tang et al.,
2016; Ugarte et al., 2016). Finally, modelling the social purpose is more complex and there
are three ways to incorporate this aspect: ﬁrstly, minimisation of injuries while performing
storage and ordering activities; secondly, minimisation of the hazardousness of routes by
assigning a cost factor; thirdly, maximisation of the SC’s positive social impacts to increase
the created job opportunities in relation to the unemployment rate and to enhance balanced
economic development in local communities (Zhalechian et al., 2016). It is important to
highlight this multi-objective problem as one that is difﬁcult to solve, although there are
several ways to mathematically approach it.
Thus, in light of all this, the herein proposed conceptual framework aims to suggest a
theoretical basis to develop quantitative models to design CLSCs by considering developing
the three sustainable aspects in the LIT problem as shown in Fig. 3.

5 Framework validation
Here the conceptual framework is applied to show how it can be used as a diagnostic tool
to support the design of an SSC integrating the LIT decisions by interviewing a group of
decision makers, such as a CEO, and three managers and logistics engineers, in two different
sized mining companies. Based on the research by Ahmad et al. (2017), Fabbe-Costes et al.
(2014) and Sehnem et al. (2019), the questionnaire (Appendix) was designed to ascertain
the research participants’ opinions and experiences by means of semi-structured interviews.
It consisted of seven open-ended questions to identify the sustainability elements associated
with LIT issues in the SC. A checklist was also applied to check whether companies complied
with the sustainability criteria associated with the ﬁve conceptual framework blocks. This
methodology allows the application of the conceptual proposal to be diagnosed to design an
SSC. Thus diagnosing the SC of one medium-sized and one large-sized copper mining com-
pany through the proposed conceptual model is considered. The extractive nature of these
company types has direct environmental impacts and leads to conﬂicts with local commu-
nities. All this prompts decision makers to incorporate sustainability approaches into SCs.
Firstly, the medium-sized company incorporates sustainability mainly through its electricity
supplier, an unconventional renewable energy generation company with its bio-energy plant.
The selection of suppliers considers the choice of those with minimal or zero GHG gener-
ation. As for water use, which is critical input because this element is scarce in the desert
areas where the mine is located, the company recirculates most of the water employed in its
processes with low freshwater use. In inventory management terms, the company makes its
inventory decisions in conjunction with location and transportation decisions for cost reasons
only. The main inventory management objectives are to maintain low stock levels, increase
recycled materials, reuse transport elements (pallets) and reduce packaging and full load
trucks. The large-sized company aims to align the company’s strategic indicators with sus-
tainable development goals (SDG); for example, indicators of occupational health and safety,
diversity and inclusion, responsible use of resources like water, reduction of emissions and
waste, and energy efﬁciency. In addition, suppliers and local sourcing are prioritised over
global sourcing by moving towards CE development. The local community’s perception of
the company’s activities is also measured. In inventory management terms, the company
has a policy to produce to maintain the inventory. Like the medium-sized company, water is
recirculated because this resource is critical.

6 Discussion and conclusions
This article presents a conceptual framework to develop optimisation and simulation models
3S-LIT in CLSCs. Firstly, an analysis of the 14 identiﬁed existing conceptual frameworks
on sustainable SCs was carried out to mainly identify the key factors or drivers that allow a
performance assessment or a risk analysis in SCs. Thus most researchers focus on propos-
ing frameworks to guide the incorporation of sustainability into SC processes. Firstly,
some research works seek to extend the SCM concept by including sustainability concepts
(Kalverkamp & Young, 2019; Svensson, 2007). Others focus on building a tool that facilitates
123

Annals of Operations Research
the implementation of sustainability into SCs (Dubey et al., 2017; Narimissa et al., 2020),
by speciﬁcally identifying the barriers and drivers that enable people to adopt sustainabil-
ity (Ahmad et al., 2017; Govindan, 2018). Furthermore, the identiﬁcation of practices and
the selection of strategies need to address sustainability issues (Brandenburg et al., 2019;
Michel-Villarreal et al., 2021; Najjar et al., 2020). Finally, the development of conceptual
frameworks that focus on the assessment of SC sustainability are divided into those that assess
risk (Rostamzadeh et al., 2018; Seuring & Müller, 2008), and those that assess SCs’ perfor-
mance (Qorri et al., 2018; Schöggl et al., 2016). However, no study presents a conceptual
framework to facilitate the design of an SC from the 3S-LIT perspective with an optimisa-
tion model, as herein proposed. Therefore, ﬁve constructive blocks are proposed to fulﬁl our
research objectives: sustainability input, the mathematical model, the CLSC structure, the
sustainability objectives block and inventory policies (RO1). This framework was validated
and applied as a diagnostic tool in two different sized copper mining companies (RO2). It is
concluded that: (i) the medium-sized company is recommended to incorporate: the measure-
ment of environmental impacts as GHG levels into inventory, production and transportation
activities; the amount of waste generated in inventory activities; social impacts as the injury
rates and hazardousness levels of transportation routes; (ii) the large-sized company is sug-
gested to include KPIs of social aspects in their strategy and to avoid producing to maintain
the inventory; (iii) both companies are encouraged to use optimisation and simulation mod-
els to support decision making in inventory, transportation and facility location problems.
Collecting and recycling copper scraps, and incorporating this material into the processing
stage, are also recommended.
Managerial implications aim to encourage researchers and practitioners to deﬁne and
categorize LIT problems by considering the three sustainability aspects. In addition, the
proposed conceptual framework can be act as a reference to develop an optimization model
to propose indices, parameters and variables by considering sustainability factors to be both
inputs of the system and objectives of the model. It can also act as a reference to develop a
simulation model to evaluate the performance of inventory policies based on the designed
optimal network.
Finally, a forthcoming work aims to formulate and solve a multi-objective mathematical
programming model to manage SC decisions by considering the three sustainable aspects,
integrating location, inventory and transportation decisions applying it to a mining SC.
