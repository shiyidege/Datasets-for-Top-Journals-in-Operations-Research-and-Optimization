Abstract
Replacement may occur during the mission in accordance with traditional replacement poli-
cies. However, this approach may not be feasible for deactivating a mission-oriented system
when a mission is initiated to carry out maintenance operations. In this context, we intro-
duce replacement policies for k-out-of-n systems considering mission durations. In reliability
engineering, k-out-of-n systems are a key class of conÔ¨Āgurations, consisting of n indepen-
dent components. These systems remain operational as long as at least k components are
continuously functional, with overall performance and reliability depending on the condition
that a minimum of k components are active. Each unit of the system experiences two types of
failures. When a type I failure occurs, a failed unit undergoes minimal repair, whereas in the
case of a type II failure, the unit remains idle. The system experiences complete failure when
n ‚ąík + 1 units are idle, and requires corrective replacement. Initially, a basic replacement
policy for mission-critical k-out-of-n systems is presented, along with a discussion on the
determination of the optimal number of units. Next, we incorporate the notations of ‚Äúre-
placement Ô¨Ārst‚ÄĚ and ‚Äúreplacement last‚ÄĚ separately. ‚ÄúReplacement Ô¨Ārst‚ÄĚ refers to preventive
replacement occurring at the scheduled time or when the mission is completed, whichever
happens Ô¨Ārst. ‚ÄúReplacement last‚ÄĚ means that preventive replacement takes place when either
of the above events occurs, whichever happens last. Subsequently, the optimal policy for
each model is analyzed analytically. A numerical example is then provided to illustrate the

1 Introduction
In practical applications, most systems experience degradation over time due to age and/or
usage. When a system can no longer deliver the intended output for which it was designed, it is
considered to have failed. At this point, the failed system can be restored to operational status
through appropriate maintenance operations like repair or replacement. Replacement poli-
cies include planned and unplanned actions. Planned replacement is commonly referred to as
preventive replacement, whereas unplanned replacement is termed corrective replacement.
To achieve satisfactory reliability performance, it is recommended to implement preven-
tive replacement for a degraded system. Recent studies have been conducted on models of
preventive replacement. (Jian, 2018; Sheu et al., 2023, 2024; Zhao et al., 2017).
The redundancy technique is the most effective method for enhancing system reliability.
The k-out-of-n conÔ¨Āguration is a typical form of redundancy. In this context, the parameter
k indicates the number of units in the system that must function properly for the entire
system to operate effectively. When k = 1, the model resembles a parallel system, while
when k = n, it transforms into a series system. The k-out-of-n system has a wide range of
applications in engineering, telecommunications, medicine and others Ô¨Āelds. For example,
if at least 4 of the 5 pumps in a hydraulic control system are functioning properly, the system
is functioning properly. If two or more pumps fail, the system will not operate at maximum
capacity, overall system efÔ¨Āciency will decrease, and they must be replaced at a scheduled
time to achieve the expected reliability. At the same time, we want to use the system for as
long as possible to accomplish the required work. If such systems fail during the execution
of missions, it will cause unimaginable accidents (like security monitoring systems) or huge
economic losses (like production systems). Therefore, numerous researchers (Eryilmaz &
Devrimb, 2019; Lorvand & Kelkinnama, 2023; Sheu et al., 2019) have focused on studying
this type of system.
Like other systems, the reliability of k-out-of-n systems can also be enhanced through
maintenance activities such as repair or replacement. The age replacement policy is often
considered the most replacement approach for systems prone to catastrophic failure. Both
theoreticallyandpractically,themaintenanceofk-out-of-n systemsusingtheagereplacement
policy has garnered signiÔ¨Ācant attention.
Park et al. (2019) examined a maintenance policy for a k-out-of-n system and explored
the optimal replacement age in cases where unit failure is not immediately detected prior
to failure. The optimal replacement policy for k-out-of-n systems was investigated by Sheu
et al. (2019). Mannai and Gasmi (2020) studied the optimal design of k-out-of-n system
when standard, Ô¨Ārst and last age replacements are applied. Zhang et al. (2020) dealt with
condition-based maintenance policy for k-out-of-n system where the degradation of each
component is modelled by a pure jump L√©vy process. Wang et al., (2022a, 2022b) proposed
replacement policies for a repairable k-out-of-n system, considering the system‚Äôs deviation
costs. For other research results on k-out-of-n system replacement policies, please refer to

Reza (2020), Wang and Zhu (2021), Wang et al., (2022a, 2022b), Fahrettin (2023), Zheng
et al., (2023a, 2023b, 2023c), Majid (2024), and Liu et al. (2024). However, most related

execution process. Few scholars have studied age replacement policies for k-out-of-n systems
with mission duration.
In contrast, the traditional replacement policy involves replacing the system preventively
at a scheduled time, regardless of the mission duration, even if the system is tasked with
completing a series of operational missions. However, if preventive replacement is carried
out during the execution of the mission, it may decrease the system‚Äôs required reliability
while performing the mission. Subsequently, many researchers (Guo et al., 2013; Levitin &
Finkelstein, 2018; Liu et al., 2016; O‚ÄôNeil et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024)
attempted to integrate mission considerations into preventive replacement strategies.
Zhao et al. (2021) developed a preventive replacement policy for mission-oriented sys-
tems and found that without considering mission duration, the replacement time could be
either underestimated or overestimated. Zhang et al. (2021) examined the periodic replace-
ment policy for a system designed to carry out multiple missions. Fang et al. (2022) explored
age replacement considering mission duration for multi-state systems. In Zhang et al. (2023)
extended these age replacement policies for mission-oriented systems by incorporating dis-
counting. However, the above references only considered one-unit systems. Liu et al. (2022)
presented a multi-mission selective maintenance and repairpersons assignment model con-
sidering the stochasticity of three kinds of durations. Zhang et al. (2022) analyzed a parallel
system for mission execution and proposed age replacement policies. Zheng et al., (2023a,
2023b, 2023c) investigated the optimization problem of a repair-replacement policy for sys-
tems subject to intermittent missions of random types and random durations. Feng et al.
(2024) developed a selective maintenance model for optimizing the selective maintenance
problem in mission-oriented systems operating under stochastic mission and break durations
and s-dependent competing risks. Zhang and Fang (2025) integrated mission durations into
age replacement policies for k-out-of-n systems. They assumed that after system failure, the
system is restored to ‚Äúas good as new‚ÄĚ (perfect maintenance or simple replacement) condi-
tion. However, a more realistic repair is to restore the system to a state between ‚Äúas good
as new‚ÄĚ and ‚Äúas bad as old‚ÄĚ, which is the idea of the imperfect repair model. SpeciÔ¨Ācally,
two failure mechanisms (minor or catastrophic failure) can be used to simulate imperfect
maintenance activities: minor failure undergoes minimal repair, while catastrophic failure is
replaced.
In this paper, we present an age-dependent imperfect maintenance model for k-out-of-n
systems engaged in a sequence of operational missions. First, the basic replacement model
is developed, and the optimal number of units is determined by minimizing the average cost
rate. Replacement policies should take replacement timing into account to balance main-
tainability and cost trade-offs. Thus, we analyze the concepts of ‚Äúreplacement Ô¨Ārst‚ÄĚ and
‚Äúreplacement last‚ÄĚ separately. ‚ÄúReplacement Ô¨Ārst‚ÄĚ refers to preventive replacement occurring
at the scheduled time or when the mission is completed, whichever happens Ô¨Ārst. ‚ÄúReplace-
ment last‚ÄĚ means that preventive replacement takes place when either of the above events
occurs, whichever happens last.
The main contributions of this paper are as follows. The average cost rates are derived
for the k-out-of-n system when considering mission durations and age-dependent imperfect
maintenance quality. The optimal total number of units with mission durations is studied in
the cases of basic replacement, replacement Ô¨Ārst and replacement last. We also analyze the
impact of mission durations on optimal n. The optimal scheduled time is obtained under the
cases of replacement Ô¨Ārst and replacement last.

7 Conclusion
This paper discusses replacement policies for k-out-of-n systems with mission durations.
Initially, a basic replacement policy for such systems is proposed, and the optimization
problem related to the number of units is solved. Both replacement Ô¨Ārst and replacement last
are examined to explore the trade-off between maintainability and cost. For each policy, the

In this paper, we assume that the k-out-of-n system is composed of identical and inde-

each other. Additionally, we consider mission duration to investigate the k-out-of-n system
where age replacement policy is implemented, and not consider limits on the number of
repairs. In practice, some k-out-of-n systems may implement a periodic replacement policy
or a replacement policy based on the number of repairs. Therefore, it would be meaningful
and interesting to study these topics in the future.
