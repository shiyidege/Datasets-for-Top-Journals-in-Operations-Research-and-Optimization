Abstract
The existing literature on the economic order quantity (EOQ) problem with backordering 
does not address the impact of batch shipments on backordering behavior in a business 
to customer (B2C) environment. This study develops inventory models for a retailer re­
ceiving batch shipments and managing inventory through backorders. In this scenario, a 
large quantity of items is received, some of which are found to be defective. To identify 
defective items, the retailer conducts a 100% inspection of the goods received. Once 
inspected, the saleable products are added to the warehouse inventory in batches, rather 
than individually. The retailer follows a policy of receiving equal-sized batches at regular 
time intervals, deciding on the number of batches, as well as the ordering and backorder­
ing quantities. The analysis explores two approaches for handling defective products, in­
corporating time-proportioning for the backordering cost and a penalty cost for each lost 
unit. The classical optimization technique is applied to determine the optimal policy. A 
numerical example demonstrates the theory, with results showing that partial recovery of 
customer loyalty and product repair are more profitable approaches.

1  Introduction
It has been traditionally assumed that product availability enhances customer involvement 
and, as a consequence, purchases intention. Thus, significant resources are expended on 
ensuring products are continuously available to customers (Steinhart et al., 2013). It is not 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 351:1941–1988
uncommon for customers to encounter difficulties in locating the desired product within a 
store, a situation known as a stockout (shortage). The manner in which customers respond 
to such situations appears to be multifaceted. In assessing customers’ willingness to wait 
for restocking when an item was unavailable, Corsten and Gruen (2005) found only 15% 
of customers would wait for new stock, 45% would choose a different product, 31% would 
seek another supplier, and 9% would abandon the purchase altogether. A further study indi­
cates that 23% of customers delay their purchase decisions if their preferred item is not 
in stock (Bijvank & Vis, 2011). Furthermore, stockouts have a detrimental effect on both 
retailers and manufacturers. The cancellation of purchases and the necessity of rebranding 
can result in declining sales for the manufacturer (Campo et al., 2000), while stockouts are 
particularly concerning for retailers because of the losses they incur from lost sales if cus­
tomers switch stores when they cannot find their desired product (Kumar et al., 2021). This 
also has a negative impact on order volumes (Campo et al., 2003). The financial impact of 
out-of-stocks, overstock and returns on online and in-store retailers in the US had reached 
$1.75 trillion by 2019, with this loss increasing over time (Son et al., 2019).
On the other hand, for products that consumers are unable to do without, have high brand 
value and a lack of alternatives, the majority of consumers opt to purchase the item at a 
later date when it is no longer available (Pinçe, 2021). Furthermore, some high-ticket items, 
such as furniture and appliances, are often specially ordered, rather than sold from the store 
inventory (Nahmias & Smith, 1994). Moreover, consumers' store loyalty also prompts them 
to postpone the product they prefer in case of stockout. This reduces sales losses for both the 
retailer and the manufacturer (Ru, 2024). These results show that consumers’ heterogeneous 
responses to stockouts afford retailers the opportunity to deliver some orders at a later date. 
Nevertheless, numerous studies in the inventory literature on lot size determination for an 
infinite planning horizon are concerned with what happens when the entire pre-stockout 
demand is backordered, i.e., when customers' demands are met with a new delivery. From 
the results of the aforementioned studies, it can be concluded that such models are not 
encountered in most applications in the B2C (Business to Customer) environment. In B2B 
(Business to Business) trade operations, however, a full backorder is a common occurrence.
It is also possible that the quality of the product may not always meet the expected 
requirements. However, it should be noted that product quality depends heavily on the pro­
duction process functioning correctly. Consequently, if this process does not work properly, 
it may result in the production of defective products. For instance, machine malfunctions 
in the garment industry can cause fabric faults or defects, such as broken or missing-pick 
yarns (Hanbay et al., 2016; Mahajan et al., 2009). Defective products in the textile industry 
can be purchased at a discounted price by third markets, and such clothes can then be used 
to make other derived products such as yarns, mats, rugs, squeegees, pillow and mattress 
fillings (Gautam et al., 2019). Defective products can also be restored to perfect quality rela­
tively cheaply, significantly reducing the overall costs of production and inventory mainte­
nance. In numerous manufacturing sectors, including textiles, defects can occasionally be 
concealed by means of various kinds of repair, including new dyeing or repairing surface 
damage. Economical repairs to viable imperfect parts of the product are preferred over pur­
chasing new ones due to the significant economic consequences. These products are often 
subject to cycles rather than being newly acquired. In the United States military, repairable 
items represent approximately $30 billion (Díaz & Fu, 1997). The repair of defective prod­
ucts is a strategic choice that complies with environmental challenges that can influence

In the contemporary business environment, characterised by intense competition among 
manufacturing and retailing companies, effective decision-making regarding resource allo­
cation and operational scheduling has become imperative. In the context of expanding mar­
ket sizes, enhancing efficiency in production and sales has become a pivotal factor in the 
success of manufacturing and retailing companies. The impetus for effective production and 
inventory planning is to address the competing demands of resources and customer needs 
within a stipulated timeframe. Over the past few decades, a number of effective solutions 
have been devised for relatively standard problems, which do not take into account issues 
such as stockouts and their associated costs. Nevertheless, the stockout strategy in inventory 
planning, in conjunction with the batch shipments and associated issues, remains a subject 
that has received comparatively less attention from researchers.
Within the context of industrial production and inventory management, it is widely 
acknowledged that the two most significant constraints pertain to production capacity and 
inventory capacity. In numerous process industries, production capacity can be executed 
at maximum levels; however, customer demand may be constrained. On the other hand, 
there are instances where production is unable to meet customer demands. Such instances

are characterised by the exorbitant costs associated with the initial setup and manufactur­
ing process, or the substantial costs involved in product holding and product storage, if the 
products require prolonged storage periods.
It is acknowledged that in the domain of inventory planning, two primary objectives are 
the fulfilment of customer demands and the minimisation of cost. However, it is important 
to note that these two objectives are not always aligned. For instance, an overstocking of 
inventory may maximise the satisfaction of customer demand, but the cost of holding inven­
tory may be too high, resulting in the total cost not being the lowest. Consequently, from 
a cost perspective, customer demands may not be fully met. In commercial activities, an 
unsatisfied demand constitutes a sale loss to a retail company. This paper refers to this situa­
tion as stockout. The strategy employed to address this issue involves the deliberate creation 
of stockout situations. During stockout, we allow a combination of backordered and non-
backordered demands. That is, given the interests of needs of customers, a b fraction of the 
demand in each period of shortages corresponds to customers willing to wait until a new 
batch arrives, and the rest are lost sales.
In inventory planning, there is a facet that, while not having been exhaustively delved 
into, holds significant practical importance. This is the issue where downstream retail­
ers send products in batches to the working inventory warehouse and a stockout situation 
occurs. From a theoretical and practical standpoint, the joint optimisation of inventory and

dividing them into two research streams.
The papers in the first stream posit that some of the products in the received orders are 
defective, thereby casting doubt on whether the production process of this manufacturer 
are reliable. Rosenblatt and Lee (1986) hypothesised that a problematic production process 
can be made reliable again over a period which follows an exponential distribution, while 
defective products produced can be restored to perfect condition at a cost. They also found 
that economical batch sizes diminish with the presence of defective products. Porteus’s 
(1986) model identified a correlation between item quality and the size of the lot, with 
similar outcomes. Schwaller (1988) incorporated into the classical EOQ model two key 
assumptions: first, that delivered lots contain defective products; and second, that identify­
ing such defective products incurs both fixed and variable screening costs. Furthermore, the 
supplier agrees to reimburse the buyer for defective products. Vörös (1999) devised a model 
to determine economic production quantities under the assumption of finite production. It 
was concluded that the batch size is influenced by the quality of the production process, 
with a decrease in process quality resulting in a reduction in holding and setup costs. In 
their study of an inspection policy for lot size determination in an EOQ model, Salameh and 
Jaber (2000) assumed full screening of each received lot, and sales of defective products 
at a discounted price in the secondary market as a single lot on conclusion of the screening 
period. Their numerical analysis revealed that the optimum order quantity increases with an 
increase in the proportion of defective products in the received batches.
Hayek and Salameh (2001) obtained the optimal production quantity under the assump­
tion that a known probability distribution governs the proportion of defective products. In 
the developed model, stockouts are considered and completely backordered by new orders. 
Chiu (2003) postulated that defective products are either scrapped or repaired within a pro­
duction system that sometimes creates defective products. Chiu’s developed model per­
mits backorders and derives formulae to determine the optimal quantities for production 
and backorders. Wee et al. (2007) expanded the EOQ model to include defective products, 
assuming that stockouts would occur and that backorders would be fulfilled immediately. 
Eroglu and Ozdemir (2007) derived the economic order quantity by assuming that the lot 
screening process would begin immediately and that backorders would be fulfilled entirely 
from good-quality products.
Konstantaras et al. (2007) devised two models for EOQ calculations, assuming defective 
products to exist in delivered lots. The retailer performs a 100% inspection at the inspection 
warehouse, where the products are received. The demand is to be met from good-quality 
items, and suitable items are transferred to the working inventory in fixed-size batches. They 
also considered the possibility of selling defective items as a single lot when the inspection 
process is complete, or alternatively, that defective items are reworked. Cárdenas-Barrón 
(2009) expanded Jamal et al.’s (2004) single-stage rework model to encompass planned

1945

backorders. Yoo et al. (2009) assumed that the producer's product screening process was 
faulty and that sales refunds were available for customers who purchased faulty products. 
Maddah et al. (2010) presented a method called ‘order overlap scheme’ to prevent shortages 
during the product scanning process. This method envisages placing a new order when the 
stock level on hand is sufficient to meet the demand arising during scanning. Taleizadeh 
et al. (2010) investigated an imperfect manufacturing process, hypothesising that defec­
tive products would be disposed of. The study incorporated shortages for inventory control 
purposes and considered the creation of a fully backordered inventory. Furthermore, the 
study assumed a limitation on the production capacity of a single machine that generated 
the entire output. Khan et al. (2011) reviewed emerging studies based on the model devised 
by Salameh and Jaber (2000).
Taleizadeh et al’s. (2011) research focused on the analysis of two imperfect production 
systems. These systems were modelled as multiproduct single-machine configurations, with 
and without rework, under the assumption of shortages and backorders. Tsou et al. (2012) 
divided the products produced in the production problem into three categories: good qual­
ity, defective, and discarded, and considered the sale of defective products at a reduced 
price, or reprocessing or rejecting them as soon as they are detected. Cárdenas-Barrón et al. 
(2012) studied an economic production lot size problem with partial remanufacturing and 
an approach involving multiple deliveries, and developed two solution procedures to derive 
the optimal replenishment lot size and the optimal number of shipments. Hsu and Hsu’s 
(2013) EOQ model incorporated the presence of defective products, scanning errors, stock­
out allowances, and sales returns. Taleizadeh et al. (2013) proposed a multi-product EPQ 
model with random defective items and failure in repair. The model is based on a single 
machine, so the capacity and output are limited and there will be shortages. The research 
aims to find the cycle length, how much to produce, and the amount of backordered demand, 
to make the total expected cost minimum.
Jaber et al. (2014) determined the economic order quantity in cases where defective prod­
ucts are repaired or good-quality products are equal to the number of these products pur­
chased from a local supplier. This was done under the assumption that batches are received 
from a distant supplier and therefore defective products cannot be replaced by sending them 
to the same supplier. Bazan et al. (2015) examined the environmental and economic con­
sequences of energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions within the context of an 
EOQ model. Through a case study of the tyre remanufacturing industry, they demonstrated 
that remanufacturing can be disregarded when considering the financial and environmental 
costs collectively. Zhou et al. (2016) obtained optimal order quantities and maximum stock­
out levels when trade credit and scanning errors were present. Taleizadeh and Noori-daryan 
(2016) developed an EPQ model for a supply chain with three levels. This model included 
several suppliers, a single manufacturer and several retailers, all of which competed with 
each other. It also included a process for reworking products, and looked at both centralised 
and decentralised structures. Hauck et al. (2021) proposed an imperfect EOQ model. Unlike 
Hauck and Vörös (2015), who built EOQ models to calculate the optimal order quantity and 
rate of inspection for general and specific functions of investment cost, Hauck et al. (2021) 
assumed screening speed, i.e., time for screening per unit and order lot size, as decision 
variables. Lin (2021) studied an inventory system with imperfect production, fallible repair, 
full backordering and uncertain demand. Condeixa et al.’s (2022) contribution to the litera­
ture was a proposed sustainable EOQ model, incorporating social considerations and carbon

emissions. Their findings indicate that the increased social costs arising from remanufactur­
ing facilities may negate the beneficial impact on the environmental from recycling.
The second research stream comprises studies utilising inventory models with partial 
backorders. Montgomery et al. (1973) effectively initiated research in this area, studying 
an EOQ setting where the stockout of inventory resulted in lost sales and backorders. In 
their proposed model, Montgomery et al. (1973) employed lot size and shortage quantity 
as decision variables. However, they converted these variables into two new variables: the 
total amount of demand during a cycle period, and the stock on hand at the beginning of 
each cycle. Rosenberg (1979) addressed the same problem in his work, beginning with the 
identical decision variables but substituting two additional variables. The first is the length 
of the inventory cycle, while the second is a fictitious demand rate, which is a composite of 
the inventory cycle and the original lot size and shortage quantity. Mak (1987) developed 
a model for determining the optimal EPQ in the event of stockouts that are only partially 
backordered. The model employs two decision variables: the length of a production-inven­
tory cycle and the time elapsed between the inventory level reaching zero and the com­
mencement of the subsequent production run.
Park (1982) reformulated the deterministic EOQ problem under the assumption that the 
backorder cost is time-proportional and that lost sales have a fixed penalty cost. The optimal 
operational variables, total demand per cycle and maximum shortage level, are calculated to 
minimize total cost. Nahmias and Smith (1994) investigated an integrated inventory man­
agement system with partial backorders. They assumed that deliveries from the warehouse 
to the retailers would occur instantaneously, i.e., with zero lead times for replenishment. Wu 
and Tsai (2001) defined the order quantity as a decision variable, along with the delivery 
time, within the context of an inventory model. The model allows for out-of-stock situa­
tions, with the maximum stockout level including the amount of backorders and lost sales. 
Zeng (2001) developed a mathematical model that incorporates production and order situ­
ations, including special cases such as the prohibition of backorders, full backorders, and 
partial backorders. The author begins with the order quantity and the maximum shortage 
level as decision variables, but replaces the order quantity with another variable: the produc­
tion inventory cycle.
Yang et al. (2006) proposed an integrated decision model subject to perfect and monop­
olistic competitions. The study revealed that a notable reduction in expenditure can be 
achieved when partial backordering is taken into consideration. They used the order lot size, 
stockout level and number of batches as decision variables. Pentico and Drake (2009) con­
sidered an EOQ problem with partial backordering. The inventory cycle length and the frac­
tion of demand to be met by existing stock are taken as decision variables. They presented 
a new condition for the backordering rate, which makes optimal the partial backlogging 
operating doctrine. In a related study, Pentico et al. (2009) employed an EPQ model with 
the same cost and demand assumptions as those used by Mak (1987). However, the assump­
tion was made that existing backorders would be fulfilled before any new demands were 
met, with only a proportion of the new orders that could not be immediately fulfilled being 
placed on backorder, with the remainder being lost sales. Moreover, they used two decision 
variables, which are the same as in Pentico and Drake (2009).
Roy et al. (2011) proposed a mathematical model considering defective products and 
shortages. It was assumed that the defective products were returned in one batch, at the 
price paid to the supplier, and that during the period of stockout, a proportion of the total

demand, varying with waiting times, was backordered for those customers who were able 
to wait, and was satisfied by backlogged items. In contrast, those customers who could not 
wait were unable to obtain the products they required, and this was deemed a loss of sales. 
Pentico and Drake (2011) examined deterministic inventory models that consider partial 
backordering. Wee et al. (2014) developed an EPQ model that allows partial backorders and 
takes both linear and fixed backordering costs into account. The novel approach employed 
by this model instead utilises time intervals rather than production quantity and shortage 
level as the decision variables in deriving the optimal problem solution. This approach 
enables the derivation of a critical backordering rate, thereby facilitating the identification 
of the optimal workable solution. Taleizadeh et al. (2016) expanded the EOQ model concept 
with repair of defective products through local repairers. This is done for the case of short­
ages, where partial backordering is allowed. The inventory cycle length and the fraction of 
demand to be met by existing stock are taken as decision variables. The outcomes of the 
numerical illustration indicated that the maximum total profit is obtained for the case in 
which repaired items are added to the inventory at an inventory level of zero. Cunha et al. 
(2018) proposed an EPQ model that considers partial backordering and discounts for defec­
tive products. The model assumes that the demand quantity and partial backordering rate 
are known. Through numerical analysis, it has been demonstrated that it is more profitable 
for the manufacturer to sell defective products in a timely manner and to maintain planned 
shortages. Öztürk (2022) studied an EPQ model in which shortages are permitted with par­
tial backordering and defective products are sent to a third-party facility for repair. It was 
assumed that incoming orders are initially filled at the beginning of the production period, 
with the inventory cycle length and the fraction of demand to be met by existing stock serv­
ing as decision variables. San-Jose et al. (2022) studied the inventory system that takes into 
account the order of a single product. In this system, they considered the randomness and 
predictability of the demand for the relevant product. They used the partial order approach 
in inventory control. In developing a comprehensive inventory model, Yadav et al. (2023) 
incorporated a twofold consideration: firstly, the incorporation of partial backorders, and 
secondly, the recognition of imperfections within the production process. The deterioration 
rate is constant. They observed that in actual production systems, the implementation of a 
screening process conducted by automated machines is paramount in addressing the issue of 
substandard products, under the assumption that the defect rate may vary due to random fac­
tors. Yu et al. (2024) presented a comprehensive production-inventory model, meticulously 
designed to address the ramifications of pandemic-induced demand and pricing fluctua­
tions. The primary objective of the model was to enhance the efficiency of replenishment, 
dispatching, and backordering procedures, with a strategic aim towards achieving optimal 
profit maximisation for high-tech industry.
Although the partial backorder strategy has been extensively studied in B2C environ­
ments, academic research tends to neglect the batch transfer of goods to the working inven­
tory warehouse. In practice, goods undergoing inspection are transferred to the working 
inventory in batches rather than on a unit-by-unit basis. Konstantaras et al. (2007) discuss 
how a retailer can handle a fixed batch of good items and determine optimal order quantity 
and number of batches based on deterministic demand. The references consider the retailer

and the manufacturer/distributor, who are allowed to partially backorder their demands. It is 
assumed that the manufacturer/distributor faces demands similar to those from end custom­
ers. Manufacturers and distributors often face batch demands from retailers, who in turn 
face demands from end customers (Huang & Wu, 2016). As far as we are aware, there is 
currently no EOQ literature that considers both partial backorders and batch shipments for 
retailers, despite the latter being a common issue in supply chain management.
Our study diverges from previous research that has concentrated on the manufacturer/
distributor partial backorder strategy. Instead, we adopt a more comprehensive approach by 
examining the case of a retailer who is confronted with demands from end customers and 
has opted to partially backorder their demands. Our analysis encompasses an inventory that 
is subject to a partial backordering strategy. In order to elucidate the distinctive contribu­
tion of our paper in the existing literature, we present a comparative overview in Table 1. 
Acknowledging the absence of rigorous academic inquiry in this domain and the prevalence 
of partial backordering in practical settings, our study addresses the optimization of inven­
tory under a partial backordering strategy. Additionally, we incorporate the consideration of 
imperfect products in ordered lots, and batch shipments from an inspection warehouse to a 
working inventory warehouse.

considered production partial backordering in their decision models. In this paper, we 
adopt the retailer partial backordering policy, which better reflects the business-to-cus­
tomer environment. In light of this, we have extended the models put forth by Salameh 
and Jaber (2000) and Konstantaras et al. (2007) by examining the impact of a partial 
backordering strategy on the inventory management problem that involves fixed batch 
shipments. The aim is to maximise a retailer's profit by obtaining the optimal lot size, 
number of batches and backorder position. Two additional parameters are introduced: 
the maximum stockout level per cycle and the proportion of the backorders that is lost. 
The latter is considered a fixed value and is not included within the decision variables. 
The backordering cost is calculated based on the duration of the stock out situation. As 
in Zeng (2001), we begin with order lot size and the maximum shortage quantity as our 
decision variables, but replace the order quantity with the length of the inventory cycle. 
As a result, the profit function we develop is concave, and thus the optimal operating 
policy for the retailer can be determined by setting the two partial derivatives of the 
variables of interest equal to zero and solving the resulting equations simultaneously. 
The total profits in the presence of salvage and repair options for imperfect products 
are obtained and compared. A comparison of Table 1 reveals that our work builds upon 
previous research, with the objective of enhancing the suitability of the decision model 
for practical applications and providing a more comprehensive framework for guiding 
practice. This paper sets out to achieve three principal aims. The first of these is to 
develop a joint decision model of inventory with a partial backordering strategy. The 
second is to analyse the interaction of ordering and shipment decisions, with a view to 
identifying the optimal economic shipment policy. The third is to analyse the influence 
factors of partial backorders and to put forward a number of managerial implications

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the investigated

2  Model formulation
The problem in this study is to determine the optimal inventory control policy for a 
retailer operating to meet the demand of its end customers. The following paragraph 
sets out the descriptions and assumptions that underpin the retailer's decision-making 
model. The retailer is subject to annual demand of D units, with regular orders of fixed 
size y. Similar to the full backorder EOQ models, when demand occurs, the retailer 
must attempt to satisfy the demand from their on-hand inventory. If the retailer is unable 
to fulfil the demand with the amount of inventory on hand, the demand may be partially 
backordered. Each order contains both good-quality and defective products. Each batch 
of products received at the retailer’s warehouse is scanned at a fixed rate of x units per 
unit time. The products are classified into two categories: good and defective quality. 
During the quality control process, the good quality products are sent in batches q of 
equal size n times at equally spaced time intervals tS to the working inventory ware­

3983.29 to 3944.86). So, repair is less sensitive to defectives than salvage. This result can 
be explained as follows. For the retailer, the cost of its product is the total cost divided by 
the units sold. When p takes a minimum value (p=0.01), the cost of a unit of product to 
the retailer is $86.87 (8486.83/97.69) in the disposal scheme and $81.37 (8416.71/103.45) 
in the repair scheme. When p increases (from 0.01 to 0.15), the cost of a unit of product 
increases in the disposal scheme (from $86.87 to $93.07) and decreases in the repair scheme 
(from $81.34 to $69.23). That is, in the repair scheme, although the total cost increases, the 
unit cost decreases because the quantity of good quality products sold increases with the 
increase in the order quantity. For the range of the p-values, it can be seen that it is advis­
able to repair the defectives to get more profit. When looking at the management of defec­
tive products in terms of the environmental opportunities of supply chains, repair is more 
environmentally acceptable than salvage. This is because supply chains become less effec­
tive and the sustainability of supply chains is reduced if defective products are purchased 
and then not disposed of or recovered in an ecologically conscious manner. An increase 
in p leaves the number of batches unchanged for salvage scheme. Increasing p leads to 
an increase of the optimal lot size, while a decrease of the maximum shortage quantity 
and therefore backordered quantity for salvage. An increase in p causes an increase to the 
optimal cycle length for repair scheme, while it leads to a decrease for the salvage option. 
Reducing p allows the retailer to decrease the lot size from 4 to 3. At the same time, the 
lot size and the shortage quantity decrease. A comparison of the two disposal schemes is 
illustrated in Fig. 6. Moreover, Figs. 11 and 12 in Appendix D show the scatter plot of the 
total profit against the percentage of imperfect products for both schemes. In both schemes, 
we observe a linear relationship from the graphical formats and then we provide a simple 
linear regression model.

4  Managerial insights
We determine that the partial backordering model may not always offer a clear advantage, 
given that order lot size, firm profitability, and customer purchasing intention are not inher­
ently consistent. In conjunction with the existing findings, some managerial insights are 
proposed:
(1) The findings indicate that it is not advantageous for the retailer to receive a high propor­
tion of imperfect products, which can diminish profits by reducing the overall size of 
the product lot. It is recommended that the manager should make a quality inspection 
decision and warehouse storage management, and design a quality contract to incen­
tivize the supplier to improve product quality level. Moreover, it was found that the 
repair strategy is more profitable, even when percentage of defective products is high. 
Therefore, the manager should also consider a strategy to increase the performance of 
the inventory system includes remanufacturing imperfect products and selling them 
as good ones, generating additional income for retailer.For instance, the difference in 
profit loss between receiving at 0.01% and receiving at 15% of imperfect products 
in the repair scheme is ∼= $38 (0.96%) as compared to the salvage scheme ∼= $368 
(9.26%).
(2) The findings indicate that it is advantageous for retailers to minimise their inventory 
holding cost, as this enables them to reduce their holding costs by increasing the fre­
quency and size of shipments to the working inventory store. This, in turn, enables the 
retailer to allocate space for other products. The findings further indicate that adopting 
a repair scheme enhances profitability, as it augments sales while concurrently fostering 
an environmentally conscious approach to managing imperfect products.
(3) It is evident that the total profit of the inventory system is contingent upon the propor­
tion of backordered demand. Consequently, it would be advantageous for the inventory 
manager to encourage an escalation in the number of customers willing to defer their 
orders in anticipation of the subsequent re-stocking.The implementation of policies 
designed to augment backordering may offer a viable solution to this issue. One such 
strategy may encompass the sale of the product, or indeed other products, to customers

regarding their inventory schedules when excessive demands occur. The basic tradeoffs 
the retailer and customer face is the choice between a high fill rate (the rate of demand 
that is immediately met from stock), which can be achieved through on-hand stock, and 
a lower fill rate (using backorders), which can result in lower total inventory costs. Our 
model allows the retailer to capture the costs of these tradeoffs.
The present study is subject to certain limitations. The paper's underlying assumption was 
a deterministic demand function. While the utilisation of this particular demand function 
has been a pervasive feature within extant literature, the incorporation of a demand function 
incorporating stochastic components or price-dependent features would be a fruitful avenue 
for future research. Furthermore, the paper assumed that products of a high quality are sold 
exclusively through brick-and-mortar stores. However, it would be interesting to offer such 
products through online channels. Furthermore, the assumption that the repair of imperfect 
products is conducted within the retailer's facility is a limitation of the study. It would be 
beneficial to explore the impact of outsourcing the repair of these items to third-party facili­
ties. The analysis of the potential impact of outsourcing the repair of imperfect products on 
the business model of retailers and manufacturers is a problem of considerable interest and 
potential challenge.
5  Conclusions
Partial backordering is common in the B2C environment, where the aim is to reduce inven­
tory costs and not to completely destroy customer loyalty. This paper investigates the merits 
of this business behaviour in the context of deterministic demand. The effects that policies 
for shipping products to working inventory store have on supply shortages has received 
little attention in prior literature. This paper attempts to fill this gap by providing a model 
and optimization procedure to find the optimal combination of inventory stock levels and 
shipment size when the retailer has backordering option for dealing with supply shortages, 
and imperfect products within the ordered lots. We studied partial backordering policy and 
developed a model that considers both inventory related and batch shipment costs. Regard­
ing the disposal of imperfect products, we discussed two situations: salvage at a discounted 
price and repair. The study incorporates issues into inventory systems that can be used in 
determining operational decisions. In summary, the study extends the model of Konstanta­
ras et al. (2007) to deal with the effect of partial backorders that appear in real-life problems.
To facilitate a more robust and substantiated comparison, both schemes underwent 
numerical experimentation, from which ensuing results were obtained and subsequently 
evaluated. The results showed that both the repair and salvage schemes in this paper out­
perform the traditional model in the context of partial backordering of shortages, and that 
repairing is more profitable for the retailer than selling items as salvage. However, an 
increase in repair costs is only beneficial to the retailer to a certain extent. On the other hand, 
the approach proposed in this paper is only beneficial at a certain level of the backordered 
demand ratio.
A sensitivity analysis was conducted to ascertain the effect of varying several of the 
input parameters. This investigation involved firstly the examination of the impact of utilis­
ing each parameter in isolation and then the effect of using a pair of parameters. The find­

ings indicated that modifying certain input parameters significantly influenced the inventory 
system's optimal decisions. To illustrate this point, a decrease in total profit for the system 
under both repair and salvage schemes was demonstrated as the holding cost was reduced. 
Therefore, the findings suggest that reducing holding costs enhances inventory system prof­
itability. Furthermore, the results demonstrated that the percentage of defective products 
significantly influenced the total profitability of the salvage scheme, while the fraction of 
backordered demand and lost sales costs had a substantial impact on the total profitability 
of the repair scheme. Our numerical examples showed that including higher holding costs 
in the model favors a partial backordering shortages. We showed that this policy provide 
greater value under scenarios in which the unit backordering and holding costs are close in 
magnitude. These cost savings can support inventory policy negotiations between a retailer 
and customers for setting strategies to cope with excess demand.
Possible future research directions for researchers and practitioners include the incorpo­
ration of environmental considerations, such as carbon emissions and energy use, in every 
process of ordering, purchasing, repairing, and transporting products. Another potential ave­
nue for further research is to investigate the benefits of scrapping a proportion of defective 
products while utilising the two disposal schemes that have been the subject of this paper. 
The models developed in this study can be extended by considering varying assumptions 
regarding the backorder rate and the holding cost. This study could be extended as a strate­
gic supply chain game.
