Abstract
This study considers a low-carbon supply chain model comprised of a capital-constrained
manufacturer and a retailer under a cap-and-trade system. These two parties can choose
from two ﬁnancing modes: bank ﬁnancing or mixed ﬁnancing, in which the latter combines
bank ﬁnancing with equity ﬁnancing. Two decision-making power structures are formulated,
namely the Nash game model and the retailer-led Stackelberg game model. Subsequently,
a comparative analysis of equilibrium outcomes for both parties within these power struc-
tures is performed, yielding the following insights: (1) In the bank ﬁnancing mode, retailers
ﬁnd greater proﬁtability in adopting dominance. When emission reduction efﬁciency is high,
manufacturers obtain more proﬁt within a Nash game context. However, when emission
reduction efﬁciency is low, manufacturers accrue more proﬁt in a Stackelberg game context.
In the mixed ﬁnancing mode without cap-and-trade regulations, supply chain participants
do not express a preference for any speciﬁc game structure. (2) Without cap-and-trade reg-
ulation, in both the Nash and Stackelberg game models, the supply chain participants ﬁnd
more proﬁtability in mixed ﬁnancing when carbon emission reduction efﬁciency is high.
Meanwhile, bank ﬁnancing proves more proﬁtable when the carbon emission reduction is
low. Moreover, this study investigates the impact of wholesale and retail prices of low-carbon
products on the ﬁnancing strategies of the supply chain participants under different power
structures and ﬁnancing modes. Additionally, extended models are considered, including the
manufacturer-led Stackelberg game structure and the capital-constrained manufacturer with
a certain amount of initial capital.

1 Introduction
In response to the urgent need to address global warming, governments worldwide have
introduced various regulations to curb carbon emissions. These regulations include but are
not limited to cap-and-trade systems (Drake et al., 2016; Tao et al., 2018), carbon taxes (Xu
et al., 2022), and carbon neutrality policies (Antimiani et al., 2023). Regarded as one of
the most efﬁcient mechanisms, cap-and-trade regulation has been implemented by countries
like China and the US. In a cap-and-trade regulatory framework, the production of ﬁrms is
restricted by a carbon cap allocated without cost by the regulator. When the ﬁrm’s carbon
emissionsexceedthedistributedcarboncap,theﬁrmcanbuyacarbonemissionquotafromthe
carbon trading market. Conversely, ﬁrms with less carbon emissions than the allocated carbon
cap can sell their excess carbon allowances and obtain a certain amount of revenue. Thus, cap-
and-trade regulation has a signiﬁcant effect on ﬁrms’ production decisions (He et al., 2015;
Toptal et al., 2013). Moreover, driven by the increasing social awareness of environmental
issues, consumers’ low-carbon awareness is increasing. The BBMG’s Conscious Consumer
Report indicates a growing trend towards environmental consciousness among Americans,
with 67% considering a product’s environmental impact before purchasing and 51% showing
a preference for eco-friendly options (Li et al., 2019). Among all the goods on JD’s Double
11 in 2021, goods in the ‘green consumption’ category exceeded 150 million (https://www.
xinhuanet.com). In the ﬁrst half of 2023, China’s total retail sales of consumer goods reached
22.8 trillion yuan, marking an 8.2% increase over the same period in the previous year.
The performance of green consumption is particularly outstanding. For example, the sales
of new energy vehicles increased by 44.1%, and the sales of e-commerce platform home
appliances “old for new” and green smart home appliances from the countryside increased by
67%, and 12.7%, respectively (https://www.163.com/dy/article/I9RP20TS051481OF.html).
This increasing consumer preference for low-carbon products has resulted in manufacturers
reducing their carbon emissions by actively optimizing their operations (Qin et al., 2017).
As cap-and-trade regulations and consumers’ preference for eco-friendly products con-
tinue to tighten, manufacturing ﬁrms are under increasing pressure to curb carbon emissions
in their production processes. For manufacturers, especially small and medium-sized enter-
prises, reducing carbon emissions may also entail ﬁnancial constraints. The Survey Report
on Energy Conservation and Emission Reduction in Chinese Enterprises since the 13th Five-
Year Plan points out that, due to ﬁnancial constraints, Chinese ﬁrms are limited in how much
they can invest in energy conservation and emission reduction. There is also insufﬁcient
internal motivation for ﬁrms to save energy and reduce emissions (Qin et al., 2021). Walmart
works with suppliers to jointly reduce greenhouse gas emissions across business operations
and supply chains, providing ﬁnancial and other assistance to a broad network of suppliers to
meet its goal of reducing one billion tons of greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 (walmart.cn).
Toalleviatecapitalconstraints,ﬁrmsinsupplychainshavebeenseekingdifferentﬁnancing
modes to obtain ﬁnancial support, such as bank or equity ﬁnancing. Under the bank ﬁnancing
mode, manufacturers can secure certain ﬁnancing from banks, and once the manufacturers
generate sales income, they can repay the bank with interest. For instance, in 2016, the World
Bank extended a low-interest green credit project loan of 98 million yuan to Tianji Group,
mitigating the enterprise’s shortfall in funds for emission reduction. In 2017, Jiumu Kitchen
& Bath obtained a loan of 9.9 million RMB from the Industrial Bank (Cao et al., 2019). In
2021, Sichuan Energy Investment Distributed Energy Ltd. obtained a carbon quota loan of 5
million RMB from the Agricultural Bank of China (Sichuan Energy Investment Group Co.,
Ltd. 2021).

In addition to traditional bank ﬁnancing, equity ﬁnancing is gaining increasing attention
from both governments and enterprises. Nasdaq’s ‘green label’ is divided into Green Equity
Designation and Green Equity Transition Designation. Green stocks focus on the themes
of energy conservation, emission reduction, and low-carbon transition. Moreover, China’s
green credit constitutes 90% of the total green ﬁnance ﬁnancing; green bonds and green
equity account for only 7% and 3%, respectively. By 2030, the proportion of green equity is
projected to exceed 30%, providing signiﬁcant opportunities for the capital market to support
the ‘double carbon’ strategy.
The equity ﬁnancing discussed in this paper primarily involves equity ﬁnancing between
upstream and downstream enterprises in the supply chain. This type of ﬁnancing allows
certain participants to receive income dividends. Firms in the supply chain can use equity
ﬁnancing to make production or emission reduction investments. For example, Yonghui
Supermarket, committed to sustainable development, continues to work upstream and down-
stream of the supply chain and consumers to promote innovative green development. In 2014,
Yonghui Supermarket announced its participation in the bidding for its supplier, Shanghai
Jinfeng Wine Co., Ltd.; Yonghui gained 6.54 million shares (Yonghui Supermarket 2014).
Walmart, the giant retailer, has used its substantial inﬂuence to induce its suppliers to cut emis-
sions and thus launched the Project Gigaton initiative. In 2022, Walmart acquired a minority
stake in its supplier of Sustainable Beef LLC (Walmart Announces Equity Investment in Sus-
tainable Beef LLC To Provide More High-Quality 2022). Suppliers can use equity ﬁnancing
to expand production capacity or carry out low-carbon production to meet Walmart’s goods
and emission reduction needs. Following the release of China’s "dual-carbon" strategy, low-
carbon emission reduction and environmental protection have become a key focus for many
new energy vehicle companies, which now place increased low-carbon demands on their
upstream suppliers and manufacturers. Shengxin Lithium Group, which has several energy
management system certiﬁcates (e.g., carbon footprint veriﬁcation and carbon neutral veriﬁ-
cation) has received 2.3 billion yuan of strategic investment from BYD Auto as its upstream
lithium-ion material supplier. Furthermore, after the completion of the investment, BYD Auto
now holds more than 5% of Shengxin Lithium Group’s shares (China Sustainability Tribune
2023; ce.cn 2022). Introducing equity ﬁnancing into capital-constrained supply chain sys-
tems under carbon trading allows upstream and downstream ﬁrms in the supply chain to form
shared interest communities through equity investment cooperation. This cooperation helps
alleviate the ﬁnancial constraints faced by manufacturers.
Driven by these equity ﬁnancing practices, this study delves into how supply chain par-
ticipants determine carbon emission reductions and production decisions while considering
various equity ﬁnancing modes. Additionally, this study explores whether, compared with
bank ﬁnancing, equity ﬁnancing can improve the proﬁts of the whole supply chain. Empirical
evidence from industry practices shows that power structures have a signiﬁcant impact on
ﬁrms’ ﬁnancing decisions (Fabbri & Klapper, 2016; Tang & Yang, 2020). A ﬁrm’s opera-
tions depend on that ﬁrm’s bargaining power in the market (Ranjbar et al., 2020). Thus, the
power structure must be considered when discussing ﬁnancing mode selections for carbon
emission reduction within the supply chain. The traditional view and research in the ﬁeld
often indicate that manufacturers wield considerable bargaining power within the supply
chain and typically occupy a dominant position in the game structure for making emission
reduction investments (Luo et al., 2017). However, this dynamic is shifting. As retailers gain
more market inﬂuence, they are increasingly taking on a dominant position within the supply
chain. In particular, retailers in the supply chain typically have stronger market power when
manufacturers face capital constraints caused by production and emissions reductions (Tang
& Yang, 2020). For example, major retailers like Suning and Walmart often exert signiﬁcant

inﬂuence over the business decisions of their upstream suppliers (Hu et al., 2021). Therefore,
this paper considers that, in a ﬁnancially-constrained supply chain, the retailer has strong
market power and is the leader of the Stackelberg game model, while the manufacturer is
the follower. At the same time, in some supply chains, the power of suppliers and retailers
is almost the same, thus creating a supply chain structure characterized by equal power. One
example is the case of mutual shareholding by Yonghui Supermarket and Jinfeng Wine. Thus,
this study also considers scenarios in which both forces have equal power, resembling the
Nash game model.
Motivated by the above analysis, this paper raises the following questions: (1) In the
different ﬁnancing modes, how do ﬁrms in supply chains decide their emission reduction
strategies under different power structures? (2) Within a speciﬁc ﬁnancial mode, what are the
preferences of the retailer regarding power structures? (3) Under certain power structures,
how do capital-constrained ﬁrms choose their ﬁnancing modes?
To answer these questions, this study analyzes a low-carbon supply chain system con-
sisting of a retailer and a capital-constrained low-carbon manufacturer. When faced with
capital constraints, to obtain funds for production and emission reduction, manufacturers
have the option to choose between bank ﬁnancing or mixed ﬁnancing. With bank ﬁnanc-
ing, the manufacturer obtains all the required funds through bank loans at a certain interest
rate. The mixed ﬁnancing mode combines bank ﬁnancing and equity ﬁnancing. Speciﬁcally,
the manufacturer obtains a portion of the production and emission reduction funds through
bank loans, and the remaining portion is provided by the retailer, which holds a portion of
the manufacturer’s shares. To identify the optimal ﬁnancing mode, this study compares the
production and sales decisions of supply chain participants using the two ﬁnancing modes
in the Nash and Stackelberg games.
The ﬁndings indicate that, under both bank ﬁnancing and mixed ﬁnancing modes, carbon
emission reduction and low-carbon promotion effort levels are elevated in the Stackelberg
game. Additionally, retailers tend to accrue higher proﬁts when opting for bank ﬁnancing.
However, the manufacturer’s proﬁt and the choice of the ﬁnancing mode are dependent on
the carbon emission reduction efﬁciency and power structure. Under both the Nash game
and the Stackelberg game modes, the level of carbon emission reduction and low-carbon
promotion effort is higher when choosing the mixed ﬁnancing mode. In addition, the supply
chain participants’ proﬁts are affected by the carbon emission reduction efﬁciency and the
retailer’s shareholding ratio.
This study’s main contributions to the existing literature can be summarized as follows:
First, many previous studies have focused on the choice of ﬁnancing modes, such as bank
ﬁnancing, trade credit, retailer, or supplier ﬁnancing. They have seldom taken equity ﬁnancing
modes into account. This study, however, contributes novel insights by combining carbon
emission reduction and capital constraints while discussing equity ﬁnancing, in which a
capital-constrained manufacturer could potentially ﬁnance manufacturing costs and carbon
emissions reduction costs. Second, this study extends the scope of inquiry by comparing
ﬁnancing mode choices among supply chain participants under varying power structures. This
approach goes further than many other studies, which have compared ﬁnancing modes mainly
in a manufacturer-leading Stackelberg game mode. Tang and Yang (2020) established low-
carbon supply chain ﬁnancing modes considering bank loans and early payment ﬁnancing
modes with retailer-led Stackelberg and manufacturer-led Stackelberg game models. The
study found that different power structures do not affect participants’ ﬁnancing mode choices.
Under each power structure, a ﬁnancing equilibrium always exists, namely early payment.
However, the Tang and Yang study did not consider equity ﬁnancing for the carbon emission
reduction and production and equal power structure. By addressing these gaps, the ﬁndings

2 Literature review
2.1 Carbon reduction in low-carbon supply chains
Research on carbon emission reduction has garnered signiﬁcant attention, with debates
revolving around the efﬁcacy of carbon taxes as a pathway to sustainability (Hartono et al.,
2023). For instance, Song et al. (2023) established a supply chain system consisting of two
ports and a shipping company. The study then analyzed the impact of the carbon tax on the
green shipping supply chain. Dash et al. (2019) explored optimal wholesale prices and carbon
emission levels within a low-carbon supply chain consisting of a retailer and a manufacturer.
Wang et al., (2024a, 2024b) adopted a stochastic differential game model to explore the joint
emission reduction strategy of a three-tier supply chain system from a long-term perspective.
The study established three game models to analyze and compare the equilibrium strategies
under each model. Wang et al., (2024a, 2024b) constructed two proﬁt-maximizing models
for capital-constrained manufacturers under carbon tax and low-carbon credit policies and
investigated the effects of different government intervention policies on remanufacturing and
carbon emissions. Luo et al. (2022) constructed four game-theoretic models to evaluate the
effects of carbon tax policies on manufacturing and re-manufacturing decisions in a closed-
loop supply chain consisting of manufacturers and retailers. Xu et al. (2022) investigated the
value of energy performance contracts to climate change mitigation in a two-level supply
chain under carbon tax regulations. Cao et al. (2019) investigated the interplay between car-
bon tax policies and bank interest rates on corporate proﬁts, supply chains, and consumer
surplus. The study found that the policies beneﬁted consumers but may have harmed ﬁrms
to some extent. The study concluded that governments could reduce unit carbon taxes under
certain conditions.
Researchers examining cap-and-trade systems have explored the impacts of various policy
mechanisms, such as cap-and-trade policies and carbon taxes, on ﬁrms’ decision-making
processes.Forinstance,Fanetal.(2023)studiedtheeffectsofacap-and-tradepolicy(quantity
commitment) and a carbon tax policy (price commitment) on ﬁrms’ technology investment
and production decisions. Xia et al. (2018) incorporated social preferences and low-carbon
consumerawarenessintoasupplychainunderacap-and-tradesystem.Thatstudyinvestigated
the carbon reduction strategies of the manufacturers with and without social preferences, as
well as retailers’ strategies for low-carbon promotion. Xia et al. (2022) established a low-
carbon supply chain in which the retailer had an equity share of the manufacturer under a
cap-and-trade system. The study investigated the effects of the supply chain participants’
optimal strategies, proﬁts, and co-ordination in the presence of information symmetry and

2.2 Financing decisions for capital-constrained supply chains
Given the impact of capital constraints on the operations of upstream and downstream ﬁrms
in supply chains, the ability to select the appropriate ﬁnancing method becomes crucial.
For capital-constrained retailers in a market with uncertain demand, Chen (2015) consid-
ered the problem of choosing between two ﬁnancial sources, such as competing banks and
trade credit from manufacturers. Xie et al. (2020) investigated the similarities and differ-
ences in the contagion effect and evolution of credit risk in a supply chain when retailers use
dual-channel ﬁnancing, bank ﬁnancing, and supplier trade credit. These results were then
compared to the use of single credit trade when both the suppliers and retailers have ﬁnancial
constraints. Dong et al., (2023a, 2023b) established a double helix supply chain consisting
of manufacturers and retailers of low-carbon products. The study proposed that the supply
chain members could make use of a combination of ﬁnance consisting of bank loans, trade
credit, and asset securitisation to provide a potential means to alleviate ﬁnancing constraints.
Jing et al. (2012) explored a distribution channel consisting of a manufacturer and a capital-
constrained retailer. The retailer could borrow credit to ﬁnance itself, either in the bank market
or from the manufacturer. The study also analyzed scenarios in which both the retailer and
manufacturer faced capital constraints. The results suggested that banks should ﬁnance the
manufacturer to enhance supply chain efﬁciency if production costs are low and ﬁnance
the retailer otherwise. Lu et al. (2024) considered a supply chain consisting of platforms
and farmers with yield uncertainty. A game-theoretic model was developed, with and with-
out platform digital empowerment under bank ﬁnancing and platform ﬁnancing conditions.
Liu et al. (2024) developed a textile and apparel remanufacturing supply chain consisting
of a well-funded original equipment manufacturer (OEM) and a cash-strapped third-party
remanufacturer (TPR). Both OEM ﬁnancing and subsidy-based conﬁrmed ﬁnancing were
considered on the basis of a baseline model in which no ﬁnancing was provided to the TPR.
In their research, Gu et al. (2021) investigated how manufacturers’ strategic sourcing, sup-
pliers’ ﬁnancing strategies, and downstream competitive relationships are inﬂuenced when
a manufacturer or supplier encounters capital constraints. Dong et al. (2022) developed a

2.3 Power structure in supply chains
Some researchers have explored the impact of different power structures in supply chains.
For example, Luo et al. (2017) studied a supply chain in which a retailer is supplied by
two manufacturers with differentiated brands. One is the good brand and the other is the
average brand. Horizontal competition and vertical competition were considered by exploring
differentcombinationsofpowerstructures.Maetal.(2024)andChenetal.(2024)constructed
a green supply chain and studied the inﬂuence of different power structures on supply chain
participants’ decision-making. Hu et al. (2024) and Lu and Menzes (2024) explored the
preferences of supply chain participants for different power structures. Meng et al. (2018)
considered the product selection strategies of two competing ﬁrms in the presence of a
carbon tax. That study considered the optimal strategies for the ﬁrms’ product selection under
two power structures, the Nash game and the Stackelberg game. Li et al. (2020) analyzed
the impact of power structure in a subcontracting assembly system. To investigate service
investment and pricing decisions in the presence of demand disruptions. Zhai et al. (2022)
considered a supply chain consisting of a single manufacturer and a single retailer. This chain
was used to develop a game theory model under three power structures, the manufacturer-led
Stackelberg game, the retailer-led Stackelberg game, and the Nash game. Wu et al. (2022)
discussed the effect of power structure in a dual channel supply chain on the decisions to
launch big data-driven credit payments, considering the MS and RS structures.
However, to date, few researchers have explored how the power structure affects ﬁrms’ car-
bon emissions and ﬁnancing decisions in a capital-constrained supply chain. A study by Tang
and Yang (2020) presented a relevant investigation, focusing on a low-carbon supply chain
consisting of a capital-constrained manufacturer and a capital-abundant retailer, in which
bank loans and early payments were studied. Retailer-led Stackelberg and manufacturer-led
Stackelberg game models were also analyzed. The impact of the power structure on ﬁnancing
mechanisms, carbon emissions, and performance was examined. Li et al. (2023) focused on
an agricultural supply chain with a capital-constrained manufacturer, a bank, and an insur-
ance company under three power structures: insurance company-dominated Stackelberg,
bank-dominated Stackelberg, and vertical Nash. However, these studies do not consider the
equity ﬁnancing mode in a low-carbon supply chain and the equal power structure model

the carbon emission reduction and ﬁnancing strategies in the supply chain (Table 1).
3 The model structure
This study examines a supply chain consisting of a retailer and a capital-constrained man-
ufacturer that produces only one product and begins with an initial capital of 0 (Cao et al.,
2019; Li et al., 2020). The initial capital is assumed to be 0; that is, all production and emis-
sion reduction costs need to be obtained through ﬁnancing. This study does not speciﬁcally
address pure equity ﬁnancing scenarios as a special case. If all the funds needed by the
manufacturer are obtained through equity ﬁnancing, then at this point, the absolutely con-
trolling retailer would receive all of the manufacturer’s sales revenue as an equity dividend.
This scenario would result in no proﬁt for the manufacturer. Instead, this study focuses on
the ﬁnancing mode selection of participants in a decentralized supply chain and explores
the emission reduction and ﬁnancing decisions of members in the supply chain in the more
general mode of mixed ﬁnancing, including equity ﬁnancing.
Thus, this paper investigates the impact of ﬁnancing modes, including a bank ﬁnancing
mode and a mixed ﬁnancing mode, on manufacturers’ carbon emission reduction and pro-
duction decisions. In a supply chain where upstream and downstream entities have balanced
power, the interaction between them is characterized by a Nash game. For example, the
supply chain formed by the mutual holding of Yonghui Supermarket and Jinfeng Wine is a
supply chain of equal power. In a retailer-dominated supply chain structure, the game struc-
ture of the two participants of the supply chain is a Stackelberg game. One example of this
would be the supply chain structure of Walmart and Sustainable Beef LLC. Therefore, two
power structures are discussed in the context of the Nash game and retailer-led Stackelberg
game models. The optimal decisions and proﬁts of supply chain participants under different

7.1 Extension 1: endogenous price
The price of low-carbon products was not considered in the above sections. However, in
reality, the price of a product is also one of the key inﬂuences that affect the supply chain
participants’ decisions. In addition, consumers are price sensitive. In this section, consumers
are assumed to be price-sensitive and to have a low-carbon preference. The wholesale price
of the product decided by the manufacturer and the marginal proﬁt decided by the retailer are
endogenous under both ﬁnancing models and power structures. The method used to decide the
retailer price is common in the supply chain (Choi 1991; Tang & Yang, 2020). Under the Nash
game mode, the manufacturer and the retailer simultaneously decide their wholesale price
and marginal proﬁt. Under the Stackelberg game mode, the retailer ﬁrst decides its marginal
proﬁt, and the manufacturer adjusts its wholesale price according to the retailer’s decision.
Therefore, this study discusses the effects of consumers’ price sensitivity k on the proﬁts of
the manufacturer and the retailer. The market demand is assumed to be D  θ −kp+ατ +γ z.

8 Conclusion
8.1 Main conclusions
This study assumes a low-carbon supply chain to analyze the decision-making and prof-
itability of a low-carbon supply chain comprised of a capital-constrained manufacturer and
a retailer under different power structures. The results are summarized below.
1. Under both bank and mixed ﬁnancing modes, emission reduction and low-carbon pro-
motion are higher in the Stackelberg game. In the bank ﬁnancing mode, for retailers,
dominance is more proﬁtable. However, when the emission reduction efﬁciency is low,
the manufacturer prefers the Stackelberg game structure. Conversely, when the emission
123

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2077–2113
2105
reduction efﬁciency is high, the manufacturer prefers the Nash game structure. In the
mixed ﬁnancing mode, without cap-and-trade regulation, supply chain participants have
no preference for either game structure.
2. In both Nash and Stackelberg game modes, mixed ﬁnancing results in greater carbon
emissions reduction and promotion. Proﬁts are affected by the retailer’s shareholding
ratio and the manufacturer’s reduction cost coefﬁcient. In the absence of cap-and-trade
regulation, in both Nash and Stackelberg game modes, the supply chain participants are
more proﬁtable under mixed ﬁnancing when the carbon emission reduction efﬁciency is
high. However, they are more proﬁtable with bank ﬁnancing when the carbon emission
reduction is low.
This study also examines the impact of the prices of low-carbon products, and a numerical
analysis shows the following: (1) Under both bank ﬁnancing and mixed ﬁnancing modes,
manufacturers obtain higher proﬁt in the Stackelberg game; retailers are more proﬁtable in the
Nash game. Under the mixed ﬁnancing mode, as the shareholding ratio increases, the proﬁt
difference of the supply chain participants gradually becomes smaller. (2) Manufacturers tend
to prefer mixed ﬁnancing in the Stackelberg game. For retailers, mixed ﬁnancing is preferred
under the Stackelberg game structure, while bank ﬁnancing is usually chosen under the Nash
game structure.
Additionally, in the manufacturer-led Stackelberg game, optimal decisions outperform
those in the Nash game, regardless of the ﬁnancing mode. When the manufacturer has initial
capital B, the optimal decisions of the supply chain participants are the same as when there
is no initial capital. The supply chain participants’ proﬁts are affected by the manufacturer’s
initial capital B.
8.2 Managerial implications
First, in bank ﬁnancing considering carbon trading, dominance is always the best decision
for strong retailers. However, the overall performance of manufacturers and supply chains
ultimately depends on the level of emission reduction efﬁciency. Therefore, to promote the
supply chain participants’ enthusiasm to reduce emissions as well as maximize the overall
proﬁt, retailers should not abuse their strong market position. An example of this can be
found in the supply chain of Walmart and Sustainable Beef LLC.
If the government does not have a carbon trading mechanism, whether bank ﬁnancing
or mixed ﬁnancing is used, the supply chain participants’ low-carbon decisions will not be
affected by the power structure. In a supply chain with two power structures, the willingness
of retailers to provide equity support depends on the efﬁciency of manufacturers in reducing
emissions. Therefore, for manufacturers, improving the efﬁciency of emissions reduction is
the key to accessing equity ﬁnancing. This principle ﬁnds support in real-world examples,
where ﬁrms evaluate sustainable development levels when providing equity capital support.
In the supply chain, ﬁrms do consider environmental, social, and governance (ESG) levels and
sustainable development standards when providing equity capital support. For example, in
2022, BYD Auto Group took a 2.3 billion yuan stake in Shengxin Lithium Energy Group, an
upstream lithium-ion material supplier with many environmental certiﬁcations. To strengthen
the low-carbon and sustainable development of the supply chain, BYD held more than 5%
of the supplier’s shares.
To address air pollution, governmental efforts should focus on encouraging manufacturers
and retailers to improve their emission reduction efﬁciency and low-carbon promotion, to
alleviate pollution and better meet consumer preferences. One approach is for the government
123

2106
Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2077–2113
to actively promote the implementation of carbon trading mechanisms by encouraging ﬁrms
toinvestinemissionreductiontechnologyandtherebyreducecarbonemissions.Additionally,
for emission reduction enterprises with ﬁnancial constraints, the government should actively
formulate relevant green ﬁnance policies that provide ﬁnancial support for the emission
reduction development of enterprises.
Finally, a capital-constrained manufacturing ﬁrm in a low-carbon supply chain should
choose the ﬁnancing method that is most appropriate for efﬁcient emission reduction. Equity
ﬁnancing with the retailer holding a reasonable proportion of the manufacturer’s shares is
preferable, because that mode can better improve the emission reduction and the level of
low-carbon promotion while increasing both party’s proﬁts.
8.3 Limitations of this study and recommendations for future studies
This study has some limitations that warrant future consideration. First, this study only
examines a supply chain consisting of one manufacturer and one retailer, while real supply
chains often have multiple manufacturers and retailers. Consequently, the corresponding
equilibrium decisions would be different from the results of this study. Secondly, the analysis
in this article assumes perfect symmetry of information, which is uncommon in practical
settings.Therefore,futurestudiescouldexamineinformationasymmetryintheoverallmarket
demand or the degree of consumer preference for low-carbon products.
