Abstract
We propose a heuristic method for solving the overall risk minimization problem, spe­
cifically the infinite-horizon dynamic programming problem, which uses a coherent risk 
measure of the overall discounted reward as its criterion. The original problem is very 
complex, and its optimal policies may depend on the entire history of previous decisions. 
The heuristic consists in restricting attention to Markov policies—those depending only 
on the current state—and in consecutive local approximations of the criterion, a static 
coherent risk measure, by its nested dynamic counterpart: a limit nested risk measure. The 
approximating problems with the nested measures can be reformulated using the Bellman 
equation and, as such, can be solved by standard algorithms. We introduce several vari­
ants of this heuristic. Consequently, we conduct three numerical experiments, all of which 
demonstrate the efficiency of our heuristic in finding effective solutions. Due to its speed, 
the heuristic is suitable for finding “good enough” solutions, which may be further refined.
Keywords  Coherent risk measures · Dynamic programming · Heuristics
1  Introduction
For many decision problems, it is useful to assume risk aversion rather than risk neutrality. 
Once the problem is static, there are many possible risk measures at hand to be used, and 
a solid theory of static risk measures has been developed. If the problem is dynamic (e.g. 
multi-stage or dynamic with infinite horizon), a “reasonable” dynamic risk measure has 
to be used, where “reasonable” usually means that it is coherent at any time step (Artzner 
et al., 1997, 1999, 2007) and time-consistent (Boda & Filar, 2006; Shapiro, 2009, 2012; 
Bielecki et al., 2017), the latter notion meaning that we never prefer a choice in the future

which would increase the risk seen from the past. Nested risk measures based on coherent 
risk mappings fulfill this requirement naturally, which—together with the fact that the cor­
responding dynamic problems may be solved in a similar way as the risk-neutral ones, see 
Ruszczyński (2010); Kopa and Smid (2023)—suggests that nested risk measures are suit­
able candidates for applications.
What, however, could prevent the (nested) dynamic risk measures from being used in 
practice is their certain counter-intuitiveness. Decision makers, who are potential users of 
the model, would probably be able to determine their level of risk aversion (e.g. the prob­
ability level in CV@R or the CV@R weight in Mean-CV@R), but it will be impossible for 
them to determine the risk-aversion level of the nested risk mapping. And this is a problem 
not only of the user—a decision maker—but also of the modeler. Once there are many deci­
sion steps, for instance, setting incorrect nested risk-aversion levels can lead to “degenera­
tion”. For example, say that we use nested CV@R, which means that we compute “CV@R 
of CV@R of CV@R . . . ”, i.e. “the mean of the tail of the conditional mean of the tail of the 
conditional mean of the tail ...”; once the underlying distribution is discrete, the dynamic 
measure converges to the worst-case one with an increasing number of decision steps. As 
a potential cure for this, Pichler and Schlotter (2022) suggest decreasing the risk-aversion 
level of the nesting CV@R, as well as some other risk measures, when decreasing the time 
step. In particular, for each risk measure, they construct a (risk level) function, dependent 
on the time step, such that the normalized (one-step) measures with the corresponding risk 
levels, applied to a normal variable, converge to a non-zero finite constant at (the time 
step) zero. Yet this procedure is primarily meant for the mathematical construction of a 
limit nested measure, it also suggests adjusting the risk parameters of the nesting measures 
according to their risk level functions. However, there is no guarantee that the risk level 
“works correctly” for non-infinitesimal time intervals and, moreover, the nested risk levels 
may be dependent on the choice of the (class of) underlying process(es), which is Brownian 
Motion in Pichler and Schlotter (2022), while in practice the underlying process rarely has 
i.i.d. increments, let alone being a diffusion.

Except for σκ being expectation, or some practically unusable cases (such as when σκ is 
the worst-case risk measure), the Bellman equation cannot be formulated, so the problem 
is virtually impossible to solve, as its optimal policy could be non-Markov (depending on 
more than the current state) and time-heterogeneous (different in different decision times). 
However, as we show in the present paper, when we resort to Markov time-homogeneous 
policies, we can try to solve the problem rather efficiently by heuristics, approximating the 
static risk measure by a limit nested one.
The intuition behind our idea to approximate a static risk measure by its dynamic coun­
terpart is that, as both are coherent, the approximation should roughly preserve monoto­
nicity, which is exactly the property that matters in optimization. To correct possible (and 
likely) errors stemming from the approximation, we suggest using our (computationally 
cheap) heuristic first and then performing the “final steps” by some usual numerical method, 
employing (costly) evaluation of the static measure.
The problem of adding risk aversion to a dynamic programming problem can also be 
approached from another side: say that the decision maker is content with replacing the 
expectation by a dynamic risk measure, but, for the reasons mentioned above, cannot decide 
on a specific risk level of the nesting measure. In that case, our approach can offer an opti­
mal solution (with respect to the dynamic measure) which is also “good” in terms of the 
static measure with the risk level prescribed by the user (because our algorithms always 
produce sequences of solutions with non-increasing values of the static measure).
We test the efficiency of our approach on a stylized investment problem with the result 
that our heuristics find good solutions in a short time and, in combination with a usual coor­
dinate-descent-like method, it is still very efficient in finding a better solution in a shorter 
time than the usual method used alone.

study (Sect. 5). Finally, we briefly conclude the paper (Sect. 6).

4  Computational aspects
Even when the optimal solution of a homogeneous dynamic programming problem (5) 
exists (which is the case when the state and action spaces are finite, for instance), the objec­
tive can often be evaluated only with a certain precision ϵ and, consequently, the optimiza­
tion produces only ϵ-optimal solutions. If the objective is a nested measure ρη, a required 
precision of its single evaluation can be achieved easily by value-iteration-like algorithms 
(recall that their precision decreases geometrically); however, once the objective is an over­
all risk measure σι, achieving a required precision is difficult as the underlying distribution 
generally has an infinite number of atoms: thus, the time horizon has to be truncated, i.e. 
σι(Za

be computed by a Monte Carlo method.
In the sequel, assume that the time complexity of computing σι(X) for any variable X with 
a finite support is O(n log(n)) where n is the number of the atoms of X. This assumption is 
clearly true for expectation, which is even O(n), and for CV@R, because it can be computed 
by sorting the atoms (which is O(n log(n)) by the Quicksort algorithm) and evaluating the 
expectation of their (unbroken) subset, which is O(n). Further, assume that σι(En), where 
En is an n-atom empirical distribution corresponding to X, fulfills var(σι(E)) ≤Cn−1 for 
some C possibly dependent on the (exact) distribution and ι. Note that this is trivially true 
for expectation, but also for CV@R, empriicial variant of which can be formulated as a

5.4  Discussion
Our heuristics involve three sources of approximation errors. The first source of approxi­
mation error stems from the transition to homogeneous policies, the second arises from the 
local approximation of the overall risk measure by a nested one, and the third may follow 
from uncertainty whether the algorithm is stopped at a true minimum. All of these limita­
tions imply that our heuristic may, in principle, yield significantly inferior solutions com­
pared to the original formulation.
However, in our numerical experiments, the use of our heuristic did not lead to signifi­
cantly suboptimal solutions. This outcome is most likely attributable to the relative simplic­
ity of the test problems and their structural properties. Anyway, these findings suggest that, 
at least for problems of moderate complexity, the method can achieve practically useful 
approximations.
Quite surprisingly, increasing accuracy (of the value iteration as well as overall criterion 
evaluation) did not bring much improvement while significantly increasing computational 
time (see Table 3).
Further, as expected, the computation time grows non-linearly both with increasing 
|S|(= m + 1) and with decreasing ϵ, see Tables 3 and  4. This underscores the method’s 
limitations for high-dimensional settings.
Out of the variants of our method, the relaxation heuristic appears to be the most efficient 
way of solving our problem, quite counterintuitively outperforming, both in precision and 
computation time, the Taylor heuristic , which was supposed to be a more exact approxima­
tion of the original problem. By far the best results are achieved by its combination with a 
pseudogradient method, run after RH stops; adding this step, however, extremely increases 
the time complexity; however, this combination convincingly outperforms the pseudogradi­
ent algorithm itself in time complexity.
6  Conclusions
We proposed several heuristics for solving the Overall-Risk Dynamic Programming prob­
lem and demonstrated, on stylized examples, that they yield competitive results. In particu­
lar, the relaxation heuristic appears effective for rapidly obtaining solutions of satisfactory 
quality. The best performance was observed when combining this method with a pseudogra­
dient procedure that directly evaluates the exact objective; however, this comes at the cost of 
forfeiting the extreme speed of the original approach. Future research should therefore focus 
on principled refinement schemes that, starting from a quickly obtained “good-enough” 
solution, efficiently drive the policy toward near-optimality.
