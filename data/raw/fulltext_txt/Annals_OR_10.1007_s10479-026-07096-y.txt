Abstract
In the classical ﬁreﬁghter game, a ﬁre breaks out on some vertices of an undirected connected
graph at time zero. At each subsequent time step, a ﬁxed number of ﬁreﬁghters can protect
one vertex each from catching ﬁre. Afterwards, the ﬁre spreads from each burning vertex to
every adjacent vertex that is neither burning nor defended. The game ends when the ﬁre can
no longer spread. The goal is to ﬁnd a defense strategy that maximizes the number of non-
burning (saved) vertices. In this work, we ﬁrst revisit the classical integer linear programming
formulation and then present several improvements for it, as well as two new formulations
and tighter bounds on the maximum duration of the game. Moreover, we relax the classical
assumptions that all vertices have uniform values and costs, i.e., we allow vertices to have
different values and costs for being defended. Furthermore, instead of a ﬁxed number of
ﬁreﬁghters, we are given a defense budget that we can spend each time step to defend the
vertices. We call this the cost-value ﬁreﬁghter game. We present three different integer linear
programming formulations for the problem, along with a series of inequalities to strengthen
the formulations and tight bounds on the maximum duration of the game.
Keywords Fireﬁghter game · Mixed integer programming · Combinatorial optimization

1 Introduction
The Classical Fireﬁghter Game (CFG) is a discrete-time, deterministic model that abstracts
the dynamics of ﬁre spreading on a network, where strategic protection of vertices is used
to contain the ﬁre, ﬁrst introduced by Hartnell (1995). In this problem, a ‘ﬁre’ breaks out
in a ﬁxed set of vertices of a connected undirected graph at time step 0. A vertex catching
ﬁre is called ‘burning’. In each subsequent time step, ﬁrst, a certain number of vertices can
be defended before the ﬁre spreads to all non-burning undefended neighbors of the burning
vertices. Once a vertex is either burning or defended, it remains so for the rest of the game. The
game ends when the ﬁre can not spread further. The goal of the defenders is to ﬁnd a strategy
that maximizes the number of non-burning vertices. The decision version of CFG with one
ﬁreﬁghter and starting ﬁre was shown in MacGillivray and Wang (2003) to be NP-complete
even with restricted to bipartite graphs and in Finbow et al. (2007) to be NP-complete even
on trees with a maximum degree of three if the ﬁre breaks out on a vertex of degree three.
CFG can also be seen as an abstraction of various disease intervention strategies, including
administering a vaccine that prevents the vertex from being infected or from spreading the
disease to other vertices; or an intervention that simply removes individuals from the area
and thus from the path of the disease. Such interventions have been used, for example, to
save endangered species from the chrytridiomycosis pandemic, see (Goodman, 2006). The
vertices hereby model individuals, with edges connecting individuals between whom disease
could spread (for instance, if they regularly come within a certain physical distance for
airborne transmission). Alternatively, the vertices could model population centers, with edges
joining centers that in real life are linked by transportation and regularly have individuals
traveling between them, potentially spreading the disease. Although contact tracing and ring
vaccination (vaccinating everyone who came into contact with an infected individual and
everyone who came into contact with them etc.) within population centers are frequently
used, sometimes it is preferable to vaccinate entire populations. This could be due to a lack
of information about contacts; a policy choice based on the cost of vaccines and the risk to
the public; or the logistical difﬁculty of having to visit the same population centers multiple
times to administer additional vaccines. Other applications include, e.g., stopping a virus from
spreading on a network (Finbow et al., 2000) or hampering the spread of fake news on social
media networks. Furthermore, operations research plays a signiﬁcant role in addressing open
questions in ﬁre management techniques, as stated in Rönnqvist et al. (2015), Rönnqvist et
al. (2023). In fact, it has been widely applied to optimize resource allocation in ﬁreﬁghting
strategies, as seen, for example, in Mendes and e Alvelos (2025), Ozkan and Kilic (2023),
Yang et al. (2019).
As our ﬁrst contribution, we revisit the classical Integer Linear Programming (IP) formu-
lation for CFG and introduce several improvements, including inequalities to strengthen the
formulation. Moreover, we derive tighter bounds on the duration of the game that signiﬁ-
cantly reduce the size of the formulation. Finally, we propose two new IP formulations for
CFG that empirically turn out to be much more efﬁcient than the classical formulation.
As a second contribution, we discuss a variant of this problem: the Cost-Value Fireﬁghter
game (CVFG). In CVFG, every vertex has both a cost of defense and a utility gained by
protecting that vertex from catching ﬁre. Moreover, instead of a ﬁxed number of ﬁreﬁghters,
a defense budget is given that can be spent each time step for defending vertices. The cost
for defending a vertex has to be paid only once, and the vertex remains defended for the rest
of the game. In addition, and differing from the classic game, in this variant the defense of a
vertex can be built up gradually, i.e., over multiple time steps. However, a vertex is only safe

from catching ﬁre when full protection has been achieved. The objective is then to maximize
the total utility of the non-burning vertices. CVFG can better represent some real-word
applications by setting: i) the cost is proportional to the effort needed to defend a given area
from catching ﬁre—or, equivalently, to the population size that must be vaccinated— and any
extra costs related to that location; and ii) the value proportional to the the size/importance
of the area that would be protected. There has been some recent interest in modeling the
cost of vaccinating different vertices, notably in Michalak (2020), whose model is based
on CFG but uses probabilistic disease spread and control interventions beyond vaccination.
Moreover, CVFG allows us to easily model the case where the ﬁre takes more than one period
to spread between two adjacent nodes. To do this, we simply insert as many auxiliary nodes
into the edge as the time it takes for the contagion to spread along the edge minus one. For such
auxiliary nodes, we set the value of saving the node equal to zero and its defense cost to a very
large constant. We present a ﬁrst integer programming formulation for CVFG that is derived
from the classical formulation for CFG. Afterwards, we introduce several improvements to
strengthen the formulation, tight bounds on the maximum duration of the game, and two new
integer programming formulations. We present an extensive computational study to compare
the formulations and assess the suggested improvements.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we review the related
literature. In the next section, we ﬁrst present the classical integer programming formulation
for the ﬁreﬁghter problem. Afterwards, we introduce several improvements to strengthen
the formulation and two new integer programming formulations. We also present extensive
computational results to analyze the efﬁciency of the formulations and the proposed improve-
ments. Section 4 mirrors the previous section, but now for CVFG. Finally, our conclusions
and some future research topics are included in Section 5.
2 Literature review
In the following, we provide an overview of the related literature on the ﬁreﬁghter problem.
Concerning methodological contributions, we will focus on exact algorithms, which are the
scope of this paper, and we will only brieﬂy mention recent heuristic algorithms.
TheﬁrstIPformulationforCFGwasintroducedinDevelinandHartke(2007)andmodiﬁed
in Finbow and MacGillivray (2009) by adding one set of valid inequalities. To derive the
formulation, an upper bound on the maximum duration of the game is needed. In García-
Martínez et al. (2015), the authors carry out an empirical study to assess the impact of
the value of the upper bound on the run times of the IP formulation using the formulation
proposed in Develin and Hartke (2007). Not unsurprisingly, they observed that the closer
the upper bound is to the actual duration of the game, the faster the IP formulation can be
solved, stressing for the ﬁrst time the importance of tight upper bounds. Moreover, they
also proposed a parametric IP strategy that automatically adjusts the value of the upper
bound. However, no formal optimality criterion is provided for the value of the upper bound,
rendering it a heuristic. The authors also propose several other heuristics for the problem. Two
improvements to the IP formulation of Develin and Hartke (2007) are presented in Ramos
et al. (2020). Firstly, they propose a preprocessing step to ﬁx variables and use an aggregate
version of one of the constraints. Secondly, they derive a slightly improved upper bound.
In addition, they propose a matheuristic to solve the problem. Other heuristics for CFG can
be found, for example, in Blum et al. (2014) and Hu et al. (2015). We are not aware of any

All these works are applicable to general networks. For specialized graphs, often better
algorithms or results can be obtained. For example, for graphs with bounded tree width (Cai et
al., 2010; Enright & Meeks, 2015), for planar graphs (Esperet et al., 2013; Gordinowicz, 2015;
Wang et al., 2014), for trees (Coupechoux et al., 2019; Iwaikawa et al., 2011), for different
kinds of grids (Chen et al., 2017; Develin & Hartke, 2007; Gaven˘ciak et al., 2014), or for
graphs with certain node degrees (Finbow et al., 2007; Prałat, 2014). Moreover, most works
deal with static networks, i.e., graphs whose structure does not change, and spread is assumed
to be deterministic, i.e., every non-burning vertex adjacent to a burning vertex will catch ﬁre
in the next round. Concerning the former assumption, in recent years, there has been research
on dynamic networks whose structure can change over time, see, e.g., (Enright & Kao, 2018;
Pellis et al., 2015). And with respect to the latter, in Michalak (2020), the authors consider the
situation in which a disease (i.e., ﬁre) spreads probabilistically across the graph and the goal is
to minimize the combined cost of infections, vaccinations, and (vertex) isolation. The authors
propose an evolutionary algorithm to solve the problem. Other probabilistic approaches can
be found, e.g., in Tennenholtz et al. (2017). For a more comprehensive review of the literature
on the ﬁreﬁghter problem, see (Finbow & MacGillivray, 2009; Wagner, 2021).
Concerning the cost-value ﬁreﬁghter game, in Duffy and MacGillivray (2015), the authors
introduce the weighted ﬁreﬁghter problem, which associates a weight with each vertex, and
the goal is to maximize the total weight of all non-burning vertices at the end of the game.
The authors discuss this problem for one starting ﬁre and one ﬁreﬁghter and prove that it
is NP-hard even on binary trees. A version where vertices can have arbitrary positive or
negative weights and the goal is to maximize the sum of weights of non-burning vertices
minus the sum of weights of burning vertices is considered in Costa et al. (2016). They prove
that the problem is NP-hard on binary trees and derive two greedy approximation algorithms
on trees. In a slightly different vein, in Coupechoux et al. (2019), the authors introduce the
fractional ﬁreﬁghter game. While all vertices have a cost and value of one, it is allowed to
split the defense budget to only partially, i.e., fractionally, defend a vertex in a given time
step. A partially defended vertex can still catch ﬁre, but only the unprotected portion will
count as burning. Moreover, a partially burning vertex will only spread this portion of the
ﬁre to its neighbors. The problem formulation was motivated by an online setting, where the
defense budget can vary, and one does not know how much will be available in future time
steps. We are not aware of any IP formulation or computational study for CVFG.
3 Classic ﬁreﬁghter
We start by recounting the classic ﬁreﬁghter game (CFG) and its IP formulation. Let G =
(V , E) be a simple undirected graph with vertex set V and edge set E ⊆V ×V . Moreover, let
N(i) = { j ∈V : [i, j] ∈E} be the set of vertices adjacent to i ∈V and let dist(i, j) ∈Z+
denote the shortest path distance between i, j ∈V (each edge has length one). In addition,
for W ⊆V let N(W) = ∪i∈W N(i) and dist(i, W) = min j∈W dist(i, j). Finally, let F ⊂V
and f = |F| be the set and number of initially burning vertices, respectively, and d ∈Z+ be
the number of available ﬁreﬁghters. The game adheres to the following four rules:

1. All vertices in F are burning at time 0.
2. In each subsequent time step t > 0, we ﬁrst defend up to d previously undefended vertices
and afterwards the ﬁre spreads from every burning vertex to all adjacent susceptible
vertices, i.e., vertices that are neither burning nor defended.
3. Once burning (defended), a vertex will remain burning (defended) until the end of the
game.
4. The game ends in time step T ∈Z+ if either
• a node ignites at time T , but the ﬁre cannot spread any further in the following time
steps independent of what happens defense-wise, i.e., even if we stop defending
vertices all together, or
• no node ignites at time T , but a node ignited at time T −1 and without defending in
time T , the ﬁre would have continued to spread.
The goal of the game is to maximize the number of non-burning vertices at the end of the
game; or equivalently, minimize the number of burning vertices. An (optimal) solution to the
game is called an (optimal) defense strategy. For an optimal defense strategy, we denote by
T opt the time step when the game ends. If there is more than one optimal defense strategy
and they have different values for T opt, then we deﬁne T opt as the smallest among all values,
i.e., we choose a defense strategy that ends the game the quickest. Unfortunately, even for
T opt there are potentially many alternative optimal solutions. However, we can always ﬁnd
one that satisﬁes the following:
Lemma 3.1 For any instance of CFG, there exists an optimal defense strategy S that uses
every ﬁreﬁghter at time step t ≤T opt −1 and only defends vertices that must be defended
(i.e., which would otherwise burn before the game ends).
Proof Let S be an optimal defense strategy for an instance of CFG that ends the game after
T opt time steps. Assume that S does not use all ﬁreﬁghters at time t ≤T opt −1 or ﬁreﬁghters
defend vertices that would not have needed to be defended. As the game does not end in t
and T opt is the minimum, we need to distinguish two cases:
Case 1: There is at least one susceptible vertex j ∈V at time T opt that still needs to be
defended, but no new vertices catch ﬁre. Let W ⊆V denote the set of vertices that need
to be defended in T opt, i.e., at the end of the game.
If S uses fewer than d ﬁreﬁghters at some time step t ≤T opt −1, then the unused
ﬁreﬁghters could already defend the vertices in W, resulting in an alternative defense
strategy S′ which has the same burning and also ends after T opt steps. Note that we must
have strictly less than |W| unused ﬁreﬁghters in total up to and including period T opt −1,
because otherwise we could have completely defended all nodes in W before T opt and
the game would already have ended at T opt −1. Hence, all unused ﬁreﬁghters before
time T opt can be assigned to defend vertices in W. On the other hand, if S defends one
or more vertices at time t that would not need to be defended, then again we could use
those ﬁreﬁghters to defend vertices in W instead. Again, we must have less than |W|
ﬁreﬁghters in total defending such vertices, and all of them can be reassigned to vertices
in W.
Case 2: At least one new vertex j ∈V is igniting at time T opt, but then the ﬁre is fully
contained without further defending. Let W ⊆V denote the set of vertices catching ﬁre
in T opt. As S is an optimal strategy, we cannot have any unused ﬁreﬁghters in periods
t ≤T opt −1 or ﬁreﬁghters defending vertices that did not need to be defended. Because,
otherwise, we could re-assign those ﬁreﬁghters to defend the vertices in W, resulting in
a solution that has strictly less burning than S.

3.5 Computational experiments
We coded all integer programming formulations in C++ using CPLEX 20.1 and ran the
experiments on a Dell PowerEdge R740 server running Scientiﬁc Linux 7 using a single
thread with a time limit of one hour.
3.5.1 Data generation
For the number n of nodes, we considered values of 100, 150, 200, 250, and 300. The number
of starting ﬁres f and ﬁreﬁghters d for graphs up to 150 nodes ranges from 1 to 3 in steps
of 1 and for larger graphs from 1 to 5 in steps of 2. An instance for the problem is fully
characterized by the tuple (G, F, d), where G is the underlying graph, F the set of starting
ﬁres, and d the number of ﬁreﬁghters.
Starting with the graphs, we drew coordinates for the vertices uniformly at random over the
square [0, 10]2. We then computed the Gabriel graph (Ruben Gabriel & Sokal, 1969) and the
Delaunay triangulation (Delaunay, 1934) for the point sets. Both graphs are connected and
planar, with the Gabriel graph being a proper subgraph of the Delaunay graph. We generated
ﬁve random graphs for each number of nodes. The average node degree over all generated
instances for the Gabriel and Delaunay graphs is 3.5 and 5.3, respectively. For each graph,
we then picked the set of starting ﬁres. To avoid trivial cases where a ﬁre starts, e.g., on a
leaf or on a vertex with a very high node degree, we sorted the vertices be non-decreasing
deviations from the average node degree. That is, the ﬁrst vertex in the list is the one whose
node degree is closest to the average node degree for the graph. In case of ties, vertices with a
smaller index come ﬁrst. Afterwards, we pick the ﬁrst f vertices in the list to make up the set
of starting ﬁres F. Finally, for each pair (G, F) we then set the number of ﬁreﬁghters. Using
this hierarchical procedure, the instances (G, F, ∗) all have identical graphs and starting ﬁres
and only differ in the number of ﬁreﬁghters. This allows us to assess how an increase in the
number of ﬁreﬁghters affects the computational performance of the considered formulations.
Moreover, for the same graph G a larger set of starting ﬁres F includes all smaller sets F′, i.e.,
F′ ⊂F for |F′| < |F|. For each conﬁguration, we generated ﬁve instances using different
random seeds.

4 Cost-value ﬁreﬁghter
In this section, we present the cost-value ﬁreﬁghter game (CVFG). To that end, we denote
the cost of defending vertex i ∈V by ci ∈R+ and its value for being saved by vi ∈R+.
The defense budget for each time step is denoted by B ∈R+ and we assume that ci ≤B for
all i ∈V and ci = vi = 0 for all i ∈F. CVFG adheres to the same rules as CFG, except for
replacing the second rule by:
2. In every time step t > 0, we can spend any amount between 0 and B on improving the
defense of a node, as long as the combined spending per time step across all vertices does
not exceed B.
The defense of a vertex can be built up gradually, i.e., over multiple time steps. A vertex
counts as defended iff the total amount of defense budget spent on the vertex so far is
equal to or exceeds its defense cost ci.
The goal of the game is to spend the available defense budget across vertices in each time
step so as to maximize the value of non-burning vertices at the end of the game. We point out
that this variant allows us to model the case in which the ﬁre takes more than one period to
spread between two adjacent nodes i, j ∈V , (i, j) ∈E. To achieve this, we insert as many
auxiliary nodes into (i, j) as the number of time steps required for the contagion to spread,

4.5 Computational experiments
We again coded the mixed-integer programming formulations in C++ using CPLEX 20.1
and ran the experiments on a Dell PowerEdge R740 server running Scientiﬁc Linux 7 using
a single thread with a time limit of one hour. Moreover, the graphs were generated as before.
Concerning defense costs ci and node values vi, i ∈V , we drew the former uniformly at
random from the interval [5, 25]. The latter were then drawn uniformly at random from
the interval [0.5ci, 1.5ci], i.e., roughly proportional to the defense cost. Finally, the defense
budget was set according to Bd = 25(d + 1)/2, where d was chosen as in Section 3.5. For
each type of graph and each combination of n, f , and d, we generated ﬁve random instances.

5 Conclusions
In this paper, we analyzed the classical ﬁreﬁghter game under a mathematical optimization
approach. Moreover, we introduced a new variant called the cost-value ﬁreﬁghter game which
considers heterogeneous vertex values and defense costs, i.e., the ﬁxed number of ﬁreﬁghters
has been replaced by a ﬂexible budget of defense. For both problems, new mixed-integer
programming formulations were developed together with new (valid) inequalities and tighter
bounds on the duration of the game.
Computational experiments show that our formulations signiﬁcantly improve the per-
formance of existing approaches in the literature. The proposed (valid) inequalities and
bounds increase the number of instances solved to optimality within the time limit and
reduce the computing time, making the models much more applicable for larger instances.
These advances could further beneﬁt matheuristic approaches that incorporate the problem
formulation, potentially improving their efﬁciency and solution quality. Among the different
formulations we proposed, the one that performed best in non-degenerate games for both
problems was the approach that used variables that indicate the time period when the status
of a node changes.
This work opens several avenues for future research considering alternative problem vari-
ants, such as dynamic budget allocation or network disruptions, which could further enrich
the model’s applicability. Moreover, future research could explore advanced methodologi-
cal strategies, such as parametric or decomposition-based approaches, to further exploit the
advantages of the results presented in this paper.
