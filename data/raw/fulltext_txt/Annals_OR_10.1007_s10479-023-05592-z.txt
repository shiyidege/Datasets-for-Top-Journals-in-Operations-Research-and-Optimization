Abstract
This paper addresses a stochastic job shop scheduling problem with sequence-dependent
setup times, aiming to minimize the expected maximum lateness. The stochastic nature is
modeled by considering uncertain times between failures (TBF) and uncertain times to repair
(TTR). To tackle this problem, a simheuristic approach is proposed, which combines a tabu
search (TS) algorithm with Monte Carlo simulation. A total of 320 instances were used to
conduct multiple experiments. Instances were generated with two distributions to study the
behavior of stochastic TTR and TBF under log-normal and exponential distributions. Firstly,
the performance of the simheuristic was evaluated for small instances by comparing it with the
simulation of optimal solutions obtained with a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP)
model. The simheuristic approach demonstrated an average improvement of around 7%
compared to the simulation of MILP model solutions. Secondly, the simheuristic performance
was evaluated for medium and large-size instances by comparing it with the simulation of
the solutions obtained by the earliest due date (EDD) and process time plus work in the next
queue plus negative slack (PT + WINQ + SL) dispatching rules. The results showed an
average improvement of around 11% compared to EDD and 14% compared to PT + WINQ
+ SL. Furthermore, the results highlight that even when the two distributions have the same
expected value and coefÔ¨Ācient of variation, they can yield different expected maximum
lateness values. This emphasizes the importance of precise distribution Ô¨Ātting when solving
real cases to achieve effective scheduling performance.

1 Introduction
In today‚Äôs dynamic and competitive world, the pursuit of excellence is a necessity for sur-
viving in markets. Globalization and the technological revolution of the last century have
compelledcompaniestoimprovealltheirinternalprocesses.Underthispremise,newmethod-
ologies and paradigms have emerged to address challenges such as reducing process times,
waste, labor or production and logistics costs, among others (Hill, 2011). Additionally, new
forms of customer demand and marketing are driving companies towards mass customization
of products and services based on speciÔ¨Āc customer needs. As a result, companies are forced
to continuously optimize their production planning and execution to remain competitive.
Production scheduling consists of allocating resources to operations to achieve one or
more objectives (Pinedo, 2016). The study of scheduling in manufacturing environments
began in the early twentieth century with the works of Gantt (1919) and other pioneers
such as Smith (1956), Johnson (1954) and Jackson (1956). One way to classify production
schedulingproblemsisbasedontheconÔ¨Āgurationofmachinesintheproductionenvironment.
The generic categories include single machine, parallel machines, Ô¨āow shop, job shop, and
open shop. SpeciÔ¨Ācally, the job shop scheduling problem (JSSP) refers to scheduling jobs on
a set of m machines, where each job has a single predetermined path, which is independent
of the paths of other jobs, and each machine can only process one job at a time (√áali¬łs &
Bulkan, 2015; Jain & Meeran, 1999). This type of conÔ¨Āguration occurs in industries such as
aerospace (Chiang & Torng, 2016), machine tools production systems (Kadipasaoglu et al.,
1999), construction equipment production systems (Jacobs & Lauer, 1994), among others.
The literature review presented by Abdolrazzagh and Adbullah (2017) summarizes the
characteristics studied in the JSSP, as well as the objective functions considered. In partic-
ular, the authors mention that the classic constraints considered are related to precedence,
capacity, and the inclusion of release times and due dates. However, there are other con-
straints that have been studied in fewer works due to the complexity they add to the problem.
These constraints include multi-purpose machines, deteriorating jobs, maintenance activities,
sequence-dependent setup times, availability constraints due to machine breakdowns, over-
lapping operations, controllable processing times, among others (Abdolrazzagh & Adbullah,
2017).
Additionally, Abdolrazzagh and Adbullah (2017) also reported that the most commonly
studied objective functions in the JSSP are the makespan, weighted tardiness, and maximum
tardiness. Based on the review of recent literature conducted for this project, it was found that
more than 80% of the studies on the JSSP focus on minimizing the makespan. The remaining
less than 20% of studies are divided among the minimization of weighted tardiness, process
delays, and events associated with the machines.
On the other hand, most studies conducted in JSSP have considered all parameters as
deterministic (Mohan et al., 2019). However, industrial environments are inherently com-
plex with the occurrence of events with stochastic behavior such as machine breakdowns,
maintenance time, processing times, among others (Meng et al., 2020). Most papers that
address the JSSP, assume that machines are always available, although machine breakdowns
are one of the most common and challenging issues in production environments (Zhang et al.,
2022). If machine failures are not considered during scheduling development, such schedules
can become inapplicable (Shady et al., 2021). Particularly, stochastic times between failures
and repairs happen for various reasons. Whenever a failure occurs, the maintenance crew
takes time to assess the problem, depending also on the crew availability and expertise on
the technology. Then, based on the severity of the problem, repair times can vary, whether

immediate on-site repairs can be done (e.g. oil change, part replacement, parameter setting,
among others), or the machine need be transferred to the maintenance workshop, or even
replaced (Giacaman et al., 2002). Despite the beneÔ¨Āts of including stochastic malfunctions
within the scheduling process, the problem becomes much more complex due to the large
search space (Shady et al., 2021).
One of the booming methods used in the literature to solve stochastic optimization prob-
lems are simheuristics, which involves the hybridization of metaheuristics and simulation
techniques (Juan et al., 2015). The advantage of implementing simheuristics is that they
allow for the veriÔ¨Ācation of solutions through a simulation process that takes into account the
previously deÔ¨Āned stochastic variables. As a result, the obtained solutions are more suitable
for the problem context since they consider uncertainty. Simheuristics have been success-
fully applied to various problem domains, including routing problems (Gonz√°lez-Mart√≠n
et al., 2016; Gruler et al., 2018; Quintero-Araujo et al., 2019), facility location problems (de
Armas et al., 2017), production scheduling problems (Gonz√°lez-Neira & Montoya-Torres,
2019; Juan et al., 2014), among others.
Considering that simheuristics are a hybridization between metaheuristics and simulation,
it is important to mention that several metaheuristic approaches have been used to solve JSSP
in the literature. These approaches include alternative graph (Meloni et al., 2004), simulated
annealing (SA) (Baykasoglu, 2002; Naderi et al., 2010), tabu search (TS) (Nowicki & Smut-
nicki, 2005; C. Zhang et al., 2007), iterated greedy algorithm (Pranzo & Pacciarelli, 2016),
hybridization of immune artiÔ¨Ācial and SA algorithms (Roshan et al., 2013), hybridization
of evolutionary algorithm and TS (Cheng et al., 2016) and, hybridization genetic algorithm
(GA) and SA (Shahsavari-Pour & Ghasemishabankareh, 2013).
In this paper, a TS algorithm is proposed to solve the stochastic job shop scheduling
problem (SJSSP). One of the advantages of TS according to Abido (2002) is the robustness
of the parameters and the initial solution. In addition, TS is characterized by its ability to
avoid entrapment in local optimum solutions and to prevent cycles due to the use of Ô¨āexible
memory for historical search. In fact, recent works in the Ô¨Āeld of JSSP have demonstrated the
effectiveness of TS, including studies by Aschauer et al. (2020a); Tamssaouet et al. (2018);
and Xie et al. (2023).
Consequently, this paper reports a simheuristic approach to solve SJSSP, hybridizing
a TS with Monte Carlo simulation (MCS), to minimize the expected maximum lateness,
considering stochastic times between failures (TBF) and stochastic times to repair (TTR) with
non-resumable jobs. The maximum lateness is related to the delivery date to customers, which
implies minimizing the worst violation of due dates (Pinedo, 2016) and it is recommended for
make-to-order environments (Botta-Genoulaz, 2000). Furthermore, the stochastic TBF and
TTR are modeled with two probability distributions: log-normal and exponential. The log-
normal distribution is important since it does not take negative values which it is appropriate
for reproducing machine malfunction times. In turn, the exponential distribution is often used
for breakdowns because there is a high degree of uncertainty about failures; thus, the only
information available on them is their mean.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Sect. 2 presents the literature review. In
Sect. 3, a mixed integer linear programming formulation is proposed for the deterministic ver-
sion of the problem. Section 4 explains the simheuristic approach, while Sect. 5 describes the
computational experiments carried out to evaluate the performance of the proposed approach.
Finally, Sect. 6 provides conclusions and future work.

2 Literature review
Firstly, this section reports the most recent studies on the deterministic version of the JSSP.
The second subsection focuses on articles that address SJSSP. Lastly, the third subsection
discuses simheuristic approaches to tackle both the JSSP and the SJSSP.
2.1 Deterministic JSSP
The interest in theoretical models for the resolution of the deterministic JSSP began in the
middle of the last century (Abdolrazzagh & Adbullah, 2017). Two of the Ô¨Ārst studies in JSSP
were performed by Akers and Friedman (1955) who used Boolean Algebra, and Jackson
(1956) that generalizes Johnson‚Äôs Flow Shop algorithm to JSSPs to minimize makespan.
From those researches until today, literature in JSSP has grown considerably using innovative
techniques. Methods to solve the JSSP vary from simple dispatching rules to sophisticated
algorithms that hybridize metaheuristics to obtain more accurate results. Figure 1 shows the
different techniques that have been implemented in recent years.
In the Ô¨Āeld of optimization, exact methods have been employed to solve classical JSSPs
(Zhang et al., 2019). These methods rely on input data and a series of predeÔ¨Āned rules to
determine the Ô¨āow of the process and obtain an optimal global solution. However, the runtime
of these methods is unpredictable, and for larger problems, they would require signiÔ¨Ācant
computational time (Gmys et al., 2020). One of the most commonly used exact methods in
JSSP is the Branch & Bound algorithm (Gmys et al., 2020). Additionally, there are several
mathematical models that are widely used, including mixed-integer linear programming
(Manne, 1960), Langrangian relaxation (Fisher, 1973), and integer linear programming (Li
et al., 2018).

Since exact methods require high computational times for medium to large instances,
approximation methods have been developed to Ô¨Ānd solutions proximate to global optimum
in less computational time (Fernandez-Viagas et al., 2017). In addition, optimal solutions
for small instances can be used as a standard reference to develop approximate methods
(Li et al., 2018). Among the approximation methods one can Ô¨Ānd the constructive methods
such as dispatching rules, bottleneck heuristics and insertion algorithms (Jain & Meeran,
1999). There are also iterative methods, which use different phases to approach an optimal
solution, selecting it either from a random list or with a probability function. For instance,
greedy algorithms such as GRASP (Bierwirth & Kuhpfahl, 2017; Feo & Resende, 1995) or
biased-randomization algorithms.
Generally speaking, JSSP approaches mostly focus on minimizing makespan and tardi-
ness. The makespan is deÔ¨Āned as the time taken to complete all jobs. On the other hand,
tardiness is related to due dates and measures the delay of jobs in relation to their due date
(Pinedo, 2016). Regarding the constraints considered, in the most recent works reviewed for
this paper, characteristics such as maintenance, breakdowns, and slack time represent only
around 10% of the studies.
2.2 Stochastic JSSPs
Most decision-making problems are combinatorial optimization problems that can be mod-
eled with uncertain conditions (Juan et al., 2015). Therefore, stochastic approximation
methods are used to optimize or control systems where the relationship between variables
and controllable factors of a process and its analytical model are unknown. These problems
are known as stochastic combinatorial optimization problems. Banerjee (1965) and Makino
(1965) were the Ô¨Ārst authors to conduct studies in production scheduling with stochastic pro-
cessing times. Banerjee solved a single-machine problem in which processing times follow
a known probability distribution, with the aim of minimizing the maximum probability of
delay. On the other hand, Makino carried out the study with 2 and 3 machines in a Ô¨āow
shop environment considering exponential and k-Erlang distributions for processing times,
minimizing the makespan (Juan et al., 2015). Some works assume that the starting times
and duration of disturbances (for instance, machine failures) are known in advance and Ô¨Āxed
(Yahyaoui et al., 2009), but in other works, probability distributions are used to generate such
variables.

The Ô¨Ārst paper was reported back in 1982 by Muhlemann et al. (1982). The authors included
dynamic job arrivals, uncertainty in job processing times, and the likelihood of machine
breakdowns. Various heuristics in the form of dispatching rules were applied to determine
the impact of variability in the mean ratio of Ô¨āow time to process time, mean queueing time,
mean lateness and percentage of tardy jobs. Later on, Tunali and Orhun (1990) develop a
knowledge-based system for production scheduling in a job-shop using around 300 dispatch-
ing rules with simulation-based performance evaluation. The authors studied the impact of
unexpected machine breakdowns on the predeÔ¨Āned schedule, modeling the time to failure
with a Poisson distribution. Output analysis was focused on machine utilization (under or
over) and job lateness. Likewise, Paredis and Van Rij (1992) used a simulator and an evaluator
to understand the effects of the decisions taken during the scheduling process. The simula-
tor tests the robustness of the schedules, provided by a Genetic Algorithm, with respect to

machine failures. Similarly, Holthaus (1999) also reported a simulation-based analysis of
dispatching rules for scheduling in a dynamic job shop, with respect to Ô¨āow times and due
dates. The study showed that, in terms of mean Ô¨āow time, the PT + WINQ (processing time
plus work in the queue of its next operation) outperforms other rules; but when the interest is
focused on minimizing the maximum Ô¨āow time, the AT-RPT (arrival time minus remaining
processing time) becomes superior. With respect to tardiness-based objectives, scheduling
rules showed high sensitivity to the percentage of machine downtimes and mean time to
repair. Experiments were carried out using an exponential distribution to represent TTR and
TBF for all machines.
As reported by Fang and Xi (1997), a periodic and event-driven rolling-horizon strategy
can help accommodate critical situations in job-shop environments caused by continuous
changes in job arrivals, machine breakdowns, machine repair times and job due dates. In this
work, the authors hybridized a GA with dispatching rules, while also considering sequence-
dependent setup times. All events are simulated at speciÔ¨Āc points in time, and the impact
on makespan is analyzed. In the presence of uncertainty, a robust or Ô¨āexible solution is
more valuable than an optimal solution. For instance, Tjornfelt-Jensen and Hansen (1999)
proposed a robust performance measure to identify robust solutions provided by a GA. The
robustness measure is deÔ¨Āned as the weighted average of makespan of schedules within a
neighborhood. To simulate machine failures, an operation is randomly chosen using a uniform
distribution. The starting time of this operation is called the breakdown time. The initial setup
time for the machine in failure is set to the time the broken machine becomes operational
again, which is the breakdown time plus the breakdown duration. Five different rescheduling
methods were investigated. The computational experiments revealed that, in most cases, the
rescheduling performance of schedules with a low robustness measure is signiÔ¨Ācantly better
than the performance of ordinary schedules for all tested strategies. The proposed measure
yielded good results for moderate breakdowns when compared to the slack-based measure.
Likewise, based on a rolling-horizon strategy, Upasani and Uzsoy (2008) proposed a problem-
reduction scheme to obtain global schedules with an integrated procedure that provides
priority indices for a dispatching approach in a multi-product environment with stochastic
machine breakdowns and job arrivals. The method was used to schedule operations of a wafer
fabrication facility where the presence of two unreliable stations can cause starvation in the
system. TBF and TTR were generated using gamma distributions.
Zou and Li (2006) used a GA and a hybrid allocation-based method for event-oriented
scheduling in JSSP under rush orders and machine breakdowns. If a rush order arrives,
the schedule is put on hold until the rush order is processed, but in the case of a machine
breakdown, only the affected operations are rescheduled. As pointed out by Suwa and Sandoh
(2007), the timing and frequency of rescheduling are important aspects to study. Therefore,
the authors used a tabu search (TS) to deÔ¨Āne a predictive schedule. Based on a control limit
policy, the time to conduct a schedule revision is determined based on the cumulative size
of delays. Sixteen machine breakdown scenarios were analyzed using simulation, and the
right-shift scheduling (RSS) policy was employed when a breakdown occurred. In terms of
minimizing maximum tardiness and scheduling frequency, this approach outperformed other
event-driven rescheduling approaches. Also using GA, Al-Hinai and Elmekkawy (2011)
presented a robust two-stage hybrid genetic algorithm (HGA). In the Ô¨Ārst stage, the algorithm
optimized the makespan using deterministic data. Then, in the second stage, it focused on
a bi-objective function by integrating machine assignments and operation sequencing with
the expected machine breakdown in the decoding space. While in most works, breakdown
variables are assumed to be random, the authors proposed that a machine with a higher load
is more likely to fail, and its breakdown time and duration can be assumed to be proportional

Another example of a hybrid GA is reported by Hasan et al. (2011), in which a GA is
hybridized with two simple priority rules, the partial re-ordering (PR) and the gap reduction
rule (GR), as well as the gap reduction and restricted swapping rule (GR-RS). The approach
was Ô¨Ārst tested using 40 benchmarks for the deterministic version. For the stochastic, a
uniform distribution was used to identify the machine with a failure, a Poisson distribution
generated the start time of the breakdown, and an Exponential distribution was used for
recoveryduration.First,thesolutionfortheidealJSSPisgenerated,andthentheGRalgorithm
is applied to re-optimize the affected sub-solution when a breakdown occurs. Similar to other
studies, the authors concluded that the impact of machine unavailability is quite low if the
information is known in advance. However, reactive scheduling becomes quite complex when
the breakdown is sudden. In the study conducted by Lei (2011), the SJSSP is addressed using
a simpliÔ¨Āed multi-objective GA, with exponentially distributed processing times, aiming
to minimize both makespan and total tardiness. The author recommended incorporating of
stochastic machine breakdowns into the model. In a subsequent work by Lei (2012), the
same JSSP was examined, but with stochastic normal processing times, resumable jobs, and
stochastic machine breakdowns, with the objective of minimizing makespan. To assess the
effectiveness of the proposed approach, it was compared to particle swarm optimization
(PSO), and the results demonstrated that GA outperforms PSO.
Hybrid metahaheuristic approaches have also been proposed to improve the pedictive-
reactive scheduling strategy. For instance, X. Li and Gao (2020) combined the advantage
of a GA and TS to deal with the SJSSP, coupled with event-driven rescheduling policies.
The approach was set up to minimize mean Ô¨āow time, mean tardiness, maximum tardiness,
and the number of tardy jobs. The TBF and TTR are assumed to follow an exponential
distribution. To generate unexpected events, a simulator was designed not only to reproduce
machine breakdowns but also uncertain job arrivals.
Chen et al., (1997) proposed a solution methodology that combines Lagrangian relax-
ation, stochastic dynamic programming, and ordinal optimization to address the JSSP
considering uncertain machine failures and job processing times. The machines are catego-
rized into reliable and unreliable sets, where the latter have known conditional failure and
recovery rates based on maintenance records. As a result, the number of available machines
becomes a random variable, and the algorithm‚Äôs performance is analyzed using Monte Carlo
simulation (MCS). Similarly employing Lagrangian relaxation, Zhu and Soh (1999) intro-
duced a two-stage procedure for off-line scheduling and an affected operations rescheduling
strategy (AOR) to generate new solutions in the presence of machine breakdowns, changes
in job priority, rush orders, order cancellations, and delays in raw material arrivals. The
objective is to minimize the mean weighted Ô¨āow time for solving FMS job shop problems.
Events are simulated at speciÔ¨Āc moments, and the authors concluded that among the Ô¨Āve
types of interruptions, machine breakdowns are the most challenging because their impact
depends on the timing, duration, and number of available machines. However, this conclu-
sion assumes that the breakdown time is known when it occurs. Subramaniam and Raheja
(2003) proposed a modiÔ¨Āed AOR strategy that depends on the type of disruption. The authors
discussed strategies for machine breakdowns, process time variations, arrival of unexpected
jobs, and urgent jobs. For machine failures, an idle time equivalent to the breakdown dura-
tion is inserted into the schedule, and the operation on the machine is put on hold until the
machine is repaired. Then, the schedule is repaired using the AOR heuristic. A simulation
study was conducted to examine the impact of disruptions in terms of size, occurrence time,
and schedule size. For machine breakdowns, the disruption size was analyzed for both small

Within the robust strategy to cope with uncertain machine failures, the buffering approach
can be applied to predictive schedules to reduce performance deterioration and improve pro-
duction system stability. Wu et al. (2020) proposed a multi-objective predictive scheduling
algorithm that considers three objectives: makespan, makespan risk and stability risk simul-
taneously. Two types of operation-block-based buffering strategies were implemented, and
after extensive experiments, the proposed approach was found to provide a better Pareto
solution set in terms of both diversity and convergence.
Fuzzy systems have been applied widely, mostly on control problems. Bilkay et al. (2004)
proposed a two-stage fuzzy logic-based algorithm. In the Ô¨Ārst stage, the algorithm assigns
priorities to part types, and in the second stage, an operation allocation is performed to min-
imize makespan, relative machine loads and cost. The proposed algorithm regenerates the
schedule after a machine breakdown, making it suitable for use as an on-line controller. In
the same vein, proactive scheduling aims to constructs schedules that take into account sta-
tistical knowledge of uncertainty upfront. Bollapragada and Sadeh (2004) reported proactive
technique that determines the release dates of different jobs based on measures of shop load,
statistical data of machine failures, and repairs, with a tradeoff between inventory and tar-
diness costs. Using a backward scheduling procedure, initial release dates are determined.
Then, a forward scheduling procedure is used to make sequencing decisions. Experiments
were carried out assuming normally distributed TTR and TBF. The approach was compared
with 39 other rules, and SA proved to yield better schedules even with an increasing fre-
quency of breakdowns. Petrovic et al. (2008) proposed a fuzzy approach to determine the
size of lots, considering fuzzy processing times and fuzzy due dates, to minimize the multiple
objectives such as weighted tardiness of jobs, total setup times, total tardy jobs, total Ô¨āow
time, and total machine idle times. To evaluate the efÔ¨Āciency of the solution method, data
from a printing company were used.
Neural networks have also been used, where neurons can represent processing restrictions
and resolve constraint conÔ¨āicts. For example, Y. Li and Chen (2009) combined the back-
propagation neural network (BPNN) to model machine breakdowns and new job arrivals by
detecting whether constraints are satisÔ¨Āed. Then, based on the BPNN‚Äôs feasible solution for
the JSSP, a GA is applied for sequence optimization and makespan minimization. Likewise,
the works of Zandieh and Adibi (2009) and Adibi et al. (2010) also conÔ¨Āgured an artiÔ¨Ācial
neural network with a backpropagation error learning algorithm, but in this work, the neural
network was employed to update parameters of a variable neighborhood search (VNS) algo-
rithm in response to random job arrivals and machine breakdowns. Similarly, Azadeh et al.
(2012) proposed a hybrid algorithm that evaluates its performance using a discrete-event
simulation model and artiÔ¨Ācial neural networks (ANN) to solve the JSSP. The objective of
the proposed algorithm is to select the best dispatching rule to assign to each machine with the
aim of minimizing the makespan. The authors considered random processing times measured
in three probability distributions: normal, exponential, and uniform. The standard deviation
and mean variability were kept consistent for each machine.

sent entities capable of making decisions in real-time. Liu et al. (2004) explored the use of
multiple agents employing an auction-based mechanism to schedule job shops in a dynamic
environment, utilizing a rolling horizon time procedure. Only available machines can host
auctions; therefore, if a machine breaks down, any job can be allocated to it. Similarly, based

Holonic Manufacturing Systems (HMS), as well as MAS, are distributed paradigms

The MTBF values are represented by a triangular membership function, ranging from 0 and

Recently, Deep Learning (DL) techniques have been applied to solve the dynamic JSSP
due to their capability to extract features to solve the problem, while the context changes. S.
Chen et al. (2022) proposed a spatial pyramid pooling-based transformer (SPP-Transformer)
that extracts environment features in order to select the best priority rule. The authors claim
that this approach enables fast rescheduling by leveraging the simplicity of priority rules and
the ability of DL methods to extract context features. They reported an improvement of 11%
in the makespan.
One way to test new solutions in the presence of perturbations is through simulation. For
instance, Feng et al. (2020) designed a capacity adjustment system for a multi-product job
shopbasedonaheuristicdecision-makingmodule.Thesimulationmoduleisutilizedtoassess
the performance of the solutions when machine alterations occur. The objective is to monitor
the system and propose corrective actions to minimize the delay rate, maximize the machine
utilization rate, minimize the number of used machines, and minimize the mean total waiting
time. The machine parameters follow an exponential distribution, and the results showed that
the system can enhance the agility of the production system to cope with the complex and
ever-changing industrial setting. In the following subsection, approaches coupling simulation
with the concept of simheuristics are described.
2.3 Simheuristic approaches to solve SJSSP
Few works using simheuristics have been proposed to deal with uncertain parameters in JSSP.
For instance, the following approaches use MCS to account for stochastic problem parame-
ters. Particularly, Beck and Wilson (2007) proposed four constructive algorithms and two TS
algorithms hybridized with MCS to solve a SJSSP with uncertain processing time, aiming to
obtain the solution with a high probability of a good makespan. The algorithms were tested
for instances up to 20 jobs with 20 machines. The results showed that for smaller instances,

4 A simheuristic approach based on tabu search
Inthissectiontheproposedtabusearch(TS)metaheuristicisexplained.Themaincomponents
include the coding scheme, the initial solution, the exploration of the neighborhood, and the
utilization of Monte Carlo simulation (MCS) to achieve a stochastic simheuristic approach.
4.1 Coding scheme and the initial solution
Different coding schemes have been used in JSSP literature, and one of the most popular is
the permutation-based approach. In this paper, the job-order coding is used, which denotes
the order in which the jobs‚Äô Ô¨Ārst operation is arranged. Therefore, the size of the sequence
is equal to the number of jobs. For example, a 6 √ó 6 JSSP (6 jobs and 6 machines, 36
operations), randomly generates a vector such as [6,2,1,4,5,3]. For those jobs that share the
same machine for the Ô¨Ārst operation, the priority in the machine‚Äôs queue is determined by the
position of the job in the vector. For instance, in the given example, job #2 and job #4 share
machine #1. Since job #2 is placed before job #4 in the sequence, machine #1 processes job
#2 Ô¨Ārst and then job #4. As mentioned earlier, one way to initialize the search procedure is by
generating a random permutation sequence. However, priority rules can also be utilized, and
in this case, the earliest due date (EDD) rule was explored to schedule jobs‚Äô Ô¨Ārst operation
(Su et al., 1998; Vinod & Sridharan, 2011).
The job-order coding scheme was chosen for three reasons. Firstly, the vector size remains
as small as possible, unlike the working procedure where the vector¬īs size is equal to the
number of operations (the coding determines the sequence of each job on each machine).
Secondly, considering that a resource is not kept idle if it can start an activity (i.e., non-
delay scheduling), once a job Ô¨Ānishes its Ô¨Ārst operation it immediately moves to the next
machine in its sequence, and so on, until all its operations are processed. By using the
working procedure, sequences with idle times are analyzed in the exploration process, thus
enlarging the search space and requiring more computational cost. Lastly, since the job-
ordered permutation approach determines the allocation of the Ô¨Ārst operation, it allows for
the integration of priority rules for sequencing the remaining operations. Priority rules such
as SPT, EDD, slack (SL), waiting time in the next queue (WINQ), among others, can be
chosen depending on the objective function. Those priority rules are used once a machine
Ô¨Ānishes an operation, and there are two or more jobs in the machine‚Äôs queue, allowing the
machine to select the next job according to the rule. In this paper, given that the objective
function is due-date dependent, the EDD rule was chose as the tiebreaker.

5.3 Tabu search parameter tuning
To perform the parameterization of the simheuristic, the number of iterations without
improvement (NIT) and the size of the tabu memory (TM) were considered as the main
factors in the computational experiment. Three independent experiments were conducted
based on the instance sizes. The Ô¨Ārst experiment was executed with ft06 instances, which are
considered small. The second experiment included medium-sized instances, namely la01 to
la36, which range from 10 to 15 jobs. Finally, the third experiment focused on instances with
20 jobs, speciÔ¨Ācally ft20 to yn1. For the second experiment, the 32 instances corresponding
to la36 were used, and for the third experiment, the 32 instances corresponding to yn1 were
utilized. In total, 96 instances were used for parameter tuning.
For the NIT factor, values of 5, 10, 15, 30 and 100 iterations were chosen after conducting
some preliminary experiments. Regarding the TM factor, values around the number of jobs
were utilized, speciÔ¨Ācally 5, 10, 15, and 20. In each experiment, 12 treatments were analyzed
by pairing the values of both factors. The response variable was deÔ¨Āned as the improvement
percentage of mean expected maximum lateness. To ensure proper parameterization, consid-
ering that the assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity were not met, we conducted
a separate ANOVA-Type statistic for small, medium, and large instances. The aim was to
identify the optimal combination of NIT and TL parameters for each instance size.
The ANOVA-Type statistics revealed that neither the NIT factor nor the TM factor showed
statistical signiÔ¨Ācance. Based on these results, a descriptive analysis was conducted to select
the combination of NIT and TM that provided the lowest average of expected maximum
lateness while considering computational effort. Figure 5 presents the interaction plots of TS
parameters for each instance size.
For small instances, the combinations that yield lower average values of expected maxi-
mum lateness are as follows: NIT 30 TM 5, NIT 100 TM 5, and NIT 5 TM 10. Among them,
the combination NIT 30 TM 5 requires the least computational effort. Additionally, this com-
bination has a TM value that is closest to the size of the vector codiÔ¨Ācation of the tabu for
these instances, which is 6 jobs. As a detailed example of the convergence curves provided
by the simheuristic, Fig. 6 shows the convergence curves for a speciÔ¨Āc small instance run

of NIT. It can be observed that the convergence is achieved after NIT = 25 for the log-normal
distribution, whereas for the exponential distribution, it occurs after NIT = 5. Despite both

and MTBFs values being the same for each machine in both executions, the exponential
distribution exhibits higher expected maximum lateness values compared to the log-normal

6 Conclusions and future research
This paper focuses on the study of a stochastic job shop scheduling problem (SJSSP) with
the objective of minimizing the expected maximum lateness. The main source of uncertainty
considered in this research is machine breakdowns. To address this problem, we formulate
a mixed-integer linear programming model that incorporates the expected times for repairs
and between failures. However, due to the problem‚Äôs complexity, the model is only capable
of providing solutions for very small instances. To overcome this limitation, we propose
a tabu search (TS) simheuristic that combines Monte Carlo simulation. The exponential
and log-normal distributions are employed to model the times between failures and repair
durations, respectively, using different coefÔ¨Ācients of variation. During each replica of the
Monte Carlo simulation, we generate realizations of these times while maintaining the same
expected value for each machine and for a speciÔ¨Āc combination of probability distribution
and coefÔ¨Ācient of variation. The proposed simheuristic is parameterized by deÔ¨Āning the size
of the tabu list and the number of iterations without improvement for three instance sizes:
small, medium, and large.
To evaluate the performance of the simheuristic, two computational experiments were
conducted. Firstly, for small instances, we compared the results of the simheuristic with
the simulation of solutions obtained from the MILP model, as well as the EDD and PT +
WINQ + SL dispatching rules. Secondly, for medium and large instances, we compared the
simheuristic results with the simulation of solutions provided by the EDD and PT + WINQ
+ SL dispatching rules. The average percentage improvement in expected maximum lateness
was as follows: for small instances, 7.12% compared to the simulation of the best solution
found by the mathematical model, 11.34% compared to the simulation of EDD, and 14.92%
compared to the simulation of the PT + WINQ + SL dispatching rule. For medium and
large instances, an average improvement of 11.72% and 14.08% was achieved, respectively,
when compared to the simulation of solutions provided by the EDD and PT + WINQ +
SL dispatching rules. These results demonstrate the excellent performance of the proposed
approach. It is important to note that the average execution times of the simheuristic were
56.68 s for small instances, 4.51 min for medium instances, and 14.82 min for large instances.
In contrast, the MILP model took 38.11 s for small instances, but it did not yield an optimal
solution within an 8-h execution time for medium and large instances.
The results also provide further insights into the behavior of the expected maximum
lateness under different probability distributions and coefÔ¨Ācients of variation. It was observed
thathigherexpectedmaximumlatenessvalueswereobtainedwiththeexponentialdistribution
compared to the Log-normal distribution with a coefÔ¨Ācient of variation of 1. Additionally,
in the case of the log-normal distribution alone, as the coefÔ¨Ācient of variation increases,
the expected maximum lateness also increases. These Ô¨Āndings highlight the importance
of appropriately adjusting the probability distributions for the times between damages and
repair times to obtain accurate solutions. Furthermore, it was discovered that solving the
deterministicproblemandimplementingsuchasolutionisnotsuitableasitleadstosigniÔ¨Ācant
deviations.
Future work will focus on evaluating the hybridization of TS with other simulation
paradigms, such as agent-based simulation, in order to compare their performances. Another

important recommendation to consider is the implementation of another metaheuristic com-
bined with Monte Carlo simulation, such as a genetic algorithm, GRASP, or iterated local
search. Additionally, it is suggested to introduce other parameters with random behavior,
such as processing times and ready times. Lastly, exploring the use of alternative probability
distributions to model the random behavior of times to repair and between failures is also
recommended.
