Abstract
Fairness is a critical yet underexplored dimension of tournament scheduling, where ﬁxture
asymmetries can systematically distort competition outcomes. This paper develops a Monte
Carlo simulation framework as a general decision-support tool for assessing procedural fair-
ness in league formats, isolating distortions arising from ﬁxture structure rather than team
strength or ranking rules. The approach integrates probabilistic match outcome modelling
with two complementary fairness indicators: total fairness bias, capturing the magnitude of
schedule-induced distortions, and the Gini index, measuring their distribution across partic-
ipants. By incorporating competition-speciﬁc importance weights, the framework enables a
context-aware, two-dimensional assessment applicable to any round-robin or multi-phase for-
mat, and can be embedded as a modular component into optimization, heuristics, or machine
learning–based scheduling systems. To illustrate its applicability, we apply the framework to
two European football leagues with structurally asymmetric designs. The results show how
corrective mechanisms – such as home/away balancing phases – can signiﬁcantly reduce
fairness distortions compared to uncorrected triple round-robin structures. Beyond the case
studies, the contribution is methodological: the framework provides a reproducible tool for
fairness assessment and a foundation for fairness-aware optimization and scheduling meth-
ods applicable across sports and competition formats. Although demonstrated on football,
the methodology is domain-independent and transferable to any context where fairness and
equity are of relevance.

Sports league scheduling provides a particularly transparent setting in which such pro-
cedural effects can be examined. In many professional competitions, structural constraints
of the adopted format make perfect balance unattainable. In leagues with a small number
of participants, formats requiring an odd number of encounters between each pair of teams
inevitably lead to unequal exposure to contextual advantages such as home venue. As a con-
sequence, it becomes structurally impossible to ensure an equal number of home and away
matches for all participants, even when schedules are generated through carefully designed
optimization procedures. Residual asymmetries therefore persist by construction.
Similar structural imbalances arise in a variety of professional leagues that rely on odd-
numbered round-robin formats or incomplete opponent pairings. Examples include 12-team
football leagues with 33-match regular seasons such as the Hungarian National League, the
Swiss Super League, the Scottish Premiership, the Northern Irish Premiership, and the North
Macedonian First League, as well as non-football competitions like the Big Bash League
in cricket, where an incomplete round-robin structure implies unequal opponent exposure.
These formats differ in sport and regulation, but share the common feature that perfect home–
away or opponent balance is structurally unattainable.
While team performance is primarily driven by sporting and ﬁnancialfactors such as player
quality, coaching, and resources (Peeters & Szymanski, 2014), ﬁxture-related asymmetries
may introduce unintended procedural advantages or disadvantages. These effects can be
particularly consequential in leagues where marginal differences determine qualiﬁcation for
international competitions, playoff participation, or relegation. Quantifying the extent to
which such asymmetries distort competitive outcomes is therefore essential for evaluating
the procedural fairness of a competition format.
Related concerns have also been raised in the context of tournament draws, where for-
mally random procedures can still generate systematic probability distortions due to structural
constraints (Guyon, 2015; Csató, 2025). These ﬁndings suggest that even well-intentioned
randomness may fail to ensure procedural fairness when embedded in constrained combina-
torial settings.
The sports scheduling literature has primarily addressed fairness ex ante, by incorporating
structural constraints such as home–away balance, break minimization, or carry-over effects
into schedule construction. While these approaches aim to prevent unfairness by design, they
do not quantify how residual asymmetries translate into distortions of ﬁnal outcome proba-
bilities once the competition is played. As a result, procedural fairness is typically enforced
at the construction stage rather than evaluated in terms of its probabilistic consequences.
This paper adopts a complementary perspective to the sports scheduling literature. Rather
than constructing or optimizing schedules, we focus on the ex post, outcome-based evaluation
ofproceduralfairness.Professionalleagueschedulesaretypicallygeneratedthroughcomplex
optimization processes subject to numerous structural, logistical, and commercial constraints,
including home–away balance, break minimization, travel considerations, and broadcasting
requirements (Kendall, 2008; Bartsch et al., 2006; Della Croce & Oliveri, 2006; Van Bulck
et al., 2020). Our objective is therefore not to redesign these procedures, but to diagnose
how residual ﬁxture asymmetries distort outcome probabilities within otherwise standard
and widely accepted competition formats.
To this end, we propose a simulation-based framework that integrates historical perfor-
mance data, a probabilistic match outcome model, and Monte Carlo simulations to quantify
schedule-induced distortions in ﬁnal league standings. Central to the framework is a com-

A key methodological contribution of the paper is the joint use of two complemen-

unevenly these distortions are distributed across teams. Both indicators are computed using

beyond purely structural or average-based measures.
We illustrate the proposed framework using two European football leagues with triple
round-robin structures: the Hungarian National League (NBI) and the Swiss Super League.
These leagues employ different balancing mechanisms, making them suitable case studies for
examining how ﬁxture asymmetries are mitigated or ampliﬁed by competition design. More
broadly, the choice of case studies is motivated by recent work emphasizing the importance of
outcome-based evaluation when comparing competition formats and design choices (Lasek &
Gagolewski, 2018; Devriesere et al., 2025). While the empirical analysis focuses on football,
the methodology is not sport-speciﬁc and is applicable to a broad class of round-robin and
multi-phase competition formats.
The study addresses the following research questions:
• To what extent do ﬁxture-induced asymmetries distort ﬁnal league standings?
• How do different competition designs mitigate or amplify such distortions?
• How can procedural fairness be systematically quantiﬁed in probabilistic terms?
By answering these questions, the paper contributes a diagnostic, outcome-level fairness
evaluation tool that complements existing optimization-oriented research on sports schedul-
ing and tournament design.
2 Related work
2.1 Sports scheduling and timetabling
Sports scheduling has traditionally been studied as an optimization problem subject to a
wide range of structural, logistical, and commercial constraints. These include home – away
balance, minimization of breaks, travel distance reduction, rest periods, and broadcasting
considerations (Kendall, 2008; Easton et al., 2001; Anagnostopoulos et al., 2006; Ribeiro &
Urrutia, 2007; Bartsch et al., 2006; Della Croce & Oliveri, 2006). Van Bulck et al. (2020)
provide a comprehensive classiﬁcation of round-robin scheduling problems and emphasize
that professional league schedules are typically generated through sophisticated optimization
procedures rather than simple random draws.
Fairness considerations have been incorporated into scheduling models through struc-
tural criteria, such as minimizing carry-over effects, balancing home–away sequences, or
limiting consecutive home or away matches (Zeng & Mizuno, 2013; Goossens et al., 2020).
These approaches focus on ensuring fairness at the schedule-construction level by enforc-
ing desirable structural properties. However, they do not explicitly quantify how remaining
asymmetries translate into distortions of competitive outcomes once match results are real-
ized.

Rasmussen (2008), for example, incorporated perceived unfairness into the generation of
triple round-robin schedules for the Danish Superliga, a format identical in structure to that
of the Hungarian NBI. While such approaches aim to produce fairer schedules ex ante, they
do not provide tools for evaluating fairness ex post across alternative feasible schedules.
2.2 Tournament design and outcome-based evaluation
A complementary stream of research evaluates competition formats based on the statistical
properties of their outcomes. Scarf et al. (2009) introduced a simulation-based framework to
compare knockout and round-robin formats using measures such as outcome uncertainty and
match importance. More recently, Lasek and Gagolewski (2018); Devriesere et al. (2025)
and Sziklai et al. (Sziklai et al.) assessed the efﬁcacy of tournament designs by examining
how accurately different formats identify the strongest teams.
Monte Carlo simulation has also been widely used to study competitive balance and uncer-
tainty in modern competition formats. Gyimesi (2024), for example, analyzed the post-2024
UEFA Champions League format and Swiss-system tournaments, highlighting how struc-
tural design choices inﬂuence outcome distributions. While these studies focus on accuracy,
uncertainty, or competitive balance, they do not explicitly address procedural fairness arising
from ﬁxture-induced asymmetries within a ﬁxed format.
Our work is closely related to this literature in its use of probabilistic modeling and simula-
tion, but it addresses a distinct question: rather than evaluating how well a format rewards the
strongest teams, we quantify how ﬁxture asymmetries distort outcome probabilities relative
to a fair baseline.
2.3 Ranking fairness and axiomatic perspectives
Fairness has also been examined from an axiomatic perspective, with a focus on the properties
of ranking rules. Vaziri et al. (2018) propose fundamental fairness criteria such as incentive
compatibility, opponent strength consideration, and schedule independence. However, it is
well established that no ranking system can simultaneously satisfy all desirable axioms in
general tournament settings (Csató, 2019a,b), implying that part of the perceived unfairness
in league outcomes may originate not only from ranking rules, but also from procedural
elements such as ﬁxture realization.
Beyond ranking rules, recent work has shown that procedural fairness issues may already
arise at the level of tournament draws and group allocations, even before matches are played.
While Guyon (2015) discusses how constrained draw procedures can lead to uneven out-
come probabilities, Csató (2025) formally demonstrates that, in the FIFA World Cup group
draw, certain team allocations occur with substantially higher probability due to underly-
ing combinatorial constraints. ELO-based rating systems (Elo, 1978), for instance, violate
schedule independence due to their path-dependent nature (Csató et al., 2025). In contrast,
point-based ranking systems used in round-robin leagues satisfy several key axioms, includ-
ing self-consistency (González-Díaz et al., 2014). Importantly, the present study does not aim
to evaluate or compare ranking rules. We assume a ﬁxed and widely accepted points-based
system and focus exclusively on procedural fairness issues arising from ﬁxture asymmetries
within that system.

2.4 Positioning of the present study
Insummary,existingresearchonsportsschedulingandtournamentdesignhaslargelyfocused
on either (i) optimizing schedules subject to fairness-related constraints, or (ii) evaluating
competition formats based on accuracy, uncertainty, or competitive balance. In contrast,
the present study provides an outcome-level, probabilistic evaluation of procedural fairness
within a ﬁxed competition format.
By isolating the impact of ﬁxture-induced asymmetries through simulation and compari-
son with a fair benchmark, the proposed framework complements optimization-oriented and
axiomatic approaches. It offers a transparent and reproducible tool for diagnosing fairness
distortions that remain even when schedules are generated using established best practices.
3 Competition formats
The two leagues under study – Hungarian NBI and the Swiss Super League – employ notably
different competition formats, which inﬂuence how much the ﬁxture schedule can affect ﬁnal
outcomes.
In the Hungarian NBI, 12 teams participate in a triple round-robin format. Each team
plays 33 matches: once home and once away against every other team (22 matches), and
a third match against each opponent at alternating venues (11 matches). According to the
ofﬁcial competition regulations, no team is seeded during the draw of the full schedule,
including rounds 23 to 33. This means that the allocation of home and away venues in the
third round is random rather than based on previous season rankings. As a result, some teams
may receive 17 home matches and 16 away matches, while others get the reverse, depending
on the random draw. This built-in asymmetry – despite the lack of intentional bias – can still
lead to draw-dependent advantages in the ﬁnal standings.
Similarly, the Swiss Super League adopted the current triple round-robin format with 33
matches per team starting only from the 2023/2024 season. This new regular season format
matches the Hungarian NBI’s triple round-robin structure, where each team plays every other
team three times (22 matches in home-and-away pairs plus 11 matches with alternating home
venues).
However, unlike the Hungarian league, the Swiss Super League introduced a second
phase starting with this format. After the 33-round regular season, the league table is split
into two groups of six: the Championship Group (places 1–6) and the Relegation Group
(places 7–12). In each group, teams play ﬁve additional matches – one more game against
each group opponent. Ideally, teams that had more away games in rounds 23–33 are granted
more home matches in Phase 2 (three instead of two), allowing every team to reach 19 home
games across the full season. However, due to the composition of the groups and unavoidable
pairing overlaps, it is possible for some teams to end the season with either 18 or 20 home
matches.
The position a team holds at the end of the regular season is particularly crucial in Switzer-
land because ﬁnishing 6th or 7th determines whether a team qualiﬁes for the Championship
Group or the Relegation Group. This split has signiﬁcant consequences: teams in the Cham-
pionship Group compete for the title and European competition spots, while those in the
Relegation Group ﬁght to avoid demotion. Therefore, any ﬁxture-related advantage or dis-
advantage that affects ﬁnal regular season standings – especially around these borderline
positions – can substantially impact a team’s season trajectory.

To balance these asymmetries, the league explicitly states that home venue assignments
will be compensated in the following season’s ﬁxture planning by swapping home and away
designations in corresponding matches. Despite this attempt at compensation, the imbalance
in the number of home matches between teams within a season cannot be fully eliminated,
which may still result in schedule-based advantages or disadvantages.
These structural differences are central to our analysis. While both formats are inherently
unbalanced due to the odd number of matchups, the degree and mechanism of imbalance
differ: Hungary’s format uses a single-stage random draw without seeding, whereas Switzer-
land uses a two-phase structure with systematic – but not guaranteed – home/away balancing
mechanisms.
4 Methods
4.1 Methodology of assessing fairness
This section outlines the methodological framework used to assess ﬁxture fairness in pro-
fessional football leagues. Our approach integrates historical performance data, probabilistic
match outcome estimation, and Monte Carlo simulation to evaluate how random variations
in ﬁxture schedules affect ﬁnal league outcomes. We ﬁrst describe how team-speciﬁc prob-
abilities were derived from multi-season data, then explain how these were used to simulate
match results under both real and idealized league formats. The core objective is to isolate
the impact of ﬁxture structure and randomness on sporting fairness – separating it from team
strength, match predictability, or external contingencies. By comparing real-world scheduling
scenarios to fairness-preserving benchmarks, we can systematically quantify and compare
the degree of distortion inherent in different competition formats.

team, weighted averages of home and away match outcomes (win, draw, and loss percent-

It is important to emphasize that the objective of our simulation framework is not to
maximize predictive accuracy regarding individual match outcomes, but rather to provide a
stable and consistent basis for evaluating structural fairness in league formats. The probability
estimates are computed ex ante (before the simulated season begins) and do not incorporate
dynamic factors such as form ﬂuctuations, injuries, transfers, or head-to-head history. While
such factors can affect real-world results, including them would obscure the effect of the
ﬁxture structure itself, which is the primary focus of this study. The historical performance-
based approach ensures comparability across seasons and leagues, allowing us to isolate the
impact of ﬁxture asymmetries and randomness on ﬁnal standings.
This methodology integrates team-speciﬁc historical performance metrics while situating
them within league-wide normative behaviour. Such contextualization calibrates individual
strengths and weaknesses relative to typical league patterns and mitigates biases arising from
limited sample sizes or outlier performances. Moreover, the model inherently accounts for
the well-documented home-ﬁeld advantage. The multiplicative scoring function captures the
interaction between a home team’s propensity to win and an away team’s propensity to lose,
enhancing interpretability and supporting robust probability estimates.
4.3.1 Rationale for static probabilities
It is important to clarify the rationale behind the choice of a static, historical-performance-
based outcome model. Although dynamic Elo-based approaches that update team strengths
after each simulated match are widely used in the literature (e.g., (Gyimesi, 2024)), they are
not well suited to the objective of the present study. Our aim is not to model team evolution
or form dynamics, but to isolate the distortive effect of the competition structure itself.
Accordingly, team strengths are treated as ﬁxed and estimated ex ante from multi-
season historical data. Introducing dynamic strength updates would induce path dependence

In contrast to the Hungarian NB I, discrepancies in the Swiss Super League are more
evenly distributed across teams and matches, rather than being concentrated around a small
number of clubs. Larger deviations tend to occur in matches involving teams such as Young
Boys, Winterthur, or Yverdon, reﬂecting differences between market perceptions and histor-
ically smoothed performance estimates. This pattern suggests a higher degree of match-level
variability in market expectations, rather than systematic misalignment driven by a few struc-
turally overestimated teams.
Such mismatches are an expected consequence of the static, ex ante nature of the model.
While betting markets continuously adjust to real-time information, the proposed framework
relies on temporally smoothed historical data to ensure stability and comparability across
simulations. Importantly, these deviations remain localized and do not affect the overall
alignment with market probabilities, supporting the use of the model as a credible and stable
input for the subsequent fairness analysis.
4.4 Monte carlo simulation framework
The resulting probabilities were used in a Monte Carlo simulation framework designed to
assess the fairness of different league formats. For each real-world competition structure
(Hungarian NBI and Swiss Super League), we generated 100 random ﬁxture draws, simu-
lating entire seasons by stochastically determining match outcomes based on the previously
derived probabilities. This allowed us to quantify the variability in ﬁnal standings caused
solely by randomness in ﬁxture allocation.

4.5.5 Swiss Super League: real-world simulation procedure
To simulate the Swiss Super League format under realistic conditions, we implemented a
two-phase simulation structure incorporating both ﬁxture randomization and home-game
balancing. Each of the 100 random ﬁxture draws began with a triple round-robin schedule
covering 33 rounds, with home and away venues randomly allocated for the third match in
each pair.
In each simulation run, we ﬁrst computed the regular-season point totals using match
outcome probabilities derived from the historical performance-based model. Outcomes were
sampled probabilistically for each individual ﬁxture using the corresponding result distribu-
tion. The resulting league table determined the upper (places 1–6) and lower (places 7–12)
groups for the second phase.
In Phase 2, each team played one additional match against each group peer, resulting in
ﬁve additional ﬁxtures per team. Initial venue assignments for these matches were based on
the previous head-to-head venue history from the regular season. When one team had hosted
the other twice in the triple round-robin phase, the match was assigned as an away ﬁxture
to balance exposure; however, this rule alone does not guarantee equal home match counts
across all teams.
To mitigate the imbalance in home matches caused by structural constraints, we imple-
mented a balancing algorithm in the second phase. This procedure iteratively adjusted
home/away assignments for Phase 2 matches in a way that: (i) preserved ﬁxed 19-home-
match teams (i.e., those already balanced), (ii) corrected extreme inequalities (e.g., teams
with 16 or 22 home games), and (iii) reduced remaining discrepancies through randomized
bilateral swaps, ensuring that no team had more than 21 or fewer than 17 home games by
the end of the season. This balancing step was implemented in each simulation run using a
consistent rule-based logic (see Algorithm 1), emulating the Swiss league’s efforts to provide
equitable home/away exposure in the playoff phase.
Following venue assignment adjustment, we simulated Phase 2 match outcomes using the
same probability model. Final league standings were obtained by summing points from both
phases and ranking teams separately within their respective groups. Placement counts were
recorded across Monte Carlo replications, producing empirical probability distributions of
ﬁnal standings for all teams under each schedule. This procedure captures both the random-
ness of ﬁxture allocation and the residual structural imbalance, enabling a nuanced evaluation
of fairness under the real-world Swiss Super League format.
4.5.6 Uniﬁed simulation framework
Our uniﬁed Monte Carlo simulation framework captures two dimensions of ﬁxture-related
distortion: (i) variation in ﬁnal outcomes caused by different random draws under each real-
world league format, and (ii) structural asymmetries relative to an idealized neutral-venue
benchmark. By contrasting placement distributions from 100 real schedule simulations with
those from 250,000 fair-format simulations, we quantify both the volatility introduced by
ﬁxture randomness and the intrinsic fairness of the league’s structural design.

4.6 Fairness metrics
To evaluate the fairness of ﬁxture schedules beyond intuitive or anecdotal reasoning, we
introduce a quantitative framework based on team-level probabilistic outcome distributions.
Since fairness in league competitions is not only about minimizing aggregate distortion but
also about ensuring that such distortions are equitably distributed among all participants,
our framework includes two complementary metrics. The ﬁrst, total fairness bias (TFB),
quantiﬁes the overall magnitude of schedule-induced distortion across all teams. The second,
the Gini index, measures how unequally this distortion is shared. Together, these indicators
provide a multidimensional lens through which to assess the procedural fairness of league
scheduling, allowing us to capture both systemic bias and team-speciﬁc inequality.
4.6.1 Fairness bias deﬁnition
To evaluate the fairness of different ﬁxture schedules, we introduce a team-level fairness bias
metric that quantiﬁes the direction and magnitude of the distortion each team experiences
relative to a neutral and structurally balanced benchmark distribution. For each of the 100
randomly generated ﬁxture draws in our simulation, we compare each team’s placement prob-
ability distribution under the actual schedule to the corresponding distribution obtained from
the fairness-preserving benchmark (neutral venue for every third match), which eliminates
schedule-induced asymmetries.
The proposed metric captures the total weighted deviation between the placement
probability distributions under the actual schedule and the fairness-preserving benchmark
across all league positions. These deviations are weighted by an importance vector w =
[w1, w2, . . . , wn], which reﬂects the competitive and ﬁnancial relevance of each league posi-
tion. By taking absolute differences in placement probabilities, the metric treats favourable
and unfavourable distortions symmetrically, ensuring that any deviation from the benchmark
– regardless of direction – contributes to the measured distortion. The overall sign of the fair-
ness bias is then determined by whether the expected average position improves or worsens
under the actual draw compared to the ideal case. This separation allows the metric to jointly
represent (i) the magnitude of the ﬁxture-induced distortion and (ii) its direction from the
perspective of the affected team.

5 Results
5.1 Simulation results
This section presents the key ﬁndings from our simulation experiments, comparing the fair-
ness characteristics of the Hungarian NBI and the Swiss Super League under both real-world
and benchmarked conditions. Using the fairness bias metric and the Gini index as primary
indicators, we evaluate how different ﬁxture schedules inﬂuence the equity of league out-
comes.
Our analysis proceeds in three steps. First, we assess how actual ﬁxture draws affect
overall distortion relative to a neutral, fairness-preserving benchmark. Second, we examine
how these distortions are distributed across teams. Third, we investigate the robustness of
the results with respect to alternative positional weight speciﬁcations through a systematic
sensitivity analysis.
5.1.1 Baseline fairness comparison
As shown in Fig. 2, the Swiss Super League produces relatively consistent and moderate total
fairness bias values, mostly ranging between 0.61 and 0.99. This narrow spread indicates
that the league’s structural design – particularly the second phase and its home-game balanc-
ing mechanism – effectively mitigates the inﬂuence of ﬁxture randomness on ﬁnal league
outcomes. These patterns contrast sharply with the results observed for the Hungarian NBI,
discussed in more detail in the following sections.
By contrast, the Hungarian NBI shows a substantially wider and more volatile fairness
bias distribution (Fig. 3), with several ﬁxture schedules producing signiﬁcantly higher dis-
tortion levels. This indicates a stronger dependency on how the third-round home and away
matches are assigned. The absence of any post-draw correction mechanism in NBI allows the
asymmetries introduced by the triple round-robin format to persist throughout the season.
A concrete example illustrates this effect: in the ﬁxture with the highest observed fairness
distortion, Fehérvár – the second-strongest team in the simulation – was assigned two home
matches against six of the top seven opponents. This led to an artiﬁcial boost in its league
ﬁnish probabilities. Under the neutral benchmark, Fehérvár had a 4.9% chance of winning
the league and a 34.6% chance of ﬁnishing second. In the distorted ﬁxture draw, these
probabilities increased to 5.5% and 40.8%, respectively – an advantage stemming purely
from scheduling asymmetry.

5.1.5 Summary of ﬁndings
Taken together, the results show that the uncorrected triple round-robin structure of the Hun-
garian NBI is substantially more prone to fairness violations than the Swiss Super League.
The Swiss league’s two-phase format, which incorporates explicit home-away balancing,
provides effective protection against both systemic and team-speciﬁc distortions. The sen-
sitivity analysis further conﬁrms that these conclusions are stable across a wide range of
reasonable weight speciﬁcations.
6 From fairness evaluation to fairness-aware ﬁxture design
Many professional leagues with a small number of teams rely on triple round-robin structures
to ensure an adequate number of matches per season. While such formats are logistically
efﬁcient, the absence of explicit balancing mechanisms in the additional round gives rise to
systematic ﬁxture-related asymmetries, which translate into measurable distortions in teams’
outcome probabilities, as captured by the Total Fairness Bias (TFB) and the Gini index.
Importantly, these distortions are structurally induced by the three-round format itself,
since the odd number of encounters necessarily implies unequal home–away allocations.
However, their magnitude and distribution depend critically on the speciﬁc realization of
match pairings and home–away assignments. Thus, although a certain level of asymmetry
is unavoidable in 33-match formats, its competitive impact is not predetermined. Appropri-
ate ﬁxture construction can substantially mitigate—though not entirely eliminate—fairness
distortions without altering the number of teams or rounds.
From a practical perspective, the proposed fairness indicators provide quantitative criteria
for evaluating alternative ﬁxture conﬁgurations. More desirable schedules are those that
generate smaller overall distortions (low TFB) and distribute these distortions more evenly
across teams (low Gini index), in line with the objectives typically pursued by governing
bodies seeking to avoid both systematic bias and extreme disparities among participants.

Under this interpretation, ﬁxture construction can be viewed as a constrained optimisation
problem over match pairings, home–away assignments, and potentially sequencing decisions,
subject to league regulations and logistical requirements. In triple round-robin formats, the
additional round represents a particularly sensitive design element, as it introduces unavoid-
able asymmetries that must be carefully managed to prevent systematic bias.
The fairness evaluation framework developed in this paper can naturally serve as a basis for
such optimisation. The TFB and Gini index may be combined – if desired, through weighted
or fuzzy aggregation – into a composite measure of procedural fairness, which can guide
heuristic or evolutionary search methods well suited to the combinatorial and constrained
nature of ﬁxture design problems.
The empirical comparison illustrates that while the Hungarian NBI exhibits the conse-
quences of an uncorrected triple round-robin structure, the Swiss Super League demonstrates
that such asymmetries can be substantially mitigated through balancing mechanisms. Nev-
ertheless, residual distortions remain even in the Swiss format, particularly around decisive
mid-table thresholds, suggesting that further reductions in ﬁxture-induced unfairness could
be achieved by optimising regular-season pairings within existing competition designs.
Importantly, this perspective does not aim to replace current scheduling practices, but to
complement them by providing a quantitative diagnostic tool for identifying ﬁxture construc-
tions that reduce fairness distortions under otherwise ﬁxed competition rules. In this sense, the
analysis establishes a direct link between fairness measurement and decision-making, illus-
trating how simulation-based evaluation can inform concrete design choices in professional
sports leagues.
7 Conclusions and future work
This study proposes a Monte Carlo simulation framework as a decision-support tool for
evaluating procedural fairness in league competitions. By integrating probabilistic match
outcome modelling with two complementary indicators—total fairness bias and the Gini
index—the framework quantiﬁes both the magnitude and distribution of schedule-induced
distortions in a transparent and reproducible manner. While the empirical analysis focuses
on two European football leagues, the proposed approach is conceptually general and can
be transferred to other round-robin competitions, provided that appropriate outcome models
and ranking rules are speciﬁed.
The results highlight that structural safeguards, such as home–away balancing phases,
can substantially reduce fairness distortions compared with uncorrected triple round-robin
designs. More broadly, the framework supports both ex post fairness assessment and ex ante
scenario analysis, enabling organizers to anticipate and mitigate potential unfairness before
ﬁxtures are ﬁnalized.
From a decision-support perspective, the main contributions of the paper are threefold.
First, it formalizes fairness evaluation in a simulation environment that isolates ﬁxture-
induceddistortionsfromteamstrengthandrankingrules.Second,itprovidesmodularfairness
indicators that can serve as inputs to optimisation, heuristic, or learning-based scheduling
approaches.Third,itoffersaquantitativefoundationforfairness-awarecompetitiondesign,in
which fairness considerations can be balanced against logistical and commercial constraints.
Futureresearchmayextendtheframeworktoadditionalsportsandmulti-stagecompetition
formats, and explore its integration with ﬁxture construction and scheduling algorithms that
jointly consider fairness, efﬁciency, and stakeholder objectives.
