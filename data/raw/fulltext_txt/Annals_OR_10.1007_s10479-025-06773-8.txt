Abstract
When a disaster occurs, Humanitarian Logistics and Supply Chain Management (HLSCM) 
works to ensure the timely and appropriate distribution of relief goods. One is food distri¬≠
bution, one of the most needed essential supplies after a disaster. However, delays in the 
delivery of food logistics often occur, resulting in fatal consequences for disaster victims. 
In HLSCM, delays are referred to as risks, while the causes of delays are risk agents that 
must be identified. Identifying risk agents in distribution delays is crucial because it dra¬≠
matically affects the entire HLSCM flow. This study proposes a hybrid literature search 
strategy combined with expert judgment to identify risk agents that cause food delivery 
delays in the HLSCM process. The results of this study indicate that there are 17 risk 
agents affecting food logistics delivery delays. Based on the mathematical aggregation of 
expert judgment results, the highest average values ‚Äčare shown by three risk agents: ‚Äúinven¬≠
tory quantity,‚ÄĚ ‚Äúroad conditions,‚ÄĚ and ‚Äúcommunication problems.‚ÄĚ This investigation fills 
the gap in the existing HLSCM literature by explicitly identifying and validating these 
risk factors. The hybrid literature search strategy combines a systematic literature review 
with expert judgment and provides a strong framework for future research. These findings 
offer practical insights for stakeholders to improve logistics efficiency and ensure timely 
aid delivery. While focusing on risk identification, this study lays the groundwork for 
future research to prioritize risk mitigation strategies and further refine HLSCM practices.

lion people. In addition, disasters have caused an economic loss of about US$2.97 trillion 
(CRED, 2020). Floods and earthquakes are categorized as frequent disasters worldwide 
based on the type of natural disaster. From 2004 to 2023, floods dominated disaster cases

36,876 people per annual average (CRED, 2025; Van Dijk et al., 2025).
Recent global flood and earthquake disasters have caused severe social disruption and

tive hazards, exemplified by the 2004 Asian tsunami and recent events in Haiti, Japan, and 
Indonesia, leading to massive casualties, displacement, and economic costs exceeding 
US$43.5¬†billion annually on average (CRED, 2021, 2025; Zhang, 2014). These disasters 
have underscored the urgent need for effective risk-reduction strategies to mitigate their 
profound impact on societies and economies worldwide (Rosmadi et al., 2023; World Bank, 
2022).
During disasters, victims rely on humanitarian aid from various organizations and indi¬≠
viduals. The efficient delivery of this aid is managed through the Humanitarian Supply 
Chain Management (HSCM) system, which acts as a logistical control for the entire pro¬≠
cess. HSCM encompasses four key stages: preparedness, emergency response, mitigation, 
and rehabilitation. It also oversees the planning, implementation, and control of goods flow, 
emphasizing practicality and cost-effectiveness to ensure timely delivery to those in need 
(Arismawan et al., 2025; Thomas & Mizushima, 2005).
This study refers to the logistics distribution within HSCM as ‚ÄúHumanitarian Logistics 
and Supply Chain Management‚ÄĚ (HLSCM). According to Thomas and Kopczak (2005), 
HLSCM is about planning, executing, and controlling the efficient and cost-effective move¬≠
ment and storage of goods, materials, and related information from where they originate to 
where they are consumed, all to alleviate the suffering of vulnerable people. The primary 
aim of HLSCM is to provide timely supplies and services, ensuring essential items such as 
food, water, temporary shelter, clothing, and medicine are distributed during a disaster. Fail¬≠
ing to deliver humanitarian aid can have severe consequences, including fatalities (Chap¬≠
man & Mitchell, 2018; Syahrir et al., 2015; Tadińá et al., 2025).
Furthermore, this study concentrates on distributing food logistics among the essential 
items required during a disaster. The rationale is that food distribution constitutes the most 
vital logistics operation, as food is a fundamental necessity following a disaster (Cisneros-
Garc√≠a et al., 2023; Tadińá et al., 2025; Voyer et al., 2015). The absence of food on the 
humanitarian aid list will impede post-disaster recovery efforts. Therefore, the natural disas¬≠
ter recovery plan must guarantee that food assistance and distribution are accessible and 
equitably allocated to victims (De Haen & Hemrich, 2007; Leiras et al., 2021; MacNabb & 
Fletcher, 2019; Peters et al., 2021).
Prior research has identified multiple issues with food logistics distribution. These issues 
frequently impede the food logistics distribution system, resulting in delays in distribution. 
The timing and fairness of food aid distribution are prevalent issues in disaster management 
(√áankaya et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2021; Hasnain et al., 2021; Kembro, 2012; Vitoriano et 
al., 2011; Zulfikri, 2025). The delays stem from multiple issues, including the lack of food 
logistics planning, which concerns the configuration of the amount of food, determining

Conversely, overstocking, substantial waste, shortages, storage stability of food, and the

many risks that need to be minimized. Although the disaster relief supply chain has measur¬≠

to identify the risks that cause delays in aid distribution in HLSCM. The focus is limited to 
the distribution of food logistics during floods and earthquakes, with the justification that

the direct impact of risks on the entire Humanitarian Logistics and Supply Chain Manage¬≠

identifying, managing, and monitoring these risks is essential to reduce potential losses in 
HLSCM.
While HLSCM encompasses various aspects of humanitarian aid, similar studies that 
specifically identify risks within the food logistics component are barely available. This 
research is among the first to identify risks associated with food logistics in humanitarian 
supply chain management. It also introduces a novel methodology by combining a hybrid 
literature search strategy with expert judgment for risk identification. The hybrid literature 
search strategy integrates the ‚ÄúFinding Literature Technique‚ÄĚ (Schutt, 2019), which pro¬≠

expands the search by exploring reference lists of identified articles. This hybrid approach 
ensures a comprehensive and in-depth exploration of existing literature on HLSCM risks, 
serving as the basis for risk identification. Furthermore, the ‚ÄúTwo-Stage Expert Judgment 
Process‚ÄĚ includes content validity analysis and mathematical aggregation. The hybrid litera¬≠
ture search strategy ensures a thorough exploration of existing research, while the two-stage 
expert judgment process validates and prioritizes the identified risks.
2‚Äā Risk in humanitarian supply chain: recent publications
Previous researchers generally discuss risk in the humanitarian supply chain in the context 
of risk management. Effective risk management in these contexts hinges on identifying, 
evaluating, and mitigating potential risk events (Acevedo et al., 2019; Chukwuka et al., 
2023). Risk management includes identification, assessment and mitigation (Fan & Ste¬≠
venson, 2018; Lee, 2024; Minguito & Banluta, 2023). Previous researchers have studied

The first topic is risk identification in the humanitarian supply chain, which has drawn 
attention from recent researchers. Jahre (2017) and Yang et al. (2023) have studied the 
risks humanitarian organizations face during natural or man-made disasters. They identified 
macro and micro risks, as well as economic risks along the supply chain. On the other hand, 
Beresford and Pettit (2009) investigated emergency planning and risk reduction in relation 
to the Asian tsunami in Thailand, revealing the Thai government‚Äôs establishment of regional 
communication networks, early warning systems, and risk reduction measures.
Risk identification is also carried out at the prepositioning and preparedness levels. A 
study conducted by Jahre and Heigh (2008) analyzed the risk of funding preparedness in 
significantly improving response time and effectiveness. Arnette and Zobel (2019) and 
Balcik et al. (2019) are among the researchers who identified risks in prepositioning for 
humanitarian and disaster supplies. A similar thing was studied by Roh et al. (2015) on pre-
positioned warehouses. They found that pre-positioned warehouses for humanitarian aid 
generated 17 different types of dangers, which can be visualized using Interpretive Struc¬≠
tural Modeling (ISM).
Furthermore, Tay et al. (2022) examined the risks frequently occurring during the disas¬≠
ter management cycle (DMC) Their findings showed five major risk factors in DMC: (1) 
demand risk, (2) supply risk, (3) operational risk, (4) infrastructure risk, and (5) disruption 
risk. The severity of these risks varies depending on the stage of the DMC. This study pro¬≠
posed a supply chain strategy (SCS) that increases risk awareness, collaboration, and hori¬≠
zontal and vertical coordination among key DMC stakeholders. Baharmand et al. (2017) 
studied on in-country transportation risks in humanitarian supply chains, using the 2015 
Nepal earthquake as a case study. They found supply delays, market changes, and limited 
capacity as the most significant risks, with cargo deterioration and ethical issues as minor.
L√∂ffel et al. (2025) is among the first to derive supply chain risks and categories specific 
to humanitarian aid delivery. Their study found six aggregated risk categories in humanitar¬≠
ian aid delivery. Furthermore, a differentiated view of risk types allows for stronger inter-
organizational collaboration within the sector and beyond. Miller and Engemann (2008) 
carried out more extensive research. The study covered the supply chain risks from cata¬≠
strophic and non-catastrophic localized occurrences that impact business operations, like 
fires and power outages. It depicted the hazards associated with natural disasters in the sup¬≠
ply chain and provided the Monte Carlo Simulation Model to simulate their consequences. 
The resulting model evaluated several scenarios, such as node correlation, the effectiveness 
of disaster recovery, and numerous sources.
Humanitarian supply chains are inherently vulnerable to a variety of risks. Therefore, 
Risk identification is a crucial aspect of the humanitarian supply chain, as it helps organiza¬≠
tions identify and mitigate risks associated with natural or man-made disasters. Previous 
research on this theme has identified various risks that are commonly assessed across the 
entire humanitarian supply chain. These include macro-level disruption risks (e.g., natural 
disasters, political instability) and micro-level challenges (e.g., demand fluctuations, supply 
chain disruptions). Effective risk identification is critical to successful aid delivery.
Research on risk identification topic highlights the importance of identifying and miti¬≠
gating risks across all phases of the disaster management cycle, from preparedness and

humanitarian organizations can manage risk more effectively if they have a solid contin¬≠

mitigation emphasize that a multi-faceted approach to risk mitigation, including proactive

The finding literature process has produced 15 articles in total. The Scopus database found 
five articles relating to the search string. However, only four articles were included. One 
article was excluded because it was not related to natural disasters. Similarly, the WoS data¬≠

of articles obtained from the Scopus and WoS databases was 15. These 15 articles are called 
key articles, which are the basis for finding other literature using snowball tracing method 
(Wohlin, 2014).
The articles obtained through the finding literature process continued to the next step, 
i.e., the snowballing tracing method proposed by Wohlin (2014). This study uses ‚Äúback¬≠
ward snowballing‚ÄĚ which takes relevant articles and searches their reference lists for other 
relevant articles. The first stage of backward snowballing is determining the key articles as 
the starting set of articles. Fifteen key articles were obtained in the finding literature pro¬≠
cess. Secondly, the list of references from the key articles was determined by checking two 
things: that the title must describe the topic being studied, and that the author has published 
other relevant papers in the field of HLSCM. Third, the new papers are either excluded or 
included to create the final set of articles.
In the snowballing process, the filtering criteria are applied sequentially. Initially, key 
articles from the ‚Äúfinding literature process‚ÄĚ are the starting set. When examining the refer¬≠
ences of these key articles, new papers are included only if their title clearly describes the 
study‚Äôs topic (humanitarian logistics, humanitarian supply chain, risk, or delays) and if the 
authors have a track record of publishing relevant work in the HLSCM field. This iterative 
process continues, adding new articles that meet these two core criteria until no further 
relevant papers are found. Finally, all selected articles undergo individual review to specifi¬≠
cally identify risk agents that cause delays in logistics distribution within HLSCM.
The fourth step is to iterate until no new articles are found by repeating the backward 
snowballing procedure or finishing the process depending on the result. At this step, 67 arti¬≠
cles matched the criteria, so the total number of articles was 82. The final articles from the 
hybrid literature search were then reviewed individually to obtain risk agents that roughly 
affect the delay in logistics distribution in HLSCM.
The results of this risk agents‚Äô identification do not specifically lead to food logistics dis¬≠
tribution delays in flooding and earthquakes HLSCM. According to Beaudrie et al. (2016), 
uncertainty in data can be estimated using expert judgment in the presence of adequate 
empirical data and careful elicitation. Therefore, the risk agents obtained need to go through 
an expert judgment process as justification to determine which risk agents affect food logis¬≠
tics distribution delays in flooding and earthquakes in HLSCM.

person depends on the needs of the study.

4.1‚Äā Literature review based on the flow of supplies in HLSCM
According to Balcik et al. (2010), the typical flow of supplies in a relief chain consists of 
three main stages: supply acquisition and procurement, pre-positioning and warehousing, 
and transportation. This study adapts their flow of supplies and finds that the flow of sup¬≠
plies in the HLSCM consists of three stages: supplying, warehousing, and delivering as 
shown in Fig.¬†3.
Figure¬†3 illustrates the procurement, storage, and distribution process of goods in HLSCM 
operations. At the procurement stage, various relief goods are obtained from various sources 
such as suppliers and local communities. Furthermore, the collected goods are stored in a 
central warehouse known as the warehousing stage. The central warehouse acts as a center 
for inventory management and preparation of goods before being distributed to victims. At 
the delivering stage, relief goods are then distributed to local distribution centers. These 
centers must be strategically located, namely those close to the disaster-affected area. The 
goal is to facilitate faster and more efficient distribution of aid. Finally, at the delivering 
stage, relief goods are distributed from local distribution centers to demand points.
These three stages have the potential to face various kinds of risks which impact delays 
in delivering relief items to locations affected by the disaster. Therefore, an extensive review 
of 82 articles was conducted to obtain the list of risk agents. In the following sub-chapter, 
the review articles are explained based on the three stages of HLSCM.
4.1.1‚Äā Supplying
Logistics operations in disaster situations have unique characteristics. At the procurement 
stage, the logistics chain must provide goods and equipment needed in disaster situations. At 
this stage, Humanitarian organizations are tasked with obtaining relief goods from local or 
global suppliers using various procurement techniques such as donations, direct purchases, 
or tenders (Balcik et al., 2010; Nikbakhsh & Farahani, 2011).

The main characteristics of humanitarian logistics operations are the uncertainty of 
demand, delivery with a minimum response time, and the ability to provide different items 
(Beamon & Balcik, 2008). Researchers pointed out that uncertain demand is one of the 
frequent issues in the logistics process, primarily the ‚Äúsupplying‚ÄĚ stage. This issue makes 
it difficult for stakeholders to decide what will be stocked and how many relief items are 
required.
Uncertainty in disasters requires HLSCM preparedness in the procurement of relief 
goods (Beamon & Balcik, 2008). Therefore, the humanitarian supply chain must consider:

Li et al. (2018) and Rawls and Turnquist (2011) also suggested to carefully arrange the pre-

4.1.2‚Äā Warehousing
Once procurement of goods in the supply stage is complete, humanitarian organizations 
are responsible for placing their goods and warehouse equipment in appropriate locations 
considering the location of disaster-prone areas. For this purpose, previous researchers have 
extensively investigated the warehousing stage. A major concern at this stage is ‚Äúwhere to 
store‚ÄĚ, or the placement of relief supplies in warehouses in specific locations. Beneficia¬≠
ries are supplied from pre-positioned inventories in the early days after a disaster as part 
of logistics activities. Humanitarian groups must consider the number, type, location, and 
warehouses needed to store supplies (Ňěahin et al., 2014).
According to Balcik et al. (2010), initial placement/warehousing often faces difficulties 
such as the high cost of building storage warehouses, operating warehouses, and potential 
damage to warehouses and goods. Researchers have suggested several strategies to address 
their concerns about these issues. For example, to reduce the severity and mortality of disas¬≠
ters, Patrisina et al. (2018) developed a two-stage mixed integer programming model for 
disaster relief operations used in a three-tier relief distribution network consisting of perma¬≠
nent warehouses, regional distribution centers, and refugee camps.
On the other hand, Ňěahin et al. (2014) provided new insights by using containers as stor¬≠
age facilities. Their study highlighted the benefits of using containers: containers can be 
transported from unaffected areas to the disaster zone immediately after a disaster occurs. 
Containers can be stacked one on top of the other, taking up less space than in a warehouse

Duran et al. (2011) discussed several matters related to the Pre-Positioning of Emergency

Nagurney and Qiang (2020) developed multiproduct supply chain network models for

under multiple disaster scenarios and the uncertainties associated with the demand for disas¬≠

D√∂yen et al. (2012) pointed out several problems in the logistics of humanitarian assis¬≠

Caunhye et al. (2012) conducted a literature review that used optimization models to

and the number of emergency relief items that must be stored. They developed a scenario-

design evaluation.
Ratick et al. (2008) explained the importance of backup and emergency storage facilities 
to reduce the vulnerability of disaster impacts. Backup facilities are also needed to increase 
the supply chain‚Äôs resilience and other logistics functions. The authors used set cover loca¬≠

4.1.3‚Äā Delivering
In the delivery phase, relief goods are transported from the central warehouse to local dis¬≠
tribution centers and finally to the point of demand (beneficiaries). Huang et al. (2015) 
highlighted the frequent occurrence of large-scale natural disasters, which result in many 
casualties and losses. The critical question in this situation is how to react quickly and plan 
a response that will reduce the impact of these disasters. According to this study, many 
logistics distribution problems can arise especially in the delivery phase.
The most challenging part of delivery is transportation which is hampered by various 
problems. These include weather, environmental issues, damaged or inadequate transpor¬≠
tation equipment, and local security. These problems limit the vehicles that can be used 
and result in delays (Balcik & Beamon, 2008; Suzuki, 2020; Ye et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 
2020). With regard to post-disaster weather and environmental issues, L‚ÄôHermitte et al. 
(2014) explained that the impact of the physical environment greatly influenced logistics 
operations. Lousy weather conditions caused delays in the loading and unloading process, 
hindered the movement of trucks, and prevented them from using certain airstrips. The road 
conditions also resulted in operational delays on logistics routes. Some roads are muddy and 
flooded, so they cannot be passed, or vehicles must move slowly.
Furthermore, the relief aid delivery process is also affected by the availability of supplies 
and transportation capacity (Garrido et al., 2015). L‚ÄôHermitte et al. (2014) mentioned that 
the lack of workers, insufficient land, air, and sea transportation capacity, and exacerbated 
by competition between humanitarian and commercial cargo have affected delays in aid 
delivery.
Post-disaster conditions showed that certain areas were isolated, making it difficult for 
victims to obtain relief items. The situation gets worse when a disaster occurs in a remote 
area. Therefore, it is essential to repair damaged roads to access isolated areas quickly. For 
this issue, Shin et al. (2019) proposed a mathematical model based on Mixed Integer Lin¬≠

Unmanned Air Vehicle (UAV) collaborative delivery system to be implemented in areas 
that are difficult to reach. On the other hand, Briskorn et al. (2020) mentioned that blocked 
roads by debris caused some locations to be inaccessible so that the delivery of goods was 
hampered.

shipping routes to maintain the quality of the goods to be delivered. Likewise, Gentili et al.

After a disaster, the condition of roads can be badly damaged, especially by floods and hur¬≠

5‚Äā Conclusion
This study has identified risks that cause delays in aid distribution during HLSCM. The 
results showed that there were 17 risks that were significantly related to delays in food logis¬≠
tics. The first round of expert assessment resulted in 17 RAs verified by experts, namely: 
(1) Amount of Stock; (2) Transportation Capacity; (3) Warehouse Capacity; (4) Commu¬≠
nication Constraints; (5) Amount of Demand; (6) Amount of Transportation; (7) Number 
of Warehouses; (8) Number of Workers; (9) Probability of Safety Passed; (10) Quality of 
Goods; (11) Road Conditions; (12) Distribution Sequence; (13) Size and Weight of Goods; 
(14) Length of Route; (15) Mode of Transportation; (16) Warehouse Facilities; (17) Weather 
Conditions. Meanwhile, in the second stage, expert assessments were analyzed using a 
mathematical framework to determine the extent to which each RA contributed to delays in 
food logistics distribution. Questionnaires were distributed to experts, and the results were 
compiled to determine consensus. This investigation uses mathematical aggregation as an 
analytical procedure to calculate the average value. The results show that all the proposed 
risk agents contribute to the delay in food logistics distribution. The highest average values ‚Äč
are shown by ‚Äúinventory quantity‚ÄĚ, ‚Äúroad conditions‚ÄĚ, and ‚Äúcommunication problems‚ÄĚ.
These 17 risks can be considered by stakeholders and actors involved in HLSCM to build 
an efficient logistics delivery system. The main expectation is that the delay in food logistics 
delivery can be minimized and appropriate and timely assistance can be provided to victims. 
The 17 risk agents are an epistemological contribution to the field of HLSCM. Furthermore, 
this study has filled the gap in previous studies that failed to identify risk agents in HLSCM, 
especially in the food logistics sector.
Meanwhile, methodologically, the combination of hybrid literature search strategies and 
expert judgment is an important contribution to future research that examines issues similar 
to this study. The proposed new methodology contains systematic stages to facilitate future 
researchers to find literature on a particular topic. However, this study only focuses on the 
stage of identifying risks that cause delays in food logistics delivery. Therefore, for further 
research, this study can be developed with the most significant risk ranking in food logistics 
delivery delays by suggesting risk mitigation to minimize these risks.
