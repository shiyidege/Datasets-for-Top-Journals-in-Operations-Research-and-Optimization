Abstract
In this paper, the team wage function of the football teams in the 5 major European foot-
ball leagues is estimated using information about their sports results and controlling for the
national league they play, the international competitions (i.e., Champions League and Europe
League) they participate each year and the fresh promotions. The dataset used covers seasons
2018/19 to 2024/25. The methodology used corresponds to stochastic semi-parametric envel-
opment of data with contextual variables (StoNEZD), which allows estimating the outputs
shadow prices for each team and the marginal effects of the contextual variables. The inefﬁ-
ciency score of each team, as well as their scale efﬁciency and returns to scale in each season,
have also been estimated. The results show signiﬁcant wage differences between the different
national leagues as well as higher wages for teams that participate in the international compe-
titions (higher in the case of the Champions League than in the Europa League). Also, it was
found that the wages of freshly promoted teams do not catch up immediately with respect
to those of the more established clubs. We also tested the effect of the COVID-19 pandemic
(seasons 2019/20 and 2020/21) but it was not found signiﬁcant pandemic. The estimated
average wage inefﬁciency is rather low for all 5 leagues, indicating that a substantial part of
the wage expenses is not explained by the observed ex-post performance. Scale efﬁciencies
are higher and have less dispersion, with a majority of teams (between 62 and 75%, depend-
ing on the league) exhibiting increasing returns to scale and another fraction (between 25
and 35%, depending on the league) exhibiting decreasing returns to scale. Interestingly, a
wage ﬂoor seems to exist so that the wages become insensitive to sports results below certain
thresholds.

1 Introduction
The team wages of some European football clubs have increased signiﬁcantly, well above
the increase of the average salary, in the last decades. The question that many football fans,
and society in general, may ask themselves is whether these high wages are justiﬁed from a
sportingperformanceviewpoint.Sameaswithotherﬁrmsandorganizations,thetechnicaland
economic efﬁciency of sport teams in general and football teams in particular can be assessed.
The speciﬁc difference in this industry is the existence of what can be called a virtuous circle
between sporting results and economic gains in which the ﬁrm’s ﬁnancial resources can be
used to pay high wages, which allows acquiring to playing talent, which brings about sporting
success, which produces revenues (and hopefully also proﬁts). Unfortunately, this can also
lead to a competitive imbalance in which a few rich clubs can in this way gain a sustained
competitive advantage over more modest teams. Part of the literature on team sports has
been devoted to conﬁrm, explain and estimate the magnitude of these relationships. Thus, for
example, there is extensive empirical evidence on the correlation between wage expenditure
and sporting success (e.g., Aguiar-Noury & Garcia-del-Barrio, 2022; Jewell, 2017; Madsen
et al., 2018). Although it is true that team success depends on more than just team salaries
and there are many studies that include other variables (i.e., playing, managerial and other
inputs), different researchers have argued that, as Garcia-del-Barrio & Szymanski (2009) put
it, “wage spending is far and away the single most important factor in determining league
performance”. The relationship may well be bidirectional. Thus, there are numerous studies,
particularly at the individual player level, that link pay to performance, among other variables
(Frick, 2011; Ribeiro et al., 2022; Thrane, 2019).
Actually, wage expenditure not only affects sporting success but also total revenues (e.g.,
Gasparetto & Barajas, 2022), which is not surprising, as a positive relationship between
sporting success and revenues has also been observed (e.g., Garcia-del-Barrio & Rossi, 2020;
Zambom-Ferraresi et al., 2019). The link between wage expenditures and proﬁts is another
matter. Thus, while some researchers have found a positive relationship between wage expen-
diture and proﬁtability (e.g., Rohde & Breuer, 2018), other researchers (e.g., Dimitropoulos
& Limperopoulos, 2014) have found that wage expenditures have a negative impact on ﬁnan-
cial performance. The reason for these discrepancies may lie in the multiobjective character
of the clubs’ utility maximization. Thus, although overall proﬁt and league position are pos-
itively related, a negative trade-off between proﬁt and position may be eventually reached,
where increasing wage spending does not increase proﬁts. In this regard, several authors have
argued that league success is a diminishing function of wage expenditure and that decreasing
returns to scale can be encountered (e.g., Carmichael et al., 2011). This situation is not uni-
versal, though, as other researchers have found increasing returns to scale in the production
function (e.g., Ascari & Gagnepain, 2007; Barros & Leach, 2006).
A related issue is the possibility of overinvesting in playing talent. A military analogy can
be used to express it, as many clubs enter an arms race in which they increase their wage
expenditure to acquire more playing talent in order to outgun their opponents (see, e.g., Ascari
& Gagnepain, 2007, Barajas & Rodriguez 2010, Carmichael et al., 2011, Dimitropoulos &
Limperopoulos, 2014, Rohde & Breuer, 2018, Gasparetto & Barajas, 2022). Although this
high-expenditure strategy can be based on the efﬁcient wage theory (e.g., DeBrock et al.,
2004), it is not clear whether it is ultimately successful. Thus, apart from the diminishing
returns mentioned above, since sporting success is more dependent on relative wage expen-
ditures than on their absolute levels (e.g., Garcia-del-Barrio & Rossi, 2020), this arms race
may easily lead to ﬁnancial distress for the clubs with no signiﬁcant gain in their relative

positions. To prevent this type of problems, as well as to limit competitive imbalance, Euro-
pean football regulators have established Financial Fair Play (FFP) breakeven rules, that raise
the relative importance of ﬁnancial goals with respect to that of sporting goals (Gallagher &
Quinn, 2020).
In light of the above, the aim of this paper is to estimate the team wage function of the
football teams in the 5 major European football leagues. To that end, we will use wages and
sports results of seasons 2018/19 to 2024/25. As contextual variables, we will consider the
national league the team plays, any international competition they may participate in (i.e.,
Champions League and Europe League) and fresh promotions. We propose using a stochastic
semi-parametric envelopment of data with contextual variables (StoNEZD), which allows
estimating the outputs shadow prices for each team and the marginal effects of the contextual
variables. The inefﬁciency score of each team, as well as their scale efﬁciency and returns to
scale in each season, have also been estimated. The main contributions of the paper are in the
application of the methodology to this dataset and in the interesting results obtained. Thus,
we have found a relatively low wage efﬁciency, which indicates that a signiﬁcant fraction
of the wages is not directly related to sporting performance but to managerial and other
factors (superstar effects, legacy effects, deterrence effects, etc.). Whatever the reasons, this
technical inefﬁciency translates into substantial excess wages or rent dissipation that, in any
case, depends on the contextual variables. Decreasing returns to scale seem to prevail. Also, it
has been found that there is a wage ﬂoor that protects wages from decreasing below a certain
threshold in case of a poor sporting performance. All these results provide useful insights
and a comprehensive picture of the win-wages relationship in the ﬁrst division of European
football leagues in recent times.
The structure of the paper is the following. In Sect. 2 the relevant literature is reviewed.
Section 3 introduces the methodology used, formulating and explaining the corresponding
mathematical models. Section 4 presents and discusses the results obtained. Finally, Sect. 5
summarizes and concludes.
2 Literature review
Most of the studies that model the relationship between sporting success and team wages are
based on Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression (e.g Dimitropoulos & Limperopoulos,
2014; Kringstad & Olsen, 2016; Bykova & Coates, 2020; Garcia-del-Barrio & Agnese, 2023;
Gasparetto & Barajas, 2022) or Stochastic Frontier Analysis (SFA) (e.g., Boto-García et al.,
2023). Although most of these approaches consider Win percentage, Points percentage (w.r.t.
maximum number of points), Relative points share (i.e., Points hare within the league), odds
log position within the league or some other measure of sport success as independent variable
and wage expenditure (or total wages or ex-ante team value) among the independent variables,
we are more interested in those approaches that consider wage expenditure as the dependent
variable and sporting results among the independent variables. We will speciﬁcally focus on
the wage function and not on SFA cost function approaches (e.g., Barros & Leach, 2006) or
Stochastic Distance Function approaches (e.g., Jewell, 2017) that do not explicitly estimate
wage expenditure. An exception to the above rule is Gallagher and Quinn (2020), which we
remark because, although they estimate Costs share (based on Points share, Wages share and
Talent share), they use a semi-parametric approach analogous to the one used in this paper.
Also relevant to this research is the standardized win-cost ratio analysis of the premier League
carried out in Gerrard (2010), which concluded that ﬁnancial resources are the main driver

of sporting performance. We can also relate the aim of this paper to some data envelopment
analysis (DEA) approaches that consider team wage expenditure as single input and use an
input orientation. In particular, we have the single-input (total wages), single-output (odds
of league rank achieved in the national league) DEA approach of Ribeiro & Lima (2012),
the shared-input network DEA approach proposed in Villa & Lozano (2024) that links wage
expenditure with market value using sport results (measured by number of goals and wins
in the national and in the international competitions) as intermediate variables, and the non-
convex metafrontier DEA approach in Villa & Lozano (2025), which assesses the wage
efﬁciency of Europe’s top football leagues using wins and goals in national and international
competitions and goals against in both national and international competitions (modelled as
undesirable outputs).
Thus, apart from these DEA approaches, other relevant studies for the research carried
out are the SFA approach of Ascari & Gagnepain (2007) and the OLS regression approach
of Carmichael et al. (2011). The former estimates, for the Spanish 1st and 2nd Divisions,
the wage frontier as a function of the predicted number of points, the divisional status of the
team, the number of years in that division and the season. The predicted number of points is
estimated as a function of the demand, which in turn is considered a function of the season,
the strategic scheme (3–3 or 4–2), the number of goals scored during the season, the share of
foreign players, the share of foreign players from outside Europe among the foreign players,
the share of players in the national squad, the market size and a manager change indicator.
They found signiﬁcant and growing inefﬁciency (called rent dissipation in the paper) in the
Spanish football industry (of around 400 Me for the 2002/2003 season).
In Carmichael et al. (2011), and using data on the English Premier League, apart from a
sports production and a revenue function, a hedonic wage function was estimated using as
variables various attacking, constructive and defensive playing inputs (such as Ratio of the
sum of goals scored, assists and shots on target to total shots, Total shots on target, Total
crosses and passes completed, Total dribbles and runs completed, Total blocks, clearances and
interceptions, Ratio of goals conceded to goalkeeper saves, etc.), managerial inputs (such as
Number of years manager with club at start of season) and other inputs (e.g., Ratio of points
won at home to points won away, International competitions participation). Among other
things, they found that a signiﬁcant proportion of the wage bill of the most successful clubs
being a reward for player performances in international competitions.
From the above, it can be concluded that the team wage function has been mostly esti-
mated using either stochastic parametric approaches like Ascari & Gagnepain (2007) and
Carmichael et al. (2011) or deterministic nonparametric approaches like Villa & Lozano
(2024, 2025). This paper tries to ﬁll this gap using a semi-parametric approach to estimate
the team wage function of the 5 top European leagues so that, on the one hand, random
noise (in addition to inefﬁciency) is taken into account and, on the other, no assumptions on
the functional speciﬁcation of the relationship (other than convexity and monotonicity) are
assumed. Moreover, the proposed approach provides estimations of the win and tie shadow
prices and of the effects of the contextual variables. Also, inefﬁciency score, scale efﬁciency,
scale elasticity and returns to scale for the different teams are computed.
3 Methodology
The proposed approach is based on the semi-parametric methodology known as stochas-
tic semi-parametric envelopment of data with contextual variables (StoNEZD) (Johnson &

Kuosmanen, 2011), which is the contextual variables variant of the stochastic non-smooth
envelopment of data (StoNED) method (Kuosmanen & Kortelainen, 2012), which, in turn,
is based on convex nonparametric least squares (CNLS) regression (Kuosmanen & Johnson,
2010). Unlike SFA, CNLS allows estimating the shape of the frontier without any assump-
tions about its functional form and can thus be viewed as the nonparametric counterpart of
SFA. Conversely, CNLS can be viewed as the stochastic counterpart of nonparametric DEA,
which can be considered a signed-constrained version of CNLS (see Kuosmanen & John-
son, 2010). Although CNLS is fully nonparametric, in order to disentangle inefﬁciency from
noise StoNEZD may require to impose some distributional assumptions on the inefﬁciency
and noise terms. The estimation of the effects of contextual variables (commonly assigned
symbol z, hence the name StoNEZD) also corresponds to the parametric part of the method
although this is done jointly with the estimation of the frontier, i.e. in one stage (Johnson &
Kuosmanen, 2012). As regards StoNEZD applications, these include, apart from Gallagher &
Quinn (2020) mentioned above, electricity distribution companies (Kuosmanen, 2012; Saas-
tamoinen & Kuosmanen, 2016), national tax administrations (Nguyen et al., 2020), water
and sewerage companies (Molinos-Senante & Maziotis, 2021), wastewater treatment plants
(Molinos-Senante & Maziotis, 2022), water utilities (Maziotis et al., 2023), etc.

4 Results and discussion
The proposed approach has been applied to a panel data corresponding to 7 seasons, namely
2018/19 to 2024/25. The observations correspond to the football clubs that play in the 5
top European Leagues, namely Italy’s Serie A, Spain’s La Liga, England’s Premier League,
France’s Ligue 1 and Germany’s Bundesliga. All teams that played in these leagues during
these seasons have been considered, thus taking into account promotions and relegations.
The total number of observations is 682. Of these, 140 observations correspond to the Italian,
Spanish and English leagues, each derived from seven seasons with 20 teams per season. The
French league accounts for 136 observations, corresponding to 5 seasons with 20 teams and
the last two seasons with 18 teams, while the German league contributes 126 observations,
corresponding to seven seasons with 18 teams per season. Due to promotions and relegations,
the number of distinct teams differs across leagues: 31 teams appear in the Italian league over
the sample period, compared to 27 in the Spanish league, 29 in the Premier league, 29 in
the French league, and 28 in the Bundesliga. The whole dataset, including the labels of the
different teams, is shown in the supplementary material ﬁle. The observations include the
team wage (obtained from www.capology.com and appropriately deﬂated), the number of
matches won and tied in the national league in each season and the contextual variables
indicating the corresponding national league of the team and whether they compete in the
Champions or in the Europa League in that season. Table 1 presents a summary statistics
of the dataset used. It should be noted that goals conceded (multiplied by −1) was initially
also considered as an output variable; however, since its estimated marginal cost was equal
to zero across all observations, it was removed from the model analysis.
Figure 1 shows the evolution of the average team salaries (in constant 2024 Euros) for each
of these 5 leagues. While the average salaries of French and German teams have increased
slightly in this period those of the Spanish and Italian teams have decreased signiﬁcantly and
those of the Premier league have ﬂuctuated around a level signiﬁcantly higher than those of
the other leagues. In absolute terms, the average salaries of the German and Spanish teams
have practically converged. Actually, the divergence between all leagues except the Premier
has decreased over the period.

5 Conclusions
This paper proposes to use the semi-parametric StoNEZD methodology to estimate the team
wage frontier of European football. To that end, a panel dataset of the 5 top European leagues
in the last seven seasons has been used. The team wages have been estimated using the
number of wins and ties as well as the number of goals scored as independent variables
and the national league, international competitions participation and fresh promotion as
contextual variables. The marginal costs of the output variables and marginal effects of the
contextual variables have been estimated using CNLS regression. Apart from estimating the
technical and scale efﬁciency of each observation (and its corresponding returns to scale), the
proposed approach allows estimating the efﬁcient team wage for any given vector of outputs
and contextual variables. From a methodological point of view, it is important to emphasize
that the average wage results presented have been computed in a semi-parametric way.
The results of the analysis provide the distribution of the output shadow price vectors for
the different teams. In the case of the number of wins, these marginal costs are zero up to
a threshold of 14–15 wins, above which it increases to 3.5–4 Me/win. In the case of the
number of ties, the marginal costs are generally low for the whole range of values of that
output variable. The same happens, with just a few exceptions, with the marginal cost of
goals scored. As regards the estimated marginal effects, they indicate that, compared with
the Italian league, which is used as reference, the wages in the French league are around 40%
lower, those of the German are around 15% lower, those of the and Spanish are similar and
those English league are around 85% higher, respectively. The estimated marginal costs of
the CL and EL contextual variables indicate that the wages of the teams that participate in
these international competitions are, in average, 2.1 and 1.5 times, respectively, those of the
teams that do not qualify for these competitions. As regards fresh promotions, the wages of
those teams are around 30% lower than the average, indicating that wage bill in those teams
comes from a lower level and has inertia.
An important ﬁnding of the study is the existence of a wage ﬂoor, indicating a signiﬁcant
insensitivity of wages to marginal sports performance at the lower end of the distribution
(below 14 wins, 16 ties and 35 goals scored). From an economic perspective, this ﬂoor
123

2676
Annals of Operations Research (2026) 359:2655–2679
can be interpreted as the minimum cost of competitive entry in elite professional football.
Regardless of ﬁnal sporting success, clubs must meet a baseline quality threshold in their
squadstoparticipateinhigh-levelcompetition,incurringﬁxedlaborcoststhatareindependent
of performance. This reﬂects a structural wage rigidity in the labor market for professional
athletes. Thus, players competing in elite leagues possess a ‘common value’ and status
that prevents salaries from falling below a certain market equilibrium, even when sporting
outcomes are poor. This is exacerbated by the guaranteed revenue streams (primarily from
broadcasting rights) inherent in European football, which provide clubs with a ﬁnancial safety
net that sustains this minimum expenditure level, effectively decoupling wages from wins
until a critical competitive threshold is surpassed.
As regards the team wages inefﬁciency, the expected efﬁciency is rather low (0.457), with
a distribution that differs for each league. Less dispersion seems to exist as regards scale
efﬁciency, with just a few teams operating in the most productive scale size region of the
wage frontier and most of them exhibiting IRS. Finally, as regards the estimated excess wages
they average a value between 31 and 71 Me per team, depending on the league. Teams that
participate in CL or EL, though, can have more excess salaries than that.
There are several limitations of this study. One of them is the reliability of the wages data.
Given the private nature of this information there are often no ofﬁcial ﬁgures, forcing these
specialized websites to resort to surveys of player agents, from which the corresponding
ﬁgures are estimated. The uncertainty in this important variable implies that the quantitative
results, particularly those regarding excess salaries, should be read as indicating orders of
magnitude more than as exact ﬁgures. Thus, for example, a 10–15% margin of error on the
estimated wage bills could signiﬁcantly alter the magnitude of the calculated rent dissipation.
Another consideration is that, although we have estimated wages as a function of ex-post
performance, the wage-results relationship is bidirectional and can be seen as sporting results
following wage expenditures. This means that what our approach labels as technical inefﬁ-
ciency and excess salaries could also be interpreted as an investment for future success, or
a premium for brand recognition, or simply reﬂect unmodeled endogeneity. We thank the
reviewers for pointing out these issues and limitations.
As regards possible continuations of this research, it would be interesting a comparison
with the wage function of football teams in other regions (e.g., North America, Latin America,
Asia). Also, the same methodology can be applied to estimate similar wage frontiers for other
sports. Another interesting topic for further research, which was suggested by the reviewers, is
the implications of (and the rationale for) the observed wage ﬂoor from an economic theory
perspective. Finally, the use of Convex Quantile Regression (Dai et al., 2023), Efﬁciency
Analysis Trees (Esteve et al., 2020), Support Vector Frontiers (Valero-Carreras et al., 2021)
and similar emerging frontier analysis methods can also be tried.
