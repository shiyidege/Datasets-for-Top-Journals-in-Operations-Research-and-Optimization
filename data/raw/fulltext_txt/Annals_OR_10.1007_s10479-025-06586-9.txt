Abstract
This work addresses the problem of maintaining privacy in range counting queries, with
a focus on rolling-window queries over sensitive data. By employing differential privacy
(DP), we can protect sensitive data used in these queries, as demonstrated in applications
like COVID-19 tracking. Our research, however, identiﬁes signiﬁcant limitations in existing
DP methods for small to medium-sized windows or ranges, which are commonly found
in practical applications. We introduce a novel combinatorial interval covering approach,
providing a detailed analysis of privacy-utility trade-offs within the DP framework. Our
characterization of interval covering families’ thickness provides new insights into achieving
ε-DP with minimal error. Additionally, we propose a search-based method for selecting
optimal families for differentially private range counting queries, speciﬁcally for small to
medium windows, which outperforms current solutions in relevant scenarios. This work
contributes to both the theoretical foundation and practical application of differential privacy
in data analytics, ensuring robust privacy protection without compromising query accuracy.
Keywords Range counting · Differential privacy · Rolling window · Interval coverings

ε-Differential privacy (DP) is a rigorous privacy guarantee achieved by adding random
noise to the true answers (e.g., counts), where the parameter ε > 0 determines the level of
privacy and the amount of noise needed to guarantee ε-DP. This privacy guarantee ensures
that inferences about any single data point, such as information about an individual, are
statistically insigniﬁcant. However, this comes at the cost of utility: higher privacy requires
more noise, leading to greater error.
Therefore, the crucial questions in the context of the DP framework are: How much error is
necessary to achieve ε-DP for a given ε? And what computational methods can achieve ε-DP
with minimal error? This article examines these questions in the context of rolling-window
counting queries over small to medium-sized windows (i.e., up to 6 - 7 order of magnitude).
This setting is important because asymptotically better solutions perform poorly in this range
(see Fig. 1 and Sect.3.1). In summary, our contributions are:
• We study privacy-preserving rolling-window counting queries using the differential pri-
vacy framework and highlight the shortcomings of existing approaches for small to
medium window sizes. In this range, asymptotically inferior solutions can outperform
the supposedly better ones (see Fig. 1) for hidden constants can dominate the error in
small to medium size ranges.
• We introduce a new combinatorial approach based on Z-coverings using families of
intervals to analyze DP range counting problems and characterize the privacy-utility
trade-off through the properties (e.g., thickness) of the covering families.
• We provide lower and upper bounds on the minimum thickness and exact minimum
thickness for speciﬁc families of intervals, characterizing the thickness (i.e., the number
of overlapping intervals) for various important feasible covering families.
• We apply our approach to the case of DP pandemic tracking with special constraints
(discussed in Sect.3.2) that have not been previously studied.
• We propose a search-based method to ﬁnd the best families for computing differentially
private range counting queries for a given window size. By focusing only on a set of
special families introduced in this work, we signiﬁcantly reduce the search space. Our
evaluation shows that the best family identiﬁed by the search has the least error.

2.1 Differential privacy
Differential privacy (DP) is a rigorous mathematical privacy guarantee for data analysis and
statistical algorithms Dwork (2006); Dwork et al. (2006b). DP quantiﬁes the privacy-risk of
a data analysis task, and requires a provable guarantee that the privacy-risk, incurred due
to the use of an individual data point, is within the speciﬁed bounds. We say a randomized
algorithm M is ε-differentially private if for any input D, M(D) remains probabilistically
“almost the same" when a data point is removed from D (which gives D−) or a data point is
added to D (giving D+), namely, for the privacy parameter, ε ∈R+
e−ε ≤Pr[M(D−) ∈S]
Pr[M(D) ∈S] ≤eε and e−ε ≤Pr[M(D+) ∈S]
Pr[M(D) ∈S] ≤eε
for every S ⊆Support(M). The above DP constraints result in the following effect: the
differences in the output of a DP algorithm due to a single data point are undetectable due
to uncertainty created by the randomization. The level of randomization is controlled by the
privacy parameter ε: the smaller its value, the higher the privacy, which requires higher level
of randomization. However, higher level randomization decreases the utility, resulting in a
higher error in the answers provided by the DP algorithm.
Balancing this privacy-utility trade-off, i.e., what is the best achievable utility for a
given value of ε is central to much of the work in differential privacy.
Differential privacy also has various useful properties of composition, and post-processing
that make differentially private algorithms amenable to analysis and makes DP so attractive.
The DP guarantee does not change under post-processing, i.e., further processing the output
of an ε-DP algorithm does not violate the DP guarantee. The following are the DP properties
that we rely on in this work. DP is serially composable with only linear loss in terms of ε, that
is, if the data is being used in τ-many independent α-DP computations, then the overall DP
guarantee is τα. Furthermore, if the input data is partitioned (i.e., into disjoint sets) and each
partition is processed by a different (or independent) α-DP algorithm, then the overall privacy
remains α-DP. Let us next discuss one of the widely used algorithms to achieve differential
privacy.
˛-DP laplace mechanism (M˛)
Achieving the DP guarantee for range counting queries (or counting queries) is simple: add
noise to the actual count, that is, the DP answer = actual count + noise, where noise is sampled
from a well-calibrated distribution. Furthermore, higher amount of noise, i.e., higher variance
of the noise-distribution, provides higher privacy, which is required for smaller values of ε.
Laplace mechanism, which uses noise sampled from Laplace distribution — denoted here
as Lap(1/ε) — of mean zero and variance = 2/ε2, is instrumental in developing ε-DP
algorithms for range counting queries (Deng et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2020; Asif et al.,
2022; Fan and Xiong, 2013; Chan et al., 2011). One reason for its use is its optimality for
computing ε-DP counting queries for any given ε > 0, that is, it adds the minimum amount
noise needed to achieve ε-DP guarantee. Thus, we use Laplace mechanism to compute a
range counting query f : I(Z) →N with ε-DP guarantee as follows:
M(I) = f (I) + E,

2.2 Differentially private range counting queries (related work)
Differentially private range counting queries (RCQs) have been studied in the context of
rolling-window queries for outbreak detection (Fan and Xiong, 2013; Asif et al., 2022),
for web and social media analytics, e.g., ﬁnding popular products or monitoring browsing
behavior (Chan et al., 2011; Fan et al., 2014), for IoT (Internet-of-things) data trading (Cai
et al., 2022), for ﬁnding shortest paths on graphs (Deng et al., 2023) or sum of edge-weights
for trafﬁc analytics, ride-sharing, and spatial sensing (Ghosh et al., 2020). All these works
use Laplace mechanism as the building block.
Dwork et al. (2010a) gave an algorithm for RCQs with O(log(d)3/ε2) error bound and a
lower bound of O(log(d)2/ε2) for ε-differential privacy, where d is the size of the window or
range. Many of the DP mechanisms for RCQs that use a binary tree based partitioning of the
range have the same O(log(d)3/ε2) bound — we refer to this as the log3-bound. Note that
most work model error as the standard deviation (σ), but we use variance (i.e., σ 2) because
it simpliﬁes our exposition. Thus, for ease of comparison all the error bounds discussed here
are given in variance. Bun et al. (2015) showed that under a weaker version of differential
privacy, known as (ε, δ)-DP that allows a DP algorithm to fail to satisfy ε-DP constraints
with probability at max δ, the bound can be improved to

2.3 Notation and definitions
Range Counting query and its parameters. We consider range counting queries as f :
I(Z) →N. For a ﬁxed predicate p over a data stream D = D1, D2, . . . , a range counting
query, f , provides the count (i.e., number) of data elements in D which satisfy p, and are
within range R. For example, in the context of pandemic tracking, D consists of reported
symptoms with location and time, R corresponds to spatial range (e.g., a county, or an
arbitrary axis-parallel rectangle on the map) and a temporal range (e.g., days 11 to 24), and
the predicate p is true for any data point with at least one COVID-19 related symptoms, falling
within R. In this work, our focus is on 1D range, modeled as integer intervals (i.e., a countable
enumeration of elements of the range), and thus, we consider that the predicate, data stream
and other parts of the query range (e.g., spatial range) have been ﬁxed arbitrarily. Therefore,
we only consider parameterization of f by I ∈I(Z), which greatly simpliﬁes the analysis.
Thus, in the example of COVID-19 tracking, we are to release f ([1, 14]), f ([2, 15]), . . . .

3 DP range counting via feasible Z-coverings: formulation and
motivation
Our aim is to guarantee ε-differential privacy for rolling-window counting queries with mini-
mum error for small-to-medium-sized windows, i.e., up to millions. We cast this problem as a
covering problem of d-sized intervals or d-windows (i.e., the rolling-window) on the integer
line. The collection of intervals from all the covers of d-windows deﬁne a family that can be
used to compute DP rolling-window counting queries using Laplace mechanism (Sect.2.1) as
the building block. Laplace mechanism is used for it is optimal for DP counting queries, i.e, it
gives the least error. We will consider special types of covers of d-windows (Sect.3.2), which
consist of disjoint intervals; this is because splitting d-windows into smaller sub-windows
and then applying Laplace mechanism over the counts of the sub-windows results in less
noise needed to achieve ε-DP. We will also link some fundamental characteristics of these
families with that of the error due to achieving ε-DP.

3.2 Motivation 2: a class of DP rolling-window counting queries (e.g., for pandemic
tracking) impose additional constraints on feasible covers
Differentially private rolling-window counting queries for pandemic or outbreak tracking not
only have the window-size in a small range (e.g., in weeks to months (Wang et al., 2022; He
et al., 2020), or up to hundreds in ﬁnancial applications (Maverick, 2019)) but also impose
constraints on the sizes of intervals in feasible covers (Asif, 2021; Asif et al., 2022) — we
refer to this as the covering intervals size (cize) constraint. Covering intervals size constraint
is due to the fact that pandemic tracking, in general, uses spatiotemporal range for range
counting queries while so far we have only considered the temporal range (explained below).
When answering spatial range counting queries with DP guarantee, it is better to use
hierarchical spatial-partitioning algorithms, e.g., early-pruned DP quad trees (Cormode et
al., 2012; Asif, 2021), than a ﬁxed-grid-based partitioning or even a data-driven partitioning;
this enables computing a very large number of spatial-range counting queries with a ﬁxed
privacy budget, i.e., value of ε, with lower error. In most typical settings, the input to a DP
spatial partitioning algorithm is a region and set of data points (e.g., geo-location-tagged
reported symptoms of COVID-19) from this region over a time period I, where we have
conceptualized I ∈I(Z) as an integer interval. The algorithm outputs a partitioning of the
region such that the partitions are ﬁne-grained in the sub-regions with more data points and
are coarse-grained, or none at all, for the sub-regions with a fewer data points.
We saw in Sect.3.1 that for our query f (for d = 14) it is better to use the family H2,
which achieves ε-DP with lowest error. Thus, using H2 will require each time period I to
be a unit interval, and the spatial partitioning algorithm will use data points reported on a
single day. In the case of a pandemic, for example, the level of spread varies – from none
to high – and hence, the number of data points can vary drastically across time in a region.
Therefore, the use of H2 results in higher error due to coarse-grained partitions when there
are not sufﬁciently many data points reported from one day (Asif, 2021).
Asif (2021) proposed a way to solve the aforementioned problem by imposing cize-
constraint that restricts the sizes of the intervals in feasible covers to be n, where n < d is
supposed to be the minimum value that is “sufﬁciently large,” which is determined by the
domain experts. They introduced a computational procedure that ﬁnds a sufﬁciently large

6 Conclusion
We explored the intersection of data privacy and range counting queries, focusing on rolling-
window counting queries. We used a novel combinatorial view to analyze DP range counting
problems, where we evaluate DP-induced privacy-utility trade-offs through the properties of
123

Annals of Operations Research
the covering families. We provided a detailed characterization of covering families’ thickness,
establishing lower and upper bounds and identifying exact minimum thickness for speciﬁc
families. Our approach was applied in the context of pandemic tracking, addressing unique
constraints and demonstrating its effectiveness. Additionally, we proposed a search-based
method to identify optimal families for differentially private range counting queries, which
signiﬁcantly reduces error in practice.
In summary, this work advances the theory and practice of differential privacy in range
counting queries. Our contributions pave the way for future research in balancing privacy
and utility in data analytics, safeguarding sensitive information while preserving the accuracy
and effectiveness of analytical queries.
6.1 Future directions and open questions
• While this work focused on feasible covers with pairwise disjoint intervals, feasible
covers can also be constructed using overlapping (i.e., non-disjoint) intervals. In this
approach, range counting can be performed as a linear combination of counts of cover-
ing intervals with both +1 and −1 coefﬁcients (as opposed to only +1 coefﬁcients).4
What improvements in error and thickness are possible by considering this more general
setting?
• Another way to construct feasible covers is by considering arbitrary disjoint sets instead
of intervals. For example, the union of {1, 3, 5} and {2, 4, 6} gives the cover of the interval
[1, 6]. We have found examples via computational search that reduce the minimum
thickness for families consisting of such covers (compared the feasible covering families
considered in this work). How can this setting be employed in general, and what are the
possible gains from using them?
• Can the lower and upper bounds for the general case (i.e., (ak1
1 , . . . , akm
m ) setting) and the
(ak1
1 , ak2
2 ) setting be improved if they are not tight?
• Since it is possible to construct feasible covering families, ˆH, from the union of d-periodic
extensions of a set of τ-many feasible covers, in what settings is τ = τ ∗? And if τ > τ ∗,
what is the gap?
• Feasible families constructed via systems of arithmetic interval sequences, such as the
Slim Family (Deﬁnition 4), can be reduced to systems of arithmetic sequences, which
have been studied for covering Z (Erdos, 1950; Erd˝os, 1962; Balister et al., 2020). We
plan to explore these connections.
