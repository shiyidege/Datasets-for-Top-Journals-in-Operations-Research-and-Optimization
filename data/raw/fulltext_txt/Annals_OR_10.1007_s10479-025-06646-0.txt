Abstract
In multiobjective optimization scenarios, the challenge lies in balancing several conﬂicting
objectives; classic optimization methods, which focus on a single measurable criterion, do
not adequately address this issue. The existing approaches have aimed to improve the efﬁ-
ciency of solving such problems, but ﬁnding an optimal solution across multiple objectives
remains complex. This paper proposes a new algorithm that ﬁrst optimizes each objective
function individually, using the resulting solutions as targets for further reﬁnement. Through
an ideal programming scheme, the algorithm minimizes deviations from these set goals. The
proposed algorithm is used to solve a case study. The results derived from testing the algo-
rithm demonstrate its superior performance relative to that of the other compared methods
across all the objectives.

Goal programming (GP) is another widely used method in multi-criteria decision-making.
It is an extension of linear programming that was designed to handle multiple objectives,
making it especially useful for complex decision-making scenarios. GP has been success-
fully applied to various ﬁelds, such as production planning, energy storage systems, and
project selection. Charnes and Cooper presented the ﬁrst mathematical deﬁnition of GP for
the purpose of solving multidimensional linear programming problems(Charnes & Cooper,
1977). Until the middle of the 1970s, GP applications were relatively rare in the informa-
tion technology (IT) literature. The impressive results produced by the practical applications
of GP and the technical improvements achieved for this method are due to the pioneering
research conducted by Lee (1972). Schniederjans (Burton et al., 1990), Romero (Romero,
1991), Dean (Burton et al., 1990), and (Jones & Tamiz, 2010) applied GP in activity-based
costing by integrating it with the analytic hierarchy process (AHP).

Many researchers have explored various methods for addressing the challenges of multiob-

objectives through precise mathematical modeling. Yu et al. (2025) proposed a new method

clustering and reward-penalty strategies to manage the dispersion of Pareto-optimal solutions.
Fan et al. (2025) suggested a multi-source clustering (MPC) method to improve the perfor-
mance of multi-objective evolutionary algorithms in dynamic environments (DMOEAs).

Gemeinhardt et al. (2025) applied a genetic algorithm to solve MOO problems, leveraging the

of natural selection. Hao et al. (2024) presented a multi-objective arithmetic optimization
(AOA) algorithm with stochastic search strategies to solve the combined economic-emission
distribution (CEED) problem in power generation. Each of these methods represents a unique
strategy for addressing the complexities inherent in multiobjective planning tasks, offering
different advantages depending on the speciﬁc problem and context.
The key contribution of this research is the introduction of GP to solve MOOPs. In con-
trast with single-objective programming, which is easier to execute and provides solutions
quickly, multiobjective problems (MOPs) often have conﬂicting goals. Finding solutions
that satisfy all the objectives can be very challenging. These studies demonstrate that GP and
similar optimization methods are powerful tools for solving complex decision-making prob-
lems. They provide efﬁcient solutions to real-world issues by balancing conﬂicting goals and
reducing the complexity of MOO. This research presents a practical solution for addressing
MOO problems via GP. The key idea is to convert multiple conﬂicting objective functions
into constraints, turning the MOP into a more straightforward, single-objective problem. This
allows us to identify the best possible solution more quickly. Our proposed approach is com-
pared with the methods developed by Ramos and Boix (2014) and interactive sequential GP
(ISGP) (Masud & Hwang, 1981). The results show that our model does not rely on subjective
decision-maker (DM) inputs and involves fewer calculations, making it more efﬁcient while
still delivering better results. The rest of this research is arranged as follows. Sect. 2 presents
the literature review and previous work. The essential background of MOO and GP is dis-
cussed in Sect. 3. Sect. 4 introduces the proposed approach in detail. A numerical example
and case study are presented to demonstrate the methodology in Sect. 5. The discussion is
presented in Sect. 6. Sect. 7 summarizes the paper at hand.

2 Literature
In the literature, multiobjective programming is often used to resolve optimization problems
involving conﬂicting objectives. Classic optimization techniques have evolved during the
past few decades as practical instruments for identifying the best answers to MOPs. Owing
to its useful applications in real-world issues, MOO has gained much interest in recent years
for cases in which ambiguity is present. Nonetheless, much research has been conducted on
this topic. Some of the prior works failed to consider how uncertainty affects optimization
problems.
Several methods can be used to solve MOO models, and some of the most common
approaches are discussed in this section. Decision-making problems are generally cat-
egorized into two main types: multiobjective decision-making (MODM) problems and
multiple-attribute decision-making (MADM) problems. MODM models aim to ﬁnd an opti-
mal solution, whereas MADM models are designed to evaluate and choose between different
options. Both of these approaches have been widely recognized in the literature for their
effectiveness and popularity. The aims of the DM dictate the sort of GP framework that is
used. The original GP formulations prioritized reducing the deviations exhibited by the more
signiﬁcant elements by ranking the undesired deviations according to their criticality. This
is referred to as non-Archimedean GP or lexicographic (preemptive) GP.
Whenprioritizationispertinenttothegivenobjectives,lexicographicGPmaybeemployed
(Hwang & Masud, 1979). The goals of preventative GP can be divided into classes with dif-
ferent priority levels. Here, it is assumed that no two objectives are of equal importance. Then,
in order of importance, each is addressed one after the other. To prevent underestimation,
DMs can assign multiple-choice aspiration levels (MCALs) to each goal, considering the
notion that “more/higher is better” (Qahtani et al., 2019). The advantage of employing the
multiobjective nonlinear programming (MONLP) approach for numerical problems is that it
is a nonlinear programming paradigm. It resolves an optimization issue that had been deﬁned
as an objective function of several factors that are present in different ﬁelds and a framework
of inequalities. Owing to budgetary constraints and time constraints, several goals must often
be accomplished concurrently in various domains. The selected method and the appropriate
technique are created for conducting multiobjective programming under nonlinear conditions
and the solution procedure of the GP approach.
However, because of its mathematical simplicity and graceful modeling strategy, GP is one
of the most widely used techniques for modeling and solving MOOPs. However, to address
the incommensurability of diverse objectives, the current goal-related algorithms have certain
shortcomings in terms of allocating their weights and then establishing a solution predicated
on the priority of the objectives. Singh and Yadav (2023) suggested an efﬁcient scalarization
technique for solving an MOOP by introducing a modiﬁed goal function. The application of
Singh’s method is illustrated by ﬁnding the best locations for creating intermediate municipal
solid waste (MSW) management facilities. Normal boundary intersection is another method
that was introduced by Das and Dennis for solving MOPs (Das & Dennis, 1998). Khakzad
(2023) introduced a multi-objective optimization method based on goal programming to
determine the optimal ﬁreﬁghting strategy in tank terminals. Wang and Liu (2025) sug-
gested a global robust weighted goal programming (GRWGP) model for optimizing the food
waste supply chain (FWSC) network under uncertain conditions. Cherif (2024) introduced
an approach to interval goal programming (IGP) that introduces a concept called “behav-
ioral penalty functions” to incorporate decision makers’ (DMs) attitudes toward risk into the

GP method is independent of the relative scales of the underlying functions. In posterior
methods, a set of Pareto-optimal representatives is obtained, and the DM can analyze the
tradeoff between the objectives (Cohon & Marks, 1975). This is the most commonly used
method in the literature for solving real problems. The Lexigraphic approach is one of the
prior preference articulation methods. In this method, the DM should consider the objective
functions on the basis of their absolute importance (from best to worst). The most important
objective function is subsequently minimized subject to the original constraints. If this prob-
lem has a unique solution, it is the solution of the whole MOP. Otherwise, the second-most
important objective function is minimized (Cohon & Marks, 1975). Silva Lopes Alexandrino
et al. (2020) used a genetic algorithm to solve a robust optimization problem. The optimal
value and the small variations around this optimum value were accounted for in the robust
optimization problem.
Among the methods discussed are lexicography, GP, and the compromise function (Vira
& Haimes, 1983). These techniques often require mixed preference data and quantitative
information from the DM before the target problem can be solved. Masud introduced ISGP
as a powerful approach for addressing MODM problems(Masud & Hwang, 1981). To date,
several models have been developed to solve MOPs. Each model has advantages and disad-
vantages, which are summarized in Table 1.
As mentioned in Table 1, ISGP leverages existing single-objective optimization tech-
niques, which provides it with a key advantage: the optimization methods and computer
algorithms already developed for single-objective problems can be adapted and integrated
into the ISGP framework. This adaptability not only reduces the need for entirely new com-
putational tools but also enhances the ﬂexibility and accessibility of the approach for use
in real-world applications, where DMs may need to interactively and sequentially balance
multiple competing objectives. In another signiﬁcant contribution to the ﬁeld, Ramos et al.
proposed a mathematical programming model that was designed to optimize the conﬁgura-
tions of water and heat exchanger networks (Ramos & Boix, 2014). Their two-step model
is grounded in mixed-integer linear programming (MILP), which is a versatile optimiza-
tion technique that addresses both continuous and discrete decision variables. The model
employs a Pareto front analysis process to simultaneously solve water and energy alloca-
tion problems, accounting for multiple conﬂicting objectives, such as cost minimization and
resource efﬁciency maximization.

3.2 Goal programming
In the past, GP was a popular method for addressing multicriteria optimization problems. The
development of the GP technique has been quite remarkable over the last 50 years, making it
one of the favored models for multiple-criteria decision analysis tasks. The applications of GP
consists cover a multitude of areas, such as engagement and social sciences. The structure of
123

Annals of Operations Research
a GP problem matches that of a linear programming problem. Centering on the deﬁnitions of
Charnes and Cooper (1961) and Ijiri (1965), GP is a developed linear programming method
for solving mathematical models with multiple objectives. The primary difference between
linear programming and GP is an explicit focus on goals and priorities. In a GP model, the
objective frontier of the goal program (the boundary of the solution region) is constantly
minimized and is composed of so-called deviation variables.
The formulation of the GP model includes two kinds of variables (decision variables and
deviation variables) and constraints classiﬁed into two groups (goal constraints and system
constraints). The system constraints are the original constraints with goal functions. Goals are
given priority levels and added to the constraint sets one by one while solving the problem
called the “goal constraints”. Over- and underdevotional variables are those added to the
left-hand side of the system constraints to transform inequalities into equalities. The system
constraints are the interpretations of the actual functions with a goal function, set priorities,
andpositiveandnegativedeviationvariables.GPisaneffectivemethodforﬁndingtheoptimal
solution for MODM problems in real life. The goals are assigned priorities and then solved,
where the goals are the objectives of the problem. GP methods can be classiﬁed according to
how similar their goals are in terms of importance. In nonpreemptive GP, all goals have the
same scale. In nonpreemptive methods, the goals are assigned weights, whereas in preemptive
GP, the goals are ranked according to their priority levels. They are ordered from the highest
consequential value (goal 1) to the lowest consequential value (goal m).
As an alternative, priorities form a favorable method for displaying that a goal is more
important than others. The coefﬁcients demonstrate that the weight of the ﬁrst goal is sig-
niﬁcantly greater than the value or cost of another goal or a subgoal, but the agent usually
has the option to choose its upper or lower limits. The DM can assign a preference to each
goal or subgoal on the basis of the expected achievement and rank the preferences in order
of importance. It is clear that not all goals can be achieved to the desired extent. Therefore,
the DM can associate a certain level of preference with the attainment of a speciﬁc goal,
regardless of whether GP is used. Thus, the actual value of GP describes the assistance it pro-
vides for solving decision problems related to multiple and conﬂicting goals in accordance
with a priority system determined by the DM. The GP model was introduced by Charnes and
Cooper (1961), and it can be represented mathematically as follows:

Various methods are available for solving MOOPs, and most existing MOO methods rely
on the information and priorities of the DM (Keeney & Raiffa, 1977). The existing methods
use primary information acquired from the DM; such a method is the utility function method
proposed by Kenney and Raiffa in 1977 (Keeney & Raiffa, 1977), which uses w j, ( j ∈I)

of the DM, and prone to explicit or implicit preferences toward certain objectives or solu-
tions. Instead, objective methods without dependence on information obtained from DMs
are preferred (Yu, 1973).

6 Discussion
Compared with the previously developed methods, such as ISGP and the approach of Ramos
and Boix (2014), the proposed GP model demonstrates superior performance across all three
objective functions. Although the objectives are not completely optimized in an ideal sense,
the GP approach delivers better overall solutions, particularly for balancing the tradeoffs
between multiple conﬂicting objectives. This conﬁrms the ability of the model to efﬁciently
handle MOOPs by transforming them into single-objective forms. One notable advantage of
the proposed model is its independence from the subjective preferences of the DM, marking a
signiﬁcant improvement over the model of Ramos et al., which requires the DM to determine
reference points based on their personal experience. These reference points, which are not
grounded in scientiﬁc methodology, can often be far from ideal, reducing the feasibility of
the solutions. Additionally, Ramos’ use of the ϵ- constraint approach and TOPSIS methods
for handling multiple objective functions can restrict the feasible space, especially when
addressing a larger number of objectives. This method also prioritizes certain goal func-
tions, potentially overlooking other critical objectives, as evidenced by the improved values
obtained for f1(x), f2(x) by the proposed GP model. Furthermore, the GP model offers
computational efﬁciency, achieving results with lower complexity than those of ISGP. The
need of the ISGP method for iterative DM interactions and long computational times renders
it less practical for large-scale or time-sensitive problems. In contrast, the proposed method
signiﬁcantly reduces the number of required calculations while maintaining robust optimiza-
tion capabilities, making it a more viable option in practical applications. The comparison
among the results produced by different methods reveals some limitations in the traditional
techniques such as lexicography and the compromise function. These methods tend to overly
prioritize one objective at the expense of others, effectively turning the target MOP into a
single-goal optimization problem. This results in the acquisition of suboptimal solutions for
the other objective functions. Additionally, in methods such as the compromise function,
the use of higher norm values (e.g., P > 2) adds complexity by making the goal function
nonlinear, which increases the difﬁculty of solving the problem. In conclusion, the proposed
GP approach offers a practical, efﬁcient, and less subjective alternative for solving complex
MOPs, improving both the solution quality and computational time. Future research could
explore its scalability for use with larger and more complex systems while maintaining these
advantages. The following section presents a case study that further clariﬁes the proposed
model.
7 Conclusion
Multicriteria decision-making (MCDM) methods are invaluable tools in operational research
and management science because of their extensive applications across the science, engi-
neering, and social science ﬁelds. One of the key reasons for the complexity of real-world
optimization problems is the challenge of addressing multiple conﬂicting objectives via a
single-objective approach. In this research, we focused on optimizing gas energy consump-
tion across various sectors, accounting for economic, environmental, and political factors.
We used GP as a solution method for the multiobjective linear programming model, which
allowed us to balance multiple conﬂicting goals simultaneously. From a theoretical stand-
point, this study contributes to the ongoing development of MOO methods by enhancing
the ﬂexibility and efﬁciency of the GP approach. Unlike traditional single-objective models,
123

Annals of Operations Research
MOO acknowledges the reality that many real-world decisions must address multiple, often
conﬂicting, objectives. Our proposed model simpliﬁes this process by minimizing devia-
tions from the set goals, which represent the ideal solutions for different objectives. This
not only improves the degree of balance between competing objectives but also highlights
the effectiveness of GP in terms of handling diverse constraints across various economic,
environmental, and political dimensions. The research also underscores the importance of
exploring alternatives to more complex methods, such as those proposed by Masud and
Hwang (1981) and Ramos and Boix (2014). While these methods are comprehensive, they
introduce multiple layers of complexity and demand additional computational effort. In con-
trast, our model reduces the imposed computational burden by solving the target problem in
two simple steps, thus contributing to the theoretical literature on optimization by demonstrat-
ing that simpler approaches can yield equally, if not more, effective results. This streamlining
of the optimization process could encourage further research into simplifying other complex
decision-making frameworks. From a practical perspective, the proposed algorithm provides
a more efﬁcient energy consumption management approach by balancing economic perfor-
mance with environmental sustainability and political considerations. The ability to reach
an optimal solution with fewer calculations and less computational effort is highly valuable
in real-world scenarios where time and resources are limited. This is especially relevant in
sectors such as energy, manufacturing, and environmental management, where DMs need to
quickly ﬁnd efﬁcient solutions that meet regulatory and sustainability targets. The simplic-
ity of the developed model is also its strength in practical applications. Unlike the multistep
processes required by methods such as those presented by Masud and Ramos, which are com-
putationally intensive and time-consuming, our approach reduces the complexity involved in
two key steps. Additionally, the practical implementation of this model could lead to more
sustainable business practices by enabling organizations to make informed tradeoffs between
economic growth and environmental stewardship. This aligns with the growing emphasis on
sustainability analytics, where businesses must increasingly balance their proﬁtability with
the need to reduce their environmental footprint. By integrating multiple objectives into a
streamlined model, DMs can more easily evaluate the impact of their choices on both their
ﬁnancial performance and ecological sustainability. The broader impact of this research
lies in its potential to inﬂuence future studies and applications involving multiobjective
decision-making models. The ability of the proposed model to minimize the computational
effort required while achieving optimal solutions has implications for sectors beyond energy,
including healthcare, logistics, and urban planning, where DMs must often navigate a vari-
ety of competing objectives. By demonstrating the advantages of a simpliﬁed approach, this
research opens doors to the more widespread adoption of multi-objective decision-making
tools across various ﬁelds. In conclusion, the proposed model not only provides an optimized
solution for complex problems but also reduces the imposed calculation burden, making it
a practical and efﬁcient tool for DMs. Its theoretical contributions help advance the ﬁeld of
MOO, while its practical beneﬁts have the potential to transform how industries approach
resource management and sustainability challenges.
