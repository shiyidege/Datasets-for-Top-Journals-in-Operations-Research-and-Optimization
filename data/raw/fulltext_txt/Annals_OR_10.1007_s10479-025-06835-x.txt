Abstract
This study explores the evolution of City Logistics 4.0 (CL4.0) by systematically review­
ing the literature to provide a structured framework integrating management practices 
with technology and sustainability perspectives. Using the PRISMA methodology, a sci­
entometric analysis of 899 Scopus-indexed papers reveals key research trends, authorship 
networks, and thematic developments. The findings highlight three core disciplinary per­
spectives—logistics, technology, and sustainability—offering a unified definition of CL4.0 
and outlining future research directions. Practical implications equip policymakers and 
logistics professionals with insights for implementing innovative technologies and sustain­
able practices, paving the way for City Logistics 5.0.
Keywords  City Logistics 4.0 · City Logistics 5.0 · Sustainability · Technology · Last-
mile delivery · Urban distribution · Optimization

1  Introduction
The effective design and management of logistics systems at an urban level has a major 
impact on the economic, environmental and social sustainability of cities and the surround­
ing territories (Farahani et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2020; Qi et al., 2022). Passenger transpor­
tation is crucial for the quality of life of individuals (Savelsbergh & Van Woensel, 2016) 
and has important consequences from an environmental point of view (Meng et al., 2020). 
Urban freight transportation, on the other hand, is of growing importance (D’Acierno et al., 
2011). The change in consumer habits, with a greater demand for efficiency and customiza­
tion in delivery methods, as well as the need to respect the environment, have led to impor­
tant changes both in last mile delivery methods and in the organization of the first mile in 
reverse logistics (Reul et al., 2021).
A notable push in the evolution of urban logistics systems is given by the availability of 
new digital technologies which exponentially increase the available design options (e.g., 
Bányai & Illés, 2018; Leung et al., 2022; Attari et al., 2024). In the production field, these 
technologies give rise to a new production paradigm indicated with the expression Industry 
4.0 (I4.0) (Barreto et al., 2017). Similarly, in the field of logistics, it is possible to speak 
of Logistics 4.0 to refer to the systems that emerge when technologies such as drones, 
robots or self-driving vehicles, decision support systems such as digital twins, multilateral 
platforms for information exchange, blockchain-based systems or artificial intelligence are 
introduced (e.g., Khan et al., 2021; Agnusdei et al., 2022; Ferrari et al., 2022). When atten­
tion is focused on logistics systems in the urban perimeter, we can speak of urban logistics 
(Dablanc, 2007) or city logistics (Crainic et al., 2009). We can therefore define urban or City 
Logistics 4.0 (CL4.0) as the space of design solutions for logistics systems on an urban scale 
made possible by the use of 4.0 technologies.
The logistics literature has highlighted global trends such as the need for greater sus­
tainability of transportation systems (Hahn, 2020), the urgency of greater flexibility in the 
logistics chain (Arvidsson et al., 2016), the importance of building resilient supply chains 
(Agnusdei et al., 2022). To achieve these objectives, solutions have been investigated which 
envisage the use of several modes of transportation in an integrated way (Huang et al., 2021; 
Li et al., 2012). The importance of adequately understanding the functioning of all sections 
of the logistics chain including the urban level has been stressed (Winkenbach et al., 2016). 
Even in the case of urban transportation, digital technologies offer great opportunities for 
the analysis, planning and management of logistics (Epicoco & Falagario, 2022; Agnusdei 
et al., 2022).
The theme of CL4.0 has been addressed by scholars of different disciplines with differ­
ent points of view: transportation (Barreto et al., 2017); smart cities (Cassandras, 2016); 
economy and management (Hahn, 2020). This variety of contributions, on the one hand 
represents a richness, as it provides an articulated picture of the phenomenon. On the other 
hand it represents a limit to further understanding of the problem. The literature is vast and 
fragmented. The disciplinary boundaries make it difficult to grasp the complexity of the phe­
nomenon and to integrate the different problems. For those interested in developing tools, 
models and methods for solving transportation problems, this can be a strong limitation. A 
partial point of view, indeed, could lead to solutions to local problems which, on the other 
hand, do not improve the total picture.

There is a need for a theoretical framework which, starting from a mapping of the exist­
ing literature, summarizes the problem and, in this way, represents a first reference for the 
development of more systematic and coherent future research. To fill this gap, this article 
aims to map the literature on CL4.0 considering contributions from different disciplines and 
to define the main theoretical concepts that frame the domain, thus providing a solid basis to 
deive future research and, ultimately, the choices of those who are responsible for designing 
and managing logistics systems (Durach et al., 2017, 2021). To achieve our goal we started 
from the following research questions:
 
●
RQ1: What distinct categories and variables characterize CL4.0 from a management 
point of view in current research?
 
●
RQ2: What modelling approaches are identified in the literature for addressing the chal­
lenges specific to CL4.0?
 
●
RQ3: How can CL4.0 be defined to highlight its unique features and advancements 
compared to traditional logistics systems?
We then conducted a systematic review (Kraus et al., 2022) of studies published in peer-
reviewed journals and indexed in the Scopus database. The initial set of 899 selected papers 
was analysed manually by the authors and also by using multiple scientometric techniques, 
including the qualitative and quantitative analysis conducted through visualization, biblio­
graphic coupling, co-word mapping, and an in-depth analysis of the full texts, noting: 
1. the main issues and the main variables used for design and management of logistic 
systems;
2. the role of technology;
3. the main decision problems arising in the field.
The contribution of the paper is, then threefold: 
1. we map and summarize the literature on the topic of CL4.0 highlighting the main design 
and management dimensions as well as the main optimization problems;
2. we provide an original definition of CL4.0;
3. by confronting the main design variables with the existing modelling approaches we 
propose an agenda for future research.
The contribution of our article is useful both for researchers in the field of logistics and 
for those in the field of supply chain management, as it gives an integrative vision of the 
phenomenon by linking the two disciplinary fields. It is useful for logistics, purchasing and 
supply chain managers interested in understanding what the opportunities of CL4.0 are. 
Finally, it is important for policy-makers interested in the planning of economic systems, 
transportation and, in particular, the planning of urban territories, on which logistics systems 
have a decisive influence. By examining the impact of both emerging technologies and a 
sustainable orientation on city logistics, this paper paves the way for the development of a 
new paradigm that could be termed City Logistics 5.0. It should be noted that an analysis 
of optimization methods is beyond the scope of this study. Instead, this paper focuses on 
examining specific classes of optimization problems in city logistics systems, providing a

2  Methodology
This systematic literature review was conducted using the PRISMA methodology (Page 
et al., 2021), combined with a theory-building approach to literature review (Swanson & 
Chermack, 2013; Kraus et al., 2022). To collect relevant papers, a structured search was 
performed on Scopus. Each author contributed synonyms for key study terms by analyzing 
the keywords of pertinent articles. Additionally, terms representing subproblems or specific 
aspects of the main topics were considered. The final set of keywords was reviewed collab­
oratively until consensus was reached. The following search string was applied in Scopus on 
December 28, 2024, targeting relevant keywords in titles, abstracts, and keyword sections 
of each article:
(TITLE-ABS-KEY ("last mile") OR TITLE-ABS-KEY (urban) OR TITLE- ABS-KEY 
(city)) AND (TITLE-ABS-KEY (logistics) OR TITLE-ABS-KEY ("supply chain") OR 
TITLE-ABS-KEY (distribution)) AND TITLE-ABS-KEY ((4.0) OR (5.0) OR (cyber-phys­
ical) OR (synchromodal*) OR ("digital twin") OR (inter*modal*) OR (multimodal*) OR 
(multi AND modal*) OR (intermodal*)) AND (LIMIT-TO (SRCTYPE , "j")) AND (LIMIT-
TO (SUBJAREA , "COMP") OR LIMIT-TO (SUBJAREA , "ENGI") OR LIMIT-TO (SUB­
JAREA , "SOCI") OR LIMIT-TO (SUBJAREA , "MATH") OR LIMIT-TO (SUBJAREA , 
"DECI") OR LIMIT-TO (SUBJAREA , "ECON") OR LIMIT-TO (SUBJAREA , "MULT")) 
AND (LIMIT-TO (DOCTYPE , "ar") OR LIMIT-TO (DOCTYPE, "re")) AND (LIMIT-TO 
(LANGUAGE , "English")).
We obtained a set of 899 papers. Based on the PRISMA methodology in Fig. 1 (Page et 
al., 2021; Moher et al. 2009), we followed four steps: identification, screening, eligibility 
and inclusion.
After identifying potentially relevant articles in Scopus, we checked for duplicates and 
assessed each abstract to determine its relevance and suitability for our review. We removed 
six duplicates and excluded 542 papers that did not align with the scope of our study. Addi­
tionally, 157 papers have been excluded after full-text analysis based on the inclusion/exclu­
sion criteria outlined in Table 1. As a result, the final dataset consists of 194 papers.
The analysis of full papers has been conducted by all the authors, and their evaluations 
have subsequently been shared among the team of authors. All selected papers were also 
analysed using the R-studio software app Biblioshiny (Aria & Cuccurullo, 2017).
Then, the articles included in the systematic literature review have been analysed by the 
authors with the theory building approach, according to Swanson and Chermack (2013). 
In fact, theory building is the most important potential contribution of a literature review 
(Okoli, 2022; Rowe, 2014; Tsiotsou et al., 2022; Paul et al., 2023), while entrenching differ­
ent research streams for building theory (Leidner, 2018).

3.2  Disciplinary perspectives
Our analysis of the sample of articles highlighted three main thematic macro-areas, as 
concluded in Section 3.1. A first area concerns new issues that emerge for researchers in 
the logistics field as a consequence of the application of I4.0 technologies; a second area 
focuses on recent development in I4.0 technologies; finally, a third area of interest concerns 
the implications of innovations in logistics and technology for social and environmental 
sustainability. The main results emerging from the analysis are discussed below and sum­
marized in Figure 5.
This figure illustrates a framework for exploring the CL4.0 domain, grounded in three 
key perspectives: Logistics, Technology, and Sustainability. Each perspective is character­
ized by specific sub-areas of intervention and contributes, through a coordinated approach, 
to optimizing city logistics operations:
 
●
the logistics perspective emphasizes strategies to improve city logistics by coordinat­
ing multiple transportation modes, redesigning distribution networks, integrating long-
distance and urban logistics, and adapting operations to demand. The objective is to 
enhance efficiency, reduce congestion, and address the dynamic needs of urban environ­
ments;
 
●
the technology perspective highlights the use of technology to enhance automation, 
flexibility, coordination, data security, and decision-making support in city logistics, 
thereby improving efficiency while augmenting human capabilities;
 
●
the sustainability perspective in city logistics emphasizes minimizing environmental 
impact through eco-friendly distribution methods, leveraging crowd-based and on-de­
mand services, utilizing, for example, waterways as alternative transportation modes, 
and implementing policies and planning strategies that promote sustainability (Table 2).

3.2.1  Logistics perspective
The availability of I4.0 technologies provides new solutions to city logistics problems. Four 
main themes have been identified, addressed in the sample of articles analysed: manage­
ment of intermodal/multimodal city logistics; new options for city logistics network design; 
integration of long distance and last mile/first mile (reverse) logistics; demand-based city 
logistics.
Management of multimodal/intermodal city logistics. The design and management of 
transportation systems cannot be done by isolating a mode, but must consider the combined 
use of different modes (He & Haasis, 2019; Deng et al., 2021; Zheng & Geroliminis, 2013; 
Reul et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2021) (including, for example, on-foot movements (Lopik 
et al., 2020), autonomous agents such as drones (Moshref-Javadi & Winkenbach, 2021)), 
urban trains and tramways (Alessandrini et al., 2012)) as well as contextual factors (Ferrari 
et al., 2023). This implies that it is necessary to consider both that in the same journey a 
user employs different means of transportation, and that the same user can choose different 
means of transportation in successive instances of the same journey (e.g., Fontaine et al., 
2021; Sluijk et al., 2023; Paricio and Lopez-Carmona, 2019; Meng et al., 2020). I4.0 tech­
nologies facilitate the design and management of these systems as they allow information 
and decisions to be centralized (Jiang et al., 2020; Fumasoli et al., 2015), and allow complex 
simulations in order to anticipate the behavior of the system before implementing it (Akkad 
et al., 2022; Perboli et al., 2018; Human et al., 2023). Moshref-Javadi and Winkenbach 
(2021) underline that I4.0 technologies introduce new modes of transportation, for example 
autonomous driving, thus opening up opportunities for transportation systems design and 
management. Janjevic et al. (2021) underlines the availability of multiple transportation 
options calls for a more fine grained analysis of transportation networks. Several papers deal 
with port cities and the integration between the logistic activities in the ports and those in the 
surrounding city (Cuda et al., 2015; Peña Zarzuelo et al., 2020).
New options for city logistics network design. New technologies allow for new design 
options for urban logistics (Savelsbergh & Van Woensel, 2016). According to Ambrosino 
and Sciomachen (2012), on the basis of a study on the city of Genoa, the availability of 
richer and more precise information allows the activities in urban hub to be better man­
aged. In view of this, there are more options for designing these hubs. For example, Roso 
et al. (2009) propose the concept of dry ports, intended as inland hubs for the exchange of 
transportation mode that work just like ports. With reference with the construction industry, 
Brusselaers and Mommens (2022) underline the importance of multimodal consolidation 
hubs to reduce traffic congestion. Correia et al. (2021) consider that I4.0 technologies allow 
for the customization of products in small workshops or factories close to the customer. 
Flexible logistics services need to be integrated with these distributed manufacturing net­
works. In port cities, the design of the interface between port and urban logistics is relevant 
(Cuda et al., 2015). Monios et al. (2018) underline that I4.0 facilitates the creation of inter­
modal hubs both close to ports (where they are traditionally located) and outside cities 
(where they have less impact on traffic and pollution). The role of ports as hubs is expected 
to grow in importance, and digital technologies can help managing the growing complexity

signed exploiting the options offered by technologies such as the blockchain (Makhdoom et

Integration of long distance and last mile/first mile (reverse) logistics. Technology facili­

Cuda et al. (2015) suggest that the use of satellites would allow a better management of the 
integration between arrivals of goods and people from outside the city and the exchange 
with means of urban transportation at hubs located on the edge of the urban area. Ish­
faq and Sox (2012) proposes that decision support systems would improve the integration 
between railways transportation and urban logistics. The integration of last mile (and first 
mile) logistics with long distance transportation is very often addressed by articles studying 
port cities (e.g., Cuda et al.,, 2015).
Demand-based city logistics. The new I4.0 technologies make it possible to customize 
city logistics services and modify them in real time, increasing the overall flexibility of the 
system. According to Bi and Ye (2021), big data allow to better understand passengers’ 
behavior, thus favoring the use of car-hailing services. Apps facilitate shared ride-hail trips 
(Dean & Kockelman, 2021; Lyu et al., 2021). The use of simulation tools allow more pre­
cise forecast of transportation demand, thus allowing to adapt the transportation offer to the 
needs of users in an urban context (Perboli et al., 2022; Bogataj et al., 2017). Sanaullah et 
al. (2021), suggest that with the use of new technologies it is easier to make passenger trans­
portation systems flexible during the day, replacing fixed-route services with On-Demand 
transportation at night. Piatkowski (2021) studies self organizing of transportation service 
among passengers, underlining the benefits for traffic.
3.2.2  Technology perspective
Many of the articles analysed help to clarify the purpose of using I4.0 technologies in the 
field of urban logistics. These articles consider technologies such as IoT, blockchain, drones, 
robots, autonomous vehicles, digital twins, just to consider some of the most frequently 
mentioned. Such different tools help to achieve common results. In particular, six main 
effects of the use of I4.0 technologies in city logistics have been identified: automation; 
increased flexibility; augmented intelligence; improved coordination; improved security 
and reliability; enhanced human capacities.
Automation. Replacing humans with artificial agents, such as robots or drones, can make 
some processes more efficient (Hoffmann & Prause, 2018). For example, Liu et al. (2021) 
propose the use of autonomous delivery robots in combination with traditional means (i.e., 
trucks) in last mile logistics. Similarly, Moshref-Javadi and Winkenbach (2021) consider 
the combined use of trucks and drones. The introduction of autonomous vehicles is a dis­
ruptive innovation that will change the approach to transportation modes and their effect on 
traffics, potentially reducing social and environmental negative effects (Reul et al., 2021).

Increase flexibility. One of the problems of urban logistics is to adapt to the needs of 
individuals which are: (a) multiple and diverse; (b) changeable. City logistics must be flex­
ible in the sense of allowing for different options, but also in the sense that it must foresee 
rapid changes in the processes. According to Bogataj et al. (2017), with reference to food 
supply chains, IoT can provide real-time data on the environment (e.g. humidity, tempera­
ture), product and transportation that allow to dynamically optimize the process. Advanced 
applications of GPS technology enable, according to Sanaullah et al. (2021), effective on-
demand transportation services. Correia et al. (2021) highlight how I4.0 technologies allow 
new modes of distributed production, with the personalization of products in sites located 
close to the customer. This modes ask for customized logistics that are also enabled by 
technologies like the IoT.
Augmented intelligence. Big data and artificial intelligence make it possible to analyze 
complex situations, facilitating the optimization of processes or the anticipation of prob­
lems and the prevention of disasters (Gutierrez-Franco et al., 2021). Artificial intelligence 
allows to analyze complex data, thus improving the accuracy of traffic forecasting in case 
of multimodal transportation (Lyu et al., 2021). For example, Bi and Ye (2021) apply AI in 
the analysis of commuter behavior, providing insight into traffic forecast and transportation 
planning. Boussier et al. (2009) discuss the case of urban logistics in ancient city centers and 
highlight how simulation can help analyzing the phenomenon and find effective solutions. 
According to Miao and Lan (2021) the ability to understand the functioning and problems 
in complex logistics systems can be facilitated by visualization technologies. For example 
digital twins, augmented reality or other image-based technologies can make it easier to 
understand phenomena than text and numbers. IoT and information systems enable track 
and trace capability and connectivity among actors in order to monitor logistics processes in 
urban ports (Fahim et al., 2021).
Improved coordination. One of the advantages of I4.0 technologies is the timely sharing 
of information, which improves coordination and integration across actors and transporta­
tion modes, as highlighted by Alkaabi (2014) in his review of the literature. Digital twins 
help simulate complex systems, thus allowing better analysis prior to assuming design deci­
sions (Human et al., 2023). Bayliss et al. (2023) propose the combined use of artificial 
intelligence and digital twins to analyze last mile delivery and improve the level of coordi­
nation in the system by synchronizing orders, processes and information. Digital technolo­
gies allow timely exchange of information that contribute to the harmonization of traffic 
schedules and to integrated information to passengers (Alkaabi, 2014).
Information security and reliability. Logistics processes in general involve risks related 
both to the management of information (for example risks related to the protection of pri­
vacy) and to the health of the actors involved. Technologies can reduce these. Above all 
the use of the blockchain has been seen as a way to improve some aspects of reliability and 
security of information in urban logistics systems. For example, Makhdoom et al. (2019) 
propose a blockchain-based system that can improve privacy protection. Blockchain is also 
useful for guaranteeing the traceability of transports and, thus, avoid opportunistic or dis­
honest behavior (Dziatkovskii et al., 2022). According to Sharma et al. (2020), robots can 
make logistics processes safer, avoiding, for example, the transmission of diseases related 
to human contact.
Enhance human capabilities. Technology does not replace human action (at least not 
always). It is often used to enhance human abilities. Some technological tools can improve

the capabilities of human beings involved in logistics processes. For example, according ton 
Brusselaers and Mommens (2022), in-sight (monocular) navigation tools allow for more 
efficient delivery and with fewer accidents than traditional ones because they allow for 
hands-free driving and walking. Several apps exist that can support users in their urban 
shifts, with an overall reduction of use of private transportation and a beneficial effect on 
traffic and pollution. Many articles underline how the different technologies mentioned best 
express their potential when used in combination (e.g., Cassandras, 2016; Correia et al., 
2022). IoT, artificial intelligence, simulation, autonomous driving and robots or drones can 
change the paradigm in urban transportation by creating what we have identified as CL4.0.
3.2.3  Sustainability perspective
Many innovations have been introduced in the last decade in urban logistics at both the 
organizational and technological levels. Such innovations brought relevant implications 
in terms of sustainability at large, and, in particular, for the implementation of a circular 
approach. Four key themes have emerged from our analysis: more sustainable distribution 
solutions and intermodal/multimodal city logistics; alternative crowd-based solutions and 
on-demand logistics services; waterborne transportation/inland waterways; policy, planning 
and regulation for sustainability.
More sustainable distribution solutions. As freight transportation at the urban level is 
one of the key drivers of congestion, disorder and pollution (e.g. emissions, noises), new 
and more sustainable solutions are being implemented in order to reduce emissions, trans­
portation costs and traffic issues (Cuda et al., 2015; Qi et al., 2022; Sluijk et al., 2023; 
Bayliss et al., 2023; Farahani et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021; Boussier et al., 2009; Arvidsson 
& Arvidsson, 2017; Yoon & Le, 2013; Marcucci et al., 2020; Buestán et al., 2020; Arvids­
son et al., 2016; Piatkowski, 2021; Dziatkovskii et al., 2022; Gordijn, 1999; Gerigk, 2022; 
Kabashkin, 2007; Brusselaers & Mommens, 2022; Alessandrini et al., 2012; Onyemechi & 
Ekwenna, 2012). Among such solutions, the intermodal and multimodal ones take the lion’s 
share (Cuda et al., 2015; Qi et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Brusselaers & Mommens, 2022; 
Gerigk, 2022; Jiang et al., 2020; Mommens & Macharis, 2014; Moeremans et al., 2022), for 
instance, by means of the implementation of the two-echelon or multi-echelon systems for 
urban distribution consisting in lockers, positioning points, depots and (sometimes mobile) 
satellite facilities generally situated in the outskirts of the city for the (un)loading, sort­
ing and consolidation of freight to be delivered by ecofriendly vehicles in the last mile 
(Cuda et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2021) like electric vehicles or cargo bikes (Sluijk et al., 2023), 
seaways (Arvidsson & Arvidsson, 2017), micro-mobility at large (Lopik et al., 2020) or 
crowdsourced solutions (Arvidsson et al., 2016) appropriate to infrastructurally-constrained 
historical centers of small-medium cities (Boussier et al., 2009). Moreover, such solutions 
tend to implement also synchromodality approaches in order to further improve the emis­
sion reduction results as well as van mileage reduction, cost efficiency, energy consumption 
efficiency and costs (Farahani et al., 2018). Last, but not least, new and more sustainable 
solutions are increasingly related to integration of existing solutions with a more sustainable 
exploitation of waterways (Démare et al., 2017; Mommens & Macharis, 2014; Brusselaers 
& Mommens, 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Lam et al., 2013; Alessandrini et al., 2012; Onyem­
echi & Ekwenna, 2012; Moeremans et al., 2022; López-Bermúdez et al., 2020; Gerigk, 
2022; Kabashkin, 2007) as well as the enhancement of alternative solutions like pedestrian

ones (Lopik et al., 2020), autonomous agents (e.g., drones) (Moshref-Javadi & Winkenbach, 
2021), and public transportation means (Alessandrini et al., 2012).
Alternative crowd-based solutions and on-demand logistics services. The increasing 
adoption of crowdsourced logistics services in urban contexts, also by means of vehicle 
sharing solutions and devoted technology platforms, is aimed at improving the social and 
environmental sustainability of cities (Meng et al., 2020; Arvidsson et al., 2016). In fact, 
although passengers and freight transportation utilize the same infrastructures, they are gen­
erally treated as separated systems. Hence, the lack of synergies between the two deter­
mines the wasting of scarce resources and the increase in congestion that could, instead, be 
reduced through crowdshipping solutions (Arvidsson et al., 2016). Moreover, crowdsources 
solutions and on-demand logistics converge whereas sustainable transportation solutions are 
integrated into online Mobility-as-a-Service (MaaS) platforms (Piatkowski, 2021), achiev­
ing an increased transaction efficiency compared with private carriers (Kong et al., 2021).
Waterborne transportation/inland waterways. Notwithstanding the high degree of com­
plexity, the most recent transportation solutions are increasingly focused on the minimi­
zation of distribution time and trans-shipment times in supply chains, while ensuring an 
eco-friendly approach (Gerigk, 2022). Such objectives can be achieved through the imple­
mentation of large-size unmanned fleets in connection with multifunctional port facilities 
and marine waterways, including their connection to renewable power supply sources, thus, 
integrating land and sea transportation structures with logistics facilities and complemen­
tary services, according to a urban overview (Gerigk, 2022; López-Bermúdez et al., 2020; 
Démare et al., 2017; Mommens & Macharis, 2014; Lam et al., 2013; Moeremans et al., 
2022). Moreover, some areas are facing a remarkable increase in trade and freight volumes, 
thus, leading to the need of a major restructuring in order to integrate logistics centers with 
modern ports and other operators (Kabashkin, 2007). Even developing third world nations 
and some Construction Consolidation Centers (CCC), which are distribution platforms for 
the construction industry, are currently endowed with multimodal connections and linked 
to marine or inland waterways for the transportation of freight (e.g building materials) in 
order to make cheaper and more sustainable the transportation services (Kabashkin, 2007; 
Onyemechi & Ekwenna, 2012).
Policy, planning and regulation for sustainability. The fast pace of development of tech­
nology and innovation at large in urban logistics pushed the stakeholders to work on and 
update policy, planning and regulation frameworks for the integration of such innovations 
and technologies into a circular and sustainable approach, like in the case of digital twins 
utilized for simulation of complex systems and, namely, of traffic control and optimization 
aimed at reducing pollution impacts (Human et al., 2023; Makhdoom et al., 2019). More­
over, some studies dealt with location planning decisions, while including environmental 
criteria in the decision-making process like air and noise pollution reduction (Sopha et al., 
2018). In China, also measures related to behavioral aspects have been tested by the logis­
tics authority in order to impact on the carrier’s transportation behavior by adopting low-
carbon regulations (e.g. subsidizing railway transportation) without damaging the interests 
of the carrier (Jiang et al., 2020). Moreover, product tracking, quality and sustainability data 
are constantly monitored by logistics firms through a blockchain technology that ensures 
cost efficiency, timely delivery, product ownership, privacy, security and traceability (Dzi­
atkovskii et al., 2022).

3.3  Modelling approaches
A subset of 102 contributions from the selected papers in this literature review is discussed 
and analysed in this section to provide a comprehensive overview of the recent advance­
ments and state-of-the-art research in CL4.0 from an optimization perspective. The find­
ings aim to highlight existing research gaps and suggest future directions for investigation. 
As noted by Savelsbergh and Van Woensel (2016), city logistics represents a field with 
immense potential for societal impact and sustainability. However, it is also characterized 
by complex challenges, presenting numerous opportunities for the scientific communities of 
transportation science and logistics.
Emerging trends in city logistics–such as the growing need for rapid operational deci­
sions, real-time services, the proliferation of data, and the advent of new technologies within 
hyperconnected logistics ecosystems–demand a strong focus on optimization and simula­
tion techniques capable of addressing the challenges posed by this dynamic environment. 
In this context, analyzing optimization models and methods to tackle these challenges is of 
paramount importance.
Out of the 102 papers reviewed, 28 are themselves literature reviews (see, for example, 
Golinska-Dawson and Sethanan 2023; Pan et al., 2021; Silva et al., 2023). Their examina­
tion allowed us to propose an original classification of all analysed contributions into four 
core areas of CL4.0: (1) Distribution Facility Network, (2) Low-emission/Hi-Tech Vehicles, 
(3) Collaborative Logistics, and (4) Information and Communication Technologies (ICT). 
Each of these areas is briefly described in the following sections.
3.3.1  Distribution facility network
It plays a central role in enabling efficient, sustainable, and flexible city logistics operations. 
It encompasses a variety of interconnected facilities, each designed to address specific chal­
lenges in urban freight distribution and to optimize last-mile delivery processes (Mara et 
al. 2021). These facilities contribute to reducing delivery times, improving service levels, 
and minimizing environmental impact in complex urban environments (see Dukkanci et al., 
2019; Ortiz-Astorquiza et al., 2018).
At the core of this network are Urban Distribution Centres (UDCs), which act as central 
hubs for consolidating goods before their redistribution to smaller facilities closer to end 
customers. These centres are complemented by satellites, smaller-scale hubs strategically 
located within urban areas to facilitate the swift redistribution of goods and reduce vehicle 
travel distances (Nguyen et al., 2012; Roozkhosh & Farimani, 2023; Ishfaq & Sox, 2012).
Pickup points, which may be either attended or unattended, provide a convenient solu­
tion for customers to collect their orders. They reduce the need for home deliveries and 
help streamline last-mile operations. Similarly, lockers are becoming increasingly popular 
in urban areas. These automated and secure storage units allow customers to retrieve pack­
ages at their convenience, making them a highly effective option for contactless deliveries 
(Dell’Amico et al., 2023; Silva et al., 2023; Yu et al., (2023).
Delivery stations serve as operational nodes where packages are sorted and prepared for 
final delivery. These stations are crucial for ensuring timely and accurate shipments. Mean­
while, micro depots and mobile depots introduce flexibility into the logistics network. Micro 
depots are small, localized facilities that support eco-friendly modes of transportation, while 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
mobile depots are temporary facilities that can be relocated as needed to meet fluctuating 
demand in different areas (Pan et al., 2021; Ji et al., (2019).
Finally, mobile lockers represent an innovative addition to the distribution network. 
These dynamic solutions combine the functionalities of lockers and mobility, enabling 
goods to be delivered to predefined locations while offering customers the flexibility to 
retrieve their orders from mobile units (Schwerdfeger & Boysen, 2020).
By leveraging these diverse facilities, the distribution facility network can be mathemati­
cally modelled as a multi-echelon network, where goods are moved through multiple tiers 
of facilities before reaching the final customer.
3.3.2  Low-emission/Hi-Tech Vehicles
This category is a cornerstone of CL4.0, focusing on sustainable and technologically 
advanced solutions to meet the growing demand for efficient and eco-friendly urban freight 
transportation. These vehicles are essential for reducing greenhouse gas emissions, mini­
mizing congestion, and addressing the unique challenges of last-mile delivery in dynamic 
urban environments (Bayliss et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2022; Savelsbergh & Van Woensel, 
2016).
Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), commonly known as drones, offer a novel solution 
for delivering lightweight parcels over short distances, particularly in congested areas or 
remote locations (Sacramento et al., 2019; Moshref-Javadi et al., 2020; Leon-Blanco et al., 
2022; Mirzapour Al-e-hashem et al. 2024). They enable rapid deliveries while avoiding 
traditional road networks, making them highly suitable for time-sensitive goods (Salama & 
Srinivas, 2020; Mulumba et al., 2024).
Autonomous bots and autonomous delivery robots are ground-based robotic solutions 
capable of navigating urban landscapes autonomously (Morim et al., 2024; De Maio et al., 
2024; Chen et al., 2021). These devices are designed to operate on sidewalks or bike lanes, 
delivering small parcels directly to customers’ doors. Their integration with advanced AI 
and IoT systems ensures precise navigation and efficient route optimization (Su et al., 2024).
E-vehicles, including electric vans and trucks, provide a sustainable alternative to tradi­
tional fossil-fuel-powered vehicles. They are especially effective for medium- to large-scale 
deliveries within urban areas, as they significantly reduce noise pollution and CO2 emis­
sions while maintaining operational capacity (Yancho et al., 2024; Xu et al. 2022).
Cargo bikes and e-cargo bikes represent another eco-friendly solution, ideal for navigat­
ing crowded urban centers and pedestrian zones. These vehicles are not only emission-free 
but also highly maneuverable, making them an excellent choice for small to medium deliv­
eries over short distances (Papaioannou et al., 2023; Shramenko et al., 2024).
E-tricycles expand the capabilities of cargo bikes by providing greater stability and 
carrying capacity, while still retaining the benefits of zero emissions and enhanced urban 
mobility. These vehicles are particularly effective for deliveries in narrow streets or areas 
with limited vehicle access.
Together, these vehicles demonstrate the potential to transform urban logistics by inte­
grating sustainable practices with cutting-edge technology, paving the way for greener, 
more efficient last-mile delivery solutions (Perboli et al., 2018).

3.3.3  Collaborative logistics
This paradigm in CL4.0 emphasizes cooperation among various stakeholders to enhance 
efficiency, reduce costs, and minimize the environmental impact of urban freight transporta­
tion. It involves sharing resources, infrastructures, and information to achieve mutual ben­
efits. Horizontal collaboration refers to partnerships between companies operating at the 
same level of the supply chain, such as competing logistics providers. By sharing resources 
like vehicles, warehouses, or delivery networks, businesses can optimize operations, reduce 
redundant trips, and lower environmental footprints (Roselan et al., 2024).
Vertical collaboration, on the other hand, involves coordination between companies at 
different levels of the supply chain, such as suppliers, distributors, and retailers. This form 
of collaboration fosters end-to-end visibility and enhances supply chain efficiency by align­
ing objectives and leveraging shared data (Labarthe et al., 2024).
Freight sharing and cargo sharing focus on the joint utilization of transportation assets to 
consolidate shipments. This practice reduces the number of vehicles on the road, leading to 
cost savings and lower emissions. Cargo sharing is particularly effective in urban environ­
ments, where small shipments can be grouped into a single delivery route (Silva et al., 2023; 
Hu et al., 2023).
Van sharing builds on the sharing economy concept by enabling multiple businesses or 
drivers to use a single vehicle for deliveries. This approach is especially beneficial in dense 
urban areas, where optimizing vehicle use can significantly reduce congestion and parking 
issues (Silva et al., 2023).
Crowdshipping leverages individuals (often non-professional couriers) to deliver parcels 
using their own transportation methods. This innovative solution taps into the gig economy 
and reduces costs, while offering flexible delivery options, especially for last-mile logistics 
(Yu et al., 2023; Di Puglia Pugliese et al., 2023; Silva et al., 2023).
Integrating public and freight transportation (Rzesny-Cieplinska, 2023), including prac­
tices like cargo hitching (using passenger transit systems such as buses, trams, or subways 
for freight transport), maximizes the use of existing public infrastructure. This approach 
reduces the need for dedicated delivery vehicles in crowded urban areas, easing congestion 
and lowering emissions (Gong et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Ghaderi et al., 2022).
Synchromodality takes collaborative logistics to the next level by allowing real-time 
switching between different transportation modes–such as road, rail, and water–based on 
current conditions, cost, and sustainability goals. This flexible approach optimizes resource 
utilization and minimizes delays (Bayliss et al., 2023).
Through these collaborative strategies, CL4.0 addresses the challenges of urban freight 
transportation, fostering more sustainable, efficient, and adaptive logistics systems. By 
embracing collaboration, stakeholders can overcome the complexities of last-mile delivery 
while contributing to a greener urban environment.
3.3.4  ICT
The ICT driving CL4.0 play a crucial role in transforming urban freight operations into 
smarter, more efficient, and sustainable systems. These technologies enable seamless inte­
gration, real-time decision-making, and advanced optimization to address the challenges of 
urban logistics (Su et al., 2024; Athanasopoulos et al. 2024).

●
Internet of Things (IoT): IoT connects physical devices–such as vehicles, sensors, and 
delivery points–to the digital world. In city logistics, IoT enables real-time tracking of 
goods, monitoring of vehicle conditions, and dynamic routing adjustments based on 
traffic or weather conditions (Liu et al., 2024). This connectivity enhances supply chain 
transparency and supports predictive maintenance for vehicles and equipment.
 
●
Artificial Intelligence (AI): AI provides advanced algorithms and machine learning 
techniques to optimize logistics operations. AI is instrumental in route optimization, de­
mand forecasting, and autonomous vehicle control. By analyzing vast amounts of data, 
AI can predict delivery times, optimize fleet performance, and even automate ware­
house operations.
 
●
Digital Twin: A digital twin is a virtual replica of physical logistics systems, such as 
warehouses, delivery networks, or urban areas. It enables simulation, testing, and op­
timization of logistics processes without disrupting real-world operations. In City Lo­
gistics 4.0, digital twins are used to evaluate the impact of new delivery strategies or 
technologies before deployment (Adreani et al., 2024; Yancho et al., 2024; Schnieder, 
2024; Abdo Abouelrous & Zhang, 2023) .
 
●
Cloud Computing: Cloud technology provides scalable and flexible computing power 
for managing large volumes of logistics data. It supports real-time data sharing and col­
laboration among stakeholders, enabling seamless coordination across the supply chain. 
Cloud-based platforms also facilitate integration of multiple services, such as inventory 
management, transportation planning, and customer communication (Gutierrez-Franco 
et al., 2021; Bányai and Illés, 2018).
 
●
Blockchain: Blockchain ensures transparency and security in logistics operations. By 
creating an immutable record of transactions, it enhances trust among stakeholders. In 
city logistics, blockchain is used to verify delivery records, improve supply chain trace­
ability, and enable secure payment systems. This technology is particularly valuable for 
managing shared logistics platforms and collaborative networks.
 
●
Big Data: The massive amounts of data generated by IoT devices, delivery operations, 
and customer interactions require advanced tools for analysis. Big data analytics helps 
identify patterns, optimize processes, and predict trends. It is essential for understanding 
consumer behavior, managing demand, and reducing inefficiencies in urban logistics 
(Pan et al., 2021; Silva et al., 2023).
 
●
Data Analytics: Advanced data analytics goes beyond raw data processing by provid­
ing actionable insights through sophisticated techniques like predictive and prescriptive 
analytics. In City Logistics 4.0, data analytics supports decision-making by identifying 
optimal delivery routes, forecasting demand surges, and evaluating the performance of 
logistics operations (Leung et al., 2022; Paricio & Lopez-Carmona, 2019).

3.3.5  Key trends in core areas
The classification of optimization papers in the field of CL4.0 highlights key trends and 
intersections among the four core areas. Each area addresses distinct aspects of city logistics 
while also exhibiting significant overlap, underscoring the complexity and interconnected 
nature of these challenges.
 
●
Distribution facility network: this area primarily addresses the challenges of facility 
location, multi-echelon networks, and last-mile delivery optimization. Key studies focus 
on the design and operation of UDCs, satellites, and lockers, aiming to reduce costs, 
delivery times, and environmental impacts. These efforts highlight the critical need to 
optimize the interplay between facility design and transportation planning. Scientific 
approaches to determining the placement of logistics facilities examine factors such 
as accessibility, costs, environmental impact, and proximity to customers or suppliers. 
Methods employed include multi-criteria decision-making (Sopha et al., 2018), what-if 
analysis (Rabe et al., 2021), and trade-off evaluations (Mommens & Macharis 2014), 
as well as optimization models that balance costs, service quality, and sustainability 
while addressing challenges like traffic congestion and resource constraints. Specific 
optimization problems, such as location-allocation problems (Almeida Guimarães et 
al., 2021; Roozkhosh & Farimani, 2023) and location-routing problems (Mommens & 
Macharis, 2014; Liu et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2012; Mancini, 2013; Castillo et al., 
2024), integrate facility location with transportation issues to minimize costs and bal­
ance service levels under constraints like delivery times and vehicle capacities. Fur­
thermore, VRPs and their extensions tackle last-mile delivery challenges by balancing 
demand and reducing environmental impact. These studies address a variety of features, 
including: vehicle capacity and time windows (Wang et al., 2024; Dell’Amico et al., 
2023); multi-echelon routing (Stodola & Kutej, 2024; Ramirez-Villamil et al., 2023; 
Sluijk et al., 2023; Bayliss et al., 2023); truck-and-trailer configurations (Derigs et al., 
2013; Mancini, 2013); green logistics considerations (Kahalimoghadam et al., 2024; 
Xu et al., 2022); delivery options, such as lockers or home delivery (Grabenschweiger 
et al., 2021; Dumez et al., 2021; Saker et al., 2023); uncertainty in demand or delivery 
conditions (Torres et al., 2022; Bányai & Illés, 2018).

(Roselan et al., 2024), such as crowdshipping, freight-sharing networks (cargo-hitching)

trips, costs, and environmental impact. Freight collaboration and multimodal solutions 
often intersect with ICT, as advanced digital platforms are required to enable real-time

hand, managing freight-shared networks (Gong et al., 2023) is focused on strict sched­
uling design (Mourad et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2023) and VRPs with synchronisation 
constraints (Huang et al., 2020; Bayliss et al., 2023; Hu et al. 2023).

3.3.6  Intersections among core areas
The four core areas of city logistics are not isolated; rather, they exhibit significant intersec­
tions, reflecting the interconnected nature of challenges and solutions in this field. These 
intersections highlight opportunities for integrating concepts and technologies to address 
complex city logistics problems more holistically. Below, we explore some key overlaps 
between the core areas.
Distribution Facility Network and Low-emission/Hi-Tech Vehicles: research often com­
bines facility planning with vehicle routing to optimize the deployment of electric vehicles 
or drones. For example, the strategic placement of charging stations for e-vehicles or drone 
hubs depends heavily on facility network design. Studies on multi-echelon routing systems 
frequently integrate low-emission vehicles to support urban sustainability goals.
Collaborative Logistics and ICT: real-time platforms powered by ICT are essential in 
fostering collaboration, enabling solutions such as crowdshipping or shared vehicle fleets. 
Technologies like blockchain and digital twins play crucial roles in ensuring transparency, 
efficiency, and reliability. Multi-stakeholder optimization, supported by AI and IoT, is a 
recurring theme in this area, driving coordinated and efficient logistics operations.
Low-emission/Hi-Tech Vehicles and ICT: the use of drones, e-vehicles, and autonomous 
robots in city logistics depends heavily on ICT-driven solutions. Real-time data, IoT-enabled 
vehicle monitoring, and AI-based algorithms for dynamic routing highlight the strong syn­
ergy between these areas. Predictive analytics and sensor technologies further enhance the 
adaptability and efficiency of logistics operations involving advanced vehicle technologies.
Distribution Facility Network and Collaborative Logistics: shared use of city logistics 
facilities, such as lockers and urban warehouses, represents a growing trend in optimizing 
resources and reducing redundancy. These collaborative approaches align with resource-
sharing models, which are extensively studied within the framework of collaborative logis­
tics to maximize efficiency and sustainability.
The intersections between the different areas also lead to considering the optimization 
problems described earlier, which are more representative for each area, in a transversal 
manner (see Table 8). In fact, many articles of practical interest need to address optimization 
problems in CL4.0 in a multidisciplinary way. This aspect is of fundamental importance for 
tackling the mathematical challenges of the CL4.0 ecosystem. From the solving perspec­
tive, as introduced in Tables 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7, heuristic, math-heuristic, and metaheuristic 
techniques are fundamental tools for tackling CL4.0 complex optimization problems, espe­
cially when exact solutions are impractical due to the size or complexity of the problem. 
By way of example, but not limited to, the most flexible and used approaches are related to: 
genetic algorithms (Shramenko et al., 2024; Papaioannou et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2019; Cui 
et al. 2023), simulated annealing (Yu et al., 2023; Yu et al. 2022), particle swarm optimiza­
tion (Roozkhosh & Farimani, 2023), neighbourhood search (Wang et al., 2024; Mulumba 
et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2023). Additional methods are based on ad-hoc heuristics based 
on problem decomposition (Ghaderi et al., 2022; Grabenschweiger et al., 2021; Liu et al.,

3.4  Reconceptualizing city logistics: research implications and new definition of 
City Logistics 4.0
The literature review highlighted three thematic areas, or points of view, that the introduc­
tion of 4.0-type technologies in the city logistics field tends to profoundly modify: (1) the 
logistics field; (2) the technological one and 3) the sustainability one (see Fig. 6).
From the point of view of research in the logistics field, the introduction of 4.0-type 
technologies in turn translates into three main themes: (1) City Logistics 4.0 has introduced 
new means of transporting goods, such as autonomous delivery vehicles (ADVs), electric 
vehicles, and UAVs (Janjevic et al., 2021; Moshref-Javadi & Winkenbach, 2021), as well 
as the possibility of combining passenger and freight delivery options. This makes possible 
new and more flexible transportation options, with improved performance for the end user 
(Bogataj et al., 2017; Sanaullah et al., 2021; Correia et al. 2022); (2) the capacity for data 
analysis and simulation provided by technologies such as artificial intelligence and digital 
twins enables more precise and reliable planning, making it possible to manage systems 
previously considered excessive in size and complexity (Liu et al., 2021; Bi & Ye, 2021; 
Human et al. (2023). Additionally, increasingly complex optimization problems can now be 
addressed thanks to the growing availability of computational power.; (3) 4.0 technologies, 
in particular those useful for the exchange of data and information, such as the IoT, allow 
the effective integration of different carriers, in order to increase the efficiency of the overall 
Fig. 6  The City Logistics 4.0 concept under the logistics, sustainability and 4.0-type technology 
perspectives
 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
logistics system (Islam, 2014; Alkaabi, 2014; Gutierrez-Franco et al. 2021). From the point 
of view of scholars interested in the development of technologies, there are several charac­
teristics of generation 4.0 tools that are particularly relevant in the city logistics field. These 
include the ability to automate some expensive activities for human operators (Hoffmann & 
Prause, 2018) or the possibility of carrying out analyzes of quantities of data that cannot be 
managed by humans (Gutierrez-Franco et al., 2021). In many cases, however, it seems that 
the role of 4.0 technologies is to complement and enhance human capabilities rather than 
replace them (Cassandras, 2016). Several authors underline the importance of communica­
tion technologies or forms of combined man–machine action such as those enabled by aug­
mented reality (Miao & Lan, 2021). Finally, our analysis highlights the importance that the 
literature examined gives to the concept of sustainability (e.g., Meng et al., 2020; Arvidsson 
et al., 2016). The performance of city logistics systems is often measured using the "triple 
bottom line" (economic, social and environmental sustainability), highlighting how a purely 
efficient vision of freight transportation tends to be outdated (Khan et al., 2021).
Overall the results of our analysis allow to introduce a new definition of City Logistics 
4.0 (or Urban Logistics 4.0): City logistics 4.0 refers to the application of advanced digital 
technologies, such as Internet of Things (IoT), Artificial Intelligence (AI), and Big Data, 
to design and operate freight transportation systems in urban areas. These technologies 
enable the development of more flexible and complex logistics systems that enhance human 
capabilities and make city logistics more sustainable.
4  Conclusions and future trends
This paper provides a comprehensive contribution to understanding the complexities and 
challenges of CL4.0. First, it integrates management-related studies and design approaches 
that guide the implementation of CL4.0 solutions in real-world contexts. Second, it maps the 
fragmented literature, identifying three primary disciplinary perspectives: logistics, tech­
nology, and sustainability. Third, a novel definition of CL4.0 has been proposed, reflect­
ing recent advancements in the field. Lastly, the study highlights future research directions 
emerging from a systematic literature review. From a methodological perspective, this work 
employs the PRISMA framework and adopts a mixed approach combining qualitative and 
quantitative methods. The qualitative evaluation involved reviewing papers to ensure rel­
evance and deepen understanding of the three perspectives, while the quantitative analysis 
used Biblioshiny to explore bibliometric trends, such as co-word mapping, co-authorship 
networks, and thematic evolution. The systematic review reveals critical gaps and opportu­
nities for future research in different areas, as follows.
Logistics perspective. The systematic review of the literature on the topic of "City Logis­
tics 4.0" reveals several gaps and areas for further research. By highlighting the emergence 
of more integrated and flexible logistics systems, the literature also expresses the need for 
more in-depth research on these aspects (Sanaullah et al., 2021; Correia et al., 2021) . The 
availability of new 4.0 technologies drives the search for more efficient solutions, meth­
ods and models that fully exploit their potential (Liu et al., 2021). The analysis highlights 
the need for research on technologies such as ADVs, electric vehicles, and UAVs (Reul et 
al., 2021). Besides, it is evident that certain aspects of city logistics, specifically first-mile 
reverse logistics (FM) and the simultaneous management of last-mile and first-mile reverse 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
logistics, have not been deeply investigated (Gutierrez-Franco et al., 2021). The design of 
auxiliary network services and logistics-relevant sites, such as depots, recharging stations, 
and automated stations for collection and delivery, also requires further exploration (Savels­
bergh & Van Woensel, 2016).
The identified research directions include the integration of last-mile, first-mile reverse 
logistics, and their simultaneous management, considering the potential short-, medium-, 
and long-term effects on supply chain flows. The impact of changing urban environments 
on logistics policies and regulations, and the need for effective solutions to fragmented 
logistics processes are additional areas for future research. A focus on the integration of 
sustainability aspects and the development of holistic visions through integrated research 
projects is also necessary.
Technology perspective. Among the results of our review, we can include guidelines for 
the development of technology, which in addition to aiming to relieve human operators of 
the heaviest and most dangerous tasks (Sharma et al., 2020), must also be oriented towards 
a more harmonious relationship between man and machine (Brusselaers & Mommens, 
2022). Information management also deserves great attention and more studies are needed 
on the subject of privacy protection and IT security (Makhdoom et al., 2019; Dziatkovskii 
et al. 2022). In conclusion, this systematic review identifies several areas within CL4.0 that 
require further investigation. By addressing the gaps in the current literature and exploring 
the potential of emerging technologies, researchers can contribute to the advancement of 
knowledge and the development of practical solutions in the field of urban freight and pas­
senger logistics.
Sustainability perspective. The CL4.0 ecosystem represents a transformative shift in 
the way city logistics is conceptualized and executed, with sustainability emerging as a 
critical focal point. The integration of innovative business models—such as horizontal col­
laborations, crowdsourcing-driven shipping (crowdshipping), ridesharing, and Mobility-
as-a-Service (MaaS)—illustrates a growing trend toward optimizing resource utilization 
and minimizing environmental impact. Similarly, enabling technologies like drones, ground 
delivery robots, smart lockers, and connected and automated mobility systems not only 
improve operational efficiency but also align with the push toward greener solutions by 
reducing emissions and traffic congestion. Finally, City environment needs to define new 
and smart ways to manage solid and industrial waste collection, specifically for urban area 
with a strong industrial presence and intermodal freight transport hubs, rather than those 
characterized by the presence of millions of inhabitants (e.g., megacities).
Emerging trends in optimization. Future studies should focus on: (a) routing problems, 
exploring dynamic VRPs that leverage continuous data streams from 4.0 technologies and 
addressing stochastic and multi-echelon variants for same-day deliveries, waste collection, 
and multimodal distribution. Collaboration between vans, autonomous vehicles, and pub­
lic transport networks also presents significant potential; (b) location and network design 
problems, investigating realistic scenarios like dynamic, open, and green location-routing 
problems, as well as intra-route facilities for recharging, refueling, or reloading goods; (c) 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
simulation techniques, developing simulation-optimization methods, agent-based systems, 
and simheuristics to address disruptions, bottlenecks, and network resilience in urban logis­
tics. Digital twins emerge as a promising tool for real-time experimentation and logistics 
planning.
Practical recommendations for urban managers and policymakers. Based on the findings 
of this systematic review, several actionable recommendations can be proposed to enhance 
the design and management of urban logistics systems. Urban managers and policymak­
ers are encouraged to invest in the development of multimodal hubs—for example, dry 
ports, city-edge micro-consolidation centers, and facilities integrating diverse transportation 
modes – to optimize urban freight flows and reduce congestion and emissions. Furthermore, 
it is advisable to promote specific training and education programs aimed at public admin­
istrators and urban planners to support the integration of Industry 4.0 technologies, such as 
IoT, blockchain, and digital twins, into city logistics strategies. These measures would foster 
the creation of more resilient, sustainable, and efficient urban freight ecosystems.
Limitation of the study. While this research provides valuable insights, some limitations 
must be acknowledged. From the topic point of view, policy implications include insights 
into planning transportation systems, managing urban territories, and fostering sustainable 
solutions are not considered, as well as managerial studies from the firms point of view 
that can benefit from focusing on innovative transport modes, advanced data analytics, and 
sustainability-driven strategies for implementing CL4.0 solutions. Indeed, the CL4.0 eco­
system revolves around various stakeholders who can influence its dynamics. From the 
methodology point of view, one limitation lies in the use of only one database for identifying 
relevant papers. Besides, the qualitative assessment may introduce subjective bias, despite 
efforts to minimize it through systematic methods. Additionally, the exclusion of non-Eng­
lish publications may limit the comprehensiveness of the review, potentially overlooking 
valuable contributions from research published in other languages. The focus on logistics, 
technology, and sustainability perspectives may overlook other relevant aspects, such as 
social or policy implications. Finally, the selection criteria and inclusion process, while 
designed to ensure relevance, might have inadvertently excluded some studies that could 
offer complementary insights. Moreover, the rapid evolution of CL4.0 could render some 
findings outdated, underscoring the need for ongoing research to address emerging trends.
In conclusion, technology is transforming city logistics in unprecedented ways. The 
emphasis on technological innovation, however, must not overshadow the principle that 
logistical systems exist to serve people. A sustainable approach to urban logistics reinforces 
this concept, placing human needs at the core. Researchers are called to further develop their 
reflections through a human-centered lens, fostering a transition toward a new paradigm that 
could be termed City Logistics 5.0. In this vision, emerging technologies, including artificial 
intelligence, are tools to be used to enhance human well-being and improve the quality of 
urban life, rather than as ends in themselves.
