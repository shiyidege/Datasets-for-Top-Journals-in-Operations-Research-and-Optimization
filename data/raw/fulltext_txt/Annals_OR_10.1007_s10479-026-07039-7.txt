1‚Äā Introduction
Actuaries have been historically interested in the analysis of mortality rates for the assess¬≠
ment of their financial impact on portfolios of life insurance policies and annuities. The 
availability of granular information about each policyholder or pension scheme member 
provides an opportunity to obtain a more precise estimate of the individual mortality profile.
The papers of Richards (2008, 2012), Richards et al. (2014) and the book of Macdonald 
et al. (2018) outline the advantages of a regression modelling approach based on a paramet¬≠
ric form for the hazard function to characterize the probability distribution of the individual 
future lifetime random variable, especially in comparison to the construction of life tables 
based on the stratification of the mortality experience.
In this paper we follow their approach, and propose to extend these regression models by 
allowing for individual-specific parameters, similar to the random effects in mixed models 
(Laird & Ware, 1982). Those individual-specific parameters allow to capture the additional 
unobserved sources of heterogeneity, while at the same time allow to borrow information 
across subjects.
To enhance the flexibility of the modeling approach, we assume that individual specific 
parameters are random draws from a discrete distribution, whose probability masses jointly 
follow a Dirichlet Process (DP). The DP provides a flexible characterization of a discrete 
probability distribution. Since most random phenomena involve continuous random vari¬≠
ables, in its simplest form, the DP is used to characterize the randomness of the parameters 
indexing continuous probability distributions. The convolution of a random discrete prob¬≠
ability distribution following a DP with a continuous kernel, allows to obtain flexible shapes 
for continuous random variables, capturing features such as multimodality, heavy tails and 
skewness.
When the DP is used to model the discrete distribution of the regression parameters, one 
can obtain a Dependent Dirichlet Process Mixture (DDPM, see Quintana et al., 2020 and 
MacEachern, 2000) model, since the mixture components are now characterized by the 
value of individual covariates. We consider future lifetime distributions in the spirit of the 
Bayesian Nonparametric Nonproportional Hazard (BNNH) model of De Iorio et al. (2009). 
Unlike the BNNH, which considers only a log-Normal distribution for the lifetime distribu¬≠
tion, the approach proposed in this paper finds wider application to the hazard functions 
used in actuarial practice, see Richards (2012). Indeed, the log-Normal distribution shows 
a poor performance when used for the analysis of the mortality profile of pension scheme 
members, since it does not account for the hazard function increase as the individual ages 
throughout the observational period, similar to the simple Cox proportional hazard model 
(Richards, 2008).
The proposed approach, which we define as the Actuarial Dependent Dirichlet Process 
Mixture (ADDPM) model has the following characteristics:
 
‚óŹ
The effect of the covariates can be non-linear in the regression function, nor monotonic 
with respect to the value of the covariates. Therefore, survival functions are allowed to 
cross or to converge, as the data may suggest;
 
‚óŹ
The use of a common distribution for the individual-specific parameters allows to bor¬≠
row information across the units in the sample;
 
‚óŹ
Right censoring and left truncation typical of actuarial datasets can be handled effec¬≠

‚óŹ
The discrete distribution allows to characterize a posteriori groups of units with differ¬≠

for additional analyses of the mortality determinants (Ungolo & Laub, 2025).

reasonableness of the model.

6‚Äā Results
6.1‚Äā Convergence of the MCMC sampler
The data augmentation MCMC sampler used to estimate the posterior distribution outlined 
in Sect.¬†5.3 is run for 20,000 iterations. The first 18,000 draws are discarded to mitigate 
the influence of starting values and to allow the sampler to converge towards a stationary 
distribution. The remaining draws are thinned by retaining every 20th iteration to reduce 
autocorrelation, resulting in a final sample of 100 posterior draws.
The trace plots for each parameter (see Fig.¬†6 in Appendix B) indicate satisfactory con¬≠
vergence of the chains, particularly for the elements of the parameter vector ő≤, which are 
common to all observations, and for the variance of the base distribution ŌÉ2
őī. Similar behav¬≠
iour is observed for the parameter Ōē after approximately 8,000 iterations. Chains initialized 
at different starting values also exhibit good mixing. Furthermore, the marginal densities of 
all parameters are unimodal, providing additional evidence of convergence of the sampler.1
As expected, this is not the case for the mixture-specific parameters őī‚ąó
k and ŌÄk (Fig.¬†7). 
This is due to the label-switching problem inherent to the Bayesian analysis of mixture 
models (Betancourt, 2017; Marin et al., 2005), since the label of each mixture component 
can permute across iterations. Nevertheless, this does not affect the overall interpretability 
of the model, as highlighted in the model analysis, nor the predictive performance of the 
model (Sect.¬†6.3).
6.2‚Äā Model analysis
The histogram of the number of occupied classes across all iterations (see Fig.¬†8 in Appen¬≠
dix B) shows that between 1 and 16 mixture components contain at least one observation. 
The posterior means of the component allocations indicate that the first component includes 
approximately 97.4% of all observations, while the second accounts for about 2.1%. Hence, 
for this dataset, the truncation level set at K = 25 is more than sufficient to approximate the 
distribution of Ti.
Figure¬†3 (left panel) shows the logarithm of the hazard function for a pension scheme 
member aged between 60 and 100, for geo-demographic profiles 0 and 2, and for two benefit 
levels. The plot is obtained from Eqs.¬†(11) and¬†(14), with the numerator and denominator 
separately averaged over the posterior draws.
The resulting hazard function respects the expected ordering across geo-demographic 
profiles: members living in less affluent areas experience higher mortality rates, all else 
being equal (see also Table¬†1 showing the summary statistics of the posterior distribution of 
the model parameters). A similar pattern is observed across benefit levels, with lower earn¬≠
ers exhibiting higher mortality rates.
More interestingly, the inclusion of a random effect for the benefit allows the model to 
capture the decreasing influence of covariates at older ages, a well-documented phenom¬≠
enon in demography known as the compensation law of mortality (Gavrilov & Gavrilova, 
1991), which was also observed in the preliminary analysis of the data in Sect.¬†4. This pat¬≠
tern is illustrated through the analysis of the difference between the log-hazard functions 
corresponding to different geo-demographic profiles, shown in Fig.¬†3 (right panel). It can

7‚Äā Conclusion
This paper contributes to the actuarial literature by introducing the Actuarial Dependent 
Dirichlet Process Mixture (ADDPM) model, which extends traditional survival models by 
modelling the randomness of the regression parameters, and therefore accounts for addi¬≠
tional sources of heterogeneity which may interact with currently observed individual 
covariates.
The Dirichlet Process Mixture (DPM) characterization for the lifetime distribution 
enables the model to capture complex features of the data while retaining the interpretabil¬≠
ity of the covariate effects.
The application of the ADDPM was illustrated through the empirical analysis of the indi¬≠
vidual mortality experience of a UK pension scheme. The resulting fitted model is capable 
of capturing additional features observable in mortality datasets, such as the compensation 
law of mortality due to a reduced influence of individual risk factors at older ages. The simu¬≠
lation study based on the available dataset showed how, although in-sample performance 
is comparable to that of simpler models, the ADDPM yields a substantial improvement in 
predictive accuracy.
The model introduced in this paper is particularly useful in realistic settings where the 
data are generated under a complex probabilistic mechanism that reflects the stylized facts 
of human mortality. In such contexts, the human lifespan may depend on a large number 
of factors (level of education, type of job, health, relationship status), while only a limited 
subset of these risk factors is observable to the researcher.
The Bayesian inferential framework adopted here naturally accommodates the incor¬≠
poration of prior information as available to the researcher. Future work will focus on the 
exploration and development of novel mixture distribution structures beyond the Dirichlet 
Process, see Lijoi and Pr√ľnster (2010) for a preliminary discussion. A complementary direc¬≠
tion of research is the extension of this modelling framework to include a variable selec¬≠
tion procedure, along the lines of the stochastic search approach of George and McCulloch 
(1993). This way, it can be possible to identify and exclude variables that are not statistically 
significant, thereby improving the out-of-sample performance of the model.
Another promising direction for future research involves the development of efficient 
approximation methods for posterior inference, such as Laplace-based techniques (Gelman 
et al., 2013). Such methods would allow practitioners to rapidly evaluate a broader range of 
models for their datasets and feasibly to explore more complex model specifications.
