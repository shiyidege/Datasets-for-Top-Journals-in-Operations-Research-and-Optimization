Abstract
It is well-known that minimally repaired systems are characterized by non-homogeneous
Poisson processes. When the underlying lifetime distribution function or its associated inten-
sity function is completely known, the statistical inference of the cumulative number of
failures/repairs is made through some standard techniques. On the other hand, when the para-
metric form of the lifetime distribution or the intensity function is not known, non-parametric
inference techniques such as non-parametric maximum likelihood estimate (NPMLE) and
kernel-based estimates are employed. Since these techniques are based on the underlying life-
time data, the resulting estimates of the cumulative number of failures/repairs with respect
to the system operation time are discontinuous and troublesome in assessing the reliability
measures such as reliability function and mean time to failure, etc. In this paper, we propose a
novel lifetime analysis of the repairable systems via Daubechies wavelets. More speciﬁcally,
we generalize the seminal Daubechies wavelet estimate by Kuhl and Bhairgond (Winter
Simul Conf Proc 1:562–571, 2000) by combining the ideas on NPMLE and KMBs. In both
simulation experiments and real data analyses, we investigate applicability of our Daubechies
wavelet-based approaches and compare them with the conventional non-parametric methods.
Keywords Repairable systems · Non-homogeneous poisson processes · Non-parametric
inference · Daubechies wavelets · Simulation experiments · Real data analyses

Especially, the repairable system reliability modeling plays a central role in reliability engi-
neering practices (Ascher & Feingold, 1984), because non-repairable systems are maintained
by the so-called perfect repair, which is equivalent to replace the failed units/components by
new ones. That is to say, the perfect repair offers a new unit which is as good as new when the
operating unit fails. On the other hand, the minimal repair only restores the failed unit to its
functioning condition just prior to the failure, where the minimal repair means the age (in the
sense of effectiveness) of the unit will not be changed after a repair activity. Since the seminal
contribution by Barlow and Hunter (1960), a number of researchers have discussed the min-
imal repair models and their mathematical features. Nakagawa and Kowada (1983) used the
failure rate to deﬁne a minimally repaired process, and gave an insight to deal with the mini-
mal repair process mathematically. Aven (1983) considered the optimal replacement policies
under a minimal repair assumption. Brown and Proschan (1983) introduced the imperfect
repair by combining the minimal repair with the perfect repair. Block et al. (1985) extended
the notion of imperfect repair by introducing the time dependency. Kijima (1989) introduced
the general repair and succeeded to represent a wider class of repair activities. Although the
general repair is a well-deﬁned stochastic model to describe the repair activities, its analytical
treatment is rather troublesome. In other words, due to the analytical difﬁculty of the general
repair model, it has not been frequently used in the industrial modeling. Instead, since the
minimal repair approximately represents a variety of repair effects under a plausible assump-
tion, i.e., it only restores the failed unit to the latest functioning condition just prior to the
failure, the research for the minimal repair has been still conducted in some literatures (Aven
& Jensen, 2000; Finkelstein, 2004; Tadj et al., 2011).
In this paper we concern the statistical inference problem of the minimal repair process,
which denotes the cumulative number of failures/minimal repairs. It is well known that the
minimal repair process is given by a non-homogeneous Poisson process (NHPP). Hence, the
statistical inference problem of the minimal repair process is equivalent to one for the NHPPs.
TherearetwostatisticalestimationapproachesfortheNHPPs;parametricestimationandnon-
parametric estimation. For the representative parametric models in the NHPPs, the power law
(Weibull process) model by Duane (1964) and Cox and Lewis model (Cox & Lewis, 1966)
have been often used in the lifetime analysis. Lee and Lee (1978) and Crow (1982) gave
the fundamental results for the power law model; point estimation based on the maximum
likelihood method and the conﬁdence intervals, respectively. Muralidharan et al. (2006, 2007)
derivedthepredictivedistributionofthepowerlawmodelandtheconditionalintervalestimate
for the ratio of intensity parameters, respectively. Hence, when the intensity function is not
known in advance, the parametric estimation does not work in the real data analyses. On the
other hand, it is worth mentioning that the intensity function to describe the ﬁeld lifetime data
cannot be known in many cases. In such cases, any non-parametric estimation will be applied
to the lifetime analysis. Boswell (1966) and Zielinski et al. (1993) considered the constrained
non-parametric maximum likelihood estimate (NPMLE) as an extension of the naive (natural)
estimate of NHPPs. Diggle and Marron (1988) noticed the equivalence of estimations in the
failure rate function and the intensity function, and developed the kernel-based estimation
(KBE) method. Saito et al. (2014); Saito and Dohi (2016) applied both parametric and
non-parametric bootstrapping techniques to estimate the optimal preventive replacement
times with minimal repair, respectively. Apart from the lifetime data analysis of hardware
products/equipment, the NHPPs have been used traditionally to described the software-fault
count processes in software system testing (Lyu, 1996). Since the seminal contribution by
Goel and Okumoto (1979), a number of parametric software reliability growth models based
on NHPPs have been proposed in the literature (Abdel-Ghaly et al., 1986; Achcar et al.,

ﬁnite improvement over the well-known naive intensity function estimation. In this paper,
we demonstrate that the Kuhl and Bhairgond estimate (Kuhl & Bhairgond, 2000) can be

Moreover, we propose an estimator that is more generalized compared to the Kuhl and Bhair-

we introduce two Daubechies wavelet estimators, which can be considered as an extension
and enhancement of the NPMLE and KBEs. So, we provide a generalization framework of

experiments, we compare our Daubechies wavelet approaches with the classical parametric
and non-parametric estimation methods, and investigate the goodness-of-ﬁt throughout the
real data analyses with both hardware failure/repair time data and software fault count data.

In the parametric lifetime analysis, it is common to assume the power law model (Duane,

cases, if we have an enough number of data and can know the real intensity form, it is
possible to infer the failure/repair process with minimal repair accurately. Of course, since
knowing the real intensity function in advance is difﬁcult in general, the parametric method
above has some limitations.

5.2 Discussions
In the simulation experiments, we compare the representative parametric models (Cox &
Lewis, 1966; Duane, 1964), the existing non-parametric methods with four wavelet shrinkage
estimates; NWE, RNWE, NPMLWE and KBWE in terms of goodness-of-ﬁt, where we turn
up the parameters as r = 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 and m = 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, and the support
width of Daubechies wavelet is given by 7. Figure2 shows the dependence of the turning
parameters r and m in the cumulative number of failures/repairs on the operation time, where
each set of methods; (a) NE, RNWE, NWE and RNWE, (b) NPMLE and NPMLWE, and
(c) KBE and KBWE, are compared against the parametric models (Power Law, Cox-Lewis)
and the real intensity function.
For KBWEs, we select the best kernel function with the smallest MAE. In Fig. 2a, we
can see that all RNWE, NWE and Power Law could ﬁt the true mean value function (Real)
very well. It is seen that the goodness-of-ﬁt result by Cox-Lewis model is obviously poor, so
the parametric model selection is essential to get accurate goodness-of-ﬁt result. Also it is
observed that the naive estimate (NE) deviates from the true mean value function signiﬁcantly
and poorly works for the estimation. This is due to the highly discontinuous nature of the
intensity function by NE, so that the estimation error may be caused by the relatively rapid
frequency of ﬂuctuated intensity estimates by NE with respect to the sampling frequency of
equidistant sample points. After applying the wavelet method to NE, we could smooth the
NE by RNWE and NWE, and achieve the high estimation accuracy under the equidistant
sample point spacing. In Fig. 2b, we observe the similar trend for NPMLE. Although the
estimate by NPMLE was relatively smoother than that by NE, NPMLE is still discontinuous
everywhere. Hence, even when NPMLWE could ﬁt the true mean value function better, the
estimation result by NPMLE deviated signiﬁcantly from the true mean value function. In
addition, Fig. 2c shows that the kernel-based estimate, especially, based on Gaussian kernel
function, could ﬁt the true mean value function. However, in the kernel-based approaches, the
wavelet method, KBWE, could not give accurate result than KBE. This is a quite interesting
observation. In fact, the use of kernel functions motivates us to smooth the discontinuous
estimates such as the naive estimate. Wavelet methods can be interpreted as a smoothing
technique. They can improve the roughness of estimators. However, since kernel estimation
itself is a smooth estimator, applying wavelet methods again does not further improve its
smoothness, and therefore, it actually leads to a reduction in estimation performance. That is
to say, the denoising the kernel-based estimates by the Daubechies wavelet does not always
lead to the better estimation results.

gave the minimum MAE = 0.93. This is a natural result. Because when the form of the real

7 Conclusions
In this paper, we have developed novel non-parametric estimation methods based on the
Daubechies wavelets for failure/repair time data arising in the lifetime analysis. More specif-
ically, we have revisited Kuhl and Bhairgond estimate (Kuhl & Bhairgond, 2000), and given
the exact solution based on the naive estimate of the intensity function for NHPP and alterna-
tive methods based on the non-parametric maximum likelihood method by Boswell (1966)
and kernel-based estimates. Throughout simulation experiments, we have compared our
Daubechies wavelet approaches with the classical maximum likelihood estimation for two
parametric models and the existing four non-parametric estimation methods. The results
of the simulation experiments showed that when we completely know the real mean value
function, the power-law model achieved the smallest mean absolute error, which is also the
theoretically optimal result. Our proposed NPMLWE method provided the second best result
even though the real model (intensity function) to describe the failure/repair process is not
known. We also tested the goodness-of-ﬁt performance of 13 parametric models, 4 existing
non-parametric models, and 3 wavelet methods with ﬁve data sets. The experiments with
real data suggested that our proposed wavelet method can achieve the best goodness-of-ﬁt
performance in most cases. We also examined the sensitivity of turning parameter m and
resolution parameter r in the wavelet-based approach, and showed that in most cases, as r
decreases, the mean absolute error, which is a criterion on the goodness-of-ﬁt, decreases. As
a practical suggestion in using the wavelet-based approach in lifetime analysis of repairable
systems for m, we could empirically suggest to set the range of resolution level as m ≥7.
In the future, we will develop a prediction method by means of the wavelet approach. Since
the Daubechies wavelet-based inference is a non-parametric method, it is difﬁcult to assess
the predictive lifetime distribution. Recently, Wu et al. (2021) developed W-SRAT: Wavelet-
based software reliability assessment tool, which is based on the discrete Haar wavelet, and
implemented an idea to continue sequential predictions in the wavelet shrinkage estimation.
The similar idea may be applied to the lifetime analysis with the Daubechies wavelets.
