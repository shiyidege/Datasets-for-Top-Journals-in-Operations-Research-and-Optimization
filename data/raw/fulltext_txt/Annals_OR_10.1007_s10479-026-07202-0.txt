Abstract
In the era of the Paris Agreement and global ambitions to reduce carbon emissions, an
increasing amount of investments must align with net-zero targets to ﬁnance the transition
towards a low-carbon future. This article presents two novel approaches for investors to align
their portfolios with net-zero targets. In this context, we solve two stochastic optimal con-
trol problems. Both problems aim to maximize the expected portfolio log-return while (I)
simultaneously minimizing the time-weighted portfolio carbon footprint, or (II) minimiz-
ing the time-weighted quadratic (relative) deviation from a net-zero target path. For both
problems, we ﬁnd optimal investment strategies. In the ﬁrst problem, the optimal strategy is
obtained from the optimal investment strategy without considering (I) and (II) by a simple
drift adjustment, while the second problem requires a drift and covariance adjustment. Our
work generalizes selected results from other authors. When applied to real-world data, our
strategies signiﬁcantly reduce portfolio emissions while maintaining comparable ﬁnancial
performance to the unadjusted solution. In total, our strategies enable investors to align their
portfolios with self-determined net-zero targets, and they provide a theoretical justiﬁcation
for drift and covariance adjustments for practitioners.

1 Introduction
Limiting global warming requires a large-scale reallocation of capital towards climate-
compatible technologies and activities. The Paris Agreement embodies this ambition: 195
parties committed to "Making ﬁnance ﬂows consistent with a pathway towards low green-
house gas emissions and climate-resilient development.” (United Nations, 2015, Art. 2(1)(c)).
Achieving this alignment of ﬁnancial markets with climate targets has since become a pri-
ority in major economies. For instance, the European Union (EU) intends to reach climate
neutrality by 20501. Supporting this transition entails substantial investment needs2. Accord-
ingly, net-zero investing, that is, allocating capital in alignment with net-zero trajectories, has
emerged as a key concept in climate ﬁnance and the decarbonization of the economy. In the
literature, adding such non-pecuniary considerations to portfolio selection has been exten-
sively studied Pástor et al. (2021); Pedersen et al. (2021); Korn and Nurkanovic (2023);
Chen et al. (2025); Lazanas et al. (2025). This article contributes novel approaches to align
investments with net-zero targets.
Pástor et al. (2021) include Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) characteristics
as non-pecuniary components in the utility within an equilibrium model. Similarly, Pedersen
et al. (2021) derive an ESG-efﬁcient frontier by maximizing the Sharpe ratio subject to ESG
constraints. Continuous-time formulations have been developed by Korn and Nurkanovic
(2023) and Chen et al. (2025), who impose constraints on portfolio sustainability and car-
bon risk exposure, respectively. Lazanas et al. (2025) concern net-zero investing by solving
selected Mean-Variance problems. Our work builds on these sources by including alignment
with net-zero pathways in the investor’s utility in a continuous-time setting.
In particular, we consider a stochastic model for emissions and solve two continuous-time
optimization problems that maximize portfolio log-return while incorporating non-ﬁnancial
elements related to emissions (see Section 3). The problems are solved in Section 4 and
Section 5, giving rise to two novel investment strategies: One enables investors to minimize
the carbon footprint of their portfolio through a drift adjustment; the other enables investors to
follow self-determined net-zero target paths by a covariance and drift adjustment. In Section
6, these strategies are applied to real-world data.
We ﬁnd that both strategies can drastically reduce portfolio emissions, while maintaining
similar ﬁnancial performance as the optimal unadjusted strategy. In addition, we provide a
detailed interpretation of the drift and covariance adjustments that characterize our strategies.
Moreover, we retrieve results from Korn and Nurkanovic (2023) and Chen et al. (2025) as
special cases of our work, when assuming deterministic emissions.

(Lazanas et al., 2025) into portfolio construction. An example beyond the scope of sustainable

three common ways to incorporate non-pecuniary aspects into investment decisions, namely
screening, introducing constraints, or adjusting the objective function. Screening involves
excludingcertaincompaniesfromtheinvestmentuniversebasedonspeciﬁccriteria.Itseffects

to bound certain aspects of a portfolio from above or below. Korn and Nurkanovic (2023)
use constraints to prevent the portfolio ESG score from falling below a speciﬁc minimum
requirement, and Chen et al. (2025) use constraints to bound portfolio carbon risk from above.
Screening and constraints both provide a binary cut-off: Either a company is ﬁltered or not;
either a constraint is fulﬁlled or not. In contrast, adjustments of the objective function enable
a smooth trade-off between two or more goals. In the literature, Dorﬂeitner et al. (2012)
and Jessen (2012) extend Markowitz’s two-dimensional (2D) Mean-Variance framework by
a third dimension (3D) representing sustainability aspects. This tri-criterion approach was
further investigated by Hirschberger et al. (2013); Utz et al. (2014), and Dorﬂeitner and
Nguyen (2017) and is present in recent work by Pedersen et al. (2021), Blitz et al. (2024);
Howard and Chen (2024), and Lazanas et al. (2025).
Similar to the extension of Markowitz’s portfolio problem, one may also adjust the utility
function in standard utility theory to include non-pecuniary aspects into mathematical opti-
mization. For example, Jessen (2012) and Dorﬂeitner and Nguyen (2017) use a weighted
average of a ﬁnancial utility and ESG utility, whereas Pástor et al. (2021) directly include
ESG scores at the level of returns. Further, Escobar-Anel and Jiao (2025) maximize a mul-
tiplicative utility, where the sustainability metric is given by cumulative ESG scores. This
paper employs similar cumulative processes but in the context of cumulative emissions and
cumulative deviations from the investor’s target path.
Most of the above sources assume that ESG ratings are deterministic, meaning their
(future) values are known when setting up the portfolio. However, Berg et al. (2022) show
that ESG scores are inherently noisy and subject to uncertainty, rather than ﬁxed inputs.
To account for this, Avramov et al. (2022) generalize the work of Pástor et al. (2021) to
stochastic ESG ratings. Moreover, Nakagawa et al. (2025) consider stochastic ESG ratings
with an additive utility similar to Jessen (2012) and Dorﬂeitner and Nguyen (2017). In this
paper, we model emissions as a stochastic process, similar to the work by Escobar-Anel and
Jiao (2025), who model ESG scores as a Vasicek process.
In the sub-ﬁeld of net-zero investing, where sustainability aspects are given by emissions,
existingliteratureprimarilyconsidersMean-Varianceoptimization(Blitzetal.,2024;Howard
and Chen, 2024; Lazanas et al., 2025) or non-mathematical approaches (Cano and Katiyar,
2021) to align investments with net-zero pathways. For example, Lazanas et al. (2025) for-
mulate and solve selected Mean-Variance problems with a carbon budget constraint or an
adjusted objective function and Cano and Katiyar (2021) consider three non-mathematical
approaches for aligning portfolios with net-zero pathways; namely, rebalancing the portfolio
towards lower emitters, rebalancing towards “emission improvers”, or a combined approach.
To the best of our knowledge, we are the ﬁrst to approach net-zero investing from the
perspective of expected utility theory in continuous time with an adjusted objective function.
We contribute to existing literature by providing novel closed-form solutions to two stochastic
optimal control problems with stochastic emission processes. Similar to Pástor et al. (2021),
we include emissions as a non-pecuniary aspect in the investor’s utility. We deviate from their
work, by consideing a continuous-time setting as Escobar-Anel and Jiao (2025); Korn and
Nurkanovic (2023), and Chen et al. (2025). Further, when assuming deterministic emissions,

dividend that the asset has to pay to offset its emissions, where h is the continuous cost per
unit of emissions, i.e., continuous carbon price, that sets the size of a necessary carbon offset.
Note, however, that this is not a physical payment, but the investor perceives each asset from
a world in which it pays this continuous offsetting cost. Moreover, the continuous carbon
price h is subjectively set by the investor and choosing an (in some sense) optimal carbon
price is not considered in this paper but left for future research. For example, if h(t) = 0.01,
then the investor demands a ﬁctional continuous dividend of 0.01 for each unit of emissions.
This decreases the ﬁnancial drift of an asset by 0.01 · Ei. For calibrating h, it should be
chosen such that the values hE0, h ˆE are of comparable sizes to ﬁnancial drifts r, μ and
that it corresponds to the continuous payment an investor would expect from a company
to offset their emissions. In summary, the drift adjustment in ˆπ∗makes assets with higher
emissions less attractive, because their perceived drift is reduced by a value proportional to
their emissions.

5.2 Interpretation
Similarly to Section 4, the optimal ˆπ∗is independent of the correlation ρ of the Brownian
motions W P and W E. However, while the solution for (NZP) only differed from the Merton
solution ˆπ M by a drift adjustment, the optimal ˆπ∗for (NZTP) contains two adjustments:
1. An adjustment of the ﬁnancial drift μ by h · (N −E0)( ˆE −1E0).
2. A rank-1 modiﬁcation of the log-covariance matrix σPσ T
P by h ·( ˆE −1E0)( ˆE −1E0)T .
Surprisingly, neither of these adjustments depends directly on the difference of the risky asset
emissions ˆE from the target N. However, one may rewrite the drift adjustment to include
this difference:
(N −E0)( ˆE −1E0) = (N −E0)21 −(N −E0)(1N −ˆE).
(14)
Generally, a positive (negative) drift adjustment makes asset i more (less) attractive to the
investor, as it corresponds to increasing (decreasing) the ﬁnancial drift μ. Therefore, if E0 is
far from the target (i.e., (N −E0)2 is large), investing in the bank account is not attractive for
the investor, as it would push the portfolio emissions away from N. In (14), this is entailed
by the fact that each risky asset is made more attractive by the same positive drift adjustment
(N −E0)2. Additionally, the drift of asset i is further modiﬁed by:
i) A positive value if N lies between E0 and Ei, i.e. (N −E0)(N −Ei) ≤0. This increases
the attractiveness of asset i. Intuitively, this is because the investor can achieve the target
emissions N by combining long positions in the asset i and the bank account. This long-
long position is more desirable than a long-short position (i.e., a leveraged position), as
leverage carries more risk.
ii) A negative value if N lies above or below both of E0 and Ei, i.e. (N −E0)(N −Ei) ≥0.
This decreases the attractiveness of asset i. Intuitively, this is because when restricted to
using asset i and the bank account, the investor can only achieve the target emissions N
by going long in one asset and short in the other. Due to increased risk, this long-short
combination (i.e., leveraged position) is less desirable than the long-long combination
from above.
123

Annals of Operations Research
Note that if N ≥E0, the drift adjustment of asset i increases linearly with Ei. Contrary
to intuition, this would imply that asset i becomes inﬁnitely attractive to the investor as its
emissions increase to inﬁnity. However, this is not the case as the adjustment of the covariance
matrix counteracts the increasing drift adjustment. Here, the perceived volatility of asset i
increases quadratically in |Ei −E0|, making high-emitting assets less attractive. Additionally,
one may wonder, why the drift adjustment only considers the relation of N to E0 and E0
to Ei, not the cross-relations between assets i and j. The answer, too, lies in the covariance
adjustment, which we dissect in the following.
Generally, a positive (negative) modiﬁcation to the i, j-th entry of the covariance matrix
σPσ T
P makes the asset pair i, j less (more) attractive to the investor because if i = j, the risk
of asset i is increased (decreased), making the asset less (more) attractive, and if i ̸= j, the
correlation of assets i and j is increased (decreased), making the pair less (more) attractive
for exploiting diversiﬁcation effects. With this knowledge in mind, we are ready to interpret
the adjustment of σPσ T
P by the outer product ( ˆE −1E0)( ˆE −1E0)T . The perceived log-
covariance of asset-pair (i, j) is decreased if the product ( ˆEi −E0)( ˆE j −E0) is (I) negative,
and increased if the product is (II) positive. In case (I), the bank account emissions lie between
those of asset i and j. Similar to the interpretation of the drift adjustment, the investor can
offset (in terms of emissions) a short position in the bank account by entering a long-long
position in both assets. This gives the investor more freedom to set up the portfolio, making
the asset pair i, j more attractive. In case (II), the bank account emissions lie above or below
both asset emissions. This restricts the investor’s freedom to build the portfolio as the bank
account emissions can only be offset by entering a long-short position in assets i and j,
making this asset pair less attractive. In the case i = j, the volatility of asset i is increased
by (Ei −E0)2 ≥0, making the asset less attractive the more its emissions differ from those
of the bank account.
Lastly, before the drift and covariance adjustments are applied to the Merton solution, they
are multiplied by the investor’s preferences h. This means that if h increases (decreases), all
adjustments explained above are ampliﬁed (damped) because with increased (decreased)
preferences for emissions, the investor is willing to take on more (less) aggressive positions
to achieve the goal of staying close to N. One limitation of (NZTP) is that we did not ﬁnd a
similar economic interpretation for the preference parameter h as in (NZP). This increases
the difﬁculty of choosing an appropriate h in practice. In Section 6, we provide suitable
values for h for the considered dataset.
Equation (12) shows that the investor combines the same three portfolios, as in Section 4:
(1) The bank account; (2) the Merton solution in the ﬁnancial world ˆπ M; and (3) the Merton
solution in the emission world ˆπ E where ﬁnancial drifts are replaced by emissions. This
time, the short position in the emission market is not only multiplied by h but also by the
stochastic factor η(t)(E ˆπ M (t)−N(t)), which is proportional to the difference of the Merton
emissions from the target. The further the Merton emissions E ˆπ M lie above the target N and
the larger h, the more the investor shorts the emission market.
Further, Equation (13) shows that the portfolio emissions under ˆπ∗are a convex combi-
nation of the Merton emissions E ˆπ M and N. Therefore, E ˆπ∗will always lie closer to the
target than the Merton emissions. The convex coefﬁcient η depends on the preferences h
and emission-Sharpe ratio ∥w∥2. If the preferences for emissions h increase (decrease), the
coefﬁcient η moves closer to zero (one) and E ˆπ∗moves closer to the target N (the Merton
emissions E ˆπ M ). Increasing (decreasing) emissions per unit of ﬁnancial risk ∥w∥2 have the
same effect, as a higher ∥w∥2 makes it easier to control portfolio emissions without sacriﬁcing
signiﬁcant ﬁnancial performance.

6.1 A Vasicek model for emissions
For the analysis in this paper, we assume the following model:

Two limitations of our analysis are the following: First, we ﬁtted and evaluated the strate-
gies on the same time frame. A more robust approach would be to calibrate and test the
strategies on different time periods. However, as we only had access to data spanning the past
6 years, we stuck to the ﬁrst approach. Therefore, the above analysis is more of a proof-of-
concept than a comprehensive and robust analysis. Second, since our strategies only consider
current emissions, they generally favor assets with lower emission levels, regardless of their
intentions to reduce emissions. For example, between two companies with the same value
chain, our approach would favor the smaller company (lower absolute emissions), even if
the larger company has more initiatives in place to reduce its emissions. To account for this,
our models can be cast in terms of normalized emissions, carbon intensity, or other suitable
metrics as long as these metrics can be modeled by suitable continuous time processes.
Moreover, an investor following our strategies decarbonizes his portfolio by decreasing
investments in high-emission ﬁrms. If enough investors adopt our strategies, this will drive
out high-emitting companies and lead to decarbonization of the economy. As our model is
not a full equilibrium model, it does not capture this trend directly.
In total, this paper contributes to reducing physical CO2 emissions by providing easy-to-
implement strategies for investors to align their portfolios with self-deﬁned net-zero targets.
This makes net-zero investing more accessible and, in the long term, pushes high-emitting
companies out of the market.

7 Conclusion
In this article, we solve the stochastic optimal control problems (NZP) and (NZTP), aiming to
minimize the portfolio carbon footprint and stay close to a net-zero target path N, respectively,
while maximizing the expected portfolio log-return. Compared to the unadjusted Merton
solution, the optimal investment strategy in (NZP) is achieved by a simple drift adjustment,
and in (NZTP) by a drift and covariance adjustment. We outline the following three use cases
of our strategies:
1. Choosing h(t) ≡b in (NZP), minimizes the overall carbon budget of the portfolio.
Controlling the carbon budget was also the main focus in previous work by Bolton et al.
(2022, 2024), and Lazanas et al. (2025). Our work contributes by providing a strategy
that balances maximizing returns with minimizing carbon budget (as opposed to a hard
constraint).
2. Choosing h(t) = ˜hN(t) according to (9) in (NZP), allows investors to follow their target
path N in a probabilistic sense but introduces time-inconsistency.
3. Choosing h(t) = b exp(−β(T −t))
N(t)2
in (NZTP) enables the investor to follow the net-zero
target path N in a time consistent way, while having numerous possibilities to adjust
overall preferences b and time preferences β.
In our empirical analysis, we show that our strategies can drastically reduce portfolio
emissions while maintaining comparable ﬁnancial performance. Applying our strategies to
real-world data only requires estimates of the drift and volatility of assets, as well as their
current emissions. Since detailed analyses or forecasts are not necessary, our strategies enable
more investors to adopt net-zero strategies. This shifts more capital towards low-emitting
companies and ultimately drives high-emitting companies out of the market. Hence, our
results support the transition to a low-carbon future.
Future research might explore the inclusion of a constraint ˆπ ≥0 to disallow portfolio
emissions from being artiﬁcially decreased by short positions in high-emitting assets. More-
over, the constraint 1T ˆπ ≤1 may be added to exclude short positions in the bank account,
i.e., disallowing leveraged strategies. Other meaningful generalizations could include, allow-
ing the emission-preference h to depend on the level of the investor’s wealth, considering
a broader class of utility functions, such as power or exponential utility, or dropping the
additive structure of stochastic optimal control problems and working with a multiplicative
setting, as in Escobar-Anel and Jiao (2025).
In summary, this paper offers a straightforward and practical approach for investors to align
their investments with self-determined net-zero targets. Our strategies drastically reduce port-
folio emissions while maintaining ﬁnancial performance. To apply the strategies in practice,
only current emissions data and estimates of stock returns and volatility are needed, making
the goal of aligning investments with net-zero targets broadly accessible to investors.
