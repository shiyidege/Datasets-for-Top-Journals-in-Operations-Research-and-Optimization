Abstract
Inthisstudy,weanalyzetherenegingbehaviorofstrategiccustomersinaMarkovianqueueing
system with a single server which is subject to breakdowns. In the event of a breakdown,
the server is replaced with a secondary backup unit which serves at a diminished service
speed. The restoration of the primary unit is possible only when the system is empty and
through a repair process. Reneging is possible and the customers may decide to abandon
the system at any time. To focus on the effect of reneging, we also consider the case where
reneging is prohibited and the customers decide exclusively, whether to join or balk. We also
consider the case where the repair duration is instantaneous. For these models, we derive
the equilibrium strategies for the customers and evaluate the performance of the system
under both scenarios. Moreover, we discuss the effects of the server repairing procedure and
present numerical experiments that reveal key differences in the customer strategic behavior.
We identify cases where reneging is beneﬁcial in terms of social welfare, as well as cases
where prohibiting reneging ensures greater welfare.
Keywords Queueing · Strategic customers · Reneging · Server breakdown and repair
1 Introduction
While waiting for their service, customers may be tempted to abandon their position in the
queue if they observe a slower server has replaced the one they were initially waiting for.
This is particularly true for strategic customers who are standing far from the start of their
service. However, any such decision to renege is far from trivial since it can be inﬂuenced not
only by the current state of the system but also by their anticipation of how other customers
will react. Consider for example a machine that relies on an internet connection. A sudden
malfunction on the system’s server may cause the machine to operate at a much slower pace
and it might need to be cleared out of any customer petition in order to be repaired. Customers
who use regularly the machine and are aware of its status, may start considering reneging if,
for example, a malfunction occurs while they stand at the end of the queue.

In this paper, we present a general Markovian model that captures these features and we
analyze the impact of reneging behavior on system performance. The main objective of this
paper is twofold: First, we model the decisions of a customer who is currently in the queue,
waiting for service, and in the event of a breakdown, the server is replaced temporarily by
a secondary unit, which is known to be less effective. We ask, how a customer’s decision to
renege would be inﬂuenced by both, the available information and the actions of the others.
Secondly, we examine how much the system’s performance would be affected in case the
reneging option was not available and in case there is a signiﬁcant delay in the repair duration.
Could the difference be high enough to compensate for the potential loss of customers due
to the reneging option being allowed? To address these questions, we use a game-theoretic
approach to derive the equilibrium behavior of the customers under both scenarios where
reneging is allowed or prohibited.
In a typical Markovian observable single-server queue, the customers, upon arrival on the
system, observe its state and by using this information, they decide whether to join or to
balk. Those who would initially decide to join, have no incentive to renege at a later stage
since there are no other exogenous factors that may affect their waiting time. However, in the
event of a server breakdown, vacation, or replacement, the waiting time is directly affected,
leading customers to potentially reconsider their decision to stay.
While it may appear unrealistic to prohibit customers from reneging, most of the relevant
literature has adopted this practice as a convention, due to the complexities that are associated
with modeling reneging decisions. Indeed, for a tagged customer who decides whether to
renege or stay, it becomes necessary to consider the future decisions of all other present
customers, often resulting in an intractable problem.
One way to facilitate the study of strategic reneging behavior is by making simpliﬁcations
in the model’s structural assumptions. In this direction, it was only recently, in the study of
Economou et al. (2022) that the reneging behavior of strategic customers was incorporated
into queueing models involving server breakdowns or vacations.
Following this path, the paper’s main goal is to identify the potential beneﬁts that might
stemfromallowingstrategiccustomerstorenegeinservicesystemswithworkingbreakdowns
and repairs under a server replacement policy. Additionally, we aim to investigate the impact
of the repair time on the system’s performance under the customer’s equilibrium strategy.
The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows.
1. A Markovian system with a server replacement policy. To study the impact of reneg-
ing as well as the effect of repair duration, we consider different scenarios, regarding
whether reneging is allowed or not and whether the repair process is instantaneous or
not. Combining these scenarios would result in four different models that we analyze in
detail.
2. Derivation of the equilibrium strategies. We fully analyze the customer strategic behavior
for the four cases mentioned above. In particular, we derive the equilibrium strategies
of the customers, and we calculate the steady state distribution under equilibrium. We
ﬁnd that the equilibrium strategy is of threshold type and we fully characterize these
thresholds.
3. Effect of reneging. We establish that allowing customers to renege, will result in higher
joining thresholds, compared to the cases where reneging is prohibited. We also derive
the equilibrium throughput and social welfare for all the aforementioned models, and
present several interesting results regarding their comparisons.
4. Quantitative results. We conduct several numerical experiments to further explore the
impact of reneging and repair time duration. These experiments provide additional

2 Literature review
From a methodological standpoint, this study belongs to the ﬁeld of research of strategic
customer behavior in queueing systems. The central notion is that the customers are active
agents who make decisions, inﬂuenced by the potential actions of others and by the available
information which results in a game among them.
These ideas were ﬁrst explored in the pioneering works of Naor (1969) and Edelson &
Hilderbrand (1975), who studied the join-or-balk dilemma in the M/M/1 system. In partic-
ular, Naor (1969) studied the strategic customer behavior and the associated social welfare
and proﬁt maximization problems for an informational scenario in which the customers are
informed about the queue length before making their decisions, while, Edelson & Hilder-
brand (1975) considered its unobservable counterpart. Since then, research on strategic
customer behavior in queueing systems has expanded signiﬁcantly and especially in recent
years. The monographs of Hassin and Haviv (2003), Stidham (2009) and Hassin (2016)
present overviews of the main methodological approaches and the fundamental references
in this area while an extensive survey is provided in the book of Hassin (2016).
Our work is strongly related to the part of this literature that deals with the strategic
behavior of customers in Markovian queues with removable servers. There is a fair amount
of models that can be classiﬁed as such, including queueing systems with (working or non-
working, forced or strategic) vacations, systems with breakdowns and repairs, systems with
setup times, etc.
From a performance evaluation point of view, these models have been studied extensively,
due to their strong applicable nature in communication systems, computer networks, and
manufacturing systems, and this analysis goes back to Yechiali and Naor (1971) and Yechiali
(1973). A detailed survey can be found in the book of Tian and Zhang (2006).
However, the study of those models from an economic perspective is a new endeavor.
The ﬁrst study on customer strategic behavior for this family of systems was conducted in
Burnetas and Economou (2007) for the M/M/1 system with exponential setup times under
four information scenarios, according to whether the customers are informed or not about
the queue length and/or the status of the server (serving or on vacation) upon their arrivals.

Economou and Kanta (2008) initiated the study of this family of models by considering

exponentially distributed duration and under two information scenarios (fully observable and
almost observable). This study was complemented by Li et al. (2014), who considered the
almost unobservable and fully unobservable information levels. The general case, where the
server alternates between two working environments (with different service rates) was treated
in Zhang et al. (2013) (fully observable and fully unobservable cases) and in Yu et al. (2017)
(almost observable and almost unobservable cases).
All of the aforementioned studies are grounded on the central premise that reneging is
not permitted for the customers once they have entered, and the analysis of their strategic
behavior is focused solely on the join-balk dilemma.
As mentioned in the introduction, the ﬁrst study to analyze the effects of reneging behavior
in queueing models with vacations and strategic customers was conducted in Economou et
al. (2022). The authors considered a queueing system with non-working server vacations and
explored two distinct cases: one where customers are allowed to renege and one where they
are prohibited from doing so. Speciﬁcally, they considered an M/M/1 model with a server
that alternates between working and non-working/vacation periods of exponential duration.
During the server’s vacation periods, service would cease while new arrivals could still join
the queue.
A key feature of Economou et al. (2022), which helped with the comparison of the two
versions, is the introduction of the value of reneging (VoR) which measures the signiﬁcance of
the reneging option. The ﬂuid version of this model was studied in Logothetis et al. (2023).
These papers reveal that balking alone is insufﬁcient to improve the performance of the
system. In particular, the authors showed that allowing customers to renege has a signiﬁcant
impact on the system’s performance under the equilibrium strategy.
The general Markovian model with server replacement which we consider in this paper,
appears to be unexplored in the literature. In addition, we have found no other studies on
strategic reneging behavior for this family of queueing systems, except for the two papers
mentioned earlier. In particular, the general literature on the study of reneging behavior in
strategic queueing is quite limited. The most signiﬁcant results from such studies can be found
on Hassin and Haviv (2003) (paragraphs 5.1, 5.2) and Hassin (2016) (paragraphs 2.1.4, 4.2.1
and 11.4).

3 Model description
Baseline model: Consider a service system where the service consists of two servers: a regular
(primary) unit with an exponential service time X1 ∼Exp(μ1) and a secondary (backup)
unit with an exponential service time X0 ∼Exp(μ0). Only one server can be operational at
a time, and we assume that 0 < μ0 < μ1, reﬂecting that the primary unit serves at a faster
rate than the secondary unit.
The primary unit is subject to random breakdowns/malfunctions, which occur according
to a Poisson distribution with rate θ. We use the r.v. Sb to represent the amount of time the
system stays online. In case of a breakdown, the system implements the following server
replacement policy: the primary unit is instantly replaced with the secondary unit, which
continues to serve customers at a reduced service speed. When the system becomes empty,
the repair process begins which restores the primary unit.
Additionally, there could be a delay in restoring the primary server’s operation. To analyze
the impact of this delay, we differentiate between two models: one where the delay duration is
assumedtobenegligible,andonewherethedelaydurationfollowsanexponentialdistribution
with a rate ζ. The latter will be denoted as the D model. During this duration, customers
continue to accumulate without receiving any service. The system can be fully described by
the continuous time Markov chain (CTMC) {(N(t), I(t)), t ≥0}, representing the state of
the system. Speciﬁcally, N(t) denotes the number of customers present within the system at
time t, and I(t) represents the state of the server at that time. The server state is categorized
as either the secondary unit (denoted by 0), primary unit (denoted by 1), or repair mode
(denoted by r), which is applicable only in the D model. We note that for the D model, the
server might breakdown even in the idle state.
Strategic nature of customers: Customers arrive into the system under a Poison process
with rate λ and seek service under the ﬁrst-come-ﬁrst-served (FCFS) discipline. Upon arrival,
a customer, as an individual optimizer and by acting rationally, makes a joining decision by
assessing the potential service reward against the corresponding expected waiting cost, which
accumulates until the completion of his/her service. More importantly, these quantities are
inﬂuenced by the behavior of the other customers, as well as, the available information that
is conveyed by the system’s manager.
Information availability: The operational and economic parameters constitute common
knowledge for the arriving customers. In addition, they are informed about the actual number
of customers who are present in the system (N(t)) and the exact state of the server (I(t)),
and this information is continuously updated.
Strategic actions: An arriving customer evaluates the available information to decision
regarding whether to join the system or not. This is a one-time decision that is also inﬂuenced
by the actions of the other customers. In the case of joining, the customer is continuously
facing the dilemma of whether to stay in the system or to renege. To focus on the impact
of the reneging decision, we consider a modiﬁed version of the model, denoted as the N R
model on which the customers can only decide whether to join or to balk, upon arrival, and
reneging is prohibited. Conversely, in the R model, customers may renege from the system
at any time without receiving service.
Expected utility: The key quantity for the analysis is the expected net beneﬁt of a tagged
customer who is offered (or who currently occupies) position n, and the server is in state
i. We denote this quantity by U M

assume that each customer receives a reward of K units only in cases where his/her service

4 Equilibrium analysis
4.1 R case
We begin the analysis by studying the R model, where reneging is allowed and the delay
for repair is negligible. An important feature of this model is that customer’s decisions to
join or balk upon arrival, are identical to the decisions regarding staying or reneging of those
customers who are already in the system.
To see this, consider a tagged customer who is already in the system and constantly facing
the dilemma of staying or reneging. Since customers have complete information about the
system’s state and due to the memoryless property of the exponential distribution, the only
moments where reneging may be advantageous are when the system’s state changes due to an
arrival, service completion, or server replacement. However, since the queueing discipline is
FCFS, there is no incentive for the customer to renege when other customers arrive or when
a service is completed, as this increases their expected net beneﬁt.
Therefore, the only possible instances where a customer might choose to renege are when
the primary server is replaced with the secondary unit. Note that at this exact instance, the
tagged customer’s decision is completely identical to that of a customer who has just arrived
and must decide whether to join or balk. Both customers have the same incentives to stay or
renege/join or balk, as they are making their decisions based on the same information and
the same queueing discipline (FCFS).
This symmetry between the decisions of customers who have just arrived and are offered
a position and those who are already in the system, occupying that same position, is a
fundamental property of the R model, and it helps to simplify the analysis by allowing us to
consider only one type of tagged customer rather than two. Speciﬁcally, we can conclude that
the equilibrium strategy is a pair of thresholds (nR

5 The impact of repair mode
In the previous subsections, we analyzed the reneging and non-reneging cases of the model
under the assumption that the repair time, when the system becomes empty, is instantaneous.
In this Section, we study a more general case, where the repair time has a random duration,
represented by an exponential distribution with parameter ζ. Thus, if the primary server is
replaced and all customers are served, the system undergoes through a repair mode before
the primary server is restored. To represent this mode, the stochastic process I(t) has an
additional state, r. We consider again two cases, regarding whether customers are allowed to
renege or not and we study the respective models, namely DR, DN R, separately. We start
with the reneging case.
5.1 DR model
When the server is on repair mode, customers continue to accumulate. However, the service
process is ofﬂine during the repair mode. An arriving customer who observes the server in
that state will decide initially whether to join or balk and if he/she joins, will continuously
face the dilemma of whether to stay or renege. Similar to the R model, reneging will not be
beneﬁcial when the backup server is in operation (i.e. when I(t) = 0). Finally, only in case
of an empty system, the server can enter the repair state. Thus, incoming customers who ﬁnd
the server under repair would only have to decide whether to join or balk. If they join, there
is no incentive for them to renege since the only event that may occur is the restoration of
the primary server.

7 Discussion–extensions
In this paper, we analyzed the reneging behavior of strategic customers in a queueing system
with a single server which is subject to random breakdowns. In such an event, the server is
replaced by a back up unit which operates with a diminished service rate. The primary unit can
be restored only when the system is empty. For this system, we determined the equilibrium
strategies and evaluated the performance of the system. Besides the theoretical contribution,
the main component of our analysis is the consideration and the quantiﬁcation of the impact
of the reneging behavior. The numerical comparisons between the models where reneging
is allowed with those models where reneging is prohibited showed that there are signiﬁcant
differences between these policies in terms of social welfare and throughput.
Regarding future research directions, we want ﬁrst to point out the importance of studying
the value of reneging in queueing systems with strategic customers by comparing the closed
and open reneging policies. In this regard, our study follows methodologically the work of
Economou et al. (2022) who initiated this research direction. Our work can be extended to
several natural directions. First of all, the speciﬁc replacement policy we studied in this paper
has no yet been considered in the literature of queueing games. In particular, it dictates that
the repair procedure for the primary unit will initiate only when the system is empty. This
assumption, however, could be viewed as a strategic decision made by the manager rather than
a strict constraint. Investigating alternative policies, such as initiating repairs immediately
upon failure, especially in cases of quick repairs, could provide valuable insights. Depending
on the queue length, it may be optimal to stop service and start repairs immediately after the
breakdown.
One possible extension in this direction could be to consider a more general
K−replacement policy. According to that policy, the repair process could potentially ini-
tiate when there are at most K customers in the system. Our model then corresponds to the
special case where K = 0. Studying this generalized version of our model may reveal several
interesting results.
Additionally, the question of when it is optimal to start the repair in the context of this
model is interesting on its own, even in the case where customers are not strategic.
Finally, recall that throughout our study we assumed that customers are fully informed
regarding the state of the system. In particular, they continuously observe the state of the
server I(t), and queue length N(t). One interesting future research direction would be to
consider a partial observable informational scheme where the customers only observe I(t).
