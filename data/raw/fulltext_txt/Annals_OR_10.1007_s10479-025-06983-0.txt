Abstract
Recent advancements in technology have broadened the capabilities of unmanned aerial 
vehicles (UAVs) in urban areas. However, the increase in UAV activities in low-altitude 
airspace brings substantial risks to people, structures, and other air traffic, necessitating 
regulated air traffic movements and thorough airspace information services for users. This 
study specifically aims to create and optimize an operational concept for managing UAV 
traffic in cities with high-rise buildings, focusing on Dubai in the United Arab Emirates. 
The concept includes UAV airspace design and operating heights and incorporates a grid 
system aligned with Dubai’s Road networks to avoid collisions. To maximize profitability 
and manage traffic load efficiently, two mathematical models are developed, and a genetic 
algorithm is proposed for solving large-scale instances. The concept’s efficacy in regulat­
ing UAV air traffic and its impact on service performance due to airspace constraints are 
validated through simulation using Dubai’s maps and building heights. This research, a 
collaboration involving international and national academic, industrial, and governmental 
entities, addresses the gap in research on UAV operations in densely populated urban areas 
and lays the foundation for further enhancements in UAV routing models.

1  Introduction
By 2025, the commercial unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) fleet is expected to exceed 800,000 
units in the United States (US), a growth of over 70% from 2020 (Federal Aviation Admin­
istration, 2021). The European Union, in comparison, anticipates a fleet of over 400,000 
units to be used for civilian applications in 2050, with an estimated market value exceeding 
10 billion euros annually by 2050 (SESAR Joint Undertaking, 2016). Due to advances in 
sensing and computing technologies and smart city transformation, UAVs, or drones, are 
gradually becoming a part of everyday life (Liu & Sengupta, 2017; Vattapparamban et al., 
2016). For instance, UAVs have been used by police departments in the US, Canada, the 
United Kingdom, and Germany to monitor areas and detect crime (Finn & Wright, 2012). 
Another example is the use of UAVs called Parcelcopter by DHL to deliver parcels to the 
North Sea in Germany (Kitjacharoenchai et al., 2019).
As the demand for UAVs grows, air traffic congestion in low-altitude airspace will inevi­
tably increase, posing a high risk to humans, properties, and other aircraft (Lundberg et al., 
2018a, 2018b, 2018c). Consequently, designing an infrastructure and an intelligent trans­
portation system to accommodate the increased influx safely is imperative (Fontanella et al., 
2017; Liu & Sengupta, 2017). One of the research initiatives in this field was the introduc­
tion of a conceptual framework for UAV traffic management (UTM) by the National Aero­
nautics and Space Administration (NASA) in 2013. The UTM system expands the scope 
and scale of the existing air traffic management system by the Federal Aviation Administra­
tion (FAA) to enable small UAVs (sUAVs) to integrate into low-altitude airspace and oper­
ate safely (Allouch et al., 2021; Federal Aviation Administration, 2020). Since its inception, 
the UTM concept has seen widespread adoption in Europe, Australia, and a few Asian coun­
tries, including India, Japan, and Singapore (National Aeronautics & Space Administration, 
2020).
Despite the worldwide development of the UTM concept, safe large-scale operations of 
UAV fleets within visual line of sight (VLOS) or beyond visual line of sight (BVLOS) at 
low altitudes are currently unavailable (Kopardekar et al., 2016). Therefore, this study aims 
to propose an operational concept for unmanned aerial traffic in urban cities with high-rise 
buildings. The proposed operational concept defines the operating heights of UAVs and the 
vertical and horizontal separation distances between UAVs and other UAVs and objects. 
The study also aims to develop UAV routing and traffic load balancing models and inte­
grate them with the developed operational concept. Given that this is an NP-hard problem, 
a genetic algorithm (GA) solution approach is proposed. In addition, three other solution 
methods are introduced and compared to the GA method, namely Tabu Search (TS), Simu­
lated Annealing (SA), and Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS). Finally, this 
study integrates the proposed concept and models into a simulation environment in which 
the maps and building heights of the city of Dubai have been integrated.
Therefore, this study introduces an innovative and integrated framework for managing 
UAV traffic in dense urban environments, addressing a critical gap in the literature by unify­
ing four essential components, including an operational concept for airspace, a drone rout­
ing model, a traffic load balancing mechanism, and a simulation-visualization environment, 
into a single, coherent decision-support system. Unlike most existing studies that address 
only isolated aspects of UAV operations such as routing or operational concepts, this work 
delivers a comprehensive solution tailored to the complexity of real-world urban air mobil­

ity. At its foundation is a grid-based operational concept that structures the 3D airspace into 
altitude-separated, directionally defined corridors aligned with urban infrastructure, ensur­
ing safety and scalability in high-rise cities such as Dubai. A routing model is developed 
to assign UAVs to feasible and conflict-free flight paths from origin to destination, which 
can be considered as an extension to the prize-collecting vehicle routing problem with time 
windows (PCVRPTW) (Sakarya et al., 2025) in which two types of demand are considered. 
In addition, a load balancing layer minimizes the maximum number of UAVs traversing 
any airspace segment, promoting fair access and reducing the risk of localized congestion. 
Another contribution lies in the introduction of a tailored GA solution approach that allows 
solving large-sized instances of the routing problem within a reasonable time and with a 
good solution quality. In addition, this algorithm is compared to other existing solution 
algorithms in the literature to confirm its suitability for the studied problem. Complement­
ing these models is a simulation and visualization module that allows for dynamic testing 
of routing strategies, airspace designs, and traffic scenarios, thereby transforming abstract 
optimization results into actionable operational insights.
The practical value of this integrated framework is far-reaching. Urban delivery com­
panies and drone logistics operators can use the routing and load-balancing results to plan 
more efficient and reliable UAV missions, reduce delays due to airspace congestion, and 
enhance customer satisfaction. By integrating these models into route planning software, 
fleet managers can dynamically assign routes that are both regulation-compliant and per­
formance-optimized. For aviation regulators and airspace authorities, the framework offers 
a powerful tool for policy design, scenario evaluation, and capacity planning. It supports 
evidence-based regulation by allowing authorities to simulate the impact of introducing new 
flight corridors, imposing altitude restrictions, or managing peak-time drone demand. Fur­
thermore, the visual simulation capability can assist urban planners and smart city architects 
in evaluating how UAV operations interact with existing infrastructure, supporting long-
term planning and cross-sector coordination. Overall, this study bridges the gap between 
theoretical modeling and practical deployment, contributing not only to academic discourse 
but also to the development of operational, safe, and scalable urban UAV systems for next-
generation logistics and smart mobility applications.
The remainder of the article is organized as follows. Section  2 reviews the relevant 
works. Section 3 defines and formulates the studied problem. Section 4 introduces the pro­
posed operational concept. The model development methodology is presented in Sect. 5. 
This includes model formulation and numerical examples for the routing and traffic load 
balancing models, as well as the GA solution approach for the routing model and the other 
algorithms used to solve it. Section 6 discusses the integration of the operational concept 
with the developed models. The applied value of the work of the proposed concept is dem­
onstrated in Sect. 7 by simulation and visualization of service performance indicators in the 
city grid and in response to grid changes (e.g., new grid routes or new constraints). Sec­
tion 8 discussed some managerial insights. Finally, concluding remarks and future research 
avenues are highlighted in Sect. 9.

2  Literature review
International organizations have proposed global frameworks and international standards 
that aim to harmonize UTM systems and regulate UAV operations. For instance, the Interna­
tional Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) proposed a common UTM framework to facili­
tate the global harmonization of UTM systems (International Civil Aviation Organization 
n.d.). Based on the ICAO regulations, the International Organization for Standardization 
(ISO) developed standards for UAV operational procedures (International Organization for 
Standardization 2019). Similarly, many countries have developed a set of rules and regula­
tions for controlling and operating UAVs safely within the airspace. In addition, several 
initiatives have been developed worldwide to introduce a concept of operations for sUAVs. 
A summary of relevant works is provided in subsequent sections. Regulations of the US, 
France, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), and Japan are summarized in Table 16 in Appen­
dix A.
2.1  NASA concept of operations
NASA developed an operational concept for the UTM research initiative that focuses on 
enabling UAV operations in uncontrolled (Class-G) airspace, with a possible transition 
towards segregated operations within controlled airspace. The research initiative suggests 
five guiding principles to accelerate UAV access to the airspace. The first principle is to use 
technologies to facilitate BVLOS UAV operations. The second principle is to make non-
sensitive information on the operations publicly accessible to ensure transparency. The third 
principle is to hold a diverse inventory of UAVs with varying configurations, capabilities, 
and control mechanisms. The fourth principle is to provide UAV operators with access to 
Class-G airspace with varying degrees of flexibility based on their capabilities and size of 
operation: one (simple worksite inspection) or multiple concurrent operations (large pack­
age delivery services). The last principle is to provide opportunities for new types of mis­
sions in the future (Kopardekar et al., 2016).
NASA proposed four technical capability levels (TCL) in UTM to test different types of 
applications, geographical areas, and risk levels of UAV operations at each TCL. The level 
of complexity of UAV operations increases at each successive TCL (Dukowitz, 2019). A 
summary of the four TCLs is presented in Table 1.
2.2  European concepts of operations
2.2.1  U-Space model
The concept of U-space was introduced in 2016 in Warsaw, Poland, and is a framework 
developed to support UAV operations in all types of missions and operating environments, 
as well as all classes of airspace. It comprises a set of services and procedures that allow 
a large influx of UAVs to access the airspace safely and efficiently. Additionally, it pro­
vides an effective interface between UAVs and manned aviation, air traffic control, and 
relevant authorities (SESAR Joint Undertaking, 2017). A set of U-space services is outlined 
in Table 2.

2.2.2  Risk-based approach
The European Aviation Safety Agency (2015) classifies UAV operations into three catego­
ries based on their risk level and need for authorization. In the open operation category, 
flight authorization is not required, but operations must remain under VLOS within the 
defined limitations and away from restricted areas, such as airports. The specific operation 
category entails a higher risk to people and requires performing a safety risk assessment. 
Operations authorization with specific limitations is required, and mitigation measures must 
be approved by the authorities in advance of the operation. The final category is the certified 
operation category, which has the highest risk level amongst the categories due, for exam­
ple, to kinetic energy considerations or the complexity of the drone in terms of autonomy. 
In addition, multiple certificates are required by organizations providing services such as 
remote piloting or equipment, such as detect-and-avoid.

In Sweden, Lundberg et al. (2018a, 2018b, 2018c) proposed basic interventions to control 
the airspace, which include a detect-and-avoid system to manage conflicts, geofences to 
prohibit UAV operations in restricted areas, and altitude separation layers. The altitude sepa­
ration layer is formed by assigning an altitude step for each angle based on flight direction, 
resulting in 360 altitude steps, and head-on traffic is separated by 51 m vertically. However, 
this concept was limited to cities and areas with low-rise buildings and cannot be applied to 
cities with high-rise buildings. It was subsequently developed further into the UTM CITY 
simulator to visualize abstract as well as temporal constraints that can be set up in an inter­
active visualization (Westberg et al., 2022).
2.3  Vehicle path planning
Path planning entails determining an optimal path for a vehicle to move from an origin 
to a destination. It is central to both manual and automated operations and is employed 
across industries from logistics and public transportation to industrial automation and aerial 
surveying. Path planning for ground vehicles has been studied extensively in the context 
of logistics and delivery problems. A well-known type of problem is the Vehicle Routing 
Problem (VRP), which seeks to optimize the routes of a fleet of vehicles delivering goods or 
services to a set of locations. It comprises a range of objectives, such as minimizing travel 
time, distance, fuel consumption, or cost while accounting for constraints like delivery time 
windows and vehicle capacity (Toth & Vigo, 2014). In urban settings, real-time path plan­
ning may also consider dynamic factors such as traffic congestion, road closures, customer 
demand, or weather conditions (Zhang et al., 2022).
VRP has been a significant area of research and continues to be a fundamental challenge 
in logistics and operations research. Over the decades, a wide variety of VRP variants have 
been developed to reflect real-world complexities (Tan & Yeh, 2021). These include capaci­
tated VRP (Praveen et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2018), VRP with time windows (X. Liu et al., 
2023), multi-depot VRP (Montoya-Torres et al., 2015; Sharma et al., 2018), stochastic VRP 
(Oyola et al., 2017, 2018), green VRP (Asghari & Mirzapour Al-e-hashem, 2021; Garside 
et al., 2024; Moghdani et al., 2021; Park & Chae, 2014), and, more recently, electric VRP 
(Erdelić & Carić, 2019; Kucukoglu et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2021).
Besides traditional vehicles, path planning has also become a key component of autono­
mous systems, where it assists in finding feasible, collision-free routes (Gasparetto et al., 
2015). Within the field of electrical engineering, particularly in industrial control and auto­
mation, path planning is essential for automated guided vehicles, which are widely deployed 
in manufacturing, logistics, and warehouse environments. These systems rely on precise 
navigation algorithms to transport materials, products, and other objects efficiently while 
avoiding stationary and moving obstacles (Ho et al., 2025). Mobile robots used in agri­
culture, mining, military, search and rescue, and healthcare fields also depend heavily on 
path-planning capabilities (Patle et al., 2019). In smart factories, path planning enables the 
coordination of multiple autonomous systems, contributing to increased efficiency, safety, 
and flexibility in production and logistics operations (Nie & Chen, 2024).
Among the most advanced autonomous systems are UAVs, which introduced new chal­
lenges and opportunities in path planning. While the primary objective of path planning

is the same, the operational domains of traditional vehicles and UAVs impose different 
constraints and requirements. Firstly, traditional vehicles move in two-dimensional planar 
surfaces, whereas UAVs operate in three-dimensional airspace, which increases the com­
plexity of their planning and control. Additionally, traditional vehicles typically operate in 
well-structured environments with defined lanes, traffic signals, and relatively predictable 
dynamics. In contrast, UAVs often operate in unstructured or semi-structured environments, 
where obstacles may be unknown, dynamic, or partially observable (Aggarwal & Kumar, 
2020; Gugan & Haque, 2023; Jones et al., 2023). This necessitates the integration of simul­
taneous localization and mapping and real-time obstacle detection into path planning.
Furthermore, unlike traditional vehicles, UAVs are constrained by dynamic factors, 
including limited battery life, maximum thrust, and aerodynamic stability. As a result, UAV 
path planning often involves trajectory optimization methods that consider time and energy 
consumption. Also, traditional vehicles are subject to well-established traffic laws and 
infrastructural constraints. Although drone operations are increasingly regulated by civil 
aviation authorities, the regulatory landscape remains less mature and varies significantly 
across jurisdictions. UAVs must often adhere to altitude restrictions, avoid no-fly zones, and 
maintain line-of-sight with operators (Gugan & Haque, 2023). These legal constraints must 
be encoded within the planning algorithms to ensure regulatory compliance. These differ­
ences between drone and traditional vehicle path planning necessitate specialized planning 
approaches for UAVs.
2.4  UAV routing and management
Extensive research has been conducted to develop mathematical models and heuristic 
approaches to optimize UAV routing for civil applications. Some of these models consid­
ered a single UAV (Bouzid et al., 2017; Ermağan et al., 2022; Guerrero & Bestaoui, 2013; 
Phung et al., 2017; Sundar & Rathinam, 2012, 2014; Xie et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020). On 
the other hand, many studies addressed the routing problem of a fleet of UAVs (Bahabry et 
al., 2019; Chowdhury et al., 2021; Coelho et al., 2017; Dai et al., 2018; Dorling et al., 2016; 
Hong et al., 2018; Khan et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2017; Kyriakakis et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; 
Liu et al., 2014; Luo et al., 2018; Scott et al., 2016; Song et al., 2018; Venkatachalam et al., 
2018; Wen et al., 2016; Zhen et al., 2019).
A common objective function in UAV routing problems is minimizing cost. Sundar and 
Rathinam (2012, 2014) developed a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) model to 
minimize the total cost comprising traveling and fuel costs of a UAV, with the aim of reduc­
ing its fuel consumption. Xie et al. (2019) formulated a cost minimization model to plan 
the path of a UAV performing a coverage mission. Other cost minimization models were 
proposed by Chowdhury et al. (2021), Khan et al. (2021), and Kim et al. (2017). The model 
developed by Chowdhury et al. (2021) aimed at minimizing post-disaster inspection costs, 
whereas Kim et al. (2017) proposed two models to minimize the initial cost of setting up 
drone centers and the operating costs. Khan et al. (2021) proposed a path-planning algo­
rithm that minimizes computational time and transportation cost of UAVs that deliver medi­
cal supplies to emergency locations.
Minimizing distance has also been considered as an objective function in a few studies. 
Phung et al. (2017) minimized the traveling distance of a UAV performing infrastructure 
inspection, taking into account coverage and obstacle avoidance. Two real-world inspection

datasets were used to validate the model. Venkatachalam et al. (2018) considered a two-
stage optimization problem where the first stage determined the routes without uncertain­
ties, and the second stage considered recourse actions due to refueling needs. Accordingly, 
the model was developed to minimize the total traveling distance in the first stage and the 
additional distance from refueling trips in the second stage.
Other objective functions that were considered include minimizing maximum energy 
consumption (Dai et al., 2018; Scott et al., 2016), total time (Bahabry et al., 2019; Guer­
rero & Bestaoui, 2013; Luo et al., 2018; Zhen et al., 2019), and the sum of arrival times 
(Kyriakakis et al., 2021), as well as maximizing covered demands (Hong et al., 2018). 
Dai et al. (2018) and Scott et al. (2016) considered sensing applications in urban areas or 
other areas that are geometrically complex. Bahabry et al. (2019) focused on optimizing the 
routes of UAVs operating in urban areas with buildings of various height levels to ensure 
collision-free navigation. Several studies also proposed multi-objective models (Bouzid et 
al., 2017; Coelho et al., 2017; Dorling et al., 2016; Ermağan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; Liu 
et al., 2014; Song et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2020).
To overcome the challenges associated with solving UAV routing problems, which are 
NP-hard, many heuristic and metaheuristic approaches were proposed in the reviewed 
studies. Some of these approaches include GA algorithms (Dai et al., 2018; Guerrero & 
Bestaoui, 2013; Luo et al., 2018; Scott et al., 2016), k-opt heuristic (Sundar & Rathinam, 
2012, 2014), simulated annealing (Dorling et al., 2016; Hong et al., 2018), and Tabu search 
(Venkatachalam et al., 2018; Zhen et al., 2019). Additionally, some studies implemented 
multi-objective smart pool search (Coelho et al., 2017), multi-objective evolutionary algo­
rithms (Liu et al., 2014; Wen et al., 2016), differential evolution algorithm (Yu et al., 2020), 
hybrid large neighborhood search (Li et al., 2021), discrete particle swarm optimization 
(PSO) (Phung et al., 2017), learn and fly algorithm (Ermağan et al., 2022), and receding 
horizon task assignment heuristic (Song et al., 2018). The remaining studies presented a 
hybrid approach integrating two or more algorithms (Bahabry et al., 2019; Bouzid et al., 
2017; Chowdhury et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2017; Kyriakakis et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2019).
Another variation of the UAV routing problem is the truck-drone system, in which UAVs 
work in collaboration with ground vehicles. The proposed truck-drone systems consist of 
one truck and one drone (Agatz et al., 2018; Ha et al., 2018; Murray & Chu, 2015), one 
truck and multiple drones (Ferrandez et al., 2016; Murray & Raj, 2020; Peng et al., 2019), 
or multiple trucks and multiple drones (Kitjacharoenchai et al., 2019; Schermer et al., 2019).
2.5  Literature summary, research gaps, and contribution
Table 3 presents a summary of the literature. Several operational concepts and regulations 
that support UAV deployment in low-altitude airspace have been proposed, as indicated 
by the comprehensive literature review. However, to the best of the authors’ knowledge, 
the literature lacks a thorough framework for operating and managing unmanned air traffic 
safely in dense urban areas in which buildings have different heights. Additionally, no study 
has integrated an operational concept with UAV routing and with UAV traffic visualization 
based on a case study. Most existing studies remain limited by a lack of real-world context 
and often overlook critical operational challenges, such as traffic load balancing and air­
space integration.

In contrast, this study introduces a comprehensive framework that integrates UAV rout­
ing optimization with spatial visualization in a real-world urban context. Specifically, the 
model is tailored to Dubai, incorporating actual road networks and urban infrastructure to 
simulate practical drone deployment scenarios. This study also takes into consideration the 
practical constraints of Dubai’s urban landscape. For instance, weather effects such as snow 
or high winds are excluded, as Dubai’s climate is typically stable and does not present major 
challenges to UAV operations. Similarly, battery recharging is not modeled, given that the 
urban scope of this study focuses on short-range, intra-city deliveries where energy deple­
tion is not a critical constraint. These exclusions do not compromise the model’s realism 
for the intended operational setting. Another critical innovation is the integration of routing 
with traffic load balancing, which significantly increases the computational complexity of 
the problem.
To address the research gaps, this study contributes to the body of knowledge as follows: 
(1) An operational concept for UAVs operating in urban areas with high-rise buildings is 
developed. (2) UAV routing and traffic load balancing models are formulated to maximize 
total profit and balance traffic load, respectively. (3) A GA-based approach is developed to 
solve large-scale instances of UAV routing problems. Other heuristic solution algorithms 
are proposed and compared with the GA approach. (4) The operational concept is integrated 
with the two models and implemented in a UAV traffic simulation and visualization tool that 
integrates the maps and building heights of the city of Dubai in the UAE.
3  Problem formulation and proposed framework
We consider the problem of managing the routing and allocation of a fleet of Unmanned 
Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) operating in a structured urban airspace. The objective is to design 
a framework that enables safe, conflict-free, and efficient drone operations over a dense 
metropolitan area, while balancing the utilization of shared airspace resources.
This research tries to answer the following research questions:
 
●
How can urban airspace be systematically structured to enable the safe, efficient, and 
scalable operation of UAVs in complex, high-rise urban environments such as Dubai?
 
●
Given the structured airspace, how can UAVs be assigned feasible flight paths from 
origins to destinations in a manner that ensures regulatory compliance and operational 
efficiency?
 
●
How can the load on individual airspace segments be balanced to avoid congestion and 
promote equitable usage of the urban airspace among multiple UAV operators?
To answer these research questions, the proposed solution builds upon three main compo­
nents: (i) a structured operational concept for managing UAV traffic in three-dimensional 
urban airspace, (ii) a drone routing model that assigns each UAV a feasible path from origin 
to destination, and (iii) a traffic load balancing model that ensures fair distribution of UAVs 
across the airspace.

area segmented into a 3D navigable grid. This grid-based airspace is structured to reflect 
ground infrastructure (e.g., road networks) and building layouts, with specific air lanes

The airspace is partitioned into a set of directed air segments, which represent the elemen­
tary building blocks of drone paths.
Each UAV must travel from a given origin (e.g., a take-off depot or hub) to a customer 
location situated somewhere in the grid. Airspace constraints such as no-fly zones, geofenc­
ing, and altitude limitations are embedded in the grid structure and reflected in the genera­
tion of feasible routing alternatives.
A set of N customer requests is considered. Each customer i ∈N\ {0} has two types of 
demand with two different amounts of goods to be delivered: a high-priority demand δi (kg) 
that must be delivered, and a low-priority demand Di (kg) that may be delivered. The cor­
responding revenues per kg are respectively R1i and R2i. Each customer must be visited 
by exactly one UAV from a homogeneous fleet consisting of U drones within a prespecified 
time window [Ti; Ti]. Each UAV u ∈{1, ..., U} has a finite carrying capacity Ku (kg) and 
a fixed cost F u and must be routed through the 3D grid from its origin to the corresponding 
destination.
The airspace is composed of a fixed set of R directed segments (air lanes), indexed 
by r ∈{1, ..., R}. Based on these segments, for each UAV u ∈{1, ..., U} a finite set of 
feasible path alternatives is generated, Au, to travel from one point to another, indexed by 
a = 1, . . . , Au, taking into account airspace design rules and operational constraints. Each 
path is composed of a sequence of adjacent air segments, P a
u , that define the UAV's flight 
corridor. These alternatives allow for flexibility in routing decisions and form the basis for 
further optimization. Based on this airspace design, a variable cost, Cij, between each node 
i and node j in the network is defined.
These airspace segments can be shared by multiple UAVs, but excessive concentration 
of UAV traffic on certain segments can lead to congestion and reduced service performance. 
To avoid this, the optimization framework seeks to balance the load across segments. Spe­
cifically, the model identifies the number of UAVs assigned to each segment (based on their 
selected paths) and aims to minimize the maximum load on any segment.
In addition to the operational concept that defines the airspace, the optimization problem 
involves two layers. First, a drone routing layer in which, for each UAV, one path must be 
selected among a set of feasible alternatives that comply with the operational constraints 
imposed by the 3D grid structure. Second, a traffic load balancing layer that aims to choose 
the solution that, among all feasible routing combinations, minimizes the maximum number 
of UAVs using any single segment, thereby promoting fairness and reducing local conges­
tion. The UAV routing problem studied in this work can be seen as an extension to the 
PCVRPTW (Sakarya et al., 2025) in which two types of demand are considered.
The operational concept defines the structure of the airspace; the routing model ensures 
feasible assignments for all UAVs; and the load balancing model ensures that traffic is dis­
tributed equitably across the network. The details of the operational concept are provided 
in Section 4, while the problem mathematical formulations, including decision variables, 
objective functions, and constraints, are presented in Sects. 5.1 and 5.2.

4  Proposed operational concept
The proposed operational concept defines the airspace design, such as the altitudes at which 
a UAV will operate, as well as the horizontal and vertical separation. It consists of a bi-lay­
ered grid system, which overlaps with the existing road network and above buildings, and 
geofences to restrict flights over some areas. Safety and risk assessments, on the other hand, 
are not included. The concept was developed based on existing regulations, presented in 
Appendix A, and the UTM elements proposed by Lundberg et al. (2018a, 2018b, 2018c). A 
showcase of the proposed concept was developed considering the downtown area of Dubai, 
UAE, which contains high-rise buildings, such as Burj Khalifa, the world’s tallest building, 
standing at 828 m.
The operational concept divides the city into two layers based on building heights. The 
first layer, the red (higher) layer, consists of high-rise buildings with heights greater than or 
equal to 72 m. UAVs in this layer will operate on top of the main road network while main­
taining a separation, or remain well clear (RWC), distance. In addition, one-way or two-way 
directions are considered depending on the road width, taking into account that the required 
road width of the one-way direction is 60 m so that the required horizontal separation from 
the buildings is maintained. The second layer is the yellow (lower) layer, which consists of 
low-rise buildings with heights less than 72 m. In this layer, UAVs can operate on top of 
both the buildings and the existing road network in two-way directions while maintaining 
the RWC distance. The RWC distances in each layer, provided in Table 4, and the maximum 
allowable height for UAV operations, which is 122 m, are suggested by the SAAB group 
and FAA, respectively. Figure 1 illustrates the lower layer, highlighted in yellow, along with 
the directions of UAV operations. The two red areas in Fig. 1 represent the no-fly zones 
defined by the General Civil Aviation Authority (GCAA) of the UAE.

Figure 2 summarizes the methodology followed to develop and solve the UAV routing and 
traffic load balancing optimization problems. To solve the first model (i.e., routing model),

design; it can be used for various airspace designs connecting the customers and grid points, 
as long as the sequences of airspace segments, i.e., the sequences of waypoints, and the 
distances between every two points in the airspace are known. The model is then solved to 
determine the optimal routes of each UAV such that the maximum profit is generated, taking 
into consideration the capacity and endurance constraint of the UAVs and the time windows.

5.1  Drone routing model
A VRP-based MILP model is proposed to identify the optimal sequence of customer visits 
for a fleet of UAVs. The objective of the model is to maximize the profit obtained from 
satisfying two types of demands, normal and high-priority, within a specified time window. 
The former generates more profit but also adds more pressure to fulfill. The model was 
developed mainly for parcel delivery applications; however, it can be extended to a wide 
variety of applications by changing the parameters in the input file. This model can be seen 
as an extension to PCVRPTW in which two types of demand are considered. The following 
assumptions were made during model formulation:
 
●
The flight speed of all UAVs is constant.
 
●
The impact of weather conditions on the flight is ignored.
 
●
The demand at each location can be fully satisfied by a single UAV.
 
●
The model considers a single depot. In the case of different companies (multiple de­
pots), the model needs to be solved for each separately.

5.1.2  Genetic algorithm solution approach
For small-scale instances, the routing problem can be solved using an exact solution 
approach by using a commercial solver such as CPLEX, which can be called from a plat­
form such as MATLAB. However, since the problem is using a formulation based on a VRP 
formulation, which is classified as NP-hard, obtaining the optimal solution for large-scale 
instances is complex and challenging (Toth & Vigo, 2002). Thus, we proposed a GA solu­
tion approach that produces near-optimal solutions to address this challenge. The algorithm 
was adapted from Joseph Kirk (2008) and updated to fit the model detailed in Sect. 5.1.1. 
More specifically, the chromosome generation process was modified, a different set of muta­
tion operators was implemented, and new constraints were incorporated.
5.1.2.1  Chromosome encoding  The first chromosome used in the algorithm encodes the 
route or sequence of customers’ visits. The second chromosome encodes the breakpoints or 
indices that divide the route, defined in the first chromosome, into smaller individual routes. 
Each UAV is allocated to one of the individual routes, and each individual route is visited 
by one UAV only. Figure 3 illustrates an example of the chromosomes used in a scenario 
with a single depot (encoded as {1}), nine customers, and four available UAVs, as well as 
the individual UAV routes determined by the route breakpoints. In this example, the first 
breakpoint is 3, which means that the first UAV visits the first three customers in the route 
{7, 4, 2}, and its full route is {1, 7, 4, 2, 1}. The second breakpoint is 5, which means that the 
second UAV visits the fourth and fifth customers in the route, and its route is {1, 10, 5, 1}.
The size of the first chromosome is constant, which is equal to the total number of cus­
tomers. The size of the second chromosome, however, may change based on the number of 
UAVs used. Its size is equal to 1 less than the number of UAVs deployed, as shown in Fig. 3.

5.1.2.2  GA algorithm  We explain in this section the main features of the GA algorithm that 
is provided in Appendix B. The algorithm has been implemented in MATLAB R2020b.
Population initialization: The algorithm begins by generating an initial population of P 
individuals or feasible solutions that represent the route chromosome. The size of the popu­
lation must be a multiple of the number of mutation operators used in the algorithm, which is 
eight in this study, as will be discussed under Mutation operators. Each route chromosome 
in the population is generated randomly using the MATLAB function randperm. Thereafter, 
the route breakpoints chromosome for each individual is determined by developing a sub­
routine in MATLAB such that the resulting route of each UAV does not violate the capacity, 
time, and endurance constraints. The subroutine will be explained under Algorithm.
Fitness function: After randomly generating the initial population of P routes and deter­
mining their breakpoints, a fitness function is developed to assess the ‘fitness’ of each indi­
vidual. In this study, the fitness is measured in terms of total profit, which is defined in 
the objective function (1) of the model. The algorithm runs for a predefined number of T 
iterations, wherein the P individuals are evaluated in each iteration to determine the ‘fittest’ 
individual that generates the highest profit in the population (rmax) for that iteration.
Following the first iteration, the algorithm compares the current value of rmax to a pre­
defined global optimal value of the objective function (ropt). If rmax > ropt value, the algorithm 
updates ropt to the current rmax value. After each iteration, a new generation of feasible solu­
tions is produced by applying the GA mutation operators, and the fitness evaluation process 
is repeated. If the value of ropt does not improve after a predefined maximum number of iter­
ations (Tmax), a new population is generated randomly. The population continues to evolve 
towards the optimal solution with each iteration until the algorithm completes T iterations.
Mutation operators: Four basic mutation operators are used, and additional operators are 
defined as combinations of the basic operators. Figure 4 demonstrates the basic mutation 
operators. The Flip operator randomly selects two genes (positions) in the chromosome, 
defines a sub-route between them, and then reverses their sequence in the sub-route. The 
Swap operator selects two genes randomly and swaps their positions in the chromosome. 
The Slide operator randomly chooses two genes in the chromosome and shifts the first gene 
to place it after the second gene. The Reverse Slide operator randomly chooses two genes 
in the chromosome and pushes the second gene backward to place it before the first gene. 
Finally, one operator keeps the individual unchanged and reintroduces it without any change 
into the population.
Algorithm: Algorithm B.1, presented in Appendix B, presents the proposed GA approach. 
The algorithm starts with a population of P randomly generated route chromosomes (P = 40). 
The global maximum profit (ropt) is initially set to {−∞}, and the number of iterations to run 
the algorithm is set to T. In each iteration, the algorithm evaluates each of the P individuals 
in terms of profit. Taking one individual at a time, the algorithm calculates the demand load 
and the arrival and departure times for each customer in the route chromosome to determine 
the route breakpoints chromosome. If one of the constraints (i.e., capacity, time, or endur­
ance) is violated, the route cuts off at that point, and the next available UAV is assigned to 
the following customer. It is worth noting that if the demand load of a customer exceeds 
the available capacity of the UAV visiting that customer, the UAV will only fulfill the high-
priority demand, given that it is within the capacity limit; else, the customer will be assigned 
to the next available UAV.
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
Once all the customers in a chromosome are assigned a UAV, the route breakpoints chro­
mosome is defined accordingly. The algorithm then computes the costs and revenues to 
derive the profit and identifies rmax. If rmax for the current iteration is better than ropt, the 
value of ropt is updated to rmax. After each iteration, a new generation of solutions is pro­
duced for the next iteration by running the GA mutation operators. To begin with, the P 
individuals in the current generation are randomly divided into groups of eight (number 
of operators). Thereafter, the fittest individual (i.e., with the highest profit) in each group 
is selected. The eight mutation operators are then applied to the fittest individual of each 
group to generate new individuals that replace the existing individuals in that group. Note 
that one of the eight mutation operators keeps the fittest individual in the group unchanged, 
so it remains in the new group.
The process repeats for the remaining groups until a new generation of solutions of the 
same size P is formed. If the value of ropt does not improve after Tmax = {200}, a new popula­
tion is generated randomly, and the counter is reset to zero. The algorithm continues until it 
completes T iterations. After completing all the iterations, the algorithm identifies the best 
individual that generates the highest total profit and provides the corresponding sequence of 
customer visits for each selected UAV and the satisfied demands as the heuristic solution.

5.1.3  Solution approach: additional algorithms
In this section, we introduce three new algorithms, namely TS, SA, and ALNS, and we com­
pare them with the GA and the exact approach in terms of solution quality and computation 
time.
5.1.3.1  Tabu search  TS is a metaheuristic optimization technique designed to explore com­
plex solution spaces by intelligently navigating local neighbourhoods while avoiding cycles 
and premature convergence. Unlike basic local search methods that may become trapped 
in local optima, TS uses adaptive memory structures, such as a tabu list, to record recently 
visited solutions or attributes and prohibit their immediate reuse. This short-term memory 
helps prevent the search from revisiting previously explored areas, encouraging explora­
tion beyond local optima. The algorithm may also include mechanisms such as aspiration 
criteria to override tabu status and diversification strategies (e.g., shaking) to explore new 
regions of the solution space. TS has been effectively applied to a wide range of combinato­
rial optimization problems, including vehicle routing with time windows (VRPTW) (Zhou 
et al., 2025).
To solve the problem defined in Sect. 5.1, we develop a Tabu Search algorithm. The TS 
heuristic, given in Algorithm D.1, in Appendix D, iteratively explores the neighbourhood of

obtained from deliveries. High-priority deliveries generate a lump-sum revenue R1i if the 
full high-priority demand δi is delivered, and optional low-priority demand deliveries con­

The tabulist.add(move) mechanism records recently applied moves and prevents reversing 
them within a specified tenure unless an aspiration criterion is met (i.e., the move leads to 
a new global best). This mechanism ensures both intensification and diversification in the 
search space. To escape local optima, a shake subroutine procedure, as shown in Algo­

iterations. This procedure injects unserved customers back into the solution in a greedy yet 
randomised manner, promoting diversification and exploration of less-visited regions of 
the solution space. This helps the search escape local optima and encourages exploration of 
new regions in the solution space. TS balances intensification (neighborhood search) with 
diversification (shake) to effectively explore the solution space, delivering both mandatory 
and profitable optional demands under time‐window constraints.
5.1.3.2  Simulated annealing  SA is a well-known stochastic local search metaheuris­
tic inspired by the physical annealing process in metallurgy, where a material is slowly 
cooled to reach a low-energy, stable state. The SA algorithm, as shown in Algorithm E.1, 
Appendix E, iteratively explores the space of feasible route assignments to maximize net 
profit, computed as the difference between revenue and total cost. Each solution comprises 
drone routes that start and end at a central depot, visiting customers with high and/or low-
priority demands while satisfying drone capacity and time window constraints. The algo­
rithm begins with a greedy, revenue-aware initial solution (generated using Algorithm E.2 
(Appendix E)), and progressively modifies it via a neighbor function (given in Algorithm 
E.3 (Appendix E)) that performs random swap or relocation moves between drones. The 
feasibility of each candidate is checked ensuring both demand and time constraints are met. 
The solution quality (i.e. value) is evaluated by applying Eq. (1), which returns total profit. 
A new candidate is accepted if it improves profit or, probabilistically, if it is slightly worse—
controlled by a temperature parameter that cools over iterations. This allows occasional 
uphill moves to escape local optima. The algorithm maintains and returns the best solution 
found throughout the process.
The initial solution subroutine (Algorithm E.2) constructs a feasible starting solution for 
the model by greedily assigning customers to routes based on their value-to-cost ratio. The 
algorithm begins by initializing each drone's route to start and end at the depot and sets the 
corresponding load to zero. It then ranks customers (excluding the depot) in descending 
order using a heuristic score that combines their high- and low-priority revenue potentials 
(R1i + R2i) with the minimum travel cost to or from the depot, promoting customers with 
the highest revenue efficiency. Each customer is then sequentially inserted into the most 
profitable position across all drones. For every drone u, and at each possible insertion point 
within its route, the algorithm evaluates the change in objective value (r) if the customer 
were inserted at that position. It selects the best insertion (if feasible with respect to capac­
ity and time windows) and updates the route and load of the selected drone accordingly. 
The final output is a set of routes—each beginning and ending at the depot—that maximize 
initial profit while satisfying all constraints.
The neighbor subroutine (Algorithm E.3.) is responsible for generating a neighboring 
solution from the current set of drone routes, introducing controlled diversification in the 
search process. It implements two types of local perturbations: customer swap and customer 
relocation, each applied probabilistically. In a swap move, one customer (excluding the 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
depot) is selected at random from each of two distinct drones and exchanged. In a relocation 
move, a customer is removed from one route and inserted into a randomly chosen position 
in another. After applying the move, both affected routes are checked for feasibility, ensur­
ing that time window and drone capacity constraints are satisfied. If the resulting solution 
is feasible, it is returned as the neighbor; otherwise, the original routes are retained. This 
mechanism allows the search to explore alternative route configurations while maintaining 
constraint satisfaction.
5.1.3.3  Adaptive large neighbourhood search  Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search 
(ALNS) is a metaheuristic optimization technique inspired by the idea of intensifying and 
diversifying the search process through large-scale modifications of solutions. Unlike clas­
sical local search methods that explore small, incremental changes, ALNS allows for signifi­
cant perturbations to the current solution by dynamically destroying and repairing parts of it. 
The "adaptive" element comes from its ability to learn over time to identify which removal 
and insertion heuristics are most effective in improving the solution, thus adjusting their 
selection probabilities accordingly. Conceptually, ALNS mimics problem-solving strategies 
where, instead of tweaking one detail at a time, a person might step back, reconfigure large 
portions of a plan, and rebuild it with new insights.
The ALNS algorithm, given in Algorithm F.1 (Appendix F), iteratively refines a solu­
tion. It combines construction, destruction, reinsertion, and local improvement phases to 
explore a wide solution space. It starts by generating an initial feasible solution (defined in 
Algorithms F.2 and F.3 in Appendix F) using a greedy insertion approach. In each iteration, 
it applies a removal phase, where a portion of visited customers is randomly removed, and 
a reinsertion phase, which attempts to reinsert them using a profit-gaining strategy under 
feasibility constraints. The solution is then refined using a local search method (2-opt) on 
each route (Algorithm F.4 in Appendix F). The best solution found throughout all iterations 
is retained and returned.
The initial_solution subroutine (Algorithm F.1 in Appendix F) generates a starting solu­
tion that assigns customers to vehicle routes while respecting feasibility and maximizing 
profit gain per insertion. It initializes empty routes for all vehicles, with depots at the start 
and end. It processes customer nodes sorted by the earliest service time to increase the 
feasibility chances. For each customer, the subroutine tests all insertion positions in each 
vehicle’s route. It selects the best insertion position and vehicle based on the highest profit 
improvement while maintaining feasibility. The demand decision (high or low) is initialized 
using a binary indicator from the input (Y R1). The feasible route subroutine (Algorithm 
F.3) checks whether a given route for a specific vehicle satisfies time window and capacity 
constraints under the current demand decisions. It iterates over each pair of nodes in the 
route to calculate cumulative travel time and vehicle load. If the vehicle arrives too early 
at a customer, it waits until the earliest service time. It then checks whether the arrival time 
exceeds the customer's latest allowable service time (Ti) or if the cumulative load exceeds 
the drone’s capacity (Ku). If either constraint is violated, the route is declared infeasible. 
Otherwise, the route is accepted as valid. The 2-opt local search subroutine (Algorithm F.4 
in Appendix F) improves a given drone route using a local search strategy that explores 
2-opt moves. It evaluates all possible pairs of non-adjacent edges in the route and attempts 
to reverse the nodes between them. Each candidate's reversal is checked for feasibility. If a 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
move increases profit, it is accepted, and the process continues until no further improvement 
is found. This local refinement helps escape small local optima and improves the compact­
ness and efficiency of each route.

5.2  Traffic load balancing model

paths with the same minimum distance, leading to increased congestion on some segments 
of the grid if several drones use that segment as part of their paths. In this section, a model is 
developed to minimize traffic congestion by distributing the traffic load evenly over the grid. 
The model finds all possible routes between any two points on the grid and then allocates 
UAVs to routes with the least traffic density.

estimate whether the maximum capacity would be exceeded in some areas and, therefore, 
potential conflicts would happen. This allows us to consider that capacity and probably use 
alternative solutions by guiding some drones to use less populated segments, even if this 
can be at the expense of longer flight time for some flights, which can be justified by safety 
reasons.

workshops with different stakeholders in charge of developing UTM solutions, urban plan­
ning, public transportation authorities, air traffic control, airports, civil aviation authorities, 
and aviation technology companies in the UAE and Sweden. Many of the needs of these 
stakeholders have been taken into account in the development process, such as the no-fly 
zones, the areas where there is a potential need for passenger pickup, the safety distances 
with the ground and the infrastructure, and the results have been validated by the stakehold­
ers. The proposed UTM framework for Dubai has confirmed the possibility of developing 
UTM solutions for Dubai, in particular, and for other similar cities with high-rise buildings, 
in general, without compromising safety. These results included routing and simulating 
drone movements in the proposed airspace design with drone movements optimized, opti­
mized utilization of airspace, and route flexibility in light of changing airspace constraints 
(e.g., geofences, route interference due to congestion, or unregistered drone activity). Con­
tingency planning has also been included through the possibility of adding geofences and 
rerouting drones around them.

and other emergency contingency strategies may be included. More stakeholder inputs may 
also be considered, such as conducting an acceptability study for the proposed solution by 
taking the opinion of the public into account. Moreover, from a research perspective, con­
sidering recharging stations and their locations in the airspace may add value to the optimi­
zation models since it increases the battery life of drones and allows them to reach further 
customers. In addition, including artificial intelligence-based support to proactively manage 
the airspace for intense drone traffic would be considered in future studies.
8  Managerial insights
The growing adoption of UAVs for commercial, governmental, and emergency services 
in urban settings presents both unprecedented opportunities and operational challenges. 
As cities move toward integrating drones into last-mile logistics, infrastructure inspection, 
emergency response, and surveillance, decision-makers must ensure that UAV operations 
are not only efficient and scalable but also safe, conflict-free, and compatible with urban 
life. The integrated framework proposed in this study offers several managerial insights for 
stakeholders across the UAV and aviation ecosystem.
For delivery and logistics operators, the study provides a clear pathway to deploying 
large-scale drone fleets in cities without risking delays or airspace congestion. By com­
bining optimized routing with load balancing, operators can ensure that deliveries follow 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
efficient, regulation-compliant paths while minimizing overlap with competing flights. This 
is particularly valuable for time-sensitive operations, such as medical supply deliveries or 
food distribution, where reliability is critical. The ability to evaluate multiple routing alter­
natives within structured corridors empowers fleet managers to maintain high service levels, 
even under growing demand.
Airspace regulators and civil aviation authorities benefit from the framework’s ability to 
simulate and evaluate the operational implications of new policies before implementation. 
For instance, the load balancing model can reveal how restricting certain altitude layers 
or opening new corridors would redistribute drone traffic. This provides a foundation for 
adaptive, evidence-based regulations that protect public safety while enabling commercial 
growth. It also helps develop equitable rules for shared airspace usage among multiple ser­
vice providers.
Urban planners and smart city officials can leverage the simulation and visualization 
tools to better understand how UAV operations interact with buildings, roads, and critical 
infrastructure. This supports long-term planning decisions regarding drone ports, vertical 
take-off hubs, and urban airspace zoning, ensuring that future developments are drone-ready.
UTM system developers and technology providers can use the proposed framework as 
a blueprint for integrated drone traffic management systems. Its modular structure allows 
integration into real-time platforms capable of routing, monitoring, and reassigning UAVs 
dynamically, opening the door for autonomous, self-managed drone ecosystems.
Ultimately, the study highlights how proactive planning, combined with integrated 
modeling and simulation, is key to realizing the full potential of drones in tomorrow’s 
urban mobility systems while balancing innovation with safety, efficiency, and regulatory 
readiness.
9  Conclusion
Over the last decade, UAVs have gained significant attention worldwide due to their eco­
nomic, environmental, and societal benefits. The rapidly growing demand for UAV services 
poses a greater risk of collisions on the ground and mid-air due to increased traffic den­
sity, which necessitates developing a traffic management system. Despite previous efforts 
to develop operational concepts that allow UAVs safe access to the airspace, no study has 
clearly defined how to operate UAVs in cities, especially around high-rise buildings. The 
objective of this study was to develop an operational concept for UTM with service opti­
mization deployed in cities with high-rise buildings, considering the UAE context, demon­
strating the applied value in Dubai. This study introduced an operational concept comprising 
airspace design and operating heights of UAVs and a grid concept with two zones based on 
building heights.
The study also developed two mathematical models to maximize the total profit and 
minimize the traffic load on grid segments. Moreover, a GA-based approach was developed 
to solve large-scale instances efficiently, which was validated through a numerical study. 
The results of the numerical study confirmed the effectiveness of the proposed approach 
in obtaining near-optimal solutions within a fraction of the time required by the CPLEX 
solver. The operational concept and two models were implemented in the simulation envi­
ronment UTM CITY with the maps and building heights of the city of Dubai to visualize 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
UAV air traffic. The simulation was used to show the applied value for service operators 
and decision-makers in Dubai who validated the results in multiple workshops. The results 
of the optimization and visualization, based on the case study of Dubai, have confirmed the 
feasibility of developing and using a UTM system in which drone operators and a central 
authority controlling the airspace may safely use the proposed framework in an optimized 
way.
While the results demonstrate the value and operational relevance of the proposed mod­
els, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the framework operates under a static 
and deterministic planning approach, where all flight paths are pre-assigned before opera­
tions begin. This does not account for real-time events such as weather changes, dynamic 
obstacles, or emergency priority missions. As a result, the system may lack adaptability in 
live urban environments where such factors are prevalent. Second, the models assume com­
plete information and do not incorporate uncertainty in demand, UAV availability, or sto­
chastic travel times. This limits the robustness of the solution in handling variable operating 
conditions, especially in large-scale deployments. Another limitation lies in the absence of 
communication and coordination protocols between UAVs. The current framework assumes 
centralized control and does not model distributed decision-making or potential conflicts 
between drones operated by different stakeholders. Lastly, while the simulation and visual­
ization component provide useful qualitative insights, it is primarily designed for pre-oper­
ational evaluation. A more advanced simulation environment, i.e., capable of integrating 
real-time flight data and interacting with live UAV traffic, would enhance the framework’s 
utility for real-time monitoring and decision support.
These limitations suggest several promising directions for future research. Integrating 
real-time data and adaptive routing algorithms could significantly enhance the framework’s 
responsiveness. Developing stochastic and robust optimization models would improve the 
ability to manage uncertainty. Exploring multi-agent coordination mechanisms could sup­
port decentralized UAV ecosystems, while enhancing the simulation module with digital 
twin technologies could allow real-time, high-fidelity evaluations of airspace performance. 
Moreover, recharging stations may be considered in future works. Another area of research 
concerns extending the dashboard to include more KPIs about the different services. This 
would result in a more complex, multidimensional space that would require more advanced 
visualization techniques. However, this could significantly aid operators in planning and 
decision-making. Addressing these challenges will be essential for transitioning from plan­
ning-based UAV traffic management to truly autonomous, scalable, and resilient urban air 
mobility systems. These aspects constitute directions for future research.
