Abstract
Many credit scoring studies suffer from potential sample selection bias due to their exclusive
focus on accepted applicants. To address this issue, previous works have proposed reject
inference (RI) strategies to ﬁrst estimate the repayment ability of rejected applicants and
then incorporate these estimates as additional supervision signals to reﬁne their credit scor-
ing models. However, existing RI methods often fail to effectively account for the local
characteristics and default patterns inherent in diverse applicant groups. Our study intro-
duces a novel ‘divide and conquer’ graph-based RI framework, named SAIL, that effectively
captures inter-individual differences relevant to credit scoring. This framework comprises (1)
Spectral clustering for the categorisation of accepted and rejected applicants, (2) isolation
forests for identifying Anomalies among rejected cases, (3) Iterative relabelling mechanisms
incorporating the Label spreading and self-learning algorithm for relabelling rejected sam-
ples, and (4) binary classiﬁcation for the relabelled dataset. Using a unique loan dataset,
we ﬁnd that our proposed framework signiﬁcantly enhances the efﬁcacy of credit scoring
models and outperforms other popular RI techniques in predicting defaults. Furthermore, our
ablation studies conﬁrm the crucial role of each component of our framework in enhancing
prediction accuracy. Our work provides a comprehensive and adaptative RI framework for
ﬁnancial institutions to improve their loan decision-making and risk management.

1 Introduction
Credit scoring, used to estimate the default risk of applicants, has become a ﬁrmly estab-
lished domain within ﬁnance, broadly recognised as a fundamental component of the lending
decision-making process (Anderson et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). With tech-
nological advancements, the ﬁeld of credit scoring has seen a rapid expansion of methods,
signiﬁcantly enhancing risk assessment and decision support, and fuelling the growth of the
borrowing and lending market (Lessmann et al., 2015; Lu et al., 2022). However, most exist-
ing credit scoring practices rely on samples that systematically exclude rejected applications,
known as the sample selection bias problem in credit scoring (Li et al., 2017; Mancisidor
et al., 2020). Using biased samples to estimate credit scoring models can lead to several prob-
lems. First, it results in biased model parameters, reducing effectiveness when applied to a
broader applicant pool (Bücker et al., 2013; Kozodoi et al., 2020). Second, it may lead to an
underestimation of applicants’ probabilities of default, affecting risk premiums and as a con-
sequence, the lenders’ capacity to make proﬁt (Marshall et al., 2010). Furthermore, sample
selection bias can create additional challenges, such as inefﬁcient utilisation of information
resources and a contraction of the customer base (Verstraeten & Van den Poel, 2005).
To tackle the challenges posed by sample selection bias in credit scoring, reject infer-
ence (RI) has been the object of longstanding interest from academia and industry alike
(Anderson et al., 2023; Banasik et al., 2003; Hand & Adams, 2014; Li et al., 2017). RI
involves a process which infers possible default outcomes for rejected applications and then
integrates these predicted results into credit scoring models. However, traditional RI studies
focus on training a single relabelling model which treats all rejected applicants as homoge-
nous entities, thereby ignoring the distinct default distributions and local characteristics of
diverse applicant groups. In this study, we propose a ‘divide and conquer’ approach, conduct-
ing ﬁner-grained relabelling for heterogeneous rejected groups to reduce the sample bias in
credit scoring models. Our RI framework, named SAIL, consists of four key components: (1)
Spectral clustering identiﬁes homogeneous groups among both accepted and rejected appli-
cants based on similar default tendencies. This differentiation allows for the implementation
of distinct methodologies tailored to each cluster. (2) Isolation forests are then employed to
identify Anomalies among the rejected applicants within each cluster, only retaining those
of the highest quality. Additionally, to avoid introducing noise into subsequent modelling,
rejected cases that are most similar to the accepted samples are also removed. (3) An Iter-
ative relabelling mechanism incorporating the Label spreading and self-learning algorithm
is applied to reclassify the ﬁltered rejects. (4) Finally, binary classiﬁers are utilised to esti-
mate the default probability of the comprehensive relabelled dataset, including both ﬁltered
rejected and accepted applications.
The main contributions of this study are as follows. First, we develop a holistic and
innovativeRIframeworkthatleveragesinformationfromspeciﬁcdatadistributionstoidentify
valuable rejected applications, thereby strengthening the credit scoring model. To the best
of our knowledge, we are the ﬁrst to conduct a ‘divide and conquer’ relabelling strategy
for rejected applications via graph-based techniques, thus enhancing the model’s ability to
accurately identify rejects. Second, through the application of a real-world commercial loan
dataset, our RI framework demonstrates a signiﬁcant improvement in the performance of
traditional credit scoring models, surpassing other well-established RI methods in default
prediction. By implementing further ablation studies, we conﬁrm that each component of
our RI framework, especially the isolation forests used for ﬁltering rejects, plays a vital role
in improving prediction performance. Our proposed framework provides practical insights

2 Literature review
An extensive body of literature explores the development, application, and evaluation of
credit scoring systems in the consumer credit sector (Gunnarsson et al., 2021; Kriebel &
Stitz, 2022; Kumar & Ravi, 2007; Lessmann et al., 2015). These systems allow lenders to
estimate the default risk of applicants based on a set of explanatory variables that encompass
traditionalﬁnancialdata,demographicdata,etc.,withalternativedatasuchastexts(Stevenson
et al., 2021), images (Iyer et al., 2016) and digital footprints (Berg et al., 2020) gradually
attracting increased attention (Chen et al., 2023). However, a signiﬁcant challenge arises
from these models’ reliance on data samples that often omit rejected applicants, leading to
sample selection bias that in turn results in a number of severe problems for credit scoring
models (Mancisidor et al., 2020).
In the context of credit scoring, sample selection bias is essentially treated as a missing
data problem. As summarised by Banasik and Crook (2007), two main types of missing
data mechanisms are relevant to credit scoring, namely missing not at random (MNAR) and
missing at random (MAR).1 To illustrate, let’s deﬁne the outcome label of a loan as y ∈
{0, 1}, where y  1 represents a defaulter and y  0 denotes a non-defaulter. An auxiliary
variable, a ∈{0, 1}, indicates whether an applicant is accepted (a  1) or rejected (a  0)
by the lender. Note that the outcome variable y is only observed when a  1. We also assume
that the feature vector x is observed for each applicant. MNAR occurs when the probability
of acceptance depends on the outcome label y, even when we condition on x. Formally,
we have that P(a  1|x, y) ̸ P(a  1|x). This suggests that the acceptance decision
may be based on some unobserved variables that are not captured in x but that inﬂuence
the outcome y, for example, the ‘general impression’ that the loan ofﬁcer may have of the
applicant (Feelders, 1999). Due to the difﬁculty caused by the unobserved variables, few
empirical studies have investigated MNAR problems in the context of credit scoring (Bucker
et al., 2013; Tian et al., 2018). Another type of missing data mechanism is MAR, which
assumes the probability of acceptance solely depends on the characteristics of applicants
(x) and speciﬁc criteria (e.g., a cut-off value), but not on y. This implies that P(a  1|x,
y)  P(a  1|x). In other words, the probability of default, given a set of model variables,
remains the same regardless of whether a loan applicant is accepted or rejected. In practice,
several industry-speciﬁc factors suggest that missingness in credit risk assessment often leans
towards MAR assumption. Credit scoring systems and reporting mechanisms used by lenders
are typically standardised and rely heavily on structured, observable variables. These systems
usually use quantiﬁable thresholds (e.g., minimum credit score, income level, or debt burden),
1 Missing completely at random (MCAR) is considered another type of missing data mechanism. In this case,
the observed label y is independent of both the value of y and the value of x (i.e. features). It suggests that loan
applications are accepted or rejected completely at random, with no selection mechanism. However, as most
lending institutions have their own acceptance procedures or formal section models, MCAR rarely occurs in
actual lending practice.

Early RI studies used statistical techniques to mitigate the sample selection bias in credit
scoring modelling, with the appropriateness of each method depending on the missing data
mechanism (Banasik & Crook, 2010). For example, Hsia (1978) proposed a crude approach
called ‘deﬁned as bads’ to assign ‘bad’ labels to all rejected cases for a short time. However,
this approach does not consider potentially creditworthy applicants in rejects and intro-
duces too much noise into subsequently generated credit scoring models. Augmentation, also
referred to as re-weighting in practical applications, aims to redress the absence of rejected
data by applying adjusted weights to accepted applicants and then using these weighted
accepts to represent the through-the-door population (Hsia, 1978; Kang et al., 2021). How-
ever, this method is often criticised because the strict loan approval process generally leads
to a high rejection rate, resulting in fewer accepts that therefore cannot represent a more
signiﬁcant number of rejects. To overcome the drawback of augmentation, Zeng and Zhao
(2014) proposed fuzzy augmentation, which creates dual records for each rejected case: one
labelled ‘bad’, weighted by the probability of default, and the other labelled ‘good’, weighted
by the probability of non-default. In conjunction with the original acceptances weighted as
one, these newly generated rejection records are subsequently used to formulate an improved
credit scoring model. Extrapolation, proposed by Hand and Henley (1993), is a frequently-
used RI method that uses a ﬁtted credit scoring model built on accepts to predict the default
outcomes of rejects. Crook and Banasik (2004) ﬁrst examined the effectiveness of extrapo-
lation and showed that extrapolation provides limited predictive performance improvement
compared to acceptance-only-based models. Survival analysis is another popular credit scor-
ing method used to address RI in earlier studies (Li et al., 2017). Notably, Sohn and Shin
(2006) deﬁned that a rejected applicant should be accepted if the lower limit of the 90%
conﬁdence interval for his ‘median life’ exceeds that of accepted applicants in their survival
analysis model. Banasik and Crook (2010) integrated survival analysis with augmentation
and examined how augmentation helps correct survival functions to attempt to address the
RI issue. However, their empirical analyses suggest that survival analysis for RI negatively
affects the predictive performance of credit scoring results. Considering the shortcomings of
these early RI methods, researchers began to turn their attention to more effective algorithms.
Given the breakthrough performance of machine learning models in various domains,
researchers have also explored the potential of these advanced algorithms to solve the RI
problem. Since the default status of rejected applicants is unknown, some studies suggest
that semi-supervised models are more suitable for addressing RI issues (Kang et al., 2021;
Kozodoi et al., 2020).2 In the RI ﬁeld, strong evidence shows that leveraging semi-supervised
learning algorithms to infer the outcomes of rejected cases can signiﬁcantly enhance default
prediction performance. Li et al. (2017) ﬁrst introduced a semi-supervised support vector
machine (S3VM) approach to incorporate rejected data into the credit scoring modelling
process and compared its performance with the industrial benchmarks of LR and support

that homogenously treats all rejected applicants, neglecting the unique default distributions
and local characteristics of these heterogeneous groups. In this paper, we attempt to ﬁll these
research gaps by proposing a comprehensive and adaptative RI framework that conducts
ﬁner-grained relabelling for different rejected groups. We believe that this approach could
provide important implications for broader ﬁnancial and decision-support systems.
3 Methodology
In this study, our proposed SAIL approach, structured into four stages, aims to identify
local characteristics and default patterns inherent within diverse accepted and rejected
populations.3 Speciﬁcally, we ﬁrst deploy spectral clustering to divide all instances into
heterogeneous groups using the pre-processed rejects and the training subset extracted from
the accepts. This allows us to apply customised models for each cluster, thereby (it is hoped)
improving the accuracy and effectiveness of relabelling rejects in subsequent stages, as they
can be adapted to speciﬁc group characteristics. Second, we use isolation forests to identify
and remove anomalies within the rejects of each cluster, helping minimise the impact of out-
liers or spurious data points on subsequent relabelling procedures. We also exclude rejects
most similar to the accepted cases, as labelling such cases may potentially introduce noise
and offer little new information for the scorecards (Kozodoi et al., 2020). Third, the ﬁltered
rejects within each cluster are relabelled by an extended self-learning algorithm that itera-
tively employs label spreading. The iterative nature can intuitively increase the accuracy of
the relabelling process over the iterations, leveraging the strengths of label spreading (using
the structure of the data) and self-learning (using the model’s conﬁdence in its predictions).
In the ﬁnal classiﬁcation phase, we train supervised credit scoring models using the entire
dataset of relabelled information and validate the model prediction results on the test set.

3.1 Spectral clustering between accepts and rejects
Given a set of data points x1, . . . , xn, spectral clustering aims to make the cases within the
same group as similar as possible, while the instances between different groups should be as
distinct as possible (Chen et al., 2012; Li et al., 2020). Unlike representing the relationship
between all pairs of data points xi and x j in the form of similarity si j ≥0, spectral clustering
uses the similarity graph G  (V , E) to show the data distribution (Von Luxburg, 2007).
Thus, the unsupervised clustering problem can be reformulated into partitioning a graph G
such that the edges between different groups have low weights wi j and the edges within the
same group have high weights wi j. In light of the advantage of spectral clustering in that it
does not make assumptions about the data shape and its superior performance on many tasks
(Ng et al., 2001), we ﬁrst utilise spectral clustering to create groups of objects that include
both accepts and rejects. Here, we deﬁne some notations and describe its detailed modelling
process for our RI framework. YL  {y1, . . . , yl} is the actual set for repayment outcomes

3.2 Anomalies detection for rejects
Anomaly detection has been applied to address the RI problem in the studies of Tian et al.
(2018), Xia (2019) and El Annas et al. (2023), demonstrating improved credit scoring perfor-
mance (Aduenko et al., 2018). Thus, in the second stage of our proposed SAIL framework,
we use the isolation forest technique to ﬁlter and remove certain rejected cases in each clus-
ter before assigning labels. This ﬁltering serves two purposes. First, we aim to eliminate
rejected cases that come from the most disparate part of the distribution compared to the
accepts. Removing these outliers would reduce the risk of error propagation and noise since
predictions of a model trained on accepts become less reliable when the distribution of cases
to be predicted are very different from the training data. Second, we also exclude rejects

3.3 Iterative label spreading for relabelling rejects
After obtaining clusters with different default patterns, including accepts and ﬁltered rejects,
we build an iterative relabelling mechanism to infer the outcomes of these rejects in the third
stage of our SAIL framework. This mechanism involves two main algorithms: label spread-
ing and self-learning. Label spreading is a graph-based semi-supervised learning algorithm
proposed by Zhou et al. (2004) that proves effective in solving real-world problems with
missing labels (Kang et al., 2021; Timsina et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2017). It is built on two
prior consistency assumptions: (1) nearby points tend to have the same label, and (2) points
on the same structure (e.g., a cluster) are likely to have the same labels. Considering the
nature of our proposed method, in that it divides data into heterogeneous groups, we try to
use label spreading to relabel our unknown rejects in each group. Besides, we note that most
previous RI studies have used only the features of accepts to relabel rejected samples, ignor-
ing the information that can be extracted from such rejects. To make use of the previously
unused rejected information, we integrate label spreading into the self-learning framework
to establish an iterative relabelling mechanism. Self-learning is a common semi-supervised
learning method that enables the model to learn from itself and make improvements iter-
atively (Maldonado & Paredes, 2010). Algorithms 3 and 4 demonstrate in detail the label
spreading and the iterative relabelling mechanisms for our SAIL framework, respectively.

Algorithm 4 Iterative relabelling mechanism in SAIL framework
For the iterative relabelling mechanism shown in Algorithm 4, we ﬁrst use a speciﬁc label
spreading model trained on accepted cases to predict the pseudo labels of ﬁltered rejects.
Then, we sort all rejects by comparing their predicted probabilities of default and choose the
most conﬁdent ones based on a predeﬁned threshold for subsequent relabelling. Afterwards,
we add the relabelled rejects to the original accepts and repeat the above process until no
rejected cases are left or the modelling stop condition has been satisﬁed. Note that since we
have obtained different groups after the ﬁrst two stages of SAIL, in the third stage, we apply
this iterative relabelling mechanism to each group based on its accepts and ﬁltered rejects,
respectively. For the hyperparameters of the iterative relabelling mechanism, we adopted the
threshold θ  0.5 suggested by Huang et al. (2007), and used p  0 and n  1 to incorporate
as much information from the rejected samples as possible into the relabelling process.
3.4 Binary classification for credit scoring
Once ﬁltered rejects have been relabelled in each cluster, we merge all clusters and obtain
a complete labelled dataset. To verify the effectiveness and superiority of the proposed RI
framework for subsequent credit scoring modelling, we choose ﬁve machine learning models
that are widely used in various ﬁnancial classiﬁcation tasks due to their prediction efﬁcacy
(Katsafados et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2020; Wu & Li, 2021). These models include LR,
extreme gradient boosting (XGB), LightGBM (LGBM), SVM and multilayer perceptron
(MLP). A brief introduction to these models is available in Appendix B of the online sup-
plementary materials.

4.1 Data and variables
In this study, a consumer loan dataset provided by a European commercial bank is employed
to verify the applicability and effectiveness of our proposed RI approach. After excluding
records with apparent errors, the dataset consists of 2102 rejected loans and 3600 accepted
loans with durations ranging from 1 to 37 months, originating between February 2018 and
February 2021.5 All the accepted loans are closed—3527 loans have been fully paid off at
maturity or early, and 73 loans have been charged off.6 Default is deﬁned as 90 days past due
over the 18 months of observation, in alignment with the Basel Accords deﬁnition (Steven-
son et al., 2021). The dataset contains 24 standard credit scoring features for both accepts
and rejects,7 though the repayment status is unavailable for rejected cases. Considering the
regulations around the conﬁdentiality of data disclosure, we made some alterations here and
renamed the continuous variables as VC and the dummy variables as VD. Table 1 shows a
brief description and statistics for these variables.
4.2 Sample division
Complex models, such as XGB and MLP, often suffer from an overﬁtting problem when the
in-sample performance is no longer sufﬁcient to indicate the actual predictive power of the
model. Furthermore, we need to optimise a large set of relevant hyperparameters to ensure
the model achieves optimal performance. Thus, in this study, we used the stratiﬁed random
sampling method to select 60% of the accepted samples as the training set, and the remaining
40% as the test set.8 To eliminate the effects of different data distributions and speed up the
convergence process, all continuous features in training, test and rejected data are normalised
by removing the mean of the training set and also scaled to unit variance using the standard
deviation of the training set.
Once the repayment outcomes of rejects have been inferred through our proposed SAIL
framework, we conducted stratiﬁed ﬁvefold cross-validation9 on the combined dataset of the
training set and the relabelled rejects to establish the credit scoring model. We then evaluated
the model’s prediction performance on the test set. Figure 2 illustrates the entire sample
division process employed in our study.

4.3 Hyperparameter optimisation
To ensure the effectiveness and comparability of our credit scoring models, we implemented
the grid search method to select the optimal hyperparameters for each classiﬁer in this study
(Kang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2020; Stevenson et al., 2021). Given a pre-speciﬁed range
of hyperparameters, the grid search method exhaustively examines all possible combina-
tions of model parameters over the training set and compares the model’s stratiﬁed ﬁvefold
cross-validation performance under each combination. Finally, the optimal combination of
hyperparameters can be determined based on the best prediction performance. Table 2 shows
the hyperparameter search space for the six machine learning methods used in our credit

4.4 Evaluation models
To explore the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed SAIL framework, we compared
its performance with ﬁve other classical and state-of-the-art RI techniques. The accept-only
(Acpt-Only) model is the traditional credit scoring model that relies solely on information
from the accepts group, including goods and bads, and does not include rejected data in its
modelling. The augmentation (RI-AUG) model applies adjusted weights to accepts and uses
these accepts to represent the through-the-door applicants. The fuzzy augmentation (RI-FUZ)
model ﬁrst creates two records for each reject, one labelled ‘bad’ weighted by the probability
of default, and the other labelled ‘good’ weighted by the probability of non-default. Then, a
usual credit scoring model can be estimated using the combination of the relabelled rejects
and the original accepts (with a weight of one). The extrapolation (RI-EXT) model ﬁrst
123

396
Annals of Operations Research (2026) 361:383–412
employs a credit scoring model trained using accepts to predict possible default outcomes of
rejects and then incorporates the relabelled rejects into the subsequent credit scoring model.
The self-learning (RI-SEL) model can be viewed as an iterative RI-EXT, whereby in each
iteration, some of the most likely default and the least likely default rejects predicted by
the credit scoring model trained using accepts are incorporated into the training set of the
next iteration of the credit scoring model, and so on until all rejects have been relabelled.
The RI-BSLS model ﬁrst uses the borderline-synthetic minority oversampling technique
(SMOTE) to oversample the accepts and then predict the pseudo labels for rejects using the
label spreading model. Then, a usual credit scoring model can be estimated.
4.5 Performance metrics
In this study, we consider three widely used metrics to evaluate the prediction quality of
the credit scoring models: the area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve
(AUC), the H-measure, and the Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) statistic (Gunnarsson et al.,
2021; Katsafados et al., 2024; Kriebel & Stitz, 2022; Wu & Li, 2021). The ROC curve is
a graphical representation of a classiﬁcation model’s predictive accuracy over a range of
threshold values. The curve plots the classiﬁer’s true positive rate (i.e., sensitivity) on the
vertical axis and the false positive rate (i.e., 1- speciﬁcity) on the horizontal axis, as the
classiﬁcation threshold varies. The AUC represent the area under the ROC curve, ranging
from 0 to 1, and is used to measure the classiﬁcation ability of a model to distinguish binary
classes. Higher AUC scores imply better out-of-sample classiﬁcation power of the models.
As a coherent alternative to AUC, the H-measure proposed by Hand (2009) is a preferred
performance metric for an imbalanced dataset ranging from 0 to 1 for a random and perfect
classiﬁer, respectively (Fitzpatrick & Mues, 2021; Lessmann et al., 2015). It overcomes the
conceptual shortcomings of the AUC and speciﬁes a severity ratio as input, which allows us
to examine the relative severity of misclassifying a negative class instance when compared
to misclassifying a positive class instance. In this study, we use the default setting of the
severity ratio, which equals the reciprocal of relative class frequency (Chen et al., 2024).
The KS statistic measures the maximum vertical distance between the empirical cumulative
distribution functions of the false positive and true positive rates. Similar to AUC and H-
measure, the KS ranges from 0 to 1, and the higher the KS score, the better the model’s
performance at distinguishing between the negative and positive classes.
In most real-world cases, the labels of rejects are unknown, and the majority of RI studies
evaluate the effectiveness of proposed methods by comparing a scoring model’s performance
based on the accepted test set before and after adding the relabelled rejected cases into the
training data (as detailed above). However, this approach has a limitation: the model’s per-
formance is not evaluated on an unbiased representative sample that includes both accepts
and rejects with actual labels (Banasik et al., 2003). To address this challenge, Kozodoi et al.
(2020) proposed the kickout evaluation measure to reduce the dependence on actual labels
of rejects in evaluations. They suggested that the kickout measure is a more appropriate eval-
uation metric of RI methods than traditional evaluation approaches. To provide a robustness
analysis, we further include the kickout measure to evaluate the performance of models in
our study.
The primary concept of the kickout measure is to compare the set of applications approved
by a scoring model before and after implementing the RI method. Recall that we have data on

To provide a more comprehensive assessment of our proposed method in addressing
the RI problem, we focused on three key components from the SAIL framework: spectral
clustering, anomaly detection using isolation forest, and self-learning, and we employed

machine learning system (our SAIL framework) are removed to measure the impact of each
of these components on overall system performance. To intuitively illustrate the procedure of

the model’s predictive performance. Furthermore, when we explore the capabilities of our
model variants, we ﬁnd that A3 shows the best performance and A1 the second best. Although
these two variants used without self-learning and spectral clustering can achieve good scores
on their own, the former loses the advantage of the accurate use of rejects information, and
the latter loses the advantage of speciﬁc modelling strategies for different data distributions.
Among the variants, we identify that A4 exhibits poor performance due to the fact that it loses
support from both spectral clustering and isolation forests. When we compare and analyse
the results of A1 and A4 together, we ﬁnd that isolation forests for ﬁltering rejects contribute
the most to the SAIL framework, as removing the isolation forests signiﬁcantly decreases the
model’s performance. This result is consistent with the ﬁndings of Xia (2019), demonstrating
the necessity of ﬁltering rejects prior to the relabelling process.
Having highlighted these results, we conducted the Friedman and post-hoc Nemenyi
tests on the prediction performance of different RI approaches considering three evaluation
metrics (AUC, H-measure, and KS) (Kang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2020). Table 8 presents
the Friedman test results for our proposed RI-SAIL with its four variants in the ablation study
and six other benchmark RI models. The results show that the p-values for all metrics are
less than 0.050, and the p-value for AUC is even close to 0.000. These ﬁndings suggest that
we reject the null hypothesis that all credit scoring models perform identically. These models
are signiﬁcantly different at the 1% signiﬁcance level in terms of the AUC and KS statistic
and at the 5% signiﬁcance level in terms of the H-measure.
We then performed the post-hoc Nemenyi test to further explore the differences between
our RI-SAIL and the other ten methods on the three metrics. Figure 6 presents the critical
distance (CD) diagrams of three Nemenyi tests. In a CD ﬁgure, if the same horizontal line
connects different models, these models are not signiﬁcantly different at the 5% signiﬁcance
level. As shown in Fig. 6, the RI-SAIL achieves the best performance among all ﬁgures and
outperforms the RI-SEL, RI-FUZ and RI-SAIL-A4 methods across most metrics. Note that
even though the scores of RI-SAIL are not signiﬁcantly different from those of RI-EXT,
RI-BSLS and RI-AUG in most cases, a considerable improvement in using RI-SAIL in the
evaluation performance can reduce losses from potential defaulters. Interestingly, we also
observed that our model variants in the ablation study such as RI-SAIL-A1 and RI-SAIL-A3
already outperform some benchmark RI approaches, further demonstrating the superiority
of our proposed framework in solving the RI problem. Consequently, we believe that using
the proposed SAIL framework can signiﬁcantly improve prediction results and thus beneﬁt
the ﬁeld of RI.

6 Conclusions
In this paper, we endeavoured to address the RI issue and proposed a graph-based semi-
supervised RI framework that employs the ‘divide and conquer’ strategy to conduct
ﬁner-grained relabelling for different rejected groups. Unlike previous RI studies, our method
focuses on building targeted iterative relabelling mechanisms for the unique default distribu-
tions and local characteristics of heterogeneous groups. The experimental results show that
our proposed RI framework achieves signiﬁcantly superior prediction results compared to the
traditional credit scoring model trained on accepted data only. Our method also outperforms
many of the classical and machine learning RI approaches used in credit scoring over several
classiﬁers and evaluation metrics. Moreover, we conduct ablation studies to demonstrate the
usefulness of each component in our RI framework; in particular, the isolation forest utilised
for ﬁltering rejects derives the most salient gains. Two non-parametric tests further support
these ﬁndings.
Our research not only contributes to the growing body of literature focused on solving
the RI issue in credit scoring modelling but also has far-reaching managerial insights. As
credit and ﬁnancial institutions continue to seek growth and competitive advantage, this work
highlights the importance of understanding the internal distribution of previously rejected
credit application cases. This insight can lead to more informed decision-making for ﬁnancial
institutions, ensuring that potential creditworthy individuals or entities are not overlooked
due to a one-size-ﬁts-all approach. Financial institutions could also offer customised ﬁnancial
solutions catering to differentgroups, which would notonly enhance customer satisfaction but
also lead to improved ﬁnancial performance for the institution itself. As such, our proposed
method could be considered a forward-looking decision-making tool and complementary
methodology for ﬁnancial institutions in many key areas, including (but not limited to)
improving the credit approval process, assisting in the development of internal credit scoring
systems, identifying customer characteristics, and assessing proﬁt and loss balances.
This work inevitably has certain limitations, which also offers avenues for future research.
First, our current SAIL framework is speciﬁcally designed to address the RI challenge under
the assumption of MAR. In the context of credit risk assessment, the MAR assumption is
often justiﬁable due to standardised application and veriﬁcation procedures, reliance on third-
party data sources, and the automated data-driven approval processes commonly adopted in
FinTech lending. However, in practice, some lending decisions may be inﬂuenced by auxiliary
characteristics that are not explicitly captured in the scorecard but still affect the outcome.
This is particularly relevant in certain traditional lending settings, where manual overrides and
subjective judgments are more prevalent, potentially introducing omitted variable bias and
leading to MNAR mechanisms. Therefore, future research could explore adaptations of the
SAIL framework to better accommodate MNAR scenarios. Second, our empirical analysis is
constrained by a relatively small dataset; a larger dataset with comprehensive variables would
support further validation. Furthermore, as our empirical analysis is based on a relatively
low-dimensional dataset, the iterative relabelling mechanism in the SAIL framework may
be less effective for high-dimensional data. Future research could explore the adaptability of
our proposed framework in high-dimensional settings. Finally, since our iterative relabelling
mechanism involves signiﬁcant computational costs, more efﬁcient methods for improving
the prediction performance could be explored. Considering the potential of graph-based
algorithms, future work could explore more advanced models to reﬁne the RI process in
credit scoring further.
