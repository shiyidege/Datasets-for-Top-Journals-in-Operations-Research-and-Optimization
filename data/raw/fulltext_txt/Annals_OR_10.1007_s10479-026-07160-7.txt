Abstract
We examine a game-theoretic model of vessel sharing agreements in industries endowed
with a general class of price functions and classes of convex cost functions. We study the
equilibrium structure thereof—in particular, the existence of a unique equilibrium global
aggregate as well as the existence of a unique equilibrium—and we provide a comparative
statics analysis of consumer welfare with respect to an ordinal measure of concentration of the
industry. We show that the a “high degree" of convexity of the cost functions can generate anti-
competitiveeffects.Inthepresenceoflinearcosts,themodelsatisﬁesaweakaggregativeform
in the sense of aggregative games. By allowing for the nonlinearity of variable cost functions,
we further weaken the aggregative nature of the games considered. We provide a reﬁned
techniquefortreatingthesegamesinwhichboththeequilibriumstructureandthecomparative
statics analysis are based on the comparison of the equilibrium conditions of the players
who strictly decrease their equilibrium strategies within the groups that strictly decrease the
group’s equilibrium aggregate from an equilibrium with a smaller global aggregate associated
with a less concentrated industry to an equilibrium with a (weakly) larger global aggregate
associated with a more concentrated (but possibly identical) industry.
Keywords Vessel sharing agreements · Nash equilibrium uniqueness · Generalized
concavity · Comparative statics · Welfare analysis

The importance of analyzing agreements that involve the sharing of independently owned
means of production is widely recognized in economics (see, e.g., Chen and Ross (2000) and
Zhang and Zhang (2006), as well as the subsequent literature). Beyond a purely theoretical
interest, a real reason to examine these agreements is that they can give rise to anti-competitive
effects and, in fact, some antitrust laws prohibit them, at least in principle. For instance, vessel
sharing agreements are in principle prohibited by Article 101(1) of the Treaty on the Func-
tioning of the European Union and they can be run only by virtue of the “emending” Article
101(3) of that Treaty, which allows for pro-competitive agreements.2 Our understanding of
the effects of modes of production that call for the joint use of independently owned means
of production can thus have potential implications for the economy. Such an understanding
cannot ignore the particular mechanism used by companies to organize joint production,
which is, in fact, at the origin of a possible change in the strategic behavior of the agents
involved and of the repercussions on society.

More precisely, under the assumption of strict concavity of the revenue function and of
the linearity of variable cost functions, the mentioned article has proved that every game
generated by a system has a unique Nash equilibrium and that the enlargement of vessel
sharing agreements yields a decrease in the unique equilibrium price and, consequently, an
increase in consumer welfare.
In the present contribution we generalize the investigation in Quartieri (2017) both on the
demand side, by admitting a general class of price functions (which properly subsumes that
considered in the mentioned article), and on the supply side, by admitting strictly increasing
and convex (but possibly nonlinear) variable cost functions. Dealing with such generalization
provides a new contribution, from both a game-theoretic and mathematical viewpoint and
from an economic viewpoint, by responding not only to the need to carry an analysis based
on hypotheses on the most general primitives but also, especially on the supply side, to
remove assumptions that are not necessarily plausible. In particular, the linearity of costs
may conﬂict with the heterogeneity of the productive efﬁciency of the various ships of the
ﬂeet of a carrier. Even when we assume that each vessel operates under linear and strictly
increasing variable costs, in the presence of a ﬂeet composed of multiple vessels with different
linear variable costs, a simple microeconomic optimization exercise leads to variable costs
for the carrier that are piecewise linear, convex, and strictly increasing. It must be conceded
that the heterogeneity of the productive efﬁciency of the various ships of the ﬂeet of a carrier
is commonplace in the container shipping industry and hence that the assumption of linearity
of variable cost functions is, in fact, restrictive and unrealistic.
From a game-theoretic and mathematical viewpoint, the relaxation of the cost linearity
assumption drastically changes the structure of the games studied. It is not difﬁcult to check—
if needed, see Remark 3—that the games considered here, as well as those in Quartieri
(2017), are not typically aggregative games in the general sense of Acemoglu and Jensen
(2013, Deﬁnition 1), as well as in the sense of Corchón (1994) or of Cornes and Hartley
(2012). The main structural difference between the games examined in Quartieri (2017) and
those considered here is that, by virtue of cost linearity, in the games examined in Quartieri
(2017) the partial derivative of a player’s utility function with respect to the player’s strategic
variable is equivalent to that of a player of a Cournot game and hence of an aggregative game.
The analysis in Quartieri (2017) relies on the observation that, under certain assumptions on
the (pseudo-)concavity of players’ utility functions in their own strategic variables, only the
partial derivatives are what really matters and what actually determines the structure of the set

the mentioned conclusions can be generalized only up to a certain degree of convexity of
variable cost functions and that, in industries with “highly” convex variable cost functions,
the formation and expansion of vessel sharing agreements can actually harm consumers. This
indicates that the heterogeneity of ﬁrms’ production efﬁciency may constitute a structural
cause of a non-competitive effect.
The remainder of the article is organized as follows. Section 2 formally presents the
fundamental structures needed to model a system of vessel sharing agreements and a game
associated with it. Section 3 deals with various deﬁnitions derived from that of a price
function and recalls a notion of generalized convexity. Based on these deﬁnitions, Section 4
introduces the main object of our analysis—convex systems of vessel sharing agreements—
and discusses other conditions that a system of vessel sharing agreements might or might not
satisfy. Section 5 proves the fundamental theorems underlying the main results of the paper
and comments on the proof technique. Section 6 contains the core ﬁndings of the paper:
it investigates the uniqueness of the equilibrium aggregate, the equilibrium uniqueness, the
positivity of equilibria in convex VSA-systems and examines the implications of the increase
of concentration of a VSA-conﬁguration on the variation in consumer welfare. The analysis
is supplemented with examples that illustrate the impossibility to drop speciﬁc assumptions.
Section 7 concludes. A ﬁnal Appendix A discusses a general condition on price functions.

2.3 A measure of concentration
Many measures of concentration used in industrial organization theory (such as, e.g., the
Herﬁndahl index) are real-valued functions that endow N with a total preorder. The “more
concentrated than” used in Quartieri (2017) endows N with a partial order and allows for
the pairwise incomparability of VSA-conﬁgurations.
Deﬁnition 3 Let N = {1, . . . , ¯n} be a set of carriers and let (C◦, C•) be a pair of VSA-
conﬁgurations in N. We say that C• is more concentrated than C◦iff each VSA in C◦is
contained in some VSA in C• and write C◦⊑C•.
The pair (N, ⊑) is a bounded partial order relation on N: a reﬂexive, transitive, and
antisymmetric binary relation on N with a greatest element and a least element.
Deﬁnition 4 Let N = {1, . . . , ¯n} be a set of carriers. The VSA-conﬁguration
{{1, . . . , ¯n}}

In this Section 4 we introduce all the relevant deﬁnitions concerning VSA-systems and VSA-
conﬁgurations that are used as assumptions in the analysis of the equilibrium structure and
welfare properties conducted in Section 6. Henceforth, given a function f : X →R on a
proper real interval X, we denote the right upper Dini derivative of f at x ∈X\{sup X} by
D+ f (x) and by D−f (x) the left upper Dini derivative of f at x ∈X\{inf X}: when f is

derivative of f at x; when f is convex or left differentiable at x ∈X\{inf X}, the extended

interior of X, we write Df (x) to denote the derivative of f at x.

The basic assumption employed in the analysis of this paper is the convexity of a VSA-

5.3 Implications of the almost smoothness condition
It is well-known, in the game-theoretic literature, that some degree of smoothness of the
utility functions is needed to ensure equilibrium uniqueness. For instance, Szidarovszky and
Yakowitz (1982) shows that the differentiability of the price functions cannot be simply
dropped in guaranteeing the existence of a unique Nash equilibrium of a Cournot oligopoly
and Neyman (1997) shows that the differentiability of the potential cannot be simply dropped
in guaranteeing the existence of a unique Nash equilibrium of a potential game. Theorem
5 proves that the condition of almost smoothness of a VSA-conﬁguration (together with
the convexity of cost functions and other undemanding conditions) is sufﬁcient to establish
the existence of a unique Nash equilibrium for each equilibrium aggregate: Example 4 in
Section 6.3 shows that, for this to happen, the condition of almost smoothness cannot be
simply dropped.

7 Final discussion
We have presented a model of competition between the carriers of the container shipping
industry under general hypotheses on the demand and supply side that relax the assumptions
of a previous model in Quartieri (2017) with strictly concave revenue functions and linear
cost functions. We have examined the equilibrium structure of this model and derived a
comparative statics analysis from it. The relaxation of linearity assumptions on costs has
drastically changed the structure of strategic interaction between carriers and has had a
profound impact on the structure of strategic interaction between carriers, necessitating a
reappraisal of the proof technique employed to address equilibrium problems that cannot
be reduced to 1-dimensional ones (such as those examined, for example, in the theory of
aggregative games).
Conﬁrming and generalizing previous results on the structure of equilibria, we have shown
how the nonlinearity of convex costs can have anti-competitive implications on consumer
welfare, which were absent in the linear case. From our analysis it thus emerges that the for-
mation of vessel sharing agreements can generate price increases in the presence of “highly”
convex variable costs (due, for example, to the presence of carriers with highly heteroge-
neous ﬂeets in terms of transportation efﬁciency). The last example of the paper has shown
that this can occur even in the presence of elementary linear price functions and a set of
ﬁrms where all but one have linear costs. The present results thus conﬁrm and generalize the
previous analysis in Quartieri (2017), but at the same time show that the relaxation of cost
linearity beyond the condition that has been termed “almost linearity” is compatible with
anti-competitive effects.
From a mathematical point of view, the main novelty of this work is to offer a proof
technique—based on a particular dichotomy ﬁrst described in the Abstract and then discussed
more diffusely at the beginning of Section 5.2—to prove equilibrium uniqueness and to
perform comparative static analysis in games of competition among carriers that satisfy a
weak form of aggregativity but that cannot be classiﬁed as aggregative games (at least, in
the usual sense). Theorem 4 uses this technique based on the dichotomous set Z deﬁned in
its proof, and all the main results of this work are essentially based on that theorem. A less
sophisticated version of the mentioned technique, based on the simpler dichotomous set of
all players who increase15 their equilibrium strategy in the transition between two equilibria
with different equilibrium aggregates, has already been used in the context of certain types of
aggregative games: see, e.g., Mouche and Quartieri (2018) and the references therein. Such
a less sophisticated technique is not applicable in the present context: this paper has shown
that, instead, the more reﬁned dichotomy formed by the dichotomous set Z mentioned above
15 Clearly, such a dichotomy is equivalent to that induced by the set of all players that strictly decrease their
equilibrium strategy in the transition between two equilibria with different equilibrium aggregates.

can be. It would be interesting to examine the applicability of the new technique to other
classes of games that—though non-aggregative in the usual sense—satisfy weak forms of

is decreasing.17 As is clear from the ﬁrst speciﬁcation in Example 7, the converse of the
previous implication is generally false.
