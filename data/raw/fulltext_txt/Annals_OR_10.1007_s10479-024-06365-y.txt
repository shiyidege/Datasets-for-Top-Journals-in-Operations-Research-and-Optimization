Abstract
This paper adopts a Markowitz inspired treatment of uncertainty in inflation and the ex­
change rate in analyzing the dollarization of Ecuador in 2000. The adoption of the U.S. 
dollar set a fixed price of foreign (and domestic) currency of one, with zero nominal re­
turn and zero variance. Dollarization thus stabilized inflation, guaranteed convertibility in 
foreign trade and resuscitated the stock exchange in Quito. The dollar served the role of 
the risk-free asset, save for the risk of U.S. inflation. However, in over 20 years, Ecuador 
has paid seigniorage to the U.S. Treasury of an accumulated $20 billion in outside money, 
dollar currency and coin. In 2023, we estimate an operational expense in terms of new 
seigniorage and the inflationary tax of slightly more than 1% of its GDP. Yet Ecuador has 
stabilized prices and grown more rapidly, coincidently, by 1.2%.
Keywords  Dollarization · Seigniorage · Inflationary tax
Harry Markowitz remarked in his 1990 Nobel lecture that economics “… is concerned with 
economic agents who act under uncertainty… it is a theory which can be used to direct 
practice.…” Markowitz (1990). It is in this spirit that we consider Ecuador’s successful 
dollarization.
1  The financial crisis in Ecuador
Ecuador is a small country on the north-western coast of South America that has a stock mar­
ket of listed firms in its capital, Quito. The colonial style capital at 2,800 m above sea level 
is flanked by an active volcano, the Pichincha. It is the political capital of the country while 
the economic capital and largest city is Guayaquil with its busy port at sea level. In 1997, an 
economista at the Chamber of Commerce of Guayaquil, suggested dollarization in Ecuador as

a means to have a stable unit of account, store of value, and medium of exchange for both inter­
national and domestic trade. The decision to dollarize, however, resided with the government in 
Quito. The government was busy bailing out banks, creating more liquidity, and financing huge 
deficits by the printing press. In doing so, the Central Bank and Treasury caused high inflation 
they could not eradicate. Between 1884 and 2000, its currency was the sucre, named after 
Marshal Antonio José de Sucre who liberated Quito from the Spanish. The sucre was replaced 
in 2000 by the USD due to the resulting 1998–99 financial crisis. The market capitalization of 
listed companies in terms of sucres had fallen from 10.5% of GDP in 1995 to 2.7% in 1999.

2  Ecuador was already de facto half-dollarized
High inflation often results in nearly worthless banknotes, but countries need a stable unit 
of account, medium of exchange, and store of value. Ecuador’s monthly inflation was 4% 
in 1994. Consequently, unofficial dollarization had already taken place in Ecuador. Also, 
provinces, cities and some other non-banks issued quasi-money as they did not have the 
dollars to pay their workers and bills. The sucre was losing value at an accelerating pace. As 
indicators of dollarization at the end of 1999: 56% of bank deposits were denominated in 
dollars, 66.5% of bank loans were in dollars– at 30% interest per annum - and the percent of 
quasi-money denominated in dollars was 47.4%. (BCE, and Beckerman, 2002, p. 69) The 
market had already mostly substituted the dollar for the sucre.

Furthermore, Ecuador restructured its sovereign debt achieving a 40% reduction of its 
face value, as noted by Fischer: “Ecuador offered to exchange all of its Brady and Euro­
bonds (with a combined face value of $6.5 billion) for a combination of new 30-year and

Ecuador substantial debt reduction (by about 40% of the face value of the bond debt) as 
well as significant cash flow relief in the initial years.” (Fischer, 2000).
Indeed, the IMF and other multinational creditors disbursed $2  billion in loans in 
2000 and 2001 to support the dollarization and economic recovery, of which the IMF lent 
$300 million, the World Bank $425, the Interamerican Bank $625, and the Corporacion 
Andina de Fomento $700. In addition, there was an approximate $260 million dollars of 
interest on debt forgiven on U.S. Brady Bonds, as well as a deferment by the Paris Club in 
September 2000 of $800 in arrears and maturities due in 2000 (Fischer, op. cit.).
4  The sucre to dollar conversion
Ecuador’s president, Jamil Mahuad, had announced the dollarization of the economy on 
January 9, 2000, amidst a recession, widespread bank failures, sovereign default on Brady 
and Euro bonds, high inflation, and a massive depreciation of the sucre. Twelve days later 
on July 21, Mahuad was forced to resign after a week of demonstrations by indigenous 
Ecuadorians and a military coup. Gustavo Noboa, then vice-president, carried out the then 
unpopular dollarization and conversion of the sucre to the USD at 25,000 sucres per dollar 
from March 13, 2000 to, March 31, 2001. The Government explicitly set the 25,000 sucre 
per dollar rate so as not to exhaust its existing liquid dollar reserves (Castillo, 2017).
Stanley Fischer concludes: “There is much food for thought in the story of Ecuadorian 
dollarization—not least, that the early success of what was a desperation move, taken in 
haste, without most of what were thought of as the necessary preconditions being in place, 
requires us to reconsider the conditions under which such a switch in the monetary system 
will succeed.” (Fischer, op. cit.).
Once Ecuador adopted the dollar on January 9, 2000, it stopped issuing the sucre. The 
inflow of the reserves of the Banco Central del Ecuador (BCE) in 2000 reflected the new 
dollar loans by multilateral institutions, as well as the cash flow outflows by the BCE pur­
chasing sucres in the conversion process.

5  Once dollarized it is hard to de-dollarize
Rafael Correa, President of Ecuador from January 15, 2007– May 24, 2017 cast doubt on 
the success of dollarization; in 2014, Correa said that “… dollarization was a bad idea, it is 
like being in a boxing ring wearing a straitjacket.… I would never have entered into a dol­
larized regimen, but it’s the national currency,” Correa said in an Aug. 13, 2014 interview on 
Ecuadorian Television - the costs of exiting dollarization would be catastrophic.” It is ironic 
that he managed to pay 35 cents on the dollar on 91% on the $3.2 billion of outstanding 
debt in 2008, threatening nothing if the offer was not accepted, but did not dare to attempt 
de-dollarization (Feibelman, 2017). A further restructuring of $17.4 sovereign debt took 
place successfully while dollarized in August 2020 with a reduction in principal of 8.8% 
(Gottlieb, 2020). The IMF and other creditors continued loans in support of dollarization, 
such as a $6.5 billion dollar Extended Fund Facility arrangement on September 30, 2020 in 
support of “stabilization and recovery” from the Covid 19 pandemic. Zimbabwe dollarized 
in 2009, but to partially de-dollarize has unsuccessfully issued new local banknotes on three 
separate occasions (Ncube, 2024). The first two re-issues of Zimbabwean banknotes are 
now worthless, and the third most recent, the Zig-G is de facto trading at a 50% discount to 
the de jure par with the dollar that the government has set. The Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe 
has a credibility problem– in foreign trade, customs duties must be paid in USD. Of course, 
the BCE also had a credibility problem when it dollarized.
6  The results of dollarization
To sum up here are the results of Ecuador’s dollarization in terms of inflation: In the 21 
years before dollarization was completed, the average annual rate of inflation was nearly 
40%, in the 22 years after while dollarized, the percentage rate was 3.2%. See Trading 
­Economics. In terms of real growth, the economy’s real growth rate rose by over 1% per 
annum, from 2.5 to 3.6%. Market capitalization in Quito rose from its 1999 low of 2.9% 
of GDP to an average of slightly over 8% from 2000 to 2023 (World Bank and CEIC). The 
exchange rate was a constant $1 between 2001 and 2024, having zero variance in nominal 
terms! Net foreign investment went from a low − 0.1% of GDP in 2000 to 2.2% of GDP in 
2001, 2.7% in 2002 and 2003, stabilizing around 1% since. Table 2 reports inflation and real 
growth in GDP in Ecuador in the 21 years before and after dollarization.

second is the inflationary tax on the stock of dollars accumulated. Philip Cagan’s pioneering 
doctoral thesis at the University of Chicago, supervised by Milton Friedman, breaks down 
operational cost into two parts (Cagan, 1956):

In the case of Ecuador, we use USD currency in circulation adjusted by the U.S. price level,

8  Robert Mundell’s “shock absorber” argument for a common currency
Robert Mundell (1973) argues: “Consider a world of two islands, one south of the equator, 
Southerner, and one north of the equator, Northerner. Both islands can produce a single crop 
of perishable food each year, but Southerner’s crop ripens in the autumn and Northerner’s 
crop ripens in the spring. Food in each island can be stored for up to six months, after which 
it is unfit for human consumption.…Let us…suppose… that the world population is divided 
equally between the two islands and that there is no mobility between them,… If there were 
no inter-island shipping of food the populations would die out. For half the year the popu­
lation of each island would be without food, so eventually each island would be without 
population…. Let us suppose that both Northener and Southerner respectively produce one 
hundred units of food in the spring and autumn. In the spring Northener ships half its crop 
to Southerner, but what does it receive in exchange? It must receive an evidence of South­
erner’s debt, a claim to part of Southerner’s crop maturing in the autumn.”
These claims may be in each other’s currencies or in terms of forward contracts. The 
Southerners may deposit 50 units of their currency in Northener’s central bank, and North­
eners may deposit 50 units of their currency in Southerner’s central bank. Or issue future 
contracts for forward delivery.
“This arrangement would function satisfactorily in a world of certainty in which crops 
were known in advance and no defaults were made on the redeemability of currencies.
But in a world of uncertainty it would be subject to speculation about the convertibility 
of foreign currencies…. The important point to recognize... is that when there are different 
currencies the threat of devaluation introduces an additional element of uncertainty into the 
system.”….
For instance, either central bank can impose exchange controls that reduce the avail­
ability of foreign exchange for imports. Or, the price of foreign currency can be increased 
by the central banks.
“A common money performs the sharing function very effectively. Suppose that the two 
countries agree to the creation of a common currency.” The farmers of each country hold 
50 units of the common currency to purchase each other’s food crops when food is scarce 
at home.
At this point there will not appear to have been a gain to the system as a whole by the 
amalgamation of the two monies. But the gain appears when the variability of the crops 
is taken into account. For example, suppose again that the spring crop in Northener is 
below the average, let us say at 70 units. Then Southerner consumers hold 50 units of 
the common money as do Northerners producers. As long as competition prevails and 
there are no delays in the transmission of goods or information the 100 units of common 
money will exchange for the 70 units of food irrespective of who holds the money; this 
is because competition and freedom of arbitrage assure the single price. The price of a 
unit of food thus rises to 100/70 for both Southerners and Northeners and the burden of 
the harvest fluctuation is met equally by the two countries. The common currency assures 
an automatic and equal sharing of the risk of the fluctuations”. (Mundell, 1973). (Note: 
this version substitutes Northerner and Southerner for Capricorn and Cancer to facilitate 
understanding, (see Connolly, 2001). See (Mundell, 1961) for the theory of optimum 
currency areas.

In short, the common money guarantees convertibility– no chance of devaluation nor 
exchange controls. If there are no costs of transport nor tariffs and quotas, the outcome is an 
equal sharing of real shocks.
Milton Friedman agrees with Mundell in saying: “My own view has long been that for 
a small country, to quote from a lecture that I gave in 1972, ‘the best policy would be 
to eschew the revenue from money creation, to unify its currency with the currency of 
a large, relatively stable developed country with which it has close economic relations, 
and to impose no barriers to the movement of money or prices, wages, and interest rates. 
Such a policy requires not having a central bank.’ [Milton Friedman, Money and Economic 
Development, (Praeger,1973), p.59] Panama exemplifies this policy, which has since come 
to be called ‘dollarization.’ A currency board is a slightly less rigid version of a hard fixed 
rate than dollarization. A further movement in this direction, creating perhaps a number of 
currency blocs consisting of a major country and a number of much smaller countries with 
close economic ties to the major country, may well occur and be a good thing.” (Friedman, 
2001, p. 10),
Friedman elaborates: “Experience suggests that in a small developing country an inde­
pendent internal monetary policy is likely to be highly unstable, with occasional episodes 
of high inflation. There is every reason to believe that the monetary policy of the US, or 
Germany, now the euro, or Britain, however flawed from the large country’s own point of 
view, will provide much more stability than the small country will produce by itself. This 
has probably been the major factor that has led countries to consider or to adopt a currency 
board or dollarization. (Friedman, 2001, p. 12)”.
9  Risks of dollarization
We have already mentioned the risk of the inflationary tax being imposed on Ecuador by 
U.S. generated inflation, as occurred during the 2020–2021 Covid-19 pandemic. The U.S. 
Federal Reserve Bank pursued a Bagehot (1873) rule of money creation, and the U.S. Trea­
sury had its Payroll Protection Program and others increasing liquidity. Not being able to 
print the US dollar is a hard constraint on a Central Bank’s printing press such as Ecuador’s, 
which is often a benefit. No one can seriously argue that removing monetary discretion from 
a spendthrift government is a reckless mistake: decades of high inflation and defaults on 
sovereign debt are evidence against politicians controlling the “printing press”.
Another risk of dollarization, particularly to small countries like Ecuador, is criminality. 
Dollarization attracts not only direct foreign and portfolio investment, but also drug cartels 
and gangs that use the dollar as their functional currency. The combination of the Covid 19 
pandemic and dollarization has attracted homicide to Ecuador. In Pazmino’s words: “Mexi­
can Sinaloa and Jalisco Nueva Generación cartels have presence in Ecuador, as do Italian 
and Albanian mafias, and have recently destabilized the government and the economy.… On 
9 August 2023, eleven days before the 2023 Ecuadorian general election, presidential can­
didate Fernando Villavicencio, a tough on crime candidate, was assassinated by Colombian 
hitmen.” (Pazmino, 2024). Gangs took over the prison in Cuenca running the drug cartels 
from there, getting arms and cooperation from outside the prison. (BBC, 2024 April 4) 
Indeed, the national homicide rate rose in Ecuador from 7.7 per 10,000 inhabitants in 2020 
to 44.5 in 2023, making it one of the highest murder rates in Latin America (Statista, 2024).

The risk of increased drug cartelization due to dollarization is high, in particular to small

10  Conclusion
Tidy arguments on the pre-conditions for a successful dollarization in Latin America miss 
the point. In practice, as Harry Markowitz (op. cit.) reminds us, economic agents act under 
uncertainty and must carry out the practice of economics based on some subjective or objec­
tive probabilities. High inflation that is not vanquished will dollarize the economy infor­
mally due to the need for a stable money. A “currency reform” printing a single new 1,000 
or 10,000 peso banknote that replaces one thousand old bills is a sign that the economy is 
on the road to dollarization.
For a successful official dollarization, the country should have sufficient dollars in liq­
uid reserves, or be able to borrow them, so that it is both able to redeem the old money at 
some conversion rate and to pay its own bills in dollars. A government does not have much 
credibility in announcing immediate dollarization, then asking government employees to 
accept more of the old currency or script that will be heavily discounted on the free market. 
Dollarization is expensive - new seigniorage on a one hundred dollar bill entails an export 
of home goods, services and assets worth $100. Further, it is risky - once held as money, 
the dollar depreciates at the U.S. inflation rate and may also attract more criminal activity.
