Abstract
The global containerised trade heavily relies on liner shipping services, facilitating the world-
wide movement of large cargo volumes along Ô¨Åxed routes and schedules. The proÔ¨Åtability
of shipping companies hinges on how efÔ¨Åciently they design their shipping network; a com-
plex optimization problem known as the liner shipping network design problem (LSNDP). In
recent years, approximate dynamic programming (ADP), also known as reinforcement learn-
ing, has emerged as a promising approach for large-scale optimisation. This paper introduces
a novel Markov decision process for the LSNDP and investigates the potential of ADP. We
show that ADP methods based on value iteration produce optimal solutions to small instances,
but their scalability is hindered by high memory demands. An ADP method based on a deep
neural network requires less memory and successfully obtains feasible solutions. The quality
of solutions, however, declines for larger instances, possibly due to the discrete nature of
high-dimensional state and action spaces.

1 Introduction
Liner shipping transports containerised cargo along predeÔ¨Åned routes with Ô¨Åxed itineraries.
The term ‚Äúliner‚Äù refers to ships that are operated on such routes, similar to passenger liners
and public transportation such as bus and metro. This paper focuses on the liner shipping
network design problem (LSNDP), aiming to optimise the shipping network by minimising
operating costs and the transit time of cargo Ô¨Çow from origin to destination ports.
The LSNDP is a highly complex combinatorial optimisation problem, with a solution
space that increases exponentially with the size of the network. In reality, the network can
be very large. For example, Maersk, the world‚Äôs largest liner shipping company, transports
more than a million containers monthly from more than 300 ports globally (Maersk, 2019).
Traditional optimisation methods, such as mixed-integer programming, struggle to handle
real-life instances and are unable to solve instances with more than a dozen ports (Christiansen
et al., 2020). Even heuristic methods can only handle networks up to 197 ports (Krogsgaard
et al., 2018). Consequently, in practice, the network design process is mostly driven by expert
network designers, with only limited support from optimisation algorithms.
With an evident need for decision support and large-scale optimisation, we investigate the
potential of emerging methods known as approximate dynamic programming (ADP) (Pow-
ell, 2011) or reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 2018), drawing inspiration from
various research domains, including machine learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018), control theory
(Bertsekas, 2019) and operations research (Powell, 2011). Our motivation is the recent trend
in the application of machine learning techniques to complex optimisation problems, as exem-
pliÔ¨Åed by AlphaGo‚Äôs exceptional performance in the board game Go (Silver et al., 2016).
Driven by the success, reinforcement learning methods has been applied to various combina-
torial graph problems, including routing, scheduling and assignment problems (Vesselinova
et al., 2020). Moreover, Khalil et al. (2017) develop a learning algorithm that generalises
to new instances of NP-hard combinatorial graph problems, combining an RL algorithm
with graph embedding techniques (Dai et al., 2016). As such, we build on experience with
combinatorial optimization problems that have similar structure to the LSND.
To facilitate the application of machine learning methods, the optimisation problem is
often formulated as a sequential decision-making process, such as Markov decision process
(MDP) (Puterman, 2014), and handled by an approximate version of dynamic programming
(Mazyavkina et al., 2021). Indeed, ADP has shown promise in overcoming limitations of
traditional OR methods by integrating machine learning techniques into the MDP framework.
For instance, ADP is shown to outperform a commercial mixed-integer programming (MIP)
solver in terms of both solution quality and running time for solving large-size maritime

inventory routing problems (Papageorgiou et al., 2015). This paper makes a contribution in
the string of efforts to adapt ADP and LR to liner shipping problems.
An advantage of the dynamic programming framework over traditional mathematical
programming methods is the sequential decision-making, decomposing the problem over
time. This immediately allows for extensions to stochastic optimisation, in which decisions
must adapt to uncertainty evolving over time, e.g., according to a Markov process. Like other
exact methods, dynamic programming may not scale to larger networks, and approximation
is necessary. However, in contrast to heuristic methods for optimisation, ADP methods, e.g.,
value iteration, may have convergence guarantees, see Powell (2011).
In this paper, we formulate the LSNDP as an MDP, deÔ¨Åning appropriate state and action
spaces and cost and transition functions. We introduce a state-dependent action space that
allows us to incorporate routing and Ô¨Çow constraints. This also allows us to account for
various other problem characteristics. For smaller instances of the problem, we employ value
iteration algorithms, which maintain value estimates in a tabular format. For larger instances,
however, we apply a deep reinforcement learning algorithm that generates estimates of the
so-called state-action values using a neural network.
We summarise the contributions of our work as follows:
‚Ä¢ We develop a novel MDP framework for the LSNDP that accounts for the most important
characteristics of the problem, including co-optimisation of network design and com-
modity routing, balancing of travel costs and travel times, a weekly service frequency,
vessel capacity limits and transshipments of commodities. This framework facilitates the
application of ADP.
‚Ä¢ We are the Ô¨Årst to adopt and compare a number of ADP variants to the LSNDP, including
value iteration methods and a deep Q-network.
‚Ä¢ We examine the potential of the ADP methodology on a number of realistic test instances
and demonstrate the ability to obtain optimal solutions for smaller instances and feasible
solutions for larger instances.
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. First, Sect.2 reviews relevant work in
the scientiÔ¨Åc literature. Section3 outlines the LSNDP and formulates the problem as an MDP,
whereas Sect.4.1 and Sect.4.3 present ADP-based algorithms for solving the LSNDP. Sec-
tion5 discusses the results of the numerical experiments. Finally, Sect.6 draws conclusions
from this study and suggests potential directions for future research.

Another strand of literature applies dynamic programming (DP) (Puterman, 2014) to

Wang, 2011b), speed optimisation (Aydin et al., 2017) and ship refuelling (Zhen et al., 2017).
As a new contribution to the scientiÔ¨Åc literature, we take a similar approach to simultaneously
tackle the design of the liner shipping network and Ô¨Çowing cargo demands.
Advancements in DP have been made under the name of approximate dynamic pro-
gramming by incorporating approximation techniques to enhance scalability (Powell, 2011).
Applications of ADP include the stochastic time-staged integer multi-commodity Ô¨Çow prob-
lem (Topaloglu & Powell, 2006), large-size Ô¨Çeet management problems (Simao et al., 2009;
Sim√£o et al., 2010), sequential network design and timing problems (Chow & Sayarshad,

the Ô¨Çeet management problem contains an inÔ¨Ånite state space and 50,000 decisions variables.
With a similar need for scaling, we likewise resort to approximation, in particular, of the DP
value function.
The success of ADP is not least due to the machine learning and artiÔ¨Åcial intelligence
communities, in which ADP is also referred to as reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton &
Barto, 2018). RL has been integrated with neural networks (Goodfellow et al., 2016) to solve
combinatorial optimisation problems (COPs) on graphs (Bello et al., 2016). Notably, RL
has outperformed traditional heuristic methods like Google Optimization Tools‚Äô local search
(Perron et al., 2023) in solving the travelling salesman problem (TSP) for up to 100 nodes
(Bello et al., 2016). RL has also been applied to complex graph-based COPs, including
vehicle routing problems with time-varying locations and demands (Nazari et al., 2018).
Extensions in this domain incorporate graph embedding techniques (Dai et al., 2016; Khalil
et al., 2017), attention mechanisms (Kool et al., 2018; VeliÀáckovi¬¥c et al., 2017), and training
algorithms such as Deep Q-network (Mnih et al., 2015), REINFORCE (Williams, 1992) and
Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (Mnih et al., 2016). To the best of our knowledge, this
paper is the Ô¨Årst in the literature to adopt ADP to the LSNDP. For smaller instances of the
combinatorial problem, we rely on value function iteration. For larger instances, however,
we use a Deep Q-network for value function approximation.
3 Problem description and formulation
The LSNDP solves two interconnected problems: network design and multi-commodity Ô¨Çow.
The former problem deÔ¨Ånes a set of services that form a liner shipping network. Each service
represents a scheduled journey of multiple vessels following a predetermined sailing route.
123

Annals of Operations Research
This journey consists of a sequence of port calls, which are intermediate stops at ports for
container handling and refuelling of vessels (Koza et al., 2020). The latter problem determines
the optimal routing of commodities from their origin ports to their destination ports within
the service network. Each commodity represents cargo shipped by sea.
In this section, we present the LSNDP that jointly optimises the service network design
and commodity routing by minimising the weighted sum of the network‚Äôs weekly operating
expenses and the total transit time of all commodities. Operating expenses include vessel
deployment costs, Ô¨Åxed design costs for selecting service arcs and transshipment costs. By
including total transit time in the minimisation criterion, the LSNDP prioritises the selection
of the shortest path for shipping a commodity from its origin port to its destination port within
the service network.
Section 3.1 presents the underlying assumptions that form the basis for the rest of the paper.
We largely adopt the same assumptions as Brouer et al. (2014). In Sect.3.2, we formulate the
LSNDP as a Markov decision problem, using the dynamic programming terminology of Put-
erman (2014) and Powell (2011), and refer to Table 2 for the notation. For the corresponding
integer programming (IP) formulation of the LSNDP, we refer to Appendix A.

4 Approximate dynamic programming
The following presents our ADP algorithms for solving LSNDP, including value iteration in
Sect.4.1, our efÔ¨Åcient state-space exploration in Sect.4.2, and deep reinforcement learning

4.1 Value iteration methods
The value iteration algorithm, introduced by Bellman (1957), is an iterative method for
solving the optimality equations (20). Shown in Algorithm 4 in Appendix B, the algorithm
starts with an initial value function estimate ÀÜv (line 2) and updates the estimate for each
non-terminal state s ‚ààS \ Str (line 6) in each iteration until the convergence criterion is
satisÔ¨Åed (line 8).
The value iteration algorithm is known for its simplicity and convergence to the optimal
value function (Puterman, 2014). However, it faces computational challenges when dealing
with large state spaces. The identiÔ¨Åcation of every element s in the set of feasible states
S and its corresponding action set A(s) becomes increasingly demanding in the LSNDP
as the problem size grows, due to constraints (6)-(14). This identiÔ¨Åcation is necessary for
both initialising (line 2) and updating the value estimate ÀÜv(s) (line 6). Moreover, updating
the value estimate for each state in each iteration (line 6) can be computationally intensive,
requiring O(|S \ Str| ¬∑ |A(s)|) operations.
To address these challenges, we explore on-line variants of value iteration. These variants
update the value function estimate ÀÜv(s) while the decision maker interacts with the MDP
system, selecting an action a, receiving the cost C(s, a) and transitioning to the next state s‚Ä≤.
A comprehensive discussion of on-line value iteration methods can be found in Barto et al.
(1995). In the remainder of this section, we present a speciÔ¨Åc on-line variant of value iteration
for the LSNDP, as outlined in Algorithm 5 in Appendix C and its modiÔ¨Åcation, Algorithm
1, and discuss how these algorithms tackle the problems with large state spaces.
Algorithm 5 is based on the Real-Time Dynamic Programming (RTDP) algorithm, pro-
posed by Barto et al. (1995), but adapted for the LSNDP to handle different types of terminal
states. The algorithm employs the so-called lookup table that stores value estimates ÀÜv(s) in
(s, ÀÜv(s)) pairs and does not require pre-identifying all states s and their corresponding action
sets A(s) in the feasible state set S. Instead, as the decision maker interacts with the MDP
system and identiÔ¨Åes a new feasible state s through the transition function f (line 5), the
algorithm adds the new state to the lookup table with an initial value estimate of 0 (line 1).
We refer to an iteration as a transition from one state to another and an episode as a
sequence of transitions from the initial state to a terminal state. In each iteration, the decision
maker selects a greedy action a in state s (line 5), incurs a cost C(s, a), transitions to the next
state s‚Ä≤ (line 6), and updates the value estimate for the current state st, while leaving value
estimates for other states unchanged (line 7). When a desired or undesired terminal state is
123

reached, the episode ends and a new episode begins (line 10 and 16). This cycle repeats until
a stopping criteria is satisÔ¨Åed. Provided that the value estimate ÀÜv(s) is updated inÔ¨Ånitely often
for each state s ‚ààS, it converges to the optimal value function V‚àó(Barto et al., 1995).

5 Computational results
We implement the MDP framework and the algorithms in C++ and use Pybind11 for
an operability interface with Python. The deep neural network is implemented through
PyTorch C++ API. The computational experiments are performed on AMD EPYC 7452
32-Core Processor. For benchmarking, we formulate the LSNDP as an integer linear pro-
gram and use the Gurobi optimiser (Gurobi Optimization, 2023) to obtain the minimal
costs and the optimal routes of services and commodities.
Section 5.1 outlines the process of generating test instances for numerical experiments.
In Sect.5.2, we examine the size of the state space and its growth. In Sect.5.3, we apply
value iteration and assess the proposed state exploration strategy of Sect.4.2 by comparing
the performance of Algorithm 5 and Algorithm 1. Section5.4 evaluates the performance of
our deep reinforcement learning algorithm, Algorithm 2. To illustrate the co-optimisation
of network design and commodity routing and the quality of solutions, Sect.5.5 analyses
selected problem instances.

6 Conclusion and future research
In this study, we propose an MDP formulation for the LSNDP that accounts for the important
characteristics of the problem, including balancing of travel costs and travel times, a weekly
service frequency, vessel capacity limits and transshipments of commodities.
We illustrate the co-optimisation of network design and commodity routing for a small
problem instance. Sensitivity analysis reveals the importance of minimising travel times to
avoid excessive lengths of service routes and the potential cost and travel time reductions
from larger vessel capacities, bi-weekly service frequency, multiple services and feasibility
of transshipment.
The curse of dimensionality in dynamic programming is evidenced by the sizes of the state
and action spaces increasing exponentially with the number of services and commodities.
For instance, with 4 ports, 12 archs, 3 commodities and a single service, the number of states
exceeds 25 million. For this reason, we explore the applicability of several ADP approaches
to the MDP.
The value iteration methods rely on a lookup table to approximate the true value function.
To reduce the size of the state space, we propose an exploration strategy that can easily be
incorporated into value iteration and excludes non-hamiltonian commodity Ô¨Çows. Compu-
tational results reveal that the reduction of the state space is enormous; above 95% for most
instances. With this enhancement, our value iteration Ô¨Ånds the optimal solution for instances
of up to 4 ports, 12 archs and 3 commodities; in fact, exploring as little as a few millionth
of the original state space. For instances of up to 5 ports, 20 archs and 7 commodities, the
optimality gaps are less than 7%. In spite of the state space reduction, the value iteration
methods demand substantial memory resources for storing the lookup table, rendering them
unsuitable for solving larger instances.
In contrast, the DQN method operates with a considerably smaller memory requirement,
storing only a limited number of network parameters that determine the values of state-action
pairs. This method successfully obtains feasible solutions for instances of up to 9 ports, 40
archs and 9 commodities; however, with average optimality gaps exceeding 60%. Indeed,
it struggles to improve the quality of feasible solutions. Our algorithm selects an excessive
number of arcs in a service route. Also, a service route comprises multiple partial cyclic routes
that originate from and return to the hub. The performance of the DQN may be attributed to
the discrete nature of the true state-action function with high-dimensional state-action pairs.
Furthermore, the methodology is usually used with soft constraints penalised in the cost
function, and may not have been designed to handle hard constraints such as capacity limits
and the requirement to return to the hub.
To improve the methodology, we explore the impact of longer training times, further
parameter tuning and more layers of the DQN, but could further investigate the explo-
ration/exploitation trade-off to prevent the method from being absorbed in local optima.
Exploring so-called policy gradient methods (Sutton et al., 1999), which learns a parame-
terised policy, may likewise be relevant.
Nevertheless, scalability to real-world problems will most likely remain challenging when
the state space includes the occupied capacity for all arcs. It may be worth investigating alter-
123

Annals of Operations Research
native MDP formulations. As opposed to the current position of services and commodities,
the deÔ¨Ånition of states could be extended to include previous service and commodity trajec-
tories, rendering the inclusion of occupied capacity in the state space unnecessary. A more
scalable approximation to the LSNDP may relax the hard arc capacity constraints. This will
signiÔ¨Åcantly reduce the size of the state space, and at the same time, decompose the LSNDP
into sub-problems: service network design and commodity Ô¨Çow. These sub-problems can be
solved separately by the application of ADP methods and/or by traditional OR methods.
A highly relevant extension of the LSNDP accounts for uncertainty, e.g., in weather con-
ditions and thereby travel times. The ADP methodology immediately facilitates extensions
to stochastic optimisation, in which routing decisions must adapt to the weather while travel-
ling. The MDP allows for weather factors to follow a Markov process and the state space to
include current weather factors, such that optimisation relies on the balancing of conditional
expected future travel costs and travel time.
