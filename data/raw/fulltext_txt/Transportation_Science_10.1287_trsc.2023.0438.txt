Abstract. Humanitarian logistics operations face increasing difficulties due to rising 
demands for aid in disaster areas. This paper investigates the dynamic allocation of scarce 
relief supplies across multiple affected districts over time. It introduces a novel stochastic 
dynamic postdisaster inventory allocation problem (SDPDIAP) with trucks and unmanned 
aerial vehicles (UAVs) delivering relief goods under uncertain supply and demand. The 
relevance of this humanitarian logistics problem lies in the importance of considering the 
intertemporal social impact of deliveries. We achieve this by considering social costs (trans­
portation and deprivation costs) when allocating scarce supplies. Furthermore, we con­
sider the inherent uncertainties of disaster areas and the potential use of cargo UAVs to 
enhance operational efficiency. This study proposes two anticipatory solution methods 
based on approximate dynamic programming, specifically decomposed linear value func­
tion approximation (DL-VFA) and neural network value function approximation (NN- 
VFA) to effectively manage uncertainties in the dynamic allocation process. We compare 
DL-VFA and NN-VFA with various state-of-the-art methods (e.g., exact reoptimization 
and proximal policy optimization) and results show a 6%–8% improvement compared 
with the best benchmarks. NN-VFA provides the best performance and captures nonlinea­
rities in the problem, whereas DL-VFA shows excellent scalability against a minor perfor­
mance loss. From a practical standpoint, the experiments reveal that consideration of social 
costs results in improved allocation of scarce supplies both across affected districts and 
over time. Finally, results show that deploying UAVs can play a crucial role in the alloca­
tion of relief goods, especially in the first stages after a disaster. The use of UAVs reduces 
transportation and deprivation costs together by 16%–20% and reduces maximum depri­
vation times by 19%–40% while maintaining similar levels of demand coverage, showcas­
ing efficient and effective operations.

1. Introduction
The Global Humanitarian Overview of the United 
Nations reports a record of 339 million people that 
needed humanitarian assistance and protection in 
2023, which is a shocking increase compared with the 
136 million people just five years earlier in 2018. Con­
sidering the increasing frequency and impact of disas­
ters, humanitarian logistics is more important than 
ever. The purpose of humanitarian logistics is to pro­
vide affected people with supplies that are necessary 
for their survival and when possible alleviate the 
human suffering caused by a lack of goods and services 
(Shao et al. 2020). Typically, humanitarian operations 
face a surge in demand directly in the response phase 
after a disaster, overwhelming the abilities of relief 
agencies (Zhou et al. 2017) and creating a significant 
scarcity of supplies (Najafi, Eshghi, and Dullaert 2013). 
In the response phase, which generally ranges from a 
few days to a few weeks (Altay and Green 2006), 
humanitarian agencies face a spatio-temporal decision 
problem, requiring effective distribution of available 
supplies to multiple districts in a disaster area over 
time, in which they must consider the well-being of all 
affected people, the operational costs, and the limited 
funds that are received from donors (Huang, Smilo­
witz, and Balcik 2012, Huang and Rafiei 2019).
Given the scarcity of supplies, it is essential to utilize 
the available resources as effectively as possible to alle­
viate human suffering. Holgu´ın-Veras and Jaller (2012) 
suggest that using the concept of social costs (the sum 
of logistics and deprivation costs) is the most appropri­
ate objective function that directly incorporates human

suffering through the inclusion of deprivation costs, 
which represent the estimated value of human suffer­

of deprivation strongly impacts people’s well-being. 
Most individuals can cope with short-term shortages, 
but long-term shortages are significantly more difficult 
to bear. As the timing of delivery matters, rather than 
just the total amount delivered, the intertemporal impact 
of alleviating suffering due to delivery actions should be 
included in decision making. Holgu´ın-Veras et al. (2013) 
further define the concept of deprivation costs. Numeri­
cal experiments on a deterministic problem with five 
locations and a single vehicle show that when optimiz­
ing social costs (logistics and deprivation), the level of 
service is determined by the balance between depriva­
tion and the ability to deliver supplies. The authors 
show that proxies for deprivation (e.g., equity con­
straints or delivery penalties) in the objective fall short 
and could lead to deprivation costs that are more than 20 
times higher, illustrating that these approaches likely 
promote delivery strategies that fail to mitigate human 
suffering. The authors also argue that the delivery effects 
must be assessed with multiperiod models, as single- 
period formulations cannot account for intertemporal 
effects. Holgu´ın-Veras et al. (2016) designed an estima­
tion process for the deprivation cost function and 
obtained one for drinkable water. Other deprivation cost 
functions have also been discussed in literature (Shao 
et al. 2020). Because of the exponential growth of depri­
vation costs over time, decision makers are encouraged 
to focus on minimizing deprivation costs in situations 
with scarcity, whereas, in situations with sufficient sup­
plies, the focus leans toward minimizing transportation 
costs (P´erez-Rodr´ıguez and Holgu´ın-Veras 2016). Con­
sidering social costs in the objective could balance the 
conflicting goals and result in an operation that is both 
efficient in terms of low transportation costs and effective 
in terms of low deprivation costs. Numerical experiments 
of P´erez-Rodr´ıguez and Holgu´ın-Veras (2016) demon­
strate that this tradeoff between deprivation costs and 
delivery costs leads to optimal solutions. In these solu­
tions, locations closer to the depot receive more goods 
and are supplied more frequently, although remote loca­
tions experience higher deprivation costs.
Although multiperiod planning is important, antici­
patory approaches are needed to tackle the inherent 
uncertainties associated with dynamically changing 
postdisaster areas (Hoyos, Morales, and Akhavan- 
Tabatabaei 2015, Besiou and Van Wassenhove 2020). 
Demand in response phases is uncertain and may 
evolve quickly due to environmental dynamics such 
as the mobilization of the population (Anaya-Arenas, 
Ruiz, and Renaud 2016). Agencies have to deal with 
inaccurate and evolving information regarding supply 
and demand. Obtaining correct estimates after disas­
ters remains challenging (Lin et al. 2011), especially 
when reliable data for full planning horizons is una­
vailable (Sheu 2010). These uncertainties and dynam­
ics prohibit the use of a fixed allocation plan for an 
entire response operation (Rottkemper, Fischer, and 
Blecken 2012, Huang et al. 2015).
Besides dynamic planning, new technologies also 
offer opportunities to deal with the evolving nature of a 
disaster response phase (Besiou and Van Wassenhove 
2020). Humanitarian unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) 
exemplify such a new technology that could make 
humanitarian operations more effective and efficient. 
Cargo UAVs are flexible due to their speed, autonomous 
control, and ability to visit hard-to-reach places (Rejeb 
et al. 2021, Van Steenbergen, Mes, and Van Heeswijk 
2023). Their flexibility and ability to deliver goods to 
many different locations offers the potential to address 
the spatio-temporal challenge of allocating scarce goods 
while also adapting to the dynamically evolving disaster 
environment. Deploying multiple transportation modes 
has already yielded valuable results in humanitarian 
operations models, increasing cost-efficiency (Vanajaku­
mari, Kumar, and Gupta 2016), decreasing response 
times (Alem, Clark, and Moreno 2016), improving secu­
rity (Van Steenbergen et al. 2023), and mitigating travel 
time uncertainty (Van Steenbergen, Mes, and Van Hees­
wijk 2023).
We established that (i) incorporating social costs in a 
multiperiod perspective is crucial to accurately mea­
sure the impact of humanitarian operations and to 
solve the intrinsic spatio-temporal decision problem, 
(ii) uncertainty and changing conditions are inherent to 
disaster areas and should be incorporated through 
anticipatory planning, and (iii) UAVs could play a key 
role in this setting. In this paper, we address these chal­
lenges and opportunities. We introduce the stochastic 
dynamic postdisaster inventory allocation problem 
(SDPDIAP) with uncertainty in supply and demand. 
The aim is to effectively allocate scarce supplies to mul­
tiple affected districts over time (i.e., spatio-temporal 
allocation) using trucks and UAVs, in a way that mini­
mizes transportation costs and deprivation costs. The 
contributions of this work are (i) the formulation of a 
Markov decision process (MDP) model that captures 
the stochastic dynamic nature of the problem, (ii) the 
incorporation of social costs in this stochastic dynamic 
problem, (iii) the development of a computationally 
efficient anticipatory solution method that handles the 
uncertainty and exploits the spatio-temporal nature of 
the problem by learning value functions for each indi­
vidual district at each point in time, (iv) the develop­
ment of a second solution method that captures the 
spatio-temporal and nonlinear problem elements with 
a neural network value function approximation, (v) 
numerical experiments that offer insights into the sto­
chastic dynamic allocation of scarce supplies, tradeoffs 
between transportation costs and deprivation costs and

2. Related Literature
In this section, we cover related work regarding multi­
period allocation problems in humanitarian logistics. 
We review works regarding stochastic dynamic supply 
allocation in humanitarian operations in Section 2.1, 
reinforcement learning approaches in humanitarian 
resource allocation problems in Section 2.2, and the 
variety of objective functions in humanitarian multi­
period problems in Section 2.3. We conclude with the 
contribution statement in Section 2.4.
2.1. Stochastic Dynamic Humanitarian 
Supply Allocation
To anticipate uncertainties and adapt to changes in a 
disaster area, various stochastic dynamic solution meth­
ods have been developed. Adjustable robust optimiza­
tion is considered by Ben-Tal et al. (2011) for allocating 
response operations and guiding evacuation traffic flows 
using a polyhedral uncertainty set for the demand. In a 
dynamic setting, the robust solution outperforms both 
deterministic and stochastic programming counterparts. 
Vanajakumari, Kumar, and Gupta (2016) present an 
integrated location-allocation-routing model under bud­
get constraints to minimize response times. New plans 
are dynamically generated to include updates of the 
realized demand and forecasted demand each period. 
Analysis of the results shows that a mix of different 
truck types is more cost-efficient than a single truck 
type. Natarajan and Swaminathan (2017) study an 
inventory allocation problem for allocating scarce medi­
cal supplies to patients by formulating a finite horizon 
stochastic dynamic programming model. Their devel­
oped expected value heuristic and threshold heuristic 
obtain on average a 5% gap against perfect information 
lower bounds and at least 10% improvement compared 
with a first-come-first-serve heuristic that is often used 
in practice. Liu et al. (2019) present a multiperiod distri­
bution model with helicopters that considers both 
injured people and relief commodities, minimizing the 
unsatisfied demand. Demand and supply are uncertain 
and a robust model predictive control approach is pro­
posed to obtain robust plans and update them with new 
information. The study shows that the adjustments 
improve the results compared with a robust plan created 
for the entire horizon and the authors suggest an exten­
sion with multiple transportation modes for a more uni­
versal approach. Zhan et al. (2021) propose a dynamic 
multiperiod relief allocation model with demand uncer­
tainty. Sequential decisions are made using particle 
swarm optimization with a voting strategy. Considering 
limited supplies, decisions include serving a demand 
node now or temporarily neglecting the node. The objec­
tive is to minimize the total unmet demands weighted 
by a time-based penalty. Soghrati Ghasbeh, Pourmoham­
madzia, and Rabbani (2022) develop a robust, multicom­
modity, multistage model to address the facility location 
and relief distribution considering uncertainty in demand 
and budget. By sequentially solving the model using 
adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) and multidi­
mensional local search (MDLS), robust distribution plans 
are obtained that minimize the unsatisfied demand and 
travel time. In general, the stochastic dynamic models are 
often not computationally efficient, hence various works 
have proposed (meta)heuristics to solve the problems 
each period with new information. A more generic 
approach for these sequential decisions is reinforcement 
learning, which is discussed in the section below.
2.2. Reinforcement Learning for Humanitarian 
Allocation Problems
A variety of reinforcement learning methods, in which 
a policy is trained to make decisions, have been pro­
posed to solve humanitarian allocation problems. Yu 
et al. (2019) present a rollout algorithm for a determin­
istic multiperiod allocation problem in which for each 
period a single demand unit is allocated to one district. 
The aim is to minimize transportation and deprivation 
costs. The algorithm provides competitive results 
against exact dynamic programming. Yu et al. (2021) 
further improved results with tabular Q-learning. Q- 
learning is compared with exact dynamic programming 
and a greedy heuristic. With deterministic instances up 
to eight demand points and 12 periods, Q-learning 
yields optimal solutions with computation times up to 
six times smaller than exact dynamic programming. 
Fan et al. (2022) formulate an MDP for an allocation 
problem to minimize transportation costs and depriva­
tion costs. With problem sizes up to 10 districts and 
three allocation units each period, deep Q-learning 
performs better than tabular Q-learning in terms of 
calculation time and the objective value, whereas an 
exact algorithm fails to provide an optimal solution 
within a reasonable time limit. A related application for 
reinforcement learning in humanitarian operations is 
the allocation of response teams to specific districts.

2.3. Objectives in Humanitarian Operations
Humanitarian operations often involve multiple objec­
tives that need to be balanced. Besides transportation 
costs or travel distances, research focuses on response 
times (Najafi, Eshghi, and de Leeuw 2014, Lu, Ying, 
and Chen 2016, Vanajakumari, Kumar, and Gupta 
2016), unsatisfied demands (Balcik, Beamon, and Smi­
lowitz 2008, Lin et al. 2011, Liu et al. 2019, Yang et al. 
2023), and equity (Tzeng, Cheng, and Huang 2007, 
Huang et al. 2015, Wang and Sun 2023). De Vries and 
Van Wassenhove (2020) argue that these kinds of objec­
tives are more important than minimizing transporta­
tion costs when (i) resources are scarce and (ii) the 
urgency of incoming requests can be identified prop­
erly. In recent years, research focus has shifted toward 
including deprivation costs in the objective function 
(Rivera-Royero, Galindo, and Yie-Pinedo 2016, P´erez- 
Rodr´ıguez and Holgu´ın-Veras 2016, Moreno et al. 2018, 
Huang and Rafiei 2019, Yu et al. 2021, Sadeghi et al. 
2023). These approaches not only naturally enforce 
resolving unsatisfied demands (as deprivation occurs 
during lack of goods or services) but also promote 
equity and acceptable response times due to the super­
linear growth of deprivation costs over time. An over­
view of research on deprivation costs in humanitarian 
logistics can be found in Shao et al. (2020), which con­
cludes that most works using deprivation costs are lim­
ited to deterministic settings. In stochastic problems, 
research mainly considers minimizing unmet demands, 
costs, or response times rather than deprivation costs 
(except for the two-stage stochastic program of Moreno 
et al. 2018). A clear gap that remains unexplored 
concerns stochastic dynamic multiperiod models that 
include deprivation costs, yet it is an important problem 
class to study. Disaster areas are inherently dynamic 
and stochastic, and multiperiod problems capture the 
intertemporal effects of deprivation during an operation 
(P´erez-Rodr´ıguez and Holgu´ın-Veras 2016). Depriva­
tion costs offer the opportunity to balance the conflicting 
goals of minimizing operational costs and increasing 
beneficiaries’ welfare. In summary, incorporating depri­
vation costs in objective functions brings stochastic 
dynamic problems in humanitarian operations much 
closer to practice, yet this setup is understudied as of 
now.
2.4. Contributions
Stochastic dynamic solution methods have shown supe­
rior results in humanitarian allocation problems com­
pared with static variants as they adapt their plans to 
the evolving nature of a disaster area. Various multiper­
iod models have been proposed that include the uncer­
tainty of demand and supply. Among these models, 
often unsatisfied demand is minimized besides logistics 
costs, whereas deprivation costs, as explicit incorpora­
tion of human suffering in the objective, have not yet 
been considered in stochastic dynamic allocation pro­
blems. Nonetheless, the consideration of deprivation is 
essential to focus on human suffering, especially in 
situations with scarcity of supplies. In humanitarian 
applications, learning-based heuristics have been devel­
oped, but these only consider limited action spaces and 
mostly deterministic problems in the humanitarian allo­
cation context. Appendix A summarizes the literature 
related to our work, including works in the broader field 
of stochastic dynamic allocation problems, to which we 
refer in Section 4 regarding our solution approach. We 
contribute to the literature by addressing the stochastic 
dynamic postdisaster inventory allocation problem 
(SDPDIAP) with uncertain supply and demand that 
aims to minimize transportation costs and deprivation 
costs. To this end, we design two reinforcement learning 
methods based on approximate dynamic programming 
that anticipate the evolving nature of the aftermath of a 
disaster: one designed for the highest performance and 
the other for excellent scalability. Additionally, we 
provide practical insights into dealing with scarcity of 
supplies during dynamic disaster response operations 
through the use of deprivation costs. Finally, we show 
how the deployment of cargo UAVs offers opportunities 
to further improve transport efficiency and reduce dep­
rivation costs.
3. Model Formulation
This section formalizes the model for our humanitarian 
allocation problem. Considering the multiperiodicity 
required to fully integrate deprivation costs during 
humanitarian operations (Holgu´ın-Veras et al. 2013) 
and uncertainties and dynamics in a disaster area 
(Anaya-Arenas, Ruiz, and Renaud 2016), we describe 
the stochastic dynamic postdisaster inventory alloca­
tion problem (SDPDIAP) with uncertain supply and 
demand. The main challenge is to allocate supplies, 
which are generally limited in disaster areas (Najafi, 
Eshghi, and Dullaert 2013), such that the operation is 
efficient in terms of minimizing transportation costs 
but also effective in alleviating human suffering by 
minimizing deprivation costs. We first describe the

3.1. Problem Description
We build on the widely recognized relief supply chain 
as described by Balcik, Beamon, and Smilowitz (2008), 
in which a central warehouse (CW) forms the main 
entry point for incoming supplies into the disaster area 
and allocates the supplies to local distribution centers 
in specific districts. From the local distribution centers, 
last-mile distribution is performed to deliver supplies 
to beneficiaries in the districts. We focus on the alloca­
tion problem of the CW toward the districts.

the CW over time according to a stochastic process. 
Allocation decisions for these supplies are made by the 
humanitarian agency operating the CW at decision

4. Solution Methods
In principle, the optimal policy can be obtained by solv­
ing Equation (11); however, this is computationally 
intractable in our problem setting, as we face the three 
curses of dimensionality (Powell 2011). First, the out­
come space is large, determined by the random infor­
mation regarding supply and demand, and it increases 
exponentially with a higher number of districts. Sec­
ond, we cannot learn the values of each individual state 
as the inventory positions and demands within a state 
can take many values. Third, the decision space is vast 
due to the combinatorial nature of the supply allocation 
across multiple districts with different transportation 
modes. We adopt the approximate dynamic program­
ming (ADP) framework of Powell (2011) to address 
these problems and extend this with a decomposition 
approach and a neural network approach. ADP is a 
simulation-based solution method that aims to learn a 
value function approximation (VFA) that gives the esti­
mated future costs. Future costs are defined by the 
value of being in the next state Vt+1(St+1). The problem 
is solved by iteratively simulating the MDP and learn­
ing the value of being in a certain state by observing the 
costs. In Section 4.1, we describe how we deal with the 
high-dimensional outcome space; in Section 4.2, we dis­
cuss how we handle the large state space and input fea­
tures for the value functions; in Section 4.3, we describe 
how we solve the combinatorial action space; and the 
overall algorithmic outline is presented in Section 4.4.

4.2. State Space
The state space is large, because of the many values 
that inventory positions and demands can take. For 
moderately sized problems, we must already perform 
an intractable number of computations to visit each 
postdecision state at least once to estimate the future 
costs. Furthermore, the state space grows significantly 
with each additional district to serve, increasing the state 
dimension with four variables. The general approach to 
deal with large state spaces is to substitute the true value 
function Vt(Sx

is that it avoids full enumeration of the state space to 
obtain future costs of each state. We design two alterna­
tive VFA approaches: (i) a decomposed linear value 
function approximation (DL-VFA) aimed at scalability, 
which decomposes the state in district states and learns 
separate linear value functions for each district at each 
decision epoch, and (ii) a neural network VFA (NN- 
VFA) in which one neural network approximates all 
state values, designed to better capture nonlinearities. 
We briefly outline both methods.

4.3. Combinatorial Action Space
At each decision epoch, we decide on the combination 
of the supply quantities for (i) each district and (ii) each 
transportation mode. Such combinatorial action spaces 
are common in transportation, but they pose a chal­
lenge for standard reinforcement learning algorithms 
(Dulac-Arnold et al. 2021) that require full enumeration 
of the action space. Furthermore, standard algorithms 
can often not handle constrained action spaces, resort­
ing to masking to filter out infeasible actions.
To efficiently select actions in the large constrained 
action space in DL-VFA and NN-VFA, we formulate the 
single-stage decision problem as a mixed-integer pro­
gram (MIP) and solve this using a general-purpose 
solver. The MIP solves the approximate Bellman equa­
tion and handles the constraints, such as the limited sup­
plies in the central warehouse. In this case, the VFAs 
are directly integrated into the MIP. This immensely 
increases the potential action spaces we can handle, as 
the single-stage decision problem can be solved in a frac­
tion of the time required for enumeration while preserv­
ing optimality guarantees in selecting the best action 
under the prevailing policy. This approach, using a sin­
gle (piecewise) linear VFA in each stage, is applied in 
stochastic dynamic problems such as intermodal trans­
port (Rivera-Royero, Galindo, and Yie-Pinedo 2016, Hei­
nold, Meisel, and Ulmer 2023), ridesharing systems (Lei, 
Jiang, and Ouyang 2019, Beirigo, Schulte, and Negen­
born 2022), and delivery dispatching problems (Klapp, 
Erera, and Toriello 2018, Van Heeswijk, Mes, and Schut­
ten 2019). For the DL-VFA, our methodological contri­
bution lies in the decomposition of the state and 
accompanying VFA. The decomposition yields one VFA 
for each district and each epoch. The VFAs for all dis­
tricts in one epoch are then integrated into the single- 
stage decision problem. By solving this problem, the 
optimal combination of allocations is made given the 
district-based value functions.

This section describes the algorithms to learn the 
value function approximations in DL-VFA and 
NN-VFA. Our approach is based on ADP, which itera­
tively samples the stochastic process to update value 
functions and improve future decisions (Powell 2011). 
We describe this process for DL-VFA, detailed in 
Algorithm 1, and discuss the modifications for NN- 
VFA.

start with reasonable estimates for the initial value 
functions. Preliminary experiments showed that ran­

tiple value functions are included in the MIP and 
poor estimates preclude properly weighing the value 
functions against each other. To obtain initial esti­
mates, we generate a large set of trajectories with 
postdecision states and costs using a warm-up heuris­
tic and estimate initial value functions with linear 
regression based on this set. We refer to this set of tra­

Z1 ~ unif{1,3} and thereafter selects a random district

potential benefit of deploying cargo UAVs for the dis­
tribution of relief supplies, and (3) to analyze the 
impact of optimizing the social costs (combining logis­
tics and deprivation costs) on the humanitarian opera­
tion. To achieve this, we consider various settings of 
theoretical instances (Section 5.1) and a case study of 
the Nepal 2015 earthquake (Section 5.2). In Section 5.3, 
we describe the different benchmark methods consid­

5.1. Theoretical Instances
First, we analyze the performance, scalability, and gen­
eralizability of the proposed methods on theoretical 
instances. These instances contain standardized values 
for transportation costs and demand with one to six 
districts, inspired by the Nepal case that contains 13 
districts. The experiments on the theoretical instances 
are divided into (i) experiments with different problem 
sizes varying the number of districts and (ii) experi­
ments with different problem variants with regard to 
demand and vehicle settings.
5.1.1. Different Problem Sizes. Methods are tested 
using six theoretical instances with one central ware­
house and one to six districts portraying different levels 
of complexity to analyze how the different methods 
scale and to provide insight into the decision-making 
approaches. Each instance consists of 30 periods of six 
hours (about one week), assuming multiple decisions 
each day. Districts have distinct demand levels and 
transportation costs, and both supply and demand fol­
low normal distributions with a coefficient of variation 
(CoV) of 0.2. As transportation modes, we consider (i) 
trucks that can transport 5,000 kg of supplies and (ii) 
UAVs that can load 200 kg of supplies, inspired by 
Wings for Aid MiniFreighter (Bamsey 2021). To accu­
rately characterize UAVs, we collaborate in this study 
with the Wings for Aid foundation, which develops a 
UAV system that delivers humanitarian goods to peo­
ple affected by natural disasters and man-made crises. 
The specific demand levels and transportation costs for 
different numbers of districts are listed in Appendix D
in Table D.1, which are standardized values inspired by 
the Nepal case that is discussed in Section 5.2.
To gain more insight into the various methods, we also 
report the learning curves of the methods to reveal their 
convergence, illustrate differences in decision-making by 
analyzing single-stage decisions, and uncover the future 
cost estimates by the neural network of NN-VFA by illus­
trating estimates for a diverse set of states.
5.1.2. Different Problem Variants. For in-depth analy­
sis, we evaluate five alternative scenarios using the 
three-district instance to analyze how the various 
methods perform in different settings. In the first sce­
nario, (i) only trucks are used for supply allocation, 
serving as a baseline to quantify the added value of 
UAVs. As we aim to reveal differences between the 
trucks and UAVs instance and the truck-only instance, 
we will analyze the deprivation costs over time and 
districts for both cases to highlight the observed differ­
ences between these scenarios. In the second and third 
scenarios, different levels of uncertainty are evaluated 
as levels of uncertainty can vary between different 
disaster scenarios in practice: (ii) a low-uncertainty sce­
nario with a CoV of 0.1 and (iii) a high-uncertainty sce­
nario with a CoV of 0.3. The fourth and fifth scenario 
consider two distinct S-curved demand patterns to ana­
lyze how the methods handle dynamic demand pat­
terns: (iv) a logistic decline of demand starting at 150% 
of the supply rate and ending at 50% of the supply rate 
and (v) a logistic growth of demand starting at 50% of

at the end of the horizon. These scenarios describe the 
dynamics in the environment by either portraying a 
surge in demand at the start of the horizon or a situa­

5.2. Nepal 2015 Earthquake Case
In April 2015, a 7.8M earthquake hit Nepal and another 
7.3M earthquake two weeks later added to the catastro­
phe. Thirteen districts outside the capital city Kath­
mandu were heavily affected, with the mountainous 
surroundings of several districts hampering road trans­
port. A main central warehouse was set up at the air­
port of Kathmandu, which received supplies from all 
over the world. The Nepal Red Cross Society coordi­
nated the disaster response, and local offices in the 13 
districts set up distribution centers for the last-mile 
delivery of supplies in their districts. An overview of 
the disaster area and impact on houses is shown in 
Figure 2. We use data provided in Johnsson (2016) 
regarding the 13 districts. Truck costs are based on (i) 
the road distance from Kathmandu to the district capi­
tals, (ii) the average geographical slope in the district, 
and (iii) the road density, in which the latter two indi­
cate the level of difficulty navigating toward the dis­
tricts. UAV costs are based on Euclidean distances. 
Demand is relative to the damaged houses, assuming 
that every 24 hours, 2% of the damaged houses require 
supplies. The specific demand levels and transporta­
tion costs for the Nepal case are listed in Appendix D
in Table D.3.
For this case, we evaluate multiple scenarios to ana­
lyze the performance and flexibility of the proposed 
methods in a real-world setting and to examine the 
practical aspects of UAV deployment and the impor­
tance of considering deprivation costs.
5.2.1. Trucks and UAVs. The first scenario involves 
both trucks and UAVs, similar to that considered in 
the theoretical experiments on different problem sizes. 
This scenario involves 13 districts and can demonstrate 
the performance of the proposed methods and the 
benchmark methods on a real-world case.
5.2.2. Trucks Only. To determine the impact of incor­
porating UAVs in humanitarian operations in a real- 
world setting, we perform a second experiment that 
excludes UAVs. The comparison between the results of 
the Trucks and UAVs scenario and this scenario pro­
vides an assessment of the effectiveness and differences 
in operational decisions when including UAVs.

5.3. Benchmark Methods
To analyze the performance of the proposed methods, 
we compare the results with a range of distinctive 
solution methods. As a lower bound, we solve a perfect 
information relaxation, and as comparative approaches, 
we compare the proposed methods with an exact reop­
timization approach, a rule-based heuristic, and proxi­
mal policy optimization (PPO), which is a state-of-the- 
art reinforcement learning method. We discuss these 
methods in the following sections.

5.3.2. Reoptimization Approach. Each decision epoch, 
the reoptimization approach solves the same MIP model 
as the PI bound, but with expected values instead of 
sample realizations and only for the remaining time 
horizon [t,:::,T]. The model is updated every decision 
epoch by incorporating the previous decision and cur­
rent inventory positions based on realized supply and 
demand. Thus, it offers exact solutions given the knowl­
edge available at the current epoch. To accommodate 
demand uncertainty with this deterministic model, we 
account for an additional margin 2σdt,t+1,n for the next 
period demand, similar to the VFA approaches. Reopti­
mization follows the objective of the PI bound in Equa­
tion (41), but only for the remaining horizon [t,:::,T].
5.3.3. Rule-Based Heuristic. To mimic a practical situ­
ation without the use of advanced algorithms or sol­
vers, we evaluate a rule-based heuristic. This heuristic 
is comparable to the warm-up agent of DL-VFA and 
NN-VFA, but without stochasticity in decision making. 
For each decision epoch, if the deprivation time δn at 
district n is two, then sufficient UAVs are allocated to 
cover the expected demand. Thereafter, the district 
with the highest deprivation costs is selected and the 
UAV allocation plus all remaining goods in the CW are 
allocated to that district using trucks.

6. Numerical Results
This section presents the numerical results of DL-VFA, 
NN-VFA, and the benchmark policies. The purpose of 
the experiments is threefold, addressing (1) the algo­
rithmic performance of the proposed solution methods, 
(2) the practical insights into the benefits of deploying 
cargo UAVs, and (3) the impact of integrating social 
costs into the objective function.
First, we consider different problem sizes in Section 
6.1 to study the performance and scalability of the pro­
posed methods. Additionally, we provide insights into 
the single-stage decision-making capabilities of the dif­
ferent approaches. Thereafter, we consider different 
problem variants in Section 6.2 to study the generaliz­
ability of the methods and to identify the added value 
of UAVs under varying conditions. To analyze the 
methods in a real-world setting, we present the results 
of the Nepal case in Section 6.3. This section also stud­
ies alternative scenarios to investigate the added value 
of UAVs and the consideration of deprivation costs. 
We summarize the key insights in Section 6.4.
We implemented all methods using Python 3.11 and 
Gurobi 10.0.2. Experiments were executed on a 32-core 
2.6-GHz Intel Xeon Platinum 8272CL machine with 64 
GB RAM. We cap training time at four hours for the 
learning-based methods and solving time at four hours 
per sample path for the approximate PI bound and at 
15 minutes for each decision epoch of the reoptimiza­
tion method, granting each solution method roughly 
equal computational budget. If a problem cannot be 
solved to optimality within the computational budget, 
the best solution found up to that point is used and the 
gap with the lower bound is presented. For each 
method, all instances are solved across 10 sample paths 
to obtain estimates of the expected solution value with 
attainable computational effort, and the standard 
deviations are reported. The specific parameter settings 
for each method are summarized in Appendix E.
We recognize that the safety margin is a relevant tun­
ing parameter for the shortage threshold and the antici­
pated deprivation in the methods. We performed a 
preliminary sensitivity analysis on this parameter for 
the base instance with three districts, as illustrated in 
Figure 3. A value between 1.0 and 2.5 seems reasonable, 
with a setting of 2.0 standard deviations providing the 
lowest costs. A low margin (below 1.0) often results in 
delivering too few supplies, resulting in additional deliv­
eries in future periods. A high margin (above 2.5) results 
in delivering unnecessarily large amounts to one district, 
leaving fewer supplies available for other districts.

Problem Sizes
Considering different problem sizes, this section ana­

insights. The algorithmic performance is studied per 
method, including the scalability, computational per­
formance, and convergence behavior. From a manage­

into deprivation costs and UAV deployment costs. For 
the three-district instance, an in-depth post hoc expla­
nation of the single-stage decisions made by each 
method is provided, offering insights into the decisions 
and tradeoffs of the various methods.

algorithmic performance is divided into (1) comparison

6.1.1.1. Performance and Scalability Comparison. 
To analyze the algorithmic performance and scalability 
of the two proposed learning-based methods and the 
four benchmark methods, we perform experiments on 
instances with one to six districts. Table 1 shows all 
results obtained for the six instances.
DL-VFA and NN-VFA consistently outperform the 
reoptimization policy, demonstrating the added value 
of incorporating stochastic information in the solution 
methods. On average, the best results are obtained by 
NN-VFA, outperforming the reoptimization benchmark 
by 7.3% across all instance sizes. However, its perfor­
mance does not scale well to larger instances; with six 
districts, the largest problem in this set, the relative 
performance gain is only 2.2%. DL-VFA outperforms 
the benchmark policies as well, improving on reoptimi­
zation by 6.2% but outperforming NN-VFA in the larg­
est instance (7.8% improvement on reoptimization). 
DL-VFA and NN-VFA outperforming the reoptimiza­
tion policy illustrates the need to anticipate stochastic 
outcomes in the solution method. For all instances, the 
rule-based heuristic performs worse compared with 
DL-VFA, NN-VFA, and reoptimization, as the heuristic 
only considers basic decision rules. PPO performs rea­
sonably well in small instances, but the performance 
drops below that of the rule-based heuristic perfor­
mance in larger instances. This is because of the combi­
natorial increase of the action space, making the 
learning process for PPO increasingly complex. The 
performance of PPO and the rule-based heuristic illus­
trate that basic allocation rules or standard reinforce­
ment learning approaches do not provide adequate 
allocation outcomes.
6.1.1.2. Computational Performance. DL-VFA pro­
vides the best computational efficiency (except for the 
rule-based heuristic), requiring less than 0.002 seconds 
per decision epoch with one district and up to 0.01 sec­
onds per epoch for six districts. NN-VFA demands 
more computational effort with 0.03 seconds per epoch 
for one district and 0.20 seconds per epoch for six dis­
tricts. These results are expected because DL-VFA 
treats the linear value functions as simple constraints 
that are easy to solve. In contrast, the neural network of 
NN-VFA requires adding many constraints in the MIP, 
creating a more complex single-stage decision problem. 
Still, the computation times of both DL-VFA and 
NN-VFA are significant improvements compared with 
the 15-minute computational time limit of reoptimiza­
tion, a period in which DL-VFA can even be retrained 
if necessary. The solving times of both the PI bound 
and reoptimization models increase significantly with 
more districts, as the complete horizon with all spatio- 
temporal dependencies needs to be solved, a signifi­
cantly more complex problem than the single-stage 
decision problems of the VFA methods. With up to 
three districts, the approximate PI bound can be solved 
to optimality across all sample paths. The training time 
of PPO remains stable when increasing the number of 
districts from one to six, performing 60,000 iterations of 
30 periods in less than one hour (60.002 seconds per 
decision epoch), as only the input size and output size 
of the actor and critic networks grow linearly with an 
increasing number of districts. Although PPO compu­
tationally scales well, its performance does not. In sum­
mary, DL-VFA is computationally efficient (more than 
15 times faster than NN-VFA in most cases) and there­
fore showcases excellent scalability to larger problem 
instances, whereas PI bound and reoptimization show 
very limited scalability.

6.1.2.2. Post Hoc Explanation of Single-Stage Deci­
sions. We provide post hoc explanations of the single- 
stage decisions made by the various methods and their 
impact on long-term performance. The solutions for 
each method involve a three-dimensional tensor of allo­
cation decisions per district, period, and vehicle type. 
These allocation decisions result in certain transporta­
tion costs, deprivation times and costs, and demand 
coverages. To provide insight into the individual differ­
ences on a single-stage decision level, we define nine 
representative states (Table 2) and use the different 
methods to generate the decisions for each of these 
states and analyze their long-term impact.
For the sake of brevity, we aggregate allocations of 
both vehicle types and report the district-level alloca­
tions in Table 3. The approximate PI bound provides 
the optimal single-stage decisions using all future 
information in the MIP. Hence, these decisions serve as 
the ideal allocation. DL-VFA and NN-VFA perform 
closest to this benchmark. Decisions of DL-VFA are 
almost similar to the decisions of the approximate PI 
bound, but allocations are rounded up to full UAV 
loads, ensuring efficient vehicle usage. This demon­
strates the effectiveness of the value functions in both 
capturing future dependencies (e.g., demand, uncer­
tainties, deprivation), as well as solving the combinato­
rial single-stage decision problem. DL-VFA did only 
not allocate goods to the first district in state 4 and allo­
cated less to the second district in state 7. NN-VFA 
improves on DL-VFA for these states and generally 
matches the other decisions of DL-VFA. The improve­
ment of NN-VFA is the result of the nonlinear value 
function that provides more accurate estimates of the 
future costs than the linear combination used in 
DL-VFA. The reoptimization approach results in slightly 
higher allocations when a single district faces shortages.

6.2. Performance Analysis for Different 
Problem Variants
This section analyzes the algorithmic performance and 
generalizability of the six methods across different 
problem variants, offers managerial insights regarding 
these variants, and evaluates the added value of UAVs. 
The experiments feature scenarios with only trucks, 
different levels of uncertainty, and different patterns of 
demand over time.
6.2.1. Algorithmic Performance. We assess algorith­
mic performance and generalizability across a variety 
of scenarios. Table 4 shows the results. DL-VFA and 
NN-VFA both outperform the benchmark policies in 
the instances with different levels of uncertainty and 
demand patterns. On average, DL-VFA improves on 
reoptimization by 2% and NN-VFA does so by 7.5%; 
the latter signifies the best results but also the highest 
computational effort. The results further indicate that 
reoptimization performs worse under higher uncertainty, 
whereas DL-VFA and NN-VFA perform consistently 
across different levels of uncertainty. On the trucks-only 
instance, reoptimization performs best because of the 
large truckload allocations, which lowers the need for 
anticipation of uncertainty, reaffirming that DL-VFA and 
NN-VFA are best suited for problem settings requiring 
dynamic allocations. PPO outperforms the rule-based 
heuristic in all instance variants but does not perform 
competitively, signifying that this widely applied rein­
forcement learning algorithm is not suitable for this speci­
fic problem with a combinatorial action space.

6.3. Case Study: Social Costs and Value of UAVs 
in a Real-World Setting
With the Nepal case, we analyze the suitability and 
operational impact of the proposed methods on a 
practice-based instance. The experiments are based on 
the Nepal 2015 earthquake, during which 13 districts 
and the capital Kathmandu were heavily damaged. 
Through four scenarios, trucks and UAVs as default, 
trucks-only, an alternative objective with transporta­
tion costs only, and limited availability of trucks and 
UAVs, the experiments offer insights into algorithmic 
performance and managerial implications. First, the 
algorithmic performance is analyzed for the four sce­
narios. Second, the managerial insights are distilled 
from the operational performance of the scenarios and 
by analyzing the distribution of vehicles and supplies 
over districts and time.
6.3.1. Algorithmic Performance. We assess algorith­
mic performance in the Nepal case. The numerical 
results are presented in Table 5 for the four scenarios. 
For the transportation cost-only and the limited vehicle 
scenarios, the rule-based heuristic and PPO method are 
excluded because enforcing deliveries and limiting the 
number of vehicles would require additional assump­
tions in their heuristic allocations.
For the trucks and UAVs scenario, DL-VFA provides 
the best results with a training time of less than an hour, 
improving on reoptimization by 8%, whereas NN-VFA 
improves on reoptimization by 5%. DL-VFA and 
NN-VFA improve on the rule-based heuristic and PPO 
even more. These results confirm that, although PPO is 
suitable for continuous action spaces, it does not provide 
competitive results for the combinatorial action space at 
hand. Similar to the findings in Section 6.2, reoptimization 
achieves the best results in the trucks-only scenario. It out­
performs the learning-based methods when allocating 
only larger truckloads because they cover demand over 
multiple periods and thereby mitigate the effects of uncer­
tainty. This result affirms that reoptimization is more 
effective in situations in which allocation decisions can 
cover demand for more than 10 periods, whereas the 
learning-based methods are better suited for problems 
requiring more dynamic adjustments of inventory levels. 
Similarly, reoptimization performs best in the transporta­
tion costs-only scenario. For the limited vehicles scenario, 
similarly to the default trucks and UAVs scenario, 
DL-VFA outperforms reoptimization by 8% and 
NN-VFA does so by 5%. Overall, the results of the Nepal 
case imply that the VFA methods are best suited for sce­
narios that require dynamic allocations and nuanced tra­
deoffs between transportation costs and deprivation costs.
6.3.2. Managerial Insights. The managerial insights are 
divided into (1) operational performance and (2) the 
analysis of the distribution of vehicles and supply allo­
cations over time and over districts.
6.3.2.1. Operational Performance. The main opera­
tional insights based on the four scenarios are outlined 
here. The trucks and UAVs scenario, compared with 
the trucks-only scenario, reduces both deprivation costs 
and transportation costs by 18%. Moreover, the maxi­
mum deprivation time across all districts is reduced 
from 36 hours to 26 hours, a significant improvement 
achieved by the additional flexibility offered by UAVs. 
For the transportation costs-only instance, the reoptimi­
zation method obtains the transportation costs closest 
to the approximate PI bound. However, the resulting 
deprivation costs (calculated post hoc) are roughly a 
factor of 10–20 higher for all methods compared with 
the PI bound and also to the original scenario with dep­
rivation costs in the objective. This concurs with the 
observations of Holgu´ın-Veras et al. (2013) that proxies 
for deprivation can lead to deprivation costs that are 20 
times higher. The demand coverage is also lower than 
in cases that consider deprivation costs, illustrating that

6.4. Key Insights
We summarize the key insights based on the algorith­
mic performance of the different methods and manage­
rial insights related to the deployment of UAVs and the 
impact of using social costs in the objective function.
6.4.1. Algorithmic Performance. To summarize all 
experiments, in the instances with varying numbers of 
districts, we find that DL-VFA provides the best computa­
tional efficiency and closely matches the best results. The 
Nepal case also reflects these results, in which DL-VFA 
improves on the benchmarks by 8%–31% with limited 
training time (at most 55 minutes compared with multiple 
hours of solving time for the other approaches). For the 
smaller instances (up to five districts), NN-VFA would be 
the recommended approach because it adapts well to 
different problem variants and captures nonlinearities in 
downstream costs. For larger instances, DL-VFA is the 
preferred method, providing excellent performance, 
strong scalability, and explainability through linear 
weights. However, NN-VFA may be the preferred 
choice for larger instances when computational 
resources are abundant. Both approaches leverage 
learned value functions that estimate future costs and 
account for future uncertainty, such that only a single- 
stage decision MIP needs to be solved. This MIP is 
significantly less complex than the problems that the 
reoptimization benchmark solves over the complete 
planning horizon. The rule-based heuristic falls short as 
the predefined rules do not always provide suitable 
allocations, and the alternative reinforcement learning 
algorithm PPO demonstrates to fall short in handling 
the combinatorial action space of this problem setting.

6.4.2. Managerial Insights. Our experimental results 
highlight the significant role of UAVs in both the theo­
retical instances and the Nepal case. We observed that 
UAVs are essential in the initial and final stages to 
evenly distribute scarce supplies and to deliver smaller 
amounts to remote locations relatively cheaply. Scenar­
ios in which UAVs are deployed reduce deprivation 
costs by 18%–22% and transportation costs by 13%–18% 
while reducing maximum deprivation times by 6–14 
hours and maintaining the same levels of demand cov­
erage. Alongside this, the social costs objective function 
(including both transportation and deprivation costs) 
strikes a balance between the deprivation per district 
and the efforts to perform the delivery. The scenario in 
which only transportation costs are optimized yields 
deprivation costs that are 10–20 times higher than in the 
scenarios optimizing social costs, although supply allo­
cations are enforced after two periods of deprivation. 
This demonstrates that explicitly optimizing social costs 
is more effective than optimizing proxies for depriva­
tion. Solutions that optimize for social costs show that 
locations closer to the depot and with more demand 
received more goods, whereas remote locations are sup­
plied less frequently by trucks. These managerial 
insights are consistent with the findings of related 
works (Holgu´ın-Veras et al. 2013, P´erez-Rodr´ıguez and 
Holgu´ın-Veras 2016, Rivera-Royero, Galindo, and Yie- 
Pinedo 2016) that consider deterministic problems with 
a single vehicle type. The addition of UAVs changes the 
situation for the remote locations being less visited by 
trucks because these are then more often supplied by 
UAVs. The combination of optimizing social costs and 
deploying trucks and UAVs results in a reasonably 
even spatio-temporal distribution of supplies and depri­
vation, providing an efficient operation while striking a 
balance between the needs and efforts.
7. Conclusions
In this paper, we investigated the impact of deprivation 
costs and the role of UAVs by introducing the SDPDIAP 
with uncertain supply and demand. Deprivation costs 
are an important metric to address the spatio-temporal 
consequences of allocating scarce relief supplies over 
multiple affected districts, whereas humanitarian UAVs 
offer the potential to cost-effectively and flexibly distrib­
ute supplies in the aftermath of a disaster. We consider 
social costs, that is, the combination of transportation 
and deprivation costs, in a stochastic dynamic setting 
and formulate the SDPDIAP as a Markov decision 
process model. As this model is computationally intrac­
table to solve, we propose two anticipatory policies 
based on approximate dynamic programming. First, we 
propose a DL-VFA that decomposes the problem in 
spatio-temporal value functions, providing high perfor­
mance and excellent scalability, with the combinatorial 
allocation of supplies in each period being handled by 
solving an MIP that includes the spatially decomposed 
value functions of the respective period. Second, we 
propose an NN-VFA that captures the spatio-temporal 
and nonlinear effects in a single neural network, which 
is integrated into an MIP that is used to solve the single- 
stage combinatorial allocation problem. We compare 
the performance of DL-VFA and NN-VFA with an 
approximate perfect information bound, an exact reop­
timization approach, a rule-based heuristic, and PPO. 
Up to five districts, NN-VFA provides the best results 
(2.6% less costs than DL-VFA and 8.3% less costs than 
reoptimization on average). DL-VFA shows excellent 
scalability, with the ability to train value functions with 
limited computational effort, improving on NN-VFA by 
4.3% and reoptimization by 7% for larger instances (6 
and 13 districts). Furthermore, DL-VFA provides clear 
explainability by the linear weights of the value func­
tions, which is a valuable trait in disaster environments. 
DL-VFA improves the rule-based heuristic and PPO on 
average by 22.7% and 23.7%, respectively, whereas 
NN-VFA improves the rule-based heuristic and PPO on 
average by 16.9% and 17.5%, respectively. NN-VFA 
proves to be the preferred approach for smaller 
instances and in situations with ample computational 
resources, whereas DL-VFA is preferred for larger 
instances, situations requiring computational efficiency 
and explainability of decisions.
By considering both transportation and deprivation 
costs, the best solutions allocate goods to all districts, 
but districts with higher demands and lower transpor­
tation costs receive relatively more. This indicates a bal­
ance between the beneficiaries’ needs and the efforts to 
fulfill these needs, instead of a focus on the ones most 
straightforward to reach or providing an equal service 
to everyone. In a practical instance that minimizes 
transportation costs only and enforces deliveries to dis­
tricts that have not received sufficient supplies over 
two periods, results fall short in reducing human suf­
fering compared with the proposed models minimiz­
ing social costs, with resulting deprivation cost higher 
by a factor of 10–20. Results show that districts with rel­
atively higher transportation costs and lower demands 
are visited more by UAVs than trucks. When compar­
ing trucks-only scenarios with scenarios including 
trucks and UAVs, the addition of UAVs provides 
reductions in deprivation costs of 18%–22%, reductions 
in transportation costs between 13% and 18%, and 
reductions in maximum deprivation times of 19%–40% 
while maintaining a similar coverage of demand. Gen­
erally speaking, multiple transportation modes, for 
example, a bulk mode and a flexible mode (in our case 
trucks and UAVs) broaden the opportunities for 
humanitarian operations that are both cost-efficient 
and effective in alleviating human suffering. UAVs 
especially play a crucial role at the operations’ start and

end by distributing scarce supplies in smaller amounts 
among various districts.
We demonstrated the flexibility of the model and 
our proposed methods. With a few additional con­
straints, we were able to evaluate a more traditional 
objective and consider a scenario with limited vehicles. 
This opens up opportunities for future work beyond 
the focus of this paper. Besides limited vehicles, one 
could also consider the limited accessibility of districts, 
other practical constraints, and operating rules for 
decision making. Although we imposed deterministic 
limitations, the model could also be extended to 
stochastic vehicle availability or district accessibility 
to enrich the current model. The learning-based 
approaches DL-VFA and NN-VFA can be trained to 
adapt and mitigate the impact of these stochastic 
elements. The spatio-temporal decomposition of the 
value functions in DL-VFA also allows for different 
future research directions toward more complex allo­
cation decisions. For example, multidepot problems in 
which supplies from multiple warehouses can be allo­
cated to districts or interdistrict transhipments with 
supplies being rebalanced between districts after ini­
tial allocation.

Appendix G. Linear Weights
To better understand the value functions of DL-VFA, which 
underpin the single-stage decision problems, we visualize 
the learned linear weights for a scenario with three districts. 
These weights are determined for each period and district. 
The weights of the intercept, district inventory level, and 
anticipated deprivation are visualized in Figure G.1. Note 
that because of the decomposed structure of DL-VFA, the 
linear weights are learned separately for each period and 
district.
The intercept represents the baseline of the linear func­
tion, with discounted future costs decreasing near the end 
of the planning horizon, as intuitively expected. Intercept 
values vary across districts: District 2, with the highest 
demand, shows the highest baseline costs, whereas District 
1, with medium demand and low transportation costs, has 
the lowest. District 3 falls in between, with low demand but 
high transportation costs contributing to higher expected 
future costs.
The weights for inventory levels reveal a distinct pattern. 
Holding inventory in District 1 has relatively little impact 
on future costs, likely due to its low transportation costs. 
In contrast, high inventory levels in District 3 significantly 
reduce future costs, reflecting its higher transportation 
costs. Hence, the impact of supply levels on the estimated 
future costs largely depends on the underlying transporta­
tion costs of the supplies. These weights decrease near the 
end, as inventory levels only need to cover demand until 
the end of the horizon.
Weights for anticipated deprivation are roughly similar 
across districts and generally increase over time, likely reflect­
ing the reduction in the overall scarcity of supplies. When scar­
city reduces over time, deprivation can be resolved more 
easily, as demonstrated by the increased weights when depri­
vation is not resolved. These weights, ranging between 1.0 and 
2.5, suggest that this not only includes the anticipated costs for 
the next period but also factors in potential costs beyond, such 
as future deprivation or transportation costs.

Appendix H. Estimated Future Costs
To provide additional insight into the cost effects of the 
neural network value function of NN-VFA, we visualize 
the output of the trained neural network on the 1-district 
instance on a range of states in Figure H.1. The future 
costs, predicted by the neural network, are obtained for 
periods 1–30, with district inventory levels ranging from 0 
to 2,000 in steps of 25 and anticipated deprivation levels 
ranging from 0 to 500 in steps of 5. This results in a total 
of 245,430 states providing a fine-grained overview.
As intuitively expected, future costs decrease near the 
end of the horizon. Furthermore, higher inventory levels 
lead to significantly lower future costs, reflecting reduced 
transportation needs and the expectation of less depriva­
tion. Higher anticipated deprivation costs increase future 
costs; this effect is smaller than with the inventory levels, 
but it decreases less over the horizon. This lower decrease 
for anticipated deprivation indicates a shorter-term effect 
than with inventory positions, whose effects can last 
across the entire horizon.
Both figures show gradual changes in future costs, dem­
onstrating that the neural network provides consistent 
future costs in the single-stage decision problem. This pre­
vents erratic decisions arising from states with minor dif­
ferences, and accurate future cost estimates will result in 
valid single-stage decisions.
