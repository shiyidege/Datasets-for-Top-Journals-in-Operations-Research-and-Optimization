Abstract: Determining inventory ahead of knowing customer demand is a crucial decision for many 
businesses. Although it is possible to select the ideal order quantity given the costs of over and 
undersupplying demand, prior research demonstrates that decision-makers consistently make suboptimal 
inventory choices. Drawing on construal level theory and psychological distance, this paper explores how 
differences in spatial distance (suppliers that are geographically near or far) and temporal distance (short or 
long lead time from order to receipt) impact newsvendor decision-making. Across four experiments (N = 
663), we find that a far psychological distance increases order quantities, which improves performance for 
high-margin products but results in added costs for low-margin goods. Given that spatial and temporal 
distance occur naturally in supply chains, our research shows that psychological distance is a cognitive 
explanation for biased inventory ordering and can be applied to improve decision-making. 
Keywords: Psychological distance, newsvendor problem, behavioral operations, construal level theory, 
inventory management.

1. Introduction 
The increasing global interdependence of supply chains creates many challenges and uncertainties 
for managers (Jain et al. 2014, Graves 2021, Shih 2022). Specific to policy, supply chain design and supplier 
selection are influenced by many considerations including cost, lead time, and reliability. While supply 
chains in the early 2000s were often characterized by outsourcing to overseas suppliers, rising costs (e.g., 
for labor and transportation) and competitive pressures have led firms to consider re-shoring or multi-
sourcing to benefit from complementary competencies (Boute and Van Mieghem 2015). Typically, local 
suppliers are considered more responsive, whereas global suppliers are more cost-effective yet have greater 
lead times (Boute and Van Mieghem 2015). For example, Allon and Van Mieghem (2010) considered 
inventory ordering for a high-tech U.S. manufacturer, with a geographically closer assembly plant in 
Mexico and a geographically more distant plant in China. They note that “although the Chinese facility 
enjoyed lower costs, ocean transportation made its order lead times 5 to 10 times as long as those from 
Mexico” (p. 110). There also are considerations with respect to valuing lead time (e.g., de Treville et al. 
2014a, de Treville et al. 2014b, Biçer et al. 2018), improving lead time performance (e.g., de Treville et al. 
2009), or improving reliability by methods such as dual sourcing (e.g., Gurnani et al. 2014, Boute and Van 
Mieghem 2015). 
Although these are examples of important policy considerations, once a strategic supplier(s) is 
selected, the two key tactical decisions are when to order and how much to order. Naturally, global suppliers 
with longer fulfillment lead times require placing an order further in advance. However, deciding the order 
quantity remains a function of the expected demand and profit margin. In the simplest case, the optimal 
order quantity is characterized by the well-known newsvendor problem which balances the cost of excess 
inventory with the cost of a shortage. Newsvendor decisions are foundational to OM since the model 
captures the problem of determining supply in advance of knowing demand. In inventory management, 
newsvendor models inform the order quantity, service levels and desired safety stock in both single period 
and periodic (s-S) ordering contexts. Beyond this, the newsvendor framework widely applies to staffing 
decisions (He et al. 2012), capacity planning (de Véricourt and Gromb 2018), and revenue management 
(Kocabiyikoglu et al. 2015). Indeed, Zhang and Siemsen (2019) note that almost one-third of articles 
published in a major operations journal mention the newsvendor problem. Despite the straightforward 
solution of the newsvendor problem, a large body of empirical evidence shows that extraneous factors, 
nonstandard beliefs, and individual preferences systematically impact newsvendor decision-making (e.g., 
Schweitzer and Cachon 2000, Becker-Peth and Thonemann 2018, Cui and Wu 2018).1

In this research, we investigate if differences in the spatial (e.g., supplier location) and temporal 
(e.g., lead time) features of a supply chain influence inventory decision-making behavior. These two factors 
are important considerations for supply chain managers, yet neither are direct inputs to the order quantity 
decision and have received limited attention in prior studies. Specific to demand and lead time, some prior 
research has examined the value of demand information (Kremer et al. 2014) or how firms might value a 
reduction in the time between an order and observed demand (Biçer et al. 2018). Csermely and Minner 
(2015) examined ordering behavior under dual sourcing, where one supplier has a fast lead time and is more 
expensive and a second supplier is slower and less expensive. However, these studies do not focus on the 
psychological aspects of temporal distance, and instead consider aspects like inventory regret (Kremer et 
al. 2014), structural changes in uncertainty (Biçer et al. 2018), and individual characteristics including 
reference dependence (Csermely and Minner 2015). Specific to spatial distance, some prior newsvendor 
studies do consider different locations, yet these tend to focus on centralized versus decentralized inventory 
contexts (e.g. Ho et al., 2010) or the performance impact of making multiple simultaneous decisions (Chen 
and Li 2019). The multiple locations are not characterized by their geographic distances, suggesting that 
the psychological effects of a supplier’s spatial distance is also unexplored. 
There is a large body of literature on construal level theory (CLT) that shows important differences 
in how individuals process and consider specific items, events and outcomes (Trope and Liberman 2010). In 
general, CLT posits that individuals use mental representations of objects, people, situations, and events, and these 
exist on a continuum between high (abstract) and low (concrete) construal levels (Trope and Liberman 2010). 
Although any object can be represented at different levels of abstraction (e.g., Yudkin et al. 2020), prior research has 
shown that high-level, more abstract construals are associated with central and superordinate features, and 
low-level more concrete construals are associated with peripheral and subordinate features of outcomes 
(Liberman and Trope 2008, Trope and Liberman 2010). These differences have been shown to 
systematically impact evaluations such as the breadth of items in a category (high-level construals lead to 
broader and inclusive categories) and feasibility versus desirability considerations (where high-level 
construals place greater weight on feature desirability and low-level construals place greater weight on 
feature feasibility.) Differing levels of abstraction can impact perceptions or representations – the ‘what’ of 
an event, scenario, or object itself (Trope and Liberman 2010). A closely related concept to construal levels 
is perceptions or representations of psychological distance, which refers to attributes of when (temporal 
distance, i.e. tomorrow vs. next year), where (spatial distance, i.e., near vs. far), who (social distance, i.e., 
friend vs. stranger), and whether (hypothetical distance, i.e., 90% likely vs. 5% likely). 
 
Prior research documents that a bi-directional relationship often exists between abstraction and 
psychological distance. Increasing [decreasing] the psychological distance can cause construal levels to 
become increasingly abstract [concrete]. For example, an individual approaching a forest would (at some 
point) be able to focus on details of individual trees. A closer distance provides richer contextual details 
compared to a farther distance, increasing reliance on secondary, subordinate information (Liberman and 

4 
 
Trope 2008, Trope and Liberman 2010, Maglio 2020). Moving psychologically farther away [closer] 
displaces secondary, low-level details with primary, high-level features. This increases [decreases] the 
weight of central and superordinate features when evaluating scenarios and making decisions (Liberman 
and Trope 2008, Trope and Liberman 2010). Similarly, a higher [lower] construal level can create a greater 
[lesser] sense of psychological distance. Researchers have connected construal levels and psychological 
distance by varying the psychological distance (e.g., imagining a scenario happening tomorrow vs. next year) 
showing that individuals systematically change their behavior consistent with changes in mental abstraction. 
For example, manipulating psychological distance can change decisions by altering information processing 
(Henderson et al. 2006a, Ledgerwood et al. 2010), tradeoff preferences like short-term versus long-term 
gains (Henderson et al. 2006b), and the desirability versus feasibility of prospects and products (Sagristano 
et al. 2002, Van Kerckhove et al. 2014). Yan et al. (2016) provide evidence that the relationship between 
psychological distance and abstraction is that individuals engage in greater visual [verbal] processing at 
closer [farther] psychological distance, which increases concrete [abstract] thinking. This is consistent with 
other findings such as Liberman and Förster (2009), who show that farther psychological distance facilitated 
global visual processing and interfered with local processing, and a closer psychological distance had the 
opposite effect. 
CLT and psychological distance have been widely applied to investigate decision making in other 
business domains yet have been rarely applied in Operations Management (OM). For example, Marketing 
has used CLT to analyze consumer decision making given differences in information, product features, and 
shopping environment (e.g., Trope et al. 2007, Yan and Sengupta 2011, Aggarwal and Zhao 2015). 
Organizational Behavior has applied CLT in negotiations, information search, advice giving, and in virtual 
teams (e.g., Henderson 2011, Shani et al. 2009, Reyt et al. 2016, Wilson et al. 2013, Wiesenfeld et al. 2017). 
To the best of our knowledge, only two papers explore CLT in OM: Cantor and Macdonald (2009) used a 
mindset priming task to investigate the role of information availability in a beer game setting, where 
subjects acted as distributors in a simulated supply chain (the retailer, wholesaler, and factory were 
automated). They found that when information was not shared across the supply chain, subjects primed for 
concrete thought had higher order variability and bullwhip costs.2 Simpson et al. (2021) examined how 
psychological distance impacts how firms respond to modern slavery. They found that distance construals 
impact both risk perception and the supply chain approaches that firms use to mitigate exploitative 
practices. Literature reviews have also identified CLT and psychological distance as appropriate theoretical 
lenses for examining supply chain management (e.g., Pournader et al., 2020).

benefits of abstract or concrete thinking depend on the problem context. For example, they note “on the eve of opening a new

We use a series of scenario-based experiments to examine the impact of lead time and supplier 
location, representing temporal and spatial distance dimensions of psychological distance, on the order 
quantity decision. Although lead time and supplier location have ties to operations strategy (e.g., onshore 
vs. offshore production, single vs. dual sourcing), the profit-maximizing order quantity in standard 
newsvendor problem is independent of these factors and is based solely on the critical fractile. Therefore, 
all else equal, temporal and spatial differences in product lead time and supplier location should not affect 
the order quantity decision. Experiments using semantic differences and framing across different scenarios 
enable us to study the impact of psychological distance on newsvendor decisions. Largely based on the 
impact of feasibility and desirability considerations, we hypothesize (see §2) that farther temporal and 
spatial distances will increase newsvendor inventory levels. 
Our first set of studies (see §3) examine the impact of supplier distance and lead time on order 
decisions. Study 1A (N = 150) uses semantic differences describing the spatial distance to the supplier as 
either close or far. We find that a greater spatial distance increases order quantities. In Study 1B (N = 141), 
we use semantic differences in the description of the lead time (temporal distance). Again, we find that 
subjects in the temporally distant conditions order more than those in the close conditions. In Study 2 (N = 
112), we test the impact of distance on risk aversion in relation to the order quantity decision (see §4). 
Consistent with CLT, we find that subjects in a far distance condition expressed a higher risk preference 
than those in close condition; we find that risk partially mediates the relationship between psychological 
distance and order quantity. Study 3 (N = 60) is a brief follow up-experiment (see §5) that shows the impact 
of temporal distance: A far distance results in a preference for a higher order quantity, even when there is 
no physical distance or production lead time whatsoever. 
In Study 4 (N = 200), we build on the previous results and use a theory-based confirmation 
experiment to influence performance and rule out several alternative explanations (see §6). Framing is a 
practical and efficient method for improving behavior in inventory problems (Davis and Tong, 2021). 
Accordingly, the intervention uses near or far framing to impact the order quantity, as in all cases 
individuals order inventory from a supplier located at a fixed distance. Since the focal firm is at the same 
distance across treatments, the design removes natural differences in variability that may otherwise 
influence behavior. In addition, we provided a complete profit table for each order quantity and demand 
outcome, making it unlikely that subjects were confused or were unable to determine the payoff for their 
choice. Consistent with our prior studies, we find that framing the supplier as being close (i.e., by informing 
the participants that the firm has other suppliers located further away) improves decision making for low-
margin products and framing the supplier as being far (i.e., by informing the participants that the firm has 
other suppliers located nearby) improves decision making for high-margin products. Study 4 helps rule out 
alternative explanations (e.g., that decision-makers were considering increased natural variability consistent 
with longer distance) even if such implied variability has no bearing on the optimal order quantity. As the 

6 
 
physical distance to the focal supplier is the same across conditions, this suggests that psychological 
distance drives differences in behavior. 
We conclude that psychological distance can impact decision making in the newsvendor context. 
As psychological distances correspond with salient features of supply chains, demonstrating the cognitive 
influences of temporal and spatial distances on inventory decision-making is a novel contribution to the 
literature. Our findings offer theory-based insights into results from previous newsvendor experiments 
(which we discuss in our conclusions in §7). In addition, psychological distance provides a new dimension 
to the newsvendor problem that can be leveraged to improve decision making. 
2. Hypothesis Development 
When faced with a newsvendor problem, a decision maker could consider multiple objectives. 
Although the primary objective is to maximize profit, there is evidence to suggest that newsvendors have 
ulterior (secondary) goals, such as ensuring that all inventory is sold or avoiding surplus (Ho et al. 2010). 
When profit is a normative objective, it can be considered a superordinate, rather than a subordinate goal, 
which may provide insight into the underlying motivation(s) for selecting a particular order quantity: From 
CLT, superordinate goals are more associated with why an activity or goal is undertaken as compared to 
subordinate goals focusing on how an activity or goal is achieved. This fits the superordinate classification 
(i.e., <why> by <how>) and not a subordinate classification (<how> by <why>) (Vallacher and Wegner, 
1989). Set in the newsvendor problem context, a decision-maker maximizes profit (i.e., the why) by 
selecting an order quantity that meets demand (i.e., the how); the newsvendor does not select an order 
quantity (the how) by maximizing profit (the why). 
These goals also may be related to psychological distance. For example, Liberman and Trope 
(1998) linked psychological distance (specifically, temporal distance) with abstraction by examining the 
impact of distance on desirability and feasibility considerations. Observing that desirability of an activity’s 
outcome forms a high-level construal (e.g., the why) and feasibility represents the process of achieving the 
end-state (i.e., the how), they show that a farther temporal distance increases weight on desirability 
considerations, whereas closer temporal distance increases the importance of feasibility. In their specific 
example, when participants compare two software products differentiated by processing speed (desirability) 
and initial usability (feasibility), Liberman and Trope (1998) found that they placed greater value on 
feasibility [desirability] when using the software was framed one day [year] from now. The findings that 
psychological distance increases [reduces] the importance of feasibility [desirability] features hold across 
spatial distance (Agrawal et al. 2006), hypothetical distance (Todorov et al. 2007), and social distance 
(Liviatan et al. 2008, Lu et al. 2013). 
Desirability and feasibility also may be important considerations for newsvendor decision making, 
and these correspond to the distinction between ends and means (Sagristano et al. 2002). In the newsvendor 
problem, profit is a desirable outcome and feasibility is the probability of achieving that outcome given the 
demand distribution. Naturally, higher quantities have the potential to generate more sales and profits, a 

7 
 
more desirable outcome if demand is high. However, achieving the desirable outcomes becomes 
increasingly more difficult, as higher demand values are increasingly less likely to occur, making achieving 
the ideal profits from higher quantities less feasible. Therefore, from CLT we posit that higher orders are 
characterized by higher desirability and lower feasibility, and lower orders are characterized by higher 
feasibility and lower desirability. As farther [closer] psychological distances increase individuals’ focus on 
desirability [feasibility] features of problems (e.g., Liberman and Trope 1998), a longer lead-time or farther 
spatial distance (e.g., from suppliers) may increase newsvendor orders. 
Beyond desirability and feasibility considerations, there are other aspects of psychological distance 
that can impact inventory ordering by influencing perceptions of demand. First, numbers can be concrete 
or abstract. Smaller numbers are considered more concrete (Denes-Raj and Epstein 1994), which aligns 
with the relationship between closer psychological distances and increased visual processing. For example, 
picturing a small number of customers arriving at a store to purchase a product is straightforward. When 
picturing a select few individuals (e.g., 4 customers), these images can be rich with details of each customer. 
In contrast, visualizing many customer arrivals is more challenging, and is more abstract. The details of 
individuals fade when picturing a large group of consumers (e.g., 80 customers) arriving at a shop, and 
decision-makers require more abstract, schematic representations which are more easily construed with 
verbal processing. Second, Pecher and Boot (2011) noted that numbers also have a spatial representation: 
The concrete, smaller numbers are often considered spatially closer and to the left; the more abstract larger 
numbers are often interpreted as spatially far and to the right. Despite standard newsvendor problems 
providing exact distributions for demand, individuals may surmise their own perceptions of demand 
distributions and potential realization. Given the alignment between visual processing and psychological 
distance, a closer distance may decrease the perceived demand, which leads to lower orders. Similarly, 
individuals often have greater optimism for the more distal future outcomes (Gilovich et al. 1993). As 
optimism raises the perceived mean level of demand (Kirshner and Shao 2019), a farther psychological 
distance may also increase orders through higher optimism. 
Taken together, there are several compelling arguments supporting that psychological distance will 
influence newsvendor orders. Accordingly, we hypothesize the following: A far psychological distance will 
increase newsvendor order quantities compared to a close psychological distance. 
Given our interest in understanding the role of psychological distance on inventory order decisions, 
we created several scenario-based experiments that systematically vary spatial and temporal distances. We 
used a single period newsvendor decision (e.g., Becker-Peth et al. 2018 or the “once” treatment in Schultz 
et al. 2018) to test our hypotheses. Similar to Hsee et al. (2021), using a one-shot decision provides the 
cleanest approach for understanding cognitive factors and removes unnecessary complexity related to 
feedback, learning, and demand chasing, each of which could decrease the salience of psychological 
distances, our primary variable of interest. We also examine the influence of margins, as studies consistently 
demonstrate asymmetries in the PTC effect across margins (Zhang and Siemsen 2019). (We report the

implications of psychological distance on financial performance in Online Appendix D.1). We refer the 
reader to the Online Appendices for the experimental details for all studies. 
3. Study 1: Psychological Distance and Newsvendor Ordering 
3.1 Method 
Using a 2x2 between subject design that varied psychological distance (near vs. far) and the profit 
margin (high vs. low), we conducted two newsvendor experiments recruiting American participants from 
Mechanical Turk (MTurk). All participants were designated as MTurk Masters with an approval rating of 
at least 97% and at least 1000 completed tasks on the platform. Lee et al. (2018) tests the validity of using 
MTurk and found that newsvendor tasks replicate previous experimental newsvendor results. We offered 
each MTurk worker 0.50 USD for participating with opportunities for bonus pay based on task performance. 
Participants were presented with information on a simple newsvendor problem, including the cost, 
price, and demand distribution. They were asked to imagine that they sell products (widgets) to customers, 
initially buying the product from a supplier prior to knowing the realized customer demand. To test their 
understanding, participants completed four exercises testing specific features including costs and revenues 
(for a given order quantity), realized sales, excess inventory, and lost sales when demand was higher or 
lower than a given order quantity. If the participants incorrectly answered an exercise question, they were 
provided with an explanation on how to solve the problem. To incentivize understanding of the newsvendor 
problem, we rewarded participants with 0.25 USD for each correct pre-test exercise. As participants were 
incentivized to correctly answer the exercises, which are simpler than the decision task, we interpreted 
participants performing poorly on the exercise questions as either not reading the instructions carefully or 
not having sufficient understanding of the task. Thus, before the analysis, we removed participants who 
incorrectly answered two or more of the pre-test questions. 
After completing the pre-test questions, participants were given details (i.e., cost, price, and demand 
information) of the incentivized newsvendor task. For both product margin conditions, the selling price was 
12 experimental dollars, and demand was uniform between 0-100. For the high-margin [low-margin] 
condition, the unit cost was 3 [9] experimental dollars, making the optimal order quantity 75 [25]. 
Participants were informed that their earnings in a one period newsvendor task (based on their order and a 
randomly generated demand) would be converted at the rate of 300 experimental dollars to 1 USD, and that 
negative earnings would impact the bonus they received from correctly answering the exercises. 
 
The decision task manipulated psychological distance by presenting information on either the 
supplier location or delivery lead time. Participants in Study 1A were informed that they had a national 
[international] supplier located less than 2 [more than 8000] miles away. Those in Study 1B were informed 
that their product’s lead time was fast [slow] taking 1 week [year] to manufacture and deliver. To minimize 
the potential that subjects would conflate the location and lead time with product quality, participants were 
explicitly informed that the supplier would provide high-quality widgets. Using 7-point scales, we checked 

9 
 
participant perceptions about product quality (extremely low to extremely high) and supplier distance (i.e., 
extremely close to extremely far for Study 1A and extremely fast to extremely slow for Study 1B). 
After completing these checks, participants in the high-margin [low-margin] condition were 
reminded that the order cost is $3 [$9] per widget, the selling price is $12 per widget, and the random 
demand is equally likely to be between 0 and 100, and were asked to make their order given the 
psychological distance information. Our dependent variable of interest is the participants order quantity, 
since we hypothesize that a farther psychological distance will lead to a higher order. 
3.2 Participants 
3.2.1 Study 1A (Spatial Distance Only) 
We recruited 164 participants [77 females, 2 who identified as non-binary/preferred not to say; age: 
M = 39.16, SD = 9.55] from MTurk. In total, 13 participants were removed for incorrectly answering the 
exercise questions. Of the 151 remaining participants, 76 participants were in the close spatial distance 
conditions (38 in the high-margin setting) and 75 participants were allocated to the far spatial condition (38 
were in the high-margin setting). Participants were incentivized with a fixed payment of 0.50 USD and a 
performance payment (for the exercises and newsvendor task), which averaged 1.35 USD with a standard 
deviation of 0.91. 
3.2.2 Study 1B (Temporal Distance Only) 
We recruited 161 participants [74 females; age: M = 40.04, SD = 10.66] from MTurk. Following 
our protocol, 20 participants were removed for incorrectly answering the exercise questions. Out of the 141 
participants remaining, 71 participants were allocated to the close temporal distance condition (32 were in 
the high-margin setting) and 70 participants in the far temporal distance condition (36 were in the high-
margin setting). In addition to the fixed payment of 0.50 USD, the average performance payment for the 
exercise and newsvendor task was 1.43 USD with a standard deviation of 0.86.

3.3 Results 
3.3.1 Study 1A: Spatial Distance 
The psychological distance manipulation based on supplier location was effective. Independent 
samples t-tests show that participants in the spatially far condition perceived the supplier distance as being 
further (M = 6.13, 95% CI [5.79, 6.47], SD = 1.51) than those in the spatially close condition (M = 2.09, 
95% CI [1.85, 2.32], SD = 1.06, t(149) = -19.05, p < 0.001, d = 3.10). There were no differences in 
perceptions of product quality: Participants in the spatially distant condition rated the product quality 
(M = 5.59, 95% CI [5.34, 5.83], SD = 1.10) similar to those the close condition (M = 5.64, 95% CI [5.42, 
5.85], SD = 0.95, t(149) = 0.35, p = 0.729, d = -0.06).

3.3.2 Study 1B: Temporal Distance 
The psychological distance manipulation based on lead time was effective. Independent samples t-

small based on Cohen’s d. Similar to Study 1A, we ran a regression to test if a far lead time increases orders

3.4 Discussion 
 
Overall, Study 1’s results support that the semantic differences in psychological distance – both 
physical distance (Study 1A) and temporal distance (Study 1B) can lead to increased inventory order 
quantities. Generally, a farther spatial distance is associated with longer lead times – it takes longer to 
receive a good from a supplier some distance away than from a nearby supplier assuming identical shipping 
modes. Thus, in the next study, we compare the impact on ordering behavior attributed under simultaneous 
psychological distance (i.e., a far physical distance and a longer lead time, as compared to a close distance 
and short lead time) under different margin conditions. 
4. Study 2: Distance and Risk 
The relationship between psychological distance and preferences between desirability and 
feasibility also impact scenarios with uncertain outcomes. Focusing on gambles, Sagristano et al. (2002) 
proposed that chance occurrences have hierarchical conceptions, where the payoff is superordinate (the 
why) and the random generating process is subordinate (the how). The payoff determined the desirability

Although prior literature has established the profit-maximizing newsvendor order quantity under a 
variety of conditions, several papers have offered descriptive accounts of suboptimal (behavioral) inventory 
ordering. Risk-averse [seeking] preferences predict lower [higher] ordering quantities than the profit 
maximizing quantity (e.g., Schweitzer and Cachon 2000). Although risk preference cannot explain the pull-
to-center effect alone, experimental evidence suggests that risk-seeking and aversion preferences influence 
order quantities (de Véricourt et al. 2013, Kremer et al. 2014, and Becker-Peth et al. 2018). Given that 
larger order quantities carry greater risk in the newsvendor problem, newsvendor tasks framed as having 
greater psychological distance may increase the risk-preference and order quantities compared to when 
distances are framed as being psychologically close. Thus, one possible reason for the difference in orders 
could be that perceptions of distance also impact risk preferences, and in turn risk preference impacts order 
quantities. Given psychological distance influences risk, risk-seeking increases orders, and our overarching 
hypothesis that psychological distance increases orders, we also explore risk as a potential mediator in this 
Study. 
4.1 Participants and Method 
Study 2 was a 2 (psychological distance: close vs far) x 2 (profit margin: high vs low) between 
subject study that largely follows the same procedure as Study 1, with the following exceptions. First, Study 
2 took place in the lab of business school at a large public university in Australia.5 Consistent with similar 
studies, we recruited 112 undergraduate participants from the business school’s subject pool, targeting 25-

that the optimal order quantity was identical between margin conditions. (These demand conditions are 
similar to prior newsvendor experiments, e.g., Bolton and Katok 2008, Katok and Wu 2009). Thus, 
participants could maximize their expected payoff by selecting the optimal order quantity of 75 units. The

4.3 Discussion 
 
Study 2 uses both temporal and spatial distance to investigate newsvendor ordering behavior. In 
general, CLT has shown that different dimensions of psychological distance are often mentally associated. 
For example, Liberman and Trope (2008) observe that distant times trigger an association with remote 
locations, as individuals tend to complete the phrase “a long time ago, in a ____ place” with far away rather 
than nearby. This has implications for supply chains; although the optimal newsvendor order quantity 
should be the same, more distant suppliers are likely to have longer lead times than close suppliers. Just 
like Study 1A and 1B, Study 2 shows that a closer psychological distance leads to a lower order quantity, 
which would reduce costs for low-margin products but raise costs for high-margin goods. 
The effects of distance are stronger in Study 2 than Study 1A and 1B, which can be due to the use 
of two distance dimensions at the same time. This is consistent with previous research that shows when two 
aspects of psychological distance simultaneously occur, the overall effect is increased. For example, Huang 
et al. (2016) found that both temporal and spatial distances individually led to higher user reviews, and that 
there was also a positive interaction effect between the two distances. 
Study 2 also tests the impact of distance on risk preferences, and if risk mediates the relationship 
between psychological distance and order decisions that was observed in Study 1. Our results show that 
risk partially mediates the relationship between distance and order quantities, and this may be stronger in 
low margin conditions. A plausible explanation is that low margin products have a greater range for over-
ordering, assuming a baseline of typical PTC ordering (i.e., behavioral orders in low margin are typically 
below the mean, and order in high margin are above the mean). We note that psychological distance may 
influence other aspects of the newsvendor problem. For example, psychological distance influences the 
regret associated with errors of inaction (omission) and errors associated with actions (commission) in the

5. Study 3: Future Temporal Distance 
 
Whether temporal distance to event is near or far can influence a variety of decision-making 
behaviors. Study 3 validates the impact of temporal distance on inventory ordering and examines regret as 
a potential driver of performance. The previous studies have operationalized spatial and temporal distance 
through geographic location and supplier lead time. These distances are often related since in practice a 
greater physical distance could naturally lead to a longer lead time, especially if the same shipping mode is 
used for two suppliers that differ in their physical distance to the newsvendor. Although Study 1A and 1B 
emphasize separate spatial and temporal distances (there was no mention of lead time in study 1A, and no 
mention of physical distance in study 1B), it is possible that participants conflated spatial and temporal 
distance, perhaps implicitly assuming that a far physical distance would have a long temporal lead time. 
(By design, Study 2 aligned corresponding distance and lead time). In addition, in Study 1 and 2 the 
newsvendors’ ordering decision were made in the present, and the delivery was in either the near or distant 
future. This is a relevant operationalization of temporal distance in supply chains. However, other aspects 
of temporal distance frame the action of participants either in the near (e.g., tomorrow) or distant future 
(e.g., in one year’s time). 
Many social psychology studies use near or future framing to analyze the effects of temporal 
distance. For example, an early study on temporal construal (Liberman and Trope, 1998) had individuals 
imagine a range of tasks (e.g., attending a guest lecture; attending a concert), either in the near future (i.e., 
tomorrow) or in the distant future (i.e., in one year). Their results showed that in the near future, individuals 
rated feasibility considerations (e.g., the convenience of the time of the lecture; the price of a concert ticket) 
as more important than desirability considerations (e.g., how interesting is the lecture; whether you like the 
band). More recently, Sánchez et al (2021) conducted a high-powered study to estimate the effect of 
temporal distance on abstraction “using one of the most common operationalizations of temporal distance 
(thinking about a future point in time that is one day vs. one year from today)” (Sánchez et al. 2021; p. 
352). 
To reduce potential confounding of spatial and temporal distance, Study 3 follows Sánchez et al. 
(2021) and investigates temporal distance using a forced choice experiment. Specifically, Study 3 required 
one decision for production in two different time periods. We note that forced choice experiments are often 
used to establish relationships involving psychological distance. For example, Durkin et al. (2020) establish 
that individuals associate abstract art with farther psychological distances (i.e., abstract art was rated as 

18 
 
more likely to be displayed at a gallery opening in one year’s time) compared to figurative art which is 
associated with close psychological distances (i.e., figurative art was rated more likely to be displayed at a 
gallery opening tomorrow) using a forced-choice experiment. Similarly, Wakslak (2012) used a similar 
design to show that participants assign low [high] likelihood events to far [near] temporal and spatial distant 
contexts. 
In Study 3, participants act as manufacturers of the product rather than ordering the product from 
suppliers, and the stated production time is only one day. Crucially, participants make one single decision 
for production both in the near term (tomorrow) and distant future (in one year’s time). Moreover, the 
payoff scenario is risk neutral: Specifically, we limited demand to be a specific outcome of 100, 150, or 
200 units (in both periods) and each outcome occured with equal probability. Subjects were constrained to 
produce in multiples of 100; participants must produce 100 units for one time period and 200 units for the 
other time period: The decision is if they want to produce 100 units tomorrow and 200 in one year, or to 
produce 200 units tomorrow and 100 in one year. 
If a farther temporal distance leads to greater orders (as in Study 1B), then on average participants 
should prefer to allocate manufacturing 200 units further in the future. Moreover, this decision should be 
based on temporal distance, as the expected payoffs between the two choices are exactly the same. The cost 
(c=5) and price (p=10) were set to create a critical fractile of 0.5, which means that manufacturing 100 or 
200 units has the same expected profit ($500) in both periods. (Note that this forced-choice design holds 
risk aversion and waste aversion constant. For example, if both order quantities were an option in each 
period, a risk-averse or waste-averse subject might prefer to select 100 in both periods.) 
5.1 Method and Participants 
We conducted an experiment that varied the temporal distance of making a high inventory decision 
and a low inventory decision; see Appendix B for details of Study 3. The experiment was conducted on 
Mechanical Turk in exchange for a payment of 1.50 USD; the flat fee was used to incentivize participation 
and further reduce any potential impact of risk-seeking or risk-averse payoff preferences in Study 3. In 
total, 60 participants completed the study (32 females; 1 identified as non-binary/other; age: M = 43.97, SD 
= 11.88). 
The study began with an introduction to the newsvendor problem. Participants were asked to 
imagine that they manufacture a product (a widget) at a cost of 5 dollars per unit, which they sell to 
customers at 10 dollars per unit. Their task was to determine how many widgets to manufacture before 
knowing demand and were informed of the tradeoff between manufacturing not enough and too many units. 
Next participants were told that the demand is either 100, 150, or 200, each with a 1/3 chance of occurring, 
and that they can only manufacture widgets in batches of 100. They are then provided with another 
description of the overage and underage tradeoff given the decision to manufacture 100 and 200 units. They 
were given the formula to calculate and a table that displayed the profits for each level of demand for both 

19 
 
manufacturing quantities (e.g., Kremer et al. 2010). This information was followed by two comprehension 
check questions, where failure to correctly answer either question terminated the study. 
Next, participants were asked to imagine that they must make their manufacturing decision for two 
different time periods, tomorrow and one year from now. They are told that any inventory leftover in the 
first time period (tomorrow) cannot be used for the second time period (next year) and that any demand 
that is not satisfied in the first time period (tomorrow) cannot be satisfied in the second time period (next 
year). Two additional required comprehension questions noted that excess inventory cannot be used to meet 
future demand and that excess demand cannot be supplied with future inventory are required are then given 
to the participants; failure to correctly answer the questions results in the study ending. After the information 
stage, subjects move to the main decision: In all cases, subjects manufacture 100 widgets at one time point 
and 200 widgets at the other time point. We remind them that the demand and supply are independent and 
do not carry over from one period to the next and provide them with the table of the expected profits. Then, 
participants answer if they would prefer to manufacture “100 widgets tomorrow and 200 widgets one year 
from now” or “200 widgets tomorrow and 100 widgets one year from now”; (the presentation order of the 
two options was randomized by subject). 
Participants were asked several questions on regret preferences, using a Likert-type scale. We asked 
if they would experience greater regret from not manufacturing enough widgets or manufacturing too many 
widgets for both tomorrow and in one year (the display order was randomized). We also asked for which 
time period they might have greater regret, either from creating too many or not enough widgets. We asked 
participants about how far away they perceive tomorrow and one year’s time. Finally, participants provided 
demographic information and were thanked for participating. 
5.2 Results and Discussion 
Unsurprisingly, participants perceived one year from now as being farther away (M = 3.50, SD = 
0.81) than tomorrow (M = 1.28, SD = 0.76). Even though the expected payoff from either decision was the 
same in both the near term and far temporal distance, more participants stated they would manufacture 100 
widgets tomorrow and 200 widgets one year from now: Of the 60 total participants, 40 participants (66.7%) 
produced 200 in one year; this is statistically different from a null hypothesis of 50%, i.e., even chance (z 
= 2.58, 95% CI [53.32%, 78.32%], p = 0.0098). Thus, Study 3 provides additional support that a far 
temporal distance can lead to a greater preference for higher orders relative to a close distance. 
We also analyzed the impact of regret, yet the results were not significantly different. On average, 
subjects reported similar levels of regret in terms of over and understocking for tomorrow (M = 3.83, 95% 
CI [3.03, 3.74] SD = 1.40) and in one year’s time (M = 3.83, 95% CI [3.05, 3.71], SD = 1.30). Similarly, 
participants were evenly divided on whether they would experience greater regret now or in the future (M 
= 2.93, 95% CI [2.67, 3.20], SD = 1.04), which is not statistically different from the indifference point of 3 
(t(59) = 0.497, p = 0.621). We conducted several exploratory logistic regressions relating various measures 
of regret with the choice between over and understocking. However, none of the regressions results were 

20 
 
significant. This suggests that the preference for a larger order in the distant future was not driven by regret. 
The mean regret we observed was slightly below the indifference point, a result consistent with Ho et al. 
(2010) who found a slight asymmetry in regret where individuals experience greater disutility from having 
excess inventory compared to understocking. It is also possible that a closer temporal distance may lead to 
lower order quantities because smaller numbers are interpreted as being more concrete compared to larger 
numbers (Denes-Raj & Epstein, 1994). Even though the payoffs were identical in Study 3, participants may 
have interpreted the larger order as a higher hypothetical risk, more preferable to incur in the future. 
6. Study 4: Perceived Spatial Distance 
The previous studies used semantic differences in the lead time and supplier distance to manipulate 
psychological distances. Although the lead time and geographic distance do not impact the optimal order 
quantity in any way, it is possible that other considerations may influence participants to react differently 
to more distant suppliers. Though not part of the newsvendor calculation, participants might infer that 
sourcing from more distant suppliers might also result in increased likelihood of damage, theft, partial loss 
during transport, unforeseen disruptions or partial delays, all of which could result in lower quantity 
received compared to what was ordered. Conceivably, individuals could recognize that these considerations 
are more likely to occur at a longer distance and account for this using some sort of heuristic processing. It 
is also possible that participants were unable to calculate the optimal profit (in Study 1A, 1B and 2); in 
Study 4 we provided a complete profit table for each order quantity and demand outcome, making it unlikely 
that subjects could not determine the payoff for their choice. 
To confirm that psychological distance influences ordering and rule out several alternative 
explanations, our confirmation experiment in Study 4 fixed the spatial distance of a focal supplier and 
mentioned other suppliers. By design, this treatment framed the focal supplier as being either 
psychologically close or distant, yet the actual distance of the supplier was constant across treatments. If 
effective, this framing can effectively encourage higher or lower order quantities. Thus, Study 4 confirms 
the impact of psychological distance and suggests a possible intervention to improve ordering decisions. 
6.1 Method 
We conducted a 3x2 between subject design experiment that varied the framing of the supplier 
distance (near vs. far vs. control) and the profit margin (high vs. low); see Appendix C for details of Study 
4. The experiment was conducted on Mechanical Turk. Similar to prior studies, we offered participants 1 
USD for participating and an incentive payment based on their order and a randomly generated demand. 
Rather than using exercise questions as an attention check, we used a comprehension test to see if 
participants were carefully reading instructions (e.g., Dietvorst et al. 2018). Participants who failed this 
attention check were not allowed to proceed with the experiment. 
After a brief introduction to the newsvendor problem, participants were informed of their suppliers. 
In the near and far treatments, participants were instructed that they have five suppliers from which they 
source widgets. The instructions highlighted that each supplier provides a different type of widget and that 

21 
 
demand for each widget is entirely independent. In the far psychological distance condition, participants 
were informed that Supplier 1 is located 10 miles away, Supplier 2 is located 20 miles away, Supplier 3 is 
located 40 miles away, Supplier 4 is located 60 miles, and Supplier 5 is located 100 miles away. Participants 
were then informed that their task focuses exclusively on Supplier 5. In the close psychological distance 
condition, participants are informed that Supplier 1 is located 100 miles away, Supplier 2 is located 200 
miles away, Supplier 3 is located 400 miles away, Supplier 4 is located 600 miles, and Supplier 5 is located 
1000 miles away. Participants are then told that their task will focus on Supplier 1. In the control treatment, 
participants only had a single supplier located 100 miles away. Crucially, in all treatments, observe that the 
focal supplier is located 100 miles away. Yudkin et al. (2020) used a similar procedure, presenting five 
spatial distances to random partner where the partner’s distance was the same in each treatment. 
Next, participants were informed of the price, cost, and demand distribution. In the high-margin 
conditions, the price was $10.00, the cost was $2.50, and similar to Kremer et al. (2010) the demand 
distribution was a 7-point discrete uniform distribution with values: 50, 100, 150, 200, 250, 300, 350. In 
the low-margin conditions, the price was $10, the cost was $7.50, and the demand distribution was also a 
7-point discrete uniform distribution, but with outcomes: 450, 500, 550, 600, 650, 700, 750. Therefore, the 
optimal order quantities were 300 in the high-margin condition and 500 in the low-margin condition. We 
also provided participants a table that listed the profits for each possible demand outcome for each order 
quantity (e.g., Study 3 of Kremer et al. 2010). By labeling the order quantities options from lowest to highest

6.3 Results 
We tested the effect of our manipulation of the supplier’s location. Participants in the spatially far 
framed condition perceived the supplier distance as being further (M = 5.77, 95% CI [5.47, 
6.06], SD = 1.15) than those in the control condition (M = 4.31, 95% CI [3.92, 4.80], SD = 1.30, t(132) = -
6.57, p < 0.001, d = 1.19, independent sample t-tests). Similarly, participants in the control condition 
perceived the supplier distance as farther than those in the spatially close condition (M = 3.20, 
95% CI [2.88, 3.51], SD = 1.17, t(119) = -4.73, p < 0.001, d = 0.90). Thus, our treatment manipulating 
perceived psychological distance to the supplier was effective. 
For the low-margin condition, the close spatial distance condition is the treatment condition: Recall 
the previous experiments demonstrated that closer distance lowered orders, maximizing expected profits in 
low-margin settings. In contrast, the far spatial distance frame is the treatment condition for the high-margin 
condition since higher orders maximize expected profits.

between treatments and was fixed at 100 miles.

6.4 Discussion 
Studies 1A, 1B and 2 demonstrated that a farther distance raised order quantities, implying that 
psychological distance may alter order quantities. Study 3 showed that a further temporal distance was 
associated with higher order quantities. Study 4 confirms these results, demonstrating that psychological 

24 
 
distance impacts the order quantity, even when the geographic distance of the focal firm is identical and 
there is no mention at all of temporal lead time. Specifically, by framing high-margin products as being 
psychologically distant and low-margin products as psychologically close, we observed order quantities 
that moved towards the profit maximizing values. 
Overall, our treatment in Study 4 was effective in improving order quantities. In each case, 
participants made ordering decisions from a supplier located 100 miles away. However, with respect to the 
firm’s other suppliers, 100 miles away was framed as being relatively close (an alternative supplier was 
located 1000 miles away) or far (an alternative supplier was located 10 miles away). For the low-margin 
conditions, framing the supplier as being spatially close led to a significant decrease in orders. Similarly, 
for the high-margin conditions, framing the supplier as spatially far led to a directional (but not significant) 
increase in orders. The improvement was stronger for the low-margin setting, which could be driven by the 
stronger influence of risk in lower margins as shown in Study 2. Overall, our results show that psychological 
distance is one driver of decisions and suggests that distance framing is a potential nudge (see Thaler and 
Sunstein 2009) that can be used to improve performance. 
7. Conclusions 
In this paper, we examine the impact of psychological distance on inventory decisions in the 
newsvendor problem. By integrating behavioral newsvendor research with behavioral insights from social 
psychology based on CLT, we hypothesize that when making order quantity decisions, individuals utilize 
information related to spatial and temporal distances even when these factors do not (or should not) impact 
the optimal order quantity. We test the hypothesis in four experiments with different populations, margin 
conditions and different variants of temporal and spatial distance. In general, we found evidence that a far 
psychological distance results in higher order quantities. The difference in order quantities has measurable 
effects on performance, decreasing costs for high profit margin products and increasing costs for low profit 
margin goods (see Appendix D). Moreover, we show that risk partially mediates this relationship. We also 
show that the framing of psychological distance is sufficient to induce changes in ordering behavior even 
when the actual distance is fixed. Although framing can have profound impacts on decision-making, Davis 
(2018) observes that this is not universal within OM, calling for “further research on framing in operations 
management contexts” (p. 175). We address this call as well as other calls regarding the use of CLT in OM 
(e.g., Pournader et al. 2020) by exploring the application of distance and distance-based framing on 
inventory ordering. 
7.1 Managerial and Theoretical Implications 
Recently, COVID-19 and other disruptions have impacted supply, causing firms to reevaluate 
global sourcing strategies and carefully examine lead times. A recent HBR article noted that “Historically… 
managers didn’t give that much weight to distance and shipping when planning product sourcing strategies; 
they just assumed that it would get done. Those days are past” (Shih 2022; p.4). The article noted that as 
supply chain managers navigate the new normal, they must prioritize geography and logistics, including 

25 
 
transport distance and supplier location. As firms continue to make structural changes to their supply chains, 
our work advances our understanding of how individuals perceive and act on distance in managing 
inventory. Our results reveal that extraneous and readily available information regarding suppliers’ lead 
time and location can inadvertently affect inventory order quantity decisions. This suggests managers 
should be aware of and work to mitigate these factors through design, education, and training. In addition, 
understanding the impact of distance can help managers model behavior of competitors or customers. 
Existing models of competitor’s behavior that focus on demand related irregularities, such as demand 
chasing and regret (Ovchinnikov et al. 2015) and strategic behavior (Feng and Zhang 2017). Our results 
suggest that modeling of competitive behavior could be expanded to include spatial and temporal 
characteristics of a competitor’s supply chain, which can in turn impact the firm’s inventory strategy (Davis 
and Tong 2021). 
Behavioral solutions such as debiasing techniques and decision framing can also help address bias 
in inventory decision making (Davis and Tong 2021). Our final study that uses distance framing to examine 
the impact of distance also provides preliminary evidence that increasing or decreasing perceptions of 
psychological distances by manipulating how these distances are presented could encourage more optimal 
ordering. Thus, our work provides initial evidence that distances can be built into interventions for nudging 
newsvendor decision makers. As newsvendors exhibit heterogeneity, distance frames could also be tailored 
to individuals to appropriately raise and lower orders, potentially mitigating suboptimal behavior. 
Our findings also provide a potential cognitive explanation for behavior observed in prior literature 
in operations that could be tested in future research. For example, Lurie and Swaminathan (2009) explored 
whether frequent feedback is beneficial for newsvendors and designed interventions where the newsvendor 
makes orders that are held constant across several periods. In their Study 1, participants placed orders for a 
high-margin product at different intervals that varied by treatment (orders every round, every three rounds, 
or every six rounds) and in Study 4, participants placed orders for a low-margin product every other round, 
every six rounds, or every ten rounds. Lurie and Swaminathan (2009) observed that orders in the six and 
ten round forward conditions were higher, and performance was better for high-margin conditions and 
worse for low-margin treatments. The longer time horizons (six and ten rounds) are consistent with a further 
temporal distance. Thus, our results provide a theory-based cognitive explanation for Lurie and 
Swaminathan’s (2009) observation regarding the benefits of ordering in advance for high but not low-
margin products. 
Similarly, CLT also provides an underlying cognitive explanation for the findings of Chen et al. 
(2013) regarding the influence of payment schemes on newsvendor decisions. Using a prospective 
accounting framework, Chen et al. (2013) noted that payment schemes which coupled costs and revenues 
led to higher orders, and decoupled costs and revenues led to lower orders. This behavior is consistent with 
CLT, as decisions at a higher-level construal (such as reducing costs) and further psychological distance 
are representations typically more schematic and abstract. In contrast, at a lower-level construal and closer 

26 
 
distance, representations are more concrete, increasing the individual-specific salience of distinct features. 
Said another way, at farther distance and higher construal level, features of a decision are more likely 
evaluated together (Goodman and Malkoc 2012, Ho et al. 2020), which is consistent with the concept of 
coupling decisions factors in the framework of prospective accounting. As Chen et al. (2013) noted that 
payment schemes that coupled costs and revenues led to higher orders, their results are also consistent with 
predictions from CLT and psychological distance. 
7.2 Limitations and Future Research 
Although we focus primarily on spatial and temporal distances of suppliers, we note that compared 
to global suppliers, local suppliers are more likely to be perceived as psychologically close on other 
dimensions. For example, as local suppliers are geographically closer, firms and individuals can more easily 
undertake routine visits and establish closer social relationships. As a result, firms are likely to have 
concrete understandings and representations of their local suppliers’ operations. In contrast, global suppliers 
are likely to have more abstract representations, are less likely to be routinely visited in person, and be 
perceived as psychologically far. Thus, future research could test the impact of other dimensions of 
psychological distance, such as social distance and hypothetical distance. If our results extend to other 
dimensions such as social distance, this suggests that conventional wisdom advocating for establishing 
closer relationships with suppliers might need to account for the additional benefits or consequences from 
these relationships depending on the product’s profitability. In addition, if all four main dimensions of 
psychological distance influence newsvendor decision-making, then future research could also contrast the 
impact of other distances (perhaps using discrete choice experiments) on orders.6 
We draw on CLT to develop our hypothesis of how psychological distance influences newsvendor 
behavior. Given that abstraction and psychological distance are distinct concepts, future research should 
examine how differing degrees of abstraction can influence ordering decisions. Also, future research could 
untangle whether a far psychological distance increasing orders is driven by some combination of factors 
like decreasing risk aversion, loss aversion, and waste aversion, or if the higher orders are indeed a result 
of more abstract mental representations. 
We also note some important limitations in our current research. Our studies focused on a single 
quantity decision when inventory decisions are often recurring. Our experiments were also conducted 
online and in laboratory settings. Future research should analyze the impact of distance in settings featuring 
repeated inventory decisions. We encourage field studies and empirical data to test the external validity of 
our findings. We also note that several of our experiments used manipulation checks to gage participant

Our results provide a proof of concept that managers can nudge decision-making using the relative 
framing of psychological distances. We recommend that future research study how psychological distance 
can be used to create choice architecture structures that can improve decision making in practice. For 
example, our framing results suggest that managers could strategically select suppliers to offset biased 
newsvendor ordering: When sourcing low-margin components or products, selecting suppliers that are 
psychologically close (geographically nearby or with shorter lead times) could help nudge orders lower. 
Even if closer suppliers are slightly more expensive, the potential savings from more efficient ordering 
could compensate for the additional costs. Similarly, for high-margin components or products, our results 
suggest that managers should select suppliers that are psychologically distant (spatially far or with longer 
lead times) as this would encourage higher orders, potentially offsetting PTC behavior. More research is 
needed to study possible interventions. 
More broadly, perceptions of psychological distance may have applicability to many aspects of 
supply chain and operations management. Supply chain decisions often include several simultaneous 
aspects of distance, including physical distance like supplier location (Bray et al. 2019), temporal distance 
such as forecasting future events (e.g., Kremer et al. 2011), hypothetical distances such as perceived 
probabilities based on asymmetric supply chain information (e.g., Ma et al. 2021), and social distances 
including buyer-supplier relationships (e.g., Loch & Wu 2008). Thus, psychological distance could impact 
other supply chain and newsvendor decisions such as capacity investment decisions and staffing and service 
levels. As a general theory of behavior, CLT may also have applicability to other operations decisions. For 
example, disaster management involves forecasting (temporal), application of extreme value statistics 
(hypothetical), and coordination by stakeholders at different social and proximal distances to crisis areas 
(e.g., United Nations agencies, local/national governments, Red Cross, affected persons). New product 
development involves development and entry timing, outcome uncertainty, and managing teams that vary 
in social and physical distances from consumers and the parent firm. Thus, we believe there are additional 
opportunities to apply psychological distance and CLT to understand and improve decision making across 
many operations contexts.
