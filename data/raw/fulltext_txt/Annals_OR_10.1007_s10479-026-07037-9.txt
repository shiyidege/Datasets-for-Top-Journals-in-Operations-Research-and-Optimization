Abstract
Sudden events—such as pandemics or natural disasters—can trigger panic buying, which 
refers to the precautionary behavior of stockpiling in the face of a potential stockout and 
price hike. This study investigates a combined direct and indirect subsidization mecha­
nism, in which the supplier directly subsidizes the consumer end-price and indirectly regu­
lates the market through a price-dependent wholesale rate. We develop a non-cooperative 
game model and show that by specifying the wholesale-price function offered to the re­
tailer, the supplier (often a state-owned company) is able to regulate prices, mitigate panic 
buying, and reduce the number of leftovers. Endogenizing the wholesale price enables 
comparable price stabilization with markedly lower subsidy budgets, clarifying when in­
direct subsidization should be preferred in practice. The best response price offered by 
the retailer to consumers is derived analytically. The analysis shows that minimizing the 
end-price is not the supplier’s optimal objective and that purely direct subsidization is 
financially inefficient.

1  Introduction
1.1  Panic buying and sudden scarcity
Panic buying is an unwelcome phenomenon that may provoke higher market prices and 
product shortages. It can be defined as a sudden increase in the purchase of goods in quanti­
ties greater than those needed, thus leading to an imbalance between supply and demand 
(Arafat et al., 2020). Panic buying can have a negative impact on emotional well-being, 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 358:141–164
resulting in, for example, depression, anxiety, stress, substance abuse, and loneliness; this 
can be the case for the population in general, but is especially concerning when it applies 
to vulnerable groups (Kar et al., 2020). The phenomenon of panic buying tends to occur in 
response to an unexpected disaster and has been attributed to fear of the unknown (Dulam 
et al., 2020), which leads people to take precautionary actions to guard against stockouts. 
During the first year of the COVID-19 pandemic, the freight rates (i.e., transportation costs) 
for containers between China/South Asia and Northern Europe increased by a factor of more 
than three (Ivanov & Dolgui, 2022). This increase, which was a consequence of supply-
chain disruption, caused panic-buying behavior in the business-to-business sector, and con­
sequently led to additional shortages. One of the first consequences of the pandemic was 
the hoarding of toilet paper, which was reported even before the COVID-19 virus had been 
detected in the stool sample of a single patient (Miri et al., 2020).
Supply disruptions due to, for example, natural disasters are associated with facility 
breakdowns and transportation delays that can cause panic buying. To simulate panic buy­
ing in this paper, we assume that the total supply is less than the aggregate demand dur­
ing crisis periods, as observed by Dulam et al. (2020). The Great East Japan Earthquake 
of 2011 caused supply disruptions and consequently shortages of essential products – not 
only for survivors, but also for inhabitants of adjacent areas. Kurihara et al. (2012) report 
similar post-disaster demand surges for essentials such as fuel, bottled water, bread, and 
instant meals (similar patterns occurred in other disasters such as hurricanes and floods). 
These examples suggest that sudden disasters may cause price surges and shortages of vital 
resources for a period of at least several months.
1.2  Governmental price control
The possibility of leaving the supply chain to reach economic equilibrium without any regu­
lation, under a scenario of sudden scarcity, may have unwanted drawbacks, especially for 
consumers (see Sect. 1.1). As pointed out by Azghandi et al. (2018), during crisis periods 
importing a commodity from an alternative supplier may require a lengthy period of regula­
tory processing; therefore faster measures are needed. Long and Khoi (2020) note that the 
governments that tend, even under normal circumstances, to adopt a nationalistic approach, 
often limited or halted the export of essential products during the COVID-19 pandemic to 
prevent domestic shortages.
In scenarios where the undesired outcome of panic buying is a sudden shortage or an 
increase in price, a regulator may decide to cap the price or to subsidize the product. Mur­
phy et al. (2019) note that there are two common types of governmental price control: in the 
case where there is only one supplier (usually a state company) and no market mechanism, 
governments tend to set the product price, whereas in a market with competition (either full 
or partial), they cap the price. Examples include the Saudi government, which imposes a 
price ceiling on fresh milk (Al Rajhi Capital, 2012) and the Venezuelan government, which 
regularly reviews and updates its list of price-controlled products (Mulet, 2009). Price-cap 
regulation often fails to protect consumers and can destabilize markets by triggering ration­
ing, supplier exits, or black-market sales (Loube, 1995). Historical cases such as Zimbabwe 
and Hungary confirm that rigid price caps frequently produce shortages.
Direct subsidization of the scarce product is another common way that regulators use 
to decrease the customer’s end-price. Its main drawbacks are that it increases demand and,

These limitations motivate the regulatory mechanism developed in this study, which 
combines direct and indirect subsidization through an endogenous wholesale-price function.
1.3  Operations management models
Much of the research on consumer panic-buying originates from the psychology and sociol­
ogy literature and involves statistical analysis (see, for example, Kurihara and Maruyama, 
2012; Forbes, 2017; Islam et al., 2021). The goal of these studies has been to analyze panic-
buying behavior based on existing data or responses to questionnaires, with a view to under­
standing the causes of panic buying and suggesting methods for mitigation. For example, 
Shou et al. (2013) study panic buying under supply disruptions and find that consumers 
tend to stockpile when either the price is low or their holding cost is low. The authors also 
report that panic buying is more prevalent among people who are prone to risk aversion. 
The media can exert a significant influence (either positive or negative) on panic-buying 
behavior. News stories about hoarding during the pandemic appeared across multiple social 
media platforms, showing powerful images of empty shelves and long queues forming out­
side stores (Islam et al., 2021). We assume that governments cannot effectively control the 
media (e.g., social networks), and therefore the effect of media is considered exogenous to 
the suggested problem.
Relatively few studies have used an operational research framework to analyze the effi­
cient management of panic buying. The advantage of using an analytical approach is the 
ability to quantify the required decisions. Kogan and Herbon (2022) develop a mathematical 
model that considers the dynamics of retailing operations in response to panic buying. They 
show that neither income-tax relief for retailers nor the provision of subsidies to support 
overtime work is useful tools. Dulam et al. (2020) propose an agent-based system to decrease 
panic buying when consumers face supply disruptions due to a disaster. They assume that 
excess demand is controlled by limiting sales per person and they further assume that the 
information is shared among players. Tsao et al. (2019) address a case where a wholesaler 
sells two different brands of a single product to multiple retailers, where each brand is avail­
able in multiple weights. Under conditions of supply disruption, the wholesaler segregates 
retail stores into those that attract high-indexed customers and those that attract low-indexed 
customers, where the former yield higher profits by ordering greater quantities. The study 
considers product substitution based on brand and weight (for a given brand), and the objec­
tive is to maximize the total profit by seeking the optimal ordering quantities and the optimal 
number of substitutions. Yoon et al. (2018) investigate the sourcing strategy of a retailer 
under the assumption that consumers stockpile due to a supply disruption risk.
More closely related are the three studies by Herbon and Kogan and co-authors. First, 
Kogan and Herbon (2022) analyzed the dynamics of retailing under panic buying when a 
government intervenes through external policy instruments such as price caps, tax relief, 
or overtime subsidies. That study focused on the retailer’s operational response to con­

sumer stockpiling, but the regulatory actions were imposed exogenously and the govern­
ment itself did not optimize its policy. Second, Herbon and Kogan (2022a) considered a 
manufacturer allocating a scarce product among two competing retailers and explored how 
horizontal competition and apportionment affect equilibrium prices and consumer welfare. 
However, that model did not include any regulatory mechanism or subsidization; the whole­
sale prices were fixed, and the manufacturer did not act as a regulator. Third, Herbon and 
Kogan (2022b) examined a single-retailer framework in which a manufacturer (acting as a 
social planner) can directly subsidize the end-price paid by consumers. While that model 
demonstrated that direct subsidization can moderate panic buying, it also showed that such a 
policy is financially inefficient because it requires large budgetary support and provides the 
regulator with no indirect control over the retailer’s pricing decisions.
Building on our previous analyses of panic buying and regulatory intervention (Herbon 
& Kogan, 2022a, 2022b; Kogan & Herbon, 2022), the present paper develops a combined 
direct–indirect subsidization mechanism. The regulator specifies a price-dependent whole­
sale rate, allowing indirect influence over retail pricing while also providing a direct sub­
sidy to consumers. This endogenous formulation yields a unified game-theoretic framework 
capable of stabilizing prices and mitigating panic buying at substantially lower fiscal cost. 
These earlier studies collectively establish the analytical groundwork for the present paper.
1.4  The current problem and contribution
Although subsidizing may yield positive short-term benefits, governments might find it dif­
ficult to measure the success of subsidies. An operations-research approach, such as the one 
suggested in this study, has the potential to quantify the effectiveness of subsidizing a scarce 
product.
We consider an incentive-based regulation that uses rewards or financial incentives, such 
as subsidies or tax reductions, to induce the actor to conform to the desired behavior. The 
objective is to develop and analyze an operations-research model (specifically, a non-coop­
erative game) that reflects a vertical supply chain consisting of a supplier (a regulator or state 
company) and a retailer. The regulator (who does not have profit-making intent) supplies a 
product to the profit-driven retailer, who sells this product to end consumers. The product 
induces panic buying, defined from an operations-management perspective as a situation in 
which demand increases when inventory decreases, over a specified horizon. Zheng et al., 
(2021) develop a two-period optimization model and postulate that when the proportion 
of consumers who engage in stockpiling reaches a critical level, panic arises among the 
remaining consumers (due to online social learning), thereby inducing large-scale societal 
panic buying. This, in turn, significantly affects the quality of the service provided to con­
sumers, the retailer’s profit, and the total social welfare. We follow these ideas and assume 
that the demand rate increases as the inventory decreases. The supplier uses his own budget 
to directly subsidize the product, while indirect subsidization (using the retailer’s budget) 
takes place through the mechanism of a price-dependent wholesale unit price.
There are few analytical studies of inventory management under panic buying that con­
sider both consumer behavior and regulatory control. Unlike previous work that addresses 
the widespread practice of government involvement in price control, meaning that the price 
is either fixed or capped (see Murphy et al., 2019), we consider a market in which the retailer 
freely sets the selling price. Furthermore, existing models do not provide efficient tools to

regulate the retail price under conditions of panic buying; that is, the costs of subsidizing the 
product to the point where the consumer benefits from a substantial discount are too high 
(see example in Sect. 1.2 and the results of Herbon & Kogan, 2022b). The main objective 
of this paper is to develop a model of a relatively general nature (compared with previous 
models) that is capable of efficiently regulating market prices and product availability under 
panic buying. Our work contributes to the literature by developing the indirect subsidization 
mechanism and deriving an explicit solution for a flexible problem that combines the differ­
ent goals of the main players (i.e., the retailer and the regulator).In particular, we develop a 
non-cooperative game approach and show that by specifying the wholesale-price function 
offered to the retailer, the supplier (often a state company) can regulate prices, mitigate 
panic buying and reduce the number of leftovers.
2  The supply chain
Following the formulation in Herbon and Kogan (2022b) —which provided the baseline 
single-retailer, direct-subsidy model— we retain the same demand structure but extend it by 
endogenizing the wholesale price. Specifically, we assume that the demand rate increases 
as the retailer’s inventory decreases, reflecting panic buying. However, unlike the earlier 
model, where the wholesale price was fixed and only direct subsidization was available, 
here the wholesale price is expressed as an increasing convex function of the market price. 
This specification introduces an indirect-subsidization mechanism that allows the supplier–
regulator to influence the retailer’s behavior while simultaneously deciding on the level of 
direct subsidization. The interaction between the two parties is analyzed through a non-
cooperative game that determines the equilibrium strategies of both the regulator and the 
retailer.
2.1  System description and notations
Consider two players: a retailer located at point x = xfirm on the line [0, 1] and a sup­
plier that acts as the regulator. The consumer location x is distributed along the line [0, 1] 
with probability-density function (p.d.f.)f(x). The firm sells a single product (which may 
become scarce) to consumers at a market price p, where a proportion of the price, (1 −s), 
is subsidized by the regulator, 0 ≤s ≤1. The supplier charges the retailer a wholesale price 
per unit, cA(p), that depends on the market price. In addition to the direct subsidy provided 
by the regulator (just described), the retailer indirectly subsidizes the product by paying an 
“exaggerated” wholesale price (i.e., a price higher than the production and transportation 
costs incurred by the supplier). The regulator sets the length of the selling horizon T during 
which a single supply of N items is transported to the retailer; further replenishments are not 
accepted during this period due to the scarcity of the product. This situation may cause panic 
buying, and consequently, we assume a demand rate function that increases as the quantity 
of the product observed at the retailer decreases. The following set of notations completes 
the description of the supply chain under consideration:

6  Conclusions
6.1  Summary
The analysis of product scarcity, which may lead to panic buying, is a worthwhile endeavor, 
as it can reduce the risk that monopolistic firms will exploit their power and act against the 
interests of consumers seeking lower prices and greater product availability. In this paper, 
we analyze a situation in which the regulator has the option to gain monetary resources 
through the setting of an exaggerated wholesale price and can transform these resources into 
a subsidized price for the end consumer.
We develop a non-cooperative game model showing how the supplier, by specifying the 
wholesale-price function, can regulate prices, mitigate panic buying, and reduce leftovers 
by redirecting retailer profits to consumers. We conclude that the regulator can substantially

2022b; Kogan & Herbon, 2022)—in particular, that purely direct subsidisation or price-cap 
regulation alone cannot efficiently mitigate panic buying and may require excessive financial 
resources. However, the current paper contributes several new insights. By endogenising the 
wholesale price, the supplier (regulator) gains an additional lever of indirect influence over 
the retailer’s decisions, allowing comparable price stabilisation with significantly lower 
subsidy costs. The combined mechanism of direct and indirect subsidisation is shown to 
achieve consumer-price stability and to mitigate panic buying while significantly lowering 
the regulator’s total expenditure. This highlights a practical policy tool that maintains mar­
ket flexibility and social welfare under crisis conditions.
6.2  Managerial insights
Several practical managerial aspects arise from the results:
(a) The planning horizon T may play an important role in determining whether, and to what 
extent, panic buying is realized. The regulator who is aware of the potential for panic 
buying may set shorter horizons in order to reduce the percentage of time for which 
there is a shortage (when T is shortened, the probability of experiencing a shortage 
decreases). This, of course, would require him to replenish the supply sooner than was 
expected a priori, with significant added costs. Both options should be tested in real-life 
environments.
(b) Another tool for decreasing panic buying is to influence the retail price. In particular, 
the regulator may wish to increase the subsidized price, thus reducing the demand rate 
and consequently the proportion of time for which shortages occur. Other regulators, on 
the other hand, might choose to place higher priority on decreasing the price that end 
consumers pay.
(c) From expression (2), we conclude that under the assumption of a single firm, the dis­
tribution of consumer locations does not affect the demand rate at time t. However, the 
price paid by end consumers is expected to depend on the characteristics of their loca­
tion distribution.
(d) Through the selection of specific parameters (δ/N, m) that influence the unit wholesale 
price cA(p), a given regulator may express his policy priorities when seeking a balance 
between multiple goals (e.g., subsidizing the price and reducing panic buying).

6.3  Limitations and future research
(a) The developed model assumes a monopolistic firm. Future research should investigate 
scenarios in which there are multiple firms engaged in intrinsic competition. We believe 
that competition on its own would not be sufficient to prevent the exploitation of con­
sumers and to eliminate panic buying. Therefore, it is likely that measures for regulat­
ing the market, such as those developed in the current work, would still be needed.
(b) The developed model assumes a specific wholesale-price function. Under a multiple-
firm setting, future research may design a tailor-made wholesale-price function for each 
firm’s objective, yielding an even more effective regulatory tool for influencing con­
sumer welfare.
(c) Panic buying, according to the proposed model, is defined as the proportion of time for 
which the product is in shortage. Other definitions may be at the core of future research, 
which in turn would influence the policies suggested to mitigate panic buying. One pos­
sibility, which may be more realistic, is to assume that the phenomenon is transient over 
time rather than aggregate (e.g., the ratio between the demand rate and the inventory 
level at time t).
(d) Media may play a significant role in decreasing (or increasing) panic buying. This is 
assumed as an exogenous factor that may affect demand and the propensity for panic 
buying. Developing a model that specifies different strategies based on the intensity of 
this factor could be an important avenue for future research.
