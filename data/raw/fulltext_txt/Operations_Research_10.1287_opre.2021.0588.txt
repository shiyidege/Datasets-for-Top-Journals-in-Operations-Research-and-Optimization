On-time last-mile delivery is expanding rapidly as people expect faster delivery of goods ranging from grocery
to medicines. Managing on-time delivery systems is challenging because of the underlying uncertainties and
combinatorial nature of the routing decision. In practice, the e’¼āciency of such systems also hinges on the
driverŌĆÖs familiarity with the local neighborhood. This paper studies the optimal region partitioning policy to
minimize the expected delivery time of customer orders in a stochastic and dynamic setting. We allow both
the order locations and on-site service times to be random and generally distributed. This policy assigns
every driver to a subregion, hence making sure drivers will only be dispatched to their own territories. We
characterize the structure of the optimal partitioning policy and show its expected on-time performance
converges to that of the ’¼éexible dispatching policy in heavy tra’¼āc. The optimal characterization features
two insightful conditions that are critical to the on-time performance of last-mile delivery systems. We
then develop partitioning algorithms with performance guarantees, leveraging ham sandwich cuts and 3-
partitions from discrete geometry. This algorithmic development can be of independent interest for other
logistics problems. We demonstrate the e’¼āciency of the proposed region partitioning policy via numerical
experiments using synthetic and real-world data sets.
Key words : last-mile delivery, region partitioning, vehicle routing, queueing

In this paper, we study the optimal region partitioning policy for on-time last-mile delivery
in a stochastic and dynamic setting. In contrast to the ’¼éexible dispatching policies, we partition
the service region into multiple subregions and assign every driver to a subregion. Consequently,
each driver is dedicated to delivering orders from the assigned subregion, following a single-vehicle
dispatching policy. Partitioning policies (also called districting policies in other logistics problems)
are commonly used for practical on-time delivery problems due to their ease of implementation and
management. More importantly, they help drivers develop local knowledge and improve on-ground
operations from daily interactions.
Our model assumes a general demand arrival process with spatial heterogeneity, as captured by a
probability density function de’¼üned on a Euclidean plane. The objective is to minimize the expected
delivery time, which accounts for both the waiting time and on-site service time. We characterize
the performance of the dedicated region partitioning policy in heavy tra’¼āc, benchmarking with
the ’¼éexible dispatching policy. Our analysis reveals that the performance gap between the two
diminishes in heavy tra’¼āc. We then develop partitioning algorithms that are guaranteed to ’¼ünd
the optimal region partition to any desirable error tolerance, with polynomial time complexity.
Our results are further validated on synthetic and real-world data sets through simulations. When
comparing to alternative partitioning policies, the optimal partitioning policy delivers considerable
improvement in the on-time performance (either measured with the average performance or the
worst-case performance).

2.1. Region Partitioning in Logistics
Region partitioning, or districting, is a fundamental problem in logistics research, which belongs to
the broad class of tactical design decisions for the management of logistics systems (Daganzo 2005).
Zhong et al. (2007) propose a partitioning-based approach for day-to-day package delivery opera-
tions, with the goal of minimizing the total workload. Carlsson (2012) discusses how to partition a
service region to balance the workloads of multiple vehicles following traveling salesman problem
(TSP) solutions, given full information about the customer demand distribution function. When
only partial distributional information is available (such as moments or empirical distributions),
robust optimization approaches can be applied, see, e.g., Carlsson and Delage (2013), Carlsson
et al. (2018). In a facility location setting, Carlsson and Devulapalli (2013) characterize the optimal
partition that minimizes the maximum workload of facilities with Voronoi diagrams. These papers
only deal with the static problem and do not account for the dispatching decisions and uncertain
order arrival times in a dynamic context.
Recently, Banerjee et al. (2020) study the region partitioning problem for same-day delivery
systems using Voronoi diagrams. They simplify the stochasticity of the problem so other tactical
design issues can be addressed (including service area and ’¼éeet sizing decisions). While they focus
on maximizing the number of ful’¼ülled orders (within a day), we aim to minimize the expected
delivery time by incorporating the stochastic system dynamics explicitly. The districting ideas have
also been applied to shared mobility and urban retail problems (Qi et al. 2018, Cao et al. 2020a).
Notably, the existing partitioning literature mainly considers a partitioning problem with respect
to one measurable function (e.g., travel distance), our paper is the ’¼ürst to balance two measurable
functions in the logistics setting, which turns out to be critical to the on-time performance of
last-mile delivery systems. The proposed partitioning algorithms are guaranteed to yield optimal
system performance, overcoming the limitations of Voronoi diagrams.
Our approach builds on the discrete geometry literature that develops tools for partitioning
discrete sets of points with extensions to continuous measures. In addition to Voronoi diagrams,
the ham sandwich theorem establishes the possibility of dividing measurable objects in half with
multi-dimensional hyperplanes in general convex and polygonal regions (Beyer and Zardecki 2004,
Bereg et al. 2006, Kinsey 2012). Notably, Bespamyatnikh et al. (2000) develop an algorithm to
divide sets of red and blue points on a plane into disjoint convex polygons equitably with recursive
partitions. In this paper, we provide an algorithm that applies their result for the case where the
goal is to equitably partition a pair of measurable functions instead of sets of points.

2.2. Stochastic and Dynamic Vehicle Routing
Our work is related to stochastic and dynamic vehicle routing problems, where order arrivals are
uncertain and vehicles have to be dispatched dynamically in response to order arrivals. In terms of
methodologies, our paper follows the queueing modeling framework of Bertsimas and Van Ryzin
(1993a,b). They propose several dynamic policies based on order batching, which are proved to be
asymptotically good (with constant performance guarantees). We extend their results to analyze
the optimal region partitioning policy in a general setting that allows both order arrivals and
service times to be spatially heterogeneous. The analysis is based on continuous approximation that
seeks to replace combinatorial solution values with simpler formulas, which has been applied in
other challenging routing problems (Ansari et al. 2018, Cao et al. 2020b). We leverage the famous
TSP tour length formula from Beardwood et al. (1959) to characterize the on-time performance of
dispatching and routing policies in a tractable way.
Improved dispatching and routing policies can be developed, but due to the complexity of the
problem, optimality can be hardly guaranteed. For example, insertion heuristics, a priori policies,
rollout methods, and approximate dynamic programming approaches have been applied to solve
for dynamic dispatching and routing decisions in a stochastic environment (see, e.g., in Azi et al.
2012, Goodson et al. 2013, Klapp et al. 2018, Liu and Luo 2021). For the same-day delivery problem
with random customer arrivals, Voccia et al. (2019) propose an approximate forward dynamic
programming approach that combines scenario-based planning and a variable neighborhood search
heuristic to derive real-time solutions. We refer readers to Ulmer et al. (2020) for a comprehensive
review of recent advances in this area.

tomerŌĆÖs patience level is incorporated, Chen and Hu (2020) analyze the relative bene’¼üt of batching
as a function of market conditions in a stylized model. Liu et al. (2021) model driverŌĆÖs routing

3.2. Dedicated Region Partitioning Policy
In this paper, we study a restricted class of dispatching policies that rely on region partitioning.
Under this policy, the service region R is ’¼ürst partitioned into m subregions (districts), and then the
m vehicles are matched to those subregions such that each subregion is assigned exactly one driver.
As orders come in, each driver will be dedicated to serving orders from the assigned subregion. We
call this policy the dedicated region partitioning (districting) policy (the readers shall di’¼Ćerentiate
our dedicated region partitioning policy with the partitioning policies proposed in Bertsimas and
Van Ryzin (1993a), wherein their policies permit drivers serving orders from di’¼Ćerent subregions).
This policy can be reckoned a divide-and-conquer dispatching algorithm.
The dedicated region partitioning policy is in contrast to the dynamic vehicle routing policies
that can ’¼éexibly dispatch a driver to di’¼Ćerent areas of the service region in di’¼Ćerent periods. While
each driver has to be dispatched dynamically in his/her subregion (e.g., follows the single-vehicle
policy ŽĆŌłŚ), the dedicated partitioning policy drops the need to coordinate di’¼Ćerent drivers. When
cast as a queueing model, the comparison between the dedicated region partitioning policy and
’¼éexible dispatching policy is analogous to that between the separate (specialized) queueing system
and pooling system (with complete resource pooling).
The dedicated region partitioning policy is often preferable for practical last-mile logistics sys-
tems due to the ease of operations and management (Zhong et al. 2007, Huang et al. 2019). More
importantly, because most on-time last-mile delivery service providers operate in urban areas that
feature high-rise buildings and complicated road environments, driversŌĆÖ familiarity of the local
neighborhood is critical to the system performance. The dedicated region partitioning policy facil-
itates the driversŌĆÖ development of tacit local knowledge and thus accelerates their performance
improvement over time. In what follows, we will simply refer to this dedicated policy as region
partitioning policy whenever no confusion may arise.

5.1. Benchmark Policies
We consider the following benchmark dispatching policies for comparisons:
1. Horizontal partitioning policy. This policy applies horizontal cuts to partition the service
region to m subregions. We consider two variants of it depending on the goal of partitioning:
(1) Balancing demand arrival rates among subregions (HP1); (2) Balancing workload factors
among subregions (HP2). Note that HP1 may not be stable when the demand tra’¼āc is high,
whereas HP2 manages to be stable as long as the region-wise workload factor is less than one.
Both HP1 and HP2 are not necessarily spatially unbiased.
2. Wedge partitioning policy. This policy leverages radial cuts to partition the service region,
which gives rise to wedge-like subregions. Similar to HP1 and HP2, we test two di’¼Ćerent wedge
partitioning policies: WP1 balances demand arrival rates and WP2 balances workload factors
between partitioned subregions.
3. Modi’¼üed tour partitioning (MTP) policy. This is the dynamic dispatching policy (ŽĆŌłŚ) proposed
in Bertsimas and Van Ryzin (1993a,b). MTP builds upon wedge partitions to form feasible
tours of size q and service them FCFS using the ’¼ürst available vehicle. Speci’¼ücally, MTP ’¼ürst
collects batches of orders (with batch size b) from subregions de’¼üned by radial cuts so orders
in each batch come from the same subregion. Then the optimal TSP tours are formed on these
batches and assigned to vehicles in FCFS order. Unlike the dedicated partitioning policies,
MTP allows drivers to ’¼éexibly visit di’¼Ćerent subregions in di’¼Ćerent dispatches, which serves as
a theoretically lower bounding policy for comparisons. The batch size b is a tuning parameter
for MTP.
HP1, HP2, WP1, and WP2 are all dedicated partitioning policies, but they follow di’¼Ćerent
partitioning rules. Because these partitioning policies are restricted to a speci’¼üc type of partition
(either using horizontal cuts or radial cuts), they can only balance one measurable function. While
balancing demand arrival rates is intuitive, it can lead to unstable performance for certain subre-
gions in heavy tra’¼āc. Therefore, we also test the case where the workload factor is balanced in
partitioning (HP2 and WP2). Furthermore, we assume each driver follows the MTP policy in the
assigned subregion for the studied dedicated partitioning policies.

6. Concluding Remarks
Our paper presents the ’¼ürst attempt to characterize the optimal region partitioning policy for
on-time last-mile delivery. We prove that in heavy tra’¼āc, the optimal region partitioning policy
achieves the same performance guarantee with the best-known ’¼éexible policy with spatial unbi-
asedness. The characterization relies on two equitable conditions for di’¼Ćerent measurable functions
de’¼üned on the Euclidean space. Based on these two conditions, we develop algorithms that are
able to ’¼ünd the optimal partition to any error tolerance in polynomial time. The algorithms lever-
age developments from discrete geometry to generate ham sandwich cuts and 3-partitions (3-fans)
recursively. We con’¼ürm the promising performance of the optimal partitioning policy through
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3915544

32
Carlsson et al.: Provably Good Region Partitioning for On-Time Last-Mile Delivery
numerical studies on synthetic and real-world data sets. Compared to alternative partitioning rules,
our policy leads to shorter and more balanced expected delivery time of customer orders.
Our analysis assumes each vehicle follows the same dispatching policy as in Bertsimas and
Van Ryzin (1993a) within the assigned subregion. In theory, this policy may not be optimal outside
the heavy tra’¼āc regime. Instead, more advanced dispatching and routing algorithms can be applied
for each vehicle separately. Due to the complexity of those algorithms, it would be di’¼ācult to
analyze their performance and characterize the corresponding optimal partition. Nevertheless, we
expect the proposed partitioning policy to perform well as it captures the key driving forces of
on-time performance in last-mile delivery: travel time from/to the depot, local travel time between
customer locations, and on-site service time.
Another limitation of our study is that we restrict our attention to spatially unbiased dispatching
policies. Although spatial unbiasedness is consistent with the minimax approach (and thus relevant
for companies aiming to improve the worst-case performance), it may not be desired in some
applications. Speci’¼ücally, spatial unbiasedness may push up the overall delivery time and lower
the system e’¼āciency (Bertsimas and Van Ryzin 1993b). In practice, the delivery service provider
may ’¼ünd the price of spatial unbiasedness too high to serve all customers, particularly those in
areas far away from the depot, forcing the provider to exit certain markets. In this regard, spatial
unbiasedness may not always lead to fair outcomes. We believe more work should be done to
investigate the fairness implications of delivery service design in a spatial context (see Banerjee
et al. (2023) for an interesting discussion based on time-varying service regions).
We also assume a ’¼éeet of homogeneous vehicles. One might extend our work to consider driver
heterogeneity, which can arise when drivers have distinct prior knowledge about the service region.
Another relevant direction is to model driverŌĆÖs learning curve explicitly in region partitioning. It
is also helpful to collect real driver behavior data to quantify the value of driver learning. Lastly,
it would be interesting to analyze a partially ’¼éexible system where a driver is assigned to a major
subregion and a secondary subregion simultaneously (secondary-region orders will be assigned to
the driver when orders from the major subregion are too few). As such we may strike a better
balance between capacity utilization and delivery specialization.
