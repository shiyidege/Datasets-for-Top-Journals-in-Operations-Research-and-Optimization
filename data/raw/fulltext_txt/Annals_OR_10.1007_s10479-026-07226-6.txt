Abstract
We study countably in’¼ünite Markov decision processes with B├╝chi objectives, which ask
to visit a given subset of states in’¼ünitely often. A question left open by T.P. Hill (1979) is
whether there always exist ╬Ą-optimal Markov strategies, i.e., strategies that base decisions
only on the current state and on the clock (the number of steps taken so far). We provide a
negative answer to this question by constructing a non-trivial counterexample.

By ’¼üxing a strategy for the controller, one obtains a probability space over in’¼ünite system
executions, also called runs of the MDP. The goal of the controller is to optimize the expected
value of some objective function on the runs.
The type of strategy needed for an optimal (resp. ╬Ą-optimal) strategy for some objective
is called the strategy complexity of the objective. There are different types of strategies,
depending on whether one can take the whole history of the run into account (history-
dependent, H), or whether one is limited to a ’¼ünite amount of memory (’¼ünite memory,
F) or whether decisions are based only on the current state (memoryless, M). Moreover,
we distinguish strategies based on whether the controller can randomize (R) or is limited
to deterministic choices (D). The simplest type, MD, refers to memoryless deterministic
strategies. Markov strategies are strategies that base their decisions only on the current state
and the number of steps in the history of the run. Thus they do use in’¼ünite memory, but
only in a very restricted form by maintaining an unbounded step-counter (also called the
clock). For ’¼ünite MDPs, there exist optimal MD-strategies for many (but not all) objectives
(Chatterjee et al., 2004a,b; Chatterjee & Henzinger, 2012; Puterman, 1994), but the picture
is more complex for countably in’¼ünite MDPs (Kiefer et al., 2017; Ornstein, 1969; Puterman,
1994).
Unless otherwise stated, we consider the B├╝chi objective, where one wants to visit a given
subset F of the states in’¼ünitely often. In the simpler Reachability objective one just wants to
reach a set F at least once.
Previous work on 2-player stochastic games Our recent result in Kiefer et al. (2025) shows
a very strong upper bound on the strategy complexity of B├╝chi objectives in concurrent
(aka simultaneous-move) 2-player stochastic games with a countable number of states. For
Maximizer, ╬Ą-optimal strategies for B├╝chi require just one bit of public memory if the game
graph is acyclic, and only the clock plus one bit of public memory for general game graphs.
These results assume that MinimizerŌĆÖs action sets are ’¼ünite, but MaximizerŌĆÖs action sets may
be in’¼ünite. Moreover, MaximizerŌĆÖs strategy can be made deterministic if the game is turn-
based (aka alternating-move). This deterministic clock-plus-one-bit upper bound trivially
carries over to countably in’¼ünite MDPs, even if the action sets are in’¼ünite (Kiefer et al.,
2025).
For concurrent stochastic games, corresponding lower bounds have long been known. In
the ŌĆ£Bad MatchŌĆØ game (Maitra & Sudderth, 1996; Thuijsman, 1992), both Markov strategies
and all strategies with ’¼ünite (private) memory are worthless. I.e., these cannot guarantee any
positive attainment for Maximizer, even though the initial state has value 1. See also (Kiefer
et al. (2025), Sec. 3) for a detailed discussion. The ŌĆ£Bad MatchŌĆØ is a concurrent B├╝chi game
with ’¼ünitely many states and ’¼ünite action sets. If one instead considers ’¼ünite-state turn-
based stochastic games with ’¼ünite action sets then there always exist optimal MD Maximizer
strategies for the B├╝chi objective (Chatterjee & Henzinger, 2012), and this trivially carries
over to ’¼ünite-state MDPs with ’¼ünite action sets (see also (Puterman, 1994)).
Previous work on MDPs The situation is more complex for MDPs with a countably in’¼ünite
number of states and/or in’¼ünite action sets. For countably in’¼ünite MDPs, optimal strategies
(where they exist) and ╬Ą-optimal strategies for Reachability can be chosen MD (Ornstein,
1969; Puterman, 1994). Similarly, optimal strategies for B├╝chi (where they exist) can be
chosen MD (Kiefer et al., 2017). However, ╬Ą-optimal strategies for B├╝chi require in’¼ünite
memory (cannot be chosen FR); cf. Kiefer et al. (2017); Kr╦ćc├Īl (2009).

A partial answer was given by Hill (1979) (Proposition 5.1), who showed that ╬Ą-optimal

Our contribution We provide a negative answer to the open problem by presenting a non-
trivial counterexample. We construct an MDP with a countably in’¼ünite number of states
such that the step-counter (i.e., the clock) from the initial state s0 is implicit in every state.

other words, all action sets are of size Ōēż2 and all distributions over successor states have
a ’¼ünite support of size Ōēż2. While the initial state s0 has value 1 wrt. B├╝chi, there are no
╬Ą-optimal Markov strategies from s0 for any ╬Ą < 1. I.e., Markov strategies are worthless
here. In combination with the example from Figure 1, this shows that, for countable MDPs
(even with ’¼ünite action sets), neither ’¼ünite memory (FR) nor Markov strategies are suf’¼ücient

3 The lower bound
In this section we solve HillŌĆÖs problem ( (Hill, 1979) and (Hill (1999), Q1) by exhibiting an
MDP where the initial state has value 1 wrt. the B├╝chi objective, but every Markov strategy
achieves this objective with probability 0.

1, but for every Markov strategy Žā, we have PM,s0,Žā (B├╝chi(F)) = 0. I.e., Markov strategies
are worthless for B├╝chi.
The MDP M from Theorem 1 is constructed such that in every state s the number of steps
from the start state s0 is implicit in s. Hence using a clock does not confer any advantage to a
strategy starting in s0, and thus Markov strategies are equally good as memoryless strategies.
