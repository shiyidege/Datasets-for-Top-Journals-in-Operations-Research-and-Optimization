Abstract
We propose a direct non-parametric estimation framework for multi-state disability models
that does not rely on the Markov assumption. In this context, ŌĆ£directŌĆØ denotes a method
that does not necessitate the initial estimation of transition intensities. Previous research has
demonstrated evidence of non-Markovian properties in disability dynamics, and a number
of studies have employed semi-Markov models for Long-Term-Care products. We test the
non-Markov assumption on an up-to-date American dataset that includes more than 200,000
records, ’¼ünding insights on the non-Markovian character of disability. We then focus on
transition probabilities, showing the impact of our assumption on these crucial metrics for
actuaries. In addition, we investigate the in’¼éuence of health conditions on all the transitions
analyzed.

In this paper we deal with the estimation of transition probabilities in multi-state models for
disability, focusing our attention on the dataset provided by the Health and Retirement Study.
We put our emphasis on two aspects of the estimation. First, we adopt a direct estimation
approach which not only has the advantage of overcoming the need for the estimation of the
initial transition intensities, but also, and most importantly, allows the Markovian assumption
to be relaxed in favor of the semi-Markov assumption. Second, we attempt to exploit the
information contained in the HRS dataset about the health conditions of individuals. Previous
studies on LTC are predominantly based on the Markov assumption [(Fong et al., 2015;
Levantesi and Menzietti, 2012; Li et al., 2017; Olivieri and Pitacco, 2001; Pitacco, 1995;
Pritchard, 2006; Rickayzen and Walsh, 2002; Sherris and Wei, 2021; Stallard, 2011), to
mention just a few]. This assumption is usually justi’¼üed by the lack of suf’¼ücient information
in the available datasets and the greater simplicity of modeling, but more realistic models
can be constructed by considering semi-Markov processes.
A range of studies have explored the application of semi-Markov models in long-term care
and disability insurance. DŌĆÖAmico et al. (2009, 2012, 2013), Biessy (2015, 2016, 2017) have
contributed to this area, with DŌĆÖAmico focusing on disability insurance contracts and Biessy
on long-term care insurance. Both have used semi-Markov processes to study disability evo-
lution and duration, with Biessy speci’¼ücally considering the impact of different pathologies
on mortality. Xuanyuan and Xuanyuan (2023) has provided a broader overview of the use of
Markov and semi-Markov processes in long-term care risk and insurance, highlighting the
challenges and potential solutions. Stenberg et al. (2006) has also discussed the relevance
of semi-Markov models in disability insurance, while DŌĆÖAmico et al. (2013) and Biessy
(2017) have developed speci’¼üc models for long-term care insurance, including a multi-state
semi-Markov model to characterize the dependency process in elderly individuals. In a recent

This paper contributes to the literature of the estimation of transition probabilities for
disability insurance in several ways. First, to the best of our knowledge, this is the ’¼ürst paper
that questions the Markov hypothesis in the HRS dataset: in testing this hypothesis we take
advantage of the depth of the time series, spanning the period from 1992 to 2020, including
data up to the most recent publicly available wave. It turns out that a semi-Markov process
suits better to this dataset. Second, in comparison with other papers adopting semi-Markov
models for disability insurance, we consider explicitly the probability of recovery from the
state of disability to the state of activity. Third, we carry out a speci’¼üc focus on the dependence
of transition probabilities on the health status of the subjects analysed. Indeed, we exploit the
richness of the information contained in the HRS dataset and model explicitly the dependence
of the state-to-state transitions with the health status of the subject.

possibly semi-Markov, model we use for our analysis. Section3 presents the dataset. In Sect.4

2 Methodology
A multi-state model represents a continuous-time stochastic process with outcomes from a
discrete set. It ’¼ünds extensive application in longitudinal medical studies, particularly where
patients undergo multiple events, and data related to these events are collected over time.
The intricacy of such a model is in’¼éuenced by both the number of states it encompasses and
the potential transitions between these states. For further insights into multi-state models we
refer the reader to Van Den Hout (2016).
A speci’¼üc subset of multi-state models comprises Markov processes, where state transi-
tions occur between discrete levels and adhere strictly to the Markov property. However, many
real-world scenarios necessitate stochastic processes that demonstrate dependence between
transition times. For instance, in biostatistics, when analyzing the trajectory of patients in a
hospital, employing a Markov process as a multi-state model imposes a strict constraint on
the distribution of time spent in each state. This constraint arises from the inherent nature of
123

Annals of Operations Research
Markov processes, wherein state sojourn times follow an exponential distribution and exhibit
ŌĆ£non-agingŌĆØ behavior with a constant hazard rate. Conversely, semi-Markov processes relax
this assumption, allowing for greater ’¼éexibility in modeling dependence between transition
times. Consequently, Markov processes often lack the desired ’¼éexibility for modeling such
dependencies.
Expanding the modeling framework from Markov processes to the class of Semi-Markov
Processes (SMPs) alleviates the restriction of memoryless (exponential) state sojourn times
while maintaining the convenience of representing data as continuous-time jump processes.
In SMPs, the Markov property is preserved for the embedded chain of states, while allowing
for a relaxation of the Markov property for sojourn times. This feature makes SMPs suitable
for modeling a diverse range of phenomena across various domains, including economics,
reliability, and healthcare. For further exploration of SMPs and their applications, refer to
Janssen (2013).

3 Health and Retirement Study
The University of Michigan Health and Retirement Study (HRS) is a longitudinal study that
surveys a representative sample of people in America, supported by the National Institute
on Aging and the Social Security Administration. This dataset constitutes an extensive and
ongoing longitudinal household survey in the United States, focusing on individuals aged
50 and above since 1992. Interviews are conducted every two years, capturing a wide range
of information including demographics, income, assets, health status, healthcare expenses,
employment history, family dynamics, and more.
In addition, the dataset contains indicator variables related to health conditions and Activ-
ities of Daily Living (ADL). The variables related to the health condition are the following:
highbloodpressureorhypertension;diabetesorhighbloodsugar;canceroramalignanttumor
of any kind except skin cancer; chronic lung disease except asthma such as chronic bronchitis
or emphysema; heart attack, coronary heart disease, angina, congestive heart failure, or other
heart problems; emotional, nervous, or psychiatric problems; arthritis or rheumatism. The
ADLs provide insights into the incidence of disability and recovery and include the follow-
ing tasks: walking across room, dressing, bathing or showering, eating, getting in or out of
bed, using the toilet. Each wave has a series of questions about limits on ADLs, in the data
the answers are recoded into 5-categories that we transformed into a binary variable (0 =
no limit or 1 = limits). Table 1 displays the list of variables selected from the RAND HRS
Longitudinal File.
We follow the literature and classify the state of individuals being: (i) in state Active if
they report at most one dif’¼üculty in ADL; (ii) in state Disabled if they report two or more
dif’¼üculties in any of the ADLs. The dataset contains information on individualsŌĆÖ deaths and
their respective dates, which permits the identi’¼ücation of transitions towards state Death.
Moreover, we go a step beyond the benchmark literature by examining the potential
impact of health conditions and diseases on getting disabled/recovering from disability. For
this reason, we create a new variable, named ŌĆ£Health ConditionsŌĆØ, counting the number of
positive answers to the speci’¼üc questions related to health and contained in Table 1, with
values H = 0 in case of no positive answers, H = 1 in case of a single positive answer, H = 2
in case of two or more positive answers, to be included in the possible covariates.

In this section we illustrate the inference of semi-Markov models on the HRS dataset and the
effects on the transition probabilities. This data originates from scheduled interviews, which
may result in interval censoring, as the exact times of state transitions are approximated to
interview times. While addressing this interval censoring is important, we do not delve into
it further in this discussion and refer the reader to Commenges (2002).
We use the R package SemiMarkov (Listwon-Krol and Saint-Pierre, 2015) to perform esti-
mation and analyze the results. Details about the methodology behind SemiMarkov package
can be found in Asanjarani et al. (2022).

4.3 Markov versus semi-Markov
In this subsection we focus on the effects of alternative speci’¼ücations of the regression model
on the resulting transition probabilities. Our aim is to alert academics and practitioners about
the enormous differences between transition probabilities extracted from Markovian models
and those derived from semi-Markovian models.
Table 10 reports the differences, in percentages, between transition probabilities extracted
from W1 with respect to those coming from E1. While we recall that W1 dominates E1 in
terms of goodness of ’¼üt, we observe remarkable differences for transitions between healthy
to disabled and to death state and also from recovery. Sometimes the differences are more
than 60% in absolute values. We also observe that the shorter the duration of the period the
higher the differences. Many transitions show substantial discrepancies between W1 and E1
in the early years; the relative differences tend to decrease with the duration, indicating that
the models may align more closely in their long-term predictions.
ŌĆó The relative differences for the transition p21 (Disabled to Healthy) do not show persistent
over- or under-estimation of the quantity in relation to the duration. For example, at age

year 5, these values shift to 20.1% and 25.1%, respectively. This shift from negative to
