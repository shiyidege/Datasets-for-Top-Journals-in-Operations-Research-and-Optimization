Abstract
Harry Markowitz is known as the grandfather of behavioral ﬁnance based on his 1952 work on
utility theory. We study a behavioral issue applied to the currency market: seasonal affective
disorder (SAD). As the days grow shorter (longer) in fall (spring), investors become more
(less) risk averse due to changes in depression related to SAD. Our empirical results are
consistent with changes in risk-taking in global equities and the associated change in currency
hedging portfolios. In the spring/summer season of long daylight hours, we ﬁnd evidence
of greater short positions for the euro. This is consistent with investors taking more risk in
global equities and adding to their currency shorts to hedge the FX exposure. Such changes
in euro holdings are reversed in the season of shorter daylight hours, consistent with risky
investments being reduced due to greater risk aversion so currency hedges are reduced.
For currency returns, we ﬁnd that the greater shorting in spring–summer is associated with
currency depreciation over the season of long days. In the season of short days, currency
buying associated with cutting hedging positions leads to currency appreciation. We ﬁnd that
the SAD inﬂuence on seasonal currency returns is much like the evidence for equity returns.
Finally, we construct and backtest a SAD-inspired currency portfolio. We ﬁnd that trading
the spring/summer risk-on SAD effect from longer days and recovery from SAD-related
depression had a decent positive risk-adjusted performance and displayed fairly consistent
performance over time.

1 Introduction
One of the authors (M. Melvin) used to meet Harry Markowitz for lunch when they both
worked at U.C. San Diego in the years around his 90th birthday. Each lunch was followed
by long walks there they would discuss ﬁnancial modeling, current ﬁnancial markets, and
other issues of current relevancy. Harry would often relate stories from his past to shed
light on some current discussion we had. On one walk, an issue of behavioral ﬁnance arose,
and Harry said that he was the “Grandfather of behavioral ﬁnance”. There are at least two
interviews with Harry where he recounts the story that led him to claim ‘grandfather’ status.
Kritzman’s (2017) Financial Analysts Journal interview with Harry omitted the discussion
of behavioral ﬁnance, but Kritzman has a longer (unpublished) version of the interview that
contains Harry’s remarks in this area.1 Lo and Foerster (2021, p. 43) in their book In Pursuit
of the Perfect Portfolio interviewed Harry and cover the story. The original source document
would be Kahneman’s Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) where in Chapter 26 he talks about
how he and Amos Tversky were working on a theory of decision making that became the well-
known prospect theory. They were discussing how experiments where subjects receive a small
change in their wealth of a few dollars could be relevant for real-world behavior. Kahneman
tells the story of how Tversky “remembered that the economist Harry Markowitz, who would
later earn the Nobel Prize for his work on ﬁnance, had proposed a theory in which utilities
were attached to changes of wealth rather than to states of wealth.2 Markowitz’s idea had
been around for a quarter of a century and had not attracted much attention, but we quickly
concluded that this was the way to go, and that the theory we were planning to develop would
deﬁne outcomes as gains and losses, not as states of wealth.” In this way, Harry helped provide
theoretical underpinnings of behavioral ﬁnance. Guerard (2023, p. 1497) discusses the paper
and concludes that “the Markowitz utility theory paper became one of the great foundations
of behavioral economics and has profoundly impacted researchers in behavioral ﬁnance.”
We retell the story of Harry and behavioral ﬁnance to help motivate our work. Harry had
wide-ranging interests and had incorporated behavioral issues in his work, even if not labeling
it as such. For instance, in volume 1 of Risk and Return Analysis (2014), the ﬁrst chapter is
devoted to The Expected Utility Maxim where he compares the ‘rational decision maker’ and
the ‘human decision maker’, fully cognizant of the relevance of human limitations. Harry
would ﬁnd empirical evidence of behavioral effects an interesting topic for discussion. In
that spirit, we seek to explore behavioral effects in a market that is seldom analyzed in a
behavioral context, the foreign exchange (FX) market. We will examine ‘seasonal affective
disorder’ (SAD) effects on FX returns and holdings. The next section provides a brief review
of the ﬁnance literature on SAD.
2 Seasonal aﬀective disorder eﬀects
Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) is a form of major depressive disorder associated with
diminished daylight. The source of the condition is physiological, related to serotonin dys-
regulation in the brain. Experimental research in psychology indicates there is a direct link
between depression and both impatience and heightened risk aversion. A ﬁrst step is the
link between daylight hours and depression. Many studies have established this relationship.
Examples are Molin, et al. who study subjects in Denmark and conclude that “the amount

2.1 Impatience
Both experimental and neuroimaging research supports the hypothesis that depressed people
tend to be more impatient. Pulcu et al. (2014) conduct an experiment using subjects with
major depressive disorder (MDD) and a control group of healthy subjects and ﬁnd that
patients with current MDD, relative to healthy subjects, discounted large-sized future rewards
at a signiﬁcantly higher rate and were insensitive to changes in reward size from medium
to large. Discount rates for large-sized rewards correlated with the severity of depressive
symptoms, particularly hopelessness. Someone with a feeling of hopelessness is less likely
to choose delayed beneﬁts over current beneﬁts. Amlung et al. (2019) conduct a meta-
analysis of the psychiatry literature for different psychiatric disorders and evidence of effects
on discount rates chosen by subjects. The research includes seven studies of MDD effects
where subjects faced questions regarding monetary rewards. The depressed subjects display
signiﬁcantly higher discount rates (greater impatience) than the control group. Ludwig et al.
(2015) perform magnetic resonance imaging on subjects while they carry out a monetary
incentive task with a delay manipulation. Their research reveals that depressed participants
have a different part of the brain activated when winning rewards than healthy participants.
The analysis indicates that depression is related to stronger neural responses for immediate
relative to delayed rewards.
2.2 Risk aversion
There are several studies that support the hypothesis that depression increases risk aversion.
Pietramonaco and Rook (1987) conduct experiments with depressed and healthy subjects
and ﬁnd that the depressed assign higher weight to risks than the control group and are
more cautious than healthy subjects when facing decisions. Smoski et al. (2008) conduct
experiments involving choices over more or less risky decisions. The depressed cohort learn
to avoid risky choices faster and choose less risk than the healthy cohort. Harlow and Brown
(1990) research an individual’s “sensation seeking” and risk tolerance in an experimental
auction setting. Sensation seeking measures the preference for risky activities. Depressed
subjects score much lower in terms of sensation seeking than healthy subjects. The authors
ﬁnd that the larger the sensation seeking score, the more willing subjects are to take ﬁnancial
risk. Other studies relating higher sensation seeking scores to greater ﬁnancial risk taking
include Horvath and Zuckerman (1993), Wong and Carducci (1991) and Sciortino et al.
(1987).
If some investors suffer from SAD, then their portfolio decisions may reﬂect seasonal
changes in risk aversion and consequent preferences for risky and safe assets varying over
the seasons. The authors can ﬁnd only a couple of studies that document such portfolio choice
behavior. Ben-Rephael et al. (2012) analyze mutual fund net exchanges between equity and
bond funds in one fund family as a proxy for investor asset allocation decisions. They ﬁnd
that net exchanges into equity funds are signiﬁcantly lower in September and October when
daylight hours begin to grow shorter. Kamstra et al., (2017) also study mutual fund ﬂows and

3 Seasonal variation in daylight and SAD-related depression
Everyone knows that daylight hours vary systematically over the months of the year. Days
are longer in summer and shorter in winter. Table 1, second column, reports the average hours
of daylight for each month in three major ﬁnancial capitals: London, New York City, and
Tokyo. The third column lists the monthly change. The fourth column scales the monthly
averages by the annual average hours of daylight for each city.
The longest days in London occur around the summer solstice in late June when there can
be over 16 ½ H of daylight. The shortest days are around the winter solstice in late December
with only about 7 1/2 h of daylight. London is at a higher latitude than New York City, so
it experiences slightly longer summer days but shorter winter days compared to NYC. The
swing between the longest and shortest days is around 9 h of daylight from winter to summer
in London. For New York City, the days are longest around the summer solstice in late June
when New York City can see around 15 h of daylight. The days are shortest around the winter
solstice in late December when there is a little over 9 h of daylight. The swing between the
shortest and longest days is over 6 h of daylight from winter to summer in New York. The
longest days in Tokyo occur around the summer solstice in late June with about 14 ½ h of
daylight. The shortest days are around the winter solstice in late December with only 9 h
of daylight. Tokyo is at a lower latitude than New York City and London, so it experiences
shorter extremes in day length. The swing between the longest and shortest days is around 5
½ H from winter to summer in Tokyo. Overall, Tokyo has more evenly distributed daylight
hours throughout the year compared to higher latitude cities like New York and London.
All northern hemisphere cities have the same general pattern for average monthly hours of
daylight, where longest days occur in May, June, and July and shortest days are in November,
December, and January.
If we shift our focus from the average hours of daylight to the monthly change from
the prior month, we see in the third column that March and April are the months with the
biggest increase in daylight hours from the prior month. October is the month with the biggest
decrease in daylight hours for London and New York City, while September and October
have the same decrease in daylight hours in Tokyo. One question is when does the SAD effect

4 Currency holdings data
The Bank of New York (BNY) produces daily measures of asset ﬂows and holdings via its
iFlow platform. With approximately $48.8 trillion in assets under custody and administration
(AUCA), BNY is the world’s largest global custodian. This is approximately 20% of total
market capitalization across all asset classes globally. The iFlow program aggregates and
anonymizes data from these custody holdings at a daily frequency, on a T-1 lag. At approx-
imately 7:30 AM each trading day, ﬂows and holdings for currencies are available through
the previous day’s market close.
BNY’s global custody footprint is primarily concentrated in North America and Europe.
Approximately 70% of AUCA are from North American domiciled funds, 21% European,
4.5% Asia–Paciﬁc, and 4% Latin American. These data almost exclusively represent “real
money” investments. Hedge funds, central bank, and corporate activities are largely absent.
While this implies that iFlow is an incomplete sample of all investor classes, this primarily
long-only and unlevered sample is relatively homogenous and has the attractive quality of
persistence, as real money investors’ activity is spread out over time and not as volatile as
hedge fund ﬂows nor as “lumpy” as central bank ﬂows.

5 Empirical results
We focus on seasonal variations in currency returns and currency holdings. We compare the
FX currency returns data to ﬁndings for the equity market and ﬁnd broadly similar results.
The currency holdings data also show seasonal effects in line with the SAD hypothesis, but
only for the euro are results statistically signiﬁcant.
5.1 Currency returns
The literature on SAD effects in ﬁnancial markets indicates that as risk preferences change
from risk-on in spring to risk-off in fall, returns and portfolio holdings should change as
well. For returns, the risk-off season of fall/winter should have risky assets initially sold
off as days grow shorter and then over the season, risky returns are higher. In the risk-on
season of spring/summer, risky assets are initially bought as days grow longer and over the
season risky returns are lower. This is the return pattern found in Kamstra et al. (2014) and
others for equity returns. Kamstra et al. show US equity returns averaged across the years
1959–2012 for the spring/summer season of April-September and the fall/winter season of
October–March. For the April-September period, US equity returns averaged 1.149%. For the
October–March period, US equity returns averaged 6.284%. The seasonal change in returns
from spring/summer to fall/winter was 5.134%.
The presence of a systematic seasonal pattern in equity returns related to SAD effects
that persists year-to-year seems counter to what one might expect with efﬁcient markets.
However, SAD effects on ﬁnancial markets have only recently been studied. It may be that
over time such effects diminish if speculators take positions to proﬁt from the SAD-related
changes in returns. However, if seasonal returns reﬂect seasonal changes in risk preferences
that persist in the future, then the seasonal return pattern may persist as the required premium
for taking risk varies with season.
For currencies, we expect the seasonal mechanism to be a bit different from equities.
Consider a US-based global equity investor with equity positions in countries around the
world. If the investor is long the equities of a country, then they will also be long the currency
in which the equities are denominated. Many global equity investors will hedge at least a
portion of their FX risk as they are largely interested in earning the equity returns and do not
view themselves as having any edge in currency investing. As a result, they hedge the implied
long currency positions by shorting the currency with forwards, options, or futures positions.
This suggests that in the risk-on spring season, they would increase their equity holdings and
increase their short currency positions and, in the risk-off fall season they would decrease
their equity positions and, consequently, cut their short currency positions. This seasonal
currency selling in spring–summer leads to seasonal currency depreciation over the season
of long days. In the fall risk-off season, it is just the opposite. As global equity positions are
cut to reduce risk, we should see currency short positions reduced accordingly. This seasonal
buying of currencies should result in currency appreciation over the fall-winter season of
shorter days.

It is surprising that the SAD-effects on currency holdings revealed in the regressions of
Table 4 show no statistical signiﬁcance for the GBP, JPY, and USD. Why only the EUR
seems susceptible to SAD-related seasonal effects is unclear. One can think of potential
explanations. One might be that the largely U.S.-based real money investors alter their hedge
ratios seasonally more for the EUR than the other currencies. They may also practice dynamic
hedging in response to currency factors like carry, momentum, and value and those factors
are offsetting the seasonal SAD factor more for GBP, JPY, and USD than for the EUR. In
addition, there are other motivations for altering currency holdings over time beyond hedging
FX exposure from other asset classes. The investors may have fund inﬂows or outﬂows or
portfolio rebalances in other asset classes that will necessitate altering currency hedges. At
the margin, our results seem to be picking up seasonal hedging behavior in the EUR, while
holdings in the other currencies may reﬂect the effects of other factors that hide the seasonal
SAD effect.

6 SAD portfolio simulation
Systematic investors know that forecasting exercises are not the same as implementable
investment strategies. We construct a currency portfolio to incorporate SAD effects and
simulate the strategy as a ﬁrst exploration of the investment potential of SAD for currencies.
As discussed in section “Currency Returns”., if currencies are sold in spring–summer in
order to hedge greater risky global equity positions, the currencies should depreciate over
this season. Then in fall-winter, currencies are bought to cut currency hedges as global equity
risk is reduced in the risk-off season. This leads to currency appreciation over the fall-winter
season of short days. The evidence presented showed that the currency returns did follow
this pattern, but had large standard errors so were not statistically signiﬁcant. Such results do
not preclude investment success if we build a portfolio that seeks to time trades that capture
the seasonal effects on returns.
The investment strategy is to buy currencies after the initial selling in early spring and
then close those positions in fall after the initial SAD-related buying occurs. In fall we then
open new positions selling currencies after the early fall buying and close those positions in
spring after the initial SAD-related selling occurs. The implementation is to buy a 6-month
forward contract for EUR, GBP, and JPY in fall and then close the contract with a spot
market sell in spring. In spring we then sell a 6-month forward contract on the 3 currencies
and close those positions with a spot market buy in fall. We have data on spot and 6-month
forward rates from February 2012 to September 2023. The data are WMR 4pm London prices
from Reﬁnitiv. The exact implementation recipe is to initialize the strategy on May 1, 2012,
allocating USD1 million to each of the three currencies for an equal-weighted portfolio.
Then the strategy trades on May 1 and November 1 each year to establish the spring short

Backtest simulations were run for three versions of the SAD strategy: fall and spring
trades together (the full strategy); fall trades only (the depression-onset risk-off strategy);
and spring trades only (the depression-recovery risk-on trade). Portfolio backtest results are

strategy has fairly poor performance with a Sharpe ratio risk-adjusted return of only 0.24.6
The cumulative return in Fig. 1 shows that the strategy experiences a sharp drawdown in the

occurs. To help diagnose the performance of the full strategy, we estimate a fall and spring
strategy separately. The Fall strategy only trades the November buy and May closing of
positions. As seen in Fig. 1, the cumulative performance starts negative and remains negative
for the entire backtest period. There is a substantial initial drawdown through 2015 after
which the performance oscillates between positive and negative years but the cumulative
returns never reach a positive level. The Sharpe ratio of the fall strategy is −0.30. The best
performing strategy is the Spring depression-recovery, risk-on trade of selling in May and
closing the position in November. The Sharpe ratio is 0.50 and the cumulative return in Fig. 1
shows a steady upward path after a small drawdown in 2013.

There are other motivations for trading currencies besides seasonal hedging. The generic
currency trading signals of carry, momentum, and value and variants thereof will motivate
speculative trades. In addition, episodic events like large international M&A ﬂows, ofﬁcial

and examine if they add value in addition to the seasonal strategy in a backtest. The risk-off
fall SAD strategy is likely being adversely affected by such other factors.
7 Summary and conclusions
Harry Markowitz is known as the grandfather of behavioral ﬁnance. His inﬂuence on the
ﬁnance profession is far-reaching, even in areas where he never did formal research. We are
inspired by Harry’s contributions to study an asset class that he never researched—currency
markets. Our behavioral focus is on Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) effects on currency
returns and currency holdings. As the days grow shorter in fall, investors become more risk
averse due to depression related to SAD. As they reduce their risk-taking, they cut their

In addition to currency returns, we also examine the evidence related to currency hold-
ings. Since the holdings data largely reﬂects long-only U.S. based real money investors, we
expect their currency positions will be primarily related to hedging global equity and bond
portfolios. As discussed with regard to currency returns, in the risk-on spring–summer sea-
son, they increase their equity holdings and increase their currency hedges by greater short
currency positions. In the risk-off fall-winter season they decrease their equity positions and,
consequently, cut their short currency hedging positions. We estimate models to infer the
effect of change in daylight hours and the onset and recovery from SAD-related depression
on currency holdings. Statistically signiﬁcant results are only found for the euro. Euro cur-
rency shorts are increased as days grow longer and recovery from SAD-related depression
occurs. Just the opposite occurs as days grow shorter and onset of SAD-related depression
occurs—euro currency shorts fall.

depression-recovery risk-on trade). The fall-only strategy performed poorly with negative
cumulative returns over the entire sample period. The full strategy had positive returns but
a low risk-adjusted return. The spring strategy was the best, showing positive cumulative

Overall, the evidence is mixed with regard to SAD effects in currency markets. Statistical
signiﬁcance was lacking in our analysis of SAD effects on currency returns. There was limited
statistical signiﬁcance of SAD effects on real-money investor currency holdings. The only
signiﬁcant result was with regard to SAD effects on euro holdings. The strongest evidence
of SAD effects came from the portfolio backtest of a SAD currency investment strategy.
Trading the spring SAD effect showed a moderate risk-adjusted return. There are certainly
other motivations for trading currencies than SAD that can be explored and they may help
isolate a cleaner inference regarding SAD and currency markets. This ﬁrst look at SAD
effects in currency markets raises many questions that will, hopefully, be answered in future
work.
