Abstract
The present study provides a novel methodology to quantify ESG, using deep machine learn-
ing along with earnings conference calls, to examine the extent to which sentiment patterns
around ESG anchor words convey information about future stock crash risk. We de’¼üne ESG
anchor words as terms related to the three pillars of ESG, with the sentiment surrounding these
words revealing ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ disclosure strategies and information transparency. As Bordalo et al.
(Q J Econ 135:1399ŌĆō1442, 2020) show, an anchor may relate to higher attention being paid
by an observer. In speci’¼üc, they show that when a risk surpasses a known threshold, identi’¼üed
as a risk-anchor, people seem to pay more attention. For the task in hand, we ’¼ürst compile
expanded dictionaries of ESG-related terms using word2vec and then employ FinBERT, to
extract sentiment from sentences containing these ESG anchor words. Using stock crash risk
as our primary measure of information asymmetry and bad news hoarding, our results show
that environmental sentiment operates as a credible signal that reduces crash risk, with effects
ampli’¼üed in environmentally sensitive industries and attenuated by ’¼ünancial opacity; social
sentiment exhibits context-dependent patterns, increasing crash risk in optimistic narratives
but reducing it in balanced communications, while governance sentiment shows low impact.
Our ’¼ündings likely imply that not all ESG communication reduces information asymmetry;
credibility depends critically on dimension-speci’¼üc materiality, narrative consistency and
’¼ünancial transparency.

1 Introduction
Textual analysis of business information and speci’¼ücally ’¼ünancial reports has caught signif-
icant attention among researchers and practitioners in the last decades. A large fraction of
the relevant studies in the literature examines the effect of textual information on ’¼ürm risk
using prede’¼üned word lists (dictionaries) to gauge certain sentiments (i.e. negative, uncertain
and litigious dictionaries of Loughran & McDonald, 2015). Despite the ample use of word
dictionaries in studies utilizing sentiment analysis, Loughran and McDonald (2016) under-
line that this method works at the document level and treats each word as an independent
token in the document. In turn, the location of words at the sentence level is not taken into
consideration, leading to loss of signi’¼ücant information regarding context (see also Li et al.,
2021).
The present study uses the word embedding model, which operates on the sentence level,
to uncover the context of words related to ESG risk. In speci’¼üc, the word embedding model
uses a neural network to form a vector representation of each word in the sentence. We use the
word embedding model to examine risk in ESG related information in Earnings Conference
Calls. Earnings Conference Calls take place, in most cases, after the release of quarterly
’¼ünancial results for a ’¼ürm and contain useful information on the outlook of the ’¼ürm both
from the ’¼ürmŌĆÖs and the ’¼ünancial analystsŌĆÖ (through the Q&A part) perspectives. Using an
initial number of seed word groups for each one of the three ESG pillars, we develop expanded
dictionaries for each pillar through semantic similarity using the word2vec algorithm. We
’¼ülter each dictionary to keep only words relating directly to each pillarŌĆÖs risk. Then we use
FinBERT, a Large Language Model (LLM) which has been trained to recognize positive and
negative sentiment in ’¼ünancial texts (Huang et al., 2023). For each sentence that contains
a term from our dictionaries, we estimate the sentiment (positive/negative) of the sentence.
In this respect, we estimate the average positive and negative sentiment per ESG pillar and
quarterly conference call. Last, we aggregate the quarterly conference calls at the yearly
level. As we argue, some of these words may act as risk-anchors for the users of ’¼ünancial
disclosures, with the sentiment around these anchor words revealing how ’¼ürms communicate
about material ESG risks and opportunities. To control for the general sentiment, we also
estimate the total positive and negative sentiments using the whole conference calls and
aggregate these sentiments at the yearly level.
An anchor may relate to higher attention being paid by an observer when a risk surpasses
a known threshold, and in consequence people seem to pay more attention (Bordalo et al.,
2020). In other words, the likelihood that an investor ’¼üxates on a speci’¼üc word (i.e. the word
works as an anchor) is higher if there exists a well-positioned anchor word in the text. As
Kahneman (1992) notes, judgment under uncertainty leads in most cases the one making the
judgement to rely on a mental model. This model (described as a "norm") represents a range
of possible responses or values, is not precise and covers a spread of potential outcomes and
among these values there are some common values that will be chosen. However, this judg-
ment can be in’¼éuenced by introducing a notable reference point (the anchor) and therefore,
anchor words in business texts may in’¼éuence this ’¼ünal judgment. In our framework, ESG
anchors operate through a dual channel. First, ESG terms function as anchors in the Bordalo
et al. (2020) sense because they serve as reference points that attract disproportionate investor
attention, (i.e. Ignatov, 2023). Second, the sentiment around these anchor words may reveal

’¼ürmsŌĆÖ disclosure strategies.1 In this respect, Li et al., (2024a, 2024b) show that ’¼ürms strate-
gically shape the tone of their ESG reports and explicitly interpret this based on impression
management theory. We assert that if ESG anchor words are successful in in’¼éuencing the ’¼ünal
judgement of investors, then they should also affect information content and the accumula-
tion or release of ’¼ürm-speci’¼üc information. To measure the information asymmetry effects of
ESG communication, we use stock crash risk and hypothesize that ESG sentiment patterns
that signal information withholding or reduce transparency will increase the likelihood of
future stock crashes.
Our study is motivated by previous studies on the impact of ESG communication patterns
in Earnings Conference Calls. Those studies have shown that this type of report may include
incremental information relating to the tone used by managers (i.e. Langer et al., 2023).
Sautner et al. (2023) also use Earnings Conference Calls in their novel study, to create a
climate change exposure index. Our proposed methodology differs with the study of Sautner
et al. (2023) along three dimensions. First, regarding the scope of the study Sautner et al.
(2023) focus exclusively on climate/environmental risk exposure, while our approach relates
to all three ESG pillars, which enables us to document the heterogeneous effects across the
three pillars. Second, Sautner et al. (2023) measure exposure to climate risk based on how
much climate topics are discussed, while we measure sentiment around ESG terms that are
identi’¼üed at the sentence level. Third, from a methodological point of view regarding the
textual analysis and the construction of the index, Sautner et al. (2023) use a bigram-based
approach with manual classi’¼ücation, while our study combines word2vec expansion with
FinBERT, providing a way to measure how ’¼ürms frame ESG issues using positive/negative
sentiment rather than how much they discuss them.
Our analysis yields several ’¼ündings. Firstly, we create novel measures of positive/negative
effects of ESG risks using deep machine learning methods. Secondly, we present evidence
of distinct relationships between ESG sentiment patterns and stock crash risk conditional
on the ESG pillar examined. Speci’¼ücally, we ’¼ünd that environmental sentiment operates as
a credible signal that reduces crash risk, particularly in environmentally sensitive industries
when supported by transparent ’¼ünancial reporting. On the other hand, positive social tone
increasesfuturecrashriskinconferencecallswithoverallpositivetone,butthiseffectreverses
for conference calls with negative overall tone. Governance sentiment shows minimal direct
impact on crash risk across all speci’¼ücations. Thirdly, our results show that the relation
between environmental sentiment and future stock crash risk is conditional on ’¼ünancial
opacity with ’¼ürms with higher ’¼ünancial opacity having a less negative relation between
environmental sentiment and future stock crash risk. Overall, our ’¼ündings offer insights into
how different dimensions of ESG communication affect information asymmetry and stock
market behavior, offering likely fruitful implications for corporate disclosure strategies and
ESG integration in investment processes.
The remainder of the study is organized as follows: Sect. 2 reviews the literature and
develops the research hypotheses, Sect. 3 analyzes the textual analysis methods, the word
embedding model and the NLP AI algorithm as well as the stock crash risk proxies and the
models; Sect. 4 describes the sample and analyzes the empirical results and ’¼ünally Sect. 5
concludes the study and offers implications for future research.

2 Literature review and research hypotheses development
2.1 Literature review
The relationship between ESG disclosure and ’¼ürm risk has received growing attention in
recent years. Kim et al. (2012) show that ’¼ürms with better corporate social responsibility
(CSR) performance have lower stock price crash risk, and argue that socially responsible ’¼ürms
are less likely to hoard bad news. However, these studies focus on third-party ESG ratings
rather than ’¼ürm-initiated ESG communications, which leaves an open space in the literature
on how ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ ESG narrative, as re’¼éected in corporate communications, affects information
asymmetry. Ignatov (2023) examines the market reaction of ESG related terms using 10-K
reports and shows, among other ’¼ündings that speci’¼üc ESG words relate statistically to stock
market reaction and the sign of the reaction (positive or negative) is conditional on the ESG
category of the word.
Recent research suggests that the information content of ESG disclosures may vary sys-
tematically across dimensions. Dhaliwal et al. (2011) ’¼ünd that ’¼ürms with voluntary disclosure
of stand-alone CSR reports experience a reduction in cost of equity capital, particularly if
they have strong stakeholder engagement. However, they also note substantial heterogeneity
in disclosure quality and credibility. Christensen et al. (2021) provide evidence that manda-
tory CSR disclosure regulation has limited capital market effects, when disclosure lacks
credibility or fails to provide decision-useful information. This suggests that not all ESG
communication reduces information asymmetry and credibility may be affected by factors
beyond mere disclosure existence (e.g. Berg et al., 2022).
Environmental disclosures have unique characteristics that may affect their credibility and
information content. Matsumura et al. (2014) document that carbon emissions are negatively
associated with ’¼ürm value and that markets impose additional penalties on ’¼ürms that do
not disclose emissions, consistent with a materiality-based perspective on environmental
information. This is related to evidence that greater institutional investor ownership and
engagement are associated with subsequent reductions in corporate carbon emissions (Azar
et al., 2021), suggesting in turn that environmental performance attracts monitoring by large
investors. Dragomirescu-Gaina et al. (2021) ’¼ünd that investorsŌĆÖ behavior with respect to green
asset allocations may require better information while, Lyon and Maxwell (2011) discuss
"greenwash" in corporate environmental reporting, arguing that ’¼ürms may engage in selective
disclosure to portray themselves more favorably.
These results suggest that environmental communication credibility may be conditional
on several factors like impression management. Social disclosures may be particularly vul-
nerable to the use of impression management due to their qualitative nature. Marquis and
Qian (2014) show that corporate philanthropy in China serves reputation-building purposes
and this result is stronger for ’¼ürms facing greater regulatory scrutiny. Li et al., (2024a, 2024b)
show that ’¼ürms may strategically manage the tone of ESG reports and interpret this based
on impression management theory.
The interaction between ’¼ünancial reporting quality and ESG disclosure credibility repre-
sents an emerging area of research. Dhaliwal et al. (2012) show that ’¼ürms with superior CSR
performance exhibit higher quality accruals, suggesting complementarity between social
responsibility and ’¼ünancial reporting transparency. Persakis et al. (2025) ’¼ünd that ESG com-
mitment is positively associated with improved earnings quality, while Kim et al. (2012)
argue that corporate social responsibility constrains earnings management by increasing

stakeholder monitoring and transparency. However, ’¼ünancial opacity may also reduce ESG
credibility.
The crash risk literature may provide a framework for understanding how ESG com-
munications might affect information asymmetry. Stock price crash risk captures extreme
downside movements resulting from the non-expected release of accumulated bad news (Jin
& Myers, 2006). Hutton et al. (2009) show that opaque ’¼ünancial reporting, as proxied by
absolute discretionary accruals, increases future crash risk and in turn leads to bad news
hoarding. Kim et al., (2011a, 2011b) show that corporate tax avoidance and audit quality
affect crash risk through their impact on opacity and managerial rent extraction. If ESG
disclosures re’¼éect transparency, they should limit managersŌĆÖ ability to hoard bad news and
in turn lead to lower future stock crash risk. However, if ESG communications act as a tool
of impression management, they may increase future stock crash risk due to helping in the
buildup of bad news up to the point that the ’¼ürm is no longer able to withhold it and discloses
eventually leading to the stock price collapse. DeFond et al. (2015) show that mandatory
CSR disclosure reduces stock crash risk by constraining bad news hoarding, supporting the
transparency mechanism.
The ’¼ünancial importance of ESG issues varies across industries (Khan et al., 2016). Grewal
et al. (2021) show that disclosure of material sustainability information, as de’¼üned by the
Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB), is associated with future stock price
performance, while disclosure of immaterial sustainability topics shows no relationship.
This may imply that ESG reporting affects information asymmetry in cases where speci’¼üc
ESG dimensions are material. For environmental issues, materiality is particularly important
in environmentally sensitive industries or industries with regulatory exposure. Bolton and
Kacperczyk (2021) show the existence of a carbon premium in stock returns conditional on
the ’¼ürm operating in high-emissions industries, suggesting that carbon-related information
is priced primarily where it is material.
The use of earnings conference calls for textual analysis offers distinct advantages for
capturing unscripted communication especially due to the existence of the Questions and
Answers Section of the conference calls. Matsumoto et al. (2011) ’¼ünd that the linguistic
content of conference calls provides incremental information beyond that of quantitative
disclosures. Recent advances in natural language processing enable the use of more sophis-
ticated tools to analyze ESG communications. Li et al. (2021) develop a word embedding
approach to measure corporate culture from earnings calls, showing ability to take context
into consideration. Sautner et al. (2023) use a machine learning approach to measure ’¼ürm-
level climate risk exposure from earnings calls, showing that their measure predicts ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ
climate risk and stock returns. These methodological advances support context-aware based
analysis of ESG communications.
2.2 Hypothesis development
Building on this literature, we develop hypotheses recognizing that ESG communication
effects on information asymmetry and crash risk may differ across dimensions and depend
critically on contextual factors. These effects may run in two channels. The ’¼ürst is the informa-
tion content hypothesis, where ESG sentiment re’¼éects managersŌĆÖ private information about
future ESG-related risks and opportunities. Under this hypothesis, the negative environmen-
tal sentiment may signal material risk and in turn reduces information asymmetry and crash
risk. The second is the impression management hypothesis, where ESG sentiment re’¼éects

discretionary communication choices of the ’¼ürm aimed at managing stakeholder percep-
tions (i.e. Bingler et al., 2022). Under this view, the positive sentiment may be used to mask
underlying problems, which in turn may increase information asymmetry and crash risk.
Following Kim et al. (2012) and DeFond et al. (2015), we expect that credible environmental
disclosure reduces information asymmetry and crash risk. Environmental communications
contain operational metrics that are relatively easy to verify and face regulatory scrutiny,
making them more sensitive to credibility assessments. Following Khan et al. (2016) and
Grewal et al. (2021), this effect should be ampli’¼üed where environmental issues are material.
Following Hutton et al. (2009) and the greenwashing literature (Lyon & Maxwell, 2011),
’¼ünancial opacity should undermine environmental communication credibility:
H1a: Environmental sentiment differential (positive minus negative) is negatively related
to future stock crash risk, with this relationship ampli’¼üed in environmentally sensitive indus-
tries.
H1b: The negative relationship between environmental sentiment and crash risk is atten-
uated for ’¼ürms with high ’¼ünancial reporting opacity.
Social communications may be more vulnerable to impression management (Merkl-
Davies & Brennan, 2011). However, the credibility of social disclosures may depend critically
on the general context of the business communication. In speci’¼üc, from a legitimacy theory
perspective (i.e. Deegan et al., 2002; Suchman, 1995), ’¼ürms may use social disclosures to
maintain or repair organizational legitimacy. In turn, when ’¼ürms under legitimacy threat,
may use positive social framing as a counter measure, which is a pattern that sophisticated
stakeholders may detect. In addition, from a stakeholder theory perspective (i.e. Freeman &
Phillips, 2002), social issues involve diverse stakeholder groups with competing claims, mak-
ing positive social sentiment more prone to strategic framing than environmental disclosures,
which typically address more speci’¼üc regulatory constituencies. The context-dependency we
hypothesize thus, re’¼éects that stakeholders may evaluate social disclosures not in isolation
but relative to the overall narrative information content, which is consistent with Merkl-
Davies and BrennanŌĆÖs (2011) framework of impression management in corporate narratives.
When positive social tone is embedded in a candid, balanced narrative, it signals genuine
transparency; when it occurs in an otherwise optimistic narrative, it may signal strategic
obfuscation aimed at managing stakeholder perceptions rather than conveying true informa-
tion:
H2: Social sentiment differential exhibits a context-dependent relationship with crash
risk, with effects moderated by overall corporate tone in earnings calls.
These hypotheses acknowledge that ESG communication effects are heterogeneous across
dimensions and contexts, with credibility depending on materiality, consistency, and support-
ing transparency.
3 Research methodology
Our research methodology is based on four sequential parts. The ’¼ürst is the preparation of
the text of Earnings Conference Calls for textual analysis, the second is the training of the
word2vec algorithm to extract expanded dictionaries based on seed words, the third is the
use of FinBERT to get the tone (positive or negative) of sentences in Earnings Conference
Calls including any term of the expanded dictionaries and last the fourth is the estimation of
the stock crash risk measures and the main models of the study.

3.1 Preparation of the earnings conference calls
We follow a methodology similar to that outlined by Li et al. (2021) to prepare our sample of
Earnings Conference Calls. First, we downloaded all the available Earnings Conference Calls
using the advanced search tool of the Eikon database for the period 2001ŌĆō2022. Next, we
proceed with cleaning and preparing the transcripts for analysis. In speci’¼üc, we ’¼ürst remove
all irrelevant text (i.e. names, headers footers etc.) to end up with the clean transcript text,
which contains the full transcript (both the management presentation and the QnA section), as
both sections contain ESG-relevant communication. In speci’¼üc, the management presentation
may capture information regarding the ’¼ürmsŌĆÖ ESG disclosure strategy, while the Q&A section
may capture analyst questions about ESG discussions. Next, we used Stanza, a linguistics
NLP processing tool developed by Qi et al. (2020) to break each Earnings Conference Call in
sentences and then using StanzaŌĆÖs neural pipeline we followed a similar approach to that of
Li et al. (2021) to do tokenization, multi-word token expansion (e.g. concatenate multi word
terms), lemmatization, part-of-speech and morphological feature tagging and named entity
recognition. The last two steps replace entities with a prede’¼üned token (i.e. an organizations
name will be replaced by ŌĆ£orgŌĆØ while a date will be replaced by ŌĆ£dateŌĆØ) and identify n-grams
based on the syntactic and grammatic context. This part is important for the accurate training
of the word2vec algorithm. However, following Huang et al. (2023) the FinBERT algorithm
takes as input the raw texts.
Next from the edited sentences we further removed anything irrelevant to text such as
HTML code and punctuation. Following Li et al. (2021) we use the gensim package in python
to further identify n-grams unrelated to the syntactic and grammatic rules. For each year we
aggregate the edited sentences in a single ’¼üle. Thus, we ’¼ürst pass the gensim algorithm on
the edited text to acquire the bigrams and then repass it to acquire the ’¼ünal text with trigrams.
3.2 Word2vec implementation and expanded dictionaries
Word2vec was proposed by Mikolov et al. (2013) as a tool for quantifying the textual context
in a sentence. The algorithm is based on a neural network that learns the wordsŌĆÖ representation
within a sentence. After the initialization of the model, by setting the parameters randomly
the model is trained using 20 epochs and gradually, through backpropagation the model
acquires the ability to predict a wordŌĆÖs neighboring words. We use the gensim package in
python to train the word2vec algorithm. Based on Li et al. (2021) we use 300-dimensional
word vectors, we set the maximum distance between two words to 5 and omit any word with
an appearing count in the text of lower than ’¼üve.
After the training of the word2vec model we use it along with three groups of seed words
to get the expanded dictionaries relating to the ESG pillars. We use the seed words of Briscoe-
Tran (2024) presented in Appendix AI and relating to each one of the ESG pillars. For each
one of the three groups (environmental, social and governance) we use the methodology of Li
et al (2021) to acquire the ’¼ürst 500 synonym words of the word2vec model. We then manually
exclude the terms that do not relate directly to risk of each pillar. For the task in hand, in
each ESG pillar we kept terms with semantic relevance, which were terms semantically
similar to the seed words and related to ESG risk. Second, we excluded near-duplicates or
morphological variants already captured by other terms in each expanded dictionary and
third we excluded terms from a dimension if they were more appropriately classi’¼üed under a
different ESG dimension. The expanded dictionaries, denoted as Environmental_Exp_Dict,
Social_Exp_Dict and Governance_Exp_Dict, are presented in Appendix AII.

3.3 FinBERT and tone extraction
We use the FinBERT pretrained model of Huang et al. (2023) to extract the average senti-
ment of each sentence that includes at least one of the terms of the expanded dictionaries.
FinBERT is an expansion of the BERT model (Devlin et al., 2018), which is ’¼ürst pretrained
using, corporate ’¼ülings, earnings conference calls and analystsŌĆÖ reports. Then the model is
’¼ünetuned to learn downstream NLP tasks such as sentiment analysis. Huang et al. (2023)
report an increased performance in out of sample sentiment classi’¼ücation accuracy, which
is even higher for negative sentiment. This procedure leads to six variables. The ’¼ürst three
measure the positive sentiment for the three pillars (denoted as ENVSPOSSENT, SOCPOS-
SENT and GOVSPOSSENT), while the other three measure the negative sentiment (denoted
as ENVSNEGSENT, SOCNEGSENT and GOVSNEGSENT). To control for general senti-
ment, we also estimate the positive and negative sentiment for the whole conference call.
We aggregate the sentiment indices in the yearly frequency by taking the averages of the
quarterly measures for each ’¼ürm.

4.2 Empirical analysis
Our empirical analysis starts with the estimation of the benchmark model. The model
expresses future stock crash risk (using either NCSKEW or DUVOL) as a function of the vari-

social, and governance dimensions, as well as, the overall difference between positive and
negative sentiment), along with a number of control variables. The environmental sentiment
difference between positive and negative sentiments exhibits negative and signi’¼ücant coef-
’¼ücients for both crash risk measures signi’¼ücant at the 5% and 10% levels respectively for

counterintuitive ’¼ünding may relate to the likely strategic use of social issues in the confer-

positive but statistically insigni’¼ücant coef’¼ücients for the two crash risk measures, suggesting

5 Conclusions
The present study provides a novel methodology to quantify ESG risk based on textual
analysis of earnings conference calls using deep machine learning techniques. Our approach
starts with a number of seed words to describe the three ESG pillars and the word embedding
model (word2vec) to develop expanded word lists for each ESG pillar which we denote
as anchors. These thematic word lists capture related terms as re’¼éected in the conference
calls. We then employ FinBERT (Huang et al., 2023), a large language model ’¼üne-tuned for
’¼ünancial text, to extract positive/negative sentiment from sentences containing these ESG
anchor words. Last, we examine how these measures affect future stock price crash risk, a
key indicator of information asymmetry and bad news hoarding.
Our results reveal that environmental sentiment exhibits a consistent negative relationship
with future crash risk: ’¼ürms that maintain more positive relative to negative environmen-
tal sentiment have lower future stock crash risk and this result is robust across multiple
123

Annals of Operations Research
speci’¼ücations and ampli’¼üed for ’¼ürms operating within environmentally sensitive industries.
Furthermore, we ’¼ünd that the mitigating effect of environmental sentiment on future stock
crash risk is conditional on the presence of transparent ’¼ünancial reporting practices. These
’¼ündings collectively indicate that environmental communication serves as a credible signal
that reduces information asymmetry, but only when supported by ’¼ünancial transparency.
In stark contrast, social sentiment exhibits a positive relationship with crash risk, where net
positive social sentiment is found to relate to higher future crash risk, likely suggesting that
optimistic social framing may re’¼éect impression management. The positive effect of social
sentimentonfuturestockcrashriskisreversedinearningsconferencecallswithnegativeover-
all sentiment. This pattern suggests that stakeholders may scrutinize social communications
for consistency with broader corporate reporting, interpreting net positive social sentiment
when the overall sentiment is also positive as potentially masking underlying problems. Gov-
ernance sentiment shows minimal direct impact on crash risk across all speci’¼ücations, with
consistently insigni’¼ücant coef’¼ücients in main effects and most interactions.
Our ’¼ündings may provide implications for multiple stakeholders. For corporate managers,
the results indicate that effective ESG communication requires dimension-speci’¼üc strategies
where environmental disclosures should prioritize ’¼ünancial reporting quality, while social
communications must be carefully integrated within balanced corporate narratives rather than
used as isolated positive messaging. For investors and analysts, our methodology provides
a systematic approach to extract value-relevant ESG signals from unstructured textual data,
with environmental sentiment serving as a particularly robust predictor of future tail risk.
For regulators and standard-setters, the results show that under speci’¼üc circumstances ESG
narratives may be used for impression management practices and this may lead to higher
likelihood of an adverse future stock price outcome for investors. Last, the study contributes
to the growing literature on ESG information and capital market outcomes by showing that
ESG communication effects are highly heterogeneous across dimensions and contexts.
