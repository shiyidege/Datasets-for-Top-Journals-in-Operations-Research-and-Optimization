Abstract
In multi-view machine learning problems we have multiple descriptions of the same 
objects. Multi-view clustering is about finding cluster structures from these multi-view 
descriptions. The goal is to integrate the complementary information that is found in 
these different views. In this work, we develop a graph-based approach for multi-view 
clustering. In particular, we introduce the Joint Multiple Efficient Neighbors and Graph 
(JMEG) learning method for multi-view clustering. Our objective is to find a good balance 
between sparsity and connectivity. To do so, we introduce a post-processing technique. 
Then, JMEG also employs partition space and consensus graph learning to uncover data 
structures effectively. The whole clustering process is defined in terms of an optimization 
problem, which is solved using an iterative alternating algorithm. We illustrate the useful­
ness of our approach with experiments that outperform state-of-the-art methods. We also 
discuss in this paper the computational cost of JMEG.

1  Introduction
The amount of data currently available is increasing. This has fostered the development 
of machine learning methods. As data increase, the amount of data referring to the same 
objects also increase. For example, the same document is expressed in different languages, 
the same object is photographed from different angles, an the same person can be identified 
through photographs, signatures, and fingerprints.
Multi-view data refer to multiple-descriptions of the same objects. Multi-view clustering 
(Li et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021), refers to clustering when we have these multi-view data. 
Most multi-view clustering algorithms use graph-based models (Wang et al., 2019; Sadjadi 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
et al., 2024, 2025). That is, they build a graph representing the information existing for a 
given view, and then create either a fused graph or a low-dimensional spectral embedding. 
The goal of this process is to summarize the most relevant information in the different views 
in a single structure. Once this single structure is built, we can apply a clustering algorithm 
to detect the clusters. Clustering on the summarized graph can be e.g. k-means and spectral 
clustering.
Multi-view graph learning (Pan & Kang, 2021; Kang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019a, 
b; Zhan et al., 2018) mostly uses graph-based models. In these models, data is represented 
as a graph or network. Each node represents an entity and each edge represents a relation­
ship between two entities. In multi-view clustering, we can have multiple graphs, one for 
each view. Naturally, a fundamental aspect is the construction of the graph, and, from them, 
the construction of the aggregated graph. This graph, when weighted, is called a similarity 
matrix and also affinity matrix. Different methods to build these matrices exist. They differ 
on e.g. the evaluation of similarity. Sparsity and connectivity are key properties of a graph 
that affect any learning process. Ideally, we want nodes in a cluster to be well connected, 
while nodes in different clusters we want them not to be connected at all (i.e., implying 
sparsity in the matrix). Different graph construction procedures lead to graphs with differ­
ent sparsity levels. Then, it is of relevance to find a good trade-off between sparsity and 
connectivity.
Because of this, the challenges of multi-view graph learning are: 
1. The construction of a graph for each view so that the similarity matrix has a good trade-
off between connectivity and sparsity,
2. The effective combination of the information from different views to achieve a single 
clustering structure.
In this paper we deal with these problems by means of 
(i) Obtaining neighbourhoods for each data based on second-order connections,
(ii) Defining similarity matrices based on efficient neighbourhoods in each view,
(iii) Combining all similarity matrices of the multi-view data.
The process is defined as an optimization problem, and then the problem is solved using 
an alternating optimization problem. Our approach is similar to Li et al. (2021), with the 
difference that we compute explicitly the aggregated similarity matrix, and then we build 
segmentation matrix directly from the aggregated similarity matrix. This common segmen­
tation matrix is close to the segmentation matrix of each view. Also, the way to build the 
similarity matrix is different because we exploit the neighbors of the nodes.
In the paper we conduct extensive experiments on six real-world data sets to validate the 
effectiveness of our idea.
This work is a revised and extended version of a previous paper (Sadjadi & Torra, 2023) 
we presented at IUKM 2023. In particular, we have expanded Sects. 2 and 3, and include in 
the present work the proofs related to our main proposition in Sect. 3.
The paper starts in the next section with the preliminaries and principal concepts. In 
Sect. 3, we explain the proposed method based on an optimization problem. We also provide

2.3  Graph-based multi-view clustering
The topic of graph-based multi-view clustering has gained popularity due to its efficiency 
in capturing the complex structures and relationships present in multimedia data. However, 
existing methods have limitations. Firstly, they may be inefficient or fail when dealing with 
large-scale graph learning due to the involved graph construction and eigendecomposition. 
Secondly, they struggle to effectively utilize complementary information and spatial struc­
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
tures inherent in the graphs of different views. To overcome these challenges and maximize 
the potential of complementary information, as well as to address scalability problems in 
graph-based multi-view clustering, we propose the following approach.
MLAN (Nie et al., 2017) is a prominent method for multi-view clustering based on graph 
learning. It employs manifold learning to adaptively learn a commonly shared graph for all 
views, presenting multi-view learning with adaptive neighbors. Despite effectively explor­
ing local intrinsic characteristics of data, a major drawback MLAN and other graph-based 
methods is their inefficiency or failure when dealing with large-scale problems. This is due 
to the computational intensity associated with constructing a full-size graph and eigende­
composition of the Laplacian matrix. The computational complexity of MLAN is O(n2K) 
for graph construction and O(n3) for eigendecomposition of the Laplacian matrix, where 
n and K represent the number of data points and clusters, respectively. In many real-world 
applications, n can be very large, making computational challenges a critical concern.

2.4  Multi-view spectral clustering
Multi-view spectral clustering is a popular method for clustering multi-view data, where 
each view represents a different aspect of the same underlying data. The approach aims to 
integrate information from multiple views to improve clustering performance.
The method is based on spectral clustering, a popular approach for clustering data using 
graph theory. In spectral clustering, a similarity graph (W) is constructed from the data, 
and the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian matrix are used to embed the data in a low-
dimensional space where clustering is performed.
In the case of multi-view spectral clustering, a similarity graph is constructed for each 
view (W ν), and the eigenvectors of each view’s Laplacian matrix are used to embed the 
data from that view in a low-dimensional space. Then, the embeddings from all views are 
concatenated into a single representation, which is used for clustering. Multi-view spectral

2.5  Sparsity and connectivity
The sparsity and connectivity of the similarity matrix are crucial factors in spectral cluster­
ing, as they greatly affect the performance of the clustering algorithm. Ideally, the nodes 
in a cluster should be well connected while the others not. There are numerous methods 
proposed in the literature to enhance the connectivity of clusters, such as utilizing different 
norms and regularization terms in subspace problems. In our proposed algorithm, we adopt 
the γ-efficient neighborhood approach defined in Definition 2 (which choose among the 
k-nearest neighborhood in its definition). Then, for a given k, we would obtain the value γ so 
that the solutions are γ-efficient for all pairs of neighbors xi and xj. The selection of k plays 
a vital role in controlling the sparsity and connectivity of the similarity graph.
To assess the connectivity of a similarity graph W, we utilize the second smallest eigen­
value of the normalized graph Laplacian, which is a widely used and effective method for 
calculating algebraic connectivity. More particularly, we compute the restriction of this 
matrix W for each of the clusters. So, we have W 1 that is the similarity matrix when we 
only considers records in X that have been assigned to the first cluster, W 2 the similarity 
matrix for only those records in the second cluster, and so on until W V . Then, we compute 
the second smallest eigenvalue for each of the K subgraphs Xi. This permits to compare 
the connectivities across all clusters. Then, we can evaluate the overall connectivity of our 
method by computing the average of all clusters’ connectivities. This is the “connectivity” 
metric.

3  Proposed method
In this section we introduce our novel learning method for multi-view clustering. We call it 
joint multiple efficient neighbors and graph (JMEG) learning method for multi-view clus­
tering. JMEG comprises two key components once the similarity matrices W ν are built 
using Eq. (1) from Xν. (1) Apply a post-processing technique to optimize sparsity and 
connectivity by means of identifying efficient neighbors. This process results into matrices 
Aν from W ν. (2) Apply unified partition space learning F and consensus graph learning ¯S. 
Component (1) is to establish critical connections among samples within a cluster so that 
samples have both large affinity coefficients and strong connections to each other. These two 
key components are combined together in a single optimization problem as we will see later 
in this section. In Sect. 3.1, we describe what it means to compute Aν, but the actual process 
of computing Aν is given in Sect. 3.2 where it is combined with the computation of ¯S.
The rationale is that the partition is more robust to noise, and graph learning helps 
uncover data structures. Specifically, JMEG iteratively constructs local graph matrices 
(i.e., the matrices F 1, . . . , F V associated to each view), generates partition matrices, and 
stretches them to produce a unified partition matrix F. This is employed to learn a consensus 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
graph matrix ¯S. For the sake of efficiency, JMEG adaptively allocates a large weight to the 
stretched base partition that is close to the unified partition, determines the parameters, and 
imposes a low-rank constraint on graphs. Finally, clusters can be obtained directly from the 
consensus graph.
In the experiment section we show that a small number efficient neighbors can effec­
tively recover the clusters, and that the proposed post-processing step of identifying efficient 
neighbors complements most existing multi-view clustering algorithms. We do experiments 
on five benchmark datasets to demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms state-
of-the-art methods with negligible additional computation cost.

3.1  Finding k-nearest neighborhood and γ-efficient neighborhood
Utilizing the symmetric nonnegative weight matrix W ν, as obtained from Eq. (1), we derive 
a set of new similarity matrices {A1, A2, . . . , AV }. To obtain the new similarity matrix for 
each view, we initially calculate the k-nearest neighbors for each dataset and subsequently 
select the γ-efficient neighborhood from them.
In order to ensure sparsity, the pruning process of selecting samples with top k coefficients 
in wij for xi is commonly used. This is because larger wij values indicate stronger similar­
ity between xi and xj. However, this approach does not take into account the connectivity 
property, which may result in intra-cluster samples not forming a connected component 
in the affinity graph. The limitation of simply considering the maximum k edges for each 
sample is that it may fail to handle noise-corrupted data, as wrong connections are preserved 
due to the sensitivity of max edges to noise, outliers, and samples near the intersection of 
two clusters. Therefore, in order to ensure both sparsity and connectivity properties in graph 
multi-view learning, we propose a method in this paper that defines "efficient neighbors" for 
each sample in each view to establish crucial latent connections within a graph.
In this paper, we propose a definition of a γ-efficient neighborhood for every data in 
each view, which is a neighbor that has at least µ common neighbors within the local 
neighborhood.
Instead of simply preserving the k largest entries of wi, we preserve γ-efficient neighbors 
from the k-neighbors, where k > γ, for each sample. By doing so, the connectivity within 
each subspace is enhanced, as detailed in Sect. 4.

3.4  Convergence analysis
The function presented in Eq. (10) is not jointly convex and obtaining a globally optimal 
solution for it is a challenging task. However, by using an alternating strategy, the same as 
Proposition 1 we can transform Eq. (10) into several sub-problems, each of which is convex. 
This allows us to find the optimal solution for each sub-problem, and their convergence can 
be demonstrated as follows.
 
●
To update A1, . . . , Aν, we use Eq. (21), which is a convex function since its second-
order derivative with respect to aν
ij is 2α + 2 > 0 where α > 0. As a result, it will 
monotonically decreasing with each iteration.
 
●
To update A, we use Eq. (24), which is a convex function since its second-order de­
rivative with respect to aij is 1. As a result, it will monotonically decrease with each 
iteration.
 
●
To update F 1, . . . , F ν, we use the SVD of M in Eq. (26) to obtain each F ν. We want to 
show that the algorithm used to optimize Eq. 26 (which is typically an iterative method 
that updates F ν to minimize the objective function) converges to a solution. It is enough 
to see that the objective function in Eq. 26 is a quadratic form that can be written as 
F νT MF ν, where M is a symmetric positive semi-definite matrix. Then, naturally, the 
problem is convex, and the algorithm will converge to a solution.
 
●
In Eq. (28), we obtain F through the SVD of P (the same as the last item).
 
●
To update ω1, . . . , ων, we use Eq. (35), which is a linear function with a closed-form 
solution presented in Eq. (36). So, it is convex and convergence is ensured.
 
●
To update Γ1, . . . , Γν, we use Eq. (37), which is a linear function with a closed-form 
solution presented in Eq. (38). So, it is also convex and convergence is ensured.
 
●
In Eq. (32), we update ¯S, which is also a convex function, as its second-order derivative 
with respect to ¯sij equals β where β > 0. Hence, it will decrease monotonically with 
each iteration.
 
●
Finally, we update Q in Eq. (33), which can be obtained using the SVD of L ¯S.

5  Conclusion and future work
This research paper introduces a novel approach, JMEG, for multi-view graph learning 
that aims to address the poor connectivity problem in sparse multi-view clustering. JMEG 
utilizes a pre-processing technique that generates multiple similarity matrices by consider­
ing γ-efficient neighbors for each data point. Also, these matrices are then combined with 
segmentation matrices to create a higher-quality graph structure. Our experimental results 
on real-world data demonstrate the effectiveness of JMEG in improving clustering perfor­
mance. Future research can explore the potential of combining JMEG with other multi-view 
methods to enhance clustering quality, as well as investigate its application in incomplete 
multi-view clustering. JMEG provides a promising approach to addressing the poor con­
nectivity issue in multi-view clustering.
