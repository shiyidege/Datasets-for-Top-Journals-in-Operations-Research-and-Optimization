Abstract
We consider an important class of portfolio management problems in public and private enti-
ties: research and development (R&D) portfolios in which the projects’ potential monetary
value is not a ﬁrst-order factor in selection, and the investments are staged. We present a
robust real options approach that extends existing methodology to evaluate a portfolio of
proposals in each stage of a staggered selection process in order to obtain an optimal funding
strategy for project selection. We apply this methodology to an analysis of the Small Business
Innovation Research program of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration. We use
the aerospace standard Technology Readiness Level as a measure of factors of interest. We
demonstrate techniques that allow us to include non-economic strategic considerations and
estimate the impact of ﬂexibility on the portfolio construction. Moreover, we present one of
the few scholarly models underpinned by data drawn directly from the program of interest.
Finally, our model suggests that a public innovation program can support a dramatically
higher number of small ﬁrms without signiﬁcantly impacting the total portfolio value. This
work has important implications for management of R&D portfolios in public and private
contexts.

1 Introduction
Optimization of research and development (R&D) programs and project selection are port-
folio management problems critical to the future of many enterprises. Yet, the time horizons
from initial investment to revenue or ﬁnancial return tend to be long and uncertain. In addition,
projects may have beneﬁts other than their ﬁnancial value—for instance, in portfolios spon-
sored by public agencies, nonproﬁt entities, or other organizations with explicit missions
beyond ﬁnancial returns. Importantly, R&D portfolios often need to satisfy many criteria
simultaneously (Bitman & Sharif, 2008; Pereira & Veloso, 2009). In the corporate world,
strategic or political considerations may contribute to decisions in research programs.
Oneoptimizationapproachthatlendsitselfnaturallytothisproblemisrealoptionsanalysis
(Pennings & Lint, 1997), an extension of option pricing techniques developed in the ﬁnancial
industry (Myers, 1984) and especially useful for investments staggered over time. Financial
options give their owner the right to exercise an irreversible decision to buy an underlying
security with stochastic value. The essence of R&D considerations is to decide whether to
invest (irreversibly) in a project with stochastic value - there is an additional element of
uncertainty with respect to ﬁnancial options, as the value of the project under consideration
is, in general, estimated. Because of this strong similarity, the methodologies used to price
ﬁnancial options—which require the determination of the optimal point of exercise—can be
directly applied to R&D projects. In a real options frame, when future investments do not
require a ﬁrm commitment after the initial cash outﬂow, the investor can decide later whether
to execute the next investment, postpone it, or cancel the project altogether (Trigeorgis &
Tsekrekos, 2018). Many R&D projects ﬁt this proﬁle, as they call for an initial investment
and, depending on the results of the ﬁrst stage or changes in the environment, the investor
can choose to further fund or alternatively cancel the project.
The real options methodology can be used for the evaluation of general R&D projects
(Huchzermeier & Loch, 2001) or of government research (Eckhause et al., 2009). The sep-
aration of project valuation and the decision-making process enables the use of real options
analysis for a set of projects with heterogeneous risk proﬁles, such as R&D portfolios (Zapata
& Reklaitis, 2010).
Independently of the optimization methodology used, to better assess the quality of the
projects in the portfolio, organizations (ﬁrms or public agencies) sometimes use a structured
multi-stage program, wherein cohorts are formed and all projects are interrogated simul-
taneously prior to decisions of investment in or abandonment of projects. After the initial
evaluation stage, the organization receives information and decides whether to further support
the projects at staggered time intervals.
In this paper we apply real options methodology to a staggered R&D portfolio. However,
as with ﬁnancial options, the real options approach provides a general framework that requires
a particular implementation to address the characteristics of the R&D selection problem con-
sidered. This effort often requires non-trivial mathematical and/or computational extension
of the fundamental real options methodology.

The problem we consider in this paper involves a two-stage selection process (that we
describe later) and this adds a layer of complexity to the standard R&D evaluation case. To
address this speciﬁcation, we formulate and solve a tractable mixed-integer program whose
solution approximates the optimal two-stage selection problem. Speciﬁcally, we solve for an
optimal ﬁrst-stage funding strategy based on the expected second-stage outcomes. We then
extend this model to obtain robust funding strategies with respect to the different components
of the objective function. This is an innovation with respect to the existing literature.
In our problem, monetary factors are not a ﬁrst-order consideration. In fact, the main
beneﬁt of our solution—built on the real options framework—is that it allows for different
types of goals (for example, we describe a base case to maximize the technology improvement
of a project using standard metrics that we explain in detail below). We provide examples in
Sect. 3.
Our real options model exploits the ﬂexibility provided by the allocation of funds at the
second stage, after the ﬁrst stage is complete; moreover, with historical data to estimate state
transition probabilities for each project, our model describes uncertainty at each stage in the
project outcomes. The estimated portfolio value depends on the strategic objectives of the
program administrators and the relative weights they assign to these objectives. The impor-
tance of such considerations has been recognized in the management of public portfolios
(Subramanian et al., 2022; Bozeman et al., 2015) but they likely appear in any portfolio
developed in a complex organization.
The portfolio of projects we considered is funded by the Small Business Innovation
Research (SBIR) program, which is central to government support of technological devel-
opment as well as the creation of new companies -a main source of employment growth. In
this program, eleven different federal agencies are required to allocate a ﬁxed percentage of
their research budgets to ﬁrms with fewer than 500 employees. The grants are awarded as
a result of a competitive application process and are allocated in two stages (Phases1). The
ﬁrst Phase is typically six months long, and the second stage has a duration of roughly two
years with an award size four to six times larger. The maximum sizes of these awards are
determined at the agency level, subject to legislation. In 2020, the annual budget of all SBIR
programs totaled about 3 billion dollars. There is an extensive literature devoted to the SBIR
program that has explored outcomes such as its impact on the economy (Lerner, 1999) or its
effects on innovation (Howell, 2017; Giga et al., 2021).
We introduce an application of the real options methodology to derive the optimal portfolio
selection—namely, to determine the mix of projects that maximizes the strategic outcomes.
We explore which projects should be selected in a staggered, two-phase funding scheme. In
our benchmark case, the goal of the selection is to maximize the increase in the technological
level of the projects (that we measure as explained later). Our model is based on speciﬁc
project characteristics. A potential problem is that many factors may be under consideration,
which complicates the standard real options technique by increasing the dimensionality of
the problem such that it becomes non-tractable analytically. Here we propose a numerical
approach that enables an approximate and efﬁcient solution of the problem. Speciﬁcally, we
formulate a real option model as a mixed-integer program (MIP) wherein an optimal funding
strategy in the second phase depends on the outcomes of the ﬁrst stage. In this way, we link
the simulated projects directly to the structure of the SBIR program in that we effectively
construct a synthetic portfolio. This formulation generates a portfolio that maximizes the
expected value of the selected projects by categorizing project attributes based on stakeholder

of the state space, instead of the exact formulation we solve a smaller, alternative MIP, based

In order to validate our methodology, we use data of the SBIR program of the National
AeronauticsandSpaceAdministration(NASA).Acentralfeatureoftheproposalsistheappli-
cant’s estimates of the current and expected Technology Readiness Level (TRL) (Mankins,
2009). This metric is used extensively in the defense and aerospace industries to represent
a technology’s maturity on an ordinal scale from 1 to 9, wherein TRL 1 describes initial
basic research, and TRL 9 indicates a successfully deployed technology. At each Phase,
each proposal includes estimates of the initial TRL, and program administrators assess the
achieved TRL, as well as the technology’s potential value to the agency. We use TRL mea-
sures (initial and ﬁnal) as characteristics of interest to NASA SBIR administrators because
TRL performance must be demonstrated for further use by NASA (NASA, 2007).
We apply the approach describe before to translate a technology’s evolution into ﬁnancial
value. To date there is a scarcity of parameterized models of the impact of improved infor-
mation on the portfolio’s aggregate value. Furthermore, in order to illustrate the ﬂexibility of
the methodology proposed in this paper, we explore the impact of weighting the award dis-
tribution across different ﬁrm sizes. Headcount has previously been shown to impact project
selection in the NASA SBIR program (Belz et al., 2019). In particular, we consider ﬁrms of
1-10 employees, that we call “microﬁrms”, and 11-499 employees, termed “standard small
businesses” (SSB). As an exercise, we calibrate the parameter values of our objective function
to replicate the observed proﬁle of NASA SBIR awards. Other factors that could be easily
incorporated in the selection process are, for example, geographic or demographic diversity.
Overall, our formulation and its solution heuristic provide a method for SBIR administra-
tors to use in portfolio assembly or as a benchmark for the analysis of deviations from the ex
ante optimal estimate. In general, this methodology is broadly applicable to R&D programs
with many decision factors.
The paper is structured as follows: First, we review the literature on R&D portfolios and
real options. We discuss management of public research portfolios and the SBIR program, as
well as the role the notion of TRL plays in project management. Next, we discuss our model
architecture, parameterization, and approximations. We then report and discuss results for
our primary formulation under three distinct cases, as well as for a robustness extension.
Finally, we summarize our results and suggest next steps for the ﬁeld.
2 Literature review
2.1 Management of R&D portfolios
Management of R&D portfolios is a central problem in strategy: In addition to long time
horizons, the technical feasibility serves as a major source of uncertainty and is typically
represented by a binary outcome. Because the nature of the technology risks is similar in
the public and private sector, portfolio management displays many common characteristics
across these realms.
123

Annals of Operations Research
Ultimately, technologies are developed with the hope of infusion of a new solution, a
challenge because the process of selecting between the heritage technology and the unproven
candidate with potentially superior performance is a key dilemma (Krishnan & Bhattacharya,
2002; Chronopoulos & Siddiqui, 2015). Performance is not the only risk in the technology
translation process; the development schedule may slip, requirements can change, or the
technology value can change (Huchzermeier & Loch, 2001). These technical and market
volatilitiesactdifferentlyandpossiblyeveninoppositedirections(Perlitzetal.,1999)because
technology development is more likely to slip than to accelerate, whereas there is a greater
chance that market forces may act in favor of the solution under development. Differences
in the nature of these risks complicate portfolio construction and impact the valuation of a
ﬁrm’s R&D efforts in different ways (Oriani & Sobrero, 2008).
This portfolio management challenge can be addressed through various avenues. One is
to consider a diversity of projects, as breadth improves portfolio performance (Klingebiel
& Rammer, 2014). In addition, in the defense sector, risk is mitigated by having many
differentgroupscarryouttheprojects(Mowery,2012).Analternativeapproachistominimize
underperformance risk (Hall et al., 2015) or optimize priority instead of uncertainty; these
pathways may lead to different portfolios (Koç & Morton, 2015). In a project portfolio that
incorporates strategic considerations, values for qualitative, non-economic outcomes can be
transformed into quantitative values and estimated in a procedure equivalent to weighting
(Dutra, Ribeiro & De Carvalho, 2014).
2.2 Real options
Real options approaches are particularly useful to make the most of ﬂexibility, which can be
deﬁned as the opportunity to reverse a decision, delay it, or organize it into several smaller
stages when information about future events may inﬂuence the value of the opportunity (Tri-
georgis & Tsekrekos, 2018). This approach, developed in the ﬁnancial industry, is a useful
strategic tool when the factors that affect project value are captured correctly (Myers, 1984).
A logical avenue to achieve ﬂexibility is to stagger the investment commitments, while infor-
mation is collected. Staged investments, along with the discontinuation of underperforming
projects, generally enhance performance (Klingebiel & Rammer, 2014; Klingebiel & Adner,
2015). Indeed, it may be that an important value of the multi-stage process is to identify those
to be de-selected. This approach is the essence of real options.
Because it represents a natural way to manage risk, real options analysis has become
a central strategic tool (Trigeorgis & Tsekrekos, 2018). This method relies heavily on the
acquisition of information (Huchzermeier & Loch, 2001). Real options methodology may
overestimate success and undervalue the importance of the optionality in staged processes
(Bistline, 2016). Systems that capture additional information to incorporate ﬂexibility and
manage risk show higher project value (Wang & Yang, 2012).
The long horizons of R&D portfolios cause a different behavior pattern than in projects
with immediate cash ﬂows (Alexander & Chen, 2019). Research projects have a range of
outcomes due to technical risk—indeed, some solutions may not work at all, while others
may outperform. This outcome distribution is the central source of uncertainty that motivates
the use of real options. As a result, this approach has attracted interest in studies of research
portfolios in energy technologies (Kurth et al., 2017; Muñoz et al., 2011; Davis & Owens,
2003) and the pharmaceutical industry (Brandão et al., 2018; Perlitz et al., 1999; Hartmann &
Hassan, 2006). Some scholars have addressed uncertainty with fuzzy set approaches (Wang &
Hwang, 2007; Carlsson et al., 2007). Project selection can be framed as a binomial decision

tree, which is a useful way to model the evolution of the project’s value as options are
recognized and exercised (Brandão & Dyer, 2005).
2.3 Public portfolios and the SBIR program
Like ﬁrms, a public agency faces decisions between a proven technology or a candidate with
both potentially superior performance and uncertain viability (Krishnan & Bhattacharya,
2002). The exploration of entirely novel approaches is attractive because the agency may be
subject to traps that favor mature, familiar, or adjacent solutions (Ahuja & Lampert, 2001).
Moreover, the program administrator seeks to manage failure modes unrelated to the science
or engineering phenomena, or performer risk (we follow the terminology of a performer as
the entity named in the contract to conduct research (Lepori et al., 2023)). A public agency
is likely to factor in many considerations in selection processes, such as the technology and
business applications (Pereira & Veloso, 2009; Bozeman et al., 2015). Public organizations
can experience multiple needs that impact portfolio management (Pereira & Veloso, 2009;
Bozeman et al., 2015; Carayannis, Grigoroudis & Wurth, 2022), and these needs may be
contradictory and require trade-offs (Van Gestel et al., 2008).
In addition, public portfolio management presents other challenges. First, budgets typi-
cally must be exhausted in their entirety in a so-called “use it or lose it” structure. Moreover,
the bureaucracy’s operation can, in principle, create agency and incentive concerns (Khalil
et al., 2013). Finally, the program’s strategy must be implemented by an administrator, who
may, in principle, create “policy drift” in which the budget is not fully used to align with
the program’s strategy if the administrator hides or repurposes funds (Liebman & Mahoney,
2017).
And yet public investment in nascent technology research and development is critical.
Early-stage R&D portfolios are characterized by high skew, in which few projects contribute
disproportionately to the value of the entire portfolio. This creates a potential market failure
that can be addressed through subsidies (Wallsten, 2000; Feldman & Kelley, 2006; Belz
& Giga, 2017), such as the SBIR program. SBIR awards have been associated with many
positive outcomes [e.g., Lerner (1999), Toole and Turvey (2009), Howell (2017), Giga et al.
(2021)].
The SBIR program is required to be structured in a minimum of two stages or Phases
throughout the federal government, with maximum amounts determined annually by the
Small Business Administration (SBA, 2020). In Phase 1, the awardee conducts theoretical
or feasibility research underpinning the proposed effort, whereas Phase 2 is expected to fund
prototypes. As Phase 2 eligibility is restricted to Phase 1 awardees, a two-stage investment
process well-suited to real options analysis results, as noted previously (Belz & Giga, 2017).
2.4 Technology readiness levels
Clearly R&D portfolio models should incorporate a risk measure. Marcondes and collab-
orators proposed a Markowitz risk-return analysis to inform a project portfolio (Barucke
Marcondes et al., 2019), and Pereira and Veloso implement a related model for the R&D
portfolio of a space agency (Pereira & Veloso, 2009). At NASA, the SBIR program utilizes a
management tool deployed broadly throughout the aerospace and defense sectors, the Tech-
nology Readiness Level (TRL) scale (Mankins, 2009; Héder, 2017). TRL is an ordinal scale
ranging from 1 (idea) to 9 (ﬂown successfully) (Table 1) for technology-agnostic insights.

While imperfect, TRL is an important metric to track project progress and risk. NASA
requires that technologies achieve at least a level of TRL 6 for infusion into ﬂight projects
(NASA, 2007), in part to manage schedule slippage and costs associated with lower TRL
(Dubos et al., 2008). At the Department of Defense and NASA, development costs increase
non-linearly with TRL (Hay et al., 2013; Terrile et al., 2015), and demonstration of technology
performance becomes progressively more difﬁcult and may limit further investment (Terrile
& Jackson, 2013). TRL serves as a key measure of risk in several industries; for instance,
it has been used for real options modeling of a federally sponsored R&D portfolio in the
energy sector (Kurth et al., 2017), and it is important in technology transfer (Subramanian
et al., 2022). This is consistent with private sector evaluation of early-stage technologies
in that venture capitalists show a preference for ﬁrms with strong technologies even if the
management is weaker (Baum & Silverman, 2004).
The importance of the ﬁnal TRL leads naturally to its use as a selection criterion (Katz
et al., 2015). In addition, the total change in TRL may also drive selection decisions; in one
model, Hay et al. only consider projects with a transition of at least two TRL levels (Hay
et al., 2013). Therefore, in addition to the ﬁnal TRL, we consider the number of TRL levels
traversed by the project, known as the TRL Improvement Level (Alexander, 2018; Ma, 2021).
3 Model setup
3.1 Overview of model and methodology
In this section, we formulate a model that mimics the SBIR process - described in the
introduction—through optimization of the decisions made in a two-stage system, wherein
a set of candidate projects is funded from a set of proposals and—based on the observed
outcomes from a ﬁrst stage—a subset of those projects is selected for additional funds in
the second stage and is thus an application of a real options methodology. The goal in our
benchmark case is to maximize the increase in the technology maturity of the projects, as
measured by their TRL—the notion of which is described in Section 2. Each stage is subject to
a budgetconstraint. This modelstructure accurately represents the portfolio selection problem
faced by the SBIR program managers. The funding level for each project is identical across
all projects in the same stage. While the exact solution to the proposed real option model is
computationally intractable because of the number of projects, we propose an approximate
model whose solution yields reasonable ﬁnancial allocation decisions. An additional aspect
123

Annals of Operations Research
of our model’s value is that phenomenologically derived technology progression values serve
as realistic input speciﬁcations —that is, the parameters are derived from proﬁles observed
in historical data. The use of actual data allows us to design the model to exploit observed
portfolio characteristics. Similar to Zhang & Li (2017), we solve a tractable version of a real
options problem through the simpliﬁcation of the Phase 2 outcomes.
In the next subsection, we describe the characteristics of the SBIR process, its features,
and the model’s parameters: budgetary considerations, technical considerations (TRL pro-
gression, technical diversiﬁcation, etc.), and performer considerations. We then provide a
methodological overview of our solution approach: an exact integer programming formula-
tion that solves for an optimal strategy with and without budget ﬂexibility, its computational
limitations, a computationally tractable mixed-integer program that approximates the opti-
mal solution, and ﬁnally, the extension of the approximate model to provide robust solutions
to the estimates of the observed parameters.
3.2 Model architecture and observed parameters
Considerapublicagencytaskedwiththeallocationofabudgetacrossaspectrumofprojectsin
a two-phase competitive structure. The program administrator is interested in both short (1–2
year) and long (multiple years) horizons, but the goals over these horizons can ﬂuctuate. For
instance, some technologies may serve as platforms or offer cross-cutting use, and therefore
they are of interest for broader applications. Another example in the public context is that the
technology may also offer “outside commercial beneﬁt”, i.e., an opportunity for success in the
private market. In our model, we capture budgetary, technical, and performer considerations.
3.2.1 Budgetary considerations
We assume that the administrator must “use it or lose it”, and that there is no policy drift
(Liebman & Mahoney, 2017). We can now consider whether the allocations between the two
Phases are ﬁxed or may vary. Funding more ﬁrms in Phase 1 increases the set of grantees,
which may have a political implication.
Our data represent proposals submitted between 2009 and 2015. In the ﬁrst two years of
our study, the maximum Phase 1 award was set at $100,0002 over the course of six months,
increased to $125,000 starting in 2011. At the same time, NASA offered up to $600,000 over
the course of 24 months for Phase 2, increased to $750,000 starting in 2011. In Table 2 we
show the number of proposals funded at each of the two Phases, stratiﬁed by ﬁrm size. We
assume an identical budget for each award and estimate that the aggregate fraction of the
budget allocated to Phase 1 is approximately 32%. This value is therefore used to inform the
baseline model.

technology that has not advanced. These observations point to the risk inherent in technology
development, and that at times investment does not lead to an advancement.

same distribution of advancement probabilities as the historical portfolio, it is sufﬁcient to
inform how transition probabilities can be used to forecast future portfolio performance.

for the ith project we deﬁne this parameter as αi( fi −s′
i) and evaluate it as the difference in
TRL from the ﬁnal state, fi, to the initial state, s′
i (Table 4). Our staggered scheme reﬂects
the fact that a project with TIL  2 is generated at a steep acceleration to two TIL  1
steps, and that this beneﬁt increases at TIL  3 (Alexander, 2018). Therefore, achievement
of the increased TIL should beneﬁt the project by the same order of magnitude. To estimate
the appropriate weights, we need a proxy for the increase (decrease) in cost for a delayed
(accelerated) technology development. We use the timescale of a typical development cycle
as such a proxy. Each increment in TRL requires roughly a year (Kenley, Subramanian, and
Adams, 2023; Peisen & Schulz, 1999), and therefore an advancement of two TRL increments
in a two-year project is reasonable. This is reﬂected in our normalization of the weighting
function to 1.0 at TIL  2. For the extreme case of TIL  4, a reduction of two years for a
nominally four-year project would reduce the cost by half, and thus a premium of 50% in the
objective function if TIL  4 is appropriate.
(C) Technology diversiﬁcation. We implement a “cream of the crop” strategy in which we
capture the quality distribution of the projects through a simple weighting scheme, similar
to adjusting a geographic distribution of a public portfolio (Mavrotas et al., 2015). In our
case, a transition matrix that is quality dependent would take this into account; for instance,
a high-quality project might have a transition probability of 20% to advance from 3 to 4, but
one of lower quality has a 10% probability.
Because we only observe transition matrices for the entire project population ex post, we
do not have insight into these quality-dependent probabilities. As the average success rate is
approximately 15%, we divided each TRL subset into four unequally sized bins to give higher
sensitivity in the top three deciles, with observations in the fourth and ﬁnal decile (31–100%
in quality) assigned a weight of 0. In the top decile, each project was weighted with 1.0 of
its value, with absolute discounts of 10% applied for the second decile and a further 5%
discount for the third decile. The values for these weights are appropriate because in reality,
projects are scored on a scale of 1–100 (Giga et al., 2021). We therefore estimate that projects
a project score of 90% is worth ten percent less than one that scores 100%. Projects below
the third decile were discounted by 50%. In this fashion, we created an incentive to fund
excellent projects in all TRL bins. We denote this value with the diversiﬁcation parameter γi
for the ith project.
3.2.3 Performer considerations
The real options approach allows us to explore the question of the technical impact of the
interests of other stakeholders. As an example, we consider a political decision: If we seek to
fund higher numbers of microﬁrms, how does that affect the overall portfolio? To address this,
we segment performers along the dimension of headcount. We create two bins for microﬁrms
and standard small businesses (SSB), stratifying the data into headcounts of 1-10 (11-499)
employees. As with the quality distribution, we assign a higher value to microﬁrms. We assign
a headcount parameter, ϵi, for the ith project to differentiate the value between microﬁrms
and standard small businesses’ technological progression.

3.3 Methodology
There are several ways to model our system as a real options problem. Often, a real option
problem is expressed as a Markov Decision Process and solved as a stochastic dynamic
program (SDP) (Dixit & Pindyck, 1994). We could express the technical advances as possible
initial and ﬁnal states (to correspond to nine TRL levels), subject to budget considerations,
which may have ﬂexibility among the time periods, and solve for any number of objectives.
Given the linear nature of the objective function we wish to solve, it is possible to formulate
this real options problem as a mixed-integer program (MIP) (Eckhause et al., 2012), which
would also have particular computational beneﬁts when budgets can or must be optimized
in advance. However, in our real options problem, we are faced with nine levels and ≈
1000 possible projects, which makes the state space size (91000) computationally intractable
to obtain exact solutions, as both SDP and MIP formulations would require second-stage
decision variables for each of the possible states.
Instead, our approach is to formulate and solve a computationally tractable MIP whose
solution approximates the solution of the formal model. To inform our intuition, we ﬁrst
present the exact real options model and then we discuss approximations.

Limitations. The solution to this simple binary optimization model will provide an optimal
strategy and will deﬁne the projects to be funded in the ﬁrst and second Phases. However,
because management retains the ﬂexibility to fund any subset of projects, the second-phase

outcomes. In other words, the decision of any single project requires knowledge of the
outcomes of all the other projects. Hence, the second-stage decision variables yiS in Eq. 5
must depend on the projects’ states S at the beginning of the second stage. The goal is to
identify the values of a generic yiS that satisﬁes the constraints on the system. Thus, the size
of the set of variables yiS must be such that an optimal decision can be made for each possible
ﬁrst-phase outcome, i.e., S ∈M N.

S. For M maturity levels, this combinatorial problem translates into M N N possible binary
variables yiS and M N(N + 1) constraints. Even in a simpliﬁed TRL scheme with only ﬁve
levels and N ≈100 projects, this problem becomes intractable (∼1060 variables). In the
next section, we provide an approximation technique to solve this real options problem and
obtain reasonable funding solutions.

A range of approximation methods exists for problems whose exact formulation suffers from
the so-called “curse of dimensionality” (Powell, 2007). To solve a less complicated MIP, we
make use of recent insights on two-stage approximate dynamic programs (Zhang & Li, 2017);
namely, we solve for an initial optimal strategy based on a future stage whose value function
is approximated. In our case, we approximate the future value simply through an optimal
decision based on the expected outcome, given the ﬁrst-stage decisions. While this approach
may be a signiﬁcant limitation when applied to only a handful of Phase 1 projects, the
assumption that the exact outcomes for a set of Phase 1 decisions will be close to the expected
outcome is reasonable for cases that involve relatively large numbers of projects, especially
given identical transition probabilities. In this case, given the relatively large number of
proposals in Phase 1, solving for the Phase 1 strategy by approximating the value through
the expected resulting Phase 2 portfolio represents a straightforward approximation method,
and likely a reasonable approach that could be used by SBIR evaluation committees. From
the standpoint of an SBIR evaluation committee, generation of an optimal Phase 2 funding
strategy after the Phase 1 outcomes have been realized only requires resolving the model for
one time period, which can be expressed as a tractable knapsack problem. This approach also
extends nicely to provide an obvious method for an optimal Phase 1/ Phase 2 budget allocation
via the conversion of each time period’s budget into a decision variable.
3.3.3 Extending the model to ﬁnd robust solutions
The above approach may be limited if the resulting solution (i.e., the funding strategy) is
not particularly robust to uncertainties or disagreements in the coefﬁcients that comprise the
objective function (i.e., vi( fi)). Optimal solutions can be highly vulnerable to changes in
these parameters (Bertsimas et al., 2010).
123

Annals of Operations Research
Robust optimization strives to develop solutions that remain "close" to optimal scenarios
of the input data, which was initially proposed through the use of penalty functions (Mulvey
et al., 1995). Since then, several formulations have been developed to handle uncertainties
in these parameters, including rigorous and axiomatically grounded frameworks for min-
max and min-max regret (Aissi et al., 2009; Ben-Tal & Nemirovski, 1999; Loke et al.,
2024) for more traditional binary knapsack and MIPs, and methods such as robust Pareto
curve generation for multi-objective MIPs (Mavrotas et al., 2015). Our objective is based on
the product of four coefﬁcients, and even simple linear functions to model the coefﬁcients
constitute a phase space where the behaviors and sensitivities are not intuitively understood.
As a result, the level of uncertainty for each coefﬁcient leads to the aspiration to ﬁnd more
robust solutions.
With that purpose, we extend the real options approximation model and consider a min-
max relative regret (Dolgui & Kovalev, 2012) formulation, which aims to obtain a solution
that minimizes the maximum relative deviation over all possible scenarios between the value

nario where the maximized relative regret is 0.193 with a ﬁxed budget. The min-max relative
regret’s Phase 1 optimal strategy funds (ν1) 274 standard small business and 162 microﬁrms
projects, which we note is roughly between the ﬁrst-stage optimal strategies of Models A and
B, as shown in Table 7. The min-max relative regret approach is easily expanded to handle a
ﬂexible budget excursion if we add the constraints and variables of Eq. 6. As shown in Table
9, the maximum relative regret is reduced to 0.150 when budget ﬂexibility is allowed. Fur-
thermore, we demonstrate that while our base cases do not yield the absolute lowest relative
regret, they are in the bottom quartile, consistent with the base case parameterization. This
suggests that the price of robustness (Bertsimas & Sim, 2004) may be relatively modest.
5 Conclusion
We consider multiple interests—including non-ﬁnancial—in the optimal management of an
R&D portfolio that links the projects’ expected technical performance to the goals of the
portfolio as a whole. Our method represents these factors quantitatively to parameterize
a two-stage funding model based on real options theory, and then we evaluate potential
portfolios to select the optimal one. To illustrate how to apply the model we use proprietary
data from the NASA SBIR program. A key element in the decision of the selection team
is the TRL, assessed for each candidate project before and after each Phase. This allows us
to estimate the transition probabilities. The model is ﬂexible enough to incorporate other
goals. We then extend the model to consider more robust solutions under a min-max regret
formulation to handle uncertainties in the value coefﬁcients.
Our ﬁrst contribution is to link strategic considerations to portfolio valuation estimates. It
is important to emphasize that we have chosen a few factors that, although arguably relevant,
may not exactly reﬂect the actual goals of the NASA selection team, nor do they represent an
exhaustive list; R&D portfolios typically face this challenge of weighting multiple criteria
appropriately. In addition, the values we assign to these factors might be different from their
relative importance to NASA.
Other potentially valuable considerations that could be incorporated in our framework
are programmatic factors, residual value, and economic beneﬁt. Programmatic factors would
include the value of the completed task as part of a larger project—for example, inclusion
in a NASA ﬂight mission. We do not estimate the subsequent value to the agency of the
technologies under development, which may vary with the technology in question. Another
factor we have not considered but could easily be incorporated in our framework is the
residual value of the projects abandoned after Phase 1; in the particular model we have
used, the investment into projects funded only in the ﬁrst stage is a sunk cost and generates
no ultimate value to the portfolio. However, real options approaches can account for the
residual value of a project even if management chooses to forgo further investment. Indeed,
these projects may be restarted later. A third form of value not captured by our model is the
potential longer-term impact, such as subsequent venture capital, patents, or other spillover

effects. Finally, we note that work conducted in Phase 1 but discontinued in Phase 2 may
represent a potential technological avenue that does not work as anticipated or does not
compete favorably with other methods. The value of such a discovery is to avoid future
investment in a non-viable path—information whose value exceeds the cost to obtain it.
Next, we have demonstrated how to implement a portfolio model underpinned by real-
world data. While we construct a weighting scheme based on parameters of interest in our
industrial context of an aerospace portfolio, the principles can be applied broadly across
many types of considerations and industries. For instance, pharmaceutical industry factors
would include probabilities associated with clinical trial progressions, or potentially disease
incidence that might point to estimated market value for a treatment. Thus, this formulation
can be valuable in corporate contexts to account for systemic factors that operate in tandem
with the natural risk of a research portfolio.
Of course, our exact implementation has some limitations that could be mitigated with
additional research. The participation of decision makers in the selection of the objective
function parameter value ranges would provide a potentially more dependable set of fund-
ing options. The reformulation of our model as an explicit multiobjective optimization with
appropriate methods to explore how those solutions compare with our robust optimization
approach may provide additional funding strategies. Finally, a post-optimality analysis of the
funding decisions and project outcomes could provide additional insights from the compar-
ison of the model to actual performance.
Finally, we turn our attention to the implications of our analysis in a public R&D portfolio.
We ﬁnd that the current NASA conﬁguration is relatively close to optimal, even without
recognizing the residual value of Phase 1 projects unfunded in Phase 2. This suggests that the
current architecture used by the selection team of NASA is reasonable. Overall, the approach
presented here can be used to analyze SBIR R&D funding strategies to see if they align with
the program’s overall intent.
Our study of the ﬁrm size is important because it can be considered as a rough proxy
for ﬁrm age, an important factor in economic growth. According to our model, a preference
toward smaller ﬁrms does not signiﬁcantly impact the portfolio value; and in fact, our model
suggests that an objective function with a portfolio weighted toward small ﬁrms results
only in a minimal optimality penalty on the order of 1-2% in the original objective. The
incorporation of broader measures through the min-max relative regret analysis seems to
indicate that funding a sizable number of smaller ﬁrms can be part of a robust strategy. This
result is consistent with the observation that the smallest ﬁrms are most likely to have both
the smallest and largest advances in TRL; these extrema drive the real option value. Yet, it
is conceivable that adding to our model a factor that focuses on young (or very small) ﬁrms,
or assigning a substantially higher value to microﬁrms, would lead to a different optimal
portfolio.
In summary, we have demonstrated an approach to modeling research portfolios informed
by strategic considerations that compete with and may even supersede ﬁnancial metrics. With
measured distributions to inform the model parameterization, we have constructed synthetic
portfolios so that we can evaluate and compare potential funding strategies. This approach
enables us to make concrete suggestions for innovation policy, such as the proposal that
portfoliovaluecanbepreservedevenifprojectsfrommicroﬁrmsareselectedmorefrequently.
We demonstrate a methodology that can provide insight into R&D portfolio management.
