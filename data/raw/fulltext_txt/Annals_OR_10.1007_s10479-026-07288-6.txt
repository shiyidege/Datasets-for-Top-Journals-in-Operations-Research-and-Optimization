Abstract
The Cross-Dock door Design Problem (CDDP) consists of deciding on the number and
capacity of inbound and outbound doors for receiving commodities from origin nodes and
sending them to destination nodes. The uncertainty, realized in scenarios, lies in the sets
of nodes that must be dealt with, the volume of commodities handled and the operational
cost as well as the doors‚Äô capacity disruption. The CDDP is represented using a stochastic
two-stage binary quadratic (BQ) model. The Ô¨Ārst stage decisions are related to design of the
cross-dock infrastructure, and the second stage decisions are related to the assignments of
nodes to doors. This is the Ô¨Ārst time, as far as we know, that a stochastic two-stage BQ model
has been presented for minimizing the cost of building the platform‚Äôs infrastructure and the
expected cost of its use in the scenarios. Given the difÔ¨Āculty of solving this combinatorial
problem, a mathematically equivalent MILP formulation is introduced. However, searching
for an optimal solution is still impractical for commercial solvers. Thus, a scenario cluster
decomposition-based matheuristic algorithm is introduced to obtain feasible solutions with
only a small optimality gap and reasonable computational effort. A broad study to validate the
proposal gives solutions with a much smaller gap than the ones provided by a state-of-the-art
general solver. In fact, the proposal provides solutions with a 1.31% to 8.33% optimality gap,
while the solver does it with a gap of up to 12.45%, if any, and requires a wall time twice as
high for the largest instances, at least.

1 Introduction and motivation
Given a network with a set of supplying (i.e. origin) nodes for different types of commodity
and a set of receiving (i.e. destination) nodes for these commodities, usually in smaller
quantities, a cross-dock (CD) platform may serve as a consolidation point. From the origin
nodes, a volume of a commodity (i.e. number of pallets) enters the CD platform through
entry points, so that after being classiÔ¨Āed by type and destination they exit through exit doors
to the destination nodes. A CD infrastructure has a number of inbound doors, referred to as
strip doors, and a number of outbound doors, known as stack doors: each one has a capacity
for handling pallets during a given time period. Fig. 1 depicts a real terminal in Guadalajara
(Spain). The classical operation process of a CD platform is depicted in Fig. 2, taken from
Escudero et al. (2024).
Two main types of optimization problems arise in cross-docking. One is the so-called
Cross-Dock door Assignment Problem (CDAP). Its objective is to assign the commodity
Ô¨āows to the strip and stack doors in a way that minimizes operational costs. It is a deterministic
problem, so the set of available doors is given as well as their respective capacities. Even
considering these sets and parameters as deterministic ones, CDAP is a difÔ¨Ācult binary
quadratic (BQ) combinatorial problem. Its constraint system is composed of two Generalized
Assignment Problems: one is related to the strip doors and the other is related to the stack
doors, to which the origin nodes and the destination nodes are to be assigned, respectively.
Those two problems are linked by quadratic terms in the objective function, see Guignard et
al. (2012).
Another signiÔ¨Ācant and computationally challenging combinatorial problem in cross-
docking is the Cross-Dock door Design Problem (CDDP) under uncertainty, which is the
focus of this work. The basic CDDP involves designing the platform‚Äôs infrastructure (i.e. the
set of available strip and stack doors) to be built before the different sources of uncertainty are
unveiled, so that the cost of constructing the platform, together with the expected assignment
cost to the strip and stack doors across all scenarios, is minimized.

In the stochastic formulation, the sets of nodes are uncertain, as are the volumes of the
commodities that must pass through the CD platform, as well as the associated operational
costs. An additional source of uncertainty is related to the capacity disruptions caused by sab-
otage, misuse, or other events. These uncertainties are represented by a Ô¨Ānite set of scenarios
over a given time horizon.
The CDDP under uncertainty will be represented by a two-stage stochastic model. The Ô¨Ārst
stage decisions are about the design of the CD‚Äôs infrastructure. The second stage decisions are
about the assignments of the nodes to the doors. With this scheme, the goal is to minimize the
cost of building the CD‚Äôs infrastructure plus the expected operational costs of the assignment
of the nodes to the doors in the scenarios, subject to net capacity constraints and others.
As will be shown, the body of literature addressing this class of models is notably scarce.
In particular, few works examine the internal logistics of the cross-docking platform under
uncertainty, encompassing not only the variability of the commodity Ô¨āows and their origin-
‚Äďdestination patterns, but also possible disruptions affecting the capacities of the doors and
operations.
Literature review
The treatment of the cross-docking problem is a young discipline from an academic point
of view: as a matter of fact, most of the literature on this type of problem has been published
over the last 25 years. Bartholdi (2004) are the Ô¨Ārst ones to address this problem, focusing
on how the size of the facility and the pattern of freight Ô¨āows affect the efÔ¨Āciency of the
operations. In particular, they examine under what conditions each architectural shape of a
CD minimizes internal travel cost (and therefore labor costs), and provide design guidelines
for different scales of cross-docking operations.
Several types of cross-docking problems have been studied in the literature, such as:
strategic problems which determine a good location for the cross-docking platform and its
layout; and operational problems which determine the best assignment of truck to door,

CD doors. In fact, only the total capacity of each platform is typically considered. Shams-
Shemirani et al. (2023) apply process mining to a cross-docking platform, and develop
simulation-based algorithms to evaluate different historical scenarios, each composed of a
given set of doors and loaders within the context of designing the infrastructure of a CD.
1.1 Description of CDDP-TS as the subject of this work
As previously discussed, our proposal consists of formulating a two-stage stochastic model.
In the Ô¨Ārst stage, decisions are made regarding the selection and conÔ¨Āguration of the doors.
In the second stage, decisions are made regarding the operations for each door. The goal
of this stochastic problem is twofold: Ô¨Ārst, to select the doors and their nominal capacities
123

Annals of Operations Research
(strategic decisions); second, to assign commodity Ô¨āows to the strip and stack doors (opera-
tional decisions). Therefore, the problem considers a combination of strategic and operational
decisions. Here, the set of available strip and stack doors is not given, nor are their capacities.
The sets of origin and destination nodes are also uncertain and, so, the commodity Ô¨āows
from the origins to the destinations are also uncertain. The other sources of uncertainty lie in
the handling and transportation costs through the CD platform as well as the door capacity
disruption. There are two types of node‚Äďdoor assignments: the standard procedure and the
outsourcing one. The latter must be considered when either no strip door is available for
an inbound commodity Ô¨āow or no stack door is available for an outbound commodity Ô¨āow.
Outsourcing is required in both cases.
Then, CDDP will be formulated as a two-stage stochastic model, where the assignment
is performed under a Ô¨Ānite set of scenarios, which represent the realizations of the uncertain
parameters. In particular, the operational decisions under each second stage scenario of
CDDP-TS correspond to the solution of a CDAP, for a given Ô¨Ārst stage set of available doors.
SpeciÔ¨Ācally, the stochastic model proposed here extends the deterministic formulation of the
CDAP previously addressed in Guignard et al. (2012); Gelareh et al. (2020); Guignard (2020)
and Escudero et al. (2024), among others.
The parallelism between the deÔ¨Ānition of the operational models under each scenario of
the CDDP-TS and the CDAP formulation highlights the structural similarities between both
approaches, also in terms of their solution procedures. Thus, once the original CDDP-TS
model is presented as a binary problem with a quadratic objective function, the linear and
mathematically equivalent formulation (see Adams and Sherali (1986)) enables the intro-
duction of the two-stage stochastic model, a so-called Linearized mixed-Integer Problem
(LIP).
1.2 Contribution and positioning
The key contributions of this work are as follows:
(i) A two-stage stochastic BQ model for CD platform design (referred to as CDDP-TS) is
introduced to address uncertainty. The Ô¨Ārst stage is devoted to strategic decisions, made
before the uncertainty regarding the set of origin and destination nodes and the disrup-
tion of door capacities is unveiled. The second stage focuses on operational decisions.
The objective function to be minimized consists of the CD infrastructure construction
cost and the expected cost associated with the CDAP node-to-door assignment in the
scenarios. See Section 2.
(ii) An LIP model that is mathematically equivalent to the original BQ formulation is
developed. As a result, a large number of additional continuous variables and constraints
are introduced. See Section 3.
(iii) Two options for a Scenario Cluster Decomposition (SCD) of the LIP model are intro-
duced, exploiting the special structure of the problem. Option 1 decomposes the LIP
model into scenario clusters. Option 2 performs a deeper decomposition for the strip
and stack door types in each scenario cluster that, obviously, can be singleton. See
Section 4.
(iv) The matheuristic algorithm SCS4B (standing for Scenario Clustering and Splitting for
a Bounding solution) is developed to obtain lower bounds and feasible solutions for
the original CDDP-TS model. The approach relies on the Ô¨Ārst-stage solutions of the
SCD models, combined with a lazy scheme to obtain second stage solutions for each
scenario. See Section 5.

As will be shown, despite being a mixed 0‚Äď1 reformulation model with a linear objective
function, the special structure of model LIP, as well as that of the deterministic CDAP model
(Escudero et al., 2024), leads to high computational complexity. The latter acts as the second
stage model of the CDDP-TS formulation under each scenario, once the Ô¨Ārst-stage variables
are Ô¨Āxed. These features justify the development of an ad-hoc decomposition approach. For
the Ô¨Ārst step of the matheuristic, two Scenario Cluster Decomposition (SCD) schemes for
decomposing the LIP formulation into smaller submodels are proposed, by taking advantage
of the problem‚Äôs special structure. This decomposition provides, on the one hand, a lower
bound on the optimal solution of the problem and, on the other hand, it obtains a set of
Ô¨Ārst-stage solutions generated by each cluster submodel. Subsequently, as the second step of
the procedure, a lazy heuristic is applied to derive feasible solutions for the full model, based
on the Ô¨Ārst-stage solutions obtained from the cluster submodels.
Two observations can be made regarding this procedure. The Ô¨Ārst one concerns the lower
bound. As is known, once the SCD is deÔ¨Āned, an iterative procedure could be considered to
improve the lower bound on the optimal solution‚Äôs value and the corresponding Ô¨Ārst-stage
solutions. However, due to the characteristics of the CDDP problem, the computational cost
associated with each iteration is very high. Moreover, given the marginal improvement in
the lower bound achieved by each iteration of this class of algorithms, the additional effort
is not justiÔ¨Āed. The second observation is related with the high computational complexity
of the CDAP submodels under each scenario, once the Ô¨Ārst stage variables are Ô¨Āxed, which
arises from their speciÔ¨Āc structure. To address this difÔ¨Āculty, the second stage of the proposed
matheuristic scheme incorporates the local search heuristic (LSH) introduced in Escudero
et al. (2024), in order to obtain feasible solutions for large-scale instances. Alternatively, a
state-of-the-art solver can be used to solve either the above model or its LIP reformulation.
Additionally, the main results of an extensive computational experiment are reported
in Section 6 to validate the proposal presented in this work. The testbed of instances was
generated by extending some of the deterministic CDAP instances proposed in Guignard et
al. (2012) (see also Escudero et al. (2024)) to a stochastic setting. As a result, the proposed
approach provides solutions with an optimality gap ranging from 1.31% to 8.33%, whereas
the straightforward use of a state-of-the-art commercial solver yields solutions with gaps
of up to 12.45%. Furthermore, for the largest instances, the commercial solver requires, at
least, twice the wall time needed by the proposed approach, even when it is able to produce
a solution at all. Section 7 draws some conclusions.
Appendices A and B present illustrative examples to explain in detail the steps of
matheuristics SCS4B and LSH, respectively. Appendix C details the parallelization scheme of
the matheuristic. Appendix D introduces a frequency-based matheuristic algorithm (SC2FA)
for solving CDDP-TS; the results of the comparison between our algorithm, SCS4B, and this
scheme are also reported.

6.1 Generating the testbed
A testbed of instances has been generated by considering the stochastic extension of some of
the deterministic instances of model CDAP that have been taken from Escudero et al. (2024).
This subsection presents the related scheme. As a matter of fact, most of the deterministic
CDAP instances that have been considered in the generation of the stochastic testbed were
proposed in Guignard et al. (2012). They have a similar structure, which makes it possible to
embed them as scenarios for the second stage in model CDDP-TS (see below), given their
common framework of parameters and variables for the Ô¨Ārst stage. The dimensions of these
instances are {8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 20, 25} for |M| = |N| and {4, 5, 6, 7, 10} for |I| = |J |. As
in Guignard et al. (2012), the nonzero (25%) elements of the Ô¨āow square matrix are randomly
generated in the range from 10 to 50. On the other hand, the elements in the unit distance
matrix are in the range from 8 to 8 + |I| ‚ąí1, where a symmetric pattern is considered for
the distance between strip door i and stack door j. Additionally, the capacity of the doors
in each instance is set to the total Ô¨āow though all origins and destinations divided by the
total number of strip and stack doors, plus a slackness percentage s of the total Ô¨āow, where
s ‚ąą{5, 10, 15, 20, 30}.

7 Conclusions and research agenda
A two-stage stochastic BQ formulation and related MILP have been introduced for the Cross-
Dock door Design Problem under uncertainty regarding the sets of origin and destination
nodes. There is also uncertainty in the commodity volume that must traverse the CD platform
from the origin nodes to the destination ones in a given time period and with a potential for
the impairment of the capacities of the doors by disruptions. Two schemes, referred to as
SCD Options 1 and 2, have been proposed for obtaining strong lower bounds on the optimal
solution value of this NP-hard combinatorial problem, as well as a constructive matheuristic
algorithm, referred to as SCS4B. These schemes take advantage of the GAP structures that
are embedded in the model; in particular, they are embedded in its seemingly most efÔ¨Ācient
Linearized Integer Programming reformulation (LIP). It has been computationally proved
that the overall SCS4B scheme requires, even when Options 1 and 2 are combined, only
123

Annals of Operations Research
a reasonable wall time that is much smaller than the wall time required by straightforward
CPLEX. On the other hand, it provides practically the same value of the incumbent solution
for small- and medium-sized instances and a slightly better value for larger instances. In any
case, the optimality gap of the combined options in the matheuristic is, in general, small and
much smaller than the CPLEX‚Äôs for the largest instances.
From the experiments it can be concluded that joining Options 1 and 2 is the best choice
for most of the instances. Moreover, Option 2 is the best selection for instances I7.1 and I7.3
and Option 1 is the best one for the largest instance, I8.

Appendix D. Scenario Cluster Capacity Frequency Algorithm (SC2FA)
As mentioned above, CDDP under uncertainty is a very difÔ¨Ācult problem to solve. As a matter
of fact, even its deterministic version is an NP-Hard combinatorial problem, as it is also the
scenario CDAP. As was observed in Section 6, a high computational effort is required for
solving the Scenario Cluster (SC) c-submodels (9)-(10) for Option 1, c ‚ąąC, and (11)-(13) for
Option 2, c ‚ąąC‚Ä≤. (In any case, the wall time is much smaller than that of the straightforward
use of the solver). Those c-submodels can be related to singleton clusters (although the lower
and upper bounds can be weaker ones). The generation of the SCs and solving of the related
submodels are performed in the Steps 0, 1 and 2 of the matheuristic algorithm SCS4B, see
Section 5.2.
Note that those steps could also be required by any SCD-based iterative algorithm, such
as, e.g. the Lagrangean Decomposition Methodology (LDM), and other SCD-based ones
(see Escudero et al. (2013); Escudero and Gar√≠n (2017); Escudero et al. (2024, 2018), among
many others) and the ones based on the Progressive Hedging Methodology (PHM), intro-
duced in Rockafellar and Wets (1991) for providing the solution of stochastic programs with
continuous variables and specialized in Gade et al. (2016); Watson et al. (2010); Escudero
et al. (2018), among others, for obtaining (hopefully, good) feasible solutions in stochastic
mixed integer programs. By construction, those types of algorithms are iterative ones, it hap-
pens that variations of the SC c-submodel must be solved at each of the iterations, for c ‚ąąC,
thus making the total wall time unaffordable for solving CDDP-TS.
The appendix presents an alternative non-iterative approach to the non-iterative SCS4B,
which will be referred to as SC2FA (it stands for ‚ÄėScenario Cluster Capacity Frequency
Algorithm‚Äô), which exploits the CDDP-TS model‚Äôs structure. For ease of presentation, let
us consider some PHM terminology, although the scheme for obtaining the related elements
differs one from the other. So, an implementable solution is a unique Ô¨Ārst stage one for all SC
c-submodels, and an admissible solution is an implementable one that satisÔ¨Āes the Ô¨Ārst stage
constraint system, in our case system (2). The implementable solution is basically obtained
from assigning, in a factorized mode (see below), the most frequent nominal capacity level
to each strip and stack door as retrieved from solving the whole set of SC c-submodels.
The admissible solution is obtained by removing from the implementable one some doors to
satisfy system (2) under a given criterion.
Note that the Ô¨Ārst stage constraint system is a pure binary one and the second stage is
given by a set of scenario-based CDAPs for an admissible solution. SC2FA is based on the
generation of SCs and solving the related c-submodels, just as it is for the non-iterative
algorithm SCS4B, and it could be as well for the iterative LDM and PHM approaches. The
implementable solution is basically obtained from assigning in a factorized mode the most
frequent nominal capacity level to each strip and stack door as retrieved from solving the
whole set of SC c-submodels (9)-(10) or, alternatively, (11)-(13), for c ‚ąąC. On the other hand,
it solves the scenario ŌČ-CDAP models (14), for ŌČ ‚ąą, that results from Ô¨Āxing the SC2FA
admissible solution in the original model CDDP-TS (6) (as the other three methodologies
can do it for their own admissible, i.e. feasible Ô¨Ārst stage ones), so that the full solution is
provided jointly with the related optimality gap. Note: The lower bound can be expressed
as z = max{zop1, zop2}, where zop1 as (10) and zop2 as (13). Note: z = zop1 in the
computational experiment presented below.
