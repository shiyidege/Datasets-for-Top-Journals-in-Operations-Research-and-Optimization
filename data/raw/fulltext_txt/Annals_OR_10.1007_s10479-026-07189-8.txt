Abstract
The paper considers the assignment of students to seminars regarding three hierarchical
objectives: maximizing the students’ preferences, maximizing the within seminar diversity,
minimizing the between seminar diversity variation. While the ﬁrst objective pictures the
students, preferences, the second and third picture the school’s preference of having com-
parable seminar groups. To reach this aim the paper extends the well-known Maximally
Diverse Grouping Problem and its balanced version by the ﬁrst objective, the students’ inter-
ests. The students’ interests are pictured by a preference sequence the students have for the
offered seminars, e.g. because of the scheduled time, the topic or the lecturer. We present
solution approaches that include properties from game theory in the assignment and result in
an assignment of students to seminars including the students’ as well as the school’s prefer-
ences. Our results show that the presented solution approaches are able to solve instances of
practical relevant size within half an hour (close to) optimality. Furthermore, in our artiﬁcial
test instances, including student preferences in the assignment only led to a small reduction
of the maximal diversity for instances of realistic size (2–3% difference for seminars with
20 students).
Keywords Assignments · Groups · Game theory · Mixed-integer programming · Matching

The contribution of the paper can be summarized as follows:
• We introduce an exact solution approach to combine preference-based student assign-
ments with the school’s interest for heterogeneous seminars and similarity between
different seminar groups.
• We integrate beneﬁcial properties from game theory into student assignments.
• We present a hierarchical optimization of student preferences, maximally diverse semi-
nars, and balanced seminars.
• Our approach combines the approaches by Schulz (2021) (mixed-integer program for
the Maximally Diverse Grouping Problem (MDGP) with attribute values like academic
achievement scores but only for equal-sized groups) and Schulz (2022b) (mixed-integer
program for the general MDGP with attribute values) in the second and third step of
the solution procedure and thereby contributes to the literature on MDGP (with attribute
values).
• We evaluate the resulting solution approach in a comprehensive computational study.
The paper is structured as follows: First, a literature review (Sect. 2) is given subdivided
into a part concerning game theory (Sect. 2.1) and a part concerning operations research
approaches (Sect. 2.2). Then, we describe our problem setting in Sect. 3. Afterwards, we
present our solution procedure in Sect. 4 before it is evaluated in a comprehensive computa-
tional study in Sect. 5. The paper closes with a conclusion (Sect. 6).
2 Literature review
Preference-based assignments are considered in three research areas. First, there is a huge
body of literature in the ﬁeld of game theory. Second, like our approach, the problem is
investigated in the ﬁeld of operations research and third, preference-based assignments are
viewed in computer science. Our literature review focusses on the ﬁrst two areas. An overview
on preferences in artiﬁcial intelligence can be found in Domshlak et al. (2011).
If the number of students equals the number of seminar places, our problem setting con-
forms to the house allocation problem, where n agents own n indivisible objects (houses) and
each agent has preferences over the objects (Abdulkadiro˘glu and Sönmez (1998)). Further
variants are the assignment of individuals to positions with limited capacity, faculty members
to committees, family members to household chores, pupils to schools or students to courses
(Hylland and Zeckhauser (1979)).
We ﬁrst consider the ﬁeld of game theory, where we focus on properties of matchings,
before we view the literature in the area of operations research.
2.1 Game theory
In the game theory literature, mostly agents (students) have preferences for objects (seminars)
and objects have preferences for agents (Gale and Shapley (1962)). In game theory, several
properties of such bipartite matchings were deﬁned. We collect some of them in this section
and relate them to our problem setting and the ﬁeld of operations research. In the paper at
hand, the school (or seminars; objects) has no preferences on single students but on their
distribution over the seminars. Therefore, we only consider preferences of agents for objects
but not vice versa in the following.

Gale and Shapley (1962) introduced the concept of a stable matching. A matching is stable
if no pair of agents α and β exists such that α is assigned to object a and β to object b but
α prefers b and β prefers a. This means that any 2-optimal matching, i.e. a matching that
cannot be improved by a swap of the assignment of two agents, is always stable. However, a
stable matching is not necessarily 2-optimal (e.g. a swap exists in which one agent improves
their preference value by 2, while the other degrades their by 1). But an optimal solution
regarding (1) is even stronger. There is even no set of k students for any k such that any
different assignment of their places amongst them leads to an assignment where all k students
improve. Hence, an optimal solution for SAPP is in the core. A matching is in the core if no
subset of students and seminar places can be rematched such that all students of the subset
improve (Aziz and Klaus (2019)). However, a matching can be in the core although it is no
optimal solution regarding (1). Assume a matching that is in the core but can be improved by
swapping two students currently assigned such that both have the same preference for their
assigned seminar such that one improves its preference value and the other one downgrades
it. Objective function (1) has also disadvantages. It does not maximize the sum of preference
values and, thereby, also not the average preference value. Moreover, the minimal preference
value is not necessarily maximal. We provide an example in Appendix B. Note that in our
approach the ﬁrst objective regarding preference maximization can easily be adapted. In the
second and third step, we only ﬁx the objective value of the ﬁrst objective to its optimal value
(see (9)). This can also be done with any other objective function according to which we
need to optimize the solution in the ﬁrst step then.
A matching is called individually rational if no student is assigned to a seminar that is
worse than the no-seminar option (Balinski and Sönmez (1999)). We assume that all students
prefer each seminar to the no-seminar option. Otherwise, we can simply ﬁx xi j = 0 for the
corresponding seminar j which is worse in comparison to getting no seminar for student i.
Be aware that allowing students to exclude seminars might lead to individual strategies which
lead to an individual improvement but a deterioration of the entire solution. Moreover, it might
be possible that fewer students can be assigned in total then. We exclude this situation here
by assuming that students prefer each seminar to the no choice option. Beside, a matching is
called non-wasteful if no student prefers a seminar to their assignment that has a free place
(Balinski and Sönmez (1999)). A non-wasteful assignment cannot be optimal according to
(1) and would, hence, never be selected in our context.

There are further properties which are not included, since they depend on data and design.
A further important property of a matching is envy-freeness. A matching is envy-free if each
agentprefersitsownobjecttotheobjectsofallotheragents(Procaccia (2009)).Envy-freeness
can not be achieved if for at least one seminar the number of students who prefer this seminar
more than all other seminars exceeds the number of places in this seminar. Otherwise, an
optimal solution for SAPP is envy-free.
Moreover, the assignment strategy itself is regarded. It is strategy-proved if no single
agent can be better-off by misrepresenting the preferences (Ergin (2002)). Because the model
maximizes ﬁrst the number of the highest preferences selected, then the number of second
highest preferences, and so on, there is no beneﬁt for any student to select another but the true
most preferred seminar for the highest preference. Nevertheless, there might be a beneﬁt in

2.2 Operations research
Preference-based assignments are well-investigated in the operations research literature.
Approaches consider for example the school choice problem (Pathak (2011)), the assignment
of lecturers to courses (Schniederjans and Kim (1987)) or the assignment of code reviewers
in software development (Wang et al. (2016)). Moreover, Heitmann and Brüggemann (2014)
developed a mixed-integer program to assign students to multiple teaching groups according
to preferences.
Furthermore, the allocation of students to project groups or seminars is investigated.
Manlove et al. (2018) investigated the student-project allocation problem (SPA) where a set
of lectures offer a set of projects, which they rank in a strict order. Beside, the projects have
a restricted capacity. The students also rank each project which is acceptable for them. The
objective is to ﬁnd a maximum stable matching. Abraham et al. (2007) presented two linear-
timealgorithmsfortheSPAwhichﬁndastablematchingofstudentstoprojects.Chiarandiniet
al. (2019) also assigned students to project teams but without lecturers’ preferences. However,
they allow students to register as groups of at most three persons who want to be assigned to
the same team. In the approach by Forrester and Hutson (2014), faculty preferences are that

all seminars are balanced with regard to the number of international students and gender. A
review on the SPA can be found in Faudzi et al. (2018).
Moreover, the literature offers a huge number of approaches for variants of the Student
Assignment Problem (SAP) but without preferences. The main objectives of the SAP are to
determine balanced groups or to maximize diversity concerning students’ attributes (Dias
and Borges (2017)). Different objective functions are discussed in Baker and Powell (2002)
and Schulz (2021). The problem variant which maximizes the sum of all absolute pairwise
differences of the attribute values of students who are assigned to the same group is called
the Maximally Diverse Grouping Problem (MDGP; Rodriguez et al. (2013)). The MDGP is
studied for example by Fan et al. (2011) who implemented a hybrid genetic algorithm. An
exact model to solve the MDGP with attributes is presented in Schulz (2022b).
An early approach by Beheshtian-Ardekani and Mahmood (1986) assigns students accord-
ing to a questionnaire score to projects with the objective to create balanced project groups.
Muller (1989) showed later that this procedure has a modest advantage over randomly formed
groups.
A similar approach to the one proposed in this paper can be found in the paper by Yeoh
and Mohamad Nor (2011). As we do, they used the cumulative grade point average (CGPA)
to balance the seminars. They aimed at gender and race diversity while keeping the average
CGPA equal over all groups. To do so, they deﬁned a minimum value for every measure
by rounding down the average and a maximum which is the minimum plus 1. In contrast,
our balancing measure minimizes the spreading between the groups by ideally seeking for a
partner in each seminar who has an as equal as possible CGPA. Cutshall et al. (2007) bal-
anced groups by minimizing the maximum absolute deviation of a team’s average academic
performance from the average academic performance of the entire class.
The aim of this paper is to combine the previous two research streams, i.e. to integrate
student preferences in the Student Assignment Problem or from the other perspective to
determine among all preference maximal, and hence stable, matchings the most balanced
student-seminar allocation regarding the so far academic achievements of the students. As
in the SPA our problem includes preferences of the institution but, in contrast to the SPA,
we have no preferences of lecturers but of the entire institution. Further, the institution has
no preferences on single students but on the entire matching. The institution’s preference is
to have an as diverse and balanced assignment as possible. Thus, we seek for a most diverse
and afterwards a best balanced matching regarding the students’ academic achievements in
the following sections.
3 Problem description
We describe the problem setting in this section. We consider the assignment of students to
seminars. Each student i ∈I has a preference value pi j for all seminars j ∈J such that
• for the most preferred seminar pi j = |J|,
• for the seminar with the second highest preference pi j = |J| −1,
• ...,
• for the seminar with the lowest preference pi j = 1.
The task is to assign the students to the seminars such that for each seminar the number of
students is within the given range [r j,r j]. We assume that for each student i and each pair
of seminars j, j′ ∈J with pi j > pi j′ the beneﬁt increase of getting seminar j instead of
the one with the next highest preference after j is higher than getting seminar j′ instead of
123

1107
the one with the next highest preference after j′. For example the beneﬁt for a student to get
the seminar with preference |J| is in comparison with the seminar with preference |J| −1
higher than getting the seminar with preference |J| −1 in comparison with the seminar
with preference |J| −2. This is a realistic assumption, as students strongly prefer some
professors or topics in comparison with others. A further reason is that the scheduled time of
a seminar might ﬁt much better in the student’s schedule than the time of the other seminars.
Nevertheless, one should be aware that other measures like avoiding low preferences, e.g. by
maximizing the smallest selected preference, are also possible.
First, we maximize in that sequence the number of students assigned to the seminar with
their highest preference value, the number of students assigned to the seminar with their
second highest preference value, and so on, i.e. objective function (1). Thereby, the students’
preferences are respected and the assignment is stable, in the core, and non-wasteful. In a
typical instance, where the number of students is much larger than the number of seminars,
the number of optimal solutions regarding (1) is also large (see Section 1). Hence, we search
for the best balanced solution regarding the academic achievements of the students among
all these optimal solutions.
We use the two-step solution approach by Schulz (2023a) although we slightly adapt it, as
we do not require equal-sized seminar groups (r j > r j is possible) and introduce a further
constraint that ensures that an optimal assignment due to (1) is selected. Let ai ∈R≥0 be the
score of the academic achievement of student i. Thereby, we are indifferent whether a high
or a low score corresponds to a high academic achievement as long as the scale is identical
for all students. The reason is that we consider the difference dik = |ai −ak| between the
scores of students i and k picturing the diversity between the two students i and k. In the ﬁrst
step, a Maximally Diverse Grouping Problem (MDGP) is solved with objective function

Before we evaluate the approach in the following section, we want to give some notes.
First, the approach combines the approaches by Schulz (2021) and Schulz (2022b) in the
second and third step and thereby contributes to the literature on MDGP (with attribute
values). Second, the ﬁrst step further reduces the solution space by Constraint (9) but might
also have an important effect on the solution process. In the LP relaxation (i.e. relax (7) to
0 ≤xi j ≤1 for all i ∈I and j ∈J), objective (1) is maximized if all students are assigned
to a seminar with an as high as possible preference. As Constraints (9) picture an optimal
solution for (1), the models in the second and third step need to assign as much as possible of
a student to a seminar with an as high as possible preference to fulﬁl Constraints (9). Thus, it
is likely that many xi j variables are close to 0 or 1 in an optimal solution of the LP relaxation
which would lead to tight bounds and a good solution performance. However, this effect is
less pronounced if many students have the same preferences. We evaluate the computational
performance in the next section.

5 Computational study
In the computational study, we evaluate our three-step solution approach and compare it with
the known two-step approaches by Schulz (2022a) and Schulz (2021) which are outlined for
our setting in Appendix A. We start with a description of the instance generation in Section
5.1 before we discuss the results in Section 5.2. The models were implemented in GAMS
(version 42) and solved with CPLEX (version 22.1.1). We executed all experiments on a
single AMD EPYC 7542 32-core with 2.90 GHz and 32 GB RAM.
5.1 Data generation
We determined 30 instances for |J| = {2, 3, 5, 10, 15, 20} and |I| such that |I|/|J| equals 5,
10, 15, 20, 25, and 30 for the setting with equal-sized seminars as well as for varying seminar
sizes. In the latter case, r j was set to ⌈0.9 · |I|/|J|⌉and r j to ⌈1.1 · |I|/|J|⌉, i.e. the seminar
size can vary by 10% in both directions from the average.
For every instance and every student i the academic performance score ai was generated
randomly between 1.0 and 5.0 which is the scale for grades in Germany. Thus, we can interpret
the score as the average grade over all courses which are so far completed. The preferences
were determined as follows: For every student we determined a random sequence of all
seminars j and set pi j to the position of seminar j in the random sequence.
The time limit for all models and instances was set to 1800s. Moreover, we did not solve
instances for unequal seminar sizes and at least |J| = 15 seminars because of the required
memory (four-indexed variables).

B Example showing the disadvantages of objective function (1)
Consider an example with four students and four seminars, i.e. each seminar gets one student
assigned. The students’ preferences for the seminars are given in Table 6.
According to objective function (1), we ﬁrst assign as many students as possible to their
most preferred seminar (preference value four). Thus, student 1 or student 2 is assigned to
the ﬁrst seminar (let us assume it is student 1, they are indifferent), student 3 is assigned to
the second seminar, and student 4 is assigned to the third seminar. Then, only student 2 is
unassigned but their second and third preference is not possible. Hence, student 2 is assigned
to seminar four with the worst preference.
Given this solution, the sum of preference values can be improved by swapping the assign-
ment of students 2 and 4. By it, student 2 improves their preference value by two while student
4 reduces their preference value by one. In total, the sum of preference values is increased
by one. Moreover, the worst realized preference value is three instead of one in this solution.
Thus, objective function (1) does not maximize the sum of preference values and, thereby,
123

Annals of Operations Research (2026) 360:1101–1124
1123
given the constant number of students, also not the average preference value. Furthermore,
objective function (1) does not maximize the worst realized preference value.
