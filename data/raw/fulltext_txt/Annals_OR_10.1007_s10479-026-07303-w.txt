Abstract
In this paper, we present a conceptual design for a decision-support system to address tariff-
induced uncertainty and disruptions. The system is comprised of predictive (simulation) and
prescriptive (optimization) supply chain analytics supplemented by an LLM module for real-
time disruption detection. We illustrate applications of the system developed in AnyLogistix
supply chain optimization and simulation software and offer generalized mathematical for-
mulations. The experimental part includes several supply chain stress tests under different
tariff scenarios, conducted using discrete-event simulation, and recommendations for proac-
tive supply chain reconﬁguration via network optimization. This study has both theoretical
contributions and practical implications. For the ﬁrst time, we conceptualize a decision-
support system speciﬁcally tailored to the speciﬁcs of tariff-induced uncertainty. We also
show how the decision-support system proposed can be extended toward a digital twin. Our
models can be used immediately in practice for stress testing supply chain resilience and for
reconﬁguration in response to tariff shocks.

1 Introduction
Most supply chains have been designed in the era of relatively stable geopolitics, for efﬁ-
ciency, cost minimization, and global sourcing. These networks have become increasingly
susceptible to large-scale systemic shocks in times characterized by the disappearance of
rules and norms and the upscaling of geopolitical tensions. Within this context, tariff crises
emerge as a distinct and underexplored category of systemic shocks that challenge opera-
tional decision-making and threaten supply chain resilience and viability (Nagurney et al.
2019; Ivanov & Dolgui, 2025).
Tariff-induced disruptions are distinct from traditional supply chain risks. Unlike localized
disruptions, such as supplier failures or transportation breakdowns, tariffs result from exoge-
nous policy decisions. They immediately alter cost structures across multiple tiers of global

supply networks. These shocks typically involve both immediate effects, such as abrupt
increases in material and logistics costs, and delayed effects, including network redesign,
supplier relocation, and changes in production geography (Fang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023;
Wu et al., 2017). The global tariff crisis of 2025–2026 demonstrated the rapid destabilization
of supply chains, the emergence of shortages, and the ampliﬁcation of inﬂationary pressures
resulting from tariff implementation (Engelland, 2025; Ivanov, 2025c; Miller et al., 2025).
Historically, tariff crises have signiﬁcantly reshaped supply chains. Examples include the
British–American trade conﬂicts of the eighteenth century, the Smoot–Hawley Tariff Act of
1930, and the U.S.–China trade tensions of 2018–2020 (Handley et al., 2024). Despite differ-
ences in scale and institutional context, these episodes exhibit common patterns: short-term
disruption, increased costs, and longer-term structural adaptation through supplier diversiﬁ-
cation, regionalization, or reshoring. However, insights from past tariff crises are not directly
transferable to the present context due to substantial changes in supply chain structures, such
as increased globalization, reduced in-house production depth, and heightened interdepen-
dencies across industries (Carvalho et al., 2021; Dolgui et al., 2024).
Tariffs increase input and logistics costs, leading ﬁrms to raise prices, suspend deliveries,
reallocate supply, or close production facilities in affected regions (Hezarkhani et al., 2023;
Jena, 2026). These supply chain responses subsequently inﬂuence inﬂation, demand elastic-
ity, employment, and economic development, which in turn affect supply chain conditions
and decision-making (Dong & Kouvelis, 2020; Ivanov & Dolgui, 2025). This reciprocal
interaction of operational and macroeconomic levels distinguishes tariff crises from con-
ventional operational disruptions and establishes them as systemic shocks with signiﬁcant
macroeconomic consequences (Ivanov & Dolgui, 2022).
Recent studies indicate that tariff shocks frequently generate ripple effects, which are
cascadingdisruptionsthatpropagatethroughsupplychainnetworksanddegradeperformance
across multiple echelons (Dolgui et al., 2018; Ivanov et al., 2014; Namdar et al., 2024). These
ripple effects may be accompanied by disruption tails, such as backlog accumulation and
logistics congestion, as well as disruption overlays, which arise when ripple effects intersect
with bullwhip effects caused by panic buying or speculative demand (Dolgui et al., 2020;
Habibi et al., 2025; Ivanov et al., 2025; Osadchiy et al., 2021). These complex dynamics
substantially complicate planning and recovery, especially under conditions of profound
uncertainty.
In light of growing exposure to systemic shocks, supply chain stress testing has become
increasingly important (Dolgui et al., 2025; Ivanov, 2025c, Prathapage et al. 2025). A stress
test is a simulation designed to analyse the ability of a supply chain to withstand disruptions
and recover, the dynamic behavior of the supply chain under stress, and the performance
impact of disruptions. The main objectives of supply chain stress tests are to analyse the
ripple effect, predict disruptions and disruption scenarios, examine interlinkages between
supplier performance and resilience, analyse resilience strategies, assess the performance
impact of disruptions, identify hidden suppliers and links, and analyse the viability and the
roles of trust and collaboration.
As noted in Prathapage et al. (2025), stress testing extends risk management in resilience
analysis by three main components:
• Examining the mechanisms of why and how the supply chain degrades and recovers after
disruptions
• Understanding supply chain adaptations as responses to disruptions
• Measuring supply chain performance in the presence of disruptions

Although stress testing has been widely applied to capacity disruptions, demand shocks,
and supplier failures, tariff-induced uncertainty remains insufﬁciently addressed in current
frameworks. Most existing studies consider tariffs as static cost parameters rather than
dynamic stressors with delayed and systemic effects.
We contribute to literature by proposing a generalized concept of a decision-support sys-
tem (DSS) for ﬁrm’s responses to tariff-induced uncertainty and disruptions. We route our
conceptualization into advancements in supply chain analytics, including discrete-event sim-
ulation, network optimization, artiﬁcial intelligence, and digital twins. Simulation facilitates
predictive analysis of dynamic system behavior under uncertainty, such as lead-time vari-
ability, inventory ﬂuctuations, and disruption propagation. Optimization offers prescriptive
guidance for network reconﬁguration, sourcing decisions, and material ﬂow allocation within
cost and capacity constraints (Ivanov, 2020; Sawik, 2023). The integration of these methods
with real-time detection of tariff-related events using LLMs enables decision-makers to tran-
sition from reactive responses to proactive and informed adaptation strategies and toward
digital twins (AlMahri et al., 2026; Ivanov & Gusikhin, 2026; Kosasih & Brintrup, 2025).
Differently to prior works on methodologically fragmented stress testing, ripple effects, and
digital twins, our study proposes an integrative view, connecting a novel disruption context,
model-AI symbiosis in DSS, and managerial insights toward building supply chain viability.
The remainder of this study is organized as follows. Section 2 analyses related literature. In
Sect. 3, generalized models are presented. Illustrative examples are shown in Sect. 4. Section 5
concludes this paper by summarizing major outcomes and delineating future research direc-
tions.
2 Literature review
2.1 Tariffs and supply chain disruptions
Tariffs have traditionally been analyzed in economics and international trade literature, pri-
marily focusing on prices, welfare effects, and trade volumes. In supply chain management,
tariffs have often been regarded as exogenous cost parameters in sourcing and pricing models.
Recent research, however, highlights that tariffs function as signiﬁcant operational stressors,
directly inﬂuencing sourcing decisions, logistics ﬂows, and network conﬁgurations (Gross-
man et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).
Extant literature shows that tariff increases elevate the costs of raw materials, intermediate
goods, and transportation, frequently compelling ﬁrms to choose between margin erosion and
passing costs on to customers (Hezarkhani et al., 2023; Nagurney et al., 2019). The effects of
tariffs vary considerably across industries and products, depending on supply chain structure,
supplier concentration, and the availability of alternative sourcing options (Li et al., 2023;
Zhang et al., 2025).
Supply chains that are globally dispersed and characterized by high product complexity are
especially susceptible to tariff shocks. Although nearshoring or reshoring can reduce tariff
exposure, these strategies are often limited by long-term contracts, capacity constraints,
regulatory barriers, and sustainability requirements (Handley et al., 2025; Sawik, 2023).
Consequently, tariff-induced adjustments tend to be gradual and costly, underscoring the
necessity for analytical tools that facilitate informed decision-making under uncertainty.

2.2 Ripple effect and systemic shock propagation
The ripple effect is a central concept for understanding the propagation of disruptions through
supply chain networks. Originally developed to describe cascading failures resulting from
supplier disruptions, the ripple effect framework has evolved to encompass systemic shocks,
including pandemics, geopolitical conﬂicts, and trade wars (Li et al., 2021; Sawik, 2022).
Tariff shocks are especially likely to generate ripple effects, as they simultaneously impact
cost structures, material availability, and logistics ﬂows across multiple supply chain tiers.
Research indicates that tariff-induced ripple effects frequently interact with demand-side
dynamics, resulting in bullwhip effects, panic buying, and disruption overlays (Dolgui et al.,
2020;Habibietal.,2025).Theseinteractionscanintensifydisruptionpropagationandprolong
recovery.
Furthermore, ripple effects during tariff crises often extend across industries as a result
of intersectoral input–output linkages. Studies show that shocks in one sector can propagate
through production networks and affect aggregate economic outcomes (Carvalho et al., 2021;
Kopytov et al., 2024). This cross-industry dimension underscores the importance of shifting
from ﬁrm-level resilience to ecosystem-level analysis.
2.3 Supply chain stress testing
Supply chain stress testing has developed as a structured method for evaluating vulnerability
and resilience under extreme conditions. Stress tests generally comprise a shock scenario, a
modeling framework, and a performance impact analysis (Dolgui et al., 2025; Ivanov, 2025a).
The main objectives are to assess disruption propagation, evaluate resilience strategies, and
support preparedness planning.
Typical stress-testing methodologies are simulation-based, optimization-based, and
network-theoreticapproaches.Discrete-eventsimulationiswell-suitedformodelingdynamic
behaviors, including delays, inventory ﬂuctuations, and recovery trajectories (Dolgui et al.,
2020; Ivanov, 2024). Optimization models assist in designing resilient networks and recov-
ery policies for predeﬁned disruption scenarios (Sawik, 2023). Network theory emphasizes
structural robustness but often overlooks operational dynamics and material ﬂows (Li et al.,
2021).
Despite these advancements, stress testing for tariff-driven disruptions remains under-
developed (Ivanov, 2025c). Most existing studies do not explicitly model delayed effects,
disruption tails, or the reciprocal relationship between supply chain decisions and economic
outcomes. This limitation reduces the practical applicability of current stress-testing frame-
works in the context of tariff crises (Ivanov & Dolgui, 2025).
2.4 Decision-support systems and digital twins
While individual models of tariff-triggered supply chain stress testing are visible in liter-
ature, there is a lack of DSSs and digital twins in this area. The integration of simulation
and optimization is widely acknowledged as an effective approach for supply chain decision
support. Simulation enables predictive analysis of system behavior under uncertainty, while
optimization offers prescriptive recommendations for network design and operational plan-
ning (Ivanov, 2020; Sawik, 2023). The combination of these methods allows decision-makers
to assess both the impact of disruptions and the effectiveness of response strategies.

Recent literature identiﬁes digital twins as an advanced paradigm in supply chain man-
agement. Digital twins combine real-time data, simulation, optimization, and artiﬁcial
intelligence to enable continuous monitoring, learning, and adaptation (Ivanov, 2023; Jack-
son et al., 2026; Li et al., 2025). Although digital twins have been applied to operational
disruptions and sustainability challenges, their use in addressing tariff-induced uncertainty
remains limited.
Recent literature highlights that state-of-the-art disruption detection increasingly relies
on AI-driven web scraping techniques, particularly large language model (LLM)-based
approaches such as retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Al-Mahri et al., 2026; Jackson
et al., 2026). These methods enable continuous monitoring of unstructured data sources and
facilitate timely identiﬁcation of emerging risks.
Ivanov and Gusikhin (2026) deal with agentic AI architectures, including their design
principles, data integration strategies, and empirical validation. Their study presents a con-
crete implementation of a digital twin at Ford Motor Company, demonstrating its practical
effectiveness in real-world supply chain environments. Complementing this work, Ivanov
(2026a, 2026b) further elaborates on industrial use cases at Ford and Microsoft, illustrating
how agentic AI systems can autonomously detect disruptions through advanced text analytics
and generate actionable alerts in near real time. Within this framework, LLM-based agents
play a critical role in identifying disruption signals such as geopolitical events or policy
changes and triggering downstream analytical processes. For instance, in the case of disrup-
tions, alerts generated by these agents can be directly fed into simulation and optimization
models.
Our literature analysis shows that there is a signiﬁcant research gap leading to the absence
of DSSs speciﬁcally designed for tariff-induced systemic shocks. Such DSS should inte-
grate simulation-based stress testing with optimization-based reconﬁguration and account
for real-time disruption detection and supply chain mapping. Addressing this gap is crucial
for advancing both theoretical and practical aspects of supply chain resilience and viability–a
distinct and substantial contribution made by our study.

3.4 Extension toward a digital twin
A practical scenario of supply chain stress testing using a digital twin involves the detection of
disruptive events (in real time), mapping the supply chain structure, and deploying simulation
and optimization models to analyze disruption impacts. The state-of-the-art approaches to
disruption detection are based on web scraping using AI, speciﬁcally LLM-based methods
such as RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) (Al-Mahri et al. 2026; Jackson et al., 2026).
Ivanov and Gusikhin (2026) provide details on agentic AI design, data sources, and empir-
ical validation, showing a concrete implementation of the proposed digital twin concept at
Ford Motor Company and demonstrating its effectiveness. Ivanov (2026a, 2026b) describes
the use cases of Ford and Microsoft for how agentic AI can be used to identify disruptions.
LLM agents can identify disruptions (e.g., via text analytics) and generate timely alerts. In the
event of tariff-induced disruptions, the alert can be directed to optimization and simulation
models.
The supply chain digital twin use case for stress testing and resilience analysis can be illus-
trated as follows. The stress-testing scenario begins with identifying newly induced tariffs.
This data is then mapped with the supply chain footprint. In cases of overlapping disrup-
tions to supply chain elements (ﬁrms and/or logistics ﬂows), the simulation and optimization
models are used to predict the performance impact (simulation) and propose directions for
optimal supply chain reconﬁguration (optimization).
The application of digital twins for stress testing and resilience analysis can thus be struc-
tured as a multi-stage process. It begins with the identiﬁcation of newly imposed tariffs,
followed by the mapping of these disruptions onto the supply chain footprint. When dis-
ruptions affect multiple interconnected elements such as ﬁrms or logistics ﬂows, simulation
models are employed to estimate performance impacts, while optimization models are used
to identify optimal reconﬁguration strategies. Together, these capabilities provide a robust
framework for proactive risk management and adaptive supply chain design.

4.4 Managerial insights
Tariff-induced disruptions exhibit peculiarities that make their impact and mitigation strate-
gies different from those of single-point, immediate-impact disruptions such as ﬁres, natural
disasters, or strikes. First, it is not the tariffs themselves but the prolonged conditions of
deep uncertainty and chaos that challenge supply chain responses. Tariff shocks are a unique
123

Annals of Operations Research
type of systemic risk characterized by “(i) a combination of immediate and delayed effects,
(ii) cross-industry ripple effects, (iii) short-term responses to ensure resilience and long-
term adaptations for viability, and (iv) bidirectional reﬂexion of supply chain and economics
decisions” (Ivanov & Dolgui, 2025).
Our proposed DSS architecture help managers understand how tariffs and trade wars will
impact supply chains, how supply chains can become exposed to the shocks they exacerbate,
e.g., higher inﬂation driven by hikes in material costs, and how supply chain reactions to
tariff crises impact the economy. Managers can use the proposed DSS, with elements of a
digital twin, for a wide variety of analyses and stress tests in situations with ad hoc tariff
onset or planned reconﬁgurations for known, in advance-announced tariff changes.
First, in the event of new tariff announcements, simulation models can be run to imme-
diately inform supply chain managers of the tariff consequences for ﬁnancial, customer,
and operational performance. These predictions can help identify potential ﬁnancial losses,
bottlenecks, and affected customers/markets. On the other hand, simulation results can be
instrumental in supporting decision-making when a supply chain reconﬁguration is needed.
Different tariff scenarios can be tested.
The optimization module of the DSS can support managers in optimizing network recon-
ﬁguration for different tariff scenarios. Network optimization models can aid in identifying
new supply—demand allocations, shipment routes, and production–distribution plans. Since
network optimization can compute alternative network designs and estimate their proﬁtabil-
ity, it offers a useful decision-support model that can be extended through expert analysis,
including soft factors that were not quantiﬁed in the MILP model.
Finally, if the LLM module is used, the DSS can detect early signals of newly introduced
/ planned tariffs. This allows ﬁrms to react in a timely manner, e.g., securing some stock on
the spot market before taking on mid-term response measures. Moreover, if automatically
connected to the digital maps and constraint/event systems of optimization and simulation
models, the LLM module allows the creation of a digital twin. Every time a simulation or
optimization starts, it happens with real-life data (Ivanov, 2025d).
The DSS proposed can help understand capabilities required for ensuring business con-
tinuity during tariff-induced prolonged crisis. Because of peculiarities of tariff-induced
uncertainty shown above, traditional notions of resilience based on activating backup suppli-
ers or building short-term buffers are no longer sufﬁcient and we favor the focus on supply
chain viability (Sawik, 2025).
Supply chain viability is the capacity of a supply chain to operate and continue delivering
products and services to markets and customers despite disruptions and prolonged crises
(Ivanov, 2026b). Viability encompasses the ability to survive and persist over time, thereby
ensuring business continuity and maintaining purpose and performance at levels appropriate
to prevailing disruptions (Darmian & Sgarbossa, 2026; Ivanov & Dolgui, 2020).
Designing a viable supply chain is driven by architecting a requisite variety and feedback
mechanisms that facilitate adaptation to multiple future scenarios and disruptions, even in
the absence of prior knowledge or forecasts about them, to ensure the persistence of purpose
and performance. This includes creating system variety to absorb variety of crisis scenarios
without knowing them in advance, e.g., diversifying supplier and production bases, logistics
routes, investing in real-time visibility (i.e., digital twins), and fostering closer collaboration
across the supply network. Our approach can assist in testing different conﬁgurations of the
viable supply chain.

5 Conclusion
This paper introduced a DSS designed to address tariff-induced uncertainty and disruptions.
The proposed system integrates predictive (simulation-based) and prescriptive (optimization-
based) supply chain analytics to facilitate tariff-speciﬁc stress testing and proactive network
reconﬁguration supplemented by an LLM module for real-time disruption detection.
We offered a conceptual design of the DSS. Applications are demonstrated using anyL-
ogistix supply chain optimization and simulation software, with generalized mathematical
formulations provided to ensure transferability. The experimental part includes several supply
chain stress tests under different tariff scenarios, conducted using discrete-event simulation,
and recommendations for proactive supply chain reconﬁguration via network optimization.
Our study has both theoretical contributions and practical implications. For the ﬁrst time,
we conceptualize a decision-support system speciﬁcally tailored to the speciﬁcs of tariff-
induceduncertainty.Wealsoshowhowtheproposeddecision-supportsystemcanbeextended
to a digital twin. Our models can be used immediately in practice for stress testing supply
chain resilience and for reconﬁguration in response to tariff shocks. Additionally, the paper
outlines an extension of the proposed system toward a digital twin, enabling continuous
monitoring and learning under evolving tariff conditions.
Limitations exist as with any study. We contextualized applications of the DSS using a
single case only. We assumed that tariff announcements do not affect shipment times or
costs, and that manufacturing costs across regions are not affected by tariffs. While this can
be acceptable for the purpose of this paper which is for the presenting the generalized DSS
architecture and models are used for illustrative purpose only, in more technical papers, these
assumptions can be relaxed in the future. Application of the proposed models to different
industrial cases can help reveal nonlinear effects. For example, some shifts in supply chains
observed during recent tariff crises can be included that go beyond direct cost effects.
Some directions are outlined below. If tariffs are imposed, they can affect transportation
costs, as many companies will shift to alternative logistics routes and logistics companies
will raise prices for shipments due to higher demand. Similarly, production costs in countries
unaffected by tariffs can increase, as many ﬁrms will want to move production there. Future
research can elaborate on this empirical context and extend the models proposed in our study.
Finally, further research on AI-supported disruption detection and supply chain mapping will
advance the DSS for supply chain stress testing.
