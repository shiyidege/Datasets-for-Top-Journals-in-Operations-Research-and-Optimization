Abstract
Emerging and re-emerging infectious diseases continue to be a major threat to human 
health, and a threat to economic and financial stability worldwide. Epidemiological mod­
els have proven to be important tools for informing decisions on optimal policies, inter­
ventions, and control measures. However, the importance of surveillance and early detec­
tion is often overlooked, and the focus on control measures is mostly in terms of existing 
infections. In this work we present a framework for deciding on the optimal surveillance 
scheme that will minimize the overall cost of a disease, balancing the costs pertaining to 
surveys for early detection with other disease-related costs prior to detection. We show 
that the expected cost can be fully described for a general case of a stationary policy, 
thus supporting informed decision making between a few (fixed) strategies. In addition, 
we show that for time-dependent monitoring policies, the optimal policy is a bang-bang 
policy, whereby resources should be utilized for monitoring only after a critical time-point, 
to the maximum extent possible.

1  Introduction
The emergence and re-emergence of pathogens remains a major source of morbidity and 
mortality of human population today, as highlighted by the COVID-19 pandemic. Scientists 
have long warned of increasing emerging infectious disease (EIDs) risks (Jones et al., 2008; 
Lee et al., 2020; Sands et al., 2016; Daily et al., 1996) driven by the growing anthropogenic 
impact on the environment (Keesing et al., 2010; Lee et al., 2020; Daily et al., 1996). EIDs 
additionaly pose a major threat to the economy (Pike et al., 2014), and the cost of COVID-
19 in the US alone was estimated to reach close to 16 trillion USD (Cutler and Summers, 
2020). These enormous costs, combined with the accelerating speed at which humanity is 
challenged by EIDs, highlight the importance of surveillance and control for EIDs. Argu­
ably, efficient and rapid detection of EIDs is a critical component of any strategy aiming to 
control EIDs spread and decrease associated costs. The damage or cost of an outbreak is 
strongly governed by detection time due to the exponential growth of the initial phase of 
an outbreak. On the other hand, early detection depends on the resources allocated to sur­
veillance, carrying economic consequences. Public-health decision-makers must therefore 
balance two countervailing forces: (i) the upfront expenditure required to enhance surveil­
lance and (ii) the substantially higher downstream costs associated with larger, less tractable 
outbreaks.
The initial stages of EIDs require modeling through a stochastic framework, due the 
inherently small numbers of infectees (Bartlett, 1957; Daily et al., 1996). Incorporating 
detection as part of stochastic models is crucial, in light of the influence of early detection 
on pandemic outcomes and costs. However, previous modeling studies have not formally 
addressed the relationship between detection efforts, i.e. survelliance, and economic and 
public health consequences. In the context of surveillance modeling, Cheng et al. (Cheng 
et al., 2020) developed a framework for optimizing spatial disease survaliance. Mastin et 
al. Mastin et al. (2020) simulate data from a stochasitc model on a gridded landscape, opti­
mizing the spatial arrangement of surveillance sites. Dobson et al. (Dobson et al., 2023) 
used a deterministic SEIR (Susceptible-Exposed-Infected-Recovered) model coupled with 
an economical model, and used numerical optimization control measures to minimize epi­
demic costs. Craig et al. (Craig et al., 2018) conducted a statistical analysis to quantify the 
success of syndromic surveillance in retrospective and simultaneous disease outbreaks in 
the Solomon Islands.
Using the principles and terminology of optimal control theory (OCT), we adopt a proba­
bilistic framework to the problem of optimal detection of infectious diseases. By formulat­
ing an optimization problem, we enable a systematic trade-off between the benefits of early 
detection and its associated costs, and the risks and consequences of delayed detection, 
including increased morbidity and mortality. We develop a quantitative modeling frame­
work that incorporates a range of stochastic disease dynamics models, extending traditional 
models to include detection strategies. Coupling this framework with a novel analytical 
approach, we evaluate and optimize various surveillance policies, considering both direct 
costs of preparedness, monitoring, and diagnostics, and the broader economic burden of 
disease (e.g. hospitalization, pharmaceutical interventions, and long-term health impacts) 
(Cutler and Summers, 2020).

2  Model and results
Here we discuss optimal surveillance for detection of an epidemic outbreak and costs mini­
mization. Clearly, if monitoring the population is expensive and the costs of the disease 
itself are low we might consider leaving the population un-monitored; and conversely, if 
monitoring is relatively cheap. Our motivation is to support decisions regarding the level of 
monitoring in a quantitative and rigorous manner.
2.1  The general stochastic model

2.2.1  HIV and polio simulations and a hypothetical Scenario
In this simulation study, we evaluate detection performance in stochastic epidemic models 
under varying testing intensities, modeled through the detection-rate parameter, d. Three 
models are examined: a simple birth–death (BD) process, and two compartmental epidemic 
models—SIS (susceptible-infected-susceptible) and SIR (susceptible-infected-recovered). 
For each model, 200 stochastic realizations are simulated using the Gillespie algorithm 
(with the R GillespieSSA package (Pineda-Krch, 2008)). Key performance metrics include 
the expected detection time and the area under the curve (AUC) for the total number of daily 
infected individuals or active cases until detection. Each model’s behavior is examined over 
a range of detection rates d, derived from assumed test frequency and detection probability.

Conclusion 2  Bang-bang optimal control for any birth–death epidemic. Once a pathogen 
invasion occurred, the optimal policy is not depleting any resources until a critical time, t∗, 
arrives; thereafter you should monitor as much as possible at any time (i.e., dt = 0 before 
t∗, and dt = Dmax after t∗ until the resources are depleted completely or detection occurs).
Given λ, µ and p0 it is possible to determined t∗ numerically; notice that this requires only 
a line-search contrary to the brute-force attempt before.

should be conserved and detection efforts should begin only once the epidemic stops grow­
ing. This occurs at approximately t∗∼log(N)/(λ −µ) = 35 days.4

Bearing in mind that the extent of monitoring, and thus the detection rate, can actually be

detection-rate. Here we surprisingly find that the optimal control is a simple bang-bang 
control: wait and do nothing until t∗, and thereafter put all your heart into monitoring (see 
Conclusion 2). In hindsight, however, Conclusion 2 is very intuitive. Supercritical birth–
death processes exhibit a (quasi)-equilibrium, where the population remains infected for a 
very long time, and thus a substantially large cost is incurred if the disease goes undetected. 
Minimisation of the cost is achieved by maximising detection probability; and maximi­
sation of detection probability requires in turn avoid spending resources until the disease 
reaches its (larger) equilibrium size.
One might wonder how would a monitoring agency know if t∗, the critical time point, 
has been reached without spending resources on surveillance. While this policy thus may 
seem difficult to implement, below we illustrate some scenarios and conditions which make 
the above policy feasible.
Environmental surveillance is a classic example of a commonly-used routine for detec­
tion of outbreaks, as is currently the case for various diseases both in humans and in 
livestock. One example is sewage surveillance, which is the WHO’s recommended passive-
surveillance scheme for detection of silent outbreaks in Polio Berchenko et al. (2017). A 
similar approach is currently developed for COVID-19. In other diseases such as Bovine 
brucellosis, bulk milk samples are routinely screened for the presence of the pathogens. 
Such environmental surveillance methods are commonly used for detecting rare pathogens’ 
invasions. Once such an event is detected, public health officials may then consider active 
individuals’ surveillance. This work provides an analytic framework for optimizing the 
active surveillance module.
The framework presented here could be further developed for more detailed and realistic 
epidemiological models. The results presented here do not apply, for example, to an SEIR 
model (Dattner et al., 2020; Katriel, 2013), which is the simplest model decently captur­
ing the spread of the Corona virus (Dattner et al., 2020). To illustrate this, we examined 
via simulations the efficiency of waiting until a certain time and only thereafter investing 
a certain (fixed) amount of efforts, while trying to detect a simulated COVID-19 outbreak 
(see appendix C and Fig. 3). Presenting the total cost for different combinations of invest­
ment start-time and daily effort, the figure demonstrates that the predictions for the simpler 
birth–death model do not hold for the more complicated SEIR model: the best policy is not 
exhausting maximal possible daily effort, Dmax = 20, but rather to invest a smaller effort, 
D = 6, starting to monitor before the peak prevalence.
Another practical concern is the uncertainty regarding the disease parameters (in particu­
lar λ, µ and p0) which are difficult to estimate. Indeed, this issue is overlooked by the vast 
majority of epidemiological models which cast (future) predictions based on limited (past) 
data, taking the estimated parameters “as is” with little regard to their uncertainty. Clearly, 
we would advocate instead for the control-theory approach: instead of providing a naive 
policy, construct a policy that also decreases the uncertainty regarding the parameters. In 
other words, the vast majority of epidemiological models focus on the “exploitation” part 
of exploration-exploitation; however, for new and emerging disease the “exploration” part
