Abstract
Efficient inventory management is essential for fashion retailers seeking to reduce waste, 
curb stock-outs, and protect margins. Because¬† colour is a defining product attribute in 
apparel, balancing inventories across colour variants is both an economic and a sustain¬≠
ability challenge. We propose two complementary Markov-decision-process (MDP) tools 
to address this problem: (1) the Stochastic¬†Risk‚ÄĎAdjusted¬†Markov¬†Optimizer (SRAMO), 
a reinforcement‚ÄĎlearning procedure that samples prospective future states and rewards 
actions that minimize the expected deviation from a uniform colour distribution; and (2) 
Stochastic¬† Risk¬† Inventory¬† Analysis (SRIA), a diagnostic test that flags colours whose 
steady-state probabilities differ significantly from the uniform benchmark, signaling la¬≠
tent over- or under-stock risk. Using a dataset of products and their recommendation‚ÄĎlink 
transitions from five global e‚ÄĎcommerce platforms, we built two transition matrices and 
benchmarked SRAMO against classical Q‚ÄĎlearning and a deep Q‚ÄĎnetwork (DQN). SRA¬≠
MO reduced the average absolute deviation from uniformity to 0.042‚ÄČ¬Ī‚ÄČ0.001, a 55% im¬≠
provement over both baselines (p‚ÄČ=‚ÄČ0.003). Structural analyses show that anchor colours 
such as black centralize the MDP and mask substitution effects; removing black yields a 
more uniform steady state and elevates navy by 4.6%. These findings demonstrate that the 
SRAMO‚ÄďSRIA framework can both optimize dynamic replenishment policies and provide 
interpretable diagnostics for attribute‚ÄĎlevel inventory risk in volatile fashion markets.

1‚Äā Introduction
The fashion, or apparel, industry is one of the most lucrative globally and has a significant 
annual growth rate. It is also the second-largest polluter among industrial sectors world¬≠
wide1, currently contributing 10% of global pollution and expected to produce 26% of car¬≠
bon emissions by 2050. This substantial environmental impact could be reduced with more 
accurate forecasting of industry demand and trends, such as colour trends (a critical element 
in fashion and central to inventory and production planning of fashion-apparel products). 
Accurate forecasting and inventory management can reduce waste, save money, and provide 
an edge over competitors. Yet inventory management and forecasting are challenging in 
fashion due to the short product life cycle and high demand volatility (Boone et al., 2019). 
Artificial intelligence (AI) forecasting tools could improve forecast accuracy, making the 
industry more sustainable by reducing the impact of inaccurate forecasts and ineffective 
inventory management (Achamrah et al., 2021; Koren et al., 2023a, b).
Recent years have seen the environmental impact of ‚Äėfast fashion' in particular become 
increasingly apparent; it is estimated to consume about 2% of all energy used in the world2. 
The COVID-19 pandemic, climate change, and large fires have recently heightened aware¬≠
ness of this problem and provided an incentive for sustainable fashion to flourish, poten¬≠
tially leading us toward a greener and cleaner future (Vashisht & Mittar, 2019). With the 
continuous improvement of machine learning (ML) models, forecasting and inventory man¬≠
agement accuracy are likely to improve. As advanced technologies are adopted and sustain¬≠
ability and personalisation become more important, fashion forecasting is evolving. Yet the 
role of human experts in interpreting and curating trends will continue to be crucial even as 
AI and data-driven models become more accurate (Banerjee et al., 2021).
In this study, we tackle the essential challenge of managing inventory risk for high-variety 
product attributes, specifically colour, within the volatile fashion e-commerce sector. Our 
objective is to develop data-driven strategies using Markov decision processes (MDPs) to 
understand consumer preference dynamics between colours and promote inventory balance 
across the assortment, thereby mitigating costly overstocking and stockouts and enhancing 
operational efficiency. Our approach uses reinforcement learning principles informed by 
real-world e-commerce data. The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
(1) We introduce SRAMO, a novel reinforcement learning (RL)-based model designed 
to learn inventory management policies. SRAMO utilizes a unique reward structure 
based on sampling potential future state deviations from uniformity, guiding the system 
towards a more balanced inventory distribution across colours.
1‚ÄČ The procedures are predicated on several key assumptions that simplify the real-world complexity of inven¬≠
tory management. Firstly, we assume that customer demand for different colours remains relatively stable 
over the time horizon considered, which may not hold in highly seasonal or trend-driven markets. Secondly, 
inventory replenishment is modelled as a periodic process, ignoring potential real-time adjustments such as 
are common in modern e-commerce operations.

2‚Äā Literature review
2.1‚Äā Background: forecasting and inventory management in the fashion industry
The global fashion industry represents a substantial economic sector characterized by sig¬≠
nificant annual growth (Swaminathan & Venkitasubramony, 2023). However, effective 
inventory management and demand forecasting within this industry present considerable 
difficulties, primarily stemming from inherently short product life cycles and high demand 
volatility (Namin et al., 2022; Swaminathan & Venkitasubramony, 2023). Fashion cycles 
are frequently dictated by seasons, events, and rapidly evolving consumer tastes, making 
traditional sales forecasting challenging (Lee & Workman, 2021; Swaminathan & Venkita¬≠
subramony, 2023). Collections often transition from runway shows to retail points within 
mere weeks, and while seasons typically align with the calendar year (lasting three to four 
months), many companies introduce multiple collections per season, further shortening 
effective lifespans (Namin et al., 2022).
Fashion adoption often follows a pattern initiated by trendsetters, such as celebrities or 
influencers, before gaining wider public acceptance, eventually declining and being super¬≠
seded by new trends (Bag et al., 2019; Jin & Ryu, 2019). This dynamic nature, coupled 
with significant season-to-season shifts in consumer preferences, renders forecasting for 
subsequent periods exceptionally difficult, especially given that many items are novel with 
no direct historical sales data (Ergin et al., 2022; Lee & Workman, 2021; Machi et al., 2022). 
The available historical data often pertains only to similar, not identical, products (Ergin et 
al., 2022). Demand patterns are also markedly seasonal: for instance, winter apparel demand 
peaks in colder months and diminishes rapidly thereafter (Ergin et al., 2022).
Compounding these issues are cultural and psychological factors influencing consumer 
behaviour, alongside the significant impact of external variables like celebrity endorsements 
and social media visibility, which can cause unpredictable demand surges (Cai et al., 2022; 
Jin & Ryu, 2019; Ren et al., 2020). While some underlying trends might exhibit predict¬≠
ability over longer terms, the fast-paced nature of fashion means significant shifts can occur 
almost overnight (Jin & Ryu, 2019). Forecasting models attempt to incorporate diverse 
inputs, including economic indicators, cultural events, and climate data, to predict upcom¬≠

2.2‚Äā Machine-learning-powered inventory management
In the contemporary business landscape, data is increasingly recognized as a critical asset 
for enhancing organizational decision-making (Koren et al., 2023a, b; Li, 2022). The appli¬≠
cation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) has become widespread 
across numerous domains, proving invaluable for extracting insights and improving pro¬≠
cesses (Burkart & Huber, 2021; Sealfon et al., 2021). Data intelligence, defined as the 
capacity to analyze data and transform it into actionable knowledge, facilitates enhanced 
business operations, refined decision-making, and more accurate predictions of future 
outcomes (Koren et al., 2023a, b; Li, 2022). Its utility has been demonstrated in diverse 
applications such as customer segmentation (Li et al., 2021), financial fraud detection (Al-
Hashedi & Magalingam, 2021), risk management (Koren et al., 2023a, b, 2024; Leo et al., 
2019), and supply chain optimization (Gurtu & Johny, 2021). A recent systematic review 
highlights the growing application of various AI techniques, including ML and RL, specifi¬≠
cally within inventory management, underscoring the trend towards data-driven optimiza¬≠
tion in this field (Albayrak √únal et al., 2023).
Within fashion forecasting and inventory management, advanced data analytics and ML 
techniques are gaining traction (Boone et al., 2019; Han et al., 2022). These technologies 
enable the processing of vast datasets to generate faster and more precise trend predictions 
(Lai et al., 2023). Innovative methods combine statistical techniques, like Markov chain 
analysis, with ML to estimate sales probabilities based on user behavior, such as interactions 
with online platforms (Koren & Peretz, 2024). This data-driven approach opens significant 
possibilities for optimizing inventory decisions in the highly dynamic fashion sector (Vash¬≠
isht & Mittar, 2019).
2.3‚Äā Reinforcement learning for attribute-level inventory optimization
RL has emerged as a particularly relevant machine learning paradigm for sequential deci¬≠
sion-making problems under uncertainty, such as inventory control (Ding et al., 2020; Rolf 
et al., 2023; Sutton & Barto, 2018). In RL, an agent learns optimal behaviors (policies) 
through interaction with an environment, adjusting actions based on observed rewards with 
the goal of maximizing cumulative return (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Classical Q-learning 
(Chadi & Mousannif, 2023; Clifton & Laber, 2020; Watkins & Dayan, 1992) provides a 
value-based framework for estimating the expected return of taking an action in a given 
state, enabling optimal strategies to be identified within a MDP. Its applicability spans 
domains from gaming (Samsuden et al., 2019) and robotics to supply chain management 
tasks such as routing and replenishment (Boute et al., 2022a; Mammeri, 2019; Mosadegh et 
al., 2020; Wan et al., 2018).

3‚Äā Methodology and model development
Here, we present two procedures developed to manage inventory decisions and risk level for 
e-commerce platforms using MDPs. First, we describe SRAMO, which is designed to enhance 
decision-making by incorporating a risk parameter that adjusts rewards based on sampling 
techniques to approximate the reward function more effectively. The general procedure is 
rooted in the principles of reinforcement learning, specifically drawing from the Bellman-Ford 
equation utilised in Q-learning (Zhao, 2022). Second, SRIA focuses on identifying inven¬≠
tory states that deviate significantly from an ideal distribution, thus facilitating more targeted 
management actions. Last, we present an implementation example and proof of correctness.

4.1‚Äā Method
Our analysis utilizes a dataset comprising 85,659 products scraped from five major inter¬≠
national online fashion retailers (Mancosu & Vegetti, 2020). To protect partner anonymity, 
specific retailer identities and raw data are not disclosed. The scraping process leveraged the 
platforms' recommendation systems: for each product, metadata including price, descrip¬≠
tion, and colour were stored, along with a list of 'similar' or 'recommended' products gener¬≠
ated by the platform's algorithms.
Crucially, these recommendation links between products are interpreted as represent¬≠
ing observed consumer navigation paths or preference transitions. We use the frequency of 
transitions between products of different colours to estimate the empirical transition prob¬≠
abilities, P(s‚Ä≤|s), forming the basis of our Markov chain models. Here, state s represents 
the colour of the currently viewed product, and s‚Ä≤ represents the colour of a recommended 
product.
The states within the Markov chain models correspond to the set of¬† unique¬† distinct 
colours present in the dataset, meticulously mapped using the World Global Style Network 
(WGSN) colour codes (see Appendix A). This resulted in M distinct colour states (e.g., 
‚Äėblack‚Äô, ‚Äėnavy‚Äô, ‚Äėbeige‚Äô, etc.), forming the state space S for our MDP. The state definition is 
based on these discrete colour categories, not on their overall proportions in the raw product 
data. A preliminary analysis highlighted the dominant role of the colour ‚Äėblack‚Äô in shaping 
preference dynamics. Consequently, we adopted a dual analytical strategy by constructing 
two distinct Markov chain models:
 
‚óŹ
M1 (Original Model):¬†The transition probability matrix¬†P1¬†was estimated using the rec¬≠
ommendation links across¬†all¬†products and their identified colours (including 'black') in 
the dataset. This model captures the dynamics within the full colour spectrum.
 
‚óŹ
M2 (No-Black Model):¬†A second transition probability matrix¬†P2¬†was estimated. For 
this, we considered¬† only¬† the subset of recommendation links for which¬† neither¬† the 
source product¬†nor¬†the recommended product was 'black'. The transition frequencies 
were recalculated based¬†only¬†on these non-black interactions, and the resulting probabil¬≠
ities were normalized across the remaining colours. This model specifically isolates the 
preference dynamics¬†among the non-black colours¬†when 'black' is effectively removed 
from the choice set and its influence.
 
This approach of re-calculating transitions for M2, rather than simply removing the 
'black' row/column from¬†P1, ensures that the transition probabilities accurately reflect 
the relative preferences¬†between the remaining colours¬†in the hypothetical absence of 
the dominant 'black' option.

6.2‚Äā Limitations and future directions
Several limitations should be acknowledged. Firstly, the Markovian assumption of state 
transitions (colour choices depending only on the current colour) oversimplifies reality. 
1‚ÄĮ3

Actual consumer behavior involves memory, trends, and external factors not captured by this 
model since it is relying on aggregated transition data, is likely more suitable for capturing 
underlying, relatively stable preference dynamics between colours than for predicting rapid 
shifts driven by short-term fashion events or micro-trends. Furthermore, the model uses 
discrete time steps, and incorporating specific decision frequencies and real-time dynamics 
remains an area for future work. Secondly, SRAMO's assumption of sampling indepen¬≠

of the reward estimate via the Chernoff bound, simplifies the complex interdependencies 
and non-stationarity inherent in the overall RL process. While standard RL convergence 
relies on other conditions, the quality and stability of the reward estimate itself are impor¬≠
tant, and potential correlations in true demand shifts are overlooked by this local indepen¬≠
dence assumption. Thirdly, the¬†reliance on recommendation links¬†as a proxy for transitions 
introduces potential bias, as the recommendation algorithm itself shapes observed behavior. 
Fourthly, our comparative analysis, while showing SRAMO's superiority for the chosen 
‚Äėdeviation from uniform‚Äô metric against¬†classical Q-learning and a DQN baseline using a 
similar reward signal, does not guarantee superior performance under different objectives 
(e.g., direct profit maximization). Furthermore, the DQN comparison focused on algorith¬≠
mic effectiveness for the¬†balance¬†objective; comparing against DQN or other advanced 
algorithms optimized with potentially different reward structures remains an area for future 
work. The specific choice of aiming for a uniform distribution as an optimal state is a sim¬≠
plifying assumption for risk balancing in high-variety scenarios; the truly optimal distribu¬≠
tion might be non-uniform. Furthermore, the current reward structure does not incorporate 
potentially asymmetric costs associated with overstocking versus stockouts, which are typi¬≠
cal in fashion retail. Investigating the sensitivity of SRAMO to non-uniform target distri¬≠
butions and developing reward functions that explicitly account for asymmetric costs are 
crucial avenues for future research.
Future work could extend this research along several promising avenues. Applying 
SRAMO and SRIA to¬†other industries¬†(e.g., electronics, food) and¬†different product attri¬≠
butes¬†(e.g., style, material) would test their versatility. Integrating¬†real-time data streams¬†and 
advanced ML techniques could enhance the predictive power and responsiveness of the 
transition models. Exploring¬†non-Markovian process models¬†(e.g., using LSTMs or trans¬≠
formers) could capture more complex temporal dependencies in consumer behavior.
Methodologically, investigating¬†alternative reward functions¬†within SRAMO is crucial: 
for example, potentially incorporating direct cost/profit data, targeting specific non-uniform 
distributions, or dynamically adjusting the target based on external trend forecasts. Devel¬≠
oping¬†hybrid models¬†that integrate SRAMO's attribute-balancing policy with traditional 
quantity optimization algorithms could provide a more holistic inventory management solu¬≠
tion. Furthermore, explicitly modelling the¬†impact of retailer actions¬†(e.g., promotions, pric¬≠
ing) on colour transitions would add another layer of realism and control. Finally, this study 
demonstrates the potential of SRAMO and SRIA using real-world data, direct experimental 
validation of improved decision outcomes in a live retail application has not been performed 
and remains an important next step.
