Abstract
Free disposal hull (FDH) is a nonparametric model of production technology based on the
single assumption of free disposability of all inputs and outputs. In this paper, we consider
multicomponent production technologies in which every decision making unit (DMU) con-
sists of several parallel component processes that can in principle operate independently of
each other, provided they have sufﬁcient resources. An example is universities viewed as
DMUs, with their departments or groups of departments viewed as component processes.
Each component process uses its own set of inputs and an unknown part of the shared inputs
in order to produce its own set of outputs and an unknown part of the shared outputs. We
allow combinations of component processes taken from different DMUs in order to construct
new hypothetical DMUs, and refer to the resulting model of technology as the multicom-
ponent FDH (MFDH) model. We further develop a larger, and mathematically nontrivial,
variant of MFDH for the case in which we can specify certain bounds on the proportions in
which shared inputs and outputs are allocated to component processes. We use an illustrative
example in the context of universities to demonstrate the increasing discriminatory power of
the new MFDH models over the standard FDH models in the multicomponent setting.
Keywords Data envelopment analysis · Free disposal hull · Multicomponent · Universities
1 Introduction
Free disposal hull (FDH), as introduced by Deprins et al. (1984), is a model of production
technology whose only assumption is that all inputs and outputs are freely, or strongly,
disposable. Importantly, FDH does not assume that the technology is convex, which sets
it apart from convex technologies of data envelopment analysis (DEA). The latter include
the standard variable returns-to-scale (VRS) technology of Banker et al. (1984) and, under
the additional assumption of scalability (“ray unboundedness”), the constant returns-to-scale
(CRS) technology of Charnes et al. (1978).
The literature on FDH technologies has developed in several directions. The economic
meaning of FDH and the related notion of cost functions is explored by different authors—
see, e.g., Cherchye et al. (2000), Kerstens and Van de Woestyne (2021) and Kerstens et al.

Kerstens and Vanden Eeckaut (1999) introduce returns-to-scale (RTS) characterization of

and Kerstens (2006) and Kerstens and Van de Woestyne (2018). The latter include program-

1.1 Multicomponent technologies
In this paper, we consider production technologies consisting of several parallel component
processes. The inputs and outputs in such technologies are classed into component-speciﬁc
and shared measures. Each process uses its own speciﬁc inputs and an unknown to us pro-
portion of the shared inputs in order to produce its own speciﬁc outputs and an unknown part
of the shared outputs.
A deﬁning characteristic of a component process is that it itself represents a self-sufﬁcient
sub-technology which can operate independently of the other processes. The inputs and
outputs in such sub-technology are the measures speciﬁc to the given process and also the
parts of the shared inputs and outputs which are actually allocated but are unknown to us.
Examples of multicomponent technologies can be found in different sectors. Beasley
(1995) discusses the teaching and research components in the operation of universities. Podi-
novski (2022) uses a different characterization of universities as a combination of medical,
science and non-science groups of departments, with centralized costs and published papers
viewed as the shared input and output, respectively. Cook and Zhu (2006) identify sales of
ﬁnancial products and counter transactions as component processes in the banking sector.
Cook and Zhu (2011) consider special needs transportation, shuttle buses and other specialist
forms of transportation as components of bus operators. Silva (2018) considers component
processes in the operation of courts of justice based on the type of cases they deal with. In
the context of hotel industry, Walheer and Zhang (2018) identify accommodation, catering
and entertainment component processes.
It is worth noting that the described setting is conceptually different from the scenario
involving inputs that are jointly used by several component processes (Cherchye et al., 2013,
2016; Walheer, 2018). Examples of joint inputs are “public goods” that can be used simulta-
neously by all component processes, where any such usage does not affect access to them by
the other processes. As discussed and illustrated by an example by Podinovski et al. (2018),
declaring an input as joint (instead of allocated) leads to a signiﬁcant enlargement of the
technology. It is clear that modelling an input as a shared (allocated) and joint (unallocated)
measure is based on two different production assumptions, only one of which can be suitable
in any given application.

1.2 Related work on convex multicomponent technologies
The described multicomponent setting was explored by Podinovski (2022) for the develop-
ment of multicomponent VRS and CRS (MVRS and MCRS) technologies. In this convex
setting, the idea is to allow convex combinations of each component process independently
of the other processes. For example, in the context of higher education, Podinovski (2022)
allows separate convex combinations of the medical, science a non-science departments of
different universities, as opposed to convex combinations of universities as whole DMUs
allowed in the standard VRS model. Because the exact allocation of the shared inputs and
outputs to component processes is unknown, such shared measures for the convex combi-
nations of different processes cannot be deﬁned precisely. To address such uncertainty, the
approach of Podinovski (2022) allocates the maximum amount of shared inputs and minimum
amount of shared outputs that the described convex combinations may require, evaluated over
all possible allocations of the shared measures. This results in the minimal expansion of the
model of technology in which all DMUs are certainly producible regardless of the actual (but
unknown to us) allocation of the shared measures to component processes of the observed
DMUs. In other words, the MVRS and MCRS technologies are the smallest technologies that
are embedded in any corresponding MVRS and MCRS technology that would be deﬁned if
the allocation of shared measures to component processes were known.
Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023) provide a further reﬁnement to the MVRS and MCRS
models by assuming certain bounds on the proportions in which the shared inputs and out-
puts are allocated to component processes by each observed DMU. They use an illustrative
application to UK universities in which the proportion of shared inputs (centralized expenses)
and shared outputs (publications) of the medical, science and non-science groups of depart-
ments are approximately aligned with the relative size of the groups of departments within
the university. Such information reduces uncertainty about the actual allocation of the shared
inputs and outputs to component processes. This in turn improves (makes more demanding)
the combined DMUs which, by construction, are producible for any possible, but unknown
to us, allocation of the shared measures, within the stated bounds. As a result, the MVRS
and MCRS technologies with restricted allocations of the shared measures developed by
Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023) are generally larger than the unrestricted models of
Podinovski (2022).
The MVRS and MCRS technologies (both the original unrestricted models and the models
based on restricted allocations of the shared measures) are larger than the standard VRS
and CRS technologies, respectively. As shown by the applications in Podinovski (2022) and
Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023), the MVRS and MCRS models provide noticeably better
discrimination on efﬁciency than the standard VRS and CRS models.
1.3 Contribution
The purpose of this paper is to extend the FDH model of technology to the multicompo-
nent setting. We refer to such technology as the multicomponent FDH (MFDH) technology.
This development is aligned with the idea of convex MVRS technologies which allows
independent convex combinations of individual production processes of different DMUs.
Because the FDH model excludes the assumption of convexity, we replace the latter by the
assumption that whole component processes of different DMUs (representing self-sufﬁcient
sub-technologies) can be combined to obtain new hypothetical DMUs.

We provide a rigorous axiomatic development of the MFDH technology. In addition to
the axiom of free disposability, the MFDH technology also requires the axioms that deﬁne
combinations of component processes, taking into account uncertainty about the allocation
of the shared inputs and outputs. We treat such uncertainty using the most conservative
approach to the expansion of multicomponent technology developed by Podinovski (2022)
and its further reﬁnement of Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023). Namely, we deﬁne new
hypothetical DMUs by combining whole processes taken from different DMUs, provided
each process is allocated the maximum amount of the shared input and the minimum amount
of the shared output which are possible within the speciﬁed allocation bounds. Therefore,
the resulting combined DMUs should be technologically feasible regardless of the actual,
but unknown to us, allocation of the shared measures to component processes.
The mathematics of the formal derivation of the MFDH technology from the stated axioms
is not trivial. This task is simpliﬁed because much of this development, including the proofs,
is similar, but not identical, to the development of the MVRS model.
It is easy to show that the new MFDH models are larger than the standard FDH technology
and include the latter as a subset. However, it is also clear that the suggested approach to
the deﬁnition of new combined DMUs is very conservative. Such new DMUs require large
amounts of shared inputs and produce little of the shared outputs (none of the shared outputs
if we do not specify bounds on the allocation of shared outputs to component processes).
Therefore, a valid question arises if the MFDH models give us any advantage in the evaluation
of efﬁciency of DMUs in the multicomponent setting, compared to the standard FDH model.
We give a positive answer to this question by considering an illustrative application to a
sample of 57 UK universities whose science and non-science departments are regarded as
the two component processes. Computations show that MFDH models (especially the models
with speciﬁed bounds on the allocation of shared measures) provide better discrimination on
efﬁciency than the FDH model.
We proceed as follows. In Sect. 2, we introduce basic notation and speciﬁcation of mul-
ticomponent technologies. In Sect. 3, we develop the MFDH model for multicomponent
technologies assuming that we have no information about the allocation of shared inputs
and outputs to component processes. In Sects. 4, 5, 6, we develop the MFDH model which
incorporates speciﬁed bounds on the allocation proportions of shared measures. In Sect. 7, we
explore the structure of the MFDH technology and provide its simple graphical illustration.
In Sect. 8, we consider an illustrative application of the MFDH models in the context of
higher education. Section 9 contains concluding remarks. All mathematical proofs are given
in “Appendix A”.

2.2 The multicomponent setting
Suppose that technology T consists of K ≥2 component processes. (If K = 1, all new mod-
els developed in this paper become the standard FDH technology TFDH.) Every component
process k ∈K = {1, . . . , K} is associated with its own subsets of component-speciﬁc inputs
and outputs Ik and Ok, respectively. We also generally have the set IS of shared inputs used
by all component processes and the set OS of shared outputs produced by all component
processes. We assume that the allocation of shared inputs and outputs to component processes
is unknown.
Component-speciﬁc inputs and outputs do not need to be physically different for different
component processes k. The essential assumption here is that we know their exact allocation
to each process. An example is component-speciﬁc costs which may be viewed as total costs
whose allocation to each component is known.
In the illustrative application in Sect. 8, we consider universities as DMUs consisting of
science and non-science groups of departments regarded as the two components. Because we
know the speciﬁc departmental expenses and students for either component, we treat them
as component-speciﬁc inputs and outputs, respectively. However, in the same application,
because we do not know the exact allocation of centralized expenditure and published papers,
we treat these measures as shared.

3 MFDH technology with unrestricted allocations of shared measures
We assume that each component process k ∈K is a self-sufﬁcient sub-technology. Namely,
it uses its speciﬁc inputs Ik and an unknown to us part of the shared inputs i ∈IS in order
to produce its speciﬁc outputs Ok and an unknown part of the shared outputs OS. The only
links between the different component processes are the shared inputs and outputs whose
allocation to component processes is unknown.
Suppose ﬁrst that there are no shared inputs and outputs. In this case, the component
processes function independently of each other. We can now argue that it is reasonable
to mix and match different processes taken from different DMUs in order to obtain new
hypothetically possible production units.
An obvious problem with this approach arises if the sets of shared inputs or outputs are
not empty and their exact allocations to component processes are unknown. We address
this uncertainty using the most cautious approach of Podinovski (2022) to the deﬁnition of
combined DMUs, leading to the expanded model of technology. Namely, to guarantee that a
component of some DMU A entering the combination has sufﬁcient resources, we allocate
to it all of the shared inputs of DMU A and do not expect it to produce any of the shared
outputs.
To illustrate this approach, consider the following simple example.
Example 1 Table 1 shows DMUs A and B operating in a technology with two component
processes. The inputs X1 and X2, and outputs Y 1 and Y 2 are speciﬁc to component processes
1 and 2, while the input XS and output Y S are shared measures.
DMU C is a hypothetical DMU obtained as a combination of process 1 of DMU A and
process 2 of DMU B. Note that we do not know what proportion of the shared input of DMU
A is actually used by its process 1. In the worst case that we need to take into account, it may
be using all, or almost all, of it. To account for all possible allocations, we attribute all of this
shared input to component 1. Similarly, we do not know what proportion of the shared input
is actually used by process 2 of DMU B. Therefore, to account for all possible allocations,
we attribute all of this shared input to component 2 of DMU B. In total, the sum of shared
inputs of DMUs A and B, which is equal to 1 + 1 = 2, should be sufﬁcient for the combined
DMU C to operate.
We treat the shared output in a similar way. Because we do not know the actual proportion
of such output produced by either component 1 of DMU A and by component 2 of DMU B,
the only safe assumption is that both are equal to zero. This means that the combined DMU
C produces zero of the shared output Y S.
It is reasonable to assume that DMU C should be regarded as producible. Note that such
DMU is not included in the standard FDH technology.

Remark 2 The deﬁnition of DMU C in Example 1 assumes that the individual component
processes of observed DMUs A and B use all of their shared inputs and produce none of their
shared outputs. This may be regarded as an unrealistic assumption, resulting in the combined
DMU C which uses an excessive amount of the shared inputs and produces none of the shared
outputs. Note that, although the above assumption may be unrealistically conservative, the
combined DMU C based on it is certainly realistic and should be included in the model of
technology. Let us discuss this in detail.
We may judge that, in reality, the individual component processes of observed DMUs
A and B should be allocated at least some proportion of their shared inputs and outputs,
which would result in a more demanding combined DMU C. We consider such scenario in
Sects. 4, 5, 6. However, we currently do not assume any knowledge of the allocation of the
shared measures to component processes. Therefore, any reduction of the shared input and
any increase of the shared output of DMU C deﬁned in Example 1 will be unsubstantiated
and not based on the available information about the actual allocation of shared measures.
It is likely that the combined DMU C is excessively conservative. However, it is the only
DMU whose technological feasibility we can guarantee, given the lack of information about
the allocation of shared inputs and outputs. This DMU is always technologically realistic
because it is dominated by any other combined DMU that uses less of the shared inputs and
produces more of the shared outputs, whose exact levels are assumed unavailable.
Because DMU C is producible for any (unknown to us) allocation of the shared inputs
and outputs to component processes of DMUs A and B, we can add DMU C to the model
of technology. This will represent the minimal expansion of the technology consistent with
the available information about the possible allocations.

4 Restricting allocation proportions for the shared inputs and outputs
In developing the MFDH technology T 0
MFDH in Sect. 3, we assumed that we have no informa-
tion about the allocation of the shared inputs and outputs to component processes. Therefore,
in constructing the combined DMUs in this technology, we had to account for the most con-
servative possibility that any single process may consume all, or almost all, of the shared
inputs and produce none, or almost none, of the shared outputs.
Let us now assume that we can specify certain bounds on the proportions in which the
shared inputs and outputs can be allocated to the component processes. It turns out that such
information allows us to deﬁne less conservative (more demanding) composite DMUs that
are more valuable additions to the model of technology than the composite DMUs obtained
in the original unrestricted case. We ﬁrst illustrate this idea by the following example.
Example 2 As a modiﬁcation of Example 1, assume that it is known that each process 1 and
2 of both DMUs A and B uses between 20 and 80% of the total amount of the shared input
and produces between 20 and 80% of the shared output of the respective DMU. Let us show
how this assumption changes the calculation of the combined DMU.
If, as before, we combine process 1 of DMU A with process 2 of DMU B, each may
require up to 80% of the shared input of the respective DMU, which is equal to 1. Therefore,
the amount 0.8+0.8 = 1.6 of the shared input is sufﬁcient for the combined DMU. Similarly,
the minimal levels of the shared output that process 1 of DMU A and process 2 of DMU B
produce are each equal to 20% of 1, i.e., 0.2. Therefore, the combined DMU should be able
to produce at least 0.2 + 0.2 = 0.4 of the shared output.
The resulting combined DMU D is shown in Table 2. It has the same component-speciﬁc
inputs and outputs as the combined DMU C deﬁned in Example 1. However, its shared input
is lower than the shared input of DMU C, and its shared output is greater than that of DMU C.
Therefore, DMU D weakly dominates DMU C. The incorporation of DMU D in the model of
technology leads to a greater extension of the technology than the incorporation of DMU C.

7 The structure of MFDH technology
The standard FDH technology TFDH consists of the n observed DMUs (X j, Y j), j ∈J , and
the DMUs weakly dominated by them. In other words, technology TFDH is the union on the
n free disposal hulls generated by the observed DMUs.
Now consider the multicomponent MFDH technology with restricted allocations of shared
inputs and outputs TMFDH given by its statement (16). (As highlighted in Remark 6, this
includes the unrestricted MFDH technology T 0
MFDH as a special case.) By choosing differ-
ent vectors λk with binary components only one of which is nonzero for every component
k ∈K, we deﬁne different combined DMUs ( ˜X, ˜Y), which appear on the left-hand side of
inequalities (16a)–(16d).
Because each of the K components of the combined DMU can be taken from any of
the n observed DMUs, overall there are n × K different combined DMUs (including the n
observed DMUs as a special case) that can be obtained by their statement (14). As follows
from statement (16), the multicomponent technology TMFDH consists of all n × K combined
DMUs and the DMUs weakly dominated by them.

8.1 Bounds on the allocation of shared measures
The MFDH models with restricted allocations of the shared inputs and outputs developed in
Sect. 4 require that we specify the lower and upper bounds (9) and (10) on such allocations.
Following the approach of Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023), we assume that the actual
allocation should be approximately in line with the share (relative size) of the science and
non-science groups of departments within each university.
To evaluate the relative sizes of both groups of departments within each university, we
consider the input side ﬁrst, by calculating the total departmental expenditures (inputs 1
and 2 taken together). We subsequently identify the share of each group of departments in
this total (e.g., the proportion of input 1 in the total of the two inputs). We then repeat the same
procedure and identify the share of each group of departments with respect to the students.
We ﬁnally take the simple average of the two shares (share of departmental expenditures
and share of students in the university total) as a measure of relative size of the science and
non-science departments. Calculations show that such shares vary signiﬁcantly, from 0.02
(2%) to 0.98 (98%), depending on the university.
Although the exact allocation of centralized expenditures and published papers to the sci-
ence and non-science departments is unknown, we assume that these should be approximately
in line with their relative size within the university. For example, much of the centralized
expenditures are incurred by the central human resources, ﬁnance, student support and other
similar services that should be in some proportion to the number of staff and students. Simi-
larly, given the expectations that members of staff publish at a certain rate (generally different

9 Conclusion
In this paper, we develop an extension of the FDH model of technology (Deprins et al., 1984)
to the setting in which the technology consists of several component processes. We assume
that each process constitutes an independent sub-technology with its own set of component-
speciﬁc inputs and outputs. We also allow the use of shared inputs and production of shared
outputs whose allocation to component processes is unknown.
The basic idea of the proposed extension is the following. As long as each component
is an independent sub-technology, it can by deﬁnition operate independently of the other
component processes, provided it is given a sufﬁcient proportion of the shared inputs and is
not expected to produce too much of the shared outputs. Under this assumption, we can mix
component processes taken from different DMUs and add the resulting combined DMUs to
the technology. We refer to such model as the multicomponent FDH (MFDH) technology.
123

1584
Annals of Operations Research (2025) 351:1559–1587
The difﬁculty with this approach arises because we do not know the exact allocation of the
shared inputs and outputs to the component processes. We address this problem by utilizing
the most conservative approach to the expansion of technology developed by Podinovski
(2022) for a similar deﬁnition of multicomponent VRS technology. Namely, in the simplest
form of this approach, we assume that a component process of any DMU j can operate
independently (and be mixed with components of the other DMUs), provided it is allocated
all of the shared inputs and produces none of the shared outputs of the DMU j.
WenextadaptamorecomplexapproachdevelopedbyPapaioannouandPodinovski(2023)
for the case of multicomponent VRS models. Namely, we assume that we can specify certain
lower and upper bounds for the unknown allocation proportions of the shared measures.
Utilizing such information leads to a reduction of the levels of shared inputs and increase
of the levels of shared outputs of the hypothetical DMUs obtained by combining production
processes taken from different observed DMUs. This in turn leads to a larger model of
multicomponent technology.
Similar to the standard FDH model, the new MFDH models do not incorporate the assump-
tion of convexity and are based only on the assumption of free disposability. Structurally,
they represent free disposal hulls generated not only by the observed DMUs (as in the stan-
dard FDH model) but also by all possible combined DMUs obtained by mixing component
processes taken from different observed DMUs.
We demonstrate the use of the new MFDH models in an illustrative application to a
sample of UK universities. In line with theory, computational results show that the new
MFDH models have generally greater discriminating power on efﬁciency than the standard
FDH model, but the difference between the models depends on the assessment scenario. Such
improvement is achieved only by the information that the technology consists of independent
production processes and, optionally, leading to a greater improvement, by the incorporation
of bounds on the allocation proportions of the shared inputs and outputs.
As shown by Podinovski (2022), Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023) and the current
paper, the assumption that the technology consists of several component processes can be
used in both convex and nonconvex models, and often leads to their noticeable enlargement,
compared to the conventional single-process VRS, CRS and FDH technologies. It should
be expected that many other existing models of production technologies could be extended
by taking into account the multicomponent structure of the production process. Such exten-
sions may require revisiting the axiomatic foundations of the existing models and restating
them for the multicomponent setting. For example, the axioms of multicomponent convexity
and scalability utilized by Podinovski (2022) and the axioms of feasibility of component
combinations introduced in this paper represent signiﬁcant departures from the standard
single-process production assumptions.
An interesting question that requires further research is whether there exists a simpler
way to linearize statement (16) of the MFDH technology than the proposed use of the dual
models (17) and (18) for the evaluation of optimal values  j and ¯ j, resulting in the relatively
complex statement of technology (19). If available, such simpliﬁed approach should lead to
an equivalent but intuitively clearer statement and reduced computational complexity of the
MFDH models with restricted allocations of the shared measures.
Further research is needed for the development of cost functions in a multicomponent
setting, in a way extending their exploration for single-component technologies by Briec et
al. (2004) and Kerstens and Van de Woestyne (2021). Such extensions would be of interest
in the cases of convex MVRS and nonconvex MFDH multicomponent technologies.
A further potential research avenue is the evaluation of scale efﬁciency and returns to
scale in the MFDH technologies based on the direction to the most productive scale size. The
123

Annals of Operations Research (2025) 351:1559–1587
1585
notion of RTS has already been explored in the convex multicomponent MVRS technologies
by Podinovski (2022) and Papaioannou and Podinovski (2023), and in the conventional
FDH technology—see, e.g., Kerstens and Vanden Eeckaut (1999), Podinovski (2004a) and
Cesaroni et al. (2017). Addressing the notions of scale in the MFDH technology requires
further research which is left outside the scope of this paper.
