Abstract
In the realm of operating room management (ORM), several key challenges loom large,
including the reduction of overtime and idle time for surgeons, avoiding overutilization and
underutilization of ORs, and the equitable allocation of patients to surgeons to mitigate work-
load pressures. Moreover, the potential disruption risks associated with surgeons can lead
to surgery cancellations. This research presents a comprehensive framework for addressing
these challenges through a uniﬁed approach encompassing capacity planning and equitable
scheduling within surgery departments. An essential component of this approach involves
the identiﬁcation of backup surgeons to minimize the surgery cancellations risk attributable
to surgeon unavailability. The capacity planning phase is executed in the initial stage, lever-
aging Markovian queueing systems to optimize resource allocation. In the second stage, a
resilient scheduling model is introduced, considering the equitable assignment of patients
to surgeons. This scheduling model is developed using a goal programming approach and
is solved efﬁciently using a novel decomposition-based heuristic method designed to expe-
dite the optimal solution attainment. To validate the efﬁcacy of this innovative approach, a
real-world case study is undertaken, showcasing its practical application and demonstrating
its proﬁciency in addressing the complex challenges inherent in ORM, followed by some
valuable managerial insights. By implementing the proposed approach, the optimal number
of operating rooms is obtained from the preceding queueing model in the capacity planning
phase, and the effect of changing surgeon numbers is analyzed. Results show that optimiz-
ing unit capacity improves surgery scheduling and demonstrates the proposed framework’s
efﬁciency.

1 Introduction
In contemporary healthcare administration, administrators confront signiﬁcant challenges
stemming from resource constraints, budget limitations, and deﬁciencies in both equipment
and a qualiﬁed workforce, as noted by Mohammadi et al., (2022). Among the various depart-
ments within medical facilities, surgery departments and operating rooms (ORs) consistently
incur the highest costs (Lamiri et al., 2008), while concurrently representing the primary
revenue generators for hospitals (Al-Refaie, 2018). The optimization of operating room
scheduling (ORS) assumes paramount importance in the overarching management of health-
care institutions, given its profound implications for patient care and staff productivity. It is
worth noting that underutilization of ORs results in avoidable costs (Rahimi & Gandomi,
2021), whereas a judiciously planned ORS protocol promotes the efﬁcient utilization of ORs,
thus reducing patient waiting times and optimizing resource utilization. As such, strategic
decisions concerning operational and capacity policies become imperative to ensure timely
patient access and operational efﬁciency (Van Riet & Demeulemeester, 2015). Furthermore,
equitable patient assignment to surgeons underscores the importance of fairness to surgical
staff, a goal that can be achieved by minimizing deviations from established standard working
hours.
Following surgical procedures, patients typically require transportation to various down-
stream units within the healthcare facility. These post-surgical units, such as the post-
anesthesia care unit (PACU), the intensive care unit (ICU), and the general ward, effectively
form a network of interconnected queues, each featuring distinct server characteristics (Fer-
dinandes et al., 2017). In this context, the application of queueing systems (QSs) emerges as
a valuable tool for the purpose of capacity planning across the diverse units within medical
centers (Gökalp & Sanci, 2023). These models serve a dual role, not only averting patient con-
gestion but also mitigating patient wait times. Furthermore, it’s imperative to acknowledge
that ORs must accommodate two distinct categories of patients, as highlighted by Rahimi
and Gandomi (2021). One of the central challenges inherent in ORS pertains to the delicate
balance that must be struck between emergency cases and elective procedures. Achieving this
equilibrium hinges on the effective implementation of capacity planning strategies. Moreover,
in accordance with the ﬁndings of Van Riet and Demeulemeester (2015), the segregation of
elective and emergency patient ﬂows can yield substantial advantages by curbing ﬂow vari-
ability, a factor known to precipitate extended waiting times for patients.
The issue of surgery cancellations remains a pervasive challenge that many hospitals have
yet to effectively resolve (Cho et al., 2019). The consequences of cancelled surgeries reverber-
ate through the healthcare system, resulting in the underutilization of ORs, surgeon resources,
and other critical hospital assets (Erekat et al., 2020). Staff-related reasons, including those
stemming from surgeons, contribute signiﬁcantly to the occurrence of surgery cancellations.
The identiﬁcation and designation of backup surgeons offer a promising strategy for mit-
igating the disruptive impact of surgeon-initiated cancellations, ultimately bolstering the
resilience of OR schedules. In light of these considerations, this study endeavors to address
the following questions:
• How can the application of queueing systems in OR management facilitate the deter-
mination of the optimal number of servers within ORs and downstream units, thereby
maximizing server utilization rates?
• In what manner can quantitative methodologies, speciﬁcally mathematical modeling, be
harnessed to effectively manage both idle time and overtime experienced by surgeons and
ORs while concurrently ensuring equitable patient assignment to surgeons?

This study proposes a two-phase framework to improve the management of operating
rooms (ORs) in hospital environments. In the ﬁrst phase, we concentrate on capacity planning
by addressing the arrival rates of both elective and emergency patients while also considering
resource limitations. These constraints are thoroughly analyzed to optimize the allocation of
servers, aiming to minimize patient waiting times and reduce the likelihood of server idleness.
In the subsequent second phase, we present an innovative Operating Room Scheduling (ORS)
model. This model is dedicated to ensuring fair scheduling practices for surgeons, thereby
preventing overtime and idle periods for them, as well as avoiding the overuse or underuse
of ORs. Importantly, the optimal number of servers determined in the ﬁrst phase is utilized
in this second phase of OR planning.
In summary, this study makes several notable contributions, including:
• Introducing an all-encompassing dual-phase framework that encompasses both capacity
planning through the utilization of queuing systems and the scheduling of surgical proce-
dures.
• The enhancement of ORS resilience through the identiﬁcation of backup surgeons.
• The consideration of varying expertise levels among surgeons, accounting for variable
surgery durations.
• The establishment of a time slot-based sequence for surgeries.
• The achievement of equitable patient assignment to surgeons, coupled with the effective
management of surgeon and OR idle time and overtime through a Goal Programming (GP)
approach.

Sect. 8. Conclusion, limitations, and future directions are provided in Sect. 9.
2 Literature review
Within this section, we delve into a comprehensive examination of the key scholarly works
pertaining to the ﬁeld of Operating Room Scheduling (ORS). Numerous research endeavors
have delved into the intricacies of the ORS problem (ORSP), underscoring its signiﬁcance
within the broader healthcare domain, particularly in hospital settings. Notably, due to the
inherent complexity of ORS models, a multitude of scholarly papers have devised diverse
solution methodologies. These approaches encompass the utilization of techniques such
as Lagrangian relaxation, branch-and-price methods, various heuristics, as well as meta-
heuristics, all of which have been meticulously explored within this section. Furthermore,
we take an in-depth look at select papers that have harnessed Queueing Systems (QSs) within
the healthcare domain, followed by an analysis aimed at elucidating the existing research
gaps in this domain.
In the realm of ORS, a multitude of research endeavors have contributed signiﬁcantly
to addressing various challenges and optimizing scheduling processes. Jung et al. (2019)

ﬁrst phase involved the assignment of surgical operations to speciﬁc ORs, while the second
phase focused on sequencing these allocated operations to enhance OR utilization. Resource-
related constraints and procedural conditions were factored into their model. Augusto et al.
(2010) proposed a scheduling and planning model with deterministic parameters, leveraging
the Lagrangian method for optimization. Yin and Xiang (2012) harnessed the Ant Colony
Optimization (ACO) algorithm, considering interrelated units and the allocation of multi-
ple surgeons to a single operation. Van Huele and Vanhoucke (2015) employed the Goal
Programming (GP) approach to formulate a uniﬁed framework for surgery scheduling and
physician rostering planning. Li et al. (2017) investigated a Mixed Integer Programming
(MIP) model for elective surgery scheduling, incorporating accessibility to surgeons and
ORs. Their GP-based approach aimed to optimize various objectives, including the expected
number of patients waiting for service, OR underutilization, and patient ﬂow in the recovery
unit. Al-Refaie et al. (2018) introduced two ORS models, addressing ICU and OR scheduling
over multiple time periods. These models encompassed cost-effective scheduling and patient
sequencing. Gür et al. (2019) sought to balance equipment and resource utilization in hos-
pital ORs, employing both GP and constraint programming methods. Barrera et al. (2020)
utilized a dynamic programming (DP) model under stochastic conditions to minimize referral
costs in surgery scheduling. They also proposed a heuristic approach for efﬁcient solutions.
Kayvanfar et al. (2021) developed an open strategy ORS model to minimize OR idle time
and maximize the number of planned operations, employing binary programming and mixed
integer binary models for OR and recovery room management. Rahimi and Gandomi (2021)
conducted a comprehensive review and analysis of ORSPs, comparing various solution tech-
niques and suggesting future research directions. Keyvanshokooh et al. (2022) tackled the
problem of scheduling clinic and surgery appointments for surgical patients. They aimed to
ensure timely access to care by coordinating these appointments so that patients could see
a surgeon and schedule surgery within a maximum wait time. They addressed uncertainties
like the number of appointment requests, whether surgery is needed, and surgery durations.
They developed an integrated multi-stage stochastic and distributionally robust optimiza-
tion approach. They proposed a data-driven rolling horizon procedure for implementation.
Hashemi Doulabi and Khalilpourazari (2023) tackled a stochastic OR planning problem, aim-
ing to reduce ﬁxed OR opening costs and expected overtime costs while enhancing model
efﬁciency with fewer variables and constraints, incorporating valid inequalities. Shehadeh
and Padman (2022) examined elective ORS through a stochastic optimization lens, consid-
ering downstream capacity constraints. Arab Momeni et al. (2022) addressed the activation
of ORs, patient assignments (including COVID-19 considerations), operation scheduling
with pandemic guidelines, and specialty crew allocation. Azar et al. (2022) proposed a time-
indexed scheduling approach for ORS, introducing chance constraints based on surgery
duration probability distributions. Bargetto et al. (2023) tackled an integrated OR planning
and scheduling problem, accounting for due dates, capacity limitations, sequencing, and addi-
tional human resources. They also incorporated a sequence-dependent model for OR cleaning

scheduling and block assignment using a two-stage stochastic program and an online phase to
manage emergency insertions and cancellations. The proposed approach, which incorporates
a convex piecewise linear surrogate model and a greedy online policy, signiﬁcantly reduces
expected costs (up to 30%) compared to heuristic methods while efﬁciently solving instances
with up to 1000 patients in about one minute.
The Queuing Systems (QSs) in the planning of operating rooms have been underutilized
as an efﬁcient tool. As an illustration, Aghsami et al., (2023) harnessed Markov chains in
the context of a blood transfusion network. Ferdinandes et al. (2017) optimized accident and
emergency units, employing an M/M/3 model. In another context, Hall et al., (2023) delved
into the progression of COVID-19 in the United States, leveraging QSs. Their study involved
a comparison of methodologies for approximating actual infection and mortality rates, offer-
ing insights into the timing and dynamics of COVID-19 across different locations. Rathore
(2023) adopted an M/M/C model to effectively manage bed occupancy, determine ideal
bed counts, and facilitate the movement of patients within hospital facilities. Additionally,
Rathore explored the probability of selecting a particular patient for bed allocation. Rahi-
minia et al. (2023a) also drew upon QSs to address patient congestion and waste management
under epidemic conditions.
In light of the extensive literature review, it becomes evident that the Operating Room
Scheduling Problem (ORSP) is gaining increasing attention, primarily due to the pivotal role
of surgeries within the healthcare system and their profound impact on hospital performance.
Published studies within this domain strive to optimize OR capacities, resources, timing,
and associated costs. Furthermore, QSs have found applications in healthcare scenarios to
manage patient wait times and enhance server utilization (Zonderland & Boucherie, 2012).
It’s noteworthy that some literature exists where QSs have been employed for department
scheduling in medical centers beyond ORs, exempliﬁed by the work of Long et al., (2020).
However, one aspect conspicuously absent in prior research efforts is the emphasis on
enhancing the resilience of ORS, which is of paramount importance. A promising approach

3 Problem description
The examination of prior sections underscores the pivotal role of capacity planning and
advanced scheduling in the comprehensive management of surgery departments, both of
which are examined in an integrated fashion within this study. This integrated approach
holds the potential to signiﬁcantly enhance the operational efﬁciency of medical centers.
Hospitals encompass a multitude of interconnected units, interrelated by the patient treat-
ment process. The arrival and departure rates of upstream units wield a direct inﬂuence on
the arrival rate of downstream units, establishing a network of queues within medical cen-
ters. The utilization of queueing system relationships becomes instrumental in optimizing
service rates and calculating queueing measures. Given the resource constraints in hospitals,
including equipment and space limitations, it becomes imperative to identify bottlenecks and
determine the optimal number of servers for each unit. Such measures serve to enhance the
productivity of hospital personnel, curbing congestion and patient wait times.
In this study, the queuing dynamics of both ORs and downstream units are considered in
the context of capacity planning, elucidated in phase one. It’s worth noting that the number of
ORs, as determined during the capacity planning phase, exerts a substantial impact on subse-
quent outcomes. Post capacity planning, the scheduling of various units becomes essential.
Scheduling within ORs is inherently intricate due to the multitude of constraints character-
izing surgery departments. Within the ORS phase, effective scheduling must account for the
working hours of surgeons and ORs to minimize idle time and overtime, thereby averting
additional costs. The pursuit of a fair scheduling regime, which optimally assigns patients
to surgeons to balance surgeons’ workloads, is essential to maximize surgical team util-
ity. Moreover, the ORS model must also incorporate resilience considerations to mitigate
surgery cancellation rates attributable to surgeon-related disruptions. One strategy to achieve
this involves the identiﬁcation of backup surgeons for surgical procedures. The comprehen-
sive ORS model introduced herein, detailed in phase two, encompasses all these critical
aspects.
In this research, a novel two-phase approach has been devised to address the multifaceted
challenge at hand. First, the study leverages Queuing Systems (QSs) for capacity planning,
with a speciﬁc focus on utilizing Markovian models to determine the optimal number of
servers in both ORs and downstream units. Subsequently, the research presents a novel
mathematical model for ORS, aimed at yielding an optimal surgical schedule by optimizing
surgeon and OR service times. The proposed methodology is visually depicted in Fig. 1.

3.1 Phase one
Within the literature on OR management, Post-Anesthesia Care Units (PACUs), Intensive
Care Units (ICUs), and wards are commonly classiﬁed as downstream units in relation to ORs
(Rahimi & Gandomi, 2021). Elective patients occupy the ORs and surgeons with previous
planning, and emergency patients are allocated to the ORs of hospitals without any earlier
information. As depicted in Fig. 2, a stream of both elective and emergency patients accesses
the medical center. While the arrival rate of elective patients is a known constant, the arrival
rate of emergency patients remains a variable dependent on the number of available ORs and
unoccupied surgical staff.
Consequently, we have distinct queues of elective and emergency patients awaiting service
within the medical centers. Following surgical procedures, both patient groups necessitate
transfer to PACUs. Subsequently, a subset of patients may require admission to ICUs, with
all patients eventually spending a period in the general wards. In summation, queues of
patients emerge within PACUs, ICUs, and wards, reﬂecting various stages of their care
journey. In these units, beds can be considered servers of queueing models. In accordance
with Rahiminia et al., (2023b), patient arrivals at the medical center conform to a Poisson
distribution. Leveraging M/M/C models, one can derive the optimal number of servers
within these constituent segments. The ensuing optimization model seeks to ascertain the
optimal server count for each segment, mindful of ﬁnite server capacity constraints. Notably,
this model grapples with the inherent tradeoff between server idle time and patient wait times,
a balance addressed by the proposed framework. According to Gross (2008), and Zonderland
and Boucherie (2012), these M/M/C queues can exist in a network if the arrival of each queue
is lower than the total service rate. In this case, the departure rate of each queue is equal to
the arrival rate.

In M/M/C models, customer arrivals adhere to a Poisson distribution with a rate denoted
as λ. Within this framework, there exist C servers, each independent and identically dis-

2008). When the cumulative service rate across all units surpasses the arrival rate, customer
departures align with the arrival rate, underpinning the relationships between arrival rates as
delineated in Fig. 2 and encapsulated by Eqs. (1) and (2). In other words, Eqs. (1) and (2)
are valid when the time duration of arrival is equal to or lower than the total service rate
of servers which is assumed in the queueing model. Therefore, the departure rate of each
queueing model is equal to the arrival rate.

3.2 Phase two
In this phase, we introduce a novel approach to the Operating Room Scheduling Problem
(ORSP) employing the Goal Programming (GP) methodology. The distinctive feature of this
approach lies in its capacity to address multiple objectives, often characterized by conﬂicting
priorities. Each of these objectives is ascribed a predeﬁned target value that serves as the
benchmark for attainment, and deviations from these targets, whether exceeding or falling
short, are quantiﬁed. The overarching aim of the objective function is to minimize undesirable
deviationsfromthispredeﬁnedsetoftargetvalues.Withinthepurviewofthisresearch,several
key objectives are considered. Firstly, the objective seeks to establish an equitable scheduling
framework for surgeons, with the overarching goal of approximating standard working hours
on a daily basis, while concurrently minimizing both idle time and overtime. Secondly, it
endeavors to ensure the uniform utilization of each operating room, sidestepping scenarios
of both overutilization and underutilization. The proposed GP model accomplishes this by
facilitating the equitable assignment of patients to surgeons. Furthermore, our approach
extends to the identiﬁcation of backup surgeons for each patient, a strategic measure designed
to mitigate the risks associated with surgeon disruptions that could potentially lead to the
cancellation of scheduled surgeries.
3.2.1 Assumptions
The proposed model operates under the following foundational assumptions:
• Planning horizon: The planning horizon encompasses a one-week timeframe.
• Working hours: Each day is characterized by 8 working hours, translating to a total of 16
time slots allocated for each day. These time slots serve to delineate the hourly details and
sequencing of surgical procedures.
• Comprehensive scheduling: All patients, without exception, are subject to scheduling
within the model.
123

• Known surgery times: The durations of surgeries are known and accessible for incorpora-
tion into the scheduling process.
• Variable surgery durations: It is acknowledged that the duration of a patient’s surgery may
vary based on the surgeon assigned, primarily due to disparities in the expertise levels of
surgeons.
• Specialized surgeons: Surgeons are exclusively tasked with operating on patients within
their respective areas of professional expertise.
• Equipment availability: Since elective patients are being investigated and due to the com-
mitment of the investigated case study, it is assumed that adequate equipment is readily
available in ORs for the successful completion of every surgical procedure.
• Initial OR conditions: At the commencement of the week, all ORs are vacant and devoid
of ongoing procedures.
• Pre-determined OR count: The number of available ORs is determined as an outcome of
the preceding phase.
• Server allocation: The model is conﬁgured to ascertain the optimal allocation of servers
in each unit while simultaneously accommodating capacity constraints pertaining to the
maximum allowable number of servers in each unit and the total server count across all
units.
• Setup times: The setup time of each surgery is considered in the duration of surgeries.

5 Case study and results
This section entails the practical application of the proposed approach outlined in this study
to a real-world case study. Initially, the salient characteristics of the case study are expounded
upon, followed by the subsequent presentation of the obtained results.
This study involved collaboration with a major mid-size general hospital in Spain to obtain
the necessary data for implementing the proposed approach. In the selected case study, the
dataset focuses on 40 patients and 8 surgeons over a 7-day period. Each day is divided into
12 one-hour time slots, providing a structured framework for scheduling. While the complete
dataset includes 3,713 cases from the ﬁrst six months of 2022, a subset of data covering 7
days with 40 patients was chosen to enable optimal problem-solving within the computational
constraints.
The collected data were used to deﬁne the input parameters for the queueing model,
with the resulting outputs seamlessly integrated into the scheduling model. Additionally,
a representative dataset was selected to support and validate the scheduling model. Table

servers, and the optimal number of servers designated for each unit.
The solicitation of the arrival and service rates of these units was made in consultation
with healthcare experts from the hospital, based on a predeﬁned time interval of twelve
hours. Additionally, the prescribed upper bound for servers within each unit and the collective
maximum server capacity across all units are speciﬁed, amounting to 15 and 60, respectively.
These upper bounds were determined based on simultaneous consideration of space and
equipment capacity. It should be noted that the collected data needed preprocessing to be
prepared for use in mathematical problems.

7 Discussion and managerial insights
In this section, we elucidate the key aspects of our newly developed methodology and delve
into the managerial insights gleaned from the investigated case study:
• Role of backup surgeons: When surgeons are assigned as backups, their roles should
be dynamic based on operational needs. If disruptions to the primary surgeon do not
occur, backup surgeons can be allocated to other responsibilities, such as new patient

8 Conclusion, limitations, and future streams
In this research, a comprehensive two-phase methodology was devised to effectively manage
surgery departments within medical centers. The initial phase harnessed M/M/C models to
ascertain the optimal capacity of various units. Subsequently, utilizing this derived capac-
ity data for operating rooms (ORs), a novel mathematical model was constructed to enable
resilient scheduling of surgeries. The inclusion of backup surgeons for each surgery was a
pivotal strategy to mitigate the risk of cancellations due to surgeon unavailability, thereby
enhancing schedule resilience. The application of the goal programming (GP) approach
played a crucial role in achieving a harmonious allocation of ORs and equitable distribution
of surgeon workloads. Notably, the GP effectively addressed both of these goals concurrently,
culminating in equitable surgeon scheduling. The study demonstrated the effectiveness of
optimal capacity planning through queueing systems in attaining surgical schedules with min-
imal idle and overtime periods. Furthermore, a decomposition-based heuristic method was
developed to expedite the resolution of the scheduling model, ensuring the timely extraction
of optimal solutions.
However, it is essential to acknowledge the limitations and challenges encountered in this
study. Firstly, the comprehensive data required for both phases of the proposed methodology
was challenging to obtain due to data limitations and privacy concerns. Secondly, the high
model complexity led to signiﬁcant computational time, necessitating the development of
an efﬁcient heuristic approach. Nevertheless, it still requires a considerable amount of time
to solve large-scale instances of the problem, such as cases involving more than 50 patients
or extended planning horizons. In light of these constraints, the authors propose several
directions for future research, including:
• Exploring the scheduling of upstream and downstream units, such as Post-Anesthesia Care
Units (PACUs).
• Incorporating considerations related to pandemic conditions and accounting for sequence-
dependent cleaning times within the proposed approach.
• Investigating alternative optimization objectives, such as cost minimization or maximizing
the number of successfully completed surgeries.
• Considering the intrinsic intricacies of the scheduling model and the nonlinear characteris-
tics inherent in the queueing model, it is advisable to investigate the utilization of heuristic
and meta-heuristic algorithms.
