Abstract
This study explores the interplay between blockchain-based smart contracts and big data ana-
lytics for the supply chain value creation of micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs).
We implement our Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) procedure with the ganache blockchain,
and addresses generated by the Metamask wallet. Each supply chain player in the blockchain
isassignedawalletaddresstoobservethehashescreatedwhendataisaddedtotheblockchain.
Our ﬁndings unfold that supply chain value creation emphasises traceability, transparency,
security, and proﬁt maximisation interlocked with how effectively companies utilise big data
collected through blockchain-based smart contracts. This subsequentially assists managers
in using data types and a variety of analytics, spanning from descriptive, diagnostic, predic-
tive, and prescriptive to cognitive analytics. This synergy between the blockchain and the
types of analytics provides opportunities to identify new interactions and directions for future
research.

1 Introduction
The evolution of blockchain and data analytics for micro, small, and medium enterprises
(MSMEs) has created intense pressure to compete in an ever-changing environment. While
MSMEs increasingly adopt advanced technologies, they often struggle to fully leverage the
strategic value of these tools due to limited resources and expertise. These types of organ-
isaions play a pivotal role, accounting for over 90% of total businesses and contributing
between 60 and 70% of global employment (OECD, 2023). Despite their signiﬁcant eco-
nomic contribution, MSMEs often lag in the adoption of cutting-edge technologies like
blockchain and big data analytics due to limited resources, technical expertise, and orga-
nizational constraints. Blockchain technology, with its potential to enhance transparency,
traceability, security, and operational efﬁciency, offers signiﬁcant advantages to supply chain
management, particularly for MSMEs, which tend to operate in more opaque and fragmented
markets.
A lack of skills and understanding has created a situation where MSMEs fail to fully
grasp the potential of these technologies in developing strategy and competitive advantage
(Felsberger et al., 2020). However, to remain competitive in today’s data-driven economy,
MSMEs must integrate innovative technologies that can optimize both internal operations
and external transactions (Gao & Sarwar, 2024). Blockchain and big data analytics represent
two such technologies that, when combined, have the potential to transform the way MSMEs
create and capture value across their supply chains.
From the research there is consensus that big data analytics enables MSMEs to extract
insights from large datasets, offering enhanced visibility into their operations, products, and
services (Wu, 2024; Awan et al., 2021, Dolgui, Ivanov, Sethi, et al., 2019). Current research
on the blockchain suggests that smart contracts can provide secure, transparent, and efﬁcient
ways to execute agreements and transactions between stakeholders (Al-Breiki et al., 2020;
Antonopoulos & Wood, 2018; Wood, 2017).
Despite the individual beneﬁts of these technologies, their combined use has been rel-
atively unexplored in MSMEs. Recently, Amentae and Gebresenbet (2021) put forward
technologies such as blockchain, IoT, big data analytics, and AI as enablers for achiev-
ing sustainability and resilience in the agriculture food supply chain. In a similar vein, Liu
et al. (2020) discussed optimal investment decisions in the agri-food supply chain by coordi-
nating market demand data and the degree of freshness of agri-food. This supports the work
of Wang (2019), who proposed a big data fusion model based on blockchain technology to
address agri-food safety and quality data. Despite these advancements, it’s clear that many
applied systems remain centralised, asymmetric, and opaque. Understanding the ’interplay’
between blockchain-based smart contracts and big data analytics is critical because it can
unlock new forms of value creation through enhanced transparency, traceability, and data-
driven decision-making in supply chain operations. For example, big data analytics provides
real-time information that smart contracts can use to automate processes, verify conditions,
and ensure the fulﬁlment of agreements, leading to more efﬁcient and secure operations.
This interplay has signiﬁcant implications for MSMEs’ ability to enhance their compet-
itive advantage. Integrating these technologies can improve operational efﬁciency, reduce
transaction costs, and ensure compliance with industry regulations, which are critical for
MSMEs operating in highly competitive and resource-constrained environments. While
recent research has explored the application of blockchain or big data analytics individually

Thus, this paper aims to investigate the synergistic ’interplay’ between big data analytics

In this paper, we take an integrative approach and argue that there is synergy between
analytics, operations, and the blockchain.
2 Literature review
This literature review explores the intersection of blockchain technology, big data analytics,
and their application in supply chain operations, particularly for micro, small, and medium
enterprises (MSMEs). In this literature the authors focus is on addressing challenges related to
transparency, traceability, and security, especially within the food supply chain. By analysing
previous studies, the review highlights the potential beneﬁts and limitations of integrating
these technologies, including the use of smart contracts. Additionally, it identiﬁes existing
gaps in MSMEs’ adoption of Industry 4.0 technologies and provides a foundation for under-
standing their potential to enhance supply chain value.
2.1 Blockchain technology in supply chain operation
Blockchain technology is increasingly recognized as a tool for enhancing supply chain trans-
parency, security, and operational efﬁciency (Haynes & Hietanen, 2023; Li et al., 2023a,
2023b; Wu et al., 2024). While much of the literature highlights its technical features, for
example decentralised ledgers, immutable records, and cryptographic security (Yanjun, 2023)
our research identiﬁes a crucial gap, how can MSMEs leverage these features to create value
within their supply chains?
Kamble et al. (2023) and discuss the potential of decentralized ledgers to reduce depen-
dency on intermediaries, but the real-world applications of these technologies in MSMEs
are less clear. Studies like Hughes et al. (2019) emphasize the scalability issues associated
with decentralized models, particularly in environments where MSMEs may lack the infras-
tructure to support blockchain implementation. Thus, while the theoretical advantages of
blockchain are well-documented, their practical utility in MSMEs remains a critical gap in
the literature.
Further complicating adoption is the issue of blockchain resilience, discussed by Divine
et al. (2021). While blockchain is often described as secure and tamper-proof, the literature

reveals that its effectiveness depends largely on the quality of data inputs and the robustness
of external systems. Gao and Sarwar (2024) note that while blockchain can secure transac-
tions, its reliance on external data can compromise the integrity of supply chain operations,
especially when real-time decision-making is involved. This presents a unique challenge for
MSMEs, which may not have access to high-quality, reliable data sources.
Moreover, the literature lacks consensus on how blockchain can be integrated with other
emerging technologies like big data analytics and smart contracts, particularly in MSMEs.
While some studies propose that blockchain’s decentralized nature offers a unique opportu-
nity to enhance supply chain transparency, few studies address the operational barriers faced
by smaller ﬁrms in adopting these technologies. The authors argue that investigating this
gap is critical many MSMEs often have limited resources and technical expertise, which
may hinder their ability to implement and maintain blockchain-based systems effectively
(Ghobakhloo et al., 2022).
Thus, blockchain as a technological infrastructure tool is an emerging trend in some
industries (Kamble et al., 2023; Yadav et al., 2023; Rodríguez-Espíndola et al., 2022; Devine
et al., 2021; Dolgui, Ivanov, Sethi, et al., 2019). However, blockchain adoption to address
the challenges of transparency, traceability and security is still nascent in many countries
(Iranmanesh et al., 2023), while MSMEs are still far behind (Deng et al., 2022).
2.2 Blockchain and the food supply chain
Blockchain technology has shown signiﬁcant promise in addressing some of the most press-
ing challenges in supply chain management, particularly in industries where transparency,
traceability, and security are critical (Cui et al., 2024). The food supply chain has been high-
lighted as one such industry, where stakeholders are increasingly turning to blockchain to
improve food safety and integrity (Yadav et al., 2023; Nayal et al., 2023). This aligns with
the broader discussion of blockchain’s potential to revolutionize supply chains, especially in
the context of MSMEs, where ensuring transparency and security can be a major hurdle due
to limited resources.
The food supply chain comprises multiple stakeholders worldwide, such as suppliers,
producers, processors, distributors/wholesalers/cold storage, and retailers. Figure 1 depicts
the linear physical ﬂow in the food supply chain. The main stakeholders of the food supply
chain are described below (Caro et al., 2018):
We illustrate the key stakeholders in Fig. 1 below:
• Supplier: The supplier represents the provider of raw materials for farming, such as seeds,
nutrients, fertiliser, pesticides, and chemicals, among others.
• Producer: The producer represents the farmer who grows crops and is responsible for
seeding/planting and cultivating harvest.
• Processor: The processor is responsible for performing various actions such as processing
food for longer shelf life, digital certiﬁcates, food labelling, multiple types of packaging
as per requirement, and attaching batch codes
• Distributor: The distributor is responsible for moving the product to the retailer as per
order quantity and attaching batch codes.
• Retailer: The retailer sells the products to the end-user, the customer.
• Customer: The customer is the ﬁnal element of the chain.
The food supply chain is highly complex, involving multiple stakeholders and a constant
ﬂow of material and information (Iranmanesh et al., 2023). This complexity often leads

challenges of MSMEs relatively underexplored. The authors also note that blockchain when
combined with other technologies such as big data analytics and smart contracts, can further
enhance the value it provides to the food supply chain by enabling real-time decision-making
and automating critical processes.
As an example, Hang et al. (2020) demonstrated how blockchain, in conjunction with IoT,
could monitor temperature conditions in ﬁsh supply chains, using smart contracts to automate
responses and reduce contamination risks. Such advancements offer new opportunities for
MSMEs to improve both operational efﬁciency and food safety, but only if they can overcome
the barriers to technology adoption. Therefore, exploring how MSMEs can leverage these
technologies to enhance the food supply chain is essential to understanding how blockchain
can create value in smaller ﬁrms, addressing critical issues such as transparency, traceability,
and security. This section aims to synthesize the existing research on blockchain in the food
supply chain and highlight the gaps that need to be addressed, particularly in relation to
MSMEs and their unique challenges in adopting such technologies.
Digital technologies such as blockchain, big data, IoT, and AI are opening new opportu-
nities for supply chains to improve transparency, traceability, and smart ﬁnancial services,
the current digital infrastructure of MSMEs is weak (Deng et al., 2022). Upgrading this
infrastructure is key to enabling MSMEs to take full advantage of these technologies and
ensure that the beneﬁts of blockchain are distributed more equally across the industry. Further
research is needed to explore how MSMEs can effectively implement blockchain solutions
to enhance supply chain operations, particularly in the food sector, where transparency and
safety are paramount.
2.3 Blockchain and smart contracts in supply chains
Blockchain and smart contracts have garnered signiﬁcant attention for their potential to rev-
olutionize supply chain operations across various industries. By automating transactions and

ensuring transparency, these technologies address critical pain points like fraud, inefﬁciency,
and a lack of real-time visibility (Zhang et al., 2023). A key area of research has been the
application of blockchain in food security, where smart contracts are used to track and trace
goods, ensuring that they meet safety standards throughout the supply chain. For example,
Shahid et al. (2020) proposed a blockchain-based system on the Ethereum platform to auto-
mate and verify transactions between stakeholders, ensuring compliance and reducing fraud.
Similarly, Hang et al. (2020) leveraged IBM’s Hyperledger Fabric to create a secure ﬁsh
blockchain that integrates IoT technologies to monitor temperature and quality. Both studies
demonstrate how smart contracts can provide traceability and security in industries where
supply chain transparency is paramount.
Despite the successful implementation of blockchain in sectors like food safety and
counterfeit drug prevention (Kordestani et al., 2023), these solutions often focus on larger
enterprises with established technological infrastructures. The current study diverges from
these examples by focusing on MSMEs, which face unique challenges in adopting such tech-
nologies. For MSMEs, blockchain adoption is not only a matter of technological innovation
but also one of resource constraints, limited digital infrastructure, and insufﬁcient knowl-
edge sharing (Gupta et al., 2023). This gap in the literature highlights the need for further
exploration into how smart contracts and blockchain can be adapted for smaller-scale sup-
ply chains, particularly in food production and distribution. Furthermore, while applications
like tracking CO2 emissions in the palm oil supply chain (El Hathat et al., 2023) and reverse
logistics in power batteries (Ghomi-Avili et al., 2023) illustrate blockchain’s versatility, these
examples also emphasize that blockchain’s value lies in its ability to handle complex supply
chains involving multiple stakeholders. However, these studies do not adequately address
how blockchain might serve the unique needs of MSMEs, which typically operate in less
complex environments with fewer resources but face equally critical demands for traceability,
security, and operational efﬁciency.
Thus, the various applications of blockchain and smart contracts listed in the literature,
while relevant, must be critically re-examined in the context of MSME supply chains, where
the challenges of technology adoption differ signiﬁcantly from those in larger organiza-
tions. The current research contributes to the literature by exploring the interplay between
blockchain, smart contracts, and big data analytics speciﬁcally within MSMEs, with a focus
on enhancing transparency, traceability, and operational security in their supply chain oper-
ations. In summary, while the literature has demonstrated blockchain’s potential to enhance
various aspects of supply chain management, it has yet to fully address how these technolo-
gies can be adapted to suit the speciﬁc challenges faced by MSMEs. This study aims to ﬁll
this gap by investigating how the integration of blockchain and smart contracts can create
value in smaller supply chains through data-driven decision-making and enhanced process
automation, thereby contributing to supply chain resilience and efﬁciency in MSMEs.
2.4 The interplay between big data and smart contracts
Blockchain and big data analytics are emerging as transformative technologies capable of
reshaping supply chains by enhancing both sustainability and performance. Blockchain’s
ability to provide a decentralized and immutable ledger makes it an ideal tool for tracking
products and materials through complex supply chains, ensuring transparency, traceability,
and accountability at every stage (Yadav et al., 2023). In conjunction with blockchain, big
data analytics enables real-time data processing and decision-making across supply chains.

2.5 Theoretical background
The application of theories in the adoption of blockchain and big data analytics enables
decision-makers to explore the rapidly changing business processes and improve them to
gain a competitive advantage in supply chains (Narwane et al., 2023). The dynamic capability
view is an established theory in supply chain management (Betto & Garengo, 2023), that
can be used to investigate the impact of smart contracts on MSMEs’ competitive advantage.
Speciﬁcally, we emphasise the interplay between blockchain-based smart contracts to create
transparency, traceability, and security to collect big data. This becomes the benchmark
for other players to utilise various analytics to enhance their technological and analytical
capabilities. Teece et al. (2009) encourage organisations to consider the impact of learning
about new technologies and the importance of building new assets and transforming existing
resources, processes, and culture. One such example is the work of Dolgui et al., (2019a,
2019b) and Devine et al. (2021), who, in their research, argue that smart contracts, through
123

656

their permanent nature, can remove the reliance on intermediaries, adding another layer of
dynamism as part of creating competitive advantage.
Research by Conboy et al. (2020) highlighted the value of technological techniques,
such as business analytical technologies, in leveraging data for decision-making through the
dynamic capabilities view. The authors paid particular attention to the Vs of big data (Veracity,
Volume, Velocity) and how the Vs can be utilised to enhance operational research activities.
Their ﬁndings suggest that correctly utilising big data is crucial in locating opportunities, and
threats and the activation to reconﬁgure tangible and intangible resources. In this context,
the dynamic capabilities view provides the lens and the framework to bring these key salient
points together. Thus, the utilisation of the dynamic capabilities view is not new, and there
is signiﬁcant potential in deﬁning competitive advantage through the lens of this framework.
We chose this perspective due to the ever-changing environment within MSMEs and industry
4.0 implementation. The role of dynamic capabilities allows ﬁrms to “integrate, build, and
reconﬁgure internal and external competencies to address rapidly changing environments”
(Teece et al., 2009, p. 516).
3 Research gap
A summary of the key literature on analytics, traceability and transparency in food supply
chains using IoT, RFID, blockchain and smart contracts is presented in Table 1. During our
research, we found limited empirical evidence focusing on the role of big data analytics
in using smart contracts to execute decisions on MSMEs’ food supply chain operations.
Likewise, little research attention has been paid to peer-to-peer ledger transactions. Thus,
our goal in this paper is to set this study apart from related works (Agarwal et al., 2023;
Ghomi-Avili et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) by emphasising granularity, the implementation
of smart contracts and analytical connections.

4.2 Smart contract design
TounderstandtheinterplayofsmartcontractswithbigdataanalyticsforMSMEs’foodsupply
chain operation, we utilise the Ethereum VirtualMachine (EVM) to hostand execute the smart
contracts. This method was also utilised by Jabbar and Dani, (2020) when they investigated
the effects of gas on transaction prices within supply chain management. Another potential
solution is using an open-source tool called Remix, an online browser-based development
environment (Omar et al., 2021). The EVM was used because it provides a closer environment
to a real blockchain and has a richer data output such as gas usage and speed (Jabbar & Dani,
2020). Building on Fig. 3 we deﬁne three smart contracts, which act as the main decision-
making elements.
Smart Contract 1 (SC1) – Check conditions before adding to the blockchain (This is

Each smart contract takes a big data input which is validated based on pre-agreed con-

accurate and valid based on the sensor readings. For the smart contracts, three algorithms
underpin the smart contract logic. These algorithms are unique to MSMEs’ supply chain
operation and are explicitly designed to act as a controlling mechanism to make decisions
in real-time across the whole blockchain. Previous algorithms have been more focused on
speciﬁc tasks, as an example in the work of Agrawal et al. (2022) four algorithms have been
put forward focused on data veriﬁcation and order distribution. The algorithms proposed in
this paper take this further and provide unique functionality and decision-making capabilities
based on conditions a key difference.

4.3 Developing the smart contracts
The Ethereum virtual machine (EVM), is a prototype blockchain that mimics the real
blockchain in every way. The advantage of using such an approach is to create smart contracts
that are cheaper and more efﬁcient, and removing the need to own gas (Devine et al., 2021).
The EVM comes equipped with 100 test ether, which cannot be used on the main network
but gives the authors an indication of the cost and efﬁciency of the contracts. We store the test
ether in Metamask, a browser-based cryptocurrency wallet (Antonopoulos & Wood, 2018;
Jabbar & Dani, 2020). To fully understand the interplay between smart contracts and data
analytics we collect data on smart contracts. We collect data on smart contract deployment,
execution, and modiﬁcation in real-time (Grech et al., 2018). This paper illustrates the rela-
tionships between buyers and suppliers and looks at the interplay between analytics and smart
contracts to benchmark pre-agreed conditions for transparency and traceability.

data, harnessing real-time data, along with other applications on variety (unstructured data)
and volume (structured data).

RQ1: How does the integration of smart contracts and big data analytics enhance
supply chain value by addressing key challenges of transparency, traceability, and
security for MSMEs, which often face limited resources and higher vulnerability to
operational inefﬁciencies and fraud?
In meeting RQ1 the authors establish the signiﬁcance of MSMEs, highlighting their
challenges in adopting advanced technologies like blockchain and big data analytics in the
introduction and the literature review. We move beyond technical explanations to demonstrate
how smart contracts and big data analytics enhance transparency, traceability, and security
in supply chains. The discussion of blockchain applications, such as food traceability and
combating counterfeit goods, provide relevant examples of how these technologies create
value. In our experiments we create an environment of security and transparency/visibility,
as all stakeholders have access to the data in an MSME environment.
RQ2: How can the integration of smart contracts and big data analytics be applied
in MSMEs’ supply chain operations to demonstrate tangible improvements in trans-
parency, traceability, and security, and what speciﬁc operational insights can be gained
from this implementation?
For RQ2 we provide a deeper understanding of how smart contracts and big data ana-
lytics interact within supply chains, offering real-world examples of their application. The

6.2 Implications
In the literature review, we have developed a comprehensive overview of the key research
in MSMEs’ food traceability and transparency. In most cases, we identiﬁed that there was
research on the blockchain (Iranmanesh et al., 2023; Yadav et al., 2023) and big data (Akhtar
et al., 2019; Hopkins, 2021; Yadav et al., 2020), but very little investigated the interplay
between data analytics and smart contracts. We also found that the work was often conceptual;
for example, Farnoush et al. (2021) conducted a literature review on blockchain analytics
focusing on intent to implement. Similarly, Kaur et al. (2019) explored the use of blockchain
in the context of vehicular technology but conducted a system modelling approach. This
researchtakesanovelapproachandcreatesastrongcontributionbyinvestigatingtheinterplay
between data analytics and smart contracts on a fully functioning virtual blockchain. Thus, as
the discussion around blockchain becomes more popular, future research will focus on real-
world applications of the blockchain using virtual systems. In the MSMEs’ supply chains,
this paper is one of the ﬁrst efforts to use an applied approach to research development.
Additionally, Khan et al. (2021) believe that adoption with senior management is still an
issue, and many projects fail due to a lack of support. This can be an issue for MSMEs that
often do not have the ﬁnancial or skills-based capabilities to conduct large IT projects or
implement advanced analytics. Thus, implementing smart contracts within MSMEs may still
lead to some reluctance, primarily due to the need for pre-agreed conditions and access to data.
The other challenge is the lack of training and skills in using technical tools to supplement big
data usage. One of the key implications at this stage is managing the tension between skills
and technology adoption, encouraging managers to build big-data saving teams for better
value creation in MSMEs (Akhtar et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2019). While smart contracts and
big data analytics are a huge step forward within the MSMEs’ industry, there is a huge skills
shortage in several sectors (Clohessy & Acton, 2019). To help manage this tension, this study
provides guidelines for managers and companies on how effectively they can embed big data
collected through blockchain-based smart contracts and then utilizes a variety of analytics
for their supply chain value creation.
6.3 Limitations and future research
In developing this paper, we identify several limitations and future directions that can help
shape this interdisciplinary ﬁeld’s future. The ﬁrst limitation we identiﬁed was the need to
apply this solution to more case studies. In this paper, we propose and test a virtual blockchain
solution that can act as a testbed to investigate the interplay between smart contracts and big
data analytics in an industrial setting. The second limitation we put forward is the smart
contract dataset; future datasets might be more complex as advanced technology emerges.
