Abstract
Data envelopment analysis (DEA) models appear in envelopment and multiplier forms, which
are in a primal-dual relationship. In this paper, we derive the general multiplier form of path-
based models, encompassing radial, directional distance function, and hyperbolic distance
function models as special cases. We investigate the economic interpretation of the multiplier
models and uncover the link between shadow proÔ¨Āt inefÔ¨Āciency and technical inefÔ¨Āciency
provided by path-based models. Using the optimality conditions for the primal-dual pair,
we precisely describe the two-way relationship between the optimal solutions of the mul-
tiplier model and the supporting hyperplanes of the technology set at the projection. This
relationship serves as a mathematical justiÔ¨Ācation for extending one of the early approaches
to measuring returns-to-scale (RTS) onto the entire class of path-based models. Moreover,
we demonstrate the eligibility of this method by revealing the fact that the set of all strongly
efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks for the assessed unit in path-based models does not need to belong to a
single strongly efÔ¨Ācient face of technology set. This Ô¨Ānding changes the view on traditional
approaches to RTS measurement that rely on supporting hyperplanes encompassing a single
strongly efÔ¨Ācient face of the technology set. In this new perspective, we propose two methods
for RTS measurement. The Ô¨Ārst is based on the hyperplanes at the projection, and the second
method adapts the minimum face method to be suitable for path-based models. Both methods
are fully justiÔ¨Āed and brought to an algorithmic form.

1 Introduction
Data envelopment analysis (DEA) is a mathematical programming approach used to assess
the relative performance of organisational units. It enables the comparison of the efÔ¨Āciency
of different entities by analysing their respective inputs and outputs. By combining observed
input‚Äďoutput data with certain axioms, DEA establishes technology sets and develops various
models built on these sets. The models appear in two forms: the envelopment form and the
multiplier form. Both forms are essential for proper understanding and application of the
model.
The envelopment form evaluates the current unit relative to the enveloping surface of
all units from the inside. The model is equipped with a distance function and/or a set of
slacks, which are used to measure the distance of the current unit from a reference unit on the
surface. This model enables us to determine whether the unit is efÔ¨Ācient and, for those that
are not, it gives guidance on how to reduce inputs or increase outputs to become efÔ¨Ācient.
On the other hand, the multiplier form of the model evaluates the current unit with a set
of weights (multipliers) that reÔ¨āect the importance of particular inputs and outputs as their
shadow prices. The multipliers make it possible to construct supporting hyperplanes that
surround the surface from the outside. The hyperplane intercept can be used to recognise a
type of local returns-to-scale (RTS). This feature of multiplier models was initially discussed
in Banker‚Äôs pioneering work Banker et al. (1984) when introducing the BCC model to DEA.
Consequently, multiplier models play a signiÔ¨Ācant role in returns-to-scale analysis, as well
as in cross-efÔ¨Āciency and restricted multiplier measurements.
The envelopment and multiplier forms of the models represent a primal-dual pair of mathe-
matical programming problems. For a linear programming (LP) model, it is easy to construct
its dual and to describe the primal-dual correspondence in terms of strong duality and com-
plementarity conditions. However, if the model is not linear, it may not be straightforward to
identify its dual counterpart and describe primal-dual relationships. This was the case with
the Russell graph model, which was formulated as a non-linear envelopment model already
in F√§re et al. (1985), but its convex structure was revealed only in 2007: In the works of
Sueyoshi and Sekitani (2007a) and Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2018b) it was reformulated into
second-order cone programming (SOCP) and semideÔ¨Ānite programming (SDP) problems,
respectively, and using the techniques of these two large sub-classes of convex programming,
its duals were derived. In Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2018b), the multiplier Russell model was
further modiÔ¨Āed to a form that allowed for an economic interpretation, and its connection to
proÔ¨Āt efÔ¨Āciency was established. The hyperbolic model for variable returns-to-scale (VRS)
had a similar history, Ô¨Ārst being established in an envelopment form by F√§re et al. (1985). It
was not until 2018 that it was reformulated as an SDP problem (Halick√° & Trnovsk√°, 2018a,
2019), with the dual model expressed in an economically interpretable form.
These examples demonstrate that the non-linearity of the model itself might not be an
obstacle, as long as the model is a convex programming problem. In the last few decades,
convex programming has seen considerable progress and has been shown to have many
similarities to linear problems in terms of duality and computational tractability via interior-
point methods. Dual problems to convex problems can often be derived using the Lagrangian
duality described, for example, in the book Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004), and problems can
be solved directly using specialised software, such as the CVX modelling system Grant &
Boyd (2008), Grant et al. (2013), which does not necessitate the formulation of problems in
the form of SOCP or SDP.

Duality has proved useful in examining the properties of the entire class of non-radial,
also called slacks-based models, which includes the Russell measure model, but also the
slacks-based measure (SBM) model of Tone (2001), the additive model of Charnes et al.
(1985), or the range adjusted measure (RAM) model (Cooper, Park, & Pastor, 1999). The
characteristic feature of these models is that they derive the efÔ¨Āciency score for the assessed
unit from its benchmarks, which are strongly (Pareto-Koopmans) efÔ¨Ācient, although not
uniquely determined. Through duality, the relationship between the supporting hyperplanes
and the optimal solution of the dual problem was investigated in Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2021).
The supporting hyperplanes generated by the optimal solutions of the dual problem were
shown to contain all (ambiguously deÔ¨Āned) benchmarks of the assessed unit. The analysis
provided in Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2021) has also explained unexpected numerical results
regarding RTS presented in the work Sueyoshi and Sekitani (2007a) for slacks-based models.
There, the intercepts of the supporting hyperplanes generated by dual optimal solutions
for some units attained all values from minus inÔ¨Ānity to plus inÔ¨Ānity. Due to the duality,
it was shown that such behaviour can actually occur in slacks-based models due to the
unboundedness of the sets of optimal solutions of the dual problem for efÔ¨Ācient units. The
same duality technique used for slacks-based models could also be beneÔ¨Ācial for another
large class of problems called path-based models.
Path-based models search for the benchmarks by specifying various parametric paths
running from the assessed unit to the boundary of the technology set. In the special case
of the radial BCC input or output oriented models ( Banker et al. (1984)), the paths are
determined as rays that connect the units with the origin in the space of inputs or outputs. In
the directional distance function models ( Chambers et al. (1996); Chambers et al. (1998)) the
paths are deÔ¨Āned as rays that originate from the assessed units and head toward the frontier
in the pre-assigned directions. In the hyperbolic distance function model ( F√§re et al. (1985)),
the hyperbolic paths are obtained by combining the input and output BCC models. The point
at which the path leaves the technology set is called the projection of the assessed units. Since
the projection does not need to be strongly efÔ¨Ācient, the so-called second phase is formulated
for these models, which provides a strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmark for the assessed unit.
In the paper Halick√° et al. (2024), the path-based models were analysed using a VRS
general scheme that includes the standard models (that is, radial, directional, and hyperbolic
distance function models) as special cases and allows the creation of new models. The analysis
in the paper speciÔ¨Ācally focused on the envelopment forms of these models. While the
multiplier forms of some models in this class are already known, the class of multiplier
path-based models has not been studied in terms of its properties within a uniÔ¨Āed framework.
In this article, as our Ô¨Ārst main result, we derive the dual multiplier form for the general
envelopment scheme of path-based models and analyse its properties. We show that for the
standard models, once they are equivalently reformulated to Ô¨Āt the general envelopment
scheme, the resulting forms of the multiplier model coincide with the known duals. For some
models of this class, the dual will be derived for the Ô¨Ārst time. This opens up the possibility
of applying techniques based on multiplier models, such as the cross efÔ¨Āciency or restricted
multiplier measurements, to models where this was previously not possible.
The general scheme of the dual multiplier model allows us to establish common properties
of duals for the entire class. We explore the economic interpretation of multiplier models and
uncover the relationship between shadow proÔ¨Āt inefÔ¨Āciency and technical inefÔ¨Āciency. This
Ô¨Ānding extends similar results for the directional distance function model in Chambers et al.
(1998) and the hyperbolic distance function model in Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2019) to the
entire class of path-based models. The relationship indicates that path-based models derive
their technical inefÔ¨Āciency from minimising their shadow proÔ¨Āt inefÔ¨Āciency. Interestingly,

DEA has developed several methods to determine the characteristics of RTS. Some of these
methods are closely related to the multiplier form of the models and the geometric insight,
which connects their optimal solutions with the supporting hyperplanes of the technology
set. The importance of this geometric context for the RTS analysis was Ô¨Ārst recognised by
Banker et al. (1984) for the BCC models. Initially, the analysis was limited to efÔ¨Ācient units,
as seen in the early work of Banker & Thrall (1992), Banker et al. (1996), and Banker et
al (1996). In these works, the problem with multiple supporting hyperplanes in the corner
points of the production possibility set was solved using two auxiliary linear programmes.
Later, the RTS measurement was extended to inefÔ¨Ācient units. The methods used for
inefÔ¨Ācient units varied depending on the BCC reference point chosen for the RTS assessment.
In Seiford and Zhu (1999) and Sueyoshi (1999), Fukuyama (2000) the RTS characteristic for
an inefÔ¨Ācient unit was derived from its BCC uniquely deÔ¨Āned projection. Since the projection
may not be strongly efÔ¨Ācient, the authors‚Äô focus was therefore moved on the derivation of
RTS characteristics using a strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmark provided by the epsilon method or
the standard second phase. Banker et al. (1996) suggested using epsilon modiÔ¨Ācations of
the BCC models for the RTS analysis. However, it was observed that solving the modiÔ¨Āed
model can be numerically challenging and determining an appropriate non-Archimedean
epsilon value could be difÔ¨Ācult. Therefore, the subsequent approaches (e.g. Banker et al.
(2004), Banker et al. (2011)) derived the RTS characteristic of an inefÔ¨Ācient unit from the
RTS characteristic of its strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmark obtained by the second-phase method.
These approaches for BCC models were also analysed in Tone (1996), Seiford and Zhu
(1999), Sueyoshi (1999) and Fukuyama (2000). However, the radial models may provide
multiple strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks and the authors of these works overlooked that using
the non-Archimedean epsilon method or the standard second-phase method already involves
selecting of a subset of benchmarks for the next RTS analysis.
The multiplicity of benchmarks has become a central focus when the procedures devel-
oped for radial models have been applied to the slacks-based models. The works Fukuyama
(2001), Sueyoshi and Sekitani (2007a), and Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2021) are based on the
optimal solutions provided by the relevant multiplier models and the supporting hyperplanes
generated by these solutions. Although these hyperplanes contain all the benchmarks, if the
model is applied to an inefÔ¨Ācient unit, the resulting hyperplanes might not generate all the
necessary supporting hyperplanes (see example from Halick√° and Trnovsk√° (2021). Alter-
native methods have been used to characterise the RTS in the RAM model without using
multiplier models. Instead, these methods analyse the set of all strongly efÔ¨Ācient bench-
marks that is always convex in the slacks-based models, and select a representative point
from this set. In the studies by Sueyoshi and Sekitani (2007b), Krivonozhko et al. (2012a),
Krivonozhko et al. (2014), and Mehdiloozad et al. (2014), the concept of the minimum face
is used, and several relatively complex procedures have been developed for its identiÔ¨Ācation,
with varying degrees of success.
In this paper, as our second main result, we provide an analysis of existing RTS approaches
based on supporting hyperplanes. We suggest and justify RTS measurement methods suit-
able for the entire class of path-based models. We discover that in path-based models, if the
projection of the evaluated unit is not strongly efÔ¨Ācient, then the strongly efÔ¨Ācient bench-
marks may not necessarily belong to a single strongly efÔ¨Ācient face of the technology set.
This is a signiÔ¨Ācant difference compared to slacks-based models, where the strongly efÔ¨Ācient

benchmarks always belong to a strongly efÔ¨Ācient face of the technology set. As a conse-
quence, the methods that are effective for slacks-based models are much more intricate and
unclear when we attempt to apply them to path-based models. Utilising duality, we provide
a comprehensive explanation for this phenomenon and propose an approach grounded in the
idea that the RTS characteristic should be derived from a set of all supporting hyperplanes
passing through all strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks of the assessed unit. This approach leads
to two methods. In the Ô¨Ārst method, the hyperplanes always include the projection, and all
benchmarks. In the second method, the projection does not have to be included, and the RTS
characteristic is derived from the relative interior point of the minimum face that contains all
the benchmarks, regardless of whether the face is bounded or unbounded. We also suggest
how to identify the minimum face of all strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks and how to compute
its relative interior point simply by using standard interior point methods. Finally, we discuss
the main advantages and disadvantages of the proposed methods and demonstrate them with
numerical examples.

6.2.2 Method 2
In Method 1, the RTS characteristic is determined on the basis of all hyperplanes containing
the projection and also all benchmarks. It might be better to develop a technique for assessing
the RTS based on all hyperplanes containing solely all benchmarks and, ideally, only strongly
efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks. These hyperplanes form a superset of the set of hyperplanes used in
Method 1 and can therefore produce a different RTS characteristic.
The occurrence of multiple benchmarks in connection with RTS has been resolved for
non-radial (slacks-based) models where the Range Adjusted Measure (RAM) model (Cooper,
Park, & Pastor, 1999) was chosen as the main representative of these models. In these models,
the benchmarks are always strongly efÔ¨Ācient and form a convex subset of ‚ąāSTv. The so-called
minimum face (the face of Tv of the smallest dimension) that contains all the benchmarks is
considered (see, e.g. the works Sueyoshi and Sekitani (2007b), Krivonozhko et al. (2014),
Mehdiloozad et al. (2015)), and then, it is in accordance with DeÔ¨Ānition 1, to derive the
RTS characteristic from all those hyperplanes that contain the minimal face. In the paper
Krivonozhko et al. (2012b), all units in the relative interior of the face are shown to exhibit
the same RTS characteristics. Hence, the RTS characteristic of an inefÔ¨Ācient unit is identiÔ¨Āed
with the RTS characteristic of any point from the relative interior of the corresponding
minimum face. Based on this, the RTS characteristic of an inefÔ¨Ācient unit in slacks-based
models is deÔ¨Āned as the RTS characteristic provided by any point in the relative interior of
its benchmarks.
In this section, we show that this procedure, which is well justiÔ¨Āed in slacks-based models,
generally does not apply to path-based models. The reason is that, in the path-based models,
‚Äď the set of all benchmarks for (xo, yo) may also contain benchmarks that are not strongly
efÔ¨Ācient;
‚Äď the set of all strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks for (xo, yo) may not be convex.
As a result, it may happen that the minimum face containing all strongly efÔ¨Ācient bench-
mark will be weakly efÔ¨Ācient. We modify the aforementioned procedure to derive the RTS
from an interior point of this face. If this minimum face is strongly efÔ¨Ācient, the set of hyper-
planes contributing to the RTS assessment is a proper superset of the set of hyperplanes used
in Method 1.

7 Discussion and conclusions
This paper presents results in two closely related domains: the Ô¨Ārst involves the derivation of
a dual model and the exploration of primal-dual properties for the entire class of path-based
models. The second addresses the analysis and proposal of methods for measuring RTS.
When considering the Ô¨Ārst domain, we employed the Lagrangian duality technique to
derive a dual (multiplier) form of the general path-based model that corresponds to VRS.
When we set ŌÉ = 0, we obtain the dual for the CRS case. We have also examined the
primal-dual relations between the envelopment and multiplier forms, as stated in Theorem1.
These relations were essential for the derivation of our further results. The multiplier form
of the model allowed us to reveal the connection between technical inefÔ¨Āciency and shadow
proÔ¨Āt inefÔ¨Āciency in Corollary5. These Ô¨Āndings could be extended to explore the relationship
between multiplier path-based models and the loss function introduced by Pastor et al. (2012).
Additionally, the multiplier models could be used to establish the Fenchel-Mahler inequality,
which allows one to decompose the economic proÔ¨Āt inefÔ¨Āciency into technical and allocative
components, as shown for a variety of models in the book Pastor et al. (2022).
The crucial result is formulated in Theorem10, which establishes a connection between
the two domains. Despite its importance for RTS measurement, only little attention has been
paid to similar results in the literature. This theorem, together with Corollary11, states that
when using (31), we actually evaluate the RTS with respect to all supporting hyperplanes
passing through the projection point and also through all benchmarks of the evaluated unit.
The direct application of this theorem led to the development of our Ô¨Ārst Method 1 described
in Sect.6.2.1. It should be noted that the one-way implication of the relationship established
in Theorem10 was already recognised in Banker et al. (1984), and was subsequently utilised
in the early approaches for RTS measurement in BCC models.
In Section 6.2, we discussed alternative procedures for the BCC models and RAM non-
radial model. These procedures derive the RTS characteristic of an inefÔ¨Ācient unit from
the RTS characteristics only of its strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks (i.e. without considering
the projection or the other benchmarks). We pointed out that the methods for BCC models
can cause ambiguity because a unit can have multiple strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks with
different RTS characteristics, as shown in Example 2. Additionally, in the minimum face
method developed for the RAM model it is crucial that all strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks
always lie in a single strongly efÔ¨Ācient face of the technology set.
Our efforts to develop a method for path-based models that considers all strongly efÔ¨Ācient
benchmarks led us to a detailed analysis of the properties of these benchmarks revealing a
previously unrecognised result: the strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks do not necessarily form
a convex set, nor are they conÔ¨Āned to a single strongly efÔ¨Ācient face of the technology set.
This discovery is documented in Example 3. As a consequence, we had to relax the standard
conditions for RTS measurement and build it on all hyperplanes that support the minimal
face containing all benchmarks, including those that are not strongly efÔ¨Ācient. With this
understanding, the rationale for Method 1 became clear. Moreover, we have shown that it
is also possible to require the supporting hyperplanes to encompass all benchmarks without
necessarily including the projection. This criterion forms the foundation of Method 2, as
detailed in Section 6.2. In implementing Method 2, we proposed determining the RTS type
using the relative interior point of the corresponding face, which can be obtained by solving
the optimisation programmes using an interior-point method. We justiÔ¨Āed this approach in
Section 6.2.2 and formalised it in Algorithm 2. This algorithm employs the procedure of

In summarising our results in the second domain, we have identiÔ¨Āed two eligible

Obviously, if the projection point of the evaluated unit is strongly efÔ¨Ācient, both methods

only strongly efÔ¨Ācient benchmarks (which then form a convex set), then the corresponding

tiplier models directly, both methods are still grounded in duality, the primal-dual properties
of optimal solutions, and strict complementarity.
