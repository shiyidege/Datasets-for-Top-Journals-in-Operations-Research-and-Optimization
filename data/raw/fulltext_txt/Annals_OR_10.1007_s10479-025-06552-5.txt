Abstract
Management of production and distribution planning of perishable food products is crucial
due to their low-proﬁt margin and the increased environmental costs of manufacturing. The
production planning of perishable products is challenging as it combines multiple factors such
as temperature tracking of products, sequence-dependent facility setup cost, and uncertain
demand in one setting. This paper studies the production and distribution planning problem
for perishable food products and uniﬁes the mentioned factors in an optimization framework.
We propose an adaptive optimization approach to address the uncertainty in demand, provid-
ing ﬂexible optimization approaches for a multi-period planning horizon. The ﬁrst approach
is adjustable robust optimization that generates a resilient and Pareto-efﬁcient production
and distribution plan to tackle demand uncertainty avoiding over-conservative solutions via
decision rules. The second is the folding horizon, which re-optimizes production and dis-
tribution plans based on the realized demands over time. We assess the efﬁciency of the
adaptive approach through extensive Monte Carlo simulation experiments. Furthermore, we
perform a real-case adoption study on the production planning of a dairy factory to assess
the applicability of our model and solution approach in real-world instances. According to
the results, the adjustable approach and the folding horizon approach improve the objective
function value by 4–14% and 5–28% for that of the orthodox robust model. The numerical
results also show that the adjustable robust approach is always resistant to uncertainty, while
the percentage of unmet demand for the deterministic model can reach as high as 18%.

1 Introduction
Perishable food products such as fresh fruits and vegetables, fresh meat and seafood, dairy
products, and other agricultural products have become essential to our daily lives and have
signiﬁcantly impacted the world economy. These types of products are also known as short-
shelf life products or seasonal products, and they represented a considerable portion of
sales in US supermarkets in 2014, accounting for 59.1% of total sales (Zhou & Wang,
2023). The balance of supply and demand is one of the main challenges for products with
limited shelf life, as is the case for others, and demand uncertainty is one of the prominent
factors that complicates the production and distribution planning process (Peidro et al., 2009).
The presence of various types of products in a competitive market may lure even loyal
customers into changing their choices and buying a different product. Furthermore, external
factors, such as cultural traditions and diseases, weather patterns, and natural disasters, may
cause uncertainty in demand (Francesconi & D’Haese, 2010; NBC, 2020). When the actual
demand does not match the planned amount, there are two possible negative consequences
for the producer: (i) customer dissatisfaction or loss, or (ii) extra disposal costs and waste
of valuable (environmental) resources due to the perishability of products. Notice that the
second consequence is overproduction and the need to keep extra inventory to compensate for
the risk of losing customers’ demand. Therefore, considering the uncertainty of the demand
is crucial to provide resilient solutions to the challenges mentioned above. To overcome
the challenges related to perishability in a supply chain, producers aim to reduce the time
between production and consumption as much as possible. In addition, companies invest
in temperature-tracking technologies and operational planning at each phase of the supply
chain. However, conventional supply chain models in the operations research ﬁeld lag behind
in providing efﬁcient production and distribution management because the associated models
often do not take perishability into account and ignore the temperature tracking of products
at each phase of the supply chain, as non-perishable goods do not necessitate quality and
temperature tracking-related operations.
In this paper, we propose a production and distribution planning problem (PDP) speciﬁ-
cally designed to address the unique challenges faced by industries involved in manufacturing
perishable food products. Our PDP formulation incorporates critical factors such as the tem-
perature tracking of food products, accounting for the delicate nature of perishables, and
considering temperature sequence-dependent setup costs associated with operating the facil-
ities. Furthermore, we address the inherent uncertainty in demand, a crucial aspect when
dealing with perishable goods. By encompassing these complex aspects, our proposed PDP
model offers a comprehensive solution tailored to the requirements of industries involved
in manufacturing time-sensitive and perishable food products. We propose a mathematical
optimization model for the addressed problem to minimize total production, distribution,
and disposal costs, yielding environmentally friendly and cost-effective solutions for the
time and temperature-sensitive food product industries. Accordingly, it is critical to deter-
mine production sequences that minimize setup costs and obtain efﬁcient production and
transportation plans that meet transportation time requirements while minimizing the total
cost. To address the demand uncertainty, we adopt the robust optimization (RO) paradigm
and formulate robust PDP (RPDP) that does not need any distribution information. Instead,
we assume that the demand values reside in intervals with known lower and upper bounds
deﬁned by the decision-maker based on the historical data of demand confronted by retailers.
These intervals are called box uncertainty sets and can easily be generated from the historical
demand data even when the associated data are scarce.

Given the above-mentioned advantages of RPDP, the model may result in overproduction
and extra inventory when the demand is realized less than the worst-case value, leading
to unnecessary holding and disposal costs. To avoid the associated over-conservatism, we
apply adjustable robust optimization (ARO) to RPDP to get more ﬂexible solutions that can be
adjusted according to the revealed portion of data at different points of the planning horizon;
this problem is referred to as adjustable RPDP (ARPDP). ARPDP provides the ﬂexibility of
adjusting production and distribution decisions from holding chambers to retailers according
to the realized portion of the demand in a multi-period planning horizon, and the producer
is allowed to use the information of realized demand in the previous periods and decide on
production, warehousing, and backorder amounts accordingly for the upcoming periods. On
the negative side, the ARO reformulation may yield alternative optimal solutions that yield
signiﬁcantly different objective function value (OFV) performances for non-worst-case data
(e.g., the nominal) even though the alternative solutions yield the same worst-case OFV
performance. To tackle the related Pareto efﬁciency issue, we propose a re-optimization
approach that maintains optimality for the worst-case realization while selecting the best
ARO solution for the nominal case.
We have also adopted an alternative approach known as the folding horizon (FH), which
adjusts its decisions based on the actual demand observed in each period, allowing production
decisions to adapt to the evolving demands. It is important to note that, unlike ARO, the
FH approach does not guarantee feasibility for all potential demand scenarios. However, it
may outperform the ARO approach since it does not impose a speciﬁc functional form for
adjustable decisions. Therefore, our paper combines these two proposed approaches within a
framework referred to as the adaptive optimization approach. To compare the performance of
different methods within the adaptive approach on an equal basis, we have utilized a dedicated
Monte Carlo simulation approach. We conduct the proposed Monte Carlo experiment on a
real data set inspired by a real-life dairy industry company; the numerical results show that
the (Pareto) adjustable robust approach improves the OFV performance of the static robust
model by 4–14% on average and obtains solutions that are feasible and adjustable for different
realizations of the uncertainty parameter while the nominal solution cannot satisfy as high as
18% of the demand when demand realizes different than expected. Moreover, the simulation
results demonstrate that the FH approach improves the total cost by 5–28 % compared to the
robust solution. However, the extent of improvement is highly contingent upon the penalty
cost associated with unmet demands. The sensitivity analysis reveals that an increase in the
penalty cost may diminish the improvement achieved by the FH approach.
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section2 presents the literature
review. Section3 gives the problem deﬁnition and the proposed deterministic and (adjustable)
robust mathematical optimization models. Section4 provides the re-optimization approach
for Pareto efﬁciency, the developed heuristic to solve the problem, and the Monte Carlo simu-
lation procedure. The numerical experiments and the real-case adoption study are provided in
Sect.5. Finally, Sect.6 presents conclusions, managerial insights of the proposed approach,
and future research directions.
2 Literature review
Food logistics network management is a complex ﬁeld involving the planning, execution,
and control of the movement and storage of food products from their origin to their point of
consumption. The movement and storage of food products require the design, optimization

A substantial amount of literature is available on the topic, covering various aspects of
food logistics, such as supply chain management, transportation, warehousing, inventory
management, and food safety. This section starts with an overview of the studies that propose
quantitative models for managing food logistics networks. Then, among these studies, those
that employ robust approaches for food logistics management are highlighted to demonstrate
the contribution of our research to the relevant literature.
To have a general overview of food logistics problems, we ﬁrst searched for articles
indexed by the Web of Science Core Collection database. References of the studies obtained
are also reviewed to enhance the discussion. Table 1 presents the summary of the reviewed

characteristics of the food production planning and distribution problem addressed in this

Our literature review revealed that while many food logistics network management stud-
ies focus on problems with multi-period planning horizons, less attention has been given
to sequence-dependent setup costs, demand uncertainty, and temperature tracking. Mixed-
integer programming is the most prevalent modelling technique when formulating food
logistics problems confronted by various sectors. Our study contributes to the literature
by simultaneously considering the aforementioned characteristics, namely multi-period,
sequence-dependent setup cost, demand uncertainty, and temperature tracking (see Table
1). In the following, we will focus on food logistics problems with uncertainty and elaborate
on how our work is similar and different from the related studies in addressing uncertainty.
Govindan and Keyvanshokooh (2017) review the potential uncertainties in the design
of supply chain networks and methods to address them. Based on the review provided,
demand is one of the most inﬂuential sources of uncertainty in supply chain problems, and
RO, stochastic programming (SP), and fuzzy mathematical programming (FMP) are viable
strategies to address this problem. However, robust supply chain network design has received
less attention compared to SP and FMP and the use of afﬁnely adjustable robust counterpart
and distributionally RO approaches in this area remains an unexplored research direction.
Govindan and Keyvanshokooh (2017) also highlight that FMP can be effective in modeling
uncertainties where probability distributions are unknown but membership functions can
be deﬁned. Moreover, RO is a suitable tool for real-world applications where historical data
might be insufﬁcient. In our model, we considered demand uncertainty with an RO framework
that does not require prior probability distributions as in SP, making it particularly useful in
real-world applications with limited historical data. In line with this ﬁnding, it has also been
observed that the RO is a widely used method to deal with uncertainty in food logistics
network problems (see Table 1). For further details on SP, we refer readers to Ruszczy´nski
and Shapiro (2003) and Birge and Louveaux (2011); for further information on FMP, we refer
readers to Tanaka and Asai (1984) and Zimmermann (2011). It should be noted that this list
can be extended. Our study contributes to the relevant ﬁeld by proposing an RO model for a
real-world production and distribution problem in the dairy industry with uncertain demand.

Hasani and Nikbakhsh (2012) addressed a multi-product closed-loop supply chain prob-
lem with demand and purchasing cost uncertainty. The uncertain parameters reside in a
known interval, and RO is used to account for uncertainty. They demonstrate that the pro-
posed model is compatible with the context of decision-making in the high-tech electronic
and food industries and that it can handle uncertainty without requiring extensive histori-
cal data. Numerical results indicate that applying RO yields a solution that outperforms the
deterministic one with respect to feasibility. For a location and allocation problem charac-
terized by uncertainty in demand, Mirmajlesi and Shafaei (2016) propose an RO model. The
problem is solved using a commercial solver and a metaheuristic adopted for the allocation
problem. In Darestani and Hemmati (2019), a supply chain network problem is addressed
to minimize cost and greenhouse gas emissions. RO is employed to manage uncertainties in
demand, operational cost, transportation cost, and distribution center capacity. Another work
is Goli et al. (2019), which proposes a bi-objective optimization model for a product portfo-
lio problem in the dairy industry with uncertain product return and employs RO to manage
the associated uncertainty. The ﬁrst objective function minimizes investment risk, while the
second maximizes the portfolio’s total return. An MILP model is proposed in Rahbari et
al. (2019) for the routing problem with cross-docking centers. The model has two objective
functions: minimizing the cost of penalties for being early or late, inventory holding costs,
and transportation costs, and maximizing the total freshness of the products. The associated
bi-objective model is formulated as a single objective via the L1 metric method. By imple-
menting RO, the authors have considered the travel time and shelf-life of products to be
uncertain parameters. The numerical results show that compared to the product perishability,
the effect of travel time uncertainty is more signiﬁcant in OFV. In Shaﬁee et al. (2021), a
multi-objective RO is developed for a dairy company subject to demand, facility capacity,
and supply chain main cost uncertainties. The authors manage the perishability of their prod-
ucts using the FIFO policy for inventory management. The model enables a decision-maker
to identify the optimal network ﬂows given all of the associated uncertainties. For a supply
chain problem that seeks to minimize total cost and environmental pollution, RO is imple-
mented by the Yavari and Geraeli (2019) to deal with demand, return rate, and the quality
of returned product uncertainties. The authors provide heuristics to solve the problem and
evaluate the performance of the proposed approaches by analyzing a dairy company case
study. Apart from the reviewed RO attempts that respect food product perishability, some
other studies employ RO for the food industry but do not consider product perishability. An
and Ouyang (2016) present an RO model for a grain supply chain considering crop yields as
the uncertain parameter, Baghalian et al. (2013) develop a robust model for an agri-food sup-
ply chain by taking demand and supply uncertainty into account, and Hosseini-Motlagh and
Saadi (2020) propose a multi-objective mathematical optimization model for the wheat sup-
ply chain considering supply and demand uncertainties. Lastly, a few studies develop ARO
models for practical optimization problems, such as project scheduling (Bruni et al., 2017),
ﬂood protection (Postek et al., 2019), and supply chain management (Almaraj & Trafalis,
2020; Ben-Tal et al., 2005; Buhayenko & den Hertog, 2017), but no implementation to the
food industry speciﬁcally. As pointed out in the recent survey (Yanıko˘glu & Den Hertog,
2019), practical applications of ARO methods in the industry are lagging behind.
This paper contributes to the existing literature in the following ways.
1. This is the ﬁrst attempt to integrate the key components, such as perishability, temper-
ature tracking, sequence-dependent setup cost, and demand uncertainty, into a single

3 Production and distribution planning problem
In this section, we ﬁrst deﬁne the problem and propose the deterministic formulation of PDP
in Sect.3.1. The robust reformulation of PDP (RPDP) is presented in Sect.3.2. Section3.3
provides a less conservative adjustable robust reformulation of the PDP (ARPDP). Finally,
the overview of mathematical optimization models is given in Sect.3.4.
3.1 Problem definition and deterministic model
Given a production and distribution network with sets of production lines (P), holding cham-
bers (W), and retailers (R), PDP aims to ﬁnd the optimal production (Y), inventory (I),
backorder (B), and distribution (X) amounts of products at each period t ∈T of a multi-
period planning horizon to meet the retailers’ demand. Furthermore, the model decides on
the optimal temperature level of holding chambers (Z) at each period. Each product j ∈J is
manufactured on a production line, and is stored in a storage chamber or is shipped directly
to a retailer. Retailers are assumed to have limited storage capacity. The related inventory
has a certain capacity determined according to the demand in a predetermined time window
to avoid product perishability. The production lines and holding chambers are located in the
same facility. Figure1 demonstrates possible product ﬂows in the network.
123

In the related network, temperature tracking is required to avoid product quality degra-
dation during the storage phase, where the products are temporarily held in the holding
chambers before being distributed. The appropriate temperature levels are predeﬁned for
each product. Accordingly, different products can be stored in the same chambers as long
as they have the same temperature requirement. Some products are ﬂexible and can be
stored at different temperature levels, such as UHT milk that can be in the temperature
range of 4–20 ◦C, while other milk products are stored at a unique temperature level; for
example, cheese can only be stored at 4 ◦C. A holding chamber can operate at a single tem-
perature level at each period, and the associated temperature can be changed for the next
period by bearing a sequence-dependent setup cost. Therefore, one needs to optimize the
temperature selection decisions to minimize operational and set-up-related costs while sus-
taining a fresh product ﬂow. The chambers located in the factory have limited capacities,
and distributing a product from the factory to a retailer r ∈R takes ℓr period. Backo-
rdering is allowed and results in an additional cost for the producer. However, there is a
time limitation for backorder, and later deliveries than the demand period plus the trans-
portation time (ℓr) are not allowed. Lastly, retailers’ demands are assumed to be known
in advance, but this assumption will be relaxed in the upcoming subsections. All vehi-
cles used for transporting products must be equipped with control systems to maintain the
required environmental conditions, such as temperature, humidity, and air quality, which
vary depending on the type of product being transported. Products may require speciﬁc lev-
els of humidity or air quality to remain fresh. These control systems help ensure that the
products remain fresh and in good condition during transportation and meet the required
standards when they reach their destination. In this setting, transportation is managed by
third-party logistics (3PL) providers, who ensure that vehicles are properly equipped and
environmental conditions within each vehicle are consistently maintained at required lev-
els. This approach places the responsibility for vehicle allocation on the 3PL provider,
integrating it as part of their service offering. Consequently, the cost of distribution is
determined and deﬁned by the 3PL provider as part of their contractual agreement. The

3.2 Robust reformulation
A production plan that is designed for the expected (or nominal) demand may result in signif-
icant drawbacks to the system when the demand realizes differently; namely, the associated
plan will result either in overproduction or in lost sales. Hence, having a solution that is
resilient against demand uncertainty is essential in planning. In this setting, we do not have
any distributional information about the uncertain demand. Instead, we only know certain
lower and upper bounds for the demand of each retailer during the planning horizon. We
aim to propose a tractable approach that is capable of addressing the uncertain demand.
RO is the state-of-the-art approach that models/meets all of the aforementioned speciﬁca-
tions/requirements. RO capitalizes on computational efﬁciency and tractability and yields
solutions that are resilient against uncertainty. However, such beneﬁts come with an addi-
tional cost as the approach yields the optimal solution for the worst-case realization of the
uncertain data. For further details on RO, we refer the reader to Ben-Tal and Nemirovski
(2009), Gorissen and Den Hertog (2015).
The robust problem aims to ﬁnd the optimal solution that is feasible for all realizations of
the demands within the deﬁned bounded set with known lower and upper bounds for each
retailer. In [RPDP], the retailers’ demand in each period for each product is reformulated as

3.3 Adjustable robust reformulation
In [RPDP], we solve the addressed problem by considering retailers’ worst-case demands,
i.e., resulting in an over-conservative solution. This over-conservatism causes excessive pro-
duction, inventory, and distribution costs for the system when the worst-case demand is not
realized. To overcome the associated over-conservatism issue, in the remainder, we consider
holding chamber opening decisions as here-and-now that are decided at the beginning of the
planning horizon; and production, inventory, backorder, and distribution decisions as wait-
and-see decision variables that allow the decision-maker to wait until a part of the demand is
realized over time and update decisions accordingly. E.g., if a retailer’s demand is realized
less than its worst-case, the producer has a road map to update related wait-and-see decisions
and beneﬁt from the realized information. The corresponding road map is referred to as the
decision rule in the realm of ARO (Ben-Tal et al., 2004; Yanıko˘glu & Den Hertog, 2019).

3.4 Overview of mathematical optimization models
This section provides an overview of all the mathematical optimization models proposed in
this paper. The base deterministic model is given by [PDP]. The orthodox robust optimization
model (also called the static robust) that employs the worst-case uncertainty with respect to
the deﬁned intervals is denoted as [RPDP]. To decrease over-conservatism, we used linear
decision rules for production decisions and redeﬁned the related decisions as adjustable. This
reformulation is given by [ARPDP]. To further improve the performance of the adjustable
reformulation, we will adopt a re-optimization framework that yields Pareto-efﬁcient robust
solutions for the adjustable model. The Pareto robust is denoted as [PR]. Details of the
associated approach will be given in the next section. The ﬂow chart of all the mathematical
optimization models is shown in Fig.2.

4.1 Re-optimization approach for Pareto efficiency
A common issue in robust reformulations, which has yet to be widely acknowledged in
the literature, is the existence of alternative optimal solutions (Yanıko˘glu & Den Hertog,
123

Annals of Operations Research
2019). It is even more prevalent in adjustable robust reformulations, where linear decision
rules, like those used in this study, are typically employed. It is important to note that if an
(adjustable) robust optimal reformulation results in alternative optimal solutions, commercial
solvers may return any of these solutions, which are robust against uncertainty and provide
the same worst-case outcome. However, these solutions can lead to signiﬁcantly different
OFVs when non-worst-case data is realized. One possible remedy for this is incorporating
Pareto efﬁciency to improve the quality of ARO solutions further.
In this section, we aim to identify the best solution among these alternatives, speciﬁcally
in terms of nominal data. We refer to this solution as the Pareto-efﬁcient robust solution.
Adopting a Pareto-efﬁcient framework in a single step is impossible in this setting. Thus,
we solve the (adjustable) robust counterpart in the ﬁrst step and report the optimal OFV.
Using the OFV from the ﬁrst step as a constraint in the second step, we ensure robustness
while identifying the improved adjustable decisions for nominal data. This two-step approach
considers more than one OFV, as the robust optimal OFV is used as a constraint while
optimizing for the nominal data in the objective in the second iteration.
To this end, we initially solve [ARPDP] and record its optimal OFV as (obj*[ARPDP]);
next, we re-optimize the coefﬁcients of the decision rules for the nominal data, ensuring
that the selected coefﬁcients are still optimal for the worst-case realization of the uncertain
parameter. The related re-optimization algorithm is presented as follows.

4.2 Heuristic approaches to solve [ARPDP]
[ARPDP] is a computationally expensive MILP, and a standard personal computer usually
cannot solve realistically sized instances to optimality due to huge memory requirement;
[RPDP], on the other hand, is a relatively more tractable mathematical optimization model
than [ARPDP], and it can be solved for practically sized instances. Table 3 compares the
number of constraints and decision variables of both [RPDP] and [ARPDP] for different-

the associated mathematical optimization models become linear optimization (LO) models

[ARPDP] becomes LO problem when the binary decisions are ﬁxed to yield high-quality

4.3 Folding horizon approach
As explained in the adjustable approach, updating decisions throughout the planning horizon
based on the actual data can lead to improved production and distribution choices. ARO
utilizes decision rules to adapt solutions according to changing demand values. However, the
FH approach addresses the same issue by repeatedly solving the problem based on the demand
realized over time while maintaining past decisions and re-optimizing upcoming decisions
within the time horizon. The FH approach is speciﬁcally designed for the expected demand
scenarios and is not robust. Therefore, if the demand turns out to be worst-case, simply
re-optimizing the problem based on the realized demand may not be sufﬁcient to restore
feasibility. In such cases, penalty costs are incurred to meet the demand through outsourcing.

4.4 Overview of matheuristics
In this paper, we propose two heuristic approaches to solve ARO models and one adaptive
heuristic approach that resembles the conventional optimization-based solution approach
used in practice, referred to as the folding horizon. The ﬂow chart of the proposed heuristics
is given in Fig.4.
As highlighted on the left side of the chart, we propose two solution methods for adjustable
reformulations. Both methods begin by ﬁxing the binary decisions by solving the static robust
reformulation [RPDP] in the ﬁrst step. Once the binary decisions are ﬁxed, the mathemat-
ical complexity of the problem is reduced to linear optimization, allowing the adjustable
decisions to be optimized in the second step. The key difference between [ARPDP(RPDP)]
and [PR(RPDP)] is that the Pareto-efﬁcient reformulation also incorporates the optimal OFV
from the static robust solution as a constraint and solves the problem using nominal data in
the objective.
On the other hand, the folding horizon approach is more practice-oriented, relying on a
deterministic model based on nominal data. As demand is realized over time, the remaining
123

Annals of Operations Research
periods are re-optimized using [FHP] according to the updated information on demand and
inventory while continuing to use nominal demand estimates for the unrealized periods.
Implementing heuristic solutions in practice. It is essential to note that all the heuristic
approaches presented in this paper are adaptive, meaning they update decisions based on
demand information as it becomes available over time. While the two adjustable robust
approaches, [ARPDP(RPDP)] and [PR(RPDP)], rely on decision rules optimized at the start
of the planning horizon, the FH approach focuses on re-optimizing the deterministic model
according to the realized demand over time. In practical applications, the decision-maker
must be able to monitor updated demand closely and have the ﬂexibility to adjust decisions
at the beginning of each period based on the latest data.
4.5 Monte Carlo simulation procedure
A direct comparison among the optimal OFVs of the proposed models may lead to false
conclusions because each model yields an optimal solution for a different data set, i.e., while
[PDP] and [FHP] are solved for the nominal demand, [RPDP] and [ARPDP] are solved for
the worst-case. In this section, we propose a Monte Carlo simulation approach to analyze the
OFV performances of [PDP], [RPDP], [ARPDP] and [FHP] on equal grounds. To this end,
we uniformly sample the demand from the uncertainty set; next, if the solution at hand is
infeasible for the sampled demand, we regain the feasibility of the solution with redemptive
decisions by incurring penalty costs. On the contrary, if the resulting solution is feasible,
we re-optimize auxiliary decisions to improve the optimality performance of the solution
at hand according to the sampled data. Ultimately, we calculate OFVs of feasible models
(after the corrective decisions are taken) over sampled demands and report the average OFV
performance of each model. In the remainder, we delve into details of the Monte Carlo
simulation experiments for different models.

holding chambers may operate in two different temperatures, i.e., 4–5 ◦C and 20–22 ◦C. UHT
milk and doogh could be stored at both temperature levels, while cheese, yogurt, and cream
can only be stored in 4–5 ◦C. The per-unit production cost consists of labor, machinery, and
electricity usage costs of the related production line. The transportation cost depends on the
distance of the factory from the retailers and the temperature requirement of the product
in transportation, which is approximated to be 10% of the production cost multiplied by
the transportation time of a given retailer. The holding cost of each product depends on the
temperature setting of the related holding chamber and the product type. It is approximated
to be 15% of the production cost if the product is kept in 20–22 ◦C and 20% of production
cost if kept in 4–5 ◦C. The backorder cost of each product is determined according to the
customer’s loss of goodwill and is approximated to be 25% of the production cost. The prob-
lem is solved every week for the six active days of the week; the factory has two shifts on
the ﬁrst ﬁve days of the week, and on the sixth day, the factory has one shift, and machines
must be deactivated 5–6h earlier for sanitation. The capacities of the production lines are
ﬁxed during the weekdays; nevertheless, on the ﬁrst day of the week, they work with 60%
capacity because of the setup requirements. Lastly, daily milk, cheese, yogurt, and cream are
produced at half capacity during the night shifts. The initial inventory for each product is
equal to the average daily demand for the product. The parameter values used in this section
are given in “Appendix F”.

6 Conclusion and discussions
In this article, we addressed the production and distribution planning problem (PDP),
focusing on the challenges posed by factors such as perishability, temperature tracking,
sequence-dependent facility setup cost, and uncertain demand. We proposed a comprehen-
sive framework that integrates these factors into a uniﬁed approach for effectively managing
production and distribution processes in industries dealing with perishable products. Our
approach employed adjustable robust optimization (ARO) and folding horizon (FH) to handle
demand uncertainty and decrease conservatism in the proposed solutions. We demonstrated
the limitations of using nominal demand in production planning through extensive Monte
Carlo simulations. The deterministic production plan proved obsolete when demand was
realized differently than the nominal demand, resulting in signiﬁcant unmet demand. There-
fore, decision-makers must consider demand uncertainty to ensure resilient production and
distribution plans. Furthermore, we highlighted the issue of overproduction when employ-
ing robust approaches. To address this, we proposed an ARO- and FH-based framework
that signiﬁcantly reduces disposals compared to the static robust approach. The proposed
heuristic algorithms provided high-quality solutions for realistically sized instances within a
reasonable time frame.
In the following, we discuss how organizations can beneﬁt from the presented research.
Importance of adaptive production and distribution planning. The proposed models and
solution approaches effectively provide a uniﬁed framework for managing challenges such
as perishability, temperature tracking, sequence-dependent facility setup cost, and uncer-
tain demand, and decision-makers can make informed decisions that minimize costs and
maximize resource utilization. While the static robust approach protects against demand
uncertainty, it may lead to overproduction and increased disposals, which have signiﬁcant
environmental externalities. To address this over-conservatism issue, we introduced an adap-
tive framework that signiﬁcantly reduces disposals compared to the robust approach. Our
numerical results showed substantial improvements, with disposal reduction reaching up to
31% by adopting the adjustable approach where producers can improve their manufacturing
costs and minimize the adverse environmental effects of surplus production. The improve-
ment becomes more signiﬁcant when the FH approach is adopted.
The required infrastructure for adaptive decisions. We acknowledge that implementing
adaptive decision-making approaches like ARO and FH in supply chains requires a thor-
ough integration of technology, processes, and infrastructure to manage demand uncertainty
effectively. Essential components include a sophisticated data infrastructure for real-time
monitoring, cutting-edge optimization software capable of solving customized MILP mod-
els, and ﬂexible cloud-based computing resources to handle the computational requirements
of ongoing re-optimization. Moreover, seamless integration with current enterprise resource
planning and supply chain managementsystems is crucialfor aligning adaptive decisions with
overall operations. It is imperative to provide continuous training and implement effective
change management strategies for staff members, equipping them with the necessary skills
to use new decision support tools efﬁciently. Investing in these elements enables organiza-
tions to build resilient supply chains that dynamically adapt to ﬂuctuations, thereby reducing
waste, particularly in the perishable goods industry.
Effect of ignoring uncertainty. Our ﬁndings highlight the ﬂaw of relying solely on nominal
demand in production planning for perishable products. The Monte Carlo simulations demon-
strated that the deterministic production plan becomes obsolete when demand is realized
differentlythanthenominaldemand.Thisdiscrepancycanresultinsigniﬁcantunmetdemand,

reaching up to 18%. Therefore, decision-makers need to account for demand uncertainty to
ensure the resilience of production and distribution plans. By incorporating uncertainty into
the planning models, they can make proactive adjustments and avoid costly disruptions in
meeting customer demand. The real-case adoption study results show that the total cost may
be decreased up to 14% by adopting the proposed adjustable approach.
Aligning approach with supply chain risk. ARO and FH approaches provide managers
with dynamic tools to make production and distribution decisions that align with real-time
demand, a critical capability in the unpredictable perishable food market. These methods
help minimize costs associated with unmet demand while reducing environmental impact by
avoiding overproduction and excess inventory. Particularly in sectors like fresh food, health-
care, and catering, these approaches serve as decision-support systems that enhance resource
efﬁciency and reduce waste. ARO is best suited for supply chains facing high uncertainty
and requiring strategic stability, as it offers resilience against worst-case scenarios. However,
it may result in higher holding and production costs. In contrast, FH is more appropriate for
supply chains with moderate demand ﬂuctuations, where real-time adaptability provides cost
savings and operational ﬂexibility, especially when conservative demand scenarios are less
likely.
Managers should prioritize ARO when resilience under uncertainty is critical, such as
when demand is expected to skew higher than its nominal value. For instance, when the
underlying demand distribution is left-skewed, ARO ensures robustness while its Pareto-
efﬁcient re-optimization step mitigates over-conservatism and balances resilience with cost
efﬁciency, as evidenced by the numerical results in Sect.5.2. On the other hand, FH becomes
advantageous when demand realizations are more evenly distributed around nominal values.
As demand data becomes more precise or reliable over time, transitioning to FH allows
for greater ﬂexibility, enabling plans to adapt dynamically and reduce costs based on real-
time insights. This combination of approaches is particularly effective in perishable food
production, where demand patterns often stabilize closer to production or distribution dates.
Disposal cost considerations in uncertainty modeling. In this study, we also incorporate
disposal costs when evaluating the performance of the proposed approaches. In all the models
and solution approaches designed to address uncertainty, we may face lower-than-expected
demand, which results in excess production that must be disposed of. In the simulation,
we account for the amount of waste generated and its associated cost within the objective
function. Speciﬁcally, we track the waste amount and apply penalties accordingly in all
models, and when demand falls below the expected demand scenario, the excess amount is
disposed of directly and penalized in the simulation models. We compute the disposal cost
and report it in numerical instances to facilitate comparing the models’ performance with
respect to waste costs, thus enabling the decision-maker to select the most suitable approach
for their system.
Effects of disposal and penalty costs in approach selection. The sensitivity analysis
on penalty and disposal costs also reveals essential insights for choosing between the
adjustable robust approach and the FH approach. The FH approach signiﬁcantly improves
when demands are sampled from U(−ρ, +ρ) while the adjustable approach consistently
improves the solution across different penalty costs. Increasing the penalty cost for unmet
demand negatively affects the FH approach as it penalizes higher demands, impacting overall
improvement. When demands exceed the nominal value, a higher penalty cost can penal-
ize the FH approach excessively, resulting in no improvement compared to the static robust
solution. In contrast, the adjustable approach achieves 3–6% improvement in such cases. On
the other hand, increasing the disposal cost positively impacts both approaches. Therefore,
the choice between the adjustable robust approach and the FH approach should consider the

sampled from less conservative sets since the approach is worst-case-oriented. It has more
space for improvement compared to situations with more conservative demand distributions.
Importance of temperature tracking. Considering temperature-tracking requirements in
the model offers insights into efﬁciently managing storage chambers for supply chains that
are dealing with highly perishable goods. Managers can optimize temperature settings to
maintain product quality and minimize setup costs related to temperature changes, ensuring
fresh delivery to retailers.
