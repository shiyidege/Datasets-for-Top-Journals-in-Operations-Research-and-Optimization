Abstract
In recent years, many sports tournaments have increased the number of participating 
teams, which resulted in larger number of matches. These expansions have raised many 
calls to reduce the number of matches because of significant logistical and physiologi­
cal concerns. This paper examines the effects of reducing the number of matches in a 
widely used round-robin tournament format featuring four teams. We leverage a 2017 
structural change in professional beach volleyball, where the number of matches per team 
in the round-robin (pool) stage was reduced from three to two. Utilising data from 6976 
matches, our multivariate regression analyses reveal that this reduction does not impact 
the probability of a favourite team winning a single match nor the overall tournament's 
efficacy, measured by the final standings' alignment with teams' initial strengths. These 
findings hold across different strength definitions and genders. Our study offers insights 
for sports seeking to expand the number of teams while preserving a four-team round-
robin structure.
Keywords  Dynamic scheduling · Efficacy · OR in sports · Round-robin tournament · 
tournament design
1  Introduction
In recent years, many sports organisations have increased the number of participating teams 
in their main tournaments, which resulted in the increase in the number of matches. These 
include football (FIFA World Cup, UEFA Champions League), basketball (EuroLeague, 
FIBA World Cup), rugby (Nations Championship, Sevens series), handball (IHF World

Championship), etc. Such changes are supposed to generate higher revenue through addi­
tional matches and increase the interest in the sports.
However, the increase in the number of matches raises significant logistical and physi­
ological concerns. The already extensive international match calendar may expose players 
to a higher risk of injuries (Page et al., 2023), potentially costing (football) clubs hundreds 
of thousands of euros per month (Ekstrand, 2013). As evidence of the urgency of this topic, 
FIFPRO Europe and the European Leagues, respectively representing European football 
player unions and national leagues, recently filed a complaint with the European Commis­
sion against FIFA (the governing body of international football) over the latter’s imposition 
of another new tournament, which has taken place in the summer of 2025, onto the interna­
tional match calendar (La Liga, 2024). They claim that FIFA’s imposition of its decisions on 
the international calendar is an abuse of dominance, and that FIFA is harming the economic 
interests of national leagues and the health and safety of players in European football.
The increase in the number of matches also raises an important question regarding tour­
nament design. For example, in 2017, FIFA decided to increase the number of teams in the 
2026 FIFA World Cup from 32 to 48. Initially, it was decided that there would be 16 groups 
of three teams, with each team playing two matches and the top two teams advancing to 
the Round of 32. However, that format had the serious drawback of possible collusion à 
la ‘Disgrace of Gijón’; that is, the teams playing in the last match of a group could agree 
on a desired result that would qualify both for the next round, at the expense of the third 
team in the group (Chater et al., 2021; Guyon, 2020; Kendall & Lenten, 2017). Thus, after 
heated debates FIFA changed its mind, deciding in March 2023 that the new format would 
feature 12 groups of four teams. In this format, each team plays three matches, and the top 
two teams from each group—along with the eight best third-placed teams—advance to the 
Round of 32 (Guajardo & Krumer, 2024).
The result of this decision is an increase in the maximal number of matches per team 
from seven to eight, and the total number of matches from 64 to 104. This extends the tour­
nament duration from 28 and 31 days (in the last two tournaments, respectively) to 39 days. 
Such an increase in the duration of the tournament obviously reduces the rest time for the 
players of the teams that progress to the final stages, assuming that national associations do 
not reduce the number of matches in their leagues.
Another possible problem with the new format is that the increased number of matches 
requires a very extensive schedule in terms of the number of kick-off times per day. Accord­
ing to the proposed schedule, there will be four matches per day across four different kick-
off times over 11 consecutive days, followed by six matches per day over three different 
kick-off times in the last 4 days of the group stage. Based on the well-known diminishing 
marginal utility, according to which too many matches—even among high-quality teams—
do not necessarily increase demand (Di Mattia & Krumer, 2023), it is safe to assume that 
such an extensive schedule will not maximise the TV viewership per match, since most 
people will be unable to watch all these matches. However, it is possible that the overall TV 
viewership may increase.
Given the desire of sports organisations to expand in terms of the number of teams but 
still using a four-team round-robin structure (e.g. football, handball, basketball), the natural 
question is whether it is possible to introduce changes into this structure. To address the 
logistical and physiological concerns, the most desired change is obviously to reduce the 
number of matches within the round-robin. However, it is also important that such a change

would not affect the efficacy of the tournament, according to which teams’ final standings 
are naturally ordered according to their strengths (Arlegi & Dimitrov, 2020; Devriesere et 
al., 2025a; Groh et al., 2012; Sziklai et al., 2022).
In this paper, we take advantage of a unique opportunity to empirically investigate round-
robin tournament format between four teams, in which each team plays three matches, and 
compare it with a round-robin format between four teams in the same sport, in which each 
team plays only two matches. Given that the final standings are the result of the outcomes of 
individual matches, our aim is to investigate whether a reduction in the number of matches 
influences the favourite teams’ probabilities of winning a single match as well as the over­
all tournament's efficacy, measured by the final standings' alignment with teams' initial 
strengths.
To this end, we leverage a structural change that appeared in professional beach volley­
ball. In 2017, the governing body of international volleyball, the Fédération Internationale 
de Volleyball (FIVB), redesigned its Beach Volleyball World Tour by introducing a tourna­
ment hierarchy of five levels and reducing the number of round-robin matches per team 
from the traditional three to two. Before the change, each team played three matches (once 
against each of the other teams). In the new format, the initial matches were played between 
the highest-ranked team against the lowest and the second-highest against the third. In the 
subsequent round, winners faced winners and losers faced losers. In both cases, there was no 
change in the qualifying criterion where the winners of the group stage advanced directly to 
the round of 16, while the teams finishing second and third moved on to the playoffs.
Analysing 6976 beach volleyball matches from the round-robin stage across 169 compe­
titions (88 for men and 81 for women), our regression analyses reveal no significant differ­
ence between the probabilities of the favourite team winning a single match before and after 
the change. The reduction in the number of matches also did not affect the final standings 
based on the teams’ strengths across both genders and two different measures of strengths.
Given that the new tournament format with two rounds is scheduled dynamically, it 
essentially becomes a Swiss-system tournament with two rounds. Thus, we conclude that, 
at least for the particular schedule investigated in our paper, the efficacy of a round-robin 
tournament and a Swiss-system with two rounds coincides if there are four teams that com­
pete for one big and two small prizes.1
We obviously acknowledge that each sport has its unique characteristics, and that beach 
volleyball differs from other sports in general and football in particular (see Sect. 7 for fur­
ther discussion). Thus, it is possible that the findings obtained for beach volleyball are not 
true for football. However, our findings suggest that FIFA could at least consider using a 
similar format, with two matches per team in a group of four teams, in its secondary com­
petitions before deciding about such a change in the World Cup.
The structure of this paper is as follows: Sect. 2 covers the literature review. In Sect. 3, 
we detail the institutional settings of the primary beach volleyball competitions. Section 4 
presents the data, along with some descriptive results. The empirical strategy is outlined 
in Sect. 5. The results are presented in Sect. 6. Finally, Sect. 7 offers concluding remarks.

2  Literature review
Round-robin tournaments have been widely investigated within the operational research 
(OR) community. Previous contributions have focused on a range of topics related to sched­
uling, such as minimising breaks between home and away matches (Rasmussen & Trick, 
2007), minimising travel distances (Osicka & Guajardo, 2023; Ribeiro & Urrutia, 2007; 
Toffolo et al., 2019), rescheduling matches (Yi & Goossens, 2023), scheduling during holi­
days (Kendall, 2008), optimising venue usage (Li et al., 2023; Van Bulck & Goossens, 
2020) and making referee assignments (Durán et al., 2022).2
This paper contributes to several strands in the literature on round-robin tournaments. 
First, as it deals with the probabilities of winning, it relates to the literature on the effect 
of the schedule on performance in round-robin tournaments. Previous studies have found 
different schedule-related features to affect the probabilities of winning, ranging from the 
day of the week (Goller & Krumer, 2020) and the kick-off time (Krumer, 2020) to the serial 
number of the match within the round-robin tournament (Krumer et al., 2017a, 2017b, 2020; 
Krumer & Lechner, 2017; Laica et al., 2021; Lauber et al., 2023; Sahm, 2019). Thus, we 
contribute to the literature by providing, for the first time, empirical evidence of the effect of 
a reduction in the number of matches on the probabilities of winning in round-robin tourna­
ments with four teams, which is a widely used format.
Our paper also contributes to the literature on tournament efficacy, which is a well-
known criterion in OR literature. The main idea behind the efficacy of a tournament is 
based on the tournament’s ability to rank contestants according to their real strengths; in 
other words, tournament efficacy relates to whether the final standings in a tournament are 
naturally ordered according to the teams’ strengths (Arlegi & Dimitrov, 2020; Devriesere 
et al., 2025a; Groh et al., 2012; Sziklai et al., 2022). We build on this idea by expanding the 
analysis to the level of a single match, where we also study the probability of a favourite 
player winning a single match.
One way to achieve greater efficacy is to use a seeding system that helps stronger contes­
tants by restricting the role of luck. Following this approach, an extensive body of literature 
on optimal seeding has been developed over the years for elimination (Dagaev & Suzdalt­
sev, 2018; Hennessy & Glickman, 2016; Marchand, 2002) and round-robin tournaments 
(Cea et al., 2020; Csató, 2020; Scarf et al., 2009). However, most of these studies have used 
either an axiomatic approach or simulation techniques. We contribute to this strand of lit­
erature by comparing the above-defined efficacy in round-robin tournaments with different 
numbers of matches by means of multivariate regression analyses, in which we account for 
different deviations from randomness that may appear in real tournament settings.
As mentioned in the introduction, given the dynamic scheduling of the round-robin tour­
nament with two matches per team, our paper also relates to the literature on Swiss-system 
tournaments that has received some attention in the OR literature over the years (e.g., Csató, 
2017; Dong et al., 2023; Ólafsson, 1990). The unique feature of Swiss system is dynamic 
scheduling, which has recently been suggested to mitigate the risk of collusion in football 
(Stronka, 2024).
Finally, our paper relates to the literature on unintended consequences of badly designed 
tournaments. One such consequence is incentive incompatibility, which refers to situations 
in which teams may have incentives to lose (Csató, 2020, 2021, 2022; Dagaev & Sonin,

3  Institutional settings
Professional beach volleyball tournaments fall within three main types of competitions, held 
separately for men and women: the Summer Olympic Games, the FIVB World Champion­
ships, and the FIVB World Tour. The Olympic Games occur every 4 years, while the FIVB 
World Championships take place every 2 years. The third type of competition, the FIVB 
Beach Volleyball World Tour, was established in the late 1980s.4 As a series of international 
tournaments, the World Tour hosted multiple events in different countries throughout the 
year, making it the primary professional circuit for beach volleyball. It allowed athletes 
to compete internationally and gain the ranking points necessary to qualify for prestigious 
tournaments. The World Tour was instrumental in promoting beach volleyball worldwide.
In the World Tour, tournaments were divided according to different criteria, such as the 
level and quality of the facilities, competition format, monetary prizes, world ranking points, 
and requirements for television production and broadcasting. Before 2017, the World Tour 
comprised Grand Slams, Majors, and Opens. In 2017, the FIVB moved to a star ranking 
system with five levels, from one (least prestigious) to five stars (most prestigious), as well 
as the Finals at the end of the season. There were two main reasons behind this change. First, 
to promote beach volleyball both globally and locally. Second, to make it more spectator-
friendly since the star-rating system can distinguish more easily between the competition 
levels of the tournaments. The new system offered more events in an increasing number of 
hosting countries, opening up new commercial opportunities that were instrumental in the 
development of beach volleyball.
Following this transition, many tournaments changed their round-robin (pool) stage for­
mat. Before 2017, most top-level tournaments (Grand Slams, Majors, and Opens) had eight 
pools (groups) with four teams each. Teams used to play against each other for a total of 
three matches per team in a fixed schedule as presented in Fig. 1. Teams’ final standings 
at the end of the pool stage are determined based on the number of wins. In case of a tie 
between the teams, in 2014–2015, the tiebreaker rule was based on the head-to-head crite­
rion, according to which the winner in the head-to-head match was ranked higher. Starting 
from 2016, the team with higher rally point ratio (total points won vs total points lost) in 
the matches between all the teams in the pool, is ranked higher. The head-to-head is applied 
only if the rally points are the same among the teams. The team that finished first in the pool

Starting in 2017, the number of matches played by each team in the pool stage decreased 
from three to two (Fig. 2). More specifically, on the first match day, the highest-ranked team 
in the pool competed against the lowest, and the second-ranked team competed against 
the third-ranked. On the second (and last) match day, the two winners faced each other, 
as did the two losers. Both the qualification of teams and the elimination stage remained 
unchanged and were identical to those used before 2017. This is important, since a change 
in the award system can affect performance (Krumer et al., 2020).
The allocation of teams in the pool stage—including whether they qualified automati­
cally or had to play in the qualification round first—was based on their entry points and 
seed points, which were determined by the past performance of individual players. Players’ 
entry points were calculated based on their best six results from the last eight FIVB World

After seed points were calculated to determine the teams’ rankings, the teams were allo­
cated to different pools based on these rankings. The allocation process was not random. 
Instead, there was a deliberate and strategic placement of teams to ensure balance across 
all pools. Initially, each of the top eight ranked teams was placed into a separate pool, from 
Pool A to Pool H, as shown in Table 1. Following this initial placement, the distribution of 
the remaining teams followed a serpentine pattern to prevent any single pool from becom­
ing disproportionately strong or weak. Starting with the teams ranked 9–16, these teams 
were assigned in reverse order, beginning with Pool H and moving backwards to Pool A. 
Continuing in this fashion, the teams ranked 17–24 were then allocated, starting again from 
Pool A to Pool H. The sequence was reversed for teams 25–32, which were placed starting 
from Pool H back to Pool A.
Additionally, the seeding process included special considerations for host country teams. 
The highest-ranked host country team was awarded Rank 1 and placed in Pool A, provided 
they were among the top eight teams. If not, they were assigned Rank 8 in Pool H. Subse­
quent host country teams were ranked based on their seed points, with careful adjustments 
being made to ensure that no pool contained more than one host team. These adjustments 
enabled tournament organisers to determine each pool’s final composition, integrating the 
general seeding policy with the aforementioned exception for the host teams.

4.2  Pool-level data
To evaluate whether the final standings were affected by the structural change in the pool 
stage, we aggregated the data at the pool level, creating one observation per team in each 
pool. This allowed us to explore the relationship between the initial rankings and final stand­
ings. To do so, we ranked the teams in each pool based on their seed points (Ranking seed 
pts.) and entry points (Ranking entry pts.). If two teams had the same number of seed or 
entry points, they were assigned the same ranking.
To ensure consistency in our analysis, we followed the same idea used for the match level 
by excluding pools in which one or more matches were forfeited or retired. For the men’s 
case, we initially had 2816 observations across 704 pools. After removing all pools with for­
feited or retired matches, the number of observations decreased to 2696 (1500 under the old 
format and 1196 under the new format), and the number of pools decreased to 674. For the 
women’s case, we started with 2592 observations across 648 pools. After removing all pools 
with forfeited or retired matches, the number of observations decreased to 2496 (1448 under 
the old format and 1048 under the new format), and the number of pools decreased to 624.
The final standings of the teams were determined according to the official FIVB Sport 
Regulations Manual, in which ties are broken by match points, set ratio, rally point ratio, 
and, if necessary, by drawing lots (Fédération Internationale de Volleyball, 2016, p. 31). As 
with the match-level data, the variable Home advantage was assigned a value of one if a 
team played in its home country and zero otherwise.

5  Estimation strategy
5.1  Single-match level
We analyse the impact of a structural change in the pool stage of the FIVB World Tour 
Beach Volleyball tournaments on teams’ probabilities of winning at the level of a single 
match. Obviously, a naïve approach of correlating a dummy variable representing the new 
format with the favourite team’s probability of winning might yield biased and inconsistent 
estimates, due to several factors that might differ between the tournaments played under 
the new and the old formats. As evident from Tables 2 and 3, the tournaments may differ 
in terms of the monetary prize, home advantage, entry points and seed points, all of which 
may affect the probability of winning. These factors may also affect other characteristics, 
such as the allocation to different pools, which may in turn affect the probability of winning.
Given the binary nature of the final outcome of the beach volleyball match, we employ a 
multiple logit regression (i.e. logistic regression with multiple control variables), where we 
try to account for the above-mentioned deviations from randomness by using the following 
specification:
 
Yfupt = α1 · NewFormatfupt + γ′Xfupt + µp + εfupt
(1)
where Yfupt receives a value of one if the favourite f won against underdog u in a match in 
pool p of competition t and receives a value of zero in case of a loss. NewFormatfupt is the 
variable of main interest that receives a value of one if a match was played under the new 
format and zero otherwise. Our set of control variables Xfupt includes a measure of teams’ 
strengths, reflected by either seed or entry points of each team, depending on the specifi­
cation, as well as dummy variables of whether each team reached the pool stage via the 
qualification stage. We also include the monetary incentives in the form of the logarithm 
of each tournament’s monetary award.8 Furthermore, we use two dummy variables for the 
home advantage for both the favourite and the underdog teams. In addition, given that the 
8 Log transformations are often used in skewed datasets, such as GDP or other monetary measures. This is 
similar to our case where some tournaments have significantly higher monetary prizes than the others (see 
the distribution in Tables 2–3). Thus, log transforming the monetary prizes allows making the data more 
normal, and with lower variability.

5.2  Pool level
Following the idea presented in Arlegi and Dimitrov (2020), Devriesere et al. (2025a), Groh 
et al., (2012), and Sziklai et al. (2022), we examine whether final standings are naturally 
ordered according to the teams’ initial rankings, as measured by entry or seed points. To 
investigate this, we first compare the final standings for each rank in a single pool using the 
Wilcoxon rank-sum test. The Wilcoxon rank-sum test, also known as the Mann–Whitney U 
test, is a non-parametric statistical test used to determine whether there is a significant dif­
ference between the distributions of two independent samples (StataCorp, 2021).
In addition, as discussed above, given some changes in other variables over the years, 
such as monetary prizes, entry points, and seed points, all of which can affect the final stand­
ings, we also employed a multiple regression, where we control for these factors. Since the 
dependent variable is categorical and ordered, we employed an ordered logit regression 
using the following specification:
 
Yipt = β1 · NewFormatipt + β2 · StrengthRankipt + β3 · NewFormatipt
× StrengthRankipt + γ′Xipt + µp + εipt

(2)
where Yipt is team i’s final standing in pool p of competition t. Most importantly, we intro­
duce the variable StrengthRankipt that represents teams’ ranks based on their initial measure 
of strength. For that, we rank the teams according to their measure of strengths (entry or 
seed points) within each pool, such that the team with the highest number of relevant points 
is ranked one, second highest is ranked two and so on. Then, we interact between the New­
Format variable and a team’s relevant strength measure (NewFormatipt × StrengthRankipt). 
This interaction allows us to capture the effect of the structural change on the teams’ final 
standings for each rank of initial strength measured by the teams’ seed or entry points. Xipt is 
a set of control variables. These variables include the tournament’s monetary prize, whether 
a team played in its home country, and team’s points (i.e. seed or entry, depending on the 
measure used). Like before, µp represents pool-letter (Pools A–H) fixed effects. Finally, εipt 
is the error term.

7  Conclusion
This paper was motivated by the desire of international sports federations to increase the 
number of participating teams in their tournaments by preserving a four-team round-robin 
structure (e.g., football, handball). Such expansions result in tightened schedules, which in 
turn may affect players’ performance, their health conditions, and the interests of national 
leagues. This suggests that modifying the four-team round-robin tournaments to reduce the 
number of matches is worth considering.
This paper explored whether such a modification affects the probability of the favou­
rite team winning a single match or the teams’ final standings as a function of their initial 
strengths. Our analysis focused on a structural change in professional beach volleyball, in 
which the number of matches per team in the pool stage of the competitions was reduced 
from three to two.
Our results suggest that (at least) in beach volleyball, a shorter round-robin tournament 
does not affect the overall tournament efficacy as measured by the natural order of teams’ 
final standings based on their initial strengths (Arlegi & Dimitrov, 2020; Devriesere et al., 
2025a; Groh et al., 2012; Sziklai et al., 2022), nor the favourite teams’ probability of win­
ning a single match.
Although these results are promising, we must be careful before generalising them to 
football or other sports due to the many differences in game dynamics, team sizes and match 
durations compared with beach volleyball. Another important difference between football 
and beach volleyball is that football matches can end in a draw, which is impossible in beach 
volleyball.10 However, despite the differences between the sports, we suggest testing the 
format with two matches per team in secondary tournaments to validate its broader applica­
bility. Youth football tournaments (e.g. the Under 20 and Under 17 FIFA World Cups) can 
be used to test this format, especially given the fact that FIFA has decided to increase their 
frequencies from once in two years to every year.
While a format of two matches per team in the group stage of a tournament may address 
the criticism regarding the large number of matches and the increased duration of a tour­

would play only two matches before being eliminated compared to three in the approved 
format. Second, there will be 24 fewer matches compared with the number of matches in the 
approved format, which may result in lower revenues. However, FIFA has already agreed 
to similar conditions in its initial decision from 2017 to adopt 16 groups of three teams, 
with each team playing two matches in the group stage. That format also led to a total of 48 
matches in the group stage, which is the same as the number of matches in the new format 
from beach volleyball analysed here. Finally, another concern is that such a dynamic sched­
uling of the second round creates uncertainty for fans, since they do not know where and 
when their team should play. While not solving all the logistical problems, the “Team Spe­
cific Tickets” packages offered at the 2026 FIFA World Cup, allow fans to have tickets to the 
matches of their team in the group stage regardless the date and location of the matches.11
Further discussion is also needed regarding the scheduling of the second match day in 
the group. In our study, the winners of the first match day played against each other in the 
second match day, and the losers similarly played against each other. However, Guajardo 
and Krumer (2024) have pointed out that such a schedule may create incentives to lose in 
the first match to avoid meeting a strong opponent in the second match. A possible alterna­
tive could be for the winners of the first match to play against the losers of the first match. 
In that case, every team in every match would have strong incentives to win. That is, the 
winners of the first match have incentives to win in the second match to advance directly to 
the round of 16. A loss in the second match may result in additional playoffs round or even 
in elimination from the tournament, depending on the result of the other match of the second 
match day. The losers of the first match have incentives to win in the second match to not 
being eliminated. A win, would result in qualification to additional playoffs round or even 
to the Round of 16, depending on the result of the other match of the second match day. In 
such a case, all teams would have incentives to win in the first match as well. Unfortunately, 
our empirical study could not examine these alternatives, and we leave it for future research.
Finally, while outside of the scope of this paper, we noticed a substantial reduction in the 
number of forfeit matches after the change for both genders. Unfortunately, we lack infor­
mation on the exact reasons for these forfeits, but such a reduction is in line with previous 
findings on the positive relationship between match congestion and the incidence of injuries 
(Page et al., 2023). This could also be a result of strategic incentives inherent in the compe­
tition (Krumer et al., 2023). Future research is in place to investigate the possible strategic 
behaviour regarding forfeiting the matches.
