Abstract
Multistagestochasticprogramming(MSP)isaclassofmodelsforsequentialdecision-making
under uncertainty. MSP problems are known for their computational difﬁculties, and one
common approach to ﬁnd an approximate decision policy is to resort to the rolling-horizon
procedure (RHP). The RHP is a method that involves solving an MSP problem at each roll of
its procedure, which is referred to as a look-ahead model. The look-ahead model is typically
deﬁned with a smaller number of stages than the original MSP, making them computationally
less demanding to solve. However, the reduction in the number of stages may compromise
the quality of the resulting decision policy. This leads to an important question of how many
stages to use for each look-ahead model in the RHP. In this paper, we propose two alternative
approaches to address this question. First, we propose a surrogate model to the original MSP
as an inﬁnite-horizon MSP problem with a certain discount factor, from which we derive a
bound which can be used to prescribe an appropriate number of stages. Alternatively, we
propose a supervised learning approach that learns how to choose an appropriate number of
stages for each look-ahead model based on the state of the system in each roll of the RHP,
using data collected from ofﬂine experiments. Our numerical experiments demonstrate the
effectiveness of the proposed approaches on these problems.

1 Introduction
Multistage Stochastic Programming (MSP) is a class of mathematical programming models
for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. The goal of the decision-maker (DM) in
MSP problems is to construct an optimal policy for controlling the behavior of a probabilistic
system, sequentially over time, under some performance criterion. More precisely, given a
planning horizon of T ∈Z+ periods, in each period t ∈{1, 2, . . . , T }, the DM observes the
state of the system st and makes a decision xt according to an optimal policy.
To ﬁnd an optimal policy, one may apply the dynamic programming (DP) principle and
formulate the optimality conditions for such a policy using the Bellman equations (Bellman,
1957). This approach, however, could become impractical when the dimension of the state
variable st and/or the length of the planning horizon T is large. This is known as the curse
of dimensionality, as it renders an exponential growth in computational resources required
to solve the problem to optimality. One common way to address this challenge is to apply
a rolling-horizon procedure (RHP). The general idea of the RHP is that for each period
in the planning horizon t ∈{1, 2, . . . , T }, the DM observes the system state st, solves
a computationally less demanding problem Pt(st) to produce an immediate decision xt
of satisfactory quality for the current period t, rolls forward one period and repeats the
process starting from a new state st+1 (Powell, 2014; Sethi and Sorger, 1991). In this context,
each period is referred to as a roll, justifying the name rolling-horizon, as the horizon gets
“rolled over” in each period we repeat the process (Sethi and Sorger, 1991). In addition to its
popularity in solving large-scale dynamic optimization problems, the RHP is also extensively
studied in the control community, under the realm of model predictive control (Rawlings,
2000).
The problem Pt(st) is referred to as a look-ahead model and is typically constructed with
a certain approximation based on the system state st (Powell, 2014). There is a considerable
amount of literature in stochastic programming on how to construct the look-ahead model
Pt(st) at each roll t (Chand et al., 2002; Powell, 2014). One popular approach is to rely on
a deterministic approximation of future uncertain information. In Maggioni et al. (2014),
the authors use a deterministic approximation where all the uncertain future information is
replaced by a point estimator. The authors also provide bounds on the suboptimality of the
corresponding solution, and in Maggioni and Pﬂug (2016) the authors extend these bounds
to a stochastic approximation. A generalized framework to this deterministic approxima-
tion is the partial approximation considered in Pantuso and Boomsma (2020) where the
authors incorporate stochastic information up to a certain future period and replace uncertain
information beyond that period with a point estimator. Another common approach is stage
aggregation where multiple periods are aggregated into a single decision stage (Zou et al.,
2018).
Unlike these prior works, we do not consider aggregating any of the future exogenous
uncertain information or the stages. As we are concerned with the computational efﬁciency
of MSP problems with a long planning horizon, we focus on deﬁning the look-ahead model
Pt(st) by reducing the horizon from (t, T ) to (t, t + τ −1), where τ is an appropriately
chosen number. We refer to τ as the number of look-ahead stages (NLS).
The choice of the NLS is a delicate issue. On the one hand, the NLS needs to be small
enough to ensure the computational viability of the corresponding look-ahead model. On the
other hand, the NLS needs to be large enough to prevent the quality of the corresponding
decision from deteriorating signiﬁcantly. In fact, Bertazzi and Maggioni (2018) have shown
that the optimality gap associated with the rolling-horizon policy can be arbitrarily large

for a certain class of MSP problems in the worst case. The main goal of this paper is to
provide systematic approaches for choosing an appropriate NLS for MSP problems with a
long horizon T .
We study the selection of the NLS from two different perspectives that lead to different
selection strategies. First, we take the perspective of approximating the original ﬁnite-horizon
MSP problem, with potentially non-stationary data, by solving a discounted inﬁnite-horizon
MSP problem, with stationary data, as a way to obtain approximate decision policies. We
show that under certain assumptions, the optimality gap associated with the online RHP
policy corresponding to a static choice of NLS, compared to an optimal policy to the inﬁnite-
horizon MSP, can be upper bounded by a function of the NLS. This helps provide an explicit
guidance on how to make the static choice for the NLS according to the desired solution
quality. Alternatively, without resorting to such inﬁnite-horizon MSP approximation, we
propose a supervised learning approach that learns a dynamic selection strategy for the NLS
based on the state of the system in each roll of the RHP, using data collected from ofﬂine
experiments. This proposed approach does not rely on any assumption other than being able
to ﬁnd a feasible solution for the look-ahead model with various choices of the NLS. We
note that machine learning methods have been extensively applied to make the adaptive
choice on the length of the predictive horizon, an analogy of the NLS in the RHP for model
predictive control (Bøhn et al., 2021; Gardezi and Hasan, 2018; Krener, 2018). However, to
our knowledge, our paper is the ﬁrst that applies machine learning approaches for choosing
the NLS in the context of MSP.
Contributions. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
• We propose a new perspective on constructing the look-ahead model for the RHP in MSP.
In contrast to existing approaches that rely on deterministic or partial approximations of
future uncertain information (Maggioni et al., 2014; Maggioni and Pﬂug, 2016; Pantuso
and Boomsma, 2020) or on stage aggregation (Zou et al., 2018), we neither aggregate
stages nor replace future uncertainty with point estimators. Instead, we directly reduce
the planning horizon from (t, T ) to (t, t + τ −1) and focus on the selection of NLS τ as
the central design choice of the look-ahead model.
• We analyze the static selection of the NLS by approximating the original ﬁnite-horizon
MSP problem, with potentially non-stationary data, by a discounted inﬁnite-horizon MSP
problem with stationary data. Under certain assumptions, we derive an upper bound,
expressed as a function of the NLS, on the optimality gap of the online RHP policy
relative to an optimal policy of the inﬁnite-horizon MSP.
• We propose a supervised learning approach that selects the NLS dynamically, based
on the state of the system in each roll of the RHP, using data collected from ofﬂine
experiments. Whereas prior work in MSP typically ﬁxes the structure of the look-ahead
model a priori, our approach adapts the NLS to the evolving system state and does not
rely on any assumption other than the feasibility of the look-ahead model under various
choices of the NLS.
• We conduct computational experiments on the hydrothermal power operation planning
problem and the multistage newsvendor problem (see Appendix B). The numerical results
empirically conﬁrm the diminishing effect of distant stages on imminent decisions and
demonstrate the value of our proposed approach.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we describe the key components
of an MSP model, discuss some basic assumptions, and provide a detailed description of the
RHP. In Section 3, we provide the main theoretical result for the inﬁnite-horizon discounted
MSP model that guides on choosing a static NLS. In Section 4, we describe the proposed

3 Static selection of the NLS
In this section, we consider an RHP where a static selection of NLS is made in each roll.
We explicitly characterize an error bound for the suboptimality of such RHP online policy
for the special case of the stationary discounted inﬁnite-horizon MSP problems. This error
bound can be used for guiding the NLS selection in the RHP for solving these problems.

4 Dynamic selection of the NLS
In this section, we consider an RHP where at each period t = 1, 2, . . . , T , the DM solves
a look-ahead model Pt(st) where the NLS is dynamically selected based on the system
state st. We will propose a machine learning-based solution approach for constructing the
mapping τ(st). Although novel in MSP, to the best of our knowledge, such ideas have been
discussed in the MDP literature. For instance, Bès and Lasserre (1986) construct a dynamic
look-ahead policy for the inﬁnite-horizon discounted MDP as follows. First, the authors show
the existence of a ﬁnite sufﬁcient NLS ˇτ for any given system state. Second, they identify the
optimalﬁrst-stagedecision x ˇτ whichcorrespondstothis ˇτ.Finally,theyproposeanoptimality
criterion to this x ˇτ, and then use this result to provide a procedure for detecting the smallest
sufﬁcient NLS. The high-level idea of the procedure is to go through every possible system
state and increase the length of the NLS one stage at a time until ˇτ is identiﬁed. To do this, for
each step, the algorithm loops over every feasible action and solves a DP problem, where all
actions that do not satisfy the (predetermined) optimality criterion for x ˇτ are eliminated. This
process is repeated until the ﬁnite action space is reduced to a single element, in which case
the corresponding NLS is considered sufﬁcient. Similar procedures have also been discussed
in Hernández-Lerma and Lasserre (1988) for a more general class of MDPs.
Showing the existence of ˇτ and ﬁnding the corresponding x ˇτ are readily accessible for the
MDP setting because of the ﬁniteness assumptions on the state/action space. Extending the
same to the continuous state/action space of MSP might not be possible. Instead, we propose
a more practical approach, and we note that this proposed approach does not rely on any
assumptions other than the feasibility assumption (see Assumption 2).

5.1 An illustrating example: the hydrothermal power operation planning problem
The hydrothermal power generation system is one of the main energy sources in many
countries. For example, the Brazilian hydrothermal power system is a large scale network of
facilities that can be used to produce and distribute energy by circulating H2O ﬂuids (water).
The power plants in the Brazilian network can be categorized into two types:
1. A set of hydro plants H, which has no production cost, but for each hydro plant h ∈H
there is an upper limit ¯qh, which is the maximum allowed amount of turbined ﬂow that
can be used for power generation. These hydro plants can also be categorized further into
two types:
(a) Hydro plants with reservoirs HR, and for each h ∈HR there is an upper and lower
limit on the level of water allowed in the reservoirs, denoted by vh,t and ¯vh,t, respec-
tively.
(b) Hydro plants without reservoirs (run-of-river) HI , and thus no water storage is pos-
sible.
Moreover, for each hydro plant h, a unit of released water will generate rh units of power,
also known as efﬁciency rate. The set of immediate upper and lower stream plants for
hydro plant h in the network is given by U(h) and L(h), respectively.
2. A set of thermal plants F, where for each thermal plant f ∈F, the minimum and
maximum amount of power generation allowed is g f ,t and ¯g f ,t, respectively, ∀t =
1, . . . , T . Additionally, each plant f ∈F is associated with an operating cost of c f ,t for
each unit of thermal power generated. We denote the cost vector of generating thermal
power at time t by cg,t.
In the hydrothermal power operation planning (HPOP) problem, the goal of the DM is
to ﬁnd an operation strategy π, in order to meet production targets (demands) dt for each
decision stage t, ∀t = 1, . . . , T , while minimizing the overall production cost. To do this, at
each decision stage t and given the state of the system st (i.e., the water level at each hydro
plant h ∈HR), the DM has to decide on how much water to turbine by each hydro plant
h ∈H and how much thermal power to generate by each plant f ∈F. In the event where
the energy production level using hydro/thermal plants does not meet the demand, the DM
must pay a penalty of cp,t for each unit of unsatisﬁed demand. Such penalty can also be
thought of as a cost of buying energy from an external market, which typically has a higher
cost compared to the cost of generating energy using internal resources (thermal plants), i.e.,
cp,t > max f ∈F{c f ,t}.
The HPOP problem can be modeled as an MSP problem because of the uncertainty in the
problem data, such as the future water inﬂows (amount of rainfall) ˜bt, demand ˜dt, fuel costs
˜ct and equipment availability (Shapiro et al., 2011). For simplicity, in this paper we only
consider the situation where the only source of uncertainty is the future water inﬂow and all
other parameters are deterministic. To introduce a mathematical programming formulation
for the decision problem which the DM has to solve at every decision staget, for t = 1, . . . , T ,

5.3.3 The performance in comparison to the benchmark
From Tables 2 and 3, we can see that, with a sufﬁciently large discount factor, the inﬁnite-
horizon policy is able to reach the benchmark solution in the instances where |H| ∈{1, 3}

is able to reach the benchmark solution in all but two instances, and in the instances where
it did not reach the benchmark solution, the relative difference compared to the benchmark
solution is at most 2%. The column ¯τ in Table 1 shows the average NLS used in every roll. We
can see that ¯τ values are close to their static counterparts where the plateauing effect starts to
take place (see Subsection 5.3.2). Speciﬁcally, on average ¯τ ≈7.52 for the instances where
|H| ∈{1, 3} and ¯τ ≈21.34 for the instances where |H| = 6. This observation supports the
hypothesis that the NLS in which the plateauing effect starts to take place corresponds to the
NLS in which the corresponding immediate decisions xτ

choose the ˇτ(st) values that were used in training R(st) (see Algorithm 2) in the ﬁrst place.
5.3.4 Solution quality vs. computational time trade-off
From Figure 3, we can see that inﬁnite-horizon policies have the smallest run time compared
to the dynamic and static NLS selection strategies. Furthermore, we can see that the solution
time increases exponentially as the value of the τ and γ increases. However, the increase in
the solution time leads to a very limited (if any) reduction in the relative difference compared
to the benchmark solution. On the other hand, we can see that the dynamic NLS selection
strategy τ(st) is able to reach the same solution quality as the static selection strategy with
τ = 50 but with signiﬁcantly less computational time.

Figure 7 shows the trade-off between the solution quality and runtime. In this ﬁgure, points
farther to the right have smaller optimality gaps, while higher points correspond to larger
runtimes. Ideal points therefore lie in the bottom-right corner. The grid reports representative
static settings with τ ≈{1, 8, 29, 49} and representative inﬁnite-horizon settings with γ ∈
{0.05, 0.35, 0.65, 0.95}. The fastest points are the very myopic static policy with τ = 1 and
the smallest-γ inﬁnite-horizon policy with γ = 0.05. For example, the static policy with
τ ≈1 runs in about 3–4 seconds but has very large gaps in every panel, and the inﬁnite-
horizon policy with γ = 0.05 runs in about 4–6 seconds but also produces very large gaps.
Moving upward along the static family improves solution quality at a clear runtime cost. For
the inﬁnite-horizon family, however, the relationship is not monotone: performance improves
substantially from γ = 0.05 to intermediate values, but deteriorates again for γ = 0.95. We
will address this counterintuitive phenomenon later separately.

Overall, the ﬁgure suggests that the inﬁnite-horizon policy becomes much more competitive
only when the planning horizon is increased substantially, and that larger values of γ should
be used with caution because they quickly amplify the gap.
A plausible explanation for this behavior is that the policies with larger discount factors are
being evaluated in a setting that is still too short to reﬂect the objective they were designed for.
Although the policy is trained from an inﬁnite-horizon viewpoint, it is ultimately tested on a
ﬁnite horizon T . As γ increases, the policy places more weight on downstream consequences
and therefore relies more heavily on the long-run portion of the horizon. When γ is high,
evaluating the policy in a truncated horizon with T = 1000, or even at T = 5000, does not
reﬂect the inﬁnite horizon setting in which the policy was trained on. From this perspective,
the weaker performance of γ = 0.75, 0.85, and 0.95 at shorter horizons is less surprising:
these policies are optimized for a problem that is closer to T = ∞than to the ﬁnite-horizon
tests used to assess them.
This interpretation is consistent with the direction of change in the ﬁgure. As the evaluation
horizon grows from T = 1000 to T = 5000 and then to T = 10000, the larger-γ curves
improve substantially, especially at γ = 0.95. In that sense, the longer horizons provide a
progressively fairer test of policies that place greater emphasis on downstream consequences.
The evidence therefore suggests that the observed deterioration with increasing γ is not
simply a sign that large discount factors are intrinsically poor choices; rather, it reﬂects
a mismatch between an inﬁnite-horizon training objective and a ﬁnite-horizon evaluation
environment that becomes less severe as T increases.
