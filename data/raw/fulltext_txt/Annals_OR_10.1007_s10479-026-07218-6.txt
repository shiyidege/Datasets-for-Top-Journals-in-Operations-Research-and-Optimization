Abstract
Our model explores supplier selection within an inventory management framework, inte-
grating regional considerations and the impact of the made-in effect on demand. The ﬁrm
optimizes product pricing, geographic sourcing distribution, and batch sizes. Our analytical
results suggest that ﬁrms should expand local sourcing when product origin strongly inﬂu-
ences consumer value or when demand is relatively inelastic to price. Additionally, leveraging
smallerbatchproductioncanoptimizecostefﬁciencywhilemaintainingresponsiveness,mak-
ing local sourcing a strategic advantage in markets with uncertain demand or high service
levels. In the fashion industry, the made-in effect plays a crucial role in shaping sourcing
strategies, as a product’s country of origin signiﬁcantly impacts consumer perceptions, brand
reputation, and pricing dynamics. In line with observed sourcing strategies, we show that in
the luxury segment, known for its signiﬁcant pricing power and high service level, brands
beneﬁt from outsourcing manufacturing domestically. In contrast, economy brands, which
operateundertightermarginsandservehighlyprice-sensitivemarkets,shouldrelyonoffshore
sourcing, leading to larger safety stocks and reduced responsiveness to demand uncertainty.
Finally, a dual sourcing strategy—combining local and offshore suppliers—may be optimal,
allowing ﬁrms to balance cost efﬁciency with responsiveness. This approach is commonly
adopted by fast fashion companies to manage short product life cycles and volatile demand.

1 Introduction
The 1.5 trillion USD industry of fashion and garments is one of the most globalized industries
of all. The internationalization process has been facilitated by low entry barriers in terms of
knowledge, technology, and capital. Moreover, as a traditional industry, production knowl-
edge and skills are widely diffused and readily available across regions. Modern fashion has
a unique build-up and power structure accompanied by gatekeepers and fashion oligarchs
(Godart, 2014), which strongly inﬂuenced the geographical location, distribution, and divi-
sion of labor. Undoubtedly, the big four fashion capitals, London, Milan, New York, and Paris
are at the top of the hierarchy possessing many leading fashion brands (Dobos, 2022). These
inﬂuential capitals and brands built their reputations hand in hand based on their long-lasting
cultural backgrounds, image, strong buying power, and industrial network (Vecco, 2020). In
addition, fashion has its system of market position, quality, and quantity. The pinnacle of
fashion is set by haute couture brands, whose collections are closely watched and emulated
by the broader fashion industry. Over time, these designs are adapted and downgraded in both
value and price to make products accessible to different segments of society. The different
layers of fashion are summarized in Table 1.
The made-in effect describes the decision in which a consumer’s choice of a product,
service, brand is affected by an association of a place (of origin). This concept has been
researched widely in marketing since the original work of Schooler (1965) and became one
of the most frequently studied international aspects of consumer behavior (Magnusson et al.,
2011; Pharr, 2005; Tan & Farley, 1987). The made-in effect also plays a crucial role in the
fashion industry; studies have demonstrated that fashion products from developed countries
are favored over those from less developed regions, as they are perceived to represent status,
higher quality, and greater brand recognition (e.g., Jin et al., 2010). Many ﬁrms place strong
emphasis on their country-of-origin image. For example, Hermès highlighted its French
heritage and craftsmanship in the “The Value of Tradition” campaign, while Gucci seeking
to counteract a diluted brand image in the 2010s, launched initiatives such as the Gucci
Artisan Corners, which emphasized Italian leatherwork and handmade production. This
further underscores why many European apparel companies are increasingly reshoring and
developing local supply chains (e.g., Dachs et al., 2019), a shift also driven by the rising
importance of lead times over cost, which provides the essential ﬂexibility to keep pace with
rapidly evolving fashion trends (Backs et al., 2021). Meanwhile, new industry champions,
such as Shein, have emerged and are now dominating the lower market segments. These
companies excel at rapidly delivering a wide range of products while keeping costs low.
In this study, we model the sourcing decision of a ﬁrm within an Economic Order Quantity
(EOQ) model and explore the role of made-in effect in the optimal sourcing strategy. As
outsourcing has become the industry standard, the primary goal of the proposed model is to
determine the optimal sourcing location—whether to rely on local suppliers or to source from
offshore countries. Some numerical simulations are also presented to provide better insights
into model dynamics. We also compare the theoretical results with the sourcing strategies
adopted by leading brands across different fashion segments. Our research seeks to contribute
to the extensive literature on supplier selection (e.g., Jung, 2020), supply chain strategies (e.g.,
Mahdavi et al., 2023; Sawik, 2014), and the role of the made-in effect in reshoring (e.g., Di
Mauro et al., 2018; Longauer et al., 2023). By applying an EOQ model, we aim to capture how
the made-in effect inﬂuences strategic decisions, while also addressing the choice between
responsive and efﬁcient suppliers (Wu & Zhang, 2014) and demand uncertainty management

2 Sourcing decisions in the apparel industry
2.1 The relevance of the made-in effect
The made-in effect, i.e., the country of origin (COO)1 has been a widely researched topic
in marketing for a long time (Dichter, 1962; Hester & Yuen, 1987; Rashid & Barnes, 2017;
Thakor, 1996), and plays a crucial role in the fashion industry (Abalkhail, 2023; Godey
et al., 2012; Piron, 2000). The COO concept is based on the “Made in…” phrase displayed
on most of the products referring to the place of its manufacturing. Unlike other products,
fashion items—besides fabric details—usually hold only the brand name and the country
of origin, which is the only standard information that might be useful for comparisons and
purchase decisions. Research has proven that consumers associate certain values with given
countries (Aichner, 2014) and prefer one over another, and this inﬂuences their buying habits
and selection between products (Balabanis and Diamantopoulus, 2004). The consumers’
decision-making process in the light of the COO has been examined from various aspects.
Young age consumer groups prefer to buy foreign-made clothing over domestic (Chen, 2009),
while in other populations ethnocentrism can be identiﬁed as preferring home country prod-
ucts (Papadopoulos et al., 1990). It has also been shown that fashion products from developed
countries are preferred over those from less developed ones, as they represent status, higher
quality, and are better known by the community (Jin et al., 2010; O’Cass & Lim, 2002). This
assumption is also strengthened by the research of Riza Apil and Kaynak (2010), showing
that German and Italian fashion products are of higher quality compared to Chinese ones.
Aiello et al. (2009) found that the COO effect is rather determined by the brand, not by
the perception of the consumer, and it is noticeable only in the luxury segment. This is fur-
ther supported by the research of Lim and O’Cass (2001) and Mort and Duncan (2003),
who found that the brand origin is attached to the brand name, even if the product is not
designed, manufactured, or assembled in that country. Some research suggests, however, that
the made-in effect may be less pronounced because consumers may have limited knowledge
about a product’s actual country of origin (Liefeld, 2004), and in many cases, their presumed
information about the source of the product may be inaccurate (Samiee et al., 2005). Mag-
nusson et al. (2011) address this inconsistency by distinguishing between objective brand
origin knowledge and perceived brand origin associations, showing that the latter are more
inﬂuential in shaping consumer evaluations.
The “Made in” label is not compulsory to display. However, if a brand chooses to distribute
its products under this label, it must comply with legal regulations (e.g., in the UK with the
Sale of Goods Act), which states that the products must ﬁt the descriptions provided on them,
as it is punishable to apply any false information. In the trend of industry-wide outsourcing
and specialization, it has become challenging and obscure to determine and understand the
notion of country of origin. In practice, design takes place mostly in a (developed) country,
different parts and elements are produced in others, while the assembly of the ready-made
product happens again in another. Fashion brands are no exception, and it is common to have
products with hybrid or multiple origins (Rashid & Barnes, 2017), such as a coat, whose wool
was produced in Australia, the yarn was woven in India and was dyed in Italy, the zipper was
manufactured in Vietnam, the ﬁber was sourced in China, and was ﬁnally sewn together in
France. Consequently, despite COO is an important aspect of differentiation for customers
as they associate the product with the attributes of the producing country, it makes it difﬁcult

2.2 Industry overview
Fashion brands and companies do business globally, meaning that they do not just sell all over
the globe, but they source their materials and products from a global supply chain. The tradi-
tional product development in the apparel industry lasts very long, with ﬁxed calendar events
and a rigid rhythm over the year dictated by trade fairs, fashion shows, fabric events, and
collection releases in a two-season approach of Spring–Summer and Fall-Winter. Tradition-
ally, demand forecasts are usually based on the previous year’s sales data and the forecasting
starts a year before product release (Birtwistle et al., 2003). The product development is usu-
ally performed parallel with procurement and design after which the manufacturing operates
with long lead times and low (volume) ﬂexibility. This is followed by distribution and retail-
ing organized by their own (e.g., H&M, Inditex) or by licensed partners (e.g., Benetton) in
brick-and-mortar stores. Currently, the trend is shifting, with fashion houses favoring smaller,
more frequent collections. The Inditex brand Zara started to break the year to twenty releases,
which means a signiﬁcant change in the product life cycle, batch sizes, and the accuracy of
demand forecast (Christopher et al., 2004). Recently, the China-based brand Shein—dis-
tributing only through online channels—faded the concept of collections and redeﬁned fast
fashion to ultra-fast fashion by applying a prototyping approach. It issues 2000 new products
a day, ordered from its contractors in very small batches with very short lead times. If a
product turns out to be successful, they scale up production rapidly.
2.2.1 Design
The supply chain is motivated primarily by the design department, which is usually located in
the company’s home country. Some of the brands create their own collections, in the case of
Inditex, for example, the design is inspired by top-level fashion brand collections, based on
catwalk inspections at fashion shows. Dedicated design, sales, procurement, and production
planning staff are assigned to each product line. In order to gain competitive advantage by
reducing the usual design-to-retail cycle from 5–6 months to 2–3 weeks, there is a need for
swift, direct communication and accuracy throughout the supply chain. If this is coordinated
well and even if geographic conditions strengthen the system, this can be reduced to 3 days
as it happens at Shein. To achieve such a reduction in time, skilled workers are required
to reduce prototype creation from 1–2 weeks to a few hours (Ferdows et al., 2004). Final
pricing decisions are also made at this stage. Most of the brands price their products based
on competitors and on research about how much customers are willing to pay for them. For
example, the pricing of Zara products is based on a cost-plus margin strategy that is estimated
during the design phase and affects whether a given design can go into production, resulting
in having 12,000 ﬁnal product offers out of 40,000 design concepts annually (Mazaira et al.,
2003).

2.2.2 Procurement
Fabric production is a special part of the fashion supply chain since almost none of the
fashion houses take the responsibility to engage in this business, regardless of their size and
position. The management of procurement must be handled very carefully, as lead times are
much longer, and thus accurate quantity estimates in advance are inevitable (MacCarthy &
Jayarathne, 2010). To adjust the long fabric lead times to the short garment production cycles,
creating inventory at this step is a must.
2.2.3 Manufacturing
Manufacturing is a critical factor for achieving business success in the industry. In Europe,
traditional fashion brands emerged with in-house manufacturing. As their demand grew, they
had to manage capacity and build a network of subcontractors, who were usually at a very
close distance from the headquarters (Aftab et al., 2018). Such type of a local sourcing enables
rapid communication, short transportation times and low transportation costs, high ﬂexibility,
clear understanding, and the avoidance of cultural barriers. As competition increased, in
line with the global trends, outsourcing became the industry standard, and the reliance on
offshore suppliers offering capacities at lower costs grew rapidly. Today, the fashion industry
is one of the most globalized and labor-intensive sectors, heavily dependent on manufacturing
powerhouses such as China, Bangladesh, Vietnam, and India. However, an increasing number
of companies are reevaluating their supply chains, in which the positive impacts of the made-
in effect are playing a key role. Bringing manufacturing back to domestic locations can
enhance brand perception and translate into stronger sales performance. Several European
fashion ﬁrms have already adopted active reshoring strategies, driven primarily by the higher
quality and brand value associated with Western production (e.g., Baraldi et al., 2018; Dachs
et al., 2019; Di Mauro et al., 2018; Fratocchi et al., 2015). A prominent example is Adidas’s
Speedfactory initiative, in which the company established shoe manufacturing facilities in
Germany and the United States, employing advanced technologies such as 3D printing and
robotized production. This approach was designed to capitalize on country-of-origin effects
while enabling greater customization, smaller batch sizes, and signiﬁcantly shorter lead times
(Longauer et al., 2023).
2.2.4 Logistics and distribution
Managing the handling and shipping of products is an essential part of success in the industry.
The traditional industry standard is to ship products manufactured at different locations to
regionaldistributioncenters(DCs).Thesewarehousestypicallyservemultiplecountries,with
delivery times from the distribution center to the local market ranging from 2 to 4 weeks.
The practice can be explained by cost savings due to aggregated shipments and slow modes
of transport (Aftab et al., 2018). These strategies are often linked with ﬂexible supply chain
networks and to the Quick Response (QR) concept, with the goal to reduce inventory risk
with higher responsiveness to demand uncertainty (e.g., Shen et al., 2016). Others apply
highly automated high-speed central DCs and fast modes of transport to reduce delivery
time and cost in inventory. This approach necessitates frequent shipments, often resulting in
higher transportation expenses and an increased cost per order, which heavily depends on
the distance between the manufacturing site and the destination. In response, leading fashion
brands have signiﬁcantly shortened lead times to minimize inventory levels. Recently, ultra-
fast fashion brands have adopted supply chains driven primarily by online sales. Their model

2.2.5 Retail
Traditionally, fashion brands have relied on brick-and-mortar stores, as customers enjoy
browsing these spaces, taking time to discover the right apparel, and trying on items before
making a purchase. Retail is the main source of customer information and has serious effects
on the whole supply chain. Leading companies digitalized early to be able to generate and
share point of sales (POS) information and track demand and inventory levels to maintain
their service level. Demand planning and management are increasingly challenging in luxury
fashion, due to low stock turnover, and highly unpredictable demand leading to high service-
level requirements to always have product lines on the shelves (Lucci et al., 2016). The
information is shared throughout the supply chain and used to determine the lot sizes of orders
and manufacturing batches. Moreover, COVID-19 accelerated the rise of online shopping,
signiﬁcantlytransformingpurchasinghabitsinthefashionindustry.Additionally,itfacilitated
real-time customer data collection, enabling more data-driven decision-making. Nowadays,
each design can be easily withdrawn from production or pivoted by multiple outsourced
manufacturing suppliers if required, which can reduce the risk of major product launch
mistakes and unsold inventory but requires very close collaboration with suppliers (Fong
et al., 2022).
3 Theoretical background and problem description
3.1 Modeling background
There is an extensive body of literature on supplier selection in supply chain design. Supplier
choice is a strategic decision that plays a central role in shaping the structure and perfor-
mance of a ﬁrm’s supply chain. Recent research has increasingly addressed the complexity
of modern supply networks by examining multi-sourcing strategies. Studies by Sawik (2014)
and Mahdavi et al. (2023), for example, consider multiple suppliers with heterogeneous pric-
ing, capacity, and disruption characteristics. This stream of literature highlights the role of
multi-sourcing in mitigating risks arising from demand uncertainty and supply disruptions,
thereby enhancing supply chain resilience (Jadidi et al., 2024; Lee & Moon, 2024).
Multi-sourcing strategies also help ﬁrms overcome the inherent trade-offs of single-
supplier conﬁgurations. Prior studies emphasize trade-offs between efﬁciency and respon-
siveness (Mahdavi et al., 2023), exposure to disruptions and quality risks (Saputro et al.,
2021), and cost versus reliability (Sawik, 2014). Several contributions explicitly analyze
these trade-offs in the context of local versus offshore sourcing decisions (Jung, 2020; Sawik,
2014). In this setting, responsive sourcing typically relies on local suppliers that offer shorter
lead times, higher reliability, and superior quality at higher cost. in contrast, efﬁcient offshore
sourcing provides cost advantages at the expense of longer lead times and greater disruption
risk (Choi & Cai, 2020; Mahdavi et al., 2023; Wu & Zhang, 2014). Consequently, these
models often conclude that dual sourcing can be particularly effective, allowing ﬁrms to
combine cost efﬁciency with operational responsiveness. Relatedly, Chan et al. (2017) link
supply chain agility and ﬂexibility to ﬁrm performance, further underscoring the strategic
importance of sourcing decisions.

Although the economic order quantity (EOQ) model is not among the most dynamic
inventory management techniques, it remains widely used in retail due to its robustness and
analytical tractability. Its simplicity allows for straightforward adaptation to a wide range of
supply chain settings. Since its original formulation by Harris (1913) and later formaliza-
tion by Erlenkotter (1990), the EOQ framework has generated a large and inﬂuential body
of research. Early extensions include Hall’s (1988) multi-item EOQ model and Wortham
and Mayyasi’s (1972) analysis of learning effects within a simple EOQ setting. More recent
contributions have incorporated greater structural complexity. Roy et al. (2016) developed a
two-echelon supply chain model with dual sales channels to determine optimal order quan-
tities and pricing strategies jointly. Operational characteristics common in retail, such as
backorders and defective items, have also been extensively examined in EOQ-based models
(Chung, 2013; Huang & Wu, 2016; Mahata, 2015). Recent studies further extend the EOQ
framework to capture complex supply chain conﬁgurations. Khakbaz et al. (2024) analyze
a multi-item, multi-supplier, and multi-retailer EOQ model with cross-docking logistics,
reﬂecting systems used by large retailers such as Walmart. This framework is extended by
Sebatjane et al. (2025), who incorporate imperfect quality and stockouts under a carbon
cap-and-trade regime. Relatedly, Hauck et al. (2021) and Öztürk and Konstantaras (2025)
explicitly integrate quality inspection into EOQ-based inventory decision-making. Several
studies also link supplier selection directly to inventory replenishment decisions by embed-
ding supplier-speciﬁc constraints, such as ﬁxed production rates, into EOQ models (Mendoza
& Ventura, 2012; Ventura et al., 2021, 2022).
Uncertainty in demand and lead times has long been a central theme in the supply chain
and inventory management literature (e.g., Eppen & Martin, 1988; Ruiz-Torres & Mahmoodi,
2009). In the fashion industry context, Shen et al. (2016) provide a detailed analysis of these
uncertainties, while the trade-off between production costs and lead times is examined by
Donohue (2000) and Yang et al. (2011).
Another closely related stream of research integrates pricing decisions into supplier selec-
tion and inventory lot-sizing models. By jointly modeling supplier choice, order quantity
allocation, and product pricing, these studies reﬂect the growing complexity of contempo-
rary supply chain decision-making (e.g., Jung, 2020). In such models, demand is typically
price-sensitive rather than constant, with the demand rate depending on the ﬁrm’s pricing
decisions. This extension is particularly relevant for ﬁrms operating in competitive markets,
where optimal sourcing and inventory strategies are highly contingent on market conditions
(Duan & Ventura, 2020).
3.2 Model development and contribution
Our model presents an integrated framework for supplier selection, inventory management,
and pricing decisions within the fashion industry, emphasizing the cost-responsiveness trade-
off inherent in sourcing decisions while allowing for a dual sourcing strategy. In contrast to
previous models, we introduce demand sensitivity through the made-in effect, linking demand
to the sourcing location, i.e., the country of origin, which is particularly signiﬁcant in the
fashion industry. This connection enables us to align the sourcing strategy not only with
cost considerations but also with customer preferences, adding a demand-side perspective
that has not been fully explored in prior research. Our research also highlights how supplier
choice can vary depending on the price sensitivity of demand, reﬂecting the ﬁrm’s pricing
power. By simultaneously optimizing pricing, sourcing, and inventory decisions, our model
enhances the state-of-the-art in demand-sensitive inventory and supply chain management.

Our main contribution lies in the development and analysis of a comprehensive inventory
management model designed for supplier selection of a typical fashion industry ﬁrm that
(1) accounts for the impact of the made-in effect on demand, reﬂecting consumer prefer-
ences associated with product origin; (2) integrates the crucial trade-off between suppliers’
cost and responsiveness (i.e., lead times), recognizing the importance of demand uncertainty
and desired service level in supplier selection decisions; (3) relies on general assumptions
and functions making it adaptable across different industries and markets, and allows us
to provide strategic insights for a range of fashion ﬁrms; and (4) offers novel insights into
determining the optimal sourcing strategy, which may involve either single or dual sourcing
approaches, depending on the market conditions. Although our motivation comes from the
fashion industry, it may be applicable in other businesses, like cosmetics, footwear, or furni-
ture, where (1) country of origin is crucial in consumer decisions, (2) local procurement is
primarily associated with the luxury segment, and (3) mass market ﬁrms tend to show higher
offshore activity and apply dual sourcing.
3.3 Model formulation
We introduce an EOQ model that incorporates the decision on the sourcing location. We
suppose that the ﬁrm operates in a developed country, and it outsources the production of its
fashion products. It is possible to purchase the products from an offshore country or from
local (either domestic or regional) suppliers. Furthermore it is also possible to place orders
alternately in both regions. The ﬁrm’s objective is to decide on the order quantities used in
the two regions, the selling price of the product, and the sourcing location.2

difference between the two regions is constant and is denoted by cx. Then the average unit
sourcing cost can be expressed by a linear function c(x)  co +cxx, where co, cx > 0. Based
on this function, the average sourcing cost increases linearly in the local sourcing ratio.3
We assume that additional inventory management-related costs arise. At the beginning
of each cycle, the company orders a ﬁxed quantity from either the offshore region (Qo)
or the local region (Qn) to meet the expected demand during that period.4 The cost per
order locally is denoted by s and is independent of the ordered quantity. We also assume
that ordering from an offshore area entails an additional cost per order, and we indicate this
percentage difference by the τ parameter, so the cost per order from the offshore region is
expressed by (1 + τ)s. The reason for the higher cost of ordering from the offshore region
can be, for example, more expensive transport due to longer routes or lead times, or this
additional cost may also reﬂect the supply risks associated with the lengthening of the supply
chain (e.g., Ellram et al., 2013). We also incorporate order lead times (Lo, Ln), assuming
that shipping from the offshore region takes longer time (Lo > Ln). This, combined with
demand uncertainty, means that the ﬁrm must hold safety stock to cover demand during the
lead time; thus, the size of the safety stock depends on the sourcing location (e.g., Donohue,
2000, Yang et al., 2011):

Finally, we assume that both regions have sufﬁcient capacity and resources to supply the
ﬁrm, with no quality differences between the products sourced from the local region and the
offshore region (e.g., Mahdavi et al., 2023). The key assumptions are as follows:
1) The ﬁrm decides on the local sourcing ratio (x), selling price (p), and order quantities
(Qn, Qo).
2) The average demand rate is a negative function of the price of the product and is positively
and linearly dependent on the local sourcing ratio. Demand follows a normal distribution
with a constant coefﬁcient of variation.
3) The unit sourcing cost is a linear function of the local sourcing ratio.
4) Fixed ordering and holding costs arise, but ordering from the offshore area is more
expensive and has a longer lead time.
5) There is no quality difference between the output of the two regions, and shortages are
not allowed.

Figure 3 illustrates the effect of the main model parameters on the optimal sourcing strat-
egy, i.e., local sourcing only, dual sourcing, or offshoring only. It is clear that—in accordance
with the analytical results of the previous section—an internal solution, in which the ﬁrm
orders from both regions, can indeed occur, so that a dual sourcing strategy may turn out to
be optimal (see the gray blurred area in the ﬁgures).
Figure 3a shows that the extent of local sourcing intensiﬁes as the ordering cost (s) and/or
the holding cost (h) increase, i.e., both inventory management cost types have a positive effect
on the local sourcing ratio. Reasons include that the increase in the ordering cost strengthens
the ordering cost advantage of local sourcing (as τ is constant, the difference between the
ordering costs increases with a growing s). As the holding cost increases, local sourcing
becomes more advantageous due to the smaller economic lot sizes. Since a higher holding
cost means a lower economic order quantity, the number of orders generally increases, which
makes ordering from the offshore region more expensive due to the extra cost of orders.
Figure 3b illustrates how the percentage difference in the ordering costs (τ) and the absolute
difference in the sourcing costs (cx) affect the level of local sourcing. An increase in τ and/or
a decrease in cx makes local sourcing more attractive, which is a trivial consequence of the
increasing cost advantage of local sourcing over offshore sourcing. Figure 3c demonstrates
the impact of lead time ratios on the optimal sourcing strategy. Clearly, an increase in the
offshore lead time relative to the length of the demand period (lo) pushes the ﬁrm towards
local sourcing, while an increase in the local lead time (ln) strengthens the offshore sourcing
strategy. From our prior assumption regarding lead times (i.e. ln < lo) it follows that only
strategies under the positive diagonal are available. Figure 3d introduces the effect of demand
uncertainty (δd) and required service level (α). Both parameters improve the attractiveness
of local sourcing, as it becomes increasingly important to maintain costly safety stocks, the

demand – typically associated with higher-income consumer segments and luxury product

production, thereby increasing the attractiveness of domestic sourcing.
Supplementing the previous analysis, Fig. 4 shows the effect of the model parameters
on the selling price of the product and the applied order quantity (Qn or Qo, depending on
the location of sourcing, or in case of a dual sourcing strategy, the average order quantity is
considered, i.e., Q  x Qn + (1 −x)Qo). Overall, the relationship between the local sourcing
ratio and the order quantity appears to be inverse: increased reliance on local suppliers is
typically associated with lower order quantities but higher selling prices. Dual sourcing
plays a crucial role in transitioning between strategies leading to sudden shifts in both order
quantities and prices.
The effects of the ordering cost (s) and the holding cost (h) correspond to the well-
known mechanisms; the former increases, the latter decreases the average order quantity,
while both slightly increase the price (Figs. 4a, b). Higher s means higher ordering cost per
unit of demand, while a higher h increases the price level because of the increased costs of
inventorying. In the middle range—where the optimal sourcing strategy is dual sourcing—a
signiﬁcant change in the growth rate of the price level can be observed, which is primarily due
to the higher average sourcing cost as a result of the adaptation of local sourcing. Interestingly,
Figs. 4a, b also demonstrate that when the ﬁrm switches from a full offshoring strategy to dual
sourcing, the average order quantity jumps to a signiﬁcantly lower level, which is because
local sourcing enables the use of smaller lot sizes.
An increase in the percentage cost difference between the local and offshore orders (τ)
positively inﬂuences the order quantity as long as the ﬁrm sources exclusively from the
offshore region. However, when the ﬁrm relies solely on local sourcing, this cost difference
has no impact on the order quantity (see Fig. 4c). Again, a signiﬁcant drop in the size of the
average order quantity can be observed when the ﬁrm switches to local sourcing. An increase
in τ has a slightly positive effect on the price level as long as the ﬁrm sources only from the
offshore region, and the price level increases signiﬁcantly when the ﬁrm starts local sourcing,
which is a result of the increased sourcing costs. Further increasing the τ parameter does not
affect the price level if the company orders only locally. The sourcing cost difference (cx)
negatively affects the order quantity in case the ﬁrm applies local sourcing, which is due to
the decreasing demand as a result of higher prices (Fig. 4d). A higher sourcing cost difference
increases the price level as long as the ﬁrm sources only locally, while it drops signiﬁcantly
as soon as it adapts to offshore sourcing.

6 Concluding remarks
Many fashion brands, ranging from luxury labels to mass-market players, may strive to build
their identity around their country of origin, signiﬁcantly enhancing the perceived quality
and value of the brand. Nevertheless, mounting competition and slim proﬁt margins compel
companies to resort to outsourcing their production, thereby confronting the delicate balance
between cost, quality, and brand image.

Our model incorporates the geographical location of clothing suppliers into the decision-
making process, providing a comprehensive assessment of how manufacturing relocation
inﬂuences brand perception and consumer behavior. The analytical results highlight three
key insights. First, local sourcing becomes more prevalent when the made-in effect is strong
or when demand is less price-sensitive, as consumers are willing to pay a premium for locally
produced goods. Second, local sourcing is typically conducted in smaller batches, helping
ﬁrms reduce inventory costs and improve operational ﬂexibility. Third, both greater demand
uncertainty and a higher desired service level increase the likelihood of local sourcing, as
it enables ﬁrms to respond more swiftly to market ﬂuctuations and customer expectations.
Accordingly, ﬁrms should consider expanding local sourcing when product origin enhances
consumer value or when demand is less price sensitive. Additionally, leveraging smaller
batch production can optimize cost efﬁciency while maintaining responsiveness, making
local sourcing a strategic advantage in markets with uncertain demand or high service levels.
Our ﬁndings contribute to the modeling literature by demonstrating that, in the case of
inelastic demand, there is no viable intermediate solution (Mahdavi et al., 2023). In contrast,
in a price-sensitive environment, dual sourcing may be the optimal strategy (Jung, 2020).
While our results on the role of country of origin in the fashion industry align with established
literature,theproposedmodeloffersanovel,integrativeframeworkbysimultaneouslylinking
supplier geography, batch size, and pricing decisions. Consequently, the framework provides
theoretical and practical value, equipping decision-makers with tools to analyze and optimize
supply chain strategies in a rapidly evolving industrial environment.
Importantly, these analytical insights closely align with observed sourcing and inventory
practices in the fashion industry. Figure 5 and Table 3 in the Appendix present recent pro-
curement data for several major brands covering the period 2023–2024. The data indicate

power and high service level requirements—domestic manufacturing constitutes a rational
outsourcing strategy. This result is consistent with empirical evidence documenting a positive
relationship between country of origin and consumer purchase decisions in luxury fashion
(Abalkhail, 2023; Godey et al., 2012; Piron, 2000). In contrast, the model demonstrates that
for economy brands facing high price sensitivity and a weak made-in effect, offshore sourc-

(Fig. 5). The model further suggests that a dual sourcing strategy—combining local and
offshore suppliers—may also be optimal in certain contexts. As also shown in Fig. 5, fast
fashion companies frequently implement such dual sourcing approaches, achieving cost efﬁ-
ciency through offshore production while leveraging responsive local manufacturing in small
batches to manage demand uncertainty and short product life cycles. Well-documented cases,
including Zara’s rapid-response fulﬁllment system and the Adidas’s Speedfactory initiative
illustrate these mechanisms in practice (Ferdows et al., 2004; Longauer et al., 2023).
Our results also have important implications for industrial policy and the organization of
production in the clothing sector. The dominance of sub-luxury segments, which account
for approximately 95% of the clothing industry by volume, reinforces the decline of mass-
market clothing production capacity in developed economies. Inevitably, this trend entails
the erosion of industrial commons (e.g., Pisano & Shih, 2009), encompassing essential
know-how and skilled labor, alongside the dissolution of production networks, including
educational programs, training initiatives, and synergistic company collaborations. Conse-
quently, the branding potential associated with the "Made in" label is predominantly viable
only in scenarios involving low-volume manufacturing, speciﬁcally within the premium or
luxury segments. This is a signiﬁcant contribution to the reshoring literature, as the declining
clothing industries in developed regions hinder ﬁrms from effectively shortening their sup-
ply chains or fully reshoring production, despite potential advantages like the made-in effect,
improved inventory management, reduced obsolete stock, and a more sustainable operational
footprint. While labor-intensive processes continue to persist, low-cost offshore production
remains the dominant approach for mass-market apparel.6 Introducing new technologies and
automated manufacturing, however, offers a promising avenue to address these challenges.

– can be automated. However, manual labor, particularly in sewing, remains indispensable,
constraining synergies between production stages and limiting overall factory output (Lon-
gauer et al., 2023). Industry reports suggest that in the coming years, advances in automation
could make nearshoring and reshoring strategies economically more viable (Andersson et al.,

6.1 Limitations and suggestions for future research
As with any analytical modeling approach, this study relies on a set of simplifying assump-
tions. First, unit sourcing costs are assumed to be constant, which is well suited to capturing
seasonal planning decisions commonly observed in the fashion industry, but abstracts from
potentially time-varying or nonlinear cost dynamics that may arise in more volatile market
environments. Future work could relax this assumption by allowing for endogenous cost
changes driven by congestion, learning, scale economies, or capacity constraints.
Second, the model assumes no quality differences between products sourced from local
andoffshoreregions.Whilethisassumptionallowstheanalysistofocusoncost,leadtime,and
demand responsiveness, future research could relax it by incorporating quality heterogeneity
across sourcing locations and examining how quality interacts with pricing power, the made-
in effect, and optimal sourcing strategies.
Third, the model adopts an EOQ-based inventory framework in order to isolate the core
trade-offs between sourcing location, pricing, and inventory decisions. While EOQ-type
models remain widely studied in the retail literature and provide valuable structural insights,
extending the framework to incorporate dynamic or stochastic inventory models, data-driven
demand forecasting, or adaptive replenishment policies would allow future research to better
capture operational realities, particularly in highly volatile demand environments.
Finally, the rise of ultra-fast fashion offers promising avenues for extension. Firms such
as Shein do not rely on the made-in effect in Western markets but instead leverage proximity
to major manufacturing hubs to combine rapid responsiveness with low production costs.
This enables sourcing in small, ﬂexible batches, rapid adjustment to real-time demand, and
substantial reductions in inventory holding costs. Moreover, ultra-fast fashion ﬁrms mitigate
long delivery times through online-exclusive, direct-to-consumer models that bypass tradi-
tional distribution and retail networks, altering inventory risk and market mediation costs
in ways not captured by the current framework. Future research could explicitly model the
interaction between sourcing strategies, digital sales channels, and inventory placement, as
well as the implications of platform-based retailing for lead times, demand uncertainty, and
cost structures.
