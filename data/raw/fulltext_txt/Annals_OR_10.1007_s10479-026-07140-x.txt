Abstract
In light of the recent policy developments, such as the 2015 Paris Agreements, which
steers ﬁnancial markets towards more sustainable investments, understanding the risk-return
dynamics of environmentally focused portfolios becomes crucial. This research proposes a
new methodological approach to quantify the trade-off between sustainability and ﬁnancial
performance, the Green Mean-to-CVaR frontier. Measuring the environmental impact using
well established environmental metrics, such as the E (Environmental) score and the Green-
house gas (GHG) emissions, we explore this topic in the mean-Conditional Value at Risk
(CVaR) space where we derive the green Mean-to-CVaR (MtC) efﬁcient frontier. We focus
our analysis on the European stock market, using data from the STOXX Europe 600 for the
period 03/01/2007 30/12/2019. We ﬁnd that portfolios with higher E score (lower GHG)
have lower CVaR and lower expected return. Above a certain threshold of the green metric
only inefﬁcient portfolios are obtained. The sectoral analysis conﬁrms the ﬁndings across all
sectors except Energy, where higher E scores (lower GHG) correspond to increased risk.
Keywords Climate change · Sustainable ﬁnance · Sustainable development · Conditional
Value at Risk · Portfolio optimization
1 Introduction
Global warming is one of the most compelling issues of our times: this has been addressed
by more stringent international regulation, such as the 2015 Paris Agreements whose main
goal is to keep the global average temperature increase below 2◦C or The European Green
Deal launched in 2019 aiming at climate neutrality by 2050. Such new regulations will
reverberate to the ﬁnancial markets as well, because of their capacity to fund new investments
to strengthen our economies against the adverse effects of climate change and for the necessity
to shift capital ﬂows toward more responsible investment solutions. At the start of 2020,
sustainable investments reached 35.3 USD trillion in the markets of USA, Europe, Canada,
Japan, and Australasia combined, with an increase of 55% over the period 2016-2020.

One way to invest responsibly is ESG funds, which are funds that incorporate environ-
mental, social, and governance criteria into the investment decision process. To do so, stocks
and ﬁnancial products in general, are evaluated based on their ESG (Environmental, Social,
and Governance) score. Such scores are synthetic assessments provided by private agencies
on the company’s efforts regarding the environmental, social, and governance aspects. The
shift of investors’ preferences towards pro-environmental investments in particular can be
observed also in numbers: in 2020 13% of the new ESG funds in the European Market were
entirely environmentally focused (Global Sustainable Investment Alliance, 2020). In this
work we will rely as well on ESG scores, using the E pillar as a proxy of the environmental
impact of a company.
How does the environmental performance of an investment strategy inﬂuence its risk-
reward proﬁle? The aim of this work is to empirically study the relationship between the
environmental impact and the risk-return proﬁle of a portfolio of stocks. We use a risk-
management approach that exploits the Conditional Value–at Risk (CVaR), thanks to its
capability to better take into account the non-normal nature of the returns. Our analysis is
applied to a portfolio consisting of European stocks.
To investigate this issue, we elaborate and test three hypotheses. The ﬁrst hypothesis is
that investors need to trade off part of the portfolio performance to have their environmental
preferences met: the goal of reaching a certain required E score (or GHG) might push the
portfolio away from the tangency one, compromising the economic performance. Second, we
examine the relationship between the environmental effort of a portfolio and its risk-reward
proﬁle. Bolton and Kacperczyk (2021) ﬁnd that more sustainable stocks have lower returns
than their brown counterparts, Zerbib (2022), Pástor et al. (2021), Pedersen et al. (2021)
allow for the same results under certain model speciﬁcations. Granting lower returns, we
expect them to have also lower risk as in Ceccarelli et al. (2024), so we test the hypothesis
that environmentally responsible portfolios are less risky -and less proﬁtable- than their
brown counterparts: given the heightened attention toward sustainable investments both from
institutional investors and retailers, brown assets might actually be disregarded from investors
and be therefore treated like sin stocks, either because perceived as riskier or simply for
ethical reasons. The third hypothesis tested is that the relationship between environmental
performance and risk-reward proﬁle might be sector-speciﬁc.
We chose a portfolio selection model built in the Mean-to-CVaR (MtC) framework as
proposed by Lotﬁet al. (2025); we measure the environmental performance using the overall
E score of the portfolio, obtained by multiplying the weight of each asset and its E score. The
choice of such risk measure is motivated by the nature of the returns used for this analysis,
showing signiﬁcant skewness and exhibiting leptokurtic behaviour, which the widely used
mean-variance framework is inadequate to capture. In particular, the estimation of returns
assumingaGaussiandistributionmightunderestimatetheprobabilityofmoreextremeevents,
pushing therefore investors in inadvertently taking riskier positions. The CVaR, deﬁned as
the expected loss given that it will be greater than the Value-at-Risk (VaR), is instead a
better-suited risk measure since it is coherent1 and it is able to capture tail events.
We build a green MtC efﬁcient frontier, obtained by computing the highest achievable
MtC ratio for each level of E score required, the E score MtC efﬁcient frontier. We derive the
frontier for the entire dataset and for the dataset obtained after implementing an exclusionary
screening strategy, for no short sale and for covered short positions. The ﬁrst hypothesis is
conﬁrmed: the analysis reveals a trade-off between environmental and portfolio performance
which is non-linear with respect to the E score. Examining the expected return and CVaR

To further corroborate our results, we repeat the analysis using a different proxy for
the environmental performance, total GHG emissions. The motivation behind this choice is
twofold: ﬁrst, there is little agreement among the ESG ratings issued by different agencies
for the same company (Berg et al., 2022; Gibson Brandon et al., 2021), largely stemming
from issues in the disclosure process - such as opaque reporting practices, reliance on self-
declared information, and inconsistencies across national regulatory frameworks - leaving
environmentally motivated investors in a situation of uncertainty (Angelakoglou and Gaidajis,
2015; Chatterji et al., 2016; Morioka and Monteiro de Carvalho 2016); second, climate
action commitments are usually tied to the reduction of Greenhouse gas emissions, leading
to a potential interest from green investors in the decarbonization of their portfolios (Bolton
and Kacperczyk 2023). Overall, the results based on GHG emissions closely mirror those
obtained with E scores, thereby reinforcing the robustness of our main conclusions.
From the results obtained, it is possible to derive some conclusions for responsible
investors. First, investors must trade off part of the portfolio performance to have their green
requirements met. The strategy that allows for a more favorable trade-off is the MtC opti-
mization with covered short positions without implementing any exclusionary screening.
This is due to the release of the constraint on short sales and the widest pool of assets that
promote diversiﬁcation. Nevertheless, this may come at the cost of an environmental impact
more difﬁcult to assess: when allowing for short sales, some brown stocks are still bought
and some greener ones are sold, even in the portfolios with very high E score. In addition,
investors should be careful when choosing the benchmark E score of their portfolio, since
after a certain value only inefﬁcient allocations are found.
This work is placed in the literature investigating the relationship between responsible
investing and ﬁnancial performance. Our main contribution is the examination of this problem
using a risk-management approach, characterized by the choice of a risk measure better suited
for the characteristics of ﬁnancial data.
A ﬁrst strand of research focuses on the relationship between environmental performance
and stock returns. Event studies such as Flammer (2013), Krüger (2015), Wang et al. (2019),
Oberndorfer (2013) analyze the impact of environmentally responsible initiatives on cumu-
lative abnormal returns; Bolton and Kacperczyk (2021), Bolton and Kacperczyk (2023),
Aswani et al. (2024) focus on the link between stock returns and company emissions. In all
these papers, the emphasis is on the return, while our approach shifts the focus on the risk,
explicitly modeling the sustainability–performance trade-off in the mean–CVaR space.
The literature has investigated the characteristics of green portfolios using a variety of
tools. A strand of literature employs models that build on the CAPM model or on the assump-
tions that returns follow a Gaussian distribution, as in Pedersen et al. (2021), Pástor et al.
(2021), Zerbib (2022); while Görgen et al. (2020), Faccini et al. (2023), Hsu et al. (2023)
use portfolio sorting techniques. In this context, the methodology we propose is easier as it

al. (2020), Faccini et al. (2023), Hsu et al. (2023) applying portfolio sorting methodologies
ﬁnd that brown ﬁrms outperform the green ones.
Our insights regarding the lower performance of the portfolios obtained by implementing
an exclusionary screening strategy contribute to the strand of literature analyzing the impli-
cations of such practices, which has been analyzed mostly in the universe of SRI mutual
funds. Renneboog et al. (2008) and Capelle-Blancard and Monjon (2014) examine the rela-
tionship between the screening practices of SRI funds and their performance, showing that
screening intensity hinders the fund’s performance; Humphrey and Lee (2011), for the case
of Australian funds, ﬁnd that only positive screens reduce the fund’s risk. In Ceccarelli et
al. (2024), it is shown that while individual green securities are less risky the same cannot
be said for green mutual funds, which have higher volatility than those with median scores,
while Bauer et al. (2005) ﬁnd no evidence of signiﬁcant differences in risk-adjusted returns
between ethical and conventional funds.

in understanding the link between environmental and ﬁnancial performance. Battiston et al.
(2017), Birindelli and Chiappini (2021), Antoniuk and Leirvik (2024), and Taddeo et al.

2 Hypotheses development
We are interested in exploring the relationship between the environmental effort of a portfolio
-proxied with its E score- and its risk, measured using the CVaR. To do so, we develop and
test the following hypothesis:
H1: responsible investors need to compromise part of the portfolio performance to have
their E score (or GHG) requirements met.
123

Annals of Operations Research
The unconstrained MtC maximization leads to the tangency portfolio, i.e. the better-
performing portfolio in terms of MtC ratio. If we want to achieve a portfolio with an E score
different from that of the tangency one, we will necessarily obtain an allocation with lower
MtC. Such trade-off between E score and portfolio performance has been highlighted by
Pedersen et al. (2021) when building the ESG-SR frontier. The use of the MtC ratio extends
their work.
H2: portfolios with higher E scores (or lower GHG) have lower risk.
Many empirical papers show a negative relationship between E score and return. This
might happen for several reasons: brown stock could be considered riskier by investors who
want to be compensated with a carbon premium in order to hold them, as in Bolton and
Kacperczyk (2021), Meher et al. (2020), Görgen et al. (2020),Hsu et al. (2023). Ilhan et al.
(2021) ﬁnd similar results analyzing the options market, where the cost of option protection
against downside tail risks is higher for ﬁrms with more carbon-intense business models.
Green investment might be preferred for purely ethical reasons, and brown stocks could be
forced to grant higher returns because treated as sin stocks as in Hong and Kacperczyk (2009).
Such a relationship between return and environmental impact of a stock has been highlighted
also in Pástor et al. (2021), Pedersen et al. (2021), Zerbib (2022). Granting lower returns for
the reasons previously highlighted, green stocks should also be less risky than their brown
counterparts. Therefore we will test the hypothesis that portfolios with higher E score have
lower risk.
H3: the relationship between environmental performance and risk-reward proﬁle of the
portfolio is sector-speciﬁc.
The relationship between E score (or GHG) and risk could be affected by the choice of
the stocks composing the portfolio, since investors acting in each sector might have different
preferences about sustainability. That might cause an alteration to the relationship between
E score (or GHG) and risk highlighted in H2.
This may also be due to the fact that some sectors might be more exposed to climate policy
shocks (Battiston et al., 2017). Antoniuk and Leirvik (2024), analyzing each ETF’s reaction
to climate-related events, show that the sectors more dependent on the fossil fuel sector’s
energy supply (e.g. transport or utility sectors), will have increased uncertainty and risks.
A similar conclusion has been reached by Birindelli and Chiappini (2021), who found that
stock market reactions to climate policies vary by sector. In particular, a negative reaction
has been observed in sectors such as Energy and Industrials, due to the expected higher
compliance costs and transition risk. Taddeo et al. (2024) highlight that sectors such as
Energy, Basic Materials, and Industrials inherently carry greater environmental burdens due
to the resource-intensive nature of their production processes. Finally, Pham et al. (2019)
conﬁrm these sector-dependent patterns for the German market. Garcia et al. (2017) ﬁnd
instead that companies in sensitive industries, such as energy, chemicals, or mining and steel
making, present superior environmental performance, even when controlling for the ﬁrm’s
size and country.

3.2 E score integration
The most popular sustainable investment strategies in Global Sustainable Investment Alliance
(2020) are E score integration and exclusionary screening. Through E score integration
we include E metrics into the optimal allocation problem, while exclusionary screening is
obtained by removing from the investable assets those with poor E score or those whose
business activities are against the moral values of the investors (e.g. oil and coal companies
are commonly classiﬁed under the excluded assets). In this work, we examine the effects on
portfolio performance of both strategies.
The E score integration is obtained by adding an additional constraint to the MtC opti-
mization problem: the overall E score of the portfolio, which is given by the E score of the
single instrument times its weight, must be equal to a desired level of responsibility ¯E. The
additional constraint is:
E⊤x = ¯E.
(3.4)
The allocation having the highest possible MtC while at the same time achieving the E
score of ¯E is the result of the optimization described. The E score MtC frontier is obtained
by repeatedly maximizing the MtC for different benchmark E scores, and shows how the
portfolio performance varies with changes in the E score. To take into account the possible
effects deriving from exclusionary screening practices, the efﬁcient frontier is calculated
removing the stocks with E score lower than the 20th (exclusionary screening strategy 1)
and 30th (exclusionary screening strategy 2) percentile of the average E score distribution,
corresponding to an E-score of 73.1434 and 75.7561, respectively.
3.3 GHG emissions integration
One way to quantify the greenness of a corporation is by measuring its Greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions. The standard for measuring the GHG emission is the Greenhouse Gas
Protocol elaborated by the World Resources Institute and the World Business Council for
Sustainable Development3, which divides emissions into three Scopes. Scope 1 considers the
direct emissions, from the resources owned by the company; Scope 2 adds indirect emissions,
caused by the energy, heat, steam or cooling used during the productive processes; for Scope
3 includes all the residual emissions related to the company’s operations but that are not
already included neither in Scope 1 or Scope 2.
Another possible choice to quantify the environmental impact of a ﬁrm is using carbon
intensity, obtained normalizing the tonnes of CO2 emitted by the revenues. The CO2 or more
generally GHG emissions are usually directly proportional to the size of the ﬁrm, so the
normalization allows to evaluate ﬁrms’ carbon efﬁciency per unit of sales.
Following Bolton and Kacperczyk (2023) and Bolton et al. (2022), and the results from
Chen et al. (2025) and Aswani et al. (2024) who found little to no correlation between carbon
intensity and returns, we opted for the total GHG emissions as an environmental measure
of the company. Carbon intensity can be seen as a measure of carbon efﬁciency, so even
if bigger ﬁrm emits in absolute terms more CO2 could be seen as more green than smaller
ﬁrms. Furthermore, a reduction in carbon intensity does not necessarily imply a reduction of
total GHG emissions. In addition to that, it is important to notice that the objectives and the
commitment taken in order to mitigate the effects of climate change are always expressed in
term of a reduction of the total CO2 emissions. For this reason, environmentally motivated

4 Empirical results
4.1 Data
We collect daily data from the STOXX Europe 600, considering the period 03/01/2007
30/12/2019. The index is composed of the 600 most representative companies from European
countries, divided by 11 industry sectors: Technology, Telecommunications, Health Care,
Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Consumer Staples, Industrials, Basic Materials, Energy,
Utilities, Real Estate. As for the risk-free rate, we use the average Euribor for the period
considered.
The non-normality of the returns is highlighted in the descriptive statistics which show
mean very close to 0 and signiﬁcant negative skewness, indicating a distribution with a more
pronounced left tail i.e., investors are likely to expect frequent small gains but few large
losses. Daily returns present a high value for the kurtosis: the returns follow leptokurtic dis-
tributions, producing therefore more extreme values. The Jarque-Bera normality test rejects
the hypothesis of normality for all returns studied.
We gather the E scores for the years 2007 to 2019. It is the E component of the ESG
score, a synthetic measure of the company’s environmental, social, and corporate governance
performance, calculated using publicly available company-reported data. It is used as a proxy
for the environmental preferences of investors. All the data is collected from Reuters (2021)
(now LSEG). After cleaning the data we are left with 327 stocks for which we have availability
of E scores. Looking at the distribution of the average E score, a longer left tail is observed,
indicating that most of the companies have sufﬁcient grading Fig. 1.
Studying the evolution over the years of the average E score for each sector, it has increased
in all sectors. Furthermore, Real Estate, Utilities, Energy, Consumer Staples, and Basic
Materials perform constantly better on average than the others Fig. 2.
We retrieve the total GHG emission from LSEG database. Due to limited data availability,
the ﬁrst observation for the total GHG emission is 2015. In the evolution of average GHG
emissions by sector, we observe a remarkable heterogeneity. The Energy sector stands out
with by far the highest level of emissions, while Industrials and Basic Materials follow at a
considerable distance, with an upward trend over the sample period. The other sectors show
relatively stable and much lower emission levels. Overall, the dynamics conﬁrm that sectors
traditionally associated with resource and energy intensity remain the major contributors to
GHG emissions, while service-oriented or technology-driven sectors record comparatively
small values Fig. 3.

4.3 GHG MtC efficient frontier
The following frontiers have been obtained by repeatedly maximizing the MtC ratio consid-
ering a conﬁdence level of α = 95% and imposing different levels of target GHG emissions,
taking as GHG emissions for each stock the last observed value (at 2019).
In both the case of the no short sales (Figure 9) and covered short position (Figure 11)
strategy, we observe that higher levels of target GHG emissions hinder the portfolio perfor-
mance. In particular, in the no short sales case, reducing the GHG emissions under those
of the maximum MtC portfolio is extremely costly: trying to achieve portfolios with total
emissions below 20.16 -the level of emissions of the maximum MtC portfolio- leads to a
steep decrease in performance. This additional analysis conﬁrms what was found for the
case of the E score MtC efﬁcient frontier, regarding the strategy that grants the best trade-off
in terms of economic and environmental performance and for the presence of a trade-off
between return and environmental objectives.
As for the CVaR and expected return relationship, for both strategies we observe from
Figs. 10 and 12 that to higher levels of target GHG emissions, we ﬁnd only inefﬁcient
portfolios.

Basic Materials, inherently carry greater environmental burdens (Taddeo et al., 2024). This
implies that improving environmental performance in these sectors can be particularly costly,
possibly leading to less favorable risk-return proﬁles for high-E portfolios. In addition to that,
the works by Birindelli and Chiappini (2021) and Pham et al. (2019) show how such sector,
the Energy one in particular, had a negative reaction to the Paris Agreement announcements,
suggesting a perceived increase in risk for more environmentally compliant ﬁrms, consistent
with our empirical observation.

5 Conclusions
In light of the recent increased interest in responsible investments, this work examined the
performance of a portfolio built in a way to incorporate investors’ environmental preferences.
The main feature of our analysis is the choice of the CVaR as a risk measure since it is able
to better capture the risk when dealing with non-normal returns. Maximizing the MtC with
the additional constraint on the E score of the portfolio we obtained the E score MtC efﬁcient
frontier. Our analysis highlighted the presence of a trade-off between MtC and E score and
showed that portfolios with higher E score have lower CVaR. We further explore the link
between risk and environmental performance analyzing portfolios built by stock categorized
