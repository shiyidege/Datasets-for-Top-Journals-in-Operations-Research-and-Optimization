Abstract
The intent of the residency algorithm is to match medical graduates and programs based 
on their ranked preferences to improve applicant-program fit and patient care. The algo­
rithm has been used for more than a generation without an external validation that can 
provide feedback to improve it. This validation study runs the algorithm 2300 times using 
randomized applicant and program ranking inputs. The study finds that the algorithm’s 
matching outcomes are related to the input preferences of programs but unrelated to those 
of applicants. Odds ratio of matching related to the program’s ranking of applicants was 
0.64 (95 CI 0.63, 0.55; P < 0.001), suggesting a significant, lower odds of matching. The 
odds ratio of matching related to the program’s number of positions was 2.10 (95 CI 
2.06, 2.15; P < 0.001), again suggesting a significant, larger odds of matching to larger 
programs. The odds ratio of matching related to the applicants’ rank of programs was 1.01 
(95 CI 0.99, 1.03; P < 1), indicating that the influence of applicants’ ranks of programs 
have a significant, but small effect on odds of matching. Odds ratio of matching related to 
the size of the applicant’s rank-order-list, is 1.04 (95 CI 1.02,1.06; P < 0.01), which shows 
that while the relationship between the variables is statistically significant, the change in 
odds of matching for an applicant with a larger list is only 1.04 times the original odds.
The results suggest ways of improving the algorithm to achieve better applicant-pro­
gram fit and higher applicant matching odds.
Keywords  NRMP · Matching algorithm · Medical residency
1  Introduction
The National Residency Matching Program (NRMP) seeks to match more than 60,000 
medical graduates to 30,000 positions at 4,000 hospital programs (NRMP, 2020). The 
majority of clinical care at teaching hospitals is provided by resident physicians (Smalley

& Keskinocak, 2016). A primary purpose of the residency program is to prepare trainees to 
deliver unsupervised care (Turner et al., 2013). The programs select medical graduates for 
interviews. Interviewed graduates then rank programs and vice versa. The programs and 
applicant ranking lists are input into to the NRMP algorithm that seeks to match them based 
on their preferences.
The overarching goals of the residency algorithm used by the NRMP are to fairly, effi­
ciently, transparently, and reliably match applicants and programs (NRMP, 2020; Curtin 
& Signer, 2017). Matches that are related to preferences of both applicants and programs 
would improve applicant-program fit and patient health care.
The algorithm used by the NRMP (Gale & Shapley, 1962; Roth & Sotomayor, 1992) was 
program-proposing prior to 1997 (Roth, 1984). After commissioning a study, the NRMP 
adopted in 1997 an applicant-proposing algorithm, whereby student preferences of pro­
grams are considered initially, and program preferences are considered in later steps. These 
algorithms are deemed to be stable matching algorithms, which means they do not produce 
other matches that are more preferred by both an applicant and a program relative to their 
existing match.(Dubins & Freedman, 1981) These algorithms were found to show little 
benefit to be derived from engaging in strategic gaming ranking behavior by applicants and 
programs (Roth & Peranson, 1997). Further, there were few match outcome changes, less 
than 1%, found between the applicant-proposing and the program-proposing model (Roth 
& Peranson, 1997).
Since 1997, the current applicant-proposing algorithm has not been externally validated 
nor substantially revised. There has been a growing debate about the matching process 
(Carek & Anderson, 2001), and about whether it favors programs or students. Even though 
the match outcome may be stable, because there is no other match outcome from the algo­
rithm that is preferred by both the applicant and program matched, the algorithm may pos­
sibly favor one party over the other in arriving at the existing match. The NRMP literature 
for students does not make clear that the algorithm may be more advantageous for programs 
than for students (Williams, 1995). The matching process has been argued to be not unfair, 
since applicants receive higher-ranked matches than programs (Yuan & Gafni, 1990). The 
match process has been legally challenged as uncompetitive, but the government deemed 
that the process is ‘highly efficient, pro-competitive, and longstanding’ (Curtin & Signer, 
2017). The American Medical Student Association, the Public Citizen’s Health Research 
Group, and the Medical Student Section of the American Medical Association are among 
several groups advocating change to a residency matching algorithm that prioritizes pro­
grams over students (Yuan & Gafni, 1990). According to a majority of applicants (52%), 
there is a perceived need for improvement in the current process (Anderson & Jacobs, 2000).
Due to these pressures, emerging research has suggested alternative algorithms and 
models that give more weight to students (Mascarenhas & Puranam, 2023). A model using 
simulated student preferences at the start of the application process was found to increase 
interview invitations for most applicants when preferences were disclosed (Whipple et al., 
2019). An alternative residency matching algorithm where student preferences are primary, 
rather than program preferences, was found to result in similar overall matching rates and 
to place students in their more preferred programs, while resulting in a lower rank for the 
last student matched to a program (Mascarenhas et al., 2021). More objective evidence on 
the existing algorithm is needed to justify and inform directions for its possible revision.

Because AI technologies have the potential for algorithmic bias, it is important for them 
to be transparent and be externally validated, but these aspects are seldom observed (He et 
al., 2019). As algorithms become prevalent, there is a pressing need for their independent, 
external evaluation. The current residency matching algorithm has been used for more than 
thirty years without external validation and revision to improve it. Independent research 
evidence is needed on whether the algorithm’s matching outcomes are related to the prefer­
ences of programs and/or applicants, and its stated purpose. If residency matches are found 
to be related to both program and applicant preferences, it would improve applicant-pro­
gram fit, which has been related to patient health care. The interaction between people and 
the environment is particularly relevant in healthcare settings where compatibility affects 
health care professionals and the patient (Herkes, 2019). In a literature review, 27 of 28 
studies reported a significant association between the perception of fit and staff outcomes in 
healthcare contexts (Herkes, 2019).
The primary objective of this study is to examine whether the existing algorithm’s match 
outcomes are related to the input preferences of programs and applicants, as intended and 
expected by participants.
Second, since finding and recruiting for a residency position are difficult, resource-con­
suming, and uncertain, what strategies outside of the algorithm can applicants and programs 
pursue to increase their odds of matching? In striving to match into a program, many stu­
dents apply to more than one specialty and interview as many programs as possible. Pro­
grams want to meet their resident staffing needs with candidates who they desire and who 
fit their program.
The study examines two basic hypotheses:
Hypothesis 1  The null hypothesis is that the applicant’s program ranking is unrelated to 
the likelihood of the applicant matching to that program. The alternative hypothesis is that 
the applicant’s ranking of the program is associated with the likelihood of matching to that 
program.
Hypothesis 2  The null hypothesis is that the program’s ranking of an applicant is not associ­
ated with the likelihood of the applicant matching to that program. The alternative hypoth­
esis is that the program’s ranking of an applicant is related to the likelihood of the applicant 
matching to that program.
1.1  Methods and analysis
The analysis was conducted in two stages. The first stage obtained the match outcomes, 
whether or not each student matched to each applied program in a specialty. Randomized 
data were generated corresponding to published 2020 NRMP data parameters. The study 
does not use actual patient, student, or program data, so it is not subject to Institutional 
Review Board or ethics committee reviews.
The study first determined matching outcomes using the algorithm and macro data cor­
responding to the NRMP. The input data correspond to the macro parameters of mean and 
range for the variables reported by the NRMP. Randomization reduces researcher bias, pro­
vides natural variation around the variable parameters, and enables many more runs to be 
examined than one run may be unique to one year. Using randomized data permits isolation

and focusing on the algorithm’s procedures. We followed the algorithm procedures outlined 
and explained in the NRMP’s published video and data (NRMP, 2020; Wilde et al., 2020). 
We ran the algorithm 2300 times. Realistic synthetic data circumvent privacy issues, and its 
range includes the complexities of real data (Tucker et al., 2020).
The existing algorithm initially considers an applicant’s program preferences and seeks 
to match that applicant into their most favored program. The algorithm next seeks to match 
using the program’s ranking of applicants. Initial matches are provisional, and an applicant 
may be dematched in a later round after considering the program’s preferences which may 
rank other students higher. The matches are finalized after the preferences of all students 
and programs are considered.
The study used the number of programs and applicants corresponding to those published 
by the NRMP. The lengths of the student and program rank ordered lists are not significantly 
different from those reported by the NRMP. The computer-generated random student rank­
ing lists of 0 to 20 programs, and randomized program ranking lists of 60 to 90 students.
The ratio of the number of students per position corresponds to that published in the 
NRMP data and each program is randomly assigned a number of positions around this aver­
age ratio. This procedure compiled a dataset of positions, students, programs, and rank order 
lists. Next, this dataset generated overall match results using the current algorithm. Wilde’s 
“matching” software library (Wilde et al., 2020) was used because it is efficiently coded in 
Python and is the foundation of the algorithm used by the NRMP. The input data included 
the positions, students, programs, and their rank order lists. The library software algorithm 
produced the match results for each student and program.
Next, whether a student matched to each applied program was used as the dependent 
variable in a logistic regression. The independent explanatory variables were– the program’s 
ranking of a student, the student’s ranking of the program, the interaction of the student and 
program rankings, the program’s number of residency positions, the size of the student’s 
rank ordered list, and the interaction of the number of positions and size of the applicant’s 
rank ordered list. The model’s candidate explanatory variables included independent and 
interaction variables as well as linear and nonlinear variables.

The size of an applicant’s ranking list has little effect on the matching odds (1.04, P < 0.01).

The bottom of Table 3 shows the adjusted odds ratios for each of the 23 specialties. These 
results show that a program’s lower (better) ranking of students is consistently related to 
higher odds of matching in every specialty. The students’ ranking of the programs is not 
related to the match in most (19 of 23) specialties, and when significant is directionally 
inconsistent, suggesting that adjusting the specialty has little positive impact on matching 
odds. A program’s number of positions is consistently and positively related to the odds 
of matching in most (20 of 23) specialties. The two interaction terms are not related to the 
match in the majority of specialties and have a weak influence (range 0.96,1.02) on the odds 
of matching in the minority of specialties when they are significantly related.
Table 4 compares the algorithm’s match outcomes with the logistic regression model’s 
likelihood predicted outcomes. The table shows that the logistic regression model correctly 
predicted 33,129 or about 89% of the 37,237 (33,129 + 4,108) algorithm’s matched out­
comes, as well as correctly predicted 164,578 or about 98% of the 168,526 (3,948 + 164,578) 
of the algorithm’s non-matched outcomes. These predicted percentages are higher than a 
prediction rate of 50% that would be obtained through a random equal probability assign­
ment to the two outcomes.
Analysis in this paper has been mostly the result of logistic regressions (LR). Other 
methodologies such as Random Forests (RF), and Support Vector Machines (SVM) can also 
be used. Comparison of how the results of LR, RF and SVM may be subjects for a future 
study.
3  Conclusion
With the growth of AI, algorithms are becoming prevalent, but they are often not validated, 
so we do not know if they actually deliver as intended. The existing residency matching 
algorithm has been employed by the NRMP for decades without independent, external vali­
dation or revision. The objective of the NRMP is to fairly match medical graduates and 
programs based on both parties’ preferences. Incorporating the preferences of both appli­
cants and programs would improve applicant-program fit, which has been associated with 
improved staff outcomes and patient care in health care settings (Tucker et al., 2020).
This study examined the current algorithm’s procedures by running 2300 simulations of 
the algorithm using randomized data. Using randomized data helps to isolate and focus on

which finalizes the match, resulting in matches unrelated to the applicant’s program pref­
erences. The algorithm’s resulting matches do not meet its stated objective of effectively 
incorporating the preferences of both applicants and programs in the match outcomes, and 
a revision in that direction may yield benefits associated with better applicant-program fit.
The study’s findings also provided insights into how residency applicants and programs 
can increase their odds of matching in a recruitment process that is complex, uncertain, 
and competitive. Many applicants undertake resource-consuming activities to improve their 
odds of matching, Students have pursued additional degrees to secure a residency posi­
tion, but research suggests that match rates are not different between MD and dual-degree 
students (Christensen et al., 2020). Many students try to increase their chances of match­
ing by applying to multiple specialties and interviewing more programs, which increases 
application costs as well as spreads their energies and efforts. If all students pursue this 
tactic, it overwhelms the system and reduces the attention given to each student. As the 
average number of applications has increased, the process becomes less personal (Sullivan, 
2016). Even if it is individually better for an applicant to apply to the highest number of 
programs because they may consider their personal risk and may not be willing to accept 
application caps, it is not beneficial for the majority of applicants when they all pursue this 
strategy (Wall et al., 2019). Proposals have called for limiting the number of programs that 
applicants are permitted to apply for. This study finds that applicants cannot rely on their 
ranking preferences for programs, apply to a particular specialty, or apply to more programs 
to increase their odds of matching. This study’s findings, that applying to more programs 
which are likely to have fewer positions is associated with lower odds of matching, provide 
additional evidence for the proposal to limit the total number of programs to which students 
can apply (Hwang & Kelz, 2023).
This study’s findings suggest actions to improve the odds of matching. Specifically, appli­
cants should stay within their specialty of interest and target programs offering more posi­
tions to increase the probability of matching. A program’s number of positions is publicly 
reported and can be used by applicants in their residency search to identify these programs 
offering more positions to increase their odds of matching. This strategy of targeting pro­
grams with a higher number of positions is different from, and runs counter to the common 
practice of applying to as many programs as possible which is found to be unrelated to odds 
of matching. The number of program positions was found to be inversely related (-0.40) to 
the applicant rank order list, possibly because as candidates apply to more programs they 
increasingly apply to the many programs with fewer positions.
Research has found that larger pediatrics programs may offer residents greater training 
exposure to more severely ill patients and asthma patients (Jennings et al., 2003). Research 
has shown that larger surgery programs are associated with higher resident board exam per­

formance. Taken together, these findings suggest that students who apply to larger programs 
with more positions may increase their odds of matching, receive a different type of training 
exposure, and attain higher board examination performance.
This study finds that programs can increase their odds of matching by ranking their pre­
ferred candidates higher in the matching algorithm, and by increasing their number of resi­
dency positions offered. Larger programs with more positions, which are associated with 
more varied training exposures and higher board examination performance, may also be 
more attractive to applicants. Expanding the number of residency positions has to be con­
sidered in the context of program’s budgetary and accreditation constraints that they face.
This study found that the algorithm’s matching outcomes are related mainly on the pro­
grams’ ranking of students. Programs’ rankings of students, however, have been found 
not to predict subsequent clinical performance during residency (Borowitz et al., 2000). 
Research is needed to examine whether the applicant rankings used in the matching algo­
rithm are related to longer term resident and program performance as well as patient health 
care. Research is needed on revising the algorithm to develop matches that are based on 
simultaneous inclusion, instead of sequential inclusion which may supplant earlier input, 
of preferences of both applicants and programs. Research on the residency algorithm to 
improve applicant-program fit should include expanded data on couples’ preferences, as 
well as demographic, gender, and minority status, as the data become available. Another 
possibility for improving applicant-program fit is to replace the current algorithm in favor 
of employing a multi-criteria instrument that explicitly utilizes interview feedback on mul­
tiple dimensions, such as program clusterings, cultural values, geographic preference, and 
research orientation.
