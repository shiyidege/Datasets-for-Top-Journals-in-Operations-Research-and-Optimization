We consider a joint inventory and pricing problem with one warehouse and multiple stores, in which the
retailer makes a one-time decision on the amount of inventory to be placed at the warehouse at the beginning
of the selling season, followed by periodic joint replenishment and pricing decisions for each store throughout
the season. Demand at each store follows a Poisson distribution and unmet demand is immediately lost.
The retailer incurs the usual variable ordering costs, inventory holding costs and lost sales costs, and his
objective is to maximize the expected total proﬁts. The optimal control (or policy) for this problem is
unknown and numerically challenging to compute. We ﬁrst analyze the performance of two popular and
simple heuristic policies that directly implement the solution of a deterministic approximation of the original
stochastic problem. We show that simple re-optimization of the deterministic problem may yield a very poor
performance by causing a ”spiraling down” movement in price trajectory, which in turn yields a “spiraling
up” movement in expected lost sales quantity (i.e., the expected lost sales quantity continues to increase
as we re-optimize more frequently). This ﬁnding cautions against a naive use of simple re-optimizations
without ﬁrst understanding the dynamics of the model. We then propose two improved heuristic policies
based on the optimal solution of a deterministic relaxation of the original stochastic problem. Our ﬁrst
heuristic policy computes static prices and order-up-to levels for warehouse and stores, and then replenish
each store at the beginning of each batch of periods. Our second heuristic policy builds on the ﬁrst heuristic
and dynamically adjusts prices over time based on realized demands. We show that both policies have near-
optimal performance when the annual market size is large, with the second policy outperforming the ﬁrst
one. Finally, we also prove a fundamental theoretical lower bound on the performance of any policy that relies
on static prices. This lower bound highlights the true value of dynamic pricing, whose eﬀect on performance
in our setting cannot be duplicated by simply implementing a more sophisticated replenishment policy.
1.
Introduction
How should a retailer (he) who operates a warehouse and multiple stores jointly manage inventory
and pricing decisions to maximize his expected total proﬁts throughout a ﬁnite selling season? (In
this paper, we use the term “store” in a generic sense to refer to either an individual physical store
or a cluster of physical stores located in nearby areas, whichever is more appropriate. Similarly,

since some retailers may have multiple warehouses that are located within a relatively short driving
distance from each other, our “one warehouse” can also be seen as a cluster of nearby warehouses.)
This is a fundamental problem in retailing of both theoretical and practical importance. From a
theoretical perspective, understanding how we can solve, or approximately solve, the so-called One-
Warehouse Multi-Store (OWMS) problem can serve as a steppingstone for solving a more general
and complex real-world problem such as the multi-warehouse multi-store problem. To the best of
our knowledge, not much work has been reported in the academic literature to address this, as the
joint inventory and pricing problem is considered to be very challenging; see our literature review
discussion in section 2. From a practical perspective, retailers usually have one local warehouse,
or a cluster of them, dedicated to serving multiple local stores; thus, a “solution” to the OWMS
problem already has an immediate application in real-world settings.
In this paper, motivated by applications in retail settings, where unsatisﬁed demands are usually
lost and lost sales are highly undesirable, we adopt the standard cost-based inventory model with a
potentially large lost sales cost (see our discussion in appendix EC.1 on how the magnitude of lost
sales cost is related to the maximum allowable stock-out rate). Similar to some existing literature
on the OWMS problem, in this paper, we will assume that only the stores can be periodically
replenished (e.g., weekly or twice a week); the warehouse is never replenished. We study the perfor-
mances of several popular heuristics and we also propose easy-to-implement heuristics that can be
shown to be near optimal in the setting with Poisson demand and a large annual market size (we
will formally deﬁne its meaning later in section 3). Based on our limited interaction with practi-
tioners, we are aware that Poisson demand is sometimes used in their demand estimation; needless
to say, Poisson assumption or its discrete-time analog is also commonly used in the inventory,
queuing, pricing, and revenue management (RM) literature.
All heuristic policies analyzed in this paper are based on the solution to a deterministic approxi-
mation of the original stochastic problem. Our focus of deterministic approximation is motivated by
several factors. First, deterministic approximation is widely used in the broader academic literature
(such as in queuing, pricing, and RM) as the ﬁrst-order approximation of the stochastic models
that are intractable to solve. Indeed, as we will discuss below, in the context of inventory literature,
there is also a deep stream of papers that focus primarily on analyzing deterministic inventory
models, including variants of OWMS with deterministic demand assumption. Second, deterministic
approximation is widely used in industry for optimizing a wide variety of decision problems (as in
the so-called Certainty Equivalent method). To the best of our knowledge, these types of heuristics
are also commonly used in large-scale industrial inventory optimization, especially for dealing with
complex inventory problems. Third, the research that adopts deterministic approximation such as
in RM have shown that these heuristics usually have provably good performances with respect to

some problem parameter of interest. Motivated by the successes and wide adoption both in theory
and practice, we seek to understand the performance of some of these popular heuristics in our
OMWS setting and also to design new provably near-optimal heuristics.
While using a deterministic model to approximate the intractable original stochastic model is
perhaps quite intuitive, what is less clear is how to properly “implement” the deterministic solution
in the original stochastic system where demand arrives randomly over time. In particular, since a
deterministic model usually assumes away the stochasticity in the system, a naive implementation
of the deterministic solution without some proper modiﬁcations can yield a poor performance. A
common way of dealing with demand stochasticity in the inventory management literature is to
hold additional safety stock, whose magnitude usually depends on the magnitude of randomness
in demand and the lost sales cost. In a complex system like OWMS under non-stationary demand
arrivals, how much safety stock to hold at each location in each period becomes a challenging
question, especially since demand can be adaptively changed via dynamic pricing. Indeed, we are
not aware of any work that has studied the OWMS problem with dynamic pricing that provides
eﬃcient heuristic with a provably near-optimal guarantee. The present paper aims at shedding
light on what could be a (in)eﬀective heuristic policy. Incidentally, we are able to also show the
true value of dynamic pricing.
1.1.
Our Contributions
Our contributions in this paper are twofold. First, we analyze the performances of two popular
heuristic policies that directly implement the deterministic solution and are commonly used in
practice. These heuristics have been shown to have superior performances in some applications (e.g.,
in RM and a variety of resource allocation problems; see discussions in section 2). However, we show
that their performances for the OWMS problem are unsatisfactory. In fact, their implementation
may result in a certain spiraling phenomena. We provide the intuition for this phenomena by
analyzing the evolution of system variables. Second, we propose two improved heuristic policies
that can be theoretically shown to have much better performances. The ﬁrst heuristic policy uses
static prices whereas the second heuristic policy implements dynamic pricing. We show that the
second heuristic performs much better (in terms of upper bound for proﬁt loss) compared to the
ﬁrst one, which suggests the beneﬁt of dynamic pricing. To rigorously establish this insight, we also
prove a fundamental theoretical lower bound for the performance of any feasible policy that relies
on static prices. Our lower bound result underscores the importance of dynamic pricing in our
OWMS problem, whose eﬀect cannot be duplicated by simply implementing a more sophisticated
replenishment policy.
1. Performance of heuristics that directly implement the deterministic solution

We ﬁrst study the performance of two popular and tractable heuristic policies, which we call SDET
and RDET, respectively. Under SDET, we set the prices and the ordering decisions according to
the deterministic solution without any modiﬁcations; under RDET, we re-optimize the determin-
istic approximation at the beginning of each period and set the prices and the ordering decisions
according to the solution of the re-optimized deterministic problem. We show that (i) the optimal-
ity gap of SDET grows linearly in lost sales cost, whereas (ii) the optimality gap of RDET can
be much worse than that of SDET. While the ﬁrst result is not too surprising, the second result
is quite surprising. In fact, the re-optimization-based heuristic has been showed to have superior
performance for many dynamic optimization problems (see the discussions on relevant works in
the RM literature below). In the context of pure inventory problems, under diﬀerent settings, both
Secomandi (2008) and Ahn et al. (2018) studied the performance of re-optimization heuristics.
Their results suggest that re-optimization usually yields a signiﬁcant improvement in performance
and, even if it does not, it usually does not hurt the original performance by much. However, in
our setting, we show that not only may RDET not always improve the performance of SDET, it
can actually hurt the performance of SDET by a lot. In our numerical experiments in section 8,
we show that the loss of RDET can be up to four times larger than the loss of SDET, which is
clearly not negligible.
To shed light on the driver behind this observation, we prove that re-optimizing the deterministic
approximation of OWMS problem can cause a “spiraling down” movement in price trajectory,
which in turn may cause a “spiraling up” movement in the expected lost sales quantity. That is, the
expected total lost sales keeps increasing as we re-optimize more frequently. As a result, in a setting
where a retailer has a high lost sales cost and a high service level, having a higher expected total
lost sales naturally leads to worse performance. We discuss in more detail in section 3 the drivers
of these spiraling phenomena in our setting. It is worth noting that while the potential negative
impact of re-optimization on performance has been noted in the literature (e.g., Cooper 2002), to
the best of our knowledge, the emergence of spiraling phenomena under re-optimization has not
been previously observed in the broader Operations Management (OM) and Operations Research
(OR) literature. Thus, our analysis of the spiraling phenomena also complements existing works in
the broader literature on re-optimization by providing valuable context-speciﬁc understanding of
why re-optimization may not always work in certain settings.
2. Two provably near-optimal heuristics and the value of dynamic pricing
Motivated by our observations in studying SDET and RDET, we propose two improved heuristic
policies that use the optimal solution to the deterministic approximation with careful modiﬁcations.
The ﬁrst heuristic policy, which we call FP, is a “warm-up” for our main heuristic, which we call
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3688561

5
JIP. FP computes static prices and order-up-to levels (OULs) for the warehouse and stores and then
replenishes each store in a batched manner; that is, stores are replenished only at the beginning
of each batch of periods. The key idea behind replenishment batching is to guarantee (with a high
probability) that each store will always place a positive order quantity at replenishment times. It
also allows the safety stock to be pooled across adjacent time periods, at the cost of additional
store holding costs. We show that FP is asymptotically optimal in the setting with a large annual
market size and has a robust performance with respect to the magnitude of lost sales cost.
We further propose an adaptive heuristic policy called JIP. JIP builds on the inventory control
method used in FP, but it changes the prices dynamically over time. In particular, the design of
price adjustment in JIP combines the idea of dynamic pricing with linear rate adjustment from the
pricing literature with the novel idea of random errors averaging across all stores. The intuition
behind random errors averaging is similar in spirit to the intution behind demand pooling in the
pure inventory literature: random errors averaging allows the error (randomness) in one store to be
“cancelled out” by the errors in other stores, which results in a minimal cumulative eﬀect on the
expected total proﬁts. In a nutshell, JIP combines order-up-to policy with dynamic pricing with
linear rate adjustment policy. While both policies are known to perform well in their respective
literature (see below), what is not clear is whether a certain combination of these two policies
can also be shown to perform reasonably well in the OWMS joint inventory and pricing problem.
Fortunately, the answer is yes. We show theoretically that the asymptotic performance of JIP
improves over that of FP, and the improvement is quite pronounced numerically in many instances.
Finally, recall that a key diﬀerence between FP and JIP is that FP uses static prices while JIP
uses dynamic pricing. Although our analysis of FP and JIP suggests the value of implementing
dynamic pricing for our OWMS problem, it is curious to ask whether this gap is primarily driven
by the speciﬁc form of FP policy that we analyze. That is, can the gap between FP and JIP be
closed by a policy that still implements static prices but perhaps in combination with a more
sophisticated replenishment policy? The answer is negative. We prove a fundamental theoretical
lower bound on the performance of any policy that does not dynamically adjust prices regardless
of how sophisticated the replenishment policy is.

2.
Literature Review
Our work is related to four streams of literature: pure inventory literature, pure pricing literature,
joint inventory and pricing literature, and re-optimization literature. We discuss them in turn (due
to space limitations, our review is not exhaustive; we focus only on the most relevant papers).
Pure inventory literature. Lost sales inventory systems have been studied extensively in the
literature (e.g., see Bijvank and Vis 2011 for an excellent review). For a single-store problem with
zero lead time, it is known that order-up-to policy is optimal. When lead time is positive, the
full structure of the optimal policy is still unknown and can be only partially characterized, as
shown in Karlin and Scarf (1958), Morton (1969), and Zipkin (2008). For the OWMS system (also
called the one-warehouse multi-retailer (OWMR) system), the analysis of optimal policy is further
complicated by the inventory allocation decision. In the seminal paper of Clark and Scarf (1960),
the authors showed that the OWMS model has complicated state-dependent optimal policies,
which suﬀer from the curse of dimensionality; the diﬃculty of this problem lies in the fact that the
allocation decision depends on the stock levels of all stores. When assuming backlogged demand,
Jackson (1988) proposed a simple heuristic called “ship-up-to-S” policy (a variant of order-up-to
policy). However, they did not provide a theoretical guarantee for this heuristic. Jain et al. (2012)
studied a similar problem with ﬁxed ordering cost and lost sales under a continuous review policy.
Assuming Poisson demand, they showed that it is optimal for the retailers to order according to a
time-based order-up-to policy.
To the best of our knowledge, Marklund and Rosling (2012) were the ﬁrst to provide a provably
near-optimal heuristic for the OWMR system with backlogged demand. They used a Lagrangian
relaxation method and showed that their heuristic is asymptotically optimal in the setting with
a large number of stores. Nambiar et al. (2019) extended the analysis in Marklund and Rosling
(2012) to a system with lost sales and a more general demand model. They analyzed a Lagrangian
relaxation heuristic and showed that it is also asymptotically optimal when the number of stores is
large. Recently, Miao et al. (2020) revisited the model in Marklund and Rosling (2012), but with a
lost sales system. They proposed a modiﬁed Lagrangian relaxation heuristic and showed that it is
asymptotically optimal when either the number of stores or the number of selling periods is large.
Due to the complexity of many inventory problems, there is also a deep literature that focuses
primarily on analyzing deterministic inventory models where demands are assumed to be deter-
ministic. For example, the deterministic OWMR and its simpler variants, the single-item lot-sizing
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3688561

7
problem and the joint replenishment problem, have received considerable attention. Diﬀerent solu-
tion approaches, including dynamic programming (Zangwill 1966, Federgruen and Tzur 1999), LP
relaxation and rounding techniques (Chan et al. 2002, Levi et al. 2008), combinatorial optimiza-
tion (Shen et al. 2009), and primal-dual algorithms (Levi et al. 2006, Cheung et al. 2016), have
been explored to derive high-quality policies under diﬀerent cost structures. The main argument
for considering a deterministic inventory model despite its rather simplistic assumption is that, for
some complex inventory problems, even the deterministic version of the problems themselves are
already diﬃcult to solve, which warrants special attention. In contrast to some of the papers that
study deterministic inventory models, our deterministic formulation is much easier to solve (since
we do not consider ﬁxed ordering cost). Therefore, solving the deterministic problem itself is not
the focus of our work. However, our results serve as a cautionary tale with respect to the potential
performance of any heuristic that directly uses the solution of a deterministic approximation.
Pure pricing literature. Papers on pure pricing literature tend to focus on the problem of
dynamic pricing with ﬁnite inventory and without replenishment opportunity. Unlike the pure
inventory literature, which puts a lot of emphasis on inventory holding cost and unsatisﬁed demand
cost (through either back-order or lost sales costs), pure pricing literature tends to focus solely on
revenue (or proﬁt) maximization while ignoring the inventory holding cost and unsatisﬁed demand
cost (see Elmaghraby and Keskinocak 2003 and Bitran and Caldentey 2003 for overviews of the
literature). Some notable papers include the now-classic Gallego and Van Ryzin (1994, 1997). In
these papers, the authors showed that simple ﬁxed price policies, in which the prices are pre-
computed at the beginning of the season and never adjusted during the season (except when stock-
outs happen), are asymptotically optimal in the setting with Poisson demand and a large initial
inventory and total market size. Recent advances on the topic have shown that the performance of
ﬁxed-price heuristics can be signiﬁcantly improved using a simple price adjustment formula that is
easy to compute without having to resolve large-scale re-optimizations (e.g., Jasin 2014, Chen et al.
2016, Lei et al. 2018, Lei and Jasin 2020). Our dynamic pricing scheme in this paper is motivated
by the one developed in Jasin (2014). However, unlike the analysis in Jasin (2014), which assumes
ﬁxed inventory without replenishment, analysis of this heuristic in the current paper involves new
subtleties, including those related to its interaction with inventory replenishment, which aﬀects
not only the available inventory at the stores but also the remaining inventory at the warehouse.
Joint inventory and pricing literature. The beneﬁt of joint inventory and pricing policy
has been noted since the early development of inventory theory (Whitin 1955). Most papers in the
literature focus on the setting with a single location. Assuming backlogged demand and speciﬁc
demand functions, Federgruen and Heching (1999) showed that a base stock list price policy is
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3688561

8
optimal. Under this policy, at each period, an order is placed to raise the inventory to the base
stock level, and a list price is charged if the initial inventory is below the base stock level; otherwise,
nothing is ordered and a discount is oﬀered. Important extensions to incorporate non-convex order-
ing cost, lost sales, and random yield have been discussed in papers such as Chen and Simchi-Levi
(2004), Chen et al. (2006), and Li and Zheng (2006), among others. We refer interested readers
to Chan et al. (2004) and Chen and Simchi-Levi (2012) for extensive surveys of relevant papers.
As noted in Feng et al. (2019), works on lost sales systems often impose restrictive conditions on
the stochastic demand functions to guarantee structural properties for optimal policy. Thus, their
results may not be applicable in many contexts.
Despite its obvious practical relevance, progress on the multi-location setting is rather limited.
Papers are mostly limited to the study of deterministic settings (e.g., Chen et al. 2001a,b, Bernstein
and Federgruen 2003, Smith and Agrawal 2017). As for stochastic systems, we are aware of very few
papers that are closely related to our work. Federgruen and Heching (2002) studied a multi-store
joint inventory and pricing control problem with backlogged demand. Admitting the diﬃculty of
analyzing the problem exactly, Federgruen and Heching (2002) proposed a tractable approximation
heuristic that yields preferable structural properties. However, they assumed the central warehouse
does not carry any inventory and functions only as a cross-docking point, which is in contrast to
our setting where the warehouse can hold inventories over the selling season. They also did not
provide a theoretical guarantee for their heuristic. Oh et al. (2014) analyzed the joint production
and pricing decisions in an assembly-to-order system with backlogged demand. They proposed a
simple heuristic that is optimal when demands do not decrease over time. In contrast, we study
lost sales with general non-stationary demands under a certain distributional assumption.
Re-optimization literature. The impact of re-optimizing a deterministic approximation of the
original stochastic problem on heuristic performance has been intensively studied in the context of
pure pricing and RM problems, where the inventory decision and its related costs (such as holding
cost, backlog cost, lost sales cost) are usually not considered (e.g., Reiman and Wang 2008, Ciocan
and Farias 2012, Jasin and Kumar 2012, 2013, Jasin 2014, Bumpensanti and Wang 2020). It is
known, for example, that re-optimization may not always improve performance (e.g., Cooper 2002).
In the context of a pure inventory problem, Secomandi (2008) provided suﬃcient conditions under
which re-optimization does not deteriorate performance and Ahn et al. (2018) studied the impact
of re-optimizing a deterministic formulation in the setting of joint replenishment and inventory
allocation with a large batch size. Complementing these works, we show that re-optimization may
not only hurt performance, it can do so by a lot. We identify the spiraling phenomenon, which has
not been previously observed in the re-optimization literature. We also provide valuable context-
speciﬁc understanding of why re-optimization may not always work in certain settings.

Problem Setting and Formulations
In this section, we discuss the problem setting, the optimal control formulation of our problem,
and its deterministic relaxation. The optimal solution of the deterministic relaxation will be used
to construct the heuristics discussed in section 4 - 6.
3.1.
Problem Setting
We consider the setting of a retailer who owns one warehouse and multiple (N) stores, see Figure 1.
(As noted in section 1, in this paper, we use the terms “warehouse” and “store” in a generic sense
to also refer to clusters of nearby warehouses and stores.) The retailer needs to make a one-time
decision on the amount of inventory to be placed at the warehouse at the beginning of the selling
season, followed by periodic joint replenishment and pricing decisions for each store throughout
the season. We assume that the delivery lead time from the warehouse to the store is zero (this is
justiﬁed by the fact that we consider a nearby/local warehouse that is within a few hours of the
stores), there is no transshipment from one store to another store, and unmet demand at each store
in any period is immediately lost. The retailer incurs the usual variable ordering costs, inventory
holding costs, and lost sales costs. For simplicity, we assume zero salvage value at the end of the
season; this is without loss of generality since including a non-zero salvage value will not change
either the analysis or the key insights of the paper.
We consider a ﬁnite selling season with length κ years (κ could be fractional). In particular,
since we focus on seasonal and/or short-life-cycle products, the length of a season could be as
short as a few months (e.g., six months, or κ = 1
2) as long as a few years at most (e.g., two years,
or κ = 2). That is, throughout this paper, the variable κ = Θ(1) is treated as a constant whose
value does not scale with market size. For convenience, in this paper, we will work with a discrete-
time model where each year is divided into τ ∈Z+ small periods and, thus, the total number of

retailers to adjust prices more frequently. However, we will not consider this in the current paper.
We now describe the sequence of events. At the beginning of the selling horizon, the retailer
orders a total of Z1 units of inventory to be placed at the warehouse, each of which bears a per-unit
ordering cost of cw. We further denote the inventory levels at the warehouse and at store i at
the beginning of period t before placing orders as Zt and Xit, respectively. At the beginning of
period t, the retailer decides on the amount of inventory to be shipped from the warehouse to each
store i, denoted by Qti. Orders arrive immediately and incur a per-unit ordering cost at the store
of cs. After receiving the orders, the retailer then decides the price pit to be charged at store i,
which induces a random demand denoted by Dti(pti). (The demand distribution assumption will
be explained in the next paragraph.) At the end of each period, the retailer collects revenue on the
units sold to customers, incurs the usual holding costs at the warehouse and stores, and incurs the
lost sales costs at the stores of φs per unit of lost sales. We denote by hw and hs the annual (not
per-period) costs of carrying one unit of inventory at a store and at the warehouse, respectively.
Given our deﬁnitions, the corresponding per-period holding costs are given by ˆhw := hw/τ and

practice, the annual holding cost of a product typically equals 20%−40% of the unit cost/value of
the product (e.g., Chopra et al. 2013, Nahmias and Olsen 2015). Thus, while the annual holding
cost of a product is of the same order of magnitude as its ordering cost, the per-period (e.g., daily
or weekly) holding cost is of a smaller order of magnitude than its unit cost if the number of periods

that all store-level costs (ordering, holding, and lost sales) are identical across all stores. This is
only for simplicity since allowing store-dependent costs does not change the analysis in the paper.

be on the same order of τ, we will present all of the theoretical results in terms of τ for consistency.

Two Simple Adoptions of DET and the Spiraling Phenomena
In this section, we discuss the performance of two popular practical heuristic policies that directly
use the optimal solution of DET. The ﬁrst heuristic policy, which we call SDET (Simple DET),
simply sets the prices and ordering decisions equal to the optimal solution of DET; the second
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3688561

17
heuristic policy, which we call RDET (Re-optimized DET), sets the prices and ordering decisions
equal to the optimal solution of a re-optimized version of DET in which the inventory levels are
updated based on the realized demands (or sales). We show that both SDET and RDET can
perform poorly, which is not surprising since DET essentially fails to capture the impact of lost
sales cost when it averages out the randomness in demand. What is surprising is the fact that
RDET can perform much worse than SDET, which is rather counter intuitive. We show that re-
optimization can cause a “spiraling down” movement of prices, which in turn results in a “spiraling
up” movement in expected lost sales quantities. This highlights the potential drawback of naively
re-optimizing a deterministic model in the joint inventory and pricing setting with lost sales. In
order to clearly illustrate the intuitions behind this phenomena, in subsection 4.2, we will only
consider the simplest possible setting with a single store (i.e., N = 1).

In a nutshell, the spiraling phenomena is largely driven by the assumption that inventory place-
ment at the warehouse is only done once at the beginning of the horizon and the assumption that
unsatisﬁed demand is immediately lost instead of backlogged, which together create a “bias” on the
impact of re-optimization depending on whether the store has a positive leftover inventory at the
beginning of the re-optimization period or not. To see this more clearly, consider period τ1 where
we re-optimize (5) for the ﬁrst time and suppose that the store has a positive leftover inventory
(i.e., Xτ1 > 0) (by Lemma 1, the starting inventory at period τ1 in (6) is zero). By comparing the
objective functions of (5) and (6), it is easy to see that they diﬀer in the second term, which calcu-
lates the total costs the retailer needs to pay to order one unit of inventory from the store at the
point of re-optimization. The key diﬀerence here is that, at the point of re-optimization at period
τ1, all units of inventory to be used during the horizon have already been placed in the warehouse.
Therefore, when the retailer orders one unit of inventory for the store, she no longer needs to
consider the warehouse ordering cost (which explains the missing cw), and will actually “save” the
cost of holding that unit of inventory at the warehouse (which explain the term −ˆhw(T −v + 1)).
In other words, the marginal cost of ordering an extra unit of inventory from the store at the time
of re-optimization is smaller than that when we ﬁrst solve (6) at the beginning of the horizon.
This decrease in ordering cost means that it is “cheaper” to fulﬁll demand in the re-optimized
system (5), which creates an incentive for the retailer to increase the targeted expected demand
by decreasing the price. Moreover, as we observed in Figure 2a, the prices for all the remaining
periods also drop, which means that the targeted expected demand for all the remaining periods
also increase. This is the consequence of the concavity of revenue functions in demand rates: If the
retailer wants to increase the targeted expected demand to consume the positive leftover inventory,
it is more proﬁtable to increase the targeted expected demands across all remaining periods than
only increasing the targeted expected demands for speciﬁc periods.
While re-optimizing the deterministic relaxation at period τ1 when the store has a positive
leftover inventory encourages the retailer to increase targeted expected demands by dropping prices
as discussed above, re-optimizing the deterministic relaxation at period τ1 when the store has no
leftover inventory will not change the prices (i.e., solving (5) gives exactly the same solution as the
previously optimized solution). This is so because the state of the system starting from period τ1
looks identical to the state of the system starting from the same time under the optimal solution

21
of DET solved at the beginning of horizon (recall by Lemma 1 that, under the optimal DET
solution, the leftover inventory at the beginning of period τ1 in the deterministic system is zero).
It is important to note here that having a positive leftover inventory at the beginning of period
τ1 is likely to happen when total demands in previous periods is smaller than expected whereas
having no leftover inventory is likely to happen when total demands in previous periods is larger
than expected (due to lost sales assumption, it does not matter whether the total realized demands
in previous periods is exactly equal to, or larger than, its expected value, i.e., the actual lost sales
quantity is ignored by the re-optimized deterministic model). Hence, here is an inherent “decision
bias” introduced by re-optimization in our setting: When the total realized demands is smaller than
expected, re-optimization adjusts the solution by decreasing prices; when the total realized demands
is larger than expected, re-optimization does not make any adjustment. We show theoretically below
that this bias may continue to happen beyond the ﬁrst re-optimization time (sometimes almost
surely, sometimes with a high probability), creating the spiraling down movement of prices. Note
that an increase in targeted expected demand implies an increase in realized demand variability,
which further implies an increase in expected lost sales quantity. Hence, the spiraling down of prices
naturally implies the spiraling up in expected lost sales quantities.

A Static Heuristic: Fixed Price Policy
In the previous section, we argued that some popular heuristic policies that directly adopt the
optimal solution of DET may not perform well in our setting. Most interestingly, what initially
appears to be a “good ﬁx” might end up making the problem worse by inducing a spiraling
phenomenon. As discussed, this is driven by the structure of our problem. But it is also because
both SDET and RDET are based on direct implementations of the optimal solution of DET that
essentially ignore lost sales. In this section, we discuss our ﬁrst attempt to ﬁx this issue by proposing
a heuristic policy, which we call Fixed Price (FP) policy. Our discussion of FP will also serve as
a “warmup” for our discussion of our more sophisticated heuristic in the next section. As will be
clear shortly, FP essentially belongs to the class of order-up-to policy, where the order quantity
at each store is computed as the diﬀerence between a store-speciﬁc targeted stock level and the
on-hand inventory level. However, there is a caveat: Under FP, each store will place an order to
the warehouse at the beginning of a batch of periods instead of at the beginning of each period.
We explain the idea behind this batching below.

A simple illustration with T = 6, b = 1, γ = 3, and N = 2. We consider an “ideal” case where (1) we
always order a positive amount of inventory at the beginning of each batch, (2) the projection operator is never
active in any period, and (3) stock-outs never happen at any of the stores or the warehouse.
We make several observations. First, due to demand randomness in period 1, the order quantity
in period 2 deviates from the corresponding deterministic solution by ∆ti. If ∆ti > 0 (meaning
that we order more than the deterministic solution in period 2), ideally, we would want to adjust
our future replenishment(s) by ordering less in future period(s), whereas, if ∆ti < 0, we would
want to adjust our future replenishment(s) by ordering more in future period(s). This is exactly
what JIP does, albeit in a more subtle and sophisticated way. To start, instead of adjusting the
replenishment quantity at each store based on its own demand realization, JIP sums up the total
errors (or deviations) at both stores and then uses this to adjust future replenishment(s) at both
stores. The reason behind this is related to our dynamic pricing scheme (see Remark 4 below and
also the discussion following Theorem 3 in subsection 6.3). Next, it is important to note how this
adjustment is actually made under JIP. Observe that in Table 1, the term δ1 = 1

avoid aggressive correcting, which may result in early stock-out at the warehouse.

8.
Numerical Experiments
In this section, we perform a set of numerical experiments to evaluate the performance of JIP
as well as some other benchmarks. We introduce the simulation setup and benchmark policies
in subsections 8.1 and 8.2, respectively. Our numerical results are presented and discussed in
subsection 8.3. To better calibrate the true optimality gap, instead of using DET as an upper
bound benchmark, we implement a Lagrangian relaxation scheme to compute an upper bound
that is tighter than DET. We denote the Lagrangian upper bound by JLag; the details about the
computation of JLag are presented in appendix EC.7.
8.1.
Simulation Setup
We assume that one period is one week and set T = 52,78,104 weeks. In the main simulation, we
set N = 5,10,20 and mti = M = 20,30,50, i.e., we assume homogeneous market sizes at the stores.

8.2.
Benchmark Policies
We consider the following three benchmark policies to be compared with JIP:
1. Non-averaging JIP (NJIP). This policy is similar to JP with the only diﬀerence being the
price adjustment formula. More precisely, when adjusting the price, we do not average errors
across stores, see the formula in Remark 3 in section 6.
2. Fixed Price (FP). This is the policy discussed in section 5.
3. Simple DET (SDET). This is as described in section 4.
4. Re-optimized DET (RDET). This is as described in section 4.

Table 2 presents a comparison between JIP, NJIP, and FP under diﬀerent market conditions. We
observe that JIP incurs smaller relative losses as market size grows, which conﬁrms the close-

are relatively small, and the actual market sizes are likely to be much larger, which implies that
JIP would achieve close-to-optimal performance for many practical scenarios. As the length of the
selling season increases, we observe that JIP performs better. The performance of JIP is relatively
robust with respect to the number of stores N, and it suﬀers only a small increase as φs grows from

the proﬁt into revenue and cost (including the holding, ordering, and lost sales costs) and compute

the intuition that the linear rate adjustment scheme allows us to carry a smaller amount of safety
stock without hurting revenue by much. The improvement of JIP over NJIP primarily comes from
the revenue side, which is also consistent with our discussions in Remark 3 on the random error
averaging scheme. In Appendix EC.8, we report on our conduction of additional tests to evaluate

Closing Remarks
In this paper, we have studied a joint inventory and pricing problem in the one-warehouse multi-
store setting with lost sales. We found that simple adoptions of the deterministic solution that
are known to perform well in other literature lead to sub-optimal performances in our setting:
The optimality gap of a heuristic control that directly implements the deterministic solution as is
without any modiﬁcation grows linearly in the lost sales cost, and simple re-optimizations of the
deterministic formulation leads to even worse performance. This negative result cautions against a
naive use of simple re-optimization-based heuristics in the joint inventory and pricing problems with
lost sales without ﬁrst understanding the dynamics of the model. We proposed a novel heuristic
that combines several ideas from the inventory and pure pricing literature, and showed that this
heuristic is asymptotically optimal in the setting with a large annual market size.
Our works leave open many interesting research problems. We discuss a few of them. First, it
would be interesting to consider the setting with multiple warehouses and multiple stores. Second,
it would also be interesting to consider a more general setting with warehouse replenishment,
delivery lead time, and (potentially) ﬁxed ordering cost. Third, we have assumed throughout the
paper that the demand distribution is known. In practice, demand parameters are unknown and
need to be actively estimated from the data. It would be interesting to see whether our heuristic
can also be extended to include active demand learning. Fourth, while we focus on the asymptotic
Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3688561

40
setting where the annual market size in each store is large, another interesting and practical relevant
setting is where the number of store N is large. In such setting, we suspect that the gap between
the expected proﬁt under the optimal policy and any deterministic relaxation would scale linearly
in N, which means that deterministic relaxation would not be a good benchmark. Recent advances
in the inventory management literature (e.g., Miao et al. 2020) suggests that Lagrange relaxation
might be a good benchmark for developing near-optimal heuristic controls; exploring such problem
in the joint inventory and pricing setting is left for future research pursuit. Finally, similar to many
existing papers in the pricing literature, we have assumed that the retailer may either increase or
decrease the price. In practice, it is sometimes the case that retailers may restrict price movement in
one direction only (e.g., as in mark-down). Constructing an easily implementable dynamic pricing
heuristic with a restricted price movement is not a trivial task. We leave this for future research.

EC.1.
On the magnitude of lost sales cost
As noted in Section 1, in this paper, we adopt the standard cost-based inventory model with lost
sales. In such model, lost sales cost is typically used as a proxy to capture how undesirable lost
sales is. The magnitude of lost sales cost could depend on a variety of factors including the actual
cost incurred due to stock-out (e.g., expedited shipping cost), the lost of goodwill due to negative
customer perception, and the targeted service level (e.g., to guarantee that the stock-out rate does
not exceed a certain threshold). It is known in many applications that the magnitude of lost sales
cost can be large (e.g., Huh et al. 2009). In the context of retail industry, according to a report
published by Grocery Manufacturers of America (GMA, Gruen et al. 2002), the average stock-out
rate in the US is about 8%—this number is often used as a competitive benchmark for stock-
out rate in the retail industry. Indeed, in some cases, and also from our limited interaction with
practitioners, an 8% stock-out rate is considered not good enough and some retailers are targeting
much smaller stock-out rates in the range of 2%-5% (see, e.g., Lokad 2014, The Wall Street Journal
2015, JRJ.com 2020).
To illustrate the magnitude of lost sales costs that correspond to the above range of stock-out
rates, consider a simple single-period Newsvendor model with per unit price p, per unit ordering
cost c, per unit per period holding cost h, and per unit lost sales cost φ. For the sake of clarity,

EC.7.
Computation of the Lagrangian Upper Bound JLag
We relax the warehouse capacity constraint (1c) in OPT and, with a slight abuse of notation,
let λ > 0 denote its Lagrangian multiplier. Because we assume ˆhw = 0 throughout the simulation,
constraint (1c) is redundant as there is no need to track the inventory at the warehouse. We refer
to the optimal objective function value of the relaxed problem as V (λ). We make two further

EC.9.
On Using Price Along to Hedge Against Lost Sales
To better understand the interplay between pricing and replenishment decisions in our setting,
we implemented two versions of pricing-only (PO) heuristic where we decrease the demand by
increasing the price (as compared to the optimal solution to the deterministic relaxation) to hedge
against the lost sales risk, termed PO1 and PO2 respectively. In both heuristics, the increase of
price is treated as a tuning parameter that is chosen based on the simulation results. What makes
these two heuristics diﬀerent is the way replenishment decisions are made: In PO1, the order
quantity is set to be the same as the expected demand; in PO2, the ordering quantity equals the
optimal solution to the deterministic relaxation. Intuitively, SDET can be viewed as a special case
of both heuristics. The performance of both heuristics, as well as the performance of RDET and
SDET, is summarized in Table EC.2. We observe that PO1 delivers slightly better performance
than SDET and outperforms RDET substantially. PO2 improves signﬁcantly over PO1, but is still
considerably worse than both FP and JIP. The above observations underscore the importance of
holding safety stocks in the considered joint inventory and pricing control problem.
