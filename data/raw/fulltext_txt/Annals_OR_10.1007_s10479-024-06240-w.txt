Abstract
This paper studies the evolution of an oligopoly market where two types of companies, brown
and green, are present. Green п¬Ғrms adopt a less polluting technology that allows a reduction
in emissions. We want to investigate the possibility of an environmental-friendly transition
where insurance can give its support to cover the (endogenous) climate change loss. The
model is composed of two parts. We analyze a two-stages game in which the companies
maximize their proп¬Ғts by choosing output in the п¬Ғrst stage and insurance coverage in the
second one. Then we develop an evolutionary game to endogenize the selection of being
brown or green, according to the expected random proп¬Ғts. We derive analytically the dynamic
regimes may arise and we perform a sensitivity analysis at the stable inner steady state, where
п¬Ғrms coexist, changing the main key parameters to understand which ones may be strategic
for an ecological transition.
Keywords Climate change В· Coinsurance rate В· Environmental loss В· Evolutionary
dynamics В· Mixed oligopoly market
1 Introduction
Climate change is one of the main issues that society will have to face in the coming years to
reduce its impact at the ecosystem level. The transition to green needs to have the awareness
of polluting, but it also depends on the possibility of obtaining business proп¬Ғtability. Financial
risk analysis considerably inп¬Ӯuences the availability of resources for companies that want to
implement and maintain high performance together with low-emission business strategies.
The costs for a green transition are moreover demanding to bear. Is it possible, therefore, to
п¬Ғnd market strategies in which the agents behave environmental proactive if properly driven
by involved parties? Could the insurance sector help the change to green?

according to Stern (2006), climate change is driven by п¬ҒrmsвҖҷ emission levels. Grasso (2020)

We will present a game in which there are two types of п¬Ғrms, brown and green, operating
in an oligopoly market where an insurance sector provides coverage for environmental dam-
age potentially resulting from their business. Firms maximize their random proп¬Ғt by taking
into account the costs of production activities, the insurance coverage, and the endogenous
expected loss caused by their business. The strategical choice of being brown or green fol-
lows an evolutionary setting in which all companies are Nash players (see Sect.4 for further
details on evolutionary mechanism). Differently from Seog (2006), where the preferences
are in mean-variance, we adopt the expected utility paradigm that assumes an utility function
strictly concave in the insurance coverage. This assumption allows us to better investigate the
behavior of risk-averse п¬Ғrms. Moreover, this frameworks is suitable to study the effects of a
loss distribution with two states of natures (the loss occurs, the loss doesnвҖҷt occur; widely used
in literature, see, among others, the seminal work by Ehrlich and Becker 1972). The strictly
concave utility function gives us the possibility to study how п¬Ғrms select the optimal insur-
ance coverage, while in Boyer and Laffont (1997), MГјller-FГјrstenberger and Schumacher
(2015) and Mol et al. (2020) the coinsurance rate is п¬Ғxed. Respect to the last one, we do not
consider the coverage as mandatory since п¬Ғrms cannot be insured in the presence of too high
insurance premium. Finally, this paper differs from Colivicchi and Iannucci (2023) as we
assume that all п¬Ғrms are risk averse and we generalize the dynamics to an oligopoly in order
to include the evolutionary pressure, namely the behavior of п¬Ғrms at changes of the number
of companies on the market. When the dynamics presents a mixed composition of brown and
green п¬Ғrms, we perform a sensitivity analysis on some key parameters that can be strategical
to favor an ecological transition encouraged by the presence of external policy instruments.
Remarks and highlights will be proposed. All proofs are contained in the Appendix.

6 Conclusions
We have studied the dynamics of an industry composed of two types of п¬Ғrms, brown (more
polluting) and green (less polluting). The model is composed of two parts: a two-stages game
in which п¬Ғrms choose in the п¬Ғrst stage the output while in the second the insurance coverage,
and a dynamic game in which п¬Ғrms select the strategy to adopt. The relation between output
market and insurance market is crucial. A п¬Ғrst result of the model is that the type who produce
more (higher operative proп¬Ғts) has a lower insurance coverage. This implies that if, as in the
123

660
Annals of Operations Research (2026) 356:651вҖ“665
real world, the brown п¬Ғrms produce more, then the insurance coverage can help the green
п¬Ғrms to survive on the market and share the risk.
To better understand the evolution of the industry, when both types of п¬Ғrms coexist, we
perform numerical simulations changing some key variables, as the competitive pressure,
the output market reservation price, and both production costs. A green transition (in the
sense that the number of brown п¬Ғrms decreases) is possible increasing competition (reducing
barriers), output market reservation price (favoring the electriп¬Ғcation process), and costs of
brown technology (taxing polluting plants). On the contrary, an increase of the costs of green
technology (lower incentives to environmental friendly investments) does not favor a green
transition.
In conclusion, the model suggests a fundamental role in the coming years for the insur-
ance market, which should be ready to accept the challenge of creating new policies to
support climate risks where the companyвҖҷs behavior compliant with sustainable develop-
ment is rewarded. Moreover, if all system contributes to the reduction of environmental risk
damage, it would lead to a decrease in the overall unexpected loss, that is a very appreciated
condition from an ecological point of view. As long as the emissions can be carried out
indiscriminately, there is, therefore, no economic advantage for a full green transition. The
model shows that green behavior can be favored through policy instruments and an active
insurance sector.
