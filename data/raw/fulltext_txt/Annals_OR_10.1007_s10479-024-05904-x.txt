Abstract
This paper values Ô¨Åxed-income (discrete- and continuous-time) European Asian and Aus-
tralian options. We assume that the term structure of interest rates is modelled by the
speciÔ¨Åcation proposed in Moreno et al. (Econ Model 72:140‚Äì150, 2018, https://doi.org/
10.1016/j.econmod.2018.01.015). We obtain closed-form expressions for the premiums of
geometric average options and, for arithmetic average options, premiums are computed by
numerical methods. We also perform a sensitivity analysis with respect to different parameters
for both (geometric and arithmetic) options.

1 Introduction
This paper prices and performs a sensitivity analysis of average call options on zero-coupon
bonds and Ô¨Åxed strike1 assuming that the term structure of interest rates (TSIR) is given by
the model proposed in Moreno et al. (2018) (Moreno et al., 2018, from now on).2
In more detail, we value Asian and Australian options. The pay-off of Asian options
depends on the average of an underlying asset price over some predetermined time interval
and the pay-off of Australian options depends on the ratio of this average asset price and the
underlying asset price at option maturity. We assume that the average price can be geometric
or arithmetic, either in discrete- or continuous-time.
These options constitute an important family of derivatives with many applications. More-
over, there are some advantages in trading Asian options as, for instance, (a) their pay-off
is less volatile as it depends on an average price instead of a terminal one, implying that
Asian options are cheaper than (equivalent) European options and (b) it is more difÔ¨Åcult to
manipulate an average price than a Ô¨Ånal one. As a result, these options can be an adequate
hedging instrument for traders who act continuously over Ô¨Ånite periods.
Since it can be difÔ¨Åcult (or even impossible) to Ô¨Ånd the probability distribution of the
average price of an underlying asset, obtaining an analytical pricing expression for these
options can be a challenging task. The general conclusion of the academic literature is that
geometric Asian options can be valued analytically under certain assumptions while pricing
arithmetic Asian options requires numerical methods as, for instance, Monte Carlo simula-
tions, numerical integration, or approximations of the true distribution of the average price.
Some examples of Asian stock options pricing departing from Black‚ÄìScholes assumptions
can be found in Kemna and Vorst (1990), Turnbull and Wakeman (1991), Geman and Yor
(1993), Iwaki et al. (1993), Ritchken et al. (1993), Rogers and Shi (1995), Boyle et al. (1997),
Linetsky (2004), Sun et al. (2013), Cai et al. (2015), and Cui et al. (2018). In practice, these
assumptions are rarely satisÔ¨Åed, specially the constant volatility hypothesis. Then, stochastic
volatility models have been proposed to value Asian options. Some examples are Wong and
Cheung (2004), Hubalek and Sgarra (2011), Kim and Wee (2014), Shi and Yang (2014),
Hubalek et al. (2017), Brignone et al. (2023), and Ewald et al. (2023).
MorenoandNavas(2008)introducedAustralianoptions,thatgeneralizeVariablePurchase
Options (VPOs), presented and priced in Handley (2000). VPOs give the right to buy a
stochastic number of shares that depends on the shares price at option‚Äôs maturity. Standard
VPOs are designed to Ô¨Ånish ‚Äúin-the-money‚Äù but caps and Ô¨Çoors on the number of shares can
be included.
The underlying asset of Australian options is a ratio that can be, alternatively, (a) the
underlying asset price at option‚Äôs maturity divided by the average asset price during the
option‚Äôs life or (b) the inverse of the previous ratio. Assuming that the asset price follows a
geometric Brownian motions, Moreno and Navas (2008) provided analytical ‚ÄúBlack‚ÄìScholes
type‚Äù expressions for the geometric case while, in the arithmetic case, they applied different
numerical pricing techniques.
The continuous-time models that have been proposed to analyze the TSIR can be classiÔ¨Åed
in endogenous and exogenous. Their main features are the following:

‚Ä¢ Endogenous models assume that one or more state variables drive the TSIR and, typically,
they provide a reasonable analytical tractability and an easy numerical implementation.
However, they do not take into account the information embedded in observed interest
rates, they do not achieve a good Ô¨Åt to the observed term structure, and their determination
of the market risk premium can lead to some arbitrage opportunities. Examples of one-
factor models are those proposed in Vasicek (1977) and Cox et al. (1985).
‚Ä¢ Exogenous models take the TSIR as given and try to achieve a perfect Ô¨Åt to observed
interest rates. In contrast to the previous models, they do not need to make any assumption
on the market risk premium but, in general, it is difÔ¨Åcult to obtain an analytical derivatives
pricing and their practical implementation is computationally demanding as, in many
cases, they deal with non-Markovian processes. For instance, Hull and White (1990) and
Heath et al. (1992) propose two very well-known exogenous models.
In this paper we use the one-factor endogenous model presented in Moreno et al. (2018)
that nests the Vasicek (1977) model. These authors propose a Ô¨Çexible yet tractable model
assuming that the instantaneous risk-free interest rate converges to a certain long-term value
(mean-reversion level) that is given by a Fourier series and, then, this value displays a cycli-
cal behaviour. In this way, this model illustrates the possible links between interest rates and
the business cycle.3 Under this functional form, interest rates follow a Gaussian distribution
and, then, the price of a zero-coupon bond follows a lognormal distribution. They also pro-
vided a closed-form pricing expression for any Ô¨Åxed-income derivative and showed that a
great Ô¨Çexibility in interest rates is achieved, allowing for a better empirical behaviour while
maintaining its analytical tractability.
Under this model, we will provide analytical expressions for the premiums of geomet-
ric (discrete- or continuous-time) Asian and Australian calls and we will compare these
results with those obtained for the particular case that was mentioned, Vasicek (1977). These
expressions will be of ‚ÄúBlack‚ÄìScholes type‚Äù as the geometric average of zero-coupon bond
prices also follows a lognormal distribution. Later, we will apply numerical methods to value
arithmetic Asian and Australian calls.
This paper is organized as follows. Section2 introduces the TSIR model proposed in
Moreno et al. (2018) and some key pricing results that will be employed later. Section3
describes the main features of the derivatives that we will value and some relationships
between them. Sections4 and 5 provide closed-form expressions for the premiums of, respec-
tively, discrete- and continuous-time geometric Asian and Australian options for both the
Moreno et al. (2018) and Vasicek (1977) models. Section6 performs a sensitivity analysis
by studying the effect of changes in the different parameters on the premiums of geometric
calls, prices numerically arithmetic options, and discusses the main Ô¨Åndings. Finally, Sect.7
summarizes our main conclusions and proposes some lines of further research. Some statisti-
cal results about the multivariate Gaussian distribution and the proofs of several Propositions
are deferred to a Ô¨Ånal ‚ÄúTechnical Appendix‚Äù.

6 Sensitivity analysis
This Section analyzes the effect of changes in the different parameters on the premiums of
Asian and Australian calls. In more detail, we illustrate numerically and graphically the ana-
lytical results obtained for geometric calls, provide a numerical illustration of the premiums
for arithmetic calls, and discuss the main Ô¨Åndings. For both the Moreno et al. (2018) and
Vasicek (1977) models, we consider different values for the parameters that affect the option
premiums and several numbers of monitoring dates n. The case n = 1 (resp., n = ‚àû) corre-
sponds to standard European (resp., continuous-time) calls. We will see that the sensitivity
analysis is qualitatively the same for any value of n, including the inÔ¨Ånite case.

(ii) Depending on the initial parameters, P(rt, t, T ) may decrease and this would lead
to a lower option premium.
As a consequence, the premium of Asian calls can increase or decrease with the option
maturity depending on the relative importance of both effects. Figure5 shows that these
effects are more accentuated in the Moreno et al. (2018) model. This is a straight conse-
quence of the cyclical form of the mean-reversion level, a feature that is not incorporated
in Vasicek (1977). In more detail, the cyclical behaviour of interest rates is inherited by
the prices of zero-coupon bond affecting then the discount factor applied to value these
options and the average bond price, underlying of these options. To isolate and better
illustrate the difference between both models, we Ô¨Åx the common parameters (market
price of risk, speed of mean-reversion, constant term in the level of mean-reversion, dif-
fusion coefÔ¨Åcient, and initial interest rate) and we set the parameters of the Fourier series
such that the cyclical behaviour is emphasized.
‚Ä¢ Finally, a higher initial instantaneous interest rate implies a lower premium of Asian
calls because the discount factor, the zero-coupon bond price, and the average bond price
reach lower values.

6.3 Arithmetic Asian and Australian calls
To obtain the analytical pricing formula for arithmetic Asian options, we should know the
distributions of the weighted arithmetic average bond prices in discrete- and continuous-
time (see (9)‚Äì(10)). As these distributions are unknown, we will price arithmetic options by
applying numerical methods, namely, Monte Carlo simulations. Moreover, the distribution
of the continuous-time average is approximated by that of the discrete-time average with a
very high value of monitoring dates n.
Table 3 includes the premiums of arithmetic Asian calls using the Moreno et al. (2018) and
Vasicek (1977) models. We can see that, under the same conditions, the arithmetic premium
is higher than the geometric premium, see Table 1. The reason is that the geometric average
is a lower bound of the arithmetic average and, as we are dealing with calls, the higher the
underlying value, the higher the option premium.

calls, we can not value analytically arithmetic Australian calls. Then, these options will be
also valued numerically by applying Monte Carlo simulations. Recall that, by Proposition 3.2,
the arithmetic-discrete Australian call on the ratio A(n)/Pn can be interpreted as Asian option
but this is not the case for the Australian call on the ratio Pn/A(n).

7 Conclusions
We have priced Asian and Australian calls on zero-coupon bonds assuming that the time
evolution of interest rate is given by the model proposed in Moreno et al. (2018). This model
generalizes that proposed in Vasicek (1977), introduces a large Ô¨Çexibility in interest rates,
and may allow for a better empirical behaviour while maintaining the analytical tractability.
These authors achieve this assuming that the long-term value of interest rates is given by a
Fourier series. For both models, we have obtained analytical expressions of ‚ÄúBlack‚ÄìScholes
type‚Äù for the premium of geometric calls while arithmetic calls have been valued by Monte
Carlo simulations.
In more detail, for Asian calls, we have seen that, for the Moreno et al. (2018) model, the
premium does not depend monotonically on the speed of mean-reversion while, in the Vasicek
(1977) model, a higher speed may imply a lower (higher) premium if the mean-reversion
level is higher (lower) than the initial interest rate. In both models, the premium decreases
with both the constant term in the mean-reversion level and the initial instantaneous interest
rate, increases with the diffusion coefÔ¨Åcient, and can increase or decrease with the option
maturity.
Finally, under the Moreno et al. (2018) model, we have shown that the effect of changes in
the different parameters on Australian call premiums is very different from that in Asian calls.
A higher constant term of the mean-reversion level or a higher initial instantaneous interest
rate decrease the zero-coupon bond price and, then, its average price but‚Äîdepending on the
relative change in both numerator and denominator in the underlying ratio‚Äîthe premium
of these options can increase or decrease. Also, the Australian call premium can be a non-
monotonic function of either the constant term in the diffusion coefÔ¨Åcient, the speed of
mean-reversion, or the option maturity.
A possible future research line could be to provide closed-form expressions for option
sensitivities (Greeks) as well as their numerical intuitions and interpretations. In practice,
market makers in OTC markets would be interested in these Greeks to manage the risk of
their positions against parallel shifts in the yield curve or changes in monetary policies.
