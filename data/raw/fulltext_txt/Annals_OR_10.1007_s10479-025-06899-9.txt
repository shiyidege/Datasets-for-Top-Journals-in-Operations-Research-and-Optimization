Abstract
This study investigates how the well-documented link between economic activity and 
carbon dioxide emissions, identified in previous research in energy and environmental 
economics, can be used to improve nowcasting carbon dioxide emissions in a data-rich 
environment. We compare classic and recent improvements in dynamic factor models with 
linear and nonlinear machine learning algorithms that have been shown to be effective in 
previous research. These machine learning algorithms are implemented in the mixed data 
sampling framework. The recent improvements in dynamic factor models include the use 
of nondifferenced data, which has increased prediction accuracy, especially during eco­
nomically volatile periods. Additionally, there are structurally augmented dynamic factor 
models, which combine machine learning methods with dynamic factor models. For the 
structurally augmented models, we use machine learning algorithms to select the most 
important variables, which then augment the dynamic factor models. Our findings indicate 
that dynamic factor models outperform alternative approaches for carbon dioxide emis­
sion nowcasting. Specifically, models based on nondifferenced data demonstrate superior 
predictive ability with principal component extraction, whereas models using differenced 
data yield better results with Kalman filter extraction. These findings are essential for 
developing effective nowcasting models that enable timely emission assessments, which 
are critical for advancing ambitious climate policies. Our research, therefore, contributes 
to the ongoing discourse on the challenges of sustainable development by employing 
econometric models for the dynamic links between economic activities and environmental 
outcomes.

1  Introduction
The latest report from the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) warns that we are far 
from achieving the climate goals of the December 2015 Paris Agreement. According to the 
report, carbon dioxide is building up in the atmosphere at a rate unprecedented in human 
history (WMO, 2024). The urgent need for policies targeting the reduction in carbon dioxide 
emissions has already become increasingly apparent in light of the current global climate 
crisis (Nordic Council of Ministers, 2022). Furthermore, the COVID-19 outbreak in 2019 
made it clear that unexpected and severe events also have consequences for emission data 
since economic activities, including transportation and industrial production activities, are 
strongly related to carbon dioxide emissions (Stern, 2017). Therefore, substantial changes in 
production and economic activities also lead to equally substantial changes in carbon diox­
ide emissions. This situation further challenges policymakers and other decision makers in 
implementing carbon dioxide reduction policies since the conventional approach relies on 
time-lagged emission data.
In macroeconomics, the most common approach when basing economic policy on time-
lagged data is to nowcast (i.e. forecast the presence of a particular variable). Therefore, 
there is a rich literature on how to nowcast macroeconomic data (inter alia, Bańbura et al., 
2013; Bok et al., 2018; Goulet Coulombe et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2025). Compared to studies 
on nowcasting macroeconomic indicators, previous research on nowcasting environmental 
data, such as carbon dioxide emissions using econometric methods, is scarcer. However, 
there has recently been increased interest in providing timely information regarding the 
status of carbon dioxide emissions for the purpose of enacting prompt policy measures 
(Nordic Council of Ministers, 2022). A few papers on nowcasting carbon dioxide emissions 
have been published recently. For example, Bennedsen et al. (2021) forecasted carbon diox­
ide emissions in the US using a new dynamic factor model (DFM), wherein they initially 
identified the most important variables through the least absolute shrinkage and selection 
operator (LASSO). Then, they augmented the DFM by including these variables directly in 
the nowcasting equation. Moreover, Jensen (2021) applied machine learning techniques and 
found that neural networks work well for nowcasting US carbon dioxide emissions. Even 
more recently, Fosten and Nandi (2023) nowcasted state-level carbon dioxide emissions 
in the US using panel data methods. This body of literature emphasizes the importance of 
anthropogenic variables in explaining—and, therefore, nowcasting—emissions.
Against this backdrop, the purpose of our paper is to nowcast carbon dioxide emissions1 
using a dataset comprising various measures of economic activity, as well as electricity con­
sumption dataset2, to find a quick and more precise estimate of the current emission status.

There are several sources of emissions data. The Global Carbon Project calculates annual 
emissions from fossil fuels and cement production, and the World Bank provides data on 
carbon dioxide emissions in metric tons per capita, sourced from Climate Watch’s histori­
cal GHG Emission database. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) releases 
carbon dioxide emissions across states. Statewide carbon dioxide emissions include those 
produced from direct fuel consumption across all sectors—residential, commercial, indus­
trial, and transportation—as well as emissions from primary fuels used for electricity gen­
eration. The International Energy Agency (IEA) constructs estimates of energy demand and 
energy-related carbon dioxide emissions. These data are typically measured annually, which 
conforms with econometric analysis in a panel setting within a country. Unlike most of the 
previous studies, the present study uses quarterly carbon dioxide emissions data for Sweden, 
retrieved from DataStream. Quarterly data have started to be collected in Sweden in order 
to obtain more timely statistics for policymakers. Even though this time lag has decreased, 
it is still 5 months in Sweden and longer in neighbouring Nordic countries. Therefore, now­
casting still has an important role to play in further decreasing publication delays (Nordic 
Council of Ministers, 2022).
Nowcasting, which provides quick estimates of current emission levels, offers a useful 
tool for proactive monitoring and adaptive policy adjustments. Specifically, there may be 
two pathways for how timely data on emissions might enter decision-making frameworks. 
First, nowcasting enables continuous tracking of emissions within major sectors regulated 
under the Effort Sharing Regulation (ESR),3 where ambitious reduction targets have been 
set for Sweden (e.g., 63% lower than 1990 by 2030). By providing quick emission data, 
policymakers can identify potential shortfalls early and implement targeted interventions. 
For example, a timely monitoring of electricity consumption trends in different sectors and 
industries,4 coupled with our model’s nowcasts, could provide early warning signals if 
emissions from the energy sector are projected to deviate from the desired trajectory. This 
would allow policymakers to implement targeted interventions, such as adjusting incentives 
for renewable energy adoption. Therefore, nowcasting facilitates a feedback loop where 
regulators can observe how emissions respond to policy changes (e.g., transport regulations) 
and refine strategies accordingly.5 Second, government agencies, such as the Climate Policy

Our results show that DFMs have superior nowcasting ability compared to the machine 
learning methods. Models based on nondifferenced data demonstrate superior predictive 
ability with principal component extraction, whereas differenced data yield better results 
with Kalman filter extraction. The superiority of nondifferencing for PC extraction is consis­
tent with Choi and Jeong’s (2020) results. Nondifferencing did not work for KF extraction; 
this is potentially because it makes finding the correct model more challenging. KF extrac­
tion is more dependent on finding the correct model specification than PC extraction is see 
Poncela et al. (2021). Among the machine learning methods, random forests are usually the 
best choice, with the mean square error (MSE) comparable to the optimal DFM methods. 
However, as shown in the results for the model confidence set, the PC method is consistently 
superior for nondifferenced data, whereas KF with an MA component is preferred for dif­
ferenced data.
On the basis of these results, we can conclude that it is possible to use measures of eco­
nomic activity and energy consumption to precisely nowcast carbon dioxide emissions. Our 
approach also suggests that it is possible for policymakers to receive estimates of carbon 
dioxide emissions 3 months in advance of the release of official statistics because the carbon 
dioxide emission data are published with a 5-month delay. Using econometric models to 
nowcast carbon dioxide emissions can help policymakers when deciding on which poli­
cies to enact to reduce carbon dioxide emissions. By employing econometric models for 
nowcasting, our research contributes to the ongoing discourse on the challenges of sustain­
able development by modeling the dynamic links between economic activities and envi­
ronmental outcomes, which can inform policy decisions aimed at reducing carbon dioxide 
emissions.

2.2  Macroeconomic and electricity consumption variables
Since the seminal studies by Grossman and Krueger (1991, 1995), there has been a substan­
tial body of international research focused on examining the output-carbon emissions nex­
us.9 These studies (e.g. Brown & McDonough, 2016; Sugiawan & Managi, 2016) focus on 
the nature and relationship between economic growth and environmental quality. This rela­
tionship can be explained by the following factors.10 One factor involves the income elas­
ticity of environmental quality demand, which indicates that after a certain level of income 
is achieved, a further increase in income leads to a greater willingness to pay for a clean 
environment. The other factor encompasses scale, technological, and composition effects. 
This includes both the negative and positive effects of economic growth. The negative effect 
states that increasing output requires a larger amount of resources, thereby generating more 
waste and by-products, and the positive effect refers to structural changes in the economy 
that may lead to a shift from energy-intensive industries to service- and knowledge-inten­
sive industries. The technological progress that accompanies the achievement of a certain 
level of economic development enables this transition, which is referred to as the technique 
effect of economic growth. Another positive effect of economic growth within the industry 
presented in Rubio et al. (2009) is that if the new technologies are efficient enough, the 
economy can evolve along a balanced growth path with increasing environmental quality. 
The third factor is the international trade factor. Similar to the second explanation provided 
above, the international trade factor also involves two contradictory effects on the envi­
ronment. An increase in trade volume deteriorates environmental quality, as suggested by 
the scale effect. However, comparative advantage, which determines patterns of trade, may 
lead to the relocation of energy-intensive production activities from industrialized econo­
mies to developing economies, where environmental regulations are relatively lax (known 
as the displacement hypothesis or pollution haven hypothesis). With respect to requiring 
resources for production and consumption, another strand of literature focuses on the cau­
sality between electricity consumption and growth.11 This stream of literature also overlaps 
with the literature on renewable energy, the environment, and sustainable development. For 
example, one of the most recent studies by Yuan et al. (2022) explores the role of renewable 
energy in reducing greenhouse gas emissions.
Reflecting the above-mentioned previous literature, the selection of macroeconomic 
variables to nowcast carbon dioxide emissions is based on previous studies. Bańbura and 
Saiz (2020) develop further nowcasting models of the Euro Area, Ankargren and Lindholm

of the KF process, the exact diffuse initialization formulas are used until P∞,t becomes a 
zero matrix, after which the usual KF equations are used.

3.4  MIDAS models
One issue when nowcasting is the frequency misalignment between carbon dioxide emis­
sions, which are released quarterly, and predictors, which are often released monthly. Ben­
nedsen et al. (2021) addressed this frequency misalignment by temporally aggregating the 
data to a common low frequency (in our case, quarterly frequency), whereas Fosten and 
Nandi (2023) resolved it by using MIDAS models. We explore both solutions. MIDAS has 
been widely used since it was introduced in the seminal papers by Ghysels et al. (2004, 
2007). However, temporally disaggregating data can lead to the overparameterization of the 
model, which decreases its predictive performance. Therefore, MIDAS modeling initially 
relies on estimating distributed lag models with parameter restrictions, primarily utilizing 
nonlinear least squares. In cases where the degree of frequency misalignment is not large, 
Foroni et al. (2015) introduced an unrestricted MIDAS (U-MIDAS) model without param­
eter restrictions. We use the U-MIDAS model because carbon dioxide emissions are mea­
sured quarterly, whereas the indicators are measured monthly. As benchmarks, we consider 
least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO), adaptive LASSO (ALASSO), and 
random forests. These are sparse machine learning methods that have been shown to work 
well in a U-MIDAS context for quarterly/monthly data, as Uematsu and Tanaka (2019) 
and Medeiros et al. (2021) have demonstrated. We also consider a MIDAS version of the 
SA-DFM model introduced by Bennedsen et al. (2021). In this method, the most important 
variables are selected by ALASSO, and then they augment the temporally aggregated DFM 
models. It should be noted that, because of data limitations, it is challenging to temporally 
disaggregate the DFM model completely because many of the main predictors (e.g., GDP) 
are measured on a quarterly basis (see the data summary in the Appendix). Thus, disag­
gregation for the DFM model would result in too few variables in the different sets of tem­
porally aggregated and disaggregated data, respectively. According to Bai and Ng (2008), 
this leads to uncertainty and poor predictive performance in the DFM models. Therefore, 
we choose to use a MIDAS version of the approach introduced by Bennedsen et al. (2021), 
in which only the most important variables are disaggregated to augment the DFM model, 
as explained in Sect. 3.4.3.

4  Nowcasting
4.1  Nowcasting set-up
We perform pseudo-out-of-sample experiments for nowcasting quarterly carbon dioxide 
emissions in Sweden. To nowcast carbon dioxide emissions, we use Eq. (1), extracting 
factors using the PC and KF methods, respectively. According to the Nordic Council of 
Ministers (2022), the publication delay of carbon dioxide emission is 5 months, whereas 
the monthly predictors used to nowcast the carbon dioxide emission have a maximum 
delay of 2 months, according to information from Statistics Sweden. Therefore, through 
this nowcasting exercise, it is possible to estimate carbon dioxide emissions one quarter in 
advance of the release of official statistics. The data are collected from 1981M01/1981Q1 
to 2021M12/2021Q4, resulting in 164 quarters for the low-frequency variables. Thus, the 
number of variables with a high frequency is 492. We use 34 years, or equivalently, 136 
quarters of the data for model fitting and nowcasting the carbon dioxide emissions. We 
evaluate the nowcasting performance from 2015Q1 to 2021Q4. Huber et al. (2023) and 
Ankargren and Lindholm (2021), we include a subsample in which we focus on the post-
pandemic period, which also includes the war in Ukraine, to investigate performance dur­
ing economic crises. The nowcast evaluation of this period starts in 2020Q1 and ends in 
2021Q4 (this period is denoted as Covid). We include a third period, which is the time prior 
to the pandemic (denoted as non-Covid). Finally, we incorporate a fourth period, post-2017, 
which includes the period after Sweden established a comprehensive climate policy frame­
work. We use rolling window strategies for model fitting.

vector regressions work promisingly and stably across all periods and is often the optimal 
machine learning method for q–q cases. However, as shown in the results for the MCS in 
Table 7, the PC method is consistently superior for nondifferenced data, and KF with an 
MA component is preferred for differenced data. Therefore, it is these two methods that we 
recommend. PC is slightly more robust to different model specifications, whereas KF with 
an MA component based on y–y differenced data exhibits a somewhat smaller MSE. Nev­
ertheless, the differences in terms of MSE between PC using nondifferenced data and MA 
using differenced data are negligible.
Previous research on energy and environmental economics has shown a strong connec­
tion between economic activity and emissions [see reviews by Dinda (2004, 2005, 2018) 
and Stern (2017)]. Our results show that it is important to use the tight link between eco­
nomic activity and carbon dioxide emissions, as identified in environmental and energy 
economics, to accurately nowcast carbon dioxide emissions. The use of econometric models 
grounded in economic theory presents a valid alternative to the currently used nonecono­
metric integrated assessment models (IAMs) (Nordhaus & Sztorc, 2013).
4.3  Factor scores and variable importance
To open the black box, we choose to use two of the best-performing methods for the full 
sample. This serves as an illustration of how these models can be interpreted and, therefore, 
used to draw policy implications. Hence, we focus on the nondifferenced PC method and the 
RF approach using y–y differencing. The factor extraction of the PC approach is available in 
Fig. 2 and the variable importance is presented in Fig. 3.
As shown in Fig. 2, an analysis of the DFM model with PC extraction reveals that trade 
and consumption-related variables are consistently important when forming factors used to 
nowcast Swedish emission dynamics, although their specific roles vary. Factors 1, 2, and

3). The coefficient estimates of these factors shown in Table 8 are negative, indicating that 
Sweden’s carbon emissions have decreased, while exports from Sweden have increased. 
These findings are in line with Sweden having achieved a notable decoupling of economic 
growth from carbon dioxide emissions, reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 38% between

and vehicles, even with cleaner production methods, still contribute to domestic emissions

often overshadows the mitigating effects of service exports, resulting in a net positive effect 
on emissions. Increases in inventories also correlate with higher emissions. This reflects 
increased production levels that have not yet translated into consumption or exports, driving 
up factory and industrial process activity and, consequently, emissions. This is especially 
true in goods-producing sectors. The relationship between inventory buildup and emissions 
reinforces the dynamic observed in the analysis of Sweden’s goods-dominated exports and 
their environmental impacts.

Figure 3 displays the variable importance ranking obtained from a random forest model, 
highlighting the relative influence of each feature on the model’s predictive performance. 
The features are arranged in descending order, with the most influential variables at the top. 
This ranking aligns well with the results from the previous two methods of assessing vari­
able importance.
At the top of the list, services exports at constant prices (Svc.Exp.Con) and real GDP 
(GDP.Con) emerge as the most critical features, indicating their strong impact on the mod­
el’s predictions. Following these, electricity consumption in transport (El.Conn.Transport) 
and goods and services imports at constant prices (G&S.Imp.Con) also show considerable 
importance. Features such as car registration (CarReg), goods and services exports at con­
stant prices (G&S.Exp.Con), and MSCI Nordic appear further down the list, yet still con­
tribute noticeably to the model’s performance. Toward the bottom, features such as OMX, 
U.S. industrial production (USIndProd_MPG), and electricity consumption in manufactur­
ing (El.Cons.MFG) show lower importance.
These analyses inform preemptive and strategic policy design. First, the model can alert 
policymakers to unexpected increases in electricity use within energy-intensive sectors 
(e.g., transportation) before official emission statistics are available. Given that correlations 
with emissions vary significantly across sectors (as shown in Fig. 3), regulatory attention 
should focus on sector-specific patterns rather than aggregated electricity consumption. Sec­
ond, effective policy must recognize the decoupling of emissions from economic growth, 
acknowledging both deliberate interventions (e.g., carbon taxes) and structural economic 
shifts toward a less carbon-intensive, service-oriented economy. Macroeconomic indicators, 
such as GDP or exports, require careful interpretation, as their relationship with emissions is 
not consistently positive; increased service exports may signal continued decoupling, while 
industrial growth could indicate higher emissions. Therefore, policies should carefully con­
sider sectoral composition rather than indiscriminately favouring all forms of economic 
expansion. These nowcasting insights are strategic, enabling policymakers to identify sub­
sectors requiring immediate intervention, aligning with recommendations for adaptive cli­
mate policy (Swedish Climate Policy Council, 2025).
5  Conclusions and policy implications
This study suggests improved methods for nowcasting carbon dioxide emissions, which are 
among the most significant drivers of climate change and have adverse effects on the envi­
ronment, on the basis of recently developed DFM models and machine learning algorithms. 
Our results show that models based on nondifferenced data have superior predictive ability 
compared with those based on differenced data when PC extraction is used, whereas dif­
ferencing the data is superior for KF extraction. Our results for PC extraction align closely 
with the findings of Choi and Jeong (2020), who found that nondifferencing is superior to 
differencing when forecasting macroeconomic data, especially during economic crises. PC 
methods using nondifferenced data also yield slightly more robust results to different model 
specifications compared with KF extraction using MA filtering based on y–y differenced 
data. However, the latter method has a slightly smaller MSE than PC methods using nondif­
ferenced data, and the differences between the methods are negligible. Among the machine 
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
learning methods, random forests are usually the best choice, with MSE comparable to 
DFM methods.
These results are important for finding good nowcasting models, which are needed for 
a prompt assessment of emission status. This assessment is critical since ongoing climate 
change requires even more ambitious and active measures to be taken. Such policy mea­
sures to meet climate goals are more closely associated with economic policies than are 
earlier measures (Nordic Council of Ministers, 2022).
In addition, nowcasting emissions in a timely manner is essential for nowcasting GDP, 
which includes the environmental effects of production activities that, in turn, affect the 
environment. The Environment Accounts at the Official Statistics of Sweden publishes 
greenhouse gas emissions data on a quarterly basis with at least a 5-month lag. The method 
suggested in this paper indicates that this delay can be further shortened. Therefore, our 
research contributes to the ongoing discussions about the challenges of sustainable develop­
ment by utilizing econometric models for nowcasting emissions. Through an examination 
of the dynamic connections between economic activities and environmental outcomes, our 
research provides insights that can guide policy decisions aiming to reduce carbon dioxide 
emissions.
There has recently been an exciting development in DFMs with the introduction of quan­
tile factor models by Chen et al. (2021). This line of research was further advanced by 
Korobilis and Schröder (2025), who proposed a probabilistic dynamic factor model, applied 
within a MIDAS framework. Future econometric research includes extending these meth­
ods to the SA-DFM model introduced by Bennedsen et al. (2021), as well as to the nondif­
ferenced factor model developed by Choi and Jeong (2020). Once developed, a natural next 
step is to explore their performance in nowcasting carbon dioxide emissions.
