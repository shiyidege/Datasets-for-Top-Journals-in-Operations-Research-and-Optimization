Abstract
Repositioning products and services to entice customers is a key management strategy that
requires careful planning, timing, and budget allocation. This study is the ﬁrst to investigate
repositioning strategies by considering a Hotelling-type location model where consumer
preferences change over time with short- and long-term uncertainty. This requires the ﬁrm
to adjust its product positioning to continue to appeal to consumers. A key assumption
is that the parameters describing the dynamics of consumer preferences are modulated by a
Markov-switching regime representing long-term uncertainty. Further, this study investigates
the effects of regime shift intensity on repositioning strategies. We numerically solve a simul-
taneous ordinary differential equation system to derive a ﬁrm’s optimal strategy, represented
by repositioning thresholds that depend on the regimes. We ﬁnd that in the two-regime case,
one threshold is monotonic with respect to the regime shift intensity, whereas the other can
be non-monotonic. This suggests that a ﬁrm should simultaneously consider the value of the
current regime and option value of waiting for a regime shift, thus effectively demonstrating
the signiﬁcance of the uncertainty associated with regime-switching.
Keywords Product repositioning · Spatial modeling · Regime-switching · Stochastic
impulse control · Decision-making under uncertainty

2010), product quality (e.g., Cremer & Thisse, 1991; Desai, 2001), and entry timing (e.g.,
Lambertini, 2002; Ebina et al., 2015; Ebina & Nishide, 2024). Since Hotelling (1929), many
researchers in management science and operations research have emphasized the importance
of product positioning (or location) as a competitive strategy (e.g., Tyagi, 2000; Alcácer &

Recently, several researchers in management science and operations research have also

When repositioning, ﬁrms typically incur repositioning costs to educate consumers about
the new position, overcome internal resistance to change, and rework channel relationships
(Menon & Yao 2017; Du et al. 2019). Therefore, ﬁrms must determine how to reposition
their products and when to reposition to meet consumer preferences by anticipating future
changes while considering the repositioning costs and beneﬁts.
As Chiu et al. (2017) have mentioned, the demand for fashionable and perishable products
is uncertain, and signiﬁcantly affected by trends and market conditions. Thus, a ﬁrm needs to
re-adjust the characteristics and assortment of products. Consider fast fashion brands. Every
year, season, or week, these brands change the color and design of their clothes according
to changing fashion trends. Similarly, car manufacturers (e.g., BMW, Honda, Mercedes, and
Toyota) redesign their car shapes or introduce new colors every three to ﬁve years.2 Many
computer companies (e.g., Apple and IBM) also adjust their logos every few years (see
page 279 of Villas-Boas, 2018). Drug companies (e.g., Johnson & Johnson) are other well-
known examples (see page 1985 of Du et al., 2019). Based on recent reviews and market
research reports, Novac (2013) has elucidated that repositioning constitutes 10–50% of R&D
spending, and is considered an important part of product lifecycle management. Therefore,
repositioning is ubiquitous in many industries.
To adjust to consumer preferences using a repositioning strategy, two types of uncertainties
should be considered: long- (cyclical trend) and short-term (minute evolution) uncertainties.
This study examines the motivating example presented in Sproles (1981). The author suggests
that fashion cycles are analyzed in two frames: long-run secular trends, spanning decades and
centuries, and short-run ones, lasting several months to years.3 Over decades and centuries,
new fashions have evolved in historical continuity. In the long run, major style changes are
considered secular or occur only once a year or century. Fashion cycles also allegedly change
continuously and progressively from one extreme to another over 30–50 years or more. For
instance, this principle predicts that a cycle of dress lengths changing from extremes of short
to long, and reverting to short is a change which may take several decades or even a century.4

A Markov-switching regime can be useful for investigating long-term trend uncertainties.
For example, Cadenillas et al. (2013) consider an optimal production management problem in
which consumer demand is subject to a Markov chain representing the business cycle. They
show that business conditions affect optimal production levels, highlighting the importance
of long-term trend uncertainty. Other studies with similar motivations include Cadenillas and
Zapatero (1999), Sotomayor and Cadenillas (2013), Huamán-Aguilar and Cadenillas (2015),
and Goto et al. (2017).
This study investigates a ﬁrm’s repositioning strategies under uncertainty, represented by
regime-switching. Following Villas-Boas (2018), we construct a continuous-time model in
which a monopolistic ﬁrm adjusts its product position to match consumer preferences, which
ﬂuctuate randomly over time. The monopolistic ﬁrm decides when to reposition and the
degree of repositioning by considering the repositioning costs. Moreover, they anticipated
future preferences and uncertainties. The ﬁrm considers the trade-off between paying reposi-
tioning costs and earning higher proﬁts due to repositioning. Unlike Villas-Boas (2018) and
other related studies, we assume that the Markov-switching regime modulates the parameters
describing the dynamics of consumer preferences. Introducing this regime is realistic consid-
ering that consumer preferences evolve with two types of uncertainties–short- and long-term
ﬂuctuations–as previously discussed in the fashion industry.
However, the technical challenges encountered in this study should be noted. Market
conditions and the overall economic environment change over time. The change occurs
cyclically in many markets, indicating the importance of studying a ﬁrm’s decision-making
under regime switching. However, as Bensoussan et al. (2017) point out, introducing regime
switching in impulse stochastic control problems substantially complicates the problem and
makes it difﬁcult to characterize the optimal stopping region, which is typically described
by thresholds.6 In particular, as we see later, the dynamics of the state variable in the model
of this study and Villas-Boas (2018) switch at zero, which makes the derivation of the value
function more difﬁcult. Speciﬁcally, the number of unknowns to be solved in the simultaneous
equation system in our setting is four times that of a standard setting. However, we can solve
the value function to derive the optimal thresholds in plausible and meaningful settings, and
demonstrate the signiﬁcance of regime-switching on optimal investment policies. Speciﬁ-
cally, we present two solvable regime-switching models. The ﬁrst is a regime-switching drift
model, which we construct to consider our main problem from both practical and theoretical
perspectives. The second is a regime-switching volatility model, which we present only for
theoretical purposes. Our study is novel in that we derive an optimal repositioning strat-
egy under regime uncertainty, which has important implications for managerial decisions in
actual markets.

This study makes the following contributions. First, we derive a ﬁrm’s optimal strategy
using a model that incorporates regime switching. This is the ﬁrst study to investigate reposi-
tioning strategies using a Hotelling-type spatial model with a regime switch. As in Villas-Boas
(2018), the optimal strategy is expressed as the stopping time, with the repositioning thresh-
olds determined by value-matching and smooth-pasting conditions. We demonstrate that the
value function depends on time when the parameters are subject to regime switching. By
applying the methodology in Cadenillas and Zapatero (1999) and other related studies that
consider regime switching, we numerically derive the optimal repositioning thresholds with
a simultaneous nonlinear equation system. Importantly, when solving the value function, the
magnitude relationships of the thresholds for all regimes should be carefully considered, as
in the previous studies.
Second, we ﬁnd that regime signiﬁcantly affects ﬁrm’s repositioning decisions. Effectively
analyzing the impact of regime uncertainty requires investigating the intensity of a regime
shift. Consider two regimes in the drift, good and bad, for the current position and that
the asymmetry between the two regimes is large. By “good (bad) regime,” we mean that
a long-term trend is moving to (away from) the favorable position. We numerically show
that the optimal thresholds are higher (lower) in a good (bad) regime, which is intuitive.
Furthermore, for a more critical nature, a higher-intensity regime shift from one to the other
has two opposing effects on the repositioning threshold in a bad regime. The ﬁrst effect
allows the ﬁrm to reposition easily, considering that the overall situation should improve.
The second effect is the opposite, and makes a ﬁrm hesitant to reposition because it must
wait in the current regime and reposition to the center after a regime shift.7 These two effects
result in a nonmonotonic relationship between the repositioning threshold and intensity,
successfully illustrating how a ﬁrm should choose its repositioning timing under these two
types of uncertainty. This ﬁnding echoes that of Goto et al. (2017), who examine a regime
shift’s effect in a different context, and show how the option value of waiting and seeing is
affected by regime uncertainty. From a managerial perspective, a ﬁrm needs to consider the
effects of possible future regime changes when formulating repositioning strategies.
The remainder of this article is organized as follows: We establish our model in Sect.2.
The condition that the value function must satisfy is derived as a veriﬁcation theorem in
Sect.3. In Sect.4, while examining a speciﬁc setting with regime-switching drift as the main
problem, we solve the value functions using the veriﬁcation theorem and conduct numerical
calculations to show that regime-switching signiﬁcantly affects the optimal repositioning
strategy. After presenting and solving another model as a secondary problem in Sect.5, we
present the conclusions in Sect.6.

determined in the relative duration times. However, as discussed in the last two paragraphs
of this section, the criteria are different for an intermediate λ, and the regime shift does affect
the optimal strategy.
The intuition behind the non-monotonicity of λ on ¯ZL is as follows: Suppose that the
current center at is above the ﬁrm’s position in regime 1 (zt < 0). Here, as λ increases, two
opposing effects occur: First, the ﬁrm expects that at may return to its position because of
a regime change (from regime 1 to 2), which lowers ¯ZL. Second, a larger λ weakens the
effect of the drift b in the current setting, as explained in the previous paragraph (see also
Proposition 3). Then, even if regime 2 begins, the ﬁrm expects that at will not return to its
current position because the effect of the current trend is weak. Thus, the former (latter) effect
dominates the latter (former) when λ is small (large). Hence, ¯ZL is non-monotonic.

As discussed in Sect.1, the optimal repositioning policy when the drift is subjected to a
periodic cycle is important from both the theoretical and practical viewpoints. An important
implication from managerial and marketing viewpoints is that we need to carefully investigate
how a ﬁrm should optimally choose thresholds in response to the probability of a regime shift
depending on the drift level. Our ﬁndings contribute to the literature in this regard.
The abovementioned ﬁndings in the example imply that regime uncertainty is crucial
while studying the ﬁrm’s optimal strategy regarding thresholds and product location after
repositioning, especially when the economic environment in the two regimes differs signif-
icantly. This is in line with existing studies such as Bensoussan et al. (2017). For example,
even if the current regime is not good for sales, the ﬁrm should be hesitant to reposition
and wait for a regime shift if its probability is non-negligible. A ﬁrm should simultaneously
consider the uncertainty in the random movements of consumer preferences, as described
by the Brownian motion in our model, and uncertainty in regimes representing the stochas-
tic process. In other words, the option value of waiting and seeing with respect to regime
uncertainty matters in an optimal repositioning strategy. As a practical application, we look
at the fashion industry and consider the dress production mentioned in the Introduction. Our
numerical results indicate that when a producer makes a production plan, they should not
hasten the change in the current layout, even if the ongoing trend does not match it. A similar

First, with the other parameter values ﬁxed, ¯Z1 decreases, whereas ¯Z2 increases as λ
increases. In other words, the difference between the two thresholds decreases with higher
values of λ. Thus, the ﬁrm is more (less) willing to reposition to the center for a higher λ in
a more (less) volatile regime.
The intuition behind this observation is natural and is as follows: When a regime change
is expected, a ﬁrm must consider an immediate regime shift. Even if the current regime is less
volatile, the ﬁrm should be unwilling to reposition to the center fearing that the regime may
suddenly shift to another with higher volatility, resulting in a higher ¯Z2 with a higher λ. In a
more volatile regime, the opposite holds because ¯Z1 is lower with higher λ. Consequently,
the difference between the two thresholds decreases when the regime shift is more likely to
occur. Table 3 also conﬁrms that ¯Z1/ ¯Z2 decreases in λ under the same parameter values as
in Table 2 with σ 2

(limλ→∞Z H = −ZL), the absolute values of the higher and lower thresholds are always
equal under the regime-switching volatility.

waits to reposition when consumer preferences are unstable and volatile. This is intuitively
explained by the fact that if the two volatilities are larger, the average consumer’s preference is
more likely return to the ﬁrm’s current position. In other words, even if consumer preferences

Remark 4 We summarize the numerical results for regime-switching volatility as follows:
(i) ¯Z1 decreases and ¯Z2 increases as λ increases. In other words, the difference between the
two thresholds decreases as the value of λ increases.
(ii) As the volatilities σ1 and σ2 increase in parallel, both ¯Z1 and ¯Z2 increase.
(iii) Given σ1 is constant, ¯Z2 increases in σ2, whereas ¯Z1 is non-monotonic with respect to
σ2.
(iv) ¯Z1 ( ¯Z2) can be non-monotonic (monotonic) with respect to λ1 or λ2 when λ1 and λ2
differ.
The numerical results that are new and not shown in Villas-Boas (2018) are (i), (iii),
and (iv) in Remark 4. These new results imply that introducing regime-switching volatility
affects the ﬁrm’s repositioning strategies, demonstrating the signiﬁcance of regime-switching
on optimal investment strategies in repositioning. In contrast, Remark 4(ii) has already been
presented by Villas-Boas (2018), meaning that regime switching does not change the basic
property of the thresholds in relation to parallel shifts in volatilities. In other words, the effect
of short-term uncertainty is robust even under regime-switching volatility.
The threshold’s non-monotonicity illustrates how a ﬁrm should optimally choose its repo-
sitioning timing under regime uncertainty with volatility. The above results and those in the
case of regime-switching drift reconﬁrm the importance of regime switching in the optimal
repositioning strategy.

6 Conclusion
Extending Villas-Boas (2018), we examine the situation in which a ﬁrm optimally reposi-
tions itself when the dynamics of consumer preferences are subject to regime-switching.
Using numerical examples, we show that thresholds can be non-monotonic, especially when
the two regimes signiﬁcantly differ in terms of their economic environments. For example,
when the drift has a cyclical trend, the threshold in an unfavorable regime is U-shaped.
Non-monotonicity effectively describes the option value of waiting for and observing future
situations associated with regime uncertainty. This ﬁnding contributes to the management
science and operations research literature.
However, several important issues require further investigations. The ﬁrst is introducing
competition among ﬁrms. Recently, Ning and Villas-Boas (2021) have introduced compe-
tition between two ﬁrms that can be repositioned to adjust to customer preferences in a
Hotelling-type spatial model (see also Cong & Zhou, 2020, employing a three-stage set-
ting). Many industries, such as car manufacturing, pharmaceuticals, fashion, and computer
products (described in the Introduction), face intense competition in product positioning and
design. Hence, an analysis in this direction is crucial. Second, we introduce other spatial
factors, such as vertical product differentiation and network effects, into our model. These
factors are important in management science and operations research when considering ﬁrms’
repositioning strategies. Therefore, scholars should investigate the relationship between repo-
sitioning strategies and regime switching in the presence of these factors. The third is the
problem of whether to reposition a product or introduce a new product, which many ﬁrms
face when making managerial decisions in actual markets. Finally, the evolution of pref-
erences should be extended to include more general topics. One example is introducing a
mean-reversion type, such as the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, which is more realistic for
actual markets (e.g., Hahn & Dyer, 2008). Another example is a multidimensional stochastic
process with correlation (e.g., Ebina et al., 2022a).

A particular empirical issue of interest is the relationship between the intensity of regime
