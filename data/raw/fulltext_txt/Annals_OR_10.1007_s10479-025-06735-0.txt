 Abstract
In this paper we examine the relation between the вҖңbad outputs as inputsвҖқ with other apВӯ
proaches of modeling undesirable outputs in non-parametric production analysis and we 
end up with six equivalencies among them. Based on these, we argue that the вҖңbad outputs 
as inputsвҖқ is a valid modeling option as long as the assumption of extended strong disposВӯ
ability and thus of reverse outputs is reasonable in a particular setting. In this case, the 
вҖңbad outputs as inputsвҖқ approach can easily be implemented by means of a conventional 
input-oriented DEA model with variable returns to scale where the translated additive 
inverse transformation is applied to undesirable output quantities.

Undoubtedly, the second approach is the most widely used in the literature and also 
the longer debated one. The weak disposability axiom, introduced by Shephard (1970), 
states that, for given inputs, undesirable outputs can be reduced if and only if desirable 
outputs also decrease by the same proportion. In addition, it is assumed that some amount 
of undesirable output is always produced if a positive amount of the desirable output is to 
be produced, which is referred to as the assumption of null jointness. FГӨre and Grosskopf 
(2003) considered the by-product nature of undesirable outputs as a physical law, which is 
certainty true for almost all conventional industrial production processes, and this was the 
main reason to abandon the very first attempt of Cropper and Oates (1992) to model вҖңbad 
outputs as inputsвҖқ; for its incorporation into the non-parametric production analysis frameВӯ
work see Hailu and Veeman (2001) (HV thereafter).2,3 Nevertheless, Hampf (2018) вҖңdid not 
find [empirical] evidence that the incorporation of pollutants as weakly disposable outputs 
leads to significantly different results compared to incorporating them as good inputsвҖқ (p. 
122).4 Based on this, he argued that вҖң[t]his finding questions the theoretical literature which 
argues that the input approach is physically impossible and should not be used in applied 
analysisвҖқ (p. 123).5
There are however several cases where undesirable outputs may be treated as reverse 
outputs rather than as by-products.6 According to Lewis and Sexton (2004), reverse are 
those outputs that are measured on scales in which higher numerical values represent lower 
output realization. In addition, reverse outputs might not necessarily be accompanied by 
desirable outputs, as do by-products, and thus the assumption of null jointness does not 
apply. Based on these, Yang et al. (2008, p. 52) noticed that вҖңweak disposability may be suitВӯ
able in a case producing undesirable outputs with a characteristic of externality, but might 
not suit a case of reverse outputsвҖқ. Liu et al. (2010) argued that even pollution could freely 
increase without increasing desirable outputs by cutting down or reducing abatement effort. 
Dyckhoff and Allen (2001) referred to cases in healthcare (complications of medical operaВӯ
tions) and finance (tax payments), where undesirable outputs are more like reverse outputs 
than by-products: for instance, complications may be reduced as the number of successful

and Dyckhoff (2019) on value disposability.

The objective of this paper to examine the usefulness of the вҖңbad outputs as inputsвҖқ 
formulation in cases as the aforementioned ones, where undesirable outputs may be treated 
as reverse outputs. In all these cases, reverse outputs are directly counterproductive to the 
intended outcomes of the production processes and are not simply byproducts but rather 
detrimental results of the processes themselves. We proceed by examining the relation of the 
HV formulation with the first two of the approaches used for modeling undesirable outputs, 
namely data transformation and models with different disposability assumptions, using an 
input-oriented Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) model, which is non-parametric in the 
sense of not assuming any specific function form for the underlying production function.8
Several interesting results emerge from this exercise: first, the HV formulation may be 
obtained by applying Koopmans (1951) additive inverse transformation to undesirable outВӯ
puts into the conventional input-oriented DEA model, regardless of the nature of the returns 
to scale. If variable returns to scale are assumed, then one can also verify that the HV forВӯ
mulation is equivalent to the conventional input-oriented DEA model when the translated 
additive inverse transformation is applied to undesirable outputs. This in turn provides a 
simple way for estimating it by means of the conventional input-oriented DEA model with 
variable returns to scale using a set of transformed instead of the original undesirable output 
variables. This is a direct consequence of the fact that the conventional input-oriented DEA 
model with variable returns to scale is translation invariant with respect to outputs.
Second, the HV formulation may be seen as an alternative modeling choice where undeВӯ
sirable outputs are treated as reverse outputs in Lewis and Sexton (2004) sense rather than 
inputs. More importantly, we may associate reverse outputs with the extended strong disВӯ

3вҖӮ Extended strong disposability, reverse outputs and the HV 
formulation
Following Liu et al. (2010), the HV formulation may also be seen as a model using the raw 
data for the undesirable outputs but modifying the underlying disposability assumption.13 In 
particular, Liu et al., (2010, p. 180) have shown that вҖңвҖҰ treating undesirableвҖҰ outputs as 
desirableвҖҰ inputs, and assumingвҖҰ strong disposability are equivalent to an assumption of 
extended strong disposabilityвҖқ using the raw data for the undesirable outputs. In what folВӯ
lows we additionally verify that an input-oriented model with extended strong disposability, 
and thus the HV formulation, are both equivalent to an input-oriented model with forward 
and reverse outputs, in the sense of Lewis and Sexton (2004).
The extended strong disposability axiom states that with more inputs it is always possible 
to produce less desirable outputs and more undesirable outputs (Liu et al., 2010). This is to 
be distinguished from the weak disposability axiom which implies that, for given inputs, 
undesirable outputs can be reduced if and only if desirable outputs are also reduced by the 
same proportion; see e.g. FГӨre and Grosskopf (2003, 2004).14 In this case, the values of the 
undesirable outputs of the DMU under evaluation are equal to the linear combination of the 
undesirable outputs of the other DMUs instead of being the lower bounds as for desirable

6вҖӮ Concluding remarks
We have shown in this paper that the HV formulation of the вҖңbad outputs as inputsвҖқ modelВӯ
ing approach for undesirable outputs is a special case of Liu et al. (2010) model of extended 
strong disposability when there are no bad inputs. In this case, strong input disposability 
applies to the extended vector of inputs and bad outputs. We have also verify four other 
equivalences of the HV formulation with an input-oriented DEA model with Koopmans 
(1951) additive inverse transformation applied to undesirable output quantities; an input-
oriented Lewis and Sexton (2004) model where undesirable outputs are treated as reverse 
outputs; Dyckhoff (2018) cost-oriented MCPT model when the benefits are identical to the 
output quantities and the costs to the input and the undesirable output quantities; and KorhoВӯ
nen and Luptacik (2004) eco-efficiency model A. All these equivalences hold regardless of 
returns to scale. In addition, in the case of variable returns to scale, we can show that the 
HV formulation is equivalent to an input-oriented DEA model with the translated additive 
inverse transformation applied to undesirable output quantities. The latter provides a simple 
way for estimating the HV formulation by means of a conventional input-oriented DEA 
model with variables returns to scale using the transformed instead of the original undesirВӯ
able output variables. Thus, as long as the assumption of extended strong disposability and 
of reverse outputs is reasonable in a particular setting, the вҖңbad outputs as inputsвҖқ modeling 
approach is both valid and complete. This finding, which holds for an input-oriented model, 
should be added to Hampf (2018) results for the output-oriented model with вҖңbad outputs 
as inputsвҖқ. Taking both at face value, we may conclude that under certain circumstances 
the вҖңbad outputs as inputsвҖқ approach may not be at the end as poor of a modeling choice as 
some previous studies have suggested.
