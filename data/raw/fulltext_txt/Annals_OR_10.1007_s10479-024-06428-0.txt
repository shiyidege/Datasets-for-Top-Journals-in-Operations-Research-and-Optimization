Abstract
Principal component analysis (PCA) has been a widely used technique for dimension reduc-
tion while retaining essential information. However, the ordinary PCA lacks interpretability,
especially when dealing with large scale data. To address this limitation, sparse PCA (SPCA)
has emerged as an interpretable variant of ordinary PCA. However, the ordinary SPCA
relies on solving a challenging non-convex discrete optimization problem, which maximizes
explained variance while constraining the number of non-zero elements in each principal
component. In this paper, we propose an innovative least angle SPCA technique to address
the computational complexity associated with SPCA, particularly in ultrahigh dimensional
data, by sequentially identifying sparse principal components with minimal angles to their
corresponding components extracted through ordinary PCA. This sequential identiﬁcation
enables solving the optimization problem in polynomial time, signiﬁcantly reducing com-
putational challenges. Despite its efﬁciency gains, our proposed method also preserves the
main attributes of SPCA. Through comprehensive experimental results, we demonstrate
advantages of our approach as a viable alternative for dealing with the computational difﬁ-
culties inherent in ordinary SPCA. Notably, our method emerges as an efﬁcient and effective
solution for conducting ultrahigh dimensional data analysis, enabling researchers to extract
meaningful insights and streamline data interpretation.

1 Introduction
Principal component analysis (PCA), ﬁrst introduced by Pearson (1901), is a widely uti-
lized statistical method in the domains of dimensionality reduction and data visualization.
This linear technique aims to discover the underlying structure of high dimensional data
by identifying the most signiﬁcant orthogonal components known as principal components,
which capture the maximum variance in the data (Jolliffe 2002). PCA aims to reduce the
complexity of data with many interrelated variables while preserving as much of the original
variance as possible. The main goal of PCA is to transform the original variables into a new
set of uncorrelated variables, known as principal components. These components are linear
combinations of the original variables and are arranged in a way that the ﬁrst principal com-
ponent captures the largest portion of the variance in the data (Javed 2013; Wu et al. 2022).
Subsequent principal components explain progressively less variance and are orthogonal,
meaning they are uncorrelated with the preceding components (Daffertshofer et al. 2004).
PCA is a useful statistical technique commonly used in ﬁelds like face recognition and image
compression to identify patterns in high dimensional data, offering beneﬁts by simplifying its
complexity through representation in a lower-dimensional space, yet preserving the essential
information (Meng and Yang 2012). The initial principal components often contain the most
crucial information, and visualizing the data in this reduced-dimensional space can reveal
patterns and relationships that are difﬁcult to discern in the original data. PCA ﬁnds applica-
tions in various ﬁelds, including machine learning, image processing, and genomics (Jolliffe
2002; Hastie et al. 2009). It is used for tasks such as data compression, feature extraction, and
clustering. Additionally, PCA is commonly employed as a data preprocessing technique to
remove noise and reduce variables, thereby facilitating the construction of accurate models
and predictions (Phillips et al. 2005).
In practical scenarios, high dimensional data often comprises only a small fraction
of relevant variables, while encompassing numerous irrelevant or noise-bearing variables
(Bolón-Canedo et al. 2016). Driven by contemporary applications, recent decades have wit-
nessed a growing interest in developing techniques to handle high-dimensional data more
effectively (Abbe et al. 2022). Nevertheless, ordinary PCA is subject to limitations due to its
disregard for data sparsity, which consequently curtails its explanatory power. The incapacity
of PCA to capture sparsity is attributed to its uniform weighting of all variables, encompass-
ing redundant or irrelevant ones. As a result, this approach may yield principal components
that are sensitive to outliers and lack robustness in the presence of noisy or highly skewed
data (Meng et al. 2012).
To address this issue, Jeffers (1967) initially introduced sparse PCA (SPCA), aiming
to achieve a concise representation of high dimensional data while preserving maximal
explained variability. The key focus of SPCA is on minimizing variables within each com-
ponent to enhance interpretability, and this has led to an increased recognition of the need for
further research in this ﬁeld (Jeffers 1967). SPCA addresses the limitation of ordinary PCA
by fostering sparsity in the calculated principal components, aiming to identify a limited
number of non-zero coefﬁcients in the principal components that represent a small subset of
the original variables. Consequently, a more interpretable and robust data representation is

achieved, with principal components less inﬂuenced by outliers and noise. In essence, SPCA
is driven by the necessity for a more interpretable representation of high dimensional data.
SPCA aims to increase the proportion of zero elements in principal components while
maintaining a signiﬁcant level of explanatory power. This is achieved by constraining the
number of non-zero components in each principal component to a ﬁxed integer k, where
1 ≤k < p, with p denoting the total number of variables. Recently, there has been a surge in
research investigating SPCA due to its inherent NP-hard nature, leading to the development
of numerous algorithms that approximate the optimal solution. To enhance the sparsity of
elements, Jolliffe et al. (2003) proposed a modiﬁed version of PCA called Simpliﬁed Com-
ponent Technique-LASSO (SCoTLASS), which utilizes the LASSO methodology. Leng and
Wang (2009) extended the work of SCoTLASS by introducing an “adaptive” penalty vector
that varies by index, replacing the LASSO penalty. Additionally, Zou et al. (2006) introduced
a regression framework of SPCA that incorporates sparsity by appending an ℓ1 penalty to the
regression function. Sriperumbudur et al. (2007) introduced DC-PCA, a relaxation technique
for SPCA that utilizes an ℓ1 norm approximation for the cardinality constraint of standard
SPCA. Dey et al. (2022) leveraged ℓ1 relaxation and integer programming to obtain tight dual
bounds for SPCA. Traditional approaches, such as semideﬁnite programming relaxations,
often struggle with loose bounds and scalability. In contrast, their proposed convex integer
programming framework offers tighter dual bounds and improved scalability on real-world
datasets, particularly for large covariance matrices. Benidis et al. (2016) proposed Orthog-
onal SPCA (OrthSPCA) to address this trade-off and generate orthogonal sparse principal
components, avoiding sacriﬁcing the orthogonality attribute. Bertsimas and Kitane (2023)
introduced GeoSPCA to build all principal components simultaneously while maintaining
orthogonality, improving on traditional iterative deﬂation methods. The approach uses binary
linear optimization to approximate optimal solutions, offering signiﬁcant improvements in
variance explained and handling high-dimensional real-world problems efﬁciently.
Beck and Vaisbourd (2016) adopted a distinct approach to SPCA by considering the
correlation of variables with a speciﬁc outcome during support selection. Although this
supervised approach solves a different problem than SPCA, it shares the same underlying
objective. Seghouane et al. (2019) developed an adaptive block sparse PCA method with
preserved sparsity pattern (SPCA-PSP), ensuring that when multiple principal components
are estimated, the corresponding loading vectors share the same sparsity pattern. This method
identiﬁes a small subset of variables from the original feature space that contributes most
signiﬁcantly to the principal components. Wang et al. (2024) introduced Dynamic SPCA
(D-SPCA) to aim at establishing global sparsity in the ﬁrst few principal components. Bert-
simas et al. (2022) proposed a cutting-plane method (CP-SPCA) that allows for certiﬁable
optimal solutions, by reformulating sparse PCA as a mixed-integer semi-deﬁnite optimiza-
tion problem. The approach is effective at providing small bound gaps even at larger scales
and presents a convex relaxation scheme that offers near-optimal solutions within practical
time frames for larger datasets. In addition to these approaches, several greedy search algo-
rithms have been developed, including Generalized SPCA (GSPCA) by Moghaddam et al.
(2006) and PathSPCA by d’Aspremont et al. (2008). Rahoma et al. (2021) proposed a novel
approach to SPCA by incorporating the bootstrap method. Wang et al. (2024) proposed a
sequentially learning method for SPCA, enhancing variance and interpretability through an
alternating two-phase process. These algorithms have proven effective in extracting sparse
and interpretable principal components from various raw datasets.
The primary goal of SPCA is to identify a set of sparse principal components that can
effectively capture most of the data variability. The existing literature on sparse PCA, as
previously mentioned, can be broadly categorized into three aspects. The ﬁrst category of

methods, such as GeoSPCA, CP-SPCA involve solving a cardinality constraint for a given
sparsity pattern, which refers to the number of non-zero elements in each principal compo-
nent. Representative methods under this category achieve high total explained variability.
However, these methods have a notable drawback: they involve solving a nonlinear inte-
ger optimization problem, which is NP-hard (Magdon-Ismail 2017). As the dimensionality
increases, the computation time also increases signiﬁcantly, making these methods unsuitable
for high-dimensional or even ultrahigh dimensional scenarios. This complexity can pose a
signiﬁcant challenge to obtaining a ﬁnal solution (Zou and Xue 2018). The second category
of methods, such as SCoTLASS, DC-PCA, GSPCA, typically employs ℓ1, ℓ2 norms, or a
combination thereof, to approximate the cardinality constraint. In this category, a given regu-
larization parameter is required, which prevents them from attaining a speciﬁed sparsity level
(Sriperumbudur et al. 2007). Examples of these methods have simpliﬁed the optimization
problem through the use of approximate constraints, allowing them to be more suitable for
high-dimensional settings. However, the total explained variability of their results may not
be optimal. The ﬁrst two types of methods focus on the sparsity of each individual principal
component, often overlooking the overall sparsity pattern across all principal components.
Methods like SPCA-PSP and D-SPCA maintain a consistent sparsity pattern in the loading
vectors across all principal components. D-SPCA further considers both the sparsity of each
principal component and the overall sparsity. However, these methods prioritize achieving
uniform sparsity across all principal components, aiming to reduce the number of variables
used in all obtained principal components. They identify a small subset of variables from
the original feature space that contribute most to the principal components, often resulting
in a lower total explained variability. Additionally, in D-SPCA, the approach of dynami-
cally adding non-zero variables to each principal component sequentially have signiﬁcant
limitations in ultrahigh dimensional contexts.
Although some SPCA methods, such as those in the second category, perform better than
others in handling high-dimensional data, they face challenges in balancing total explained
variability and algorithmic efﬁciency when dealing with ultrahigh dimensional data due to
increased complexity. With the surge in ultrahigh dimensional data, this trend shows no
signs of slowing down, driven by signiﬁcant improvements in computational capabilities and
related technological advancements. Nowadays, with the surge in ultrahigh dimensional data,
driven by signiﬁcant advancements in computing power and related technologies, this trend
shows no sign of slowing. These technological leaps have empowered researchers to amass
data of an unparalleled scale and intricacy, spanning a range of ﬁelds including microarrays,
proteomics, brain imaging, video analysis, functional data, and high-frequency ﬁnancial data.
The boom in large-scale applications not only brings forth substantial challenges but also
offers thrilling opportunities for the realms of statistics and machine learning. Although great
strides have been made in recent years to traverse this intricate terrain, a signiﬁcant amount
of work remains to fully exploit these burgeoning opportunities (Fan et al. 2009).
This paper introduces the least angle (LA) method as a solution to address the compu-
tational complexity challenges associated with ordinary SPCA. The primary objective of
the proposed LA method is to reformulate the non-linear objective function of SPCA into
a linear format, thereby enabling a more efﬁcient and effective approach to solving SPCA
while preserving its crucial properties, such as identifying sparse and interpretable principal
components. By reducing the computational complexity, the LA method allows for the utiliza-
tion of established linear optimization algorithms, making it feasible to analyze large-scale
datasets. Additionally, adopting linear models enhances the interpretability of the results,
making them more accessible to researchers and practitioners. As a result, the introduction
of the LA method signiﬁcantly advances SPCA as a valuable analytical tool. We refer to the

combination of the new LA approach with SPCA as least angle SPCA (LA-SPCA). This inte-

After conducting a comprehensive review of the latest literature on SPCA (Zou and Xue
2018) and considering additional sources, it is noteworthy that no studies have utilized the
LA method or formulation for SPCA. Therefore, this presents an opportunity to introduce a
novel approach called LA-SPCA, which aims to maximize the similarity between the original
non-sparse principal component and the sparse principal component.
This paper contributes to the ﬁeld in two signiﬁcant ways. Firstly, it introduces LA-SPCA,
which utilizes a linear objective function instead of the ordinary nonlinear objective function
used in other methods. The LA-SPCA technique signiﬁcantly enhances computational speed
by adopting the linear objective function, providing a more efﬁcient and expedient solution
for SPCA. The linear format enables faster and scalable analysis, making it particularly
suitable for large-scale datasets.
Additionally, the paper presents a closed-form solution for the LA-SPCA method. This
closed-form solution reduces the computational complexity and improves computation
times, enhancing the overall efﬁciency of the LA-SPCA technique. The closed-form solu-
tion contributes to the practicality and usability of the LA-SPCA method for real-world
applications.
In summary, the contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows:
• The proposed LA-SPCA technique achieves a signiﬁcant level of explained variabil-

• The paper enhances the computational speed and efﬁciency of the LA-SPCA method,

• LA-SPCA demonstrates improved computational efﬁciency and scalability, particu-

accuracy and stability.

3 Methodology
In this section, we will delve into an in-depth exploration of the technical details surrounding
the LA method for SPCA. Firstly, we will introduce the LA method, explaining its fundamen-
tal principles and how it addresses the computational complexities associated with traditional
SPCA algorithms. Next, we will focus on the computation of the ﬁrst principal component
using the LA method, elaborating on its efﬁciency and effectiveness in identifying key pat-
terns within the data. Lastly, we will explore the computation of subsequent sparse principal
components, showcasing how the LA method continues to provide an efﬁcient and accurate
approach for extracting meaningful information from the data while maintaining sparsity
constraints.
3.1 Least angle method
The LA method is based on the principle of aligning the loading vector direction of a sparse
principal component with that of an ordinary principal component. This alignment is pur-
sued due to the property of ordinary principal components, which allows them to explain a
maximized variance.
The schematic plot presented in Fig.1 provides a visual representation of the proposed LA
method and its objective of minimizing the angles between principal components. The plot
depicts a three-dimensional coordinate system with the axes labeled as z1, z2, and z3, which
correspond to the ﬁrst principal components obtained through ordinary PCA. Speciﬁcally, z1
represents the ﬁrst principal component of the original covariance matrix, z2 represents the
ﬁrst principal component of the adjusted covariance matrix after the exclusion of a1, and z3
represents the ﬁrst principal component of the adjusted covariance matrix after the exclusion
of both a1 and a2.
In the plot, the purple, yellow, and blue arrows denoted as a1, a2, and a3, respectively,
represent the corresponding principal components derived from the proposed LA method.
These principal components are obtained by minimizing the angles θ1, θ2, and θ3, which
denote the angles between the ordinary principal components and the respective principal
components obtained from the LA method.
The schematic plot visually displays the angles formed between the principal components
obtained through LA and the original principal components obtained from ordinary PCA.
The main objective of the LA method is to minimize these angles, thereby achieving a
closer alignment between the transformed principal components and the original principal
components. The plot serves as a visual representation of how the LA method aims to enhance
the alignment of principal components by minimizing the angles. We will demonstrate the
working of the LA method by utilizing Theorem 1. The proof of Theorem 1 can be found in

We have developed a novel method for computing the ﬁrst sparse principal component
using LA method. Our approach is a signiﬁcant departure from traditional SPCA techniques
that rely on a quadratic objective function, as our linear format has demonstrated substan-
tially improved computational efﬁciency. Moreover, our closed-form solution provides direct
access to the result, further accelerating the process. Our results indicate that the LA method
approach has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners seeking to
efﬁciently and effectively compute sparse principal components.
After obtaining the result of the ﬁrst sparse principal component, the approach for com-
puting subsequent principal components may need to be modiﬁed. This is due to the fact that
previous principal components must be taken into consideration when optimizing the prob-
lem. To address this challenge, we have proposed several efﬁcient and effective techniques
for deriving subsequent principal components. These techniques build upon the methodol-
ogy used to compute the ﬁrst principal component, while also accounting for the presence of
previous principal components in the optimization process. By offering these methods, we
hope to provide to a comprehensive set of tools for efﬁciently computing subsequent prin-
cipal components, and facilitate the use of all the ﬁrst q principal components in practical
applications.

3.4 Guidelines for selecting the number of non-zero elements
In SPCA techniques, including our proposed LA-SPCA, selecting the number of non-zero
elements, denoted as k, is crucial for balancing explained variance with interpretability. Here,
we provide three practical approaches for selecting k, applicable to various domains and data
types.
Another approach involves leveraging domain-speciﬁc knowledge. In ﬁelds such as
genomics, ﬁnance, and neuroscience, researchers rely on prior expertise to guide the selec-
tion of k, identifying signiﬁcant features based on previous studies or expert understanding.
For example, in genomics, researchers may use insights into which genes are crucial for a
particular biological process, as demonstrated by Zou et al. (2006) and Witten et al. (2009),
where SPCA was applied to gene expression data. Similarly, Lê Cao et al. (2011) applied
SPCA in proteomics, where prior knowledge about biological pathways guided the choice of
k. In neuroscience, Jolliffe and Cadima (2016) highlighted how brain imaging studies inform
the selection of k based on regions of interest or neural activation. Therefore, domain-speciﬁc
knowledge plays a crucial role in guiding the selection of the optimal k, ensuring a meaningful
balance between sparsity and interpretability in various applications.

We employ four benchmark techniques to investigate and compare their performance.

through a regression-based SPCA formulation, while the SPCA-AM presents a uniﬁed refor-
mulation solved by the alternating maximization method. SPCA-PSP is designed to obtain the
same sparsity pattern across all principal components. These benchmark techniques serve as
reference methods for evaluating the effectiveness and efﬁciency of our proposed approach,
providing insights into the advantages and limitations of each method for achieving sparse
representations in PCA.
4.1 Comparison of explained variability among different PCA variants
In the experiments, we compare the explained variability achieved by different datasets to
assess the effectiveness of each method in capturing and explaining the data’s variability.
However, for the total explained variance in SPCA, the sparse principal components are not
statistically uncorrelated, leading to an overly optimistic representation of the total variance
with the expression A′A. To address this limitation, we adopt the adjusted total variance
concept proposed by Zou et al. (2006) speciﬁcally for SPCA.

4.1.3 Semiconductor manufacturing process dataset
Etching plays a pivotal role in the progression of semiconductor manufacturing, as well
as in the fabrication of microelectronic integrated circuits and micro-nano structures. This
procedure revolves around the elimination of materials, utilizing diverse methods like solu-
tions, reactive ions, and mechanical processes. Among these techniques, plasma discharge
is a widely employed approach to modify the surface properties of materials, achieved by
generating chemically active entities. The etching mechanism involves the introduction of
appropriategasesintoaconﬁnedchamber,accompaniedbytheapplicationofradio-frequency
voltage to two parallel electrodes. This action triggers the formation of a plasma sheath, which
propels ions within the cathode sheath domain, causing them to collide with the intended sur-
face of the sample, thereby imbuing it with high-energy ions (Xie et al. 2024). Consequently,
this interaction leads to sputtering and is complemented by the assistance of magnets, which
augment the path of ions towards the chamber wall, as documented by Donnelly’s research
(Donnelly and Kornblit 2013).
Virtual metrology (VM) ﬁnds extensive application in the realm of semiconductor man-
ufacturing techniques, notably within the context of plasma etching, which facilitates the
elimination of photoresist layers, as noted in a study by Dreyfus et al. (2022). In order to
evaluate the capabilities of VM, a dataset derived from real equipment and operational proce-
dures was employed. This dataset encompasses sensor information gathered from the process
equipment, alongside corresponding values derived from physical metrology. These physical
metrology values serve as benchmarks to verify the precision of the sensor data.
In the process of plasma etching, the utilization of low-pressure plasma holds great sig-
niﬁcance. The introduction of precursor gases into a vacuum chamber, after being generated
through gas injection, is a pivotal step. This vacuum chamber houses the wafer. The gas
molecules are then ionized by high-frequency radio waves, leading to the creation of plasma.
This plasma serves to erode the photoresist, transforming it into ash. The ongoing moni-
toring of these procedures is facilitated by an array of sensors, encompassing temperature,
gas, and ﬂow sensors. These sensors play a vital role in the identiﬁcation of environmental
contamination and the measurement of ﬂuctuations in variables such as temperature, gases,
humidity, and moisture levels in the ambient air (Nikolic et al. 2020). Additionally, ﬂow sen-
sors are responsible for gauging or managing the ﬂow rate of ﬂuids and gases within pipes
and tubes. They can be integrated with digital interfaces and computers for seamless control
and monitoring.
When a wafer is taken out of the vacuum chamber, data is gathered simultaneously from
all monitoring sensors. This dataset provides valuable insights into the overall functioning
of the equipment during the entire wafer processing sequence. Therefore, the simultaneous
compilation of all data is of utmost signiﬁcance (Ko and Kim 2012).
After collecting data from the complete set of sensors, a dimension reduction technique
is implemented on the dataset before applying the VM approach. The incorporation of
dimension reduction methods like SPCA can potentially increase the level of sparsity in
semiconductor manufacturing processes, particularly within the domain of plasma etching.
The response variable in this investigation is unidimensional, representing the critical dimen-
sion (CD) values located at the center of every wafer. These CD values play a pivotal role in
determining the ﬁnal product’s performance (Dreyfus et al. 2022; Xie et al. 2024).

5 Conclusions
In conclusion, the proposed LA-SPCA stands as a groundbreaking solution to tackle the
innate computational complexity associated with SPCA. By transmuting the nonlinear objec-
tive function into a linear one, the method simpliﬁes the optimization challenge and boosts
the interpretability of the outcomes. Furthermore, we introduce a closed-form solution for
LA-SPCA, aimed at effectively handling ultrahigh dimensional data. The LA-SPCA method
not only surpasses in performance in terms of explained variance and computational speed,
but it also strikes a perfect equilibrium between computational efﬁciency and data inter-
pretability. This balance is pivotal in data analysis tasks where the capability to process data
efﬁciently without compromising interpretability is of utmost importance. Overall, the LA-
SPCA method represents a signiﬁcant addition to the SPCA ﬁeld, equipping researchers and
practitioners with an optimized methodology for efﬁcient and interpretable data analysis.
For future work, we aim to build upon the current LA-SPCA method by improving its
ability to control the total number of input variables used across the ﬁrst few principal com-
ponents. While the method effectively enforces sparsity within each individual component,

it does not currently restrict the overall number of input variables involved across the ﬁrst
few principal components. This leads to a situation where a large proportion of the input
variables may still be involved collectively, even though each component is sparse. Moving
forward, our goal is to incorporate a more comprehensive sparsity constraint that focuses on
minimizing the total number of variables used across multiple principal components, thereby
achieving what is referred to as “true sparsity". This future direction will enhance both the
interpretability and efﬁciency of the solution by ensuring that fewer unique variables are
involved in explaining the variance across the ﬁrst few principal components. We believe
that incorporating this global sparsity constraint will allow for more meaningful and inter-
pretable results, particularly in high-dimensional datasets, where reducing the total number
of variables is crucial for simplifying the model and highlighting key patterns in the data.
