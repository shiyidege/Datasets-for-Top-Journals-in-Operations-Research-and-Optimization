Developing Supply Chain Resilience through Integration: An Empirical Study on 
an E-commerce Platform 
 
ABSTRACT 
The outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic has disrupted supply chains and increased 
the uncertainties faced by firms. While firms are struggling to survive and recover from 
the pandemic, Chinese e-commerce platforms have demonstrated resilient supply 
chains. We develop a framework that investigates the impacts of integration between 
an e-commerce platform and suppliers on supply chain resilience and the moderating 
effect of the suppliers’ product flexibility. An analysis of data from a Chinese e-
commerce platform using operational indicators finds that integration between the e-
commerce platform and suppliers in terms of information sharing, joint planning and 
logistics cooperation has positive impacts on supply chain resilience, while 
procurement automation has the opposite effect. Furthermore, product flexibility 
positively moderates the impacts of information sharing, joint planning and logistics 
cooperation. The results enhance current understandings of the factors that contribute 
to the development of supply chain resilience and reveal that the relationship between 
integration and resilience should be examined within a contingency framework. The 
findings also provide guidelines for managers taking measures to mitigate the negative 
influences of supply chain disruptions. 
 
 
KEYWORDS: e-commerce platform, COVID-19, supply chain integration, supply 
chain resilience, product flexibility

1. INTRODUCTION 
The COVID-19 pandemic has drastically disrupted firms’ operations and driven them 
to build resilient supply chains (Ivanov, 2020, Belhadi et al., 2021). Supply chain 
resilience refers to the ability of a supply chain to quickly return to normal operating 
performance (Brandon-Jones et al., 2014, Pettit et al., 2019). Recovering from a 
disruption requires interactions of supply chain members and coordination of supply 
chain resources (Craighead et al., 2007). 
 Supply chain integration refers to a firm working jointly with its supply chain 
partners to plan and structure supply chain operations as collaborative and synchronized 
processes (Cao and Zhang, 2011, Flynn et al., 2010). Meta-analyses reveal that 
generally there is a positive and significant relationship between integration and 
performance outcomes (Ataseven and Nair, 2017, Mackelprang et al., 2014, Chang et 
al., 2016). However, researchers argue that integration-performance relationships are 
complex and report mixed findings on the relationships between integration and 
different dimensions of performance. For example, Mackelprang et al. (2014) and 
Ataseven and Nair (2017) found integration is positively associated with delivery but 
does not significantly affect quality. They also reported inconsistent findings on the 
impacts of integration on flexibility and cost. Moreover, supply chain integration 
includes a bundle of practices that may affect performance in different ways 
(Mackelprang et al., 2014). For example, Leuschner et al. (2013) found that information 
integration improves firm performance whereas the impact of operational integration is 
not significant. Empirical evidence has also revealed that the relationship between 
integration and performance outcomes is influenced by various moderators (Ataseven 
and Nair, 2017). For example, Chang et al. (2016) found that the positive relationship 
between integration and firm performance is moderated by national culture and 
relationship quality. 
 Literature reviews have identified collaboration as an essential component of a 
resilient supply chain (Sawyerr and Harrison, 2020, Hosseini et al., 2019, Kamalahmadi 
and Parast, 2016). Empirical studies have also reported that integration is positively 

3 
 
associated with supply chain resilience (Liu and Lee, 2018, Brusset and Teller, 2017). 
However, few studies empirically investigate the impacts of integration on resilience 
from an operational perspective and use operational indicators (Shen and Sun, 2021). It 
remains a gap in the literature on how different integration practices can help e-
commerce platforms’ supply chains develop resilience (Duong and Chong, 2020, Sodhi 
and Tang, 2021). 
 Product flexibility refers to a firm’s ability to introduce new or modified products 
(Slack and Brandon-Jones, 2019). It has been viewed as a key capability in a firm’s 
management of risks and responses to disruptions in the supply chain environment 
(Sheffi and Rice, 2005). The COVID-19 pandemic has created extensive uncertainties 
in demand and shifted consumer preferences (Choi et al., 2020). It has also altered the 
relatively stable and predictable demands for e-commerce platforms (Shen and Sun, 
2021). Hence, e-commerce platforms’ supply chains must adjust their product portfolio 
to respond to the fast and dramatic changes in customer expectations (Ivanov, 2020). 
Through integration, the supply chains can improve the information and physical flows 
(Flynn et al., 2010), which increases supply chain resilience (Scholten and Schilder, 
2015). Product flexibility enables the supply chains to cope with unplanned design 
changes in the products (Jain et al., 2013). This can improve the effectiveness of 
integration, and hence it helps e-commerce platforms’ supply chains to rebound 
strongly from disruption (Azadegan et al., 2020, Shen and Sun, 2021). Existing 
empirical evidence has revealed that the development of resilience is moderated by 
environmental (Ambulkar et al., 2015), strategic (Azadegan et al., 2020) and relational 
(Fan et al., 2020) contexts. However, less attention has been given to how product 
flexibility influences the development of resilience of e-commerce platforms’ supply 
chains from an operational perspective (Mcintyre and Srinivasan, 1997, Kamalahmadi 
and Parast, 2016, Manhart et al., 2020). 
 The objective of this study is to empirically investigate the development of supply 
chain resilience in the context of an e-commerce platform. It aims to answer two 
research questions. First, what are the impacts of integration practices on the resilience 

4 
 
of e-commerce platforms’ supply chains? Second, how does suppliers’ product 
flexibility influence the impacts of integration practices on the resilience of e-commerce 
platforms’ supply chains? The findings will uncover the distinct effects of different 
integration practices between suppliers and e-commerce platforms on supply chain 
resilience and offer insights into the practices that have significant and positive impacts 
(Ataseven and Nair, 2017, Mackelprang et al., 2014). It also clarifies how integration 
and product flexibility jointly affect resilience and enhances current understandings of 
how to use integration practices to develop supply chain resilience (Duong and Chong, 
2020, Hosseini et al., 2019). 
 
2. LITERATURE REVIEW AND HYPOTHESIS DEVELOPMENT 
2.1 Supply chain resilience 
A resilient supply chain can quickly return to its original state and planned level of 
operations after disruptions (Pettit et al., 2019, Craighead et al., 2007, Hosseini et al., 
2019). Literature reviews have shown that supply chain resilience can be 
conceptualized and operationalized in different ways, including ability and 
performance outcome (Hosseini et al., 2019, Sawyerr and Harrison, 2020). Survey 
research uses multi-item scales to measure supply chain resilience as the ability to 
bounce back from a disruption (Brandon-Jones et al., 2014), adapt to a supply chain 
disruption, provide a quick response, and maintain high situational awareness 
(Ambulkar et al., 2015), and prepare for, respond to and recover from unexpected 
supply chain disruptions by returning to or maintaining continuity of operations at the 
desired level (Fan et al., 2020). Some studies take the performance outcome perspective 
and measure supply chain resilience using quantitative metrics, such as the recovery 
level after the recovery period and the supply chain’s lost performance during the 
recovery periods (Behzadi et al., 2020). 
 This study focuses on time to recover (TTR) as it captures the extent to which a 
supply chain recovers quickly and effectively from a disruption (Behzadi et al., 2020, 
Shen and Sun, 2021). TTR is the time it takes for a supply chain to return to normal 

5 
 
operations after a disruption (Simchi-Levi et al., 2014). It takes the form of a time-based 
performance measure using an operational indicator (Behzadi et al., 2020). TTR is also 
a system-based measure of a supply chain and focuses on understanding the duration 
of the disruption, regardless of its source or likelihood, because the impact of disruption 
often does not depend on the cause but rather on its duration (Gao et al., 2019). TTR 
can identify hidden vulnerabilities, avoid the need for predictions about rare events, and 
reveal supply chain dependencies and bottlenecks; hence, it can be used to measure 
supply chain resilience (Gao et al., 2019, Simchi-Levi et al., 2014). 
2.2 Supply chain integration 
Supply chain integration involves the practices and processes related to coordination 
between suppliers and an e-commerce platform to meet customers’ needs (Flynn et al., 
2010, Cao and Zhang, 2011), which is different from vertical integration that improves 
the coordination with other firms by directly taking ownership of them (Wan and 
Sanders, 2017, Zhou and Wan, 2017). Empirical studies have conceptualized and 
measured supply chain integration in different ways. For example, Swink et al. (2007) 
argued that activities commonly associated with integration include partnerships, co-
development activities, joint planning meetings, and shared information systems. Flynn 
et al. (2010) focused on practices for information exchange through information 
networks, the establishment of quick ordering systems, partnerships, and procurement 
automation. Wong et al. (2011) measured integration via information sharing using 
information technologies, joint planning, and rapid response ordering. Schoenherr and 
Swink (2012) measured integration using planning and execution synchronization and 
information exchange. Liu et al. (2016) conceptualized integration as information 
integration, synchronized planning, operational coordination, and strategic partnerships. 
In a meta-analysis, Ataseven and Nair (2017) found that information sharing, joint 
planning, the mutual provision of access to information systems, and cooperation in 
logistics and procurement are common practices in supply chain integration initiatives. 
Recent literature reviews (Duong and Chong, 2020) and meta-analyses (Manhart et al., 

6 
 
2020) have also identified information sharing and joint activities with supply chain 
partners as key integration practices for managing risks. 
E-commerce platforms are integrated online retailers that occupy leading positions 
in their supply chains. The platform provides an information and communications 
technology infrastructure that facilitates collaboration with suppliers, and hence e-
commerce platforms’ supply chains can be coordinated without the hierarchical 
governance that is common in traditional supply chains (Mcintyre and Srinivasan, 2017, 
Jacobides et al., 2018). While traditional supply chains have several distinct stages 
including suppliers, general agents, distributors and retailers, an e-commerce platform 
usually simplifies its supply chain structure by combining the stages of general agents, 
distributors and retailers and directly purchasing all products from suppliers with whom 
it has long-term contracts (Shen and Sun, 2021). Thus, procurement and logistics are 
the main supply chain processes and effective information sharing and joint planning 
play significant roles in the interactions between an e-commerce platform and suppliers. 
Therefore, we focus on four integration practices: information sharing, joint planning, 
logistics cooperation, and procurement automation (Flynn et al., 2010, Liu et al., 2016). 
2.3 Product flexibility 
Flexibility means being able to change operations in some way (Slack and Brandon-
Jones, 2019). It provides an adaptive response to environmental uncertainty and a 
coping mechanism to maintain the status quo despite changes in the environment 
(Upton, 1994, Sawhney 2006). We focus on the demonstrated flexibility that reflects 
the degree of product flexibility that a firm is currently, actually achieving (Upton, 1994, 
Jain et al, 2013). We also take an external perspective (i.e. “what the customer sees”) 
(Upton, 1994:75), and view product flexibility as customer-driven and the ability of 
firms to respond to changes in their customers’ needs and to meet an increasing variety 
of customer expectations (Zhang et al., 2003, Jain et al, 2013). Moreover, we focus on 
the range element of product flexibility (Upton, 1994). Thus, firms with high product 
flexibility can increase the range of available products. 
 Product flexibility enables a firm to handle disruptions by improving the utilisation 

7 
 
of internal and external resources (e.g. engineers and R&D facilities) available to the 
firm (Jain et al., 2013). It has been viewed as an important component of a resilient 
supply chain (Juttner and Maklan, 2011, Scholten and Schilder, 2015, Shen and Sun, 
2021). Meanwhile, researchers argue that increasing flexibility will not necessarily 
increase firm performance outcomes and there is a contingent relationship between 
flexibility and performance outcomes (Jain et al., 2013). For example, Vokurka and 
O’Leary-Kelly (2000) argued that the impact of flexibility is dependent on a firm’s 
environmental factors, strategy, organizational attributes, and technology. Narasimhan 
et al. (2004) highlighted that a firm needs execution competence to convert flexibility 
into tangible, firm-level performance outcomes. Thus, there is a need to investigate how 
product flexibility affects supply chain resilience. 
2.4 The impacts of supply chain integration on resilience 
The COVID-19 pandemic has significantly influenced e-commerce platforms’ demand 
and procurement and logistics processes (Shen and Sun, 2021, Choi et al., 2020). The 
integration between suppliers and an e-commerce platform allows them to share supply 
and demand information, coordinate procurement and business planning, and jointly 
deal with logistical disruptions (Flynn et al., 2010). Integration thus enables an e-
commerce platform’s supply chains to mitigate the negative impact of and recover 
quickly from supply chain disruption (Bode et al., 2011, Sheffi and Rice, 2005). 
 Information sharing involves exchanging a variety of information about supply 
chain activities between suppliers and an e-commerce platform using electronic data 
interchange (EDI) (Swink et al., 2007, Flynn et al., 2010). The e-commerce platform 
collects varying pandemic-related data from various sources (Shen and Sun, 2021). The 
application of EDI allows the e-commerce platform and suppliers to share data (e.g. 
order and shipment) quickly at a low cost (Liu et al., 2016). Thus, information sharing 
can enhance the speed, quantity and quality of the information flow in the supply chains 
(Schoenherr and Swink, 2012, Liu et al., 2016). Supply chain visibility is improved, 
which helps the supply chains respond to the pandemic quickly as they can make 
decisions based on the real-time pandemic scenario (Brandon-Jones et al., 2014, 

8 
 
Scholten and Schilder, 2015). Furthermore, information sharing is essential in 
identifying vulnerabilities and effectively mitigating disruptions (Kleindorfer and Saad, 
2005) as the supply chains can reallocate resources accurately to deal with the 
exceptional demands caused by the pandemic, allowing the supply chains to recover 
quickly (Choi et al., 2020). Therefore, we propose the following: 
H1: Information sharing improves the resilience of an e-commerce platform’s supply 
chains. 
 Joint planning refers to a supplier actively coordinating with an e-commerce 
platform in supply chain planning (Liu et al., 2016, Cao and Zhang, 2011). It provides 
a mechanism for the e-commerce platform and suppliers to work together at strategic 
and operational levels to ensure the synchronization of supply chain activities and to 
make joint decisions about how to optimize supply chain processes (Juttner and Maklan, 
2011, Liu et al., 2016). The COVID-19 pandemic has resulted in a misbalanced supply 
and demand for the e-commerce platform’s supply chains. Joint planning enables 
suppliers and the e-commerce platform to collaborate on designing and planning 
promotions to reduce unhealthy inventories and increase demand (Shen and Sun, 2021). 
Moreover, joint planning allows the e-commerce platform and suppliers to analyze and 
interpret information and make sense of the pandemic together, which helps the supply 
chains identify early warning signs of disruptions and reallocate resources to ensure 
strong supply capabilities for the products with growing demands. Hence, joint 
planning allows supply chains to mitigate risks and recover quickly and efficiently from 
the COVID-19 pandemic (Kleindorfer and Saad, 2005). Therefore, we propose the 
following: 
H2: Joint planning improves the resilience of an e-commerce platform’s supply chains. 
Logistics cooperation refers to an e-commerce platform and suppliers leveraging 
their capabilities and assets to improve logistics processes together (Cao and Zhang, 
2011). The COVID-19 pandemic and the necessary countermeasures dramatically 
disrupt the logistics processes of the e-commerce platform’s supply chains (Shen and 
Sun, 2021). Logistics cooperation allows the e-commerce platform to share its 

9 
 
resources, such as warehouses and vehicles, with suppliers to address the delivery 
problems caused by labour shortages and lockdowns (Shen and Sun, 2021). Hence, it 
enables the supply chains to absorb the logistical disruptions due to the pandemic (Choi 
et al., 2020). Logistics cooperation thus improves the physical flow in supply chains 
even if suppliers have limited logistics capability and capacity (Flynn et al., 2010). 
Cooperation also allows the supply chains to quickly redesign logistics networks and 
delivery processes, which can lead to the timely delivery of products (Shen and Sun, 
2021), enabling the supply chains to quickly recover from the pandemic. Therefore, we 
propose the following: 
H3: Logistics cooperation improves the resilience of an e-commerce platform’s supply 
chains. 
 Procurement automation refers to an e-commerce platform cooperating with 
suppliers to streamline its purchasing and automate its ordering processes (Liu et al., 
2016). Manual procurement is both time-consuming and error-prone (Shen and Sun, 
2021). Procurement automation increases a supply chain’s operational efficiency by 
eliminating redundant steps in the manual procurement process (Shah and Ward, 2007). 
Although researchers argue that it may make a supply chain vulnerable to disruptions 
(Kleindorfer and Saad, 2005), procurement automation allows the e-commerce 
platform to purchase products rapidly without negotiation, thereby helping the supply 
chains to respond to the exceptional demands caused by the COVID-19 pandemic (Shen 
and Sun, 2021). Procurement automation significantly expedites the supply chain 
processes and allows the supply chains to optimize inventory levels and fulfil growing 
demand, and hence it helps the supply chains to quickly recover from the pandemic 
(Wong et al., 2011, Shen and Sun, 2021). Therefore, we propose the following: 
H4: Procurement automation improves the resilience of an e-commerce platform’s 
supply chains. 
2.5 The impact of product flexibility on resilience 
Product flexibility has been viewed as a key capability for a supply chain to manage 
risks and develop resilience (Sheffi, 2007, Hosseini et al., 2019). Suppliers with high 

10 
 
product flexibility can frequently introduce new products (Sethi and Sethi, 1990, Sheffi 
and Rice, 2005). A high level of product flexibility allows demand changes caused by 
the COVID-19 pandemic to be absorbed by supply chains through adjusting product 
portfolios quickly (Juttner and Maklan, 2011, Sawyerr and Harrison, 2020). It thus 
helps an e-commerce platform’s supply chains to ensure the availability of products 
during the COVID-19 pandemic (Vokurka and O’Leary-Kelly, 2000). Moreover, 
suppliers with high product flexibility enable the e-commerce platform’s supply chains 
to reduce consumer waiting time by quickly launching a wide variety of products when 
facing unanticipated needs (Zhang et al., 2003). Thus, the e-commerce platform’s 
supply chains can quickly recover from the disruptions caused by the COVID-19 
pandemic (Scholten and Schilder, 2015, Shen and Sun, 2021). Therefore, we propose 
the following: 
H5: Product flexibility improves the resilience of an e-commerce platform’s supply 
chains. 
2.6 Moderating effects of product flexibility 
Integration improves the speed, quality and quantity of information and physical flows 
of the supply chains which allows the supply chains to address environmental 
uncertainties (Swink et al., 2007, Wong et al., 2011). It thus helps an e-commerce 
platform’s supply chains to recover from the COVID-19 pandemic by simultaneously 
responding to disruptions to demands and logistics (Shen and Sun, 2021, Ivanov, 2020). 
Suppliers with high product flexibility can modify their products to meet demand 
changes in a short period at a low cost (Zhang et al., 2003, Sawhney, 2006). Thus, 
product flexibility improves the effectiveness of the supply chain’s physical flows. It 
also allows suppliers to adjust design processes according to the market environment in 
a timely manner (Jain et al., 2013). Thus, it increases the value of the information flows 
as supply chains can quickly convert the information into changes in product designs 
to respond to the pandemic. Therefore, we argue that product flexibility positively 
moderates the impacts of integration on supply chain resilience. 

11 
 
 Information sharing using EDI allows suppliers and the e-commerce platform to 
exchange a large amount of information, such as inventory levels and demand 
forecasting, in real-time (Cao and Zhang, 2011). Product flexibility ensures that a 
supplier can effectively use the information to optimize product designs (Sawhney, 
2006), which helps the supply chains recover from the COVID-19 pandemic quickly 
(Sheffi and Rice, 2005). Thus, product flexibility can increase the benefit of the 
information flows in supply chains and hence it enhances the positive impact of 
information sharing on supply chain resilience. 
 Joint planning enables a supplier and the e-commerce platform to analyze the 
impact of the pandemic and devise recovery plans together (Cao and Zhang, 2011). It 
facilitates the supply chains to make sense of the market environment and co-create 
knowledge on how to respond to the COVID-19 pandemic (Swink et al., 2007). Product 
flexibility allows suppliers to quickly incorporate knowledge into product designs 
(Vokurka and O’Leary-Kelly, 2000). Furthermore, it enables the supply chains to use 
the knowledge to quickly and effectively reallocate R&D investment to cope with the 
disruptions caused by the pandemic (Shen and Sun, 2021). Thus, product flexibility 
allows the supply chains to take full advantage of the knowledge and hence it increases 
the positive impact of joint planning on supply chain resilience. 
 Logistics cooperation allows suppliers to quickly expand their logistics capacity 
and redesign delivery networks by using the e-commerce platform’s resources (Cao and 
Zhang, 2011). The effectiveness and speed of the logistics system depend on the degree 
to which the products match customer demands. Product flexibility enables the supply 
chains to quickly adjust product designs and hence it improves the effectiveness of the 
supply chains’ physical flows (Narasimhan et al., 2004). Thus, product flexibility can 
increase the benefits of supply chains’ logistics networks and hence it enhances the 
positive impact of logistics cooperation on supply chain resilience. 
 Procurement automation improves the speed and accuracy of the physical flows of 
the e-commerce platform’s supply chains (Wong et al., 2011, Shen and Sun, 2021). The 
effectiveness of procurement automation is determined by suppliers’ capabilities to 

12 
 
provide the right products at the right time according to purchasing orders. Suppliers 
with high product flexibility can quickly modify products (Zhang et al., 2003). As a 
result, product flexibility allows the supply chains to realize the full potential of 
procurement automation and hence it enhances the positive impact of procurement 
automation on supply chain resilience. Therefore, we propose the following: 
H6: Product flexibility enhances the positive impacts of integration on supply chain 
resilience. 
H6a: The positive impact of information sharing on supply chain resilience is stronger 
when a supplier has a higher level of product flexibility. 
H6b: The positive impact of joint planning on supply chain resilience is stronger when 
a supplier has a higher level of product flexibility. 
H6c: The positive impact of logistics cooperation on supply chain resilience is stronger 
when a supplier has a higher level of product flexibility. 
H6d: The positive impact of procurement automation on supply chain resilience is 
stronger when a supplier has a higher level of product flexibility.

3. RESEARCH METHOD 
3.1 Data 
We focus on the supply chains of a leading Chinese e-commerce platform (TK). TK’s 
supply chains provide an appropriate context for the investigation of the impacts of 
supply chain integration on the recovery from the COVID-19 pandemic. TK’s average 
order fulfilment rate was 0.93 in December 2019. Since then, it started to fall as China 
declared strict epidemic prevention and control measures. It plunged to 0.86 (almost 
dropped by 7.5%) in February 2020. After that, the average order fulfilment rate 
bounced back and returned to normal (0.93) by September 2020. This indicates that TK 
has recovered from the COVID-19 pandemic, demonstrating that it has established 
resilient supply chains. 
 TK also invests heavily in managing its supply chains and integrating with its 
suppliers. As an integrated retail giant, the “self-operated mode” is TK’s core business 
model, in which TK sources products directly from suppliers for its self-operated online 
stores that sell those products to consumers. TK actively manages inter-organizational 
processes with suppliers and normally has agreements with the suppliers for long-term 
cooperation. Specifically, TK develops sales and operations planning (S&OP) regularly. 
Suppliers who are involved in the joint business planning programme develop S&OP 
together with TK. TK places orders automatically or manually to suppliers according 
to S&OP in the procurement process and the suppliers need to confirm the orders with 
TK. Then, the suppliers prepare the products and move to the logistics process. Some 
suppliers use TK’s logistics service to complete the end-to-end delivery from their 
warehouses to TK’s facilities and TK is responsible for the delivery to consumers. 
Information related to the operational processes (e.g. planning, procurement, and 
logistics) is shared between TK and its suppliers. Suppliers who have an EDI 
connection share information with TK automatically in real-time. 
TK categorizes its products using a three-level system. Level-1 is the general 
product category and there are 17 level-1 product categories. Each level-1 product 
category includes several level-2 product categories and there are 248 level-2 product 

14 
 
categories. Each level-2 product category includes several level-3 product categories 
and there are 1672 level-3 product categories. For example, the air conditioner is a 
level-3 product category. Air conditioners, together with washing machines, 
refrigerators and others, form a level-2 product category (i.e. large appliances). Large 
appliances, together with commercial appliances, health care appliances and others, 
form a level-1 product category (i.e. home appliances). A supplier normally has more 
than one level-3 product category. Hence, we constructed a dataset based on the 
combination of a level-3 product category and a supplier and used the (level-3 product 
category, supplier) pair as a data point. 
The outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic and national lockdown started in January 
2020 in China. China’s composite PMI (purchasing managers’ index, with greater than 
50.0 indicating an expansion of the economy) was 53.4 in December 2019 and it 
dropped to 28.9 in February 2020, which was the worst during the pandemic (National 
Bureau of Statistics of China, 2020). PMI then bounced back to 55.1 in September 2020, 
which indicates that Chinese industrial output had recovered from the pandemic by then 
(National Bureau of Statistics of China, 2020). The changes in TK’s average order 
fulfilment rate also reflect similar trends. Therefore, to empirically investigate the 
impacts of integration practices on supply chain resilience, we collected monthly data 
from January 2019 to September 2020 from TK. We constructed our measure of supply 
chain resilience using data from February to September 2020, which captures the 
recovery from the pandemic, and our measures of supply chain integration and product 
flexibility using data from January to December 2019, which captures the normal 
situation before the pandemic. The data were collected from TK’s Big Data Platform. 
It includes several databases which store basic supplier information and transaction data 
with suppliers. After consulting staff in TK’s supply chain management and data 
analysis departments and with their help, we developed SQL code to extract data from 
the databases to create the dataset. 
Overall, the dataset initially contained 16652 (level-3 product category, supplier) 
pairs. We removed missing values and winsorized the data at the 1st and 99th percentiles 

15 
 
to reduce the impacts of extreme values. The final dataset included 9638 (level-3 
product category, supplier) pairs, which included 3328 suppliers and 1243 level-3 
product categories. All of the suppliers are domestic manufacturing companies. 
3.2 Measures 
3.2.1 Supply chain resilience 
We used the TTR of a supplier to measure the resilience of the supply chain between 
TK and the supplier (Simchi-Levi et al., 2014). A shorter TTR indicates that a supply 
chain has a higher level of resilience. TTR was constructed using the number of months 
it took since February 2020 for a supplier’s order fulfilment rate of a specific level-3 
product category to return to its normal level or above. The order fulfilment rate was 
defined as the percentage of the quantity of a level-3 product category received by TK’s 
warehouses from a supplier relative to the quantity confirmed by the supplier when 
receiving purchase orders of the level-3 product category from TK in a month. The 
normal level was measured as the monthly average order fulfilment rate of that supplier 
for the level-3 product category in 2019. Table 1 presents the mean and standard 
deviation (SD) of TTR in each level-1 product category.

3.2.2 Supply chain integration 

16 
 
Information sharing was measured by the application of EDI between a supplier and 
TK. EDI is the solution that facilitates the information exchange between suppliers and 
TK. Through an EDI connection, suppliers send and receive real-time purchase orders, 
order acknowledgements, shipment notifications, receipt acknowledgements, invoices, 
etc. Thus, the application of EDI represents the extent to which the supplier and TK 
mutually and quickly share their data. We used a dummy variable, EDI_flag, to measure 
whether a supplier has an EDI connection with TK till the end of 2019. EDI_flag equals 
one when a supplier and TK exchange information via EDI and zero otherwise. 
 TK initiated a joint business planning (JBP) program with its suppliers to develop 
business plans in which strategic alignments and operational processes were set in 
regular meetings. Sales and operations teams both from the suppliers and TK were 
involved in tackling the challenges in inventory, promotion and delivery to increase 
sales and profits and serve customers better. We used a dummy variable, JBP_flag, to 
measure whether a supplier and TK mutually create a joint business plan till the end of 
2019. JBP_flag equals one when a supplier was involved in the JBP program with TK 
and zero otherwise. 
 TK utilizes its incomparable logistics capabilities to provide suppliers with a 
network of transfer centres (TCs) located across the country. Suppliers can choose to 
deliver part or all of their products to TCs, and TK handles the subsequent logistics 
activities. Hence, suppliers that use this service to deliver a higher portion of products 
integrate their logistics processes with TK. Thus, we operationalized the logistics 
cooperation (TC_rate) as the monthly average of the percentage of the quantity 
delivered through TCs out of the TK’s total procurement quantity of a level-3 product 
category for a specific supplier in 2019. 
 TK places procurement orders with suppliers in two ways. First, TK manually 
places orders through employees in the purchasing department. Second, TK has 
developed an automatic order placement system (AOPS) through which it utilizes 
artificial intelligence to compute an optimal order quantity and time based on its 
massive stock of historical data on transactions with suppliers and external factors such 

17 
 
as holidays, weather, competitors’ promotions, etc. Suppliers can choose to use either 
the first or the second method, or a combination of both, to receive procurement orders. 
The higher the percentage of orders received from AOPS, the closer the integration 
between the supplier and TK. Thus, we measured procurement automation (AOC_rate) 
by the monthly average of the percentage of the number of orders placed by TK’s AOPS 
out of the total number of orders of a level-3 product category placed through both 
manual procurement and AOPS to a specific supplier in 2019. 
3.2.3 Product flexibility 
We used each supplier’s new product launch rate to measure its product flexibility (Jain 
et al., 2013). The new product launch rate (NPL_rate) was calculated by the monthly 
average of the percentage of the number of newly launched stock-keeping units (SKUs) 
out of the total number of SKUs of a level-3 product category that a supplier sold to TK 
in 2019. 
3.2.4 Control variables 
Firm size was included as a control variable since large firms tend to have more working 
capital and slack resources which are redundancy that may improve resilience (Sheffi, 
2007, Bode et al., 2011). We used the natural logarithm transformation of the registered 
capital of a supplier to measure the firm size (Registered_capital). The data was 
collected from QCC.com and Tianyancha.com both of which are officially licensed 
enterprise credit agencies. 
Since the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic, governments have taken different 
measures to tackle its negative impacts on the economy and social life. The measures 
also affect firms’ recovery from the pandemic. Thus, we used the stringency index and 
economic support index developed by the Oxford COVID-19 Government Response 
Tracker (OxCGRT) to control the effects of governments’ responses to the pandemic. 
The stringency index measures the extent of closures and containment in each region 
and the economic support index captures the extent of measures taken by the 
government to mitigate the influence of the COVID-19 pandemic on the economy (Hale 
et al., 2021). OxCGRT has developed a database about the Chinese provincial 

18 
 
governments’ policy responses to the COVID-19 pandemic since January 2020 on a 
daily basis (Zhang et al., 2021). We calculated the daily average of the stringency index 
(STR_index) and economic support index (ECS_index) from February to September 
2020 for each supplier according to where the supplier is located. 
During the COVID-19 pandemic, we can observe a surge in the demand for some 
products like medical products and food while a collapse in the demand for other 
products like household appliances and automobile supplies (Shen and Sun, 2021). The 
suppliers with increasing demand may have a longer TTR because the increasing 
demand combined with reduced supply capacity due to the pandemic may make it 
difficult for the suppliers to recover the order fulfilment rate. Thus, demand change 
(DCG) was included as a control variable. DCG was measured by the natural logarithm 
transformation of the absolute value of the range of the monthly average planned 
purchase quantity (PPQ) between 2020 and 2019. The PPQ is the quantity of a level-3 
product category TK planned to purchase from a specific supplier in a month. We 
calculated DCG using the data from February to September in 2019 and 2020 to control 
the seasonal effects of the demand. DCG was calculated as: 
𝐷𝐷𝐷𝐷𝐷𝐷= ln[abs(mean(PPQ from Feb. to Sept. 2020)
−mean(PPQ from Feb. to Sept. 2019))]. 
As we observed in TK, the majority of sales come from direct searches rather than 
casual browsing. Thus, brand search volume was used as a proxy for brand reputation. 
A supplier with a high reputation could have a strong capability to respond to 
disruptions (Bode et al., 2011). Brand search volume (BSV) was operationalized as the 
monthly average of the aggregate number of valid brand search records of a level-3 
product category of a specific supplier in 2019. 
The suppliers were classified into four regions (i.e. Northeastern China, Eastern 
China, Western China, and Central China) according to the regulation of the National 
Bureau of Statistics of China to control the regional effects. Three dummy variables 
were used to capture the four regions. 
As different sectors recover from the COVID-19 pandemic at different speeds 

19 
 
(Shen and Sun, 2021), we included the level-1 product categories, which were measured 
by 16 dummy variables, as control variables. 
The data for all variables were collected from TK’s Big Data Platform, except for 
firm size (collected from https://www.qcc.com/ and https://www.tianyancha.com/) and

4.2 Results 
The robust regression method is used to estimate the models. Robust regression assigns 
appropriate weights to observations to ensure that outliers do not have undue influence 
on the results, and hence it can derive stable estimates (Ben-Jebara and Modi, 2021). 
The robust regression results are presented in Model 1, Model 2, and Model 3 (Table 
3). In Model 1, we estimate the coefficients of the control variables. Next, we add the 
main effects of the integration practices (EDI_flag, JBP_flag, TC_rate, AOC_rate) and 
product flexibility (NPL_rate) in Model 2. In Model 3, we test the moderating effects 
of product flexibility by adding the interaction terms between NPL_rate and EDI_flag, 
JBP_flag, TC_rate and AOC_rate. 
Compared with Model 1 (Table 3), Model 2 (Table 3) shows an increase in R-
squared (∆𝑅𝑅_𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠= 0.55%, 𝑝𝑝= 0.000), indicating that the integration practices 
and product flexibility significantly increase the explanatory power over Model 1 which 
only includes the control variables. Compared with Model 2 (Table 3), Model 3 (Table 
3) shows an increase in R-squared value ( ∆𝑅𝑅_𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠𝑠= 0.12%, 𝑝𝑝= 0.017 ), 
indicating that the moderating effects of product flexibility significantly increase the 
explanatory power over Model 2 which includes the control variables and the main 
effects. The results of Model 1 (Table 3) show that demand change significantly 
increases TTR while firm size and brand search volume significantly reduce it. The 
impacts of stringency index and economic support index on TTR are not significant. 
The findings also reveal that level-1 product categories have significant impacts on TTR 
whereas the impacts of regional effects on TTR are not significant. 
 The results of Model 2 (Table 3) show that the coefficients of information sharing 
( 𝛽𝛽1 = −0.11, 𝑝𝑝= 0.007 ), joint planning ( 𝛽𝛽2 = −0.14, 𝑝𝑝= 0.050 ), and logistics 
cooperation (𝛽𝛽3 = −0.04, 𝑝𝑝= 0.002) are significant and negative, which indicates that 
they reduce TTR and hence improve resilience. Thus, H1, H2 and H3 are supported. 
However, the coefficient of procurement automation ( 𝛽𝛽4 = 0.05, 𝑝𝑝= 0.000 ) is 
significant and positive, which indicates that it increases TTR and hence reduces 
resilience. Thus, H4 is not supported. Furthermore, the coefficient of product flexibility 

23 
 
(𝛽𝛽5 = 0.05, 𝑝𝑝= 0.000 ) is significant and positive, which indicates that suppliers’ 
product flexibility increases TTR and hence reduces resilience. Thus, H5 is not 
supported. 
 The results of Model 3 (Table 3) show that the coefficients of the interaction terms 
between product flexibility (NPL_rate) and EDI_flag ( 𝛼𝛼1 = −0.12, 𝑝𝑝= 0.040 ), 
JBP_flag ( 𝛼𝛼2 = −0.21, 𝑝𝑝= 0.013 ) and TC_rate (𝛼𝛼3 = −0.02, 𝑝𝑝= 0.054) are 
negative and significant. Therefore, the negative impacts of information sharing, joint 
planning and logistics cooperation on TTR are strengthened when a supplier has a high 
level of product flexibility. As a result, product flexibility enhances the impact of 
information sharing, joint planning and logistics cooperation on resilience. Thus, H6a, 
H6b and H6c are supported. As the interaction between product flexibility and 
procurement automation is not significant, H6d is not supported. The moderating 
effects of product flexibility on information sharing, joint planning and logistics 
cooperation are illustrated in Figure 2.

increasing 1% on TC_rate could help a supply chain reduce TTR by 1.2+3.3+4.2=8.7 
days, which is critical and significant to compete and gain competitive advantages 
during the COVID-19 pandemic.

6. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS 
We find that information sharing, joint planning and logistics cooperation reduce TTR whereas 
procurement automation increases TTR. Relying on historical data, algorithms and past best 
practices, procurement automation can expedite the procurement processes and shorten the 
response time of a supply chain (Shah and Ward, 2007). It may help a supply chain recover 
from high-probability, low-impact disruptions. However, as Srai et al. (2019) suggested, one 
of the major challenges in the automation of procurement relates to forecasting uncertainty. 
The COVID-19 pandemic is a low-probability, high-impact disruption, which results in 
significant forecasting errors of artificial intelligence systems using algorithms based on the 
analyses of historical data (Shen and Sun, 2021). Thus, during the COVID-19 pandemic, 
procurement automation creates inaccurate procurement orders, especially for products with 
exceptional demand, which leads to a mismatch between supply and demand and hence 
prolongs TTR. Our results echo Kleindorfer and Saad’s (2005) argument that the automation 
process may increase efficiency but make a supply chain vulnerable to unpredictable 
disruptions and provide empirical support to Stulz’s (2008) assertion that the overreliance on 
historical data weakens supply chain resilience. 
 Our findings show a significant and positive effect of product flexibility on TTR, which 
indicates that suppliers with high product flexibility need more time to recover. One possible 

30 
 
explanation is that the COVID-19 pandemic significantly increases the complexity of the 
environment to which a firm’s supply chain needs to adapt (Ivanov, 2020). Firms with high 
product flexibility may require external resources such as supply chain partners’ engineers and 
R&D facilities to cope with such environmental changes (Jain et al., 2013). However, during 
the first wave of the COVID-19 pandemic, China took a strict and unprecedented lockdown 
policy in most provinces, which leads to the disruption of many supply chains and reduces the 
external resources available to firms. As a result, firms with high product flexibility may find 
that although they can respond quickly and successfully to past disruptions, they cannot easily 
acquire resources from external partners to adapt to the environmental complexity caused by 
the COVID-19 pandemic (Reeves et al., 2020). Thus, the firms may need more time as they 
must unlearn their past successful experiences and develop new practices and systems, and 
hence it takes longer for them to recover from the COVID-19 pandemic. Our results underline 
that the effects of product flexibility are contingent on the environment, especially the abnormal 
situation caused by unpredictable changes (Narasimhan et al., 2004, Sawhney, 2006). 
The analysis also indicates that demand change is positively associated with TTR. Some 
product categories (e.g. medical and fresh products) exhibited growth in demand during the 
COVID-19 pandemic. An increase in demand within a short period may add a lot of pressure 
on a supply chain, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic when the supply may be 
disturbed due to lockdowns, and hence it significantly prolongs TTR. Our result provides 
empirical support to Shen and Sun’s (2021) observations that product categories with growing 
demands show a decrease in resilience performance during the COVID-19 pandemic. 
 The result shows that firm size reduces TTR, which supports the argument that redundancy 
helps firms develop resilience (Sheffi, 2007). We also find the level-1 product categories have 
significant impacts on TTR, which supports the observation that industries have different 
recovery speeds (Shen and Sun, 2021). We find that the coefficients of the level-1 product 
categories of sports and outdoor products, pet supplies, food and home furnishing materials are 
significant and positive (Model 1, Table 3), indicating that they need a longer time to recover 
compared with the baseline level-1 product category (i.e. electronic and digital products). The 
finding is consistent with the results reported in Table 1, which show that these level-1 product 
categories have longer TTR compared with the others. We plot the monthly quantity received 
by TK of the level-1 product categories from January to September 2020 (Figure 3). The figure 
shows that these product categories all experienced booming demands since February 2020 
and the product categories of sports and outdoor products, pet supplies, food and home 
furnishing materials all generally have higher demand growth rates compared with electronic 

31 
 
and digital products. Therefore, product categories with high-speed demand growth may take 
a longer time to recover.

6.1 Research implications 
This study contributes to the supply chain integration and resilience literature in three ways. 
First, in the extant literature, supply chain resilience was mainly measured using theoretical 
and modelling approaches (Belhadi et al., 2021) or as a general capability using multi-item 
scales (Brandon-Jones et al., 2014, Ambulkar et al., 2015). This study is among the first 
empirical research to analyze supply chain resilience using operational indicators. Based on the 
transaction data between suppliers and an e-commerce platform, we construct three variables 
(i.e. TTR, extent of disruption, and revenue at risk) to measure different aspects of supply chain 
resilience (Behzadi et al., 2020). The method provides valuable insights for future research by 
developing practical measures of supply chain resilience. It also helps managers to evaluate the 
resilience of their supply chains. 
Second, few studies have empirically explored the effects of individual integration 
practices on supply chain resilience in the context of e-commerce platforms. Most of the extant 
literature conceptualizes supply chain integration as a holistic concept and focuses on the 
combined effects of different practices (Brusset and Teller, 2017, Liu and Lee, 2018). The 

32 
 
holistic view of supply chain integration may create confounding results as it considers 
integration as a black box that entangles various practices. Our study develops operational 
indicators for four integration practices based on an e-commerce platform’s supply chains 
which is a useful supplement to the extant literature (Liu and Lee, 2018, Fan et al., 2020, Juttner 
and Maklan, 2011). Our results show that information sharing, joint planning and logistics 
cooperation significantly shorten the time to recovery from the COVID-19 pandemic, which 
corroborates the conclusions drawn by Sarkar and Kumar (2015) and Sawyerr and Harrison 
(2020). However, procurement automation exhibits the opposite effect. These findings thus 
provide a possible explanation for the inconsistent findings on the impacts of integration on 
performance outcomes and contribute to the literature by providing empirical evidence that 
different integration practices have distinct effects on supply chain resilience (Duong and 
Chong, 2020, Mackelprang et al., 2014, Ataseven and Nair, 2017). 
Third, we add to the literature by answering the call for more empirical studies to examine 
the factors that influence the development of resilience (Kamalahmadi and Parast, 2016, 
Manhart et al., 2020). Our results show that the effects of integration on supply chain resilience 
are strengthened when a firm has a higher level of product flexibility, which is consistent with 
the findings of meta-analyses that the relationships between supply chain integration and 
performance outcomes are influenced by moderators (Mackelprang et al., 2014, Leuschner et 
al., 2013, Chang et al., 2016) and the existing empirical evidence that the impacts of 
organizational capabilities on resilience development are influenced by contingencies 
(Ambulkar et al., 2015, Azadegan et al., 2020). The findings thus highlight the importance of 
considering the joint effects on integration and firm capabilities when investigating the 
development of supply chain resilience (Ataseven and Nair, 2017). This study also provides 
empirical evidence on how product flexibility affects the development of resilience (Shen and 
Sun, 2021). It reveals that product flexibility could be a double-edged sword as it is negatively 
associated with supply chain resilience but allows firms to fully leverage the benefits of 
integration on supply chain resilience. 
6.2 Managerial implications 
While many researchers and managers agree on the importance of resilience, there are different 
views on how to respond to and recover from the COVID-19 pandemic (Reeves et al., 2020, 
Choi et al., 2020). Our results provide substantial evidence of the benefits of integration for 
developing supply chain resilience. With the continuing mutation of coronavirus, the negative 
impact of the COVID-19 pandemic will not end in the foreseeable future and firms must adapt 
to the “new normal”. Countries around the world have taken different measures to control the 

33 
 
spread of the COVID-19 pandemic and firms should be meticulous to adopt appropriate 
practices that can help them recover from the pandemic. Our results show that integration can 
shorten TTR, reduce the extent of disruption and alleviate the revenue at risk, which indicates 
that integration is a powerful tool to cope with the COVID-19 pandemic. Our results provide 
evidence for managers that integration can help firms develop resilience and recover from 
supply chain disruptions (Belhadi et al., 2021). However, managers must be aware that various 
integration practices may have different effects on resilience. 
Our findings show that the practices of information sharing, joint planning and logistics 
cooperation can reduce TTR. This indicates that improving information and physical flows in 
supply chains is essential for developing supply chain resilience. Additionally, managers must 
be cautious when automating procurement with partners. Although it may increase supply 
chain efficiency, procurement automation reduces a firm’s responsiveness to the COVID-19 
pandemic. Thus, managers should be alert to the side effects of using historical data and 
algorithms when recovering from disruptions. When facing a supply chain disruption such as 
the COVID-19 pandemic, there should be a substitute for procurement automation in which 
the orders created by digital technologies can be fine-tuned by incorporating up-to-date 
knowledge about the dramatic changes in the environment. Therefore, managers should 
carefully evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of different integration practices concerning 
the nature of the disruption in order to decide how and where to invest their limited resources. 
Our results demonstrate that product flexibility has mixed impacts on supply chain 
resilience. Although a supplier’s product flexibility prolongs TTR, it positively moderates the 
effects of information sharing, joint planning and logistics cooperation on supply chain 
resilience. Thus, to enjoy the extra benefits of these integration practices when recovering from 
the COVID-19 pandemic, managers are encouraged to use product flexibility as a criterion 
when selecting and evaluating suppliers. 
 The COVID-19 pandemic brings disruptions in demand, supply and logistics to firms in 
almost every country (Ivanov, 2020). During the pandemic, sales on Amazon and other e-
commerce platforms grow substantially and firms also collaborate with platforms to shift to 
online channels (Sodhi and Tang, 2021). We focus on integration, TTR and product flexibility. 
These concepts and their measurement are developed based on existing literature and the 
experiences of global firms (Simchi-Levi et al., 2014, Cao and Zhang, 2011, Ataseven and Nair, 
2017, Zhang et al., 2003). Chinese e-commerce platforms also tend to learn and localize the 
business models, digital technologies and management practices adopted by global leaders 
such as Amazon. Therefore, although China has a distinct business and institutional 

34 
 
environment, the experiences of the Chinese e-commerce platform on how to develop 
resilience and recover from the pandemic are broadly generalizable to firms globally (Reeves 
et al., 2020). 
6.3 Limitations and future research 
Although this study provides several implications to the literature, researchers should be aware 
of the boundary conditions of the study. First, our study focuses on an e-commerce platform’s 
supply chains. Most of the suppliers are domestic consumer product manufacturers. Therefore, 
it should be cautious in generalizing the findings to the supply chains with global suppliers and 
industrial product manufacturers. For example, supply chain relationships and culture may play 
significant roles in influencing the resilience of such deep and complex supply chains. Hence, 
future research may test the research model in global manufacturing supply chains and 
empirically examine how social capital and cultural environment influence the impacts of 
integration practices on supply chain resilience. Second, we focus on the COVID-19 pandemic, 
which is a low-probability, high-impact disruption. Therefore, it should be cautious in 
generalizing the findings to high-probability, low-impact disruptions. For example, when 
facing high-probability, low-impact disruptions, procurement automation may help a supply 
chain recover quickly. This is because such disruptions will not lead to exceptional demand 
and dramatic changes in markets and hence firms can still make accurate forecasting using 
historical data and existing algorithms. Future studies could replicate this study by empirically 
investigating the impacts of integration practices on supply chain resilience under the condition 
of high-probability, low-impact disruptions. Third, we incorporate only product flexibility as a 
moderator in the model. As flexibility is a complex concept including multiple dimensions, 
future research is suggested to explore the impact of other dimensions of flexibility (e.g. mix, 
volume and delivery flexibility) on resilience. Furthermore, other firms’ operational 
capabilities (e.g. quality and delivery) may also influence supply chain resilience. Future 
research could empirically investigate the joint effects of integration and operational 
capabilities on supply chain resilience. Fourth, we find that level-1 product categories have 
different recovery speeds. Future research could empirically investigate how product and 
industry characteristics influence the impacts of integration practices on supply chain resilience. 
Fifth, we use firm size as a proxy for redundancy. Future research could use direct measures 
such as the amount of inventory, working capital and slack resources to model redundancy and 
explore the joint effects of integration and redundancy on supply chain resilience. Last but not 
least, we focus on the impact of integration practices on supply chain resilience during the 
pandemic. Future research could empirically compare the impacts of integration practices on 

35 
 
supply chain resilience in pre-, during- and post-pandemic periods.

APPENDIX. Summary of variables and measures 
Variables 
Measures 
Supply chain resilience (TTR) 
TTR is the number of months it took since February 2020 for the supplier’s order fulfilment rate of a specific level-3 
product category to return to its normal level or above. 
Information sharing (EDI_flag) 
EDI_flag is a dummy variable. It equals one when a supplier and TK exchange information via EDI and zero otherwise. 
Joint planning (JBP_flag) 
JBP_flag is a dummy variable. It equals one when a supplier was involved in the JBP program with TK and zero otherwise. 
Logistics cooperation (TC_rate) 
TC_rate is the monthly average of the percentage of the quantity delivered through TCs out of the TK’s total procurement 
quantity of a level-3 product category for a specific supplier in 2019. 
Procurement automation (AOC_rate) 
AOC_rate is the monthly average of the percentage of orders placed by TK’s AOPS of a level-3 product category to a supplier 
out of the total number of orders placed of the level-3 product category through both manual procurement and AOPS to a 
specific supplier in 2019. 
Product flexibility (NPL_rate) 
NPL_rate is the monthly average of the percentage of the number of newly launched SKUs out of the total number of SKUs 
of a level-3 product category of a specific supplier sold to TK in 2019. 
Firm size (Registered_capital) 
Registered_capital is the natural logarithm transformation of the registered capital of a supplier, collected from QCC.com 
and Tianyancha.com. 
Stringency index (STR_index) 
STR_index is the daily average of the stringency index from February to September 2020 for each supplier according to where 
the supplier is located, calculated from indicators of the Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker (OxCGRT) 
database. 
Economic support index 
(ECS_index) 
ECS_index is the daily average of the economic support index from February to September 2020 for each supplier according 
to where the supplier is located, calculated from indicators of the Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker 
(OxCGRT) database. 
Demand change (DCG) 
𝐷𝐷𝐷𝐷𝐷𝐷= ln[abs൫mean(PPQ from Feb. to Sept. 2020) −mean(PPQ from Feb. to Sept. 2019)൯]. 
Brand search volume (BSV) 
BSV is the monthly average of the aggregate number of valid brand search records of a level-3 product category of a specific 
supplier in 2019. 
Level-1 product category (PC) 
Sixteen dummy variables are used to measure the 17 level-1 product categories (Table 1). 
Region (region) 
Three dummy variables are used to measure four regions (i.e. Northeastern China, Eastern China, Western China, and Central 
China) according to where the supplier is located.
