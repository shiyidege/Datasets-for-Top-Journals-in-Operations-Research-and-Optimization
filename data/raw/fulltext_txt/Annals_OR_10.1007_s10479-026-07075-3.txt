Abstract
We investigate changepoints in the cross-section of stock returns using an ensemble of dedi-
cated unsupervised learning methods. Our large-scale study reveals a sustained incidence of
changepoints in the mean, variance, and distribution of daily returns. This ﬁnding is robust to
the choice of the changepoint detection method. We also ﬁnd that a six-factor empirical asset
pricing model partially explained these results until the mid-1990s, but its explanatory power
has weakened considerably. Moreover, GARCH models do not account for changepoints in
the variance of the residuals. This striking ﬁnding indicates that conditional heteroskedas-
ticity is not connected to changepoints, at least not in a way standard econometric models
capture. A further study on monthly data conﬁrms that changepoints are a robust feature of
the cross-section. Predictably, the number of detected changepoints is smaller than with daily
data, yet these changepoints persist across methods, especially for variance and distribution.
Therefore, changepoints undermine the interpretability of constant-parameter Fama–French-
style factor models on long samples: least-squares estimates collapse multiple regimes into
a single set of parameters, and residual-based inference weakens. Accordingly, changepoint
detection should be incorporated as a standard component of model validation and recalibra-
tion.

1 Introduction
The study of structural breaks in times series is a mainstay of the ﬁnance and economics
literature. Stock and Watson (1996) investigate the stability of time series, Bai and Perron
(1998, 2003) study parameter stability in linear models, Hamilton (1989, 1990); Ang and
Bekaert (2002); Koop and Potter (2007) use Markov regime-switching processes to model
time series instability, and Shiryaev et al. (2014, 2015); Lleo et al. (2022) develop a stochastic
disorder model to detect asset price bubbles. Beyond economics and ﬁnance, the widespread
interest in detecting abrupt changes in the shape of the probability distribution or the value of
its parameters has sparked an explosion in the number, range, and sophistication of statistical
machine learning techniques, collectively known as changepoint detection methods.
From an operations research perspective, changepoint detection is naturally posed as a
sequential decision problem: one observes a stochastic process over time and decides when
to signal a regime shift so as to balance false alarms against detection delay under an explicit
loss function. This viewpoint originates in statistical process control. Shewhart’s control
charts (Shewhart, 1931, 1939) formalized online monitoring for quality assurance, while
Wald’s sequential hypothesis testing (Wald, 1945; Wolfowitz & Wald, 1948) introduced
optimality criteria for decisions made in real time as data arrive. Page’s CUSUM procedure
(Page, 1954) provided a canonical quickest-detection rule and remains central in modern
streaming applications. Disorder-detection models, developed by Kolmogorov and advanced
by Shiryaev (Shiryaev, 2010), further connected changepoint problems to optimal stopping
and dynamic programming, clarifying how optimal detection depends on both the assumed
dynamics and the decision costs.
Despite a mature methodological literature and a wide range of applications (e.g., Barnett
& Onnela, 2019; Fearnhead & Rigaill, 2016; Mackintosh et al., 2019), changepoint detection
has yet to gain wide acceptance in ﬁnance and economics. By bringing a changepoint lens
to the cross-section of stock returns, we provide a data-driven way to locate and characterize
structural breaks that matter for equity market modeling, and to inform how models should
be speciﬁed, calibrated, and stress-tested across regimes.
In this paper, we apply four families of discrete-time unsupervised learning changepoint
detection models to investigate the frequency and origin of changepoints observed in the
cross-section of stock returns. The question of frequency is crucial for econometric models.
While a low incidence of changepoints can easily be contained, and might even be safely
ignored in some instances, a high incidence needs to be addressed explicitly in the formulation
and estimation of asset prices and returns models. Moreover, understanding the origin of
changepoints could provide insights into the formation and behavior of asset returns and
prices. Are changepoints an empirical fact of the cross-section of stock returns? If so, do
they matter for empirical asset pricing models? Our paper investigates these two essential
questions.
We focus on unsupervised learning methods because these methods are best suited for
data exploration. Unsupervised learning does not require a training set of labeled data, unlike
supervised learning. However, assessing the prediction accuracy of unsupervised learning
algorithms poses a signiﬁcant challenge because the absence of a target makes it impossible to
use loss functions to estimate the prediction error. We address this problem with an ensemble
learning approach on a group of heterogeneous models, or learners. By diversifying across
assumptions and methodologies, ensemble learning achieves more accurate and robust results
than any single model. It also minimizes the risk of false discovery. Our ensemble includes
algorithms from four families of discrete-time unsupervised learning changepoint models:

We conduct a large-scale empirical study investigating 21,596 publicly traded U.S. stocks
over 60 years, from July 1, 1963, to December 31, 2022. We perform changepoint detec-
tion on the daily excess return of these stocks using 30 changepoint model speciﬁcations.
Then, we investigate whether a six-factor empirical asset pricing model and conditional het-
eroskedasticity explain the existence of these changepoints. The six factors comprise Fama
and French’s ﬁve factors and Carahart’s momentum (Carhart, 1997; Fama & French, 2015).
Although hundreds more factors have been studied to date, the Fama-French factors and
Carahart’s momentum are among the most extensively researched. Finally, we address con-
ditional heteroskedasticity with three GARCH models: standard GARCH (Bollerslev, 1986;
Taylor, 1986), GJR GARCH (Glosten et al., 1993), and eGARCH (Nelson, 1991).
Our research uncovers ﬁve sets of novel ﬁndings. First, our investigation reveals the fre-
quent occurrence of changepoints in the distribution of stock returns. This ﬁnding holds
cross-sectionally over the sample of stocks and chronologically over the entire 60-year hori-
zon. Moreover, this ﬁnding is robust to the choice of the changepoint detection method. All 30
speciﬁcations of changepoint detection models identify substantial instances of changepoints
in the distribution of stock returns.

Second, the changepoint detection methods provide solid evidence for changepoints in
the mean, variance, and entire distribution of returns. The results also reveal that the number
of changepoints detected in the mean shows the greatest variation between changepoints
detection methods. Those for the variance are the most stable.
Third, we discover that the six-factor empirical asset pricing model explained a substantial
number of the changepoints detected in the cross-section of stock returns between 1963 and
the mid-1990s. 90% of the changepoint model speciﬁcations rejected the null hypothesis that
the average number of changepoints in the residual of the empirical asset pricing model is
higher than that in the stocks’ excess returns. However, the empirical asset pricing model’s
explanatory power has gradually weakened since the second half of the 1990s. Interestingly,
this reversal occurred in the decade when research into empirical asset pricing models gained
popularity due in no small part to works by Fama and French (1992, 1993) and Carhart
(1997).
Fourth, we ﬁnd that the GARCH models fail to describe the effect of changepoints in
the variance of the residuals. Adding a GARCH component to the empirical asset pricing
model does not increase the number of changepoints the empirical model explains. Even
more surprising, the GARCH component leads to a signiﬁcant decrease in the number of
changepoints explained. This trend is noticeable starting in the 1990s, consistent with the
loss of signiﬁcance of the empirical asset pricing model.
Fifth, using monthly data from 1963 to 2022, we ﬁnd that changepoints remain a robust
feature of the cross-section of U.S. stock returns. Predictably, the number of detected
changepoints is smaller than with daily data, yet these changepoints persist across meth-
ods, especially for variance and distributional shifts. Importantly, even after adjusting for
standard Fama–French factors using both OLS and robust regressions, a substantial share of
changepoints remains. These results highlight that structural breaks are not merely artifacts
of high-frequency noise but reﬂect persistent regime shifts relevant to trading, asset pricing,
and portfolio management.
These ﬁve ﬁndings show that changepoints are an essential empirical fact of the cross-
section of stock returns. They also suggest that empirical asset pricing models no longer
explain changepoints. Therefore, it is necessary to apply changepoint detection methods
before implementing empirical asset pricing models.
Our work extends the literature in four main ways. First, this study is a comprehensive
investigationofchangepointsandchangepointdetectionmethodsonthecross-sectionofstock
returns. Aggarwal et al. (1999) use an iterated CUSUM algorithm to detect abrupt changes in
the variance of returns in emerging stock markets. Adams and MacKay (2007) demonstrated
their Bayesian algorithm on daily price returns on the Dow-Jones Industrial Average index
between 1972 and 1975. Loschi et al. (2007) apply their predictive product partition to the
ENDESA stock series between 1987 to 1994. Focusing on macroeconomic variables, Koop
and Potter showed that U.S GDP and inﬂation exhibit frequent changepoints (Koop & Potter,
2007). Recently, Kim et al. (2022) tested their Kruskal-Wallis Iterative CUSUM method on
32 ﬁnancial time-series, including stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities, on 18 years of
daily data from 2001 to 2020. Differently, Shiryaev et al. (2014, 2015) and Lleo et al. (2022)
use continuous-time supervised changepoint detection methods on the mean of the log return
process to construct trading strategies around asset pricing bubbles and crashes. Unlike these
prior works, we construct an ensemble of discrete-time unsupervised learning changepoint
detection methods on the mean, variance, and distribution to investigate the incidence and
origin of changepoints in the cross-section of U.S stock returns.
Our work also relates to Smith and Timmermann (2021), who propose a prediction method
for stock returns that detects structural breaks and corrects their effect on prediction models.

Similar to our work, their study concerns abrupt changes in a panel of stock returns. How-
ever, the objectives, methods, and ﬁndings differ. While Smith and Timmermann focus on
large structural breaks common to the cross-section of monthly stock returns, we investigate
changepoints in the mean, variance, and distribution of daily returns for each individual share.
Smith and Timmermann apply a single parametric method, whereas we adopt an ensemble
approach involving four families of established parametric and non-parametric changepoint
detection models. Finally, Smith and Timmermann ﬁnd evidence of 8 large structural breaks
whereas we documented from tenths to thousands of changepoints. Thus, our study signiﬁ-
cantly expands the scope of research questions and methods linking changepoint detection
and ﬁnance theory.
Second, our work brings new insights into heteroskedasticity and changepoints in the lin-
ear models underpinning empirical asset pricing models. Our results suggest that empirical
asset pricing models have a weakening explanatory power. Hence, changepoints may cause
structural breaks in coefﬁcient estimates, such as those detected in parameter stability tests
(Bai & Perron, 1998, 2003). Similarly, GARCH models do not capture the heteroskedasticity
detected by unsupervised changepoint models. Therefore, changepoint detection is an essen-
tial complement to existing econometric techniques that every researcher and practitioner
needs to adopt.
Third, changepoint detection on the variance of a ﬁtted model’s residuals provides a
powerful alternative to general heteroskedasticity tests, such as the Goldfeld–Quandt test.
Changepoint models automatically identify the number and location of changepoints, a deci-
sive advantage over general heteroskedasticity tests. They also examine the dynamics of the
residuals to convey valuable information on the nature and structure of heteroskedasticity.
Finally, our results add to the rapidly expanding literature on the application of machine
learning to empirical asset pricing models, including Gu et al. (2020), Karolyi and
Van Nieuwerburgh (2020), Bianchi et al. (2021), Giglio et al. (2022), and Chen et al.
(2023). While these papers focus on applying supervised learning technique to improve asset
return predictions, our work is unique in emphasizing unsupervised learning techniques and
exploratory data analysis.
2 Methodology
This section describes the four families of changepoint models we set out to implement,
proposes explanations within empirical asset pricing and econometric models, and introduces
the data that we use in our analysis. We refer the reader to the Online Appendix for a detailed
mathematical presentation of the changepoint models.
2.1 Changepoint detection methods
Changepoint detection methods are statistical machine learning techniques designed to detect
abrupt changes in a time series (Yt)t=1,... given observed data y1:T := {y1, . . . yT }. A change-
point occurs at time τ if the statistical properties of the distribution of the sub-sequences
{y1, y2, . . . , yτ−1} and {yτ, yτ+1, . . . , yT } differ. With multiple changepoints, we look for a
partition ρ1:T of the set {1, 2, . . . , T } such that the statistical properties of the time series,
(Yt)t=1,... remain constant within each sub-sequence of the partition but change between
sub-sequences. Mathematically, the partition ρ1:T := {i0, i1, i2, . . . , ib} is a strictly increas-
ing sub-sequence of {1, 2, . . . , T } such that 0 =: i0 < i1 < i2 < . . . < ib := T . Here,
123

Annals of Operations Research
i0 < i1 < i2 < . . . < ib are the end points of the subsequence, so the (b −1) changepoints
are the points i1 + 1, i2 + 1, . . . , ib−1 + 1.
We select a heterogeneous ensemble of four complementary families of unsupervised
changepoint detection methods. This choice is neither exhaustive nor fully representative of
the diversity of unsupervised changepoint detection algorithms. However, these families are
well-known, extensively studied, and accurate. Crucially, they differ in their assumptions and
statistical methods. This diversity helps reduce the risk of false discovery. The four families
are Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), Likelihood Ratio Methods (LRMs), Change Point
Methods (CPMs), and Bayesian Methods (BMs).
HMMs assume that the distribution of the time series (Yt)t=1,... is conditioned on a hid-
den state variable (St)t=1,.... This state variable is in one of N distinct states or regimes
S1, S2, . . . , SN at time t (Rabiner, 1989; Rabiner & Juang, 1993). Changepoints correspond
to state transition times. The transitions occur according to a discrete ﬁrst-order Markov
Chain with transition probability matrix A = (ai j), i, j = 1, . . . , N. Given a number of
states N and a sequence of T observed values y1:T = {y1, y2, . . . , yT }, HMMs aim to
estimate the transition probability matrix A = (ai j), the parameters of the conditional distri-
butions Yt | Sn, n = 1 . . . , N, and the historical sequence of states s1:T = {s1, s2, . . . , sT }.
We assume that the conditional distribution of Yt | Sn is Gaussian and estimate the HMMs
for N = 1, . . . , 8 states. Then we compute the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), Han-
nan–QuinnInformationCriterion(HQIC),andBayesianInformationCriterion(BIC)toselect
the optimal number of states. The single-state model (N = 1) assumes the time series comes
from a single Gaussian distribution, with parameters set to the overall sample mean and
variance. This minimal, transition-free model is a baseline for evaluating the other HMMs.
We implement the HMMs using the R package depmixS4 (Visser & Speekenbrink, 2010).
LRMs apply a penalized likelihood ratio test to determine the changepoints’ number
and location. We consider both At Most One Changepoint (AMOC) detection and multiple
changepoints, subject to three penalties: AIC, HQIC, and BIC. We implement these LRMs
using three standard algorithms: Binary Segmentation (BinSeg), Segmented Neighborhood
(SegNeigh), and Pruned Exact Linear Time (PELT). BinSeg provides a fast approximation
to the solution by repeatedly splitting the search space in two, each split corresponding to a
changepoint (Edwards & Cavalli-Sforza, 1965). SegNeigh relies on dynamic programming
to provide an exact solution, but it is considerably slower than BinSeg (Auger & Lawrence,
1989). The more recent PELT algorithm introduces a pruning step within the dynamic pro-
gramming procedure to produce an exact solution faster than SegNeigh (Killick et al., 2012).
We use the R packages changepoint and changepoint.np to implement these like-
lihood ratio models ofﬂine (Haynes & Haynes, 2022; Killick & Eckley, 2014).
The CPM framework by Hawkins et al. (2003) applies standard parametric and non-
parametric tests to detect single and multiple changepoints. In this paper, we consider eight
different tests. The ﬁrst three are parametric tests under a Gaussian assumption: a t-test for
changepoints in the mean, Bartlett test for changepoints in the variance, and generalized
likelihood ratio test for changepoints in both mean and variance. The next two parametric
tests remove the Gaussian assumption: Mann-Whitney test on the mean, Mood test on the
variance. The last three tests by Lepage, Kolmogorov-Smirnov, and Cramer-von-Mises are
non-parametric and concern the overall distribution. These eight tests apply to both single
and multiple changepoints. We set the probability of a Type I error to α = 5% for single
changepoints and the average run length, ARL0, to 500 for multiple changepoints. The
average run length is the expected number of observations before the ﬁrst Type I error occurs.
Under IID assumptions, ARL0 is the inverse of the probability of Type I error: ARL0 = 1
α .
We use the R package cpm (Ross, 2015).

BMs consist of Bayesian product partition algorithms. These methods apply Bayes’ for-
mula to compute the posterior distribution that identiﬁes the partition ρ1:T . However, their
development is speciﬁc to each algorithm. We choose two popular algorithms for our study:
the ofﬂine algorithm by Barry and Hartigan (1993a) and the online algorithm by Adams and
MacKay (2007).
The ofﬂine Barry-Hartigan algorithm draws an initial partition randomly according to the
product partition distribution in Barry and Hartigan (1993b). The number of sub-sequences is
a random variable b taking values in {1, . . . , T }. Then, the partition and the observations y1:T
enter Bayes’ formula to produce posterior cohesions for the sub-sequences and distributions
for the model parameters. The detection problem is solved computationally via Markov
Chain Monte Carlo methods. Moreover, the ofﬂine detection algorithm assumes that the
observations are independent and Gaussian with known variance, that is, Yt ∼N(μt, σ 2), so
it only detects changepoints in the mean. We implement this algorithm with the R package
bcp (Erdman & Emerson, 2007), with hyperparameters γ and λ set at 0.2, as in Barry and
Hartigan (1993a).
By contrast, the Adams-MacKay algorithm formulates online changepoint detection as
a Bayesian ﬁltering problem. The objective is to infer from observations y1:t whether the
current pattern, called a run, continues or stops. Speciﬁcally, denote by rt−1 the run length
at time t −1. At the next time increment, t, the current run continues or ends. If it continues,
the run length is incremented by 1, so rt = rt−1 + 1. If the run ends, record time t as a
changepoint and set rt := 0 to start new run. The changepoint posterior probability P(rt |
y1:t) determines whether the run continues or ends. The algorithm requires two inputs: a
conditional distribution P(Yt | ηρ), where ηρ represents the parameters of the model for a
given partition ρ1:t, and a hazard function H which deﬁnes the conditional prior probability
of a changepoint P(rt | rt−1). We compute changepoints based on three probabilistic criteria
with decreasing sensitivity. The ﬁrst and most sensitive, maxprob, is based on the highest
run length probability. The second, thrshld50, sets the run length probability at a 50%
threshold. We use it as a baseline. The third, maxCP, uses the highest overall probability and
is, therefore, the most stringent. Our implementation assumes that the conditional distribution
P(Yt | ηρ) is Gaussian and that the hazard function H is constant at H(t) = 1
λ, with the
hyperparameter λ set at a standard value of 100. We use the R package ocp for this method
(Pagotto, 2019).

2.4 Data
We sourced daily stock return data from the CRSP US Stock Databases1. Our research
focuses on publicly-traded U.S. common stocks (share code shrcd 10 and 11). We selected
all shares listed for at least two years (500 daily prices). We use the CRSP-calculated returns
(ﬁeld ret), which include the effect of stock splits and dividend reinvestment. Data for the
Fama-French ﬁve-factor model, momentum, and risk-free rate come from Kenneth French’s
online database2. So the data in our study extends nearly sixty years, from July 1, 1963, the
start date for the Fama-French ﬁve-factor data, to December 31, 2022.

3.2 Evolution of the number of changepoints
The results in the previous section revealed the existence and extent of changepoints in the
distribution of excess returns. We reﬁne our ﬁndings by investigating whether the occur-
rence of changepoints displays any trend. Were there periods over the past 60 years when
no changepoint was detected? How did the number of changepoints vary over time? Do
changepoints relate to market breadth, as measured by the number of issues traded on stock
exchanges?
To answer these questions, we split the 60-year study period into 12 non-overlapping
sliding windows: 10 regular 5-year windows plus two irregular windows located in the ﬁrst
and last position to account for the uneven start and end dates. The average number of
companies per sliding window is nearly 5,000, with a minimum of 2,317 for the 1963-1969
window and a maximum of 7,883 for the 1995-1999 window. On each window, we conduct
a changepoint detection on the stocks’ excess returns using the same 30 model speciﬁcations
as in our previous study.
Table 2 displays the average number of changepoints detected in the excess returns by
the 30 unsupervised changepoint model speciﬁcations over the 12 non-overlapping sliding
windows. Four observations stand out.
First, the changepoints we uncovered in the previous section have been a persistent stylized
fact of stock market returns over the entire study horizon. For example, the three AMOC
methods systematically reject the null hypothesis of no changepoint.
Second, the number of changepoints in mean-var is more closely related to those detected
in variance than in the mean. For example, the number of changepoints that the LR-PELT
detected in mean-var is strongly positively correlated with that in the variance (correlation =
123

Annals of Operations Research
0.93, signiﬁcantly different from 0 with p-value in the order of 10−5), but not to the mean
(correlation = -0.13, not signiﬁcantly different from 0). This observation is consistent with
our earlier conjecture that changepoints in variance are a more prominent feature of stock
markets than changepoints in the mean.
Third, the data exhibit less intertemporal variation than cross-sectional variation. Intertem-
poral variation is the change in the number of changepoints detected by a same method over
time across the twelve windows. Cross-sectional variation refers to ﬂuctuations in the number
of changepoints detected by several methods within the same time window. For instance, the
intertemporal CV for changepoint detection methods on the mean, which we deﬁne as the CV
computed for a single method across all 12 windows, ranged from 0.27 to 0.88. By contrast,
the cross-sectional CV for changepoint detection methods on the mean, which is the CV
computed across methods for a single window, ranged from 0.75 to 1.15. For changepoints
in the variance, the intertemporal CV range from 0.21 to 0.59 compared to 1.15 to 1.45 for
the cross-sectional coefﬁcients of variation3.
Finally, the number of changepoints does not appear correlated with the number of com-
panies included in each window. Pearson correlation coefﬁcients calculated for each method
range from -0.35 to +0.17. So the variation in the number of companies across windows did
not affect the average number of changepoints.
Together these results show that changepoints are an intrinsic characteristic of the cross-
section of stock returns. Changepoints occur frequently, across individual shares, and affect

the dependent variable.
4 Explanatory power of empirical asset pricing models
4.1 Empirical asset pricing factors
We investigate the ability of empirical asset pricing models to explain changepoints in the
distribution of stock returns. We ﬁt the empirical asset pricing model in each sliding window,
and perform changepoint detection on the residuals of the ﬁtted models. We expect to ﬁnd
fewer changepoints in the residuals than in the stock’s excess returns if the factors explain
some of the changepoints. If changepoints persist in the residuals, then asset pricing factors
do not explain all the changepoints observed in the excess returns.
Table 3 displays the average number of changepoints detected in the residuals for each
of the 30 unsupervised changepoint model speciﬁcations. These results reveal similar trends
as for the excess returns. The ﬁve AMOC methods systematically reject the null hypothesis
of no changepoint, while the other methods continue to detect changepoints in each sliding
window. The number of changepoints detected in mean-var is closely related to that in the
variance. Additionally, the number of changepoints exhibits less intertemporal variation than
cross-sectional variation.

a cursory comparison of Tables 2 and 3 shows that they have markedly reduced their number.

returns.
One possible explanation for this puzzling ﬁnding is the increasing adoption of empirical
pricing models. The six-factor model’s ability to explain changepoints began its decline in
the 1990s. This decade coincides with the publication of two seminal works on asset pricing
models by Fama and French (1993) and Carhart (1997). In the 2000s, the adoption of multi-
factor models accelerated among academics and practitioners, leading to an explosion in
the number of factors. The factor zoo was born. We therefore conjecture that the broader
adoption of multi-factor models has blunted the factors’ ability to explain changepoints.

4.2 Autoregeressive conditional heteroskedasticity
This section investigates whether the changepoints we detected in the residual of the empirical
asset pricing model manifest as autoregressive conditional heteroskedasicity. We include a
GARCH speciﬁcation in the linear model and run the changepoint detection methods on the
residuals.
Tables 4 and 5 report the average number of changepoints detected in the residuals of
a standard GARCH(1,1) and asymmetric GJR GARCH(1,1) model. We do not report the
eGARCH results because the model ﬁtting process excluded signiﬁcantly more companies
than GARCH and GJR GARCH, potentially biasing the results. The numbers in both tables
are remarkably similar, suggesting that changepoints are unrelated to asymmetric effects of
stock returns on heteroskedasticity.
Furthermore, both tables display results that are comparable to those reported in Table 3
for the residuals of the empirical asset pricing model. We expected that the GARCH models
do not explain changepoints in the mean of the return distribution, which is what we observe.
However, the number of changepoints detected in the variance does not decline noticeably.
This suggests that GARCH models are ineffective at capturing changepoints in the variance
of the distribution of excess returns.
To corroborate this observation, we apply a Welch t-test to the hypothesis that the average
number of changepoints detected in the residuals of the GARCH models equals that in the
residuals of the empirical asset pricing model. Figures 8 and 9 display the t-statistics and p-

To assess the robustness of our ﬁndings, we re-examine changepoints in the cross-section of

5.2 Explanatory power of empirical asset pricing models on monthly data
We re-estimated the six-factor empirical asset pricing model introduced in Section 2.2 on
monthly data, using OLS and three robust regression methods (Huber, Tukey bisquare, and
Hampel objective functions). We then applied the changepoint detection methods to the
regression residuals.
Figure 15 reports, for 1962–2022, the average number of changepoints detected in raw
excess returns and in residuals across the four regression models. On average, changepoint
counts decline after factor adjustment, particularly for variance- and distribution-sensitive
tests. Reductions typically range from 2% to 13% and are statistically signiﬁcant (e.g., LR—

Barry–Hartigan −7% to −12%, t ∈[−15.6, −9.6]). Robust regressions yield results nearly
identical to OLS. Thus, standard factors account for some structural breaks, but the reductions
are limited.
The magnitude of these reductions is economically modest. A 2%–13% decline corre-
sponds to fewer than one changepoint over the full sample, insufﬁcient to alter portfolio
construction or risk management materially. More important is the persistence of change-
points in the residuals: even after controlling for standard factors, many structural breaks
remain, particularly in variance and distribution-sensitive methods. This persistence sug-
gests that static Fama–French-based models capture only part of the underlying dynamics.

6 Implications for equity return modeling and model validation
Our ﬁndings have four implications for equity return models and their users.
1. Parameter stability and inference in factor models.
Consider the factor model in (1.1). Changepoints are pervasive in excess returns. A sub-
stantial share remains in residuals after controlling for Fama–French-style factors. This
pattern suggests that the constant-parameter speciﬁcation (αi, βi) is often misspeciﬁed over
long samples. Least-squares estimates are then best read as regime-averaged exposures,
not stable structural parameters. Shifts in the conditional mean are reﬂected in instability
in αi. Shifts in volatility or in the full return distribution can induce time variation in βi,
undermine homoskedasticity-based inference, and leave residual dynamics that the ﬁtted
model does not capture. The practical message is straightforward: estimation and validation
should account for breaks. Three steps are natural: estimate on break-adjusted subsamples
(piecewise-constant parameters), adopt time-varying or regime-switching speciﬁcations, and
run changepoint diagnostics on residuals as a routine model check.
2. Changepoint detection, structural breaks, and robust regression.
Changepoint detection, structural break analysis, and robust regression address different
failure modes. Robust regression (e.g., Huber or Tukey bisquare) controls the weight of

Annals of Operations Research

models. Negative percentages indicate that more changepoints were detected in the residual of the empirical
asset pricing model than in the stocks’ excess return
outliers and heavy tails in coefﬁcient estimation. Structural break tests target parameter
instability (e.g., breaks in βi) within a given regression form. The procedures used here
instead detect distributional instability in returns (mean, variance, or broader shifts). They
therefore answer a different question: when does the data-generating mechanism change?
This information is complementary. It does not presuppose whether the best description is an
outlier episode, a parameter break, or a regime shift in the distribution. In practice, the three
tools ﬁt naturally into a validation workﬂow: robust regression protects estimation against
extremes, while changepoint detection and break tests diagnose persistent shifts that call for
re-estimation, re-speciﬁcation, or regime-aware risk forecasts.
3. Model comparison and factor choice under structural instability.
If changepoints are an empirical fact of the cross-section, model comparison should reﬂect
that. A useful model should improve ﬁt and reduce residual instability. This observation
suggests using residual changepoints as a diagnostic when comparing factor sets (Fama-
French-style factors, macroeconomic factors, fundamental factors, statistical factors, or
machine-learning factors). One can compare models by the share of return changepoints
that disappear once conditioning on factors, or by the number (and proportion) of change-
points remaining in residuals. Any such comparison should control for model complexity,
for example, via factor penalties and out-of-sample validation. On this view, alternative con-
structions, including fundamental factors such as the 10-factor USER model in Guerard et
al. (2012, 2015, 2024), and statistical factors such as Liu et al. (2025), are promising if they
yield systematically more stable residuals than traditional factors.

4. Horizon dependence and implications for practitioners.
The interpretation of changepoints is horizon-dependent. At the daily frequency, mean
breaks may partly reﬂect short-lived noise or microstructure effects. They still matter for
short-horizon monitoring and trading. At the monthly horizon, changepoints look more eco-
nomically meaningful. They align more often with variance or distributional shifts, consistent
with persistent high-/low-volatility regimes and broader macro transitions that matter for port-
folio choice and risk control. The persistence of changepoints in factor-model residuals also
points to dynamics beyond linear exposures, including nonlinearities, interactions, omitted
state variables, or latent regimes. This reinforces the value of changepoint detection as (i)
a monitoring device that triggers recalibration, (ii) a complement to volatility modeling in
stress testing, and (iii) a diagnostic for misspeciﬁcation. Overall, Fama-French style factor
models mitigate structural instability in stock returns, but they do not eliminate it.
7 Conclusion
This paper studied changepoints in the cross-section of U.S. stock returns. We used an
ensemble of unsupervised changepoint methods spanning four complementary families to
achieve robust ﬁndings across models.
Three conclusions stand out. First, changepoints are pervasive. All 30 speciﬁcations
detect breaks in the distribution of excess returns. Second, frequency matters. At the daily
horizon, methods detect a large number of breaks, consistent with short-lived ﬂuctuations
123

Annals of Operations Research
and microstructure noise. After aggregation to monthly data, the count drops sharply. Yet
structural breaks persist, especially in volatility and distributions. Thus, the existence of
changepoints is not a product of higher-frequency data. While aggregation removes transi-
toryinstability,italsopreserveslonger-livedregimechange.Third,Fama-French-stylefactors
only explain a modest share of the changepoints. While their explanatory power changed over
time, it never dominated. In all periods and at both frequencies, many changepoints remain
in residuals.
These ﬁndings have clear implications. Changepoints undermine the constant-parameter
interpretation of Fama-French-style factor models over long samples. Least-squares esti-
mates become regime averages. Inference based on stable residuals becomes fragile. One
should therefore regard changepoint detection as a routine diagnostic for model validation
and recalibration. Changepoint detection also offers a practical criterion for model compari-
son: a useful model should reduce residual instability, not only improve ﬁt.
A natural progression of this work is to ask what else could explain the prevalence of
changepoints in the cross-section of stock returns. Do other factors in the zoo explain change-
points? Could corporate events or the release of corporate information explain changepoints?
A further study could also assess the use of supervised changepoint methods in empirical
asset pricing to sharpen the predictions of asset risk premia.
