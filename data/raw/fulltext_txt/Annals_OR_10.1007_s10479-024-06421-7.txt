Abstract
This study addresses the evolving landscape of the manufacturing sector, particularly in devel-
opingnationslikeMalaysia,againstthebackdropoftheinﬂuentialIndustry4.0paradigm.The
manufacturing arena, vital for economic progress, encounters heightened competitiveness
and complexity, necessitating a reevaluation of strategies. The impetus behind this research
is the imperative for manufacturing entities to enhance efﬁciency by embracing innovation as
a fundamental aspect of their operational frameworks. Practices such as knowledge oriented
leadership, electronic human resource management (e-HRM), and decentralised organisation
structures emerge as pivotal elements to navigate the challenges presented by Industry 4.0.
This study serves a dual purpose: guiding manufacturing ﬁrms in proactively preparing for
Industry 4.0 and exploring the nuanced challenges associated with an aging workforce in this
transformative context. Incorporating a mixed-method approach, the study initially adopts a
quantitative, cross-sectional design. SPSS is employed for descriptive statistics, while Smart
PLS is utilised for structural and measurement model analyses. This comprehensive method-
ology allows for a thorough exploration of the intricate relationships affecting Industry 4.0
readiness. In parallel, a qualitative facet was incorporated through thematic analysis, vali-
dating and enriching the ﬁndings on the impact of Industry 4.0 on urban planning derived
from participant interviews. The ﬁndings emphasise that knowledge oriented leadership and
eHRM are signiﬁcant contributors to organisation innovation and Industry 4.0 readiness
in the Malaysian manufacturing sector. Knowledge oriented leadership positively inﬂuences
innovation, while e-HRM facilitates technological adoption by improving recruitment, learn-
ing, and performance appraisal systems. However, a decentralised organisational structure
showed an insigniﬁcant effect on Industry 4.0 readiness, likely due to the unique challenges
faced by smaller manufacturing ﬁrms. Notably, the study’s ﬁndings on the varying inﬂuence
of an aging workforce on Industry 4.0 readiness offer novel insights, highlighting the need for
tailored workforce policies to support effective Industry 4.0 adoption. A notable contribution
lies in the pioneering inquiry into the readiness of the Malaysian manufacturing sector for
Industry 4.0. Identifying knowledge oriented leadership and e-HRM as crucial prerequisites
for innovation and Industry 4.0 adoption, the study enriches the understanding of the intri-
cate dynamics at the intersection of manufacturing, innovation, and Industry 4.0. Practically,
the study offers actionable insights for Malaysian government ofﬁcials shaping Industry 4.0
policies and stakeholders in navigating this transformative industrial era. Moreover, it will
also help the stakeholders of other countries that are working towards Industry 4.0.

1 Introduction
The manufacturing sector is a crucial driver of Malaysia’s economic development. Since the
late nineteenth century, Malysia’s economy has shifted from agriculture to manufacturing,
with manufacturing now the primary engine for the country’s economic growth. Currently,
the manufacturing sector in Malaysia is a signiﬁcant contributor to the country’s exports,
gross domestic product (GDP), and various levels of employment. In 2019, manufacturing
accounted for 22.3% of the GDP and was also among the top-20 exporters globally, with
80% of contributions coming from this manufacturing sector.
Moreover, the manufacturing industry in general has undergone signiﬁcant changes over
the last two centuries, with the introduction of industrial revolutions that have brought about
remarkabledevelopments.ThecurrentphaseisknownasIndustry4.0orsmartmanufacturing,
which involves the digital transformation of the sector. Germany introduced the concept of
“Industrie 4.0” in 2011 to improve the manufacturing sector (Pfeiffer, 2017), which involves
computer-based technology and smart machines interacting with the environment to make
automated decisions without human involvement. Industrialists consider this shift as being
not only a trend but a success, as the business world becomes increasingly competitive and
unpredictable (Ghobakhloo et al., 2021).
The advent of Industry 4.0 has also garnered attention from global businesses and gov-
ernments, as it represents a shift toward digital transformation (Ghobakhloo, 2018). Smart
manufacturing is a central idea behind Industry 4.0, which is characterised by the digital trans-
formation of production systems, including cyber-physical systems, the Internet of Things,
and artiﬁcial intelligence, through the use of advanced technologies, which are making the
manufacturing industry more competitive and complex, thus necessitating businesses to pre-
pare for Industry 4.0 and increase their economic efﬁciency (Al-Shami et al., 2021). However,
manufacturing companies remain uncertain about the outcomes of Industry 4.0 due to a lack
of information and guidance, which can hinder their readiness for the transformation (Schu-
macher et al., 2016). As a result, research is essential to help these businesses prepare for
and to understand the importance of Industry 4.0.
To fully beneﬁt from Industry 4.0 and manage its complexities, continuous innovation is
crucial to be integrated into the manufacturing system (Hee & Jing, 2018). Organisational
innovation helps businesses adapt to the rapid changes in the business environment, but
it does not happen spontaneously and requires the adoption of particular practices. The
learning organisations theory guides organisations in developing innovation (Senge, 2006).
The theory further highlights practices that are essential to enhance learning and innovation
(Theriou & Chatzoglou, 2008) and emphasises the creation and transformation of knowledge
to tackle changes. In particular, knowledge oriented leadership, electronic human resource
management, and a decentralised organisational structure are requirements for continuous
innovation (Rehman et al., 2022).
In today’s rapidly changing business environment, organisations need to constantly update
their knowledge and practices to remain competitive. One way to achieve this is by adopt-
ing knowledge oriented leadership, which emphasises the importance of bringing new and
updated information into the organisation to drive positive changes (Donate & de Pablo,
2015). Studies on knowledge oriented leadership and innovation, especially in the context

Second, electronic human resource management can be used to identify, train, and reward

The objective of this research is to examine management practices, particularly knowledge
oriented leadership, eHRM, and decentralised organisation structure for innovation to prepare
organisations for Industry 4.0. Earlier studies such as Jansen etal. (2006), Donate and de Pablo
(2015), and Carmeli et al. (2013) explored leadership’s inﬂuence on innovation. However,
the current research focuses on the contribution of these practices to the readiness of Industry
4.0. The concept of Industry 4.0 is yet to mature in developing countries, including Malaysia,
notably their manufacturing sector, which is also a major contributor to the GDP. This sector
faces distinct challenges such as resource limitations, traditional management structures,
and the complexities of adopting advanced digital technologies. These unique regional and
sectoral contexts have not been extensively addressed in previous studies, thus making the
current research highly relevant for theory and practice in navigating this rapidly evolving
industry. Noting the importance, Abdullah et al. (2017) recommended further research to
investigateorganisationpracticesthatcontributetoinnovationinIndustry4.0intheMalaysian
setting.
In addition to the above, Malaysia is also facing a demographic challenge as it moves
toward becoming an ageing society. This social phenomenon, where the difference between
birth and death rates results in an increasing proportion of older people in the population, has
signiﬁcant implications for the workforce structure (Shamim et al., 2016). The demographic
shift toward an ageing society in Malaysia is indeed a critical concern, particularly in the
context of Industry 4.0. As older workers remain longer in the labour force, organisations will
need to adapt coping strategies to maintain innovation and competitiveness. This involves
not only adjusting to technological advancements but also fostering an inclusive environment
where the ageing workforce can continue to contribute meaningfully (Hecklau et al., 2016).
This demographic consideration, which is often underexplored in the literature, particularly
in the context of Industry 4.0, will add a noteworthy contribution to the theory and practice for
countries facing challenges, e.g., Japan, Korea, the USA, and China. It will also help bridge
the gap and enhance the relevance of the study beyond national borders. Current research is
undoubtedly necessary to develop strategies, as organisations must balance the integration
of technology with the evolving demographic landscape.

2 Theoretical background
2.1 Theoretical conceptualisation
2.1.1 Learning Organisation Theory
Learning Organisation Theory (LO) posits that organisations can secure a competitive edge
by nurturing continuous innovation to adapt to dynamic changes in the business environment
(Senge, 1990). According to Senge (1990), a learning organisation embraces practices that
support ongoing learning, knowledge creation, acquisition, and transformation to thrive in a
rapidly evolving context (Garvin, 1993). Features of learning organisations include leader-
ship support for learning, ﬂatter hierarchies, open communication, risk-taking, support for
knowledge sharing, rewarding innovation, and the provision of a conducive learning envi-
ronment (Hatane, 2015; Otala, 1995; Phillips, 2003; Pool, 2000; Goh, 1998; Wilkinson &
Kleiner, 1993; Robinson et al., 1997; Loermans, 2002; Selen, 2000).
Linking these features to "knowledge oriented leadership and decentralised organisa-
tion structure" (Mabey et al., 2012), it becomes evident that both practices play crucial
roles in initiating actions that enhance innovation in response to the rapid ﬂuctuations of
Industry 4.0. Knowledge oriented leadership provides essential knowledge and information,
supporting learning and improvement, while a decentralised organisation structure fosters an
innovation-focused environment, risk-taking, and ﬂexibility (Arnold et al., 2005; Watkins,
2002; Taleizadeh et al., 2024).
In application to Learning Organisation Theory, "knowledge oriented leadership and
decentralised organisation structure" function as enablers for organisational innovation.
Knowledge oriented leadership stimulates innovation by introducing new information, and
a decentralised organisation structure acts as an enabler by promoting learning and develop-
ment, reducing communication barriers, and empowering employees in decision-making, all
contributing to innovation (Naqshbandi & Jasimuddin, 2018).
2.1.2 Dynamic Capability Theory
Dynamic Capability Theory emphasises an organisation’s capacity to navigate dynamic and
complex situations, especially in the face of rapid technological changes and an unpredictable
future, as seen in Industry 4.0 (Teece et al., 1997). This theory underscores the need for
organisations to continuously build and enhance their capabilities to respond rapidly to threats
and seize opportunities (Teece, 2018).
Knowledge oriented leadership is identiﬁed as a critical enabler for organisational inno-
vation within Dynamic Capability Theory. Leaders who prioritise knowledge based practices
facilitate continuous innovation, helping organisations adapt to the rapid changes associated
with Industry 4.0 (Mabey et al., 2012). Additionally, a "decentralised organisation structure"
is crucial in aiding organisations to build competencies, make agile decisions, and mitigate
risks, ultimately enhancing their capability to respond swiftly to change (Dedahanov et al.,
2017).
2.1.3 Integration with Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
The integration of Learning Organisation Theory and Dynamic Capability Theory aligns with
several Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), including Goal 4 (Quality Education), Goal

2.2 Study variables
2.2.1 Knowledge oriented leadership
Leadership is a vital component of organisational success as it provides direction and sets
long-term goals. Effective leadership also involves managing employees and motivating
them to achieve organisational objectives. In the current business environment, organisations
seek multi-dimensional leaders who can adapt to changing situations and employ various
leadership styles. According to philosopher Rollo May, leaders must possess knowledge and
serve as role models for their followers. As a result, followers can learn from the leader and
become more self-sufﬁcient in their work processes. Leaders must demonstrate competency
in their role to build trust with their followers. Additionally, leaders should collaborate with
followers and share information about their decision-making processes to foster trust (Kaplan
et al., 2012; Soderberg & Romney, 2022).
Knowledge oriented leadership refers to a combination of transactional and transfor-
mational leadership styles, along with motivation, which is centred on knowledge and its
development (Donate & de Pablo, 2015). This concept emphasises the creation, transfer,
storage, and application of knowledge in the organisation, which ultimately leads to innova-
tion. Communication plays a critical role in knowledge oriented leadership, as leaders must
effectively share knowledge and expectations with employees to clarify targets. Leaders also
act as advisors, reducing the employee-boss barrier by creating a friendly environment that
facilitates real-time problem-solving.
Motivation is the second important element of knowledge oriented leadership. Researchers
have extensively discussed motivation and reward systems for organisational development.
Knowledge oriented leaders must assess employee needs and prepare a motivational strat-
egy accordingly. For instance, intrinsic and extrinsic rewards can be planned to motivate
employees and enhance organisational progress (Victor & Hoole, 2021).
2.2.2 Electronic Human Resource Management (E-HRM)
Electronic Human Resource Management (E-HRM) is a strategic approach that combines
Human Resource Management (HRM) and Information Technology (IT). Previous research
has deﬁned E-HRM as the incorporation and application of IT in all HRM functions. This
is achieved by utilising the internet and electronic resources to gather, analyse, interpret and
communicate HR information for business purposes. Speciﬁcally, E-HRM is regarded as
an integrated system of HRM and IT that adds value to the entire organisation, including
every employee and function. Additionally, it is also considered the amalgamation of all IT

resources and HRM functions that manage and coordinate data to disseminate information
throughout the organisation (Bondarouk et al., 2017).
In recent years, organisations have rapidly adopted E-HRM to achieve strategic and admin-
istrative improvements in their management approaches. Implementation of E-HRM can
provide various beneﬁts to organisations, such as cost reduction, improved services, and
assisting HR professionals in making strategic decisions. Furthermore, E-HRM streamlines
all HR functions, resulting in efﬁcient, effective, and organised processes that can improve
the overall performance of the organisation. E-HRM is becoming increasingly recognised
due to the multitude of beneﬁts it brings to organisations.
E-HRM encompasses various HR functions, including e-recruitment and selection, e-
learning, e-compensation, and e-performance appraisal. E-recruitment and selection aid the
hiring process by utilising the company website and social media platforms to attract a large
pool of applicants and ﬁnd the right talent. E-learning provides an electronic form of train-
ing and development, accessible through the company portal or online space. This enables
employees to learn at their own pace, while allowing organisations to track their progress.
E-compensation integrates technology into the traditional form of compensation, allowing
employees to track their earnings and beneﬁts online and generate pay slips with a single click.
This streamlines the compensation process and improves operational efﬁciency. Finally, e-
performance appraisal offers a quick and efﬁcient way to monitor employee progress. Daily
reports and targets can be updated in real-time, providing an effective solution for continuous
improvement (Mohamed et al., 2022; Hooi, 2006).
2.2.3 Decentralised organisation structure
The organisation structure is a vital component of an organisation that forms the basis for its
effective functioning. It is the framework that divides labour into speciﬁc tasks and coordi-
nates them to achieve organisational goals. As such, it plays a signiﬁcant role in determining
the behaviour and attitude of the organisation’s workforce and is critical to strategic imple-
mentation. This includes decision-making, resource allocation, and activity execution within
the organisation. Therefore, it is essential to have an adaptable structure to market changes
that can facilitate innovation (Chu et al., 2018).
Decentralised organisation structure is widely discussed in the literature (Wright, 2021).
It is deﬁned as the distribution of decision-making power throughout the organisation, mak-
ing employees more accountable and responsible for their actions. This approach helps to
increase the ﬂow of information, as managers and employees have a greater level of auton-
omy, leading to increased accountability and responsibility, which are essential for long-term
organisational performance and sustainability. The degree of decentralisation varies, and the
structure of the organisation plays a crucial role in determining how decisions are made, tasks
are assigned, and strategies are implemented (Chu et al., 2018).
Dekoulou and Trivellas (2017) suggest that organisation structure involves coordinating
tasks and activities to achieve goals. The structure also facilitates resource allocation, smooth
internal and external communication, and adaptability to market changes. The degree of
decentralisation determines the level of responsibility and accountability of employees and
managers. Decentralisation is the degree of power to make decisions disseminated within an
organisation. Decentralised structure distributes and delegates power and control, ensuring
that the organisation’s long-term performance and sustainability are maintained (Anderson
et al., 2014).

2.2.4 Organisation innovation
Innovation refers to the introduction and application of new ideas, technologies, processes,
or practices that provide beneﬁts to the organisation and stakeholders. It can take various
forms, including technological advancements, changes in processes, or novel systems. Li
et al. (2018) deﬁne organisational innovation as the use of new perspectives, technology,
systems, processes, or practices to improve organisational performance.
Organisations that are ﬂexible in their thinking and not bound by formality tend to be
more active and efﬁcient. The deﬁnition of organisational innovation has evolved, and in the
context of Industry 4.0, it refers to the application of new management practices, systems,
approaches, or techniques aimed at achieving organisational goals. It involves new methods of
managing functions and processes, which transform operational frameworks and competitive
strategies (Azar & Ciabuschi, 2017).
In today’s highly competitive world, organisations require a competitive advantage to
outperform others. Innovation plays a vital role in achieving this goal. Conversely, rigid
organisations that are reluctant to change and rely solely on traditional methods struggle to
keep up with competitors and quickly lose their identity. Therefore, organisations today focus
on innovation to bring new ideas and improve their reputation to maintain their identity in
national and international markets and gain competitive edge.
Two primary types of innovation radical innovation and process innovation are consid-
ered relevant for the research. Radical innovation involves substantial, disruptive changes that
redeﬁne operational paradigms and market dynamics, enabling organisations to break away
from traditional methods and explore new business models (Rampa & Agogué, 2021). This
type of innovation is particularly relevant in the fast-evolving landscape, where organisa-
tions must embrace transformative technologies to remain competitive. The dynamic nature
of technological advancements in Industry 4.0 requires organisations to prioritise radical
innovations that can lead to signiﬁcant breakthroughs and operational transformations. Fur-
thermore, the urgency of adapting processes through innovation supersedes the need for a
balanced ambidextrous approach (Duwe, 2021) as ﬁrms must rapidly adopt and implement
new technologies to avoid obsolescence. On the other hand, process innovation focuses on
enhancing efﬁciency and effectiveness through new production, management, or operational
methods. This innovation type is critical in Industry 4.0, as it streamlines operations and
integrates digital technologies to optimise performance, thereby improving responsiveness
to market demands (Damanpour & Gopalakrishnan, 2001).
2.2.5 Aging workforce
The performance of employees plays a crucial role in the success of any organisation. The
demographic of the world is changing due to low fertility rates and good health facilities we
are noting a reduction in the younger population and an increase in senior citizens, which is
ultimately affecting the workforce of organisations. According to a survey conducted on For-
tune 1000 companies, most employees acknowledged a potential workforce shortage, calling
for appropriate strategies to deal with this crisis. The social problem of an ‘aging workforce’
is being faced and anticipated by many countries, including Malaysia, with individuals aged
55 years or older and still working considered part of the aging workforce. The change
in demographics means that organisations will have more older workers than young ones,
leading to issues regarding succession planning, knowledge loss and mass retirement. The

2.2.6 Industry 4.0
In recent years, there has been a surge in the development of new concepts globally, particu-
larly in the technology and manufacturing industries. One of the most prominent and widely
discussed concepts is "Industry 4.0," introduced by the German government in 2011. While
numerous studies have examined this concept in academic and private settings, the focus has
mainly been on its application. Industry 4.0 refers to the digitisation of organisations and the
integration of various technologies to improve the value chain and overall performance (Zhu
et al., 2022). It represents a complete transformation of the manufacturing sector by enabling
companies to achieve maximum output with minimum input. This revolution in manufactur-
ing also referred to as Smart Manufacturing, has not only led to changes in manufacturing
functions and operations but has also inﬂuenced customer needs and market environments
(Kamble & Gunasekaran, 2018).
Industry 4.0 involves the practical implementation of advanced technologies and processes
associated with the Fourth Industrial Revolution in a business or industrial setting. Industry
4.0 is characterised by the integration of cyber-physical systems, the Internet of Things (IoT),
big data, artiﬁcial intelligence (AI), automation, and smart manufacturing (De Vass et al.,
2021). These technologies work together to create more efﬁcient, ﬂexible, and responsive
manufacturing systems, often referred to as "smart factories." In smart factories, machines
and devices are interconnected through IoT, allowing them to communicate with each other
in real-time, share data, and make autonomous decisions. For example, sensors placed on
machines can monitor performance and detect potential issues before they become major
problems, enabling predictive maintenance and reducing downtime. The use of AI and big
data analytics enables organisations to gather insights from vast amounts of data, optimising
production processes and enhancing product quality. Automation plays a crucial role in
reducing human intervention in repetitive tasks, improving efﬁciency, and minimising errors.
However, the adoption of Industry 4.0 requires signiﬁcant changes in organisational
structures and workforce skills. Companies must develop new strategies to integrate these
advanced technologies into their operations while fostering a culture of continuous learning
and innovation. Knowledge based leadership plays a critical role in guiding this transforma-
tion, ensuring that employees are equipped with the necessary skills to operate in a digitised
environment.
Current research has examined Industry 4.0 across nine key dimensions: Strategy, Lead-
ership, Customer, Products, Operation, Culture, People, Governance, and Technology. Each
dimension highlights different aspects that companies need to consider to fully integrate
Industry 4.0 technologies. Strategy involves aligning corporate goals with digital transforma-
tion, while Leadership focuses on driving this change effectively. The Customer dimension
examines how customer expectations and relationships evolve in a digital landscape, and

Products refers to the development of smart, connected products that can communicate data
back to the manufacturer.
Similarly, Operation involves the transformation of production processes through automa-

In the Malaysian context, the government has actively promoted Industry 4.0 through
the National Policy on Industry 4.0 (known as Industry4WRD), launched in 2018. This
initiative seeks to transform the country’s manufacturing sector into a global competitor
by adopting advanced technologies such as automation, artiﬁcial intelligence (AI), and the
Internet of Things (IoT). Malaysia, as a developing economy, faces unique challenges in
implementing Industry 4.0 due to the disparity in technological readiness between large
corporations and small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) (Azhar et al., 2021). SMEs,
which form the backbone of the Malaysian economy, often lack the resources and skills
(Taleizadeh et al., 2024) to fully integrate advanced technologies. However, recognising
its importance, the government’s push to provide training and support through agencies
such as MIDA (Malaysian Investment Development Authority) highlights its value and is a
justiﬁcation for current research initiatives.
3 Hypotheses development
3.1 Knowledge oriented leadership and organisation innovation
The ability of leaders with certain knowledge oriented skills to make innovative improve-
ments and effectively use research & development (R&D) activities has been recognised as
important. Research conﬁrms that knowledge plays a crucial role in implementing innovation
(Kremer et al., 2019). As a result, organisations are increasingly adopting knowledge oriented
leadership in their innovative ﬁrms. This leadership style involves the application of knowl-
edge based practices to realise innovation opportunities. It encompasses the creation, transfer,
storage, and application of knowledge, combining transactional and transformational lead-
ership styles with communication and motivation. Applying Herzberg’s theory, knowledge
oriented leaders focus on factors that stimulate employee motivation. According to the theory,
factors that demotivate or cause dissatisfaction are hygienic factors that cannot be controlled,
while other motivational factors have the opposite effect. Knowledge oriented leadership
focuses on controlling hygienic factors to prevent employee dissatisfaction and introducing
motivational factors that increase employee satisfaction and productivity (Sahibzada et al.,
2021).
Leadership based on knowledge orientation is crucial for organisations to enhance
their performance through innovation and effective knowledge management practices. The
knowledge oriented leadership factor emphasises that knowledge management is vital for
organisations as it helps in capturing opportunities related to innovation (Donate & de Pablo,
2015). Knowledge oriented leadership is an action that leads to the creation, communica-
tion, and application of knowledge and information in a way that brings transformations
in the overall organisation. While several researchers have emphasised the importance of

management (HRM) process system in place to ensure smooth operations. HR plays a vital
role in the productivity and efﬁciency of a company by identifying employee performance
and analysing different levels of HRM adoption to meet company requirements.
Electronic human resource management (E-HRM) focuses on skills development and pro-

3.3 Decentralised organisation structure and organisation innovation
Organisational structure is a crucial factor that can affect innovation in organisations. Two
primary organisational structures are centralised and decentralised. A centralised structure,
which strictly follows rules and procedures, can be rigid and may discourage innovativeness
among employees. Research conducted by Dedahanov et al. (2017) found a negative rela-
tionship between centralised organisation structure and innovative behaviour since it restricts
creativity and fosters strict dependencies on work procedures.
On the other hand, a decentralised organisational structure promotes openness to the sys-
tem, which is a necessary precondition for innovativeness (Damanpour, 2017). A study on
Singaporean companies identiﬁed ﬁve factors that are essential in promoting organisational
innovativeness, one of which is a decentralised organisation structure. Such a structure pro-
vides ﬂexible and quick actions that are crucial in implementing new ideas and innovations.
It also helps employees share their thoughts and exchange ideas, which ultimately enhances
competitiveness (Altamimi et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2005).
Open communication and free ﬂow of information are other important elements of a
decentralised organisation structure. These elements facilitate discussion of ideas and solu-
tions with peers and other related groups, increasing knowledge and fostering creativity,
which are core aspects of organisation innovation.

3.4 Organisation innovation and Industry 4.0
In the current era of Industry 4.0, innovation is a critical factor for the success of organisations.
This latest wave of innovation covers all types of innovation, and organisations need to be
ready for its implementation to gain a competitive advantage. Innovation involves generating
and implementing new ideas, procedures, and processes that enhance the organisation’s
productivity and offer superior products and services. Therefore, innovation should be a core

the design, operations, processes, and services of production systems, and has consequences
for future jobs and management by creating new business models. Therefore, organisations
must be adaptable to innovation and implement the latest manufacturing systems derived
from Industry 4.0.
The successful implementation of Industry 4.0 requires the involvement of employees,
systems, procedures, and structure. It requires proper innovation-based strategies to develop
organisational capabilities. However, many employees are not aware of the new trends and
innovative requirements, which indicates that organisations may be disregarding the impor-

impact the company’s proﬁtability and performance. Therefore, organisations need to priori-

the current era of globalisation (´Slusarczyk, 2018).
To effectively implement the new wave of the Industrial Revolution, Industry 4.0, organisa-

systems, procedures, and structure, all of which must be aligned towards the successful
integration of Industry 4.0 applications. Proper innovation-based strategies are required to
develop the necessary organisation capabilities to handle these new technologies. Mohelska

in processes and products (Ibarra et al., 2018). Meeting these challenges requires setting
company goals and effective leadership. Leaders must have up-to-date knowledge to handle
the situation effectively, and this can only be achieved through knowledge oriented leadership.
Furthermore, knowledge based organisations face challenges in providing innovative solu-
tions for Industry 4.0 by effectively exploiting knowledge resources. This knowledge based
view requires ﬁrms to create value through effective management and competitive position-
ing. Leaders must manage knowledge acquisition and application effectively to inﬂuence

The fast-paced changes brought by Industry 4.0 require quick decisions and effective

3.6 Electronic human resource management and Industry 4.0
Industry 4.0 has signiﬁcant implications for electronic human resource management (E-
HRM), which can deﬁne the success of Industry 4.0. Studies from various countries have
indicated that the successful implementation of Industry 4.0 is highly dependent on the
successful training of individuals and competent HR staff. Therefore, E-HRM is an integral
part of the implementation as it can equip individuals to receive training and qualiﬁcations
through video training, virtual reality aids, robotic training, etc. Additionally, E-HRM tools
can help to record, assess, and analyse the fast-track Industry 4.0 performances and suggest
improvements based on the data. E-HRM has a direct hand in appointing and implementing
policies and practices that are best suited for the employees in the Industry 4.0 world (Rana
& Sharma, 2019).
The structure of the technological model has changed from being "knowledge and inno-
vation" to "human intelligence, innovation, and technologies" in the new Industry 4.0
environment, indicating that E-HRM is an essential supporting component of Industry 4.0.
HRM can contribute to the progression and success of Industry 4.0 by adapting to newer tech-
nologies. E-HRM equips the department with the tools and facilities necessary to develop a
new workforce with digital mindsets, in addition to exploring their competencies in interact-

respective roles in the Industry 4.0 workforce.
E-HRM can promote work innovation, empower people, and build their competences
through digital tools and enable them to tackle the new Industry 4.0 challenges more efﬁ-

the employees who manually operate the machines in the Industry 4.0 world (Bissola &

3.7 Decentralised organisation structure and Industry 4.0
Decentralised decision-making has become a crucial factor for the success of Industry 4.0,
which refers to the future of industrial production. This approach reduces the complexity
involved in the decision-making process and contributes to the achievement of the revolution.
To prevent digitalisation issues in the industry, it is essential to adopt proper strategies such
as horizontal and vertical integration, as well as decentralisation decision-making. Decen-
tralisation is an imperative aspect that Industry 4.0 requires for controlling and advancing
production needs, which is not possible through conventional approaches.
Future-oriented production features important characteristics such as autonomy and
decentralisation, which are key elements for the success of Industry 4.0. Hompel’s research
demonstrated the signiﬁcance of autonomy and decentralisation by explaining that engineers
at Fraunhofer IML were contemplating an ideal manufacturing system using pencil and paper.
After much discussion, they only drew a shelf at the edge of the paper, indicating that all
involved agreed that it was the main part of warehousing in logistics. The rest of the paper
was left empty, signifying that the idea of autonomy and decentralisation could cope with
volatile market demands, an uncertain future, and the complexity associated with adapting
to new systems (Ten Hompel & Henke, 2014). This illustrates that a centralised approach in
organisational structure is being replaced by a decentralised organisational structure.
To sustain and overcome the challenges involved in implementing Industry 4.0, Stock
and Seliger (2016) proposed three approaches. The ﬁrst approach is to increase efﬁciency in
training through the use of information and communication technology (ICT) technologies.
The second approach is to support decentralised decision making and intrinsic motivation,
which is crucial due to the increased complexity in the manufacturing system. A decentralised
organisationstructurecanautonomouslyconsiderlocalinformation,makingrelateddecisions
through artiﬁcial intelligence. This approach is necessary to efﬁciently allocate materials and
products in the Industry 4.0 manufacturing system. The third approach proposed by Stock
and Seliger (2016) is fostering creativity.
A sustainably-oriented decentralised organisation structure is essential in Industry 4.0
manufacturing systems. This is because the complexity of the manufacturing system can-
not be effectively managed through a centralised approach. Decentralised decision-making
allows for local information to be considered autonomously, leading to efﬁcient allocation
of resources. Holistic efﬁciency in resource allocation is a signiﬁcant advantage of Industry
4.0, and a decentralised organisation structure can help achieve this.
H7 Decentralised organisation structure has a positive relationship with readiness for Indus-
try 4.0.

3.11 Research methodology
This research employs a mixed-method approach to thoroughly examine the readiness for
Industry 4.0, combining both quantitative and qualitative methodologies to capture a holistic
view of complex phenomena. The quantitative aspect utilises a cross-sectional design, lever-
aging SPSS for descriptive statistics and Smart PLS for structural and measurement model
analysis. This statistical framework allows for a detailed examination of the intricate interac-
tions that affect Industry 4.0 readiness across diverse organisational contexts. By quantifying
relationships and measuring key variables, this approach provides a robust foundation for
generalisability and establishes patterns that can be statistically validated. The rigorous anal-
ysis enables researchers to identify trends and correlations, offering empirical evidence that
can inform strategic decision-making.
Simultaneously,theresearchincorporatesaqualitativecomponentthroughthematicanaly-
sis of participant interviews. This qualitative methodology not only validates but also enriches
the ﬁndings of quantitative analysis by offering deeper insights into the nuanced experiences
and perceptions of stakeholders regarding Industry 4.0. Qualitative data allows for the explo-
ration of context-speciﬁc factors, revealing complexities that quantitative measures alone
may overlook. Its insights can illuminate the ‘why’ behind the quantitative results, allowing
for richer interpretations and more actionable conclusions. This layer of understanding is
critical in a rapidly evolving ﬁeld like Industry 4.0, where the understanding of technological
change can vary signiﬁcantly across different organisations.
Mixed methods research effectively integrates qualitative and quantitative procedures to
mitigate the limitations inherent in each approach (Venkatesh et al., 2013, 2016). By com-
bining these methodologies, the study addresses both conﬁrmatory and explanatory research
questions, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the phenomena under investi-
gation. Furthermore, the scarcity of existing research on management practices for Industry
4.0 underscores the necessity of this mixed-methods approach. By aligning with three pri-
mary objectives; developmental, completeness, and expansion, this study aims to foster a
more nuanced understanding of readiness for Industry 4.0.
3.12 Study 1: quantitative approach
This study adopts a positivist philosophy and a deductive approach (Cooper & Schindler,
2014). The social science tactics of progressive schema from research questions, hypothesis
development and data analysis are followed to achieve objectivity in explaining the phenom-
ena (Bell et al., 2022). This approach is employed to develop ten hypotheses describing the
relationships between three independent variables (knowledge oriented leadership, electronic
human resource management and decentralised organisation structure), a mediator (organ-
isation innovation) and a dependent variable (readiness for Industry 4.0). It facilitates the
application of methodologies that are appropriate for the current research problem.
The initial part of the study employs a quantitative and cross-sectional design to collect and
analyse data. The use of a questionnaire and mathematical techniques enables the quantitative
analysis of the antecedents of organisation innovation, with a focus on management practices.
The study also examines the mediating role of organisational innovation and the moderating
effect of an aging workforce. The cross-sectional study design is suitable for collecting data
from manufacturing companies’ representatives at a speciﬁc point in time and is widely
adopted in social science research.

3.13 Data collection
The present study focuses on manufacturing companies in Malaysia that are registered with
the Federation of Malaysian Manufacturers (FMM) (Federation of Malaysian Manufacturers
[FMM] (2019). The FMM is recommended by scholars Long and Khairuzzaman (2008)
and highlighted by the Malaysian Investment Development Authority [MIDA] (2018). Most
of the manufacturing companies listed in FMM are in Selangor, followed by Johor Bahru,
Penang and Perak, which are known as business hubs in Malaysia. The research population
is deﬁned as manufacturing companies in Malaysia, and the survey respondents are restricted
to Owner, CEO/Director, General Manager, Manager and Executives due to their signiﬁcant
experience and better vision of the ﬁrm’s activities (Bahari et al., 2018).
A simple Random Sampling technique was used to select companies from the list provided
by FMM. A survey questionnaire was used to collect data, consisting of two sections. The ﬁrst
section includes the demographics of the respondent and the company, including the aging
workforce measured by a single question. The responses were divided into "Low Aging
Workforce" and "High Aging Workforce" by using the median split technique. The second
section comprises items for six variables measured on a ﬁve-point Likert scale. A total of
237 responses were received, which is sufﬁcient for Structural Equation Modelling (SEM)
(Hair et al., 2010).
3.14 Instrument
The instrument for all six variables is adapted from different well-cited research articles.
Firstly, knowledge oriented leadership was measured through six items adapted from Donate
and de Pablo (2015). Items help to seek information related to learning and innovation, actions
of workers, reward to knowledge sharer and manager’s role in implementation. Exemplary
items include “Our company managers assume the role of knowledge leaders as a mediator
for sharing and applying knowledge”.
The instrument for electronic human resource management was adopted from the study
of Hooi (2006). A total of 12 items are ﬁnalised for four dimensions of E-HRM. The dimen-
sionsincludeE.RecruitmentandSelection,E-Learning,E-CompensationandE-Performance
Appraisal. Exemplary items include “Training in our company is done through e-learning”.
The decentralised organisation structure was measured through four items adapted from the
study of Ferrell and Skinner (1988) and Willem et al. (2007). Items are added to measure the
autonomy and ﬂexibility of employees in taking any actions. An example includes “In our
company, any decision employees make needs higher management approval”.
To measure organisational innovation, six items were adapted from García-Morales et al.
(2012). Items were added to measure the product and technological innovation of the organ-
isation. An example of an item includes “Our company’s emphasis is on technological
innovation”. Finally, the readiness for Industry 4.0 is measured through the nine dimen-
sions with three items each. The dimensions include Industry 4.0 Strategy, Industry 4.0
Leadership, Industry 4.0 Customer, Industry 4.0 Products, Industry 4.0 Operation, Industry
4.0 Culture, Industry 4.0 People, Industry 4.0 Governance, and Industry 4.0 Technology. A
total of 27 items are adopted from Schumacher et al. (2016) that including but not limited to
“Our company possesses adequate resources for the realisation of Industry 4.0” and “Our
company managers are willing to face the challenges of Industry 4.0 activities”.

3.21 Study 2: qualitative approach
In the second part of the research, a qualitative study was conducted. The primary aim of the
study was to gain a nuanced understanding of participants’ comprehension of Industry 4.0,
assess organisational readiness, and explore the challenges, strategies, and future outlooks
associated with its implementation. The study focused on nine participants actively engaged
in top management roles across diverse manufacturing sectors (Food & Beverages, Construc-
tion, Oil & Gas, and Electrical & Electronics) within Malaysia. Malaysia is known for its
diverse and thriving business sectors, and these sectors are actively contributing to the coun-
try’s economic growth. Each industrial sector has its unique challenges and opportunities. The
selection of participants employed a purposive sampling method, aligning with Creswell’s
(2014) approach. This method ensured the inclusion of individuals with unique characteris-
tics and valuable insights relevant to the study’s objectives. The deliberate selection targeted
top management roles, which could include CEOs, Owners, Directors, General Managers,
or managers, to capture diverse perspectives. This targeted sampling strategy enhances the
study’s capacity to uncover nuanced insights and perspectives essential for understanding the
multifaceted dynamics of Industry 4.0 within the Malaysian urban planning landscape.
For the collection of primary data, the researcher engaged employees in semi-structured
interviews. In contrast, secondary data were sourced from reliable online platforms, scholarly
articles, books, databases, and reports related to the concept. The researcher also attempted
to review company-speciﬁc data from various ﬁrms in Malaysia, although obtaining inter-
nal data proved difﬁcult due to the companies’ reluctance to share this information. The
semi-structured nature of the interviews facilitated a more interactive session, where the
researcher could interject for further explanations or explore deeper into the responses (Bell
et al., 2022). These interviews featured open-ended questions, prompting spontaneous and

participants, interviews were carried out in English.
On an important note, participants were informed that the data they provided would be

Exploring Industry 4.0’s impact on urban planning in diverse Malaysian industries, a the-

from in-depth interviews, key points were marked, forming foundational themes. Validation
ensured coherence and alignment with participants’ perspectives. Each theme received a
clear name and was synthesised into a narrative using direct quotes, portraying how Industry
4.0 inﬂuences urban planning. This research offers insights into the understanding and inter-
section of Industry 4.0 in Malaysian urban planning, which is crucial for decision-makers
navigating technological transformations in diverse industries.
A comprehensive thematic analysis was employed to uncover key themes and sub-themes
from participant interviews. The research, delineated around intrinsic and extrinsic motiva-

a thematic synthesis, encapsulating the data in a detailed table complemented by a narrative

through in-depth interviews.
3.23 Discussion on quantitative analysis
The research embarked on a comprehensive exploration of 17 objectives, concentrating on
assessing the readiness of the Malaysian manufacturing sector for Industry 4.0. The study
employed a mixed-methods approach, with the quantitative component focusing on the dual
role of organisational innovation, speciﬁcally investigating the impact of knowledge ori-
ented leadership, electronic human resource management, and decentralised organisational
structures. In parallel, a qualitative facet was incorporated through thematic analysis, thus
enriching the ﬁndings with deeper insights derived from participant interviews.
Quantitative results unveiled a positive correlation between knowledge oriented leadership

is consistent with previous literature, which highlights the crucial role of leadership in driving
organisational change (Donate & de Pablo, 2015; Shamim et al., 2016). Knowledge oriented
leadership is especially valuable in promoting innovation, as it focuses on creating and sharing
knowledge. This type of leadership is therefore considered vital in preparing organisations
for Industry 4.0, which involves the adoption of advanced technologies and processes.
The quantitative study also found that electronic human resource management is a signif-

3.24 Discussion of qualitative analysis
Key ﬁndings of this qualitative study unveiled a spectrum of understanding, ranging from a
conceptual view of Industry 4.0 as a transformative era to a practical grasp of speciﬁc tech-
nological applications. Organisational readiness emerged as a complex interplay involving
conﬁdence, challenges, and strategic planning. Signiﬁcant hurdles such as budget constraints,
skill shortages, and resistance to change were identiﬁed, highlighting the multifaceted nature
of preparing organisations for Industry 4.0 (see Table 8). The same challenges are highlighted
in Peres et al. (2020) and Mourtzis et al. (2022). Participants articulated diverse strategies for
Industry 4.0 adoption, encompassing gradual planning, targeted technology adoption, and
a focus on process improvement, similar to Pozzi et al. (2023). The study further revealed
varying stages of adoption, from partial integration to organisations yet to embark on the
Industry 4.0 journey.
Additionally, the research delved into the roles of management practices and ageing work-
ers. The participants agree with the support of knowledge oriented leadership and structure
of the organisation for Industry 4.0. They believe that the Industry 4.0 adoption requires a
facilitator and role model, which are better served by knowledge oriented leadership with a
forward-looking approach and growth mindset (Ramezani et al., 2017). Contrary to the results
of quantitative research, the participants shared their positive attitude toward a decentralised
organisation structure. The ﬁndings revealed that this structure improves decision-making
and reﬂects the adaptability required for technological advancements.
Finally, the study helps to recognise ageing workers for their valuable experience and
the challenges associated with conservative knowledge. It suggests a balanced approach that
values experience while encouraging continuous learning and adaptation to technological
advancements. Overall, leadership emerged as a critical factor, with knowledge oriented
leaders identiﬁed as facilitators for successful Industry 4.0 adoption. The decentralised
organisational structure was scrutinised for its potential beneﬁts in decision-making and
problem-solving. The qualitative study concludes with recommendations for organisations,
thus underscoring the importance of creating awareness, starting small, prioritising process
improvement, and fostering a friendly ecosystem for Industry 4.0 adoption. These ﬁndings
contribute valuable insights into the dynamic landscape of Industry 4.0, offering a foundation
for informed decision-making and strategic planning among professionals and organisations
navigating this transformative industrial revolution.

3.25 Implications and future recommendations
To the best of the researcher’s knowledge, this study is the ﬁrst to investigate the readiness of
the Malaysian manufacturing sector for Industry 4.0. The ﬁndings provide valuable insights
and recommendations for researchers and practitioners. The study further emphasises the dual
role of organisational innovation in Industry 4.0 readiness, identifying knowledge oriented
leadership and electronic human resource management (e-HRM) as essential prerequisites for
fostering innovation and Industry 4.0 adoption. These factors not only facilitate the transition
to Industry 4.0 but also open new avenues for further research.
The ﬁndings hold signiﬁcant implications for managers and industry professionals prepar-
ing for Industry 4.0 technologies. Knowledge oriented leadership, with its emphasis on
creating and sharing knowledge, is identiﬁed as a key driver of innovation and organisational
transformation. This insight is particularly valuable for senior management and HR depart-
ments, who play instrumental roles in shaping organisational culture and driving innovation.
The study provides a foundation for companies to rethink leadership strategies, ensuring
that knowledge sharing practices are institutionalised to enhance overall readiness for digital
transformation.
Additionally, the research offers practical guidance for leveraging eHRM to improve
organisational efﬁciency and support Industry 4.0 adoption. The integration of technol-
ogy into HR practices, such as electronic recruitment, e-learning, and digital performance
appraisal systems, allows ﬁrms to streamline processes, overcome geographic constraints,
and secure the right talent for technological transitions. These systems also foster employee
engagement and trust through transparent, efﬁcient processes, thus facilitating a smooth tran-
sition to Industry 4.0. Managers are encouraged to expand the use of eHRM systems to ensure
human resources align with the organisation’s technological goals. Further, the study high-
lights the importance of addressing the unique challenges posed by an ageing workforce.
Managers should develop tailored strategies that leverage the experience of older workers
while promoting continuous learning and adaptation to new technologies. Finally, the study’s
results will beneﬁt government bodies, including the Ministry of Human Resources (MoHR),
Ministry of Finance (MOF), and Ministry of Science, Technology, and Innovation (MOSTI),
as key stakeholders in shaping Industry 4.0 policy. The ﬁndings offer critical insights for pol-
icymakers as they develop strategies to support Industry 4.0 adoption in the manufacturing
sector.
This research serves as a foundational reference for future studies, particularly in the con-
text of developing countries such as South Africa, Malaysia, India, and Pakistan. As Industry
4.0 is still in its developmental phase in these regions, future researchers can build upon
this study to further investigate organisational readiness and foster awareness about Industry
4.0’s implications. This research also laid a foundation for future researchers of developed
countries in exploring the emerging area of Industry 5.0 and its readiness for organisation.
Investigating the transition from Industry 4.0 to Industry 5.0 could provide insights into how
advanced technologies such as artiﬁcial intelligence and the Internet of Things (IoT) can
further elevate manufacturing and other sectors. In addition, a more detailed examination of
the enablers of Industry 4.0 adoption in diverse industrial contexts is needed to provide a
holistic understanding of regional disparities and sector-speciﬁc challenges. The scope can
be expanded beyond manufacturing to include services, education, and information technol-
ogy (IT). Furthermore, researchers may focus on cross-country comparisons to assess how
cultural, economic, and technological differences have an impact on Industry 4.0 adoption
rates.

4 Conclusion
This research makes a signiﬁcant contribution to academic literature and the practical under-
standing of Industry 4.0. The study successfully integrates quantitative rigour with qualitative
insights through a mixed-methods approach, offering a comprehensive perspective on key
organisational factors such as knowledge oriented leadership and electronic human resource
management (e-HRM). These factors play a crucial role in fostering innovation and enabling
companies to adapt to the evolving demands of Industry 4.0. The quantitative component
of the study effectively addresses 17 objectives, thus providing a thorough examination of
how various organisational practices drive innovation within the Industry 4.0 context. The
study further adds value by offering insights into the dynamics that stimulate organisational
readiness and adaptation in response to technological advancements. Central to this research
is the importance of knowledge oriented leadership in fostering innovation, as well as the
role of e-HRM in enhancing employee efﬁciency. These ﬁndings offer practical insights for
organisations seeking to strengthen their foundations within the Industry 4.0 framework. The
dual role of organisation innovation and its interaction with demographic groups, particu-
larly within the ageing workforce, is the highlight of this research. This often-overlooked
demographic aspect in Industry 4.0 studies is given speciﬁc attention, demonstrating how
different labour groups contribute to and are impacted by innovation in this rapidly evolving
landscape.
The study’s thematic analysis enriches the research by capturing deep insights from par-
ticipants, thus complementing the quantitative data. The qualitative investigation provides
a nuanced understanding of Industry 4.0, its related challenges, and the strategies organi-
sations should adopt. The study further identiﬁes signiﬁcant challenges and opportunities
in managing the change and the demographic shift, offering practical recommendations for
organisations to navigate the complexities of an ageing workforce in a technology-driven
environment of Industry 4.0.
The use of novel data provides fresh empirical evidence to inform the growing discourse
on Industry 4.0. This paper thus provides a comprehensive and insightful contribution to
the understanding of organisational innovation in the context of Industry 4.0. By blending
quantitative achievements with qualitative depth, the study not only advances theoretical
knowledge but also offers practical recommendations for academic researchers and industry
professionals as they navigate the intricate challenges and opportunities presented by digital
transformation.
