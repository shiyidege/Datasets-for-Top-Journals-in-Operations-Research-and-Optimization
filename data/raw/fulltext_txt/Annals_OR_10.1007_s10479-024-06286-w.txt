Abstract
In this paper, we examine the implications of using different strongly efﬁcient Decision
Making Units (DMUs) as the Most Preferred Solution (MPS) in Value Efﬁciency Analysis
(VEA) models with variable returns to scale. We provide protocols for consistent MPS
choices, where a MPS choice is consistent if and only if the resulting VEA technology does
not allow for free and/or unlimited production. In particular, we show that either a DMU that
belongs to the set of strongly CCR-efﬁcient DMUs or a combination of jointly strongly CCR-
efﬁcient DMUs constitute the only consistent MPS choices for both constant-returns-to-scale
and variable-returns-to-scale VEA models. In addition, we show that the scale properties of
the efﬁcient VEA frontier are affected by the choice of the MPS and in essence depend on
the range of the interval between the MPS’s right- and left-side scale elasticities.
Keywords Value efﬁciency analysis · DEA · Variable returns to scale · Most preferred
solution · Free production
1 Introduction
The variable-returns-to-scale (VRS) Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) model introduced by
Banker, Charnes, and Cooper (henceforth BCC model) in their 1984 Management Science
paper is widely considered as one of Rajiv Banker’s most important contributions to the
literature of non-parametric performance evaluation. The BCC model is perhaps the most
useful extension to the seminal constant-returns-to-scale (CRS) DEA model of Charnes et al.
(1978) (henceforth CCR), as well as a generalization of Afriat (1972) VRS technology and
of Førsund and Hjalmarsson (1979) notion of scale efﬁciency into the multi-output case.
By estimating pure technical efﬁciency at a given scale of operation, the BCC DEA model
provided a “ﬁne-tuning” that accounts for scale variations of the assessed decision making

are several instances in which sizeable differences between the assessed DMUs mandate that
small-scaled DMUs are not benchmarked against large-sized ones. The BCC paper is today
one of the most cited works in the DEA literature, while the BCC DEA model is–either alone
or along with the CCR model–the most frequent modeling choice in DEA applications.1
However, there are several concerns with the BCC model when applying it in certain
performance evaluation settings. The ﬁrst one is related to the estimation of super-efﬁciency
(Andersen & Petersen, 1993), where it has been noticed by Thrall (1996) and formally
shown by Seiford and Zhu (1999) that the BCC super-efﬁciency model may be infeasible for
some DMUs, something which precludes the complete ranking of efﬁcient DMUs. Different
approaches to deal with this issue have been developed by, among others, Xue and Harker
(2002), Chen (2005), Cook et al. (2009).
The second is related to the decomposition of productivity changes by means of the
Malmquist index when VRS is assumed for the reference technology, i.e., the BCC model
is used to deﬁne the associated distance functions as in Ray and Desli (1997). Then, the
estimation of mixed-period distance functions may result in an infeasible solution for some
DMUs and for these DMUs we cannot measure the impact of technical change (Färe et al.,
1997). Computationally, this issue is related to the previous one since mixed period-distance
functions are estimated by means of linear programs formally equivalent to those of super-
efﬁciency. However, the practical implications of the two issues are different.
The third is related to cross-efﬁciency (Sexton et al., 1986). When Wu et al. (2009)
introduced cross-efﬁciency evaluation by means of the BCC model, they noticed that in
its input-oriented form it is possible for the cross-efﬁciency scores of some DMUs to have
negative values, which is difﬁcult to interpret. Lim and Zhu (2015) have formally shown that
negative cross efﬁciency scores are unacceptable from a production point of view since they
are associated with the free production of output. They have proposed an approach rooted in
translating the Cartesian coordinate system to obtain cross-efﬁciency scores from the BCC
model that comply with production assumptions.
The fourth is related to weight restrictions and their dual production trade-offs, the incor-
poration of which may result in the multiplier form of the BCC model to have an infeasible
solution for some or all evaluated DMUs (Allen et al., 1997)–which consequently means
that the dual envelopment form has an unbounded solution–or to assign in some DMUs
negative efﬁciency scores. Podinovski and Bouzdine-Chameeva (2013, 2015) have shown
that such inconsistent outcomes arise when the introduction of the chosen weight restrictions
and their dual production trade-offs result in a technology allowing for free and/or unlimited
production.2
The ﬁfth is related to Value Efﬁciency Analysis (VEA) (Halme et al., 1999), where man-
agerial preferences are introduced through the choice of the Most Preferred Solution (MPS),
namely a strongly efﬁcient DMU or a combination of strongly jointly efﬁcient DMUs that has
the most preferred input/output bundle. The VEA frontier is then constructed by extending the
DEA-efﬁcient facets that intercept at the MPS. Korhonen et al. (2002) discussed the case of
a VRS VEA frontier using a set of two DMUs as the MPS, in which the input-oriented radial
target for some inefﬁcient DMU is not producible as it requires a negative input quantity.
Their explanation (pp. 62–63) was that “you can never make [the inefﬁcient DMU] equally

The aim of this paper is to address this gap in the VEA literature, by providing a com-
prehensive examination of alternative MPS choices in VRS VEA models. The need for
this assessment is attenuated by the fact that VRS constitutes the most frequent modelling
choice in empirical applications of VEA. Thus, it is necessary to establish protocols that will
facilitate the identiﬁcation of problematic MPS choices in VRS VEA models. Our point of
departure is the recent work of Ravanos and Karagiannis (2023) relating VEA with DEA
models with weight restrictions or their dual production trade-offs. Based on this, we can
associate MPS choices with the (in)consistency of the underlying production trade-offs and
to the related violations of production axioms. There is a variety of MPS choices in the lit-
erature (see Ravanos and Karagiannis (2022) for a comparative review) but the justiﬁcation
for one choice or another is grounded on an intuitive and not on a theoretical basis. The
contribution of the present paper is that it provides such a theoretical justiﬁcation for certain
MPS choices.
In particular, we prove that, in a VRS VEA model, the choice of a strongly BCC-efﬁcient
but not strongly CCR-efﬁcient DMU as the MPS results in an efﬁcient frontier, which does
not comply with production axioms. On the contrary, the choice of a strongly CCR-efﬁcient
DMU as the MPS results in an efﬁcient frontier which does not violate production axioms,
either in the VRS or the CRS VEA model. In addition, we verify that combinations of jointly
efﬁcient DMUs constitute inconsistent MPS for the VRS VEA model if they include at least
one strongly BCC-efﬁcient which is not strongly CCR-efﬁcient. We also show that consistent
MPS choices affect the scale properties of the resulting VRS VEA frontier, which are not
necessarily variable. We then illustrate how we can determine the scale properties of the VRS
VEA frontier by estimating the range of the interval between the MPS’s right- and left-side
scale elasticities and we discuss the implications of this for the choice of the MPS in VRS
VEA models.
The rest of the paper unfolds as follows: In the next section we present the materials and
methods used in the paper. These include the CCR and BCC models, the CRS and VRS VEA
models, and their relation with DEA models with weight restrictions. In the third section,
we present the main results and illustrate them by means of several examples. Concluding
remarks follow in the last section.

or some analytical criterion to choose the MPS. A number of different suggestions have
been made in the literature, which are compared and contrasted in Ravanos and Karagian-
nis (2022). An overview of alternative MPS choices in empirical VEA studies is given in

Similar problems may arise from the choice of MPS in VEA. In fact, Korhonen et al.

the MPS or to violation of technology assumptions, but it was considered as an undesirable
feature of the VEA model. Now using the relation between VEA and DEA models with
trade-offs, we can verify that such ﬁndings provide empirical evidence for the violation of
production axioms. They show up as negative input-oriented efﬁciency scores but they may
go unnoticed in an output-oriented model. From Table 1 we can see that VRS and an output
orientation are very frequent choices in empirical VEA applications. However, these studies
do not examine the possible violation of production axioms in their VRS VEA model. In the
next section we investigate these matters in detail in order to establish protocols for MPS
choices that do not result in violation of production axioms.
3 Main results
In this section we use the relation between VEA and DEA models considering production
trade-offs as our point of departure for deriving protocols for making consistent MPS choices.
Due to the equivalence of VEA and DEA models with weight restrictions such as those in (11)
or (12), our meaning of consistent MPS choices is closely related to the notion of consistent
weight restrictions in Podinovski and Bouzdine-Chameeva (2015). In particular, we deﬁne a
consistent MPS as follows:
Deﬁnition 1 A MPS choice is consistent if and only if the radial target of any inefﬁcient DMU
in the resulting VEA frontier is always producible and the VEA efﬁciency score retains its
meaning as a realistic improvement factor.
The fulﬁllment of these two conditions depends on whether the enlargement of the DEA
technology in (1) or (2), resulting from the choice of the MPS in a VEA model, is consis-
tent with stated production axioms. As with the case of inconsistent weight restrictions, an
inconsistent MPS choice will result in free and/or unlimited production. We can thus state
the deﬁnition of consistent MPS choices equivalently as follows:

choices for both CRS and VRS VEA models.

4 Concluding remarks
In this paper, we provide a number of protocols for consistent MPS choices in VEA. First,
when the MPS is a single DMU among those that are BCC-efﬁcient, it is a consistent choice
for the VRS VEA model if it is also strongly CCR-efﬁcient. Otherwise, the resulting VRS
VEA frontier does not comply with production axioms. Second, a set of BCC-efﬁcient DMUs
is a consistent MPS choice for the VRS VEA model only if these DMUs are strongly and
23 These results complements earlier ﬁndings in the literature (Podinovski and Bouzdine-Chameeva, 2020;
Tone, 2001) on the returns to scale of efﬁcient DMUs with respect to the conventional DEA and the expanded
(i.e., with weight restrictions) technology. In addition, Proposition 2 and Remark 7 provide more insights on
the assertion in Thanassoulis et al., (2004, p. 132) that “if we impose a constraint on the trade-off between
inputs and outputs (ARII) we are implicitly forcing a certain type of RTS in relation to which all DMUs are to
be assessed”. However, our results go one step further as they provide a way to gauge the scale properties of the
resulting expanded VEA technology by means of information about the MPS’s right- and left-side elasticity
estimates.

CCR-efﬁcient. Third, inconsistencies do not arise in a CRS VEA model, provided that the
MPS is a strongly CCR-efﬁcient DMUs or a set of strongly and jointly CCR-efﬁcient DMUs.
Fourth, (in)consistent MPS choices will remain as such in a (enlarged) reduced dataset of
observed DMUs that includes the chosen DMU(s).
Furthermore, we have also shown that the VRS VEA technology deﬁned by a consistent
MPS choice may not exhibit variable returns to scale, as is implied by using the BCC model,
but its scale properties depend on the choice of the MPS. We show that these can be deduced
by estimating the range of the interval between the right- and left-side scale elasticities of
the chosen DMU, or those of an artiﬁcial DMU constructed as a combination of the chosen
DMUs if the MPS is a set of jointly efﬁcient DMUs. This implies that, when choosing the
most desirable input/output bundle in a VRS VEA model, a decision maker implicitly makes
a choice regarding the type of returns to scale in the resulting technology.
Our ﬁndings provide protocols to avoid inconsistent MPS choices in VRS VEA models,
which may not be manifested by means of infeasible or unbounded models and efﬁciency
scores having unacceptable values. This is possible by restricting the MPS choice among
the strongly CCR-efﬁcient DMUs or sets of strongly CCR-efﬁcient DMUs that are jointly
efﬁcient in the CCR model. In addition to providing efﬁciency estimates based on a tech-
nology consistent with production axioms, this can facilitate the choice of the MPS by DMs
since it exposes them to a smaller array of potential MPS choices. Information about the
scale properties of the resulting VRS VEA technology associated with the alternative con-
sistent MPS choices can be used to provide DMs with additional insights about the resulting
representation of the technology deﬁned by their choices.
Nevertheless, a DM may insist on a MPS choice that is inconsistent for the set of observed
DMUs at hand, either because it reﬂects her/his underlying views on the most desirable
input/output bundle, or because the choice is mandated by a certain criterion used for choosing
the MPS (e.g., a superefﬁciency score), which the DM ﬁnds reliable. As such choices should
not be introduced in the VRS VEA model with the current set of observed DMUs, a promising
