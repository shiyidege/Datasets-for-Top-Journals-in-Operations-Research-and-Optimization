Abstract
Driven by the increasing integration of renewable energy, power dispatch has become 
a critical task in high-voltage power systems (HPS). To ensure reliable operation of the 
HPS, it is essential to evaluate the transmission capability of the HPS and identify feasible 
transmission line routings that support effective dispatch decisions. In the HPS, power ca­
pacity should be multistate since the power generators and conductors may exist in states 
such as complete failure, partial failure, and maintenance. Therefore, the HPS is modeled 
as a multistate power grid network (MPGN) in this study, where power plants, substa­
tions, and transmission lines are represented by sources, vertices, and edges, respectively. 
Network reliability, defined as the probability that the MPGN can successfully transmit 
the required power to meet demand, is adopted to assess the performance of the HPS. A 
novel algorithm is proposed based on the concept of minimal paths to identify all mini­
mal power vectors, which are used not only to calculate network reliability but also to 
determine feasible transmission line routings. To validate the practicality of the proposed 
algorithm, a real-world MPGN in Taiwan is demonstrated to measure the network reli­
ability and observe transmission line routings. Results reveal that different MPVs offer 
varying trade-offs between utilization and redundancy of the MPGN, which provides the 
administrator with practical options for dispatch planning.

1  Introduction
High-voltage power system (HPS) plays a crucial role in ensuring reliable power trans­
mission, and serves as the backbone of industrial development. Historically, HPS has pre­
dominantly relied on fossil fuel-based power plants, including natural gas, coal, and nuclear 
energy. However, growing concerns over climate change have prompted governments 
worldwide to pursue sustainable energy development as part of their decarbonization plans 
(Nations, 2015). With this global trend, renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, and 
hydropower are increasingly being integrated into the HPS. While these renewable sources 
contribute significantly to reducing carbon emissions and promoting environmental sus­
tainability, they also introduce considerable variability and uncertainty into power supply 
(Sousa et al., 2025). This results in increased uncertainty in power generation, posing new 
challenges for both power dispatch and the reliability of the HPS (Zhang et al., 2024).
To address these challenges, power dispatch has become a crucial task in the HPS, bal­
ancing supply and demand while ensuring operational safety and reliability (Wang et al.,

2023). Among the many factors, including economic dispatch (Feng et al., 2025; Ouanes 
et al., 2025) and voltage balance (Qin et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), influencing dispatch 
decisions, transmission line routing is especially significant, as it determines the minimal 
power capacity that each power plant and transmission line must support, directly impacting 
the reliability and performance of the HPS. The transmission line routing problem is widely 
discussed in computer system (Hossain et al., 2019; Okafor et al., 2023) and power system 
(Glaum & Hofmann, 2023; Yu et al., 2023). Several optimization models (Glaum & Hof­
mann, 2023; Jin et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2022, 2023, 2024) have been developed to search for 
optimal transmission line routings. Jin et al. (2019) proposed a wind scenario-driven power 
loss cost model to minimize the costs associated with offshore wind farm power systems, 
and employed an adaptive particle swarm optimization algorithm to reduce costs effectively. 
Furthermore, Yu et al. (2022) explored the optimal layout of transmission lines by using a 
multi-objective optimization model, where the objective is to maximize on-grid renewable 
energy and minimize costs, focusing on power storage and the impact of demand response. 
Although these studies have improved power dispatch from various perspectives, the HPS 
they consider are deterministic. Real-world HPS is subject to various uncertainties, such as 
generator outages, transmission failures, fluctuating renewable inputs, and demand variabil­
ity (Zhu et al., 2023). Therefore, to comprehensively evaluate the performance of the HPS 
under uncertainty, a reliability index needs to be established to evaluate the capability of the 
HPS under uncertain operating conditions.
From the perspective of power supply management, reliability has long been a crucial 
performance indicator for evaluating the capability of the HPS. Numerous studies have 
proposed methods to assess HPS reliability, most employing simulation-based approaches 
(Cervan et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2019). For instance, Yu et al. (2019) established the best-fit 
probability distribution of renewable energy in Taiwan and treated it as input to the Monte 
Carlo sampling reliability evaluation process. In parallel, dynamic thermal rating (DTR) 
(Lai & Teh, 2022; Su et al., 2023) has emerged as an effective method to enhance the reli­
ability of the HPS. Lai and Teh (2022) proposed a framework combining DTR, battery 
storage systems, and network topology optimization to enhance wind power integration 
and grid reliability, effectively addressing key challenges such as network congestion, wind 
curtailment, and high operational costs. Su et al. (2023) introduced a distributed cyber-
physical-social system for optimal dispatching in AC/DC hybrid distribution systems with 
electric vehicles. Their study used cloud-edge-device cooperation to optimize renewable 
energy and electric vehicle dispatch, enhancing efficiency and voltage stability across day-
ahead, intra-day, and real-time operations. Lawal and Teh (2023) developed a novel algo­
rithm for forecasting DTR to optimize power transmission line power capacity and enhance 
grid security by utilizing historical weather data and machine learning techniques. In con­
junction with these modeling approaches, various indices have traditionally been employed 
to measure the reliability of the HPS, including loss of load probability (LOLP), loss of load 
expectation (LOLE), loss of load hours (LOLH), and expected energy not supplied (EENS). 
In Table 1, we investigate how these indices have been used in previous research to measure 
the capability of the HPS. According to Table 1, traditional reliability indices primarily 
focus on generation adequacy while neglecting transmission constraints, thus offering lim­
ited insight into the overall performance of the HPS. Given this limitation, there is a clear 
need to adopt alternative reliability indices that can capture the capability of the HPS.

resulting in multiple power capacity levels. For instance, the power plant with m power 
generators owns (m + 1) possible power capacity levels. The lowest-level 0 corresponds 
to all power generators malfunctioning, and the highest-level m corresponds to all normal 
power generators. Similarly, each transmission line is composed of several conductors. If 
a transmission line is composed of k conductors, then it owns (k + 1) power capacity levels

Therefore, all the power plants and the transmission lines in the HPS should be modeled 
as multistate, and the overall HPS can be represented as a multistate power grid network 
(MPGN) (Huang & Lin, 2024; Huang et al., 2022, 2023, 2024; Lin et al., 2019). In the 
MPGN model, network reliability is defined as the probability that the MPGN can success­

of the HPS. Lin and Yeh (2011) focused on a multistate power network to determine the 
maximal network reliability with the optimal transmission line assignment. Furthermore, 
Lin et al. (2012) evaluated network reliability by considering correlated failures among 
transmission lines. Among these studies, there is a lack of provision for transmission line 
routing to assist the administrator. In this study, the network reliability of the MPGN is first 
calculated to evaluate the possibility of meeting demand, and the transmission line routing 
can then be determined to support the administrator in dispatch decision-making.
This study aims to provide network reliability as a performance index to assist the admin­
istrator in evaluating the capability of the HPS. Moreover, the proposed algorithm also 
generates feasible transmission line routings that support more effective power dispatch 
decisions. To demonstrate the practicality of the proposed algorithm, an instance in Taiwan 
is used, with actual network topology and data. The remainder of this paper is described 
as follows. The construction of the MPGN model is established in detail in Sect. 2. The 
network reliability evaluation and the proposed algorithm are introduced in Sect. 3. An 
instance in Taiwan that illustrates the application of the proposed algorithm is presented in 
Sect. 4. Finally, Sect. 5 concludes the paper with a discussion and insights on transmission 
line routing.
2  Construction of the MPGN for HPS
Let G = (S, E, W) denote an MPGN where S = {Sk | k = 1, 2, …, p} with Sk being the kth 
source, E = {ei | i = p + 1, p + 2, …, p + n} with ei being the ith edge, and W = {wt | t = 1, 2, 
…, p + n} with wt being the tth maximal power capacity of St or et. Each edge ei consists of 
several conductors with reliability ri. In this study, the minimal path (MP) (Huang, 2023) 
is adopted to calculate network reliability, as it is particularly beneficial for identifying fea­
sible transmission line routings. An MP is defined as a set of edges from a source vertex to 
a sink vertex without cycles. It implies that an MP is a path whose proper subsets no longer 
comprise paths. For a pair of source vertex Sk and sink vertex, there exist mk MPs connecting 
them where k = 1, 2, …, p. Let Pk,j be the jth MP linking the kth source vertex Sk and sink ver­
tex for k = 1, 2, …, p, j = 1, 2, …, mk. To develop the MPGN model, the following assump­
tions reflecting the characteristics of the HPS under study should be taken into account:
1. Each vertex is perfectly reliable.
1 3

Annals of Operations Research
2. The power capacity of the source vertices Sk and the edges ei is a random variable that 
takes several possible values according to a given probability distribution.
3. The power capacity of different edges is statistically independent.
4. Flow in G must meet the flow-conservation law (Ford Jr & Fulkerson, 1962). In 
other words, no flow will decrease or increase at the edges and vertices during flow 
transmission.
3  Network reliability evaluation for MPGN
Given the demand d MW (megawatt) power at the sink, the network reliability Rd is the 
probability that the MPGN can successfully transmit at least d from multiple power plants 
(source vertices) to the destination (sink vertex). The vector X = (x1, x2, …, xt) is defined as 
a power capacity vector, where each xt is the current power capacity of St or et. To compute 
the network reliability Rd, let Ω represent the set of all feasible power capacity vectors X that 
satisfy the demand d. A straightforward approach is to enumerate all possible X within the 
set Ω and sum their probabilities. As per assumption 3, the probability Pr{X} is determined 
by multiplying the individual probabilities Pr{x1} × Pr{x2} × … × Pr{xp + n}. However, due 
to the expansive size of the network, calculating every feasible power capacity vector X to 
determine the network reliability Rd and tallying their probabilities becomes an impractical 
endeavor. To address this, the concept of minimal power vector (MPV) is utilized in this 
study, and some applicable concepts are defined as follows:
Definition 1:  An MPV X is a minimal vector in Ω.
Definition 2:   X ≤ Y: (x1, x2, …, xn) ≤ (y1, y2, …, yn) if xi ≤ yi for each i.
Definition 3:   X < Y: (x1, x2, …, xn) < (y1, y2, …, yn) if X ≤ Y and xi < yi for at least one i.
According to these definitions, if X is an MPV and Y is a power capacity vector with Y < X, 
then Y ∉ Ω. These MPVs are chosen for their efficiency in meeting demand and facilitate 
a more streamlined calculation of the network reliability Rd by focusing on the lower X in 
the set Ω. Suppose there are o MPVs: X1, X2, …, Xo, then the network reliability Rd can be 
efficiently calculated as follows:

4  Application for the MPGN model
To better illustrate the practicality of the proposed algorithm, the HPS in Taiwan is used as 
an instance. In this instance, the system structure of the HPS is depicted in Fig. 1a, while 
the topology network is shown in Fig. 1b, and there are a total of 11 sources, 24 vertices, 
and 47 edges. 
The administrator is responsible for maintaining a stable and reliable electricity sup­
ply to meet regional demand, particularly in southern Taiwan, which has a population of 
approximately 6 million. This region is not only densely populated but also strategically 
important, as it is home to the Southern Taiwan Science Park, which recorded a production 
output of USD 73.7 billion in 2024 and ranks as the leading science park in Taiwan. Given 
the vital role of the Southern Taiwan Science Park in high-tech manufacturing, ensuring 
the stable operation of the HPS in this region is an essential task for the administrator. In 
southeastern Taiwan, three power plant provide power supply, including Takuan Hydro­

e37} connecting S9 and T, P3,1 = {S11, e34, e35, e38, e39, e40, e44}, P3,2 = {S11, e34, e35, e41, e42},

5.1  Contribution of the research
To evaluate the capability of the high-voltage power system (HPS) and determine feasible 
transmission line routings, the HPS is modeled as a multistate network through network 
analysis. Considering that both power plants and transmission lines may operate under 
multiple capacity levels, a multistate power grid network (MPGN) model is developed to 
reflect the multistate characteristics of the HPS. Based on the concept of minimal path, an 
algorithm is proposed to calculate network reliability. In addition, the minimal power vec­
tors (MPVs) identified by the proposed algorithm also serve as feasible transmission line 
routings, thereby assisting the administrator in making power dispatch decisions. A real-
world HPS in Taiwan is presented to demonstrate the application of the MPGN model. The

framework for MPGN construction and network reliability evaluation is illustrated in Fig. 3, 
offering a comprehensive overview of the process involved in executing the work.
In addition, the probability tables can be constructed from historical data to represent 
the probability of different power capacity levels. By updating these tables dynamically, the 
MPGN model supports rapidly reassessing network reliability under changing environmen­
tal conditions. For example, in extreme weather affecting specific components, the network 
reliability can be recalculated immediately, providing valuable input for operational deci­
sions. In summary, the key contributions of this study include the following:
1. A novel MPGN model that incorporates multistate power capacities of power plants and 
transmission lines.
2. Network reliability is constructed to measure the capability of MPGN.
3. An innovative algorithm is developed to obtain network reliability and transmission 
line routing using the concept of minimal path.
4. Transmission line routing serves as a feasible power dispatch for the administrator and 
further makes dispatch decisions.
5.2  Future research
Despite the valuable contributions of this study to the decision-making process of the admin­
istrator, there are still specific concerns that need to be addressed. First, power losses may 
occur due to heat dissipation and aging of transmission lines during the transmission process 
in real-world HPS. Future research needs to incorporate this factor to make the model more 
realistic. In addition, since many countries aim to develop sustainable energy sources, it is 
crucial to include cost and carbon emissions, such as the grid emission factor and value-
adjusted levelized cost of electricity, in the evaluation indices. Although these issues have 
not yet been considered in the current model, its findings offer significant implications for 
the administrator in dispatch decision-making. Future research could focus on these issues 
to bridge the gaps in reliability evaluation problems with the HPS.
